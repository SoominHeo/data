<p><b>Mithridates VI</b> or <b>Mithradates VI</b> (<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Pronunciation" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Pronunciation"><span title="This word should have a pronunciation transcription or recording.">pronunciation?</span></a></i>]</sup>; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Μιθραδάτης, Μιθριδάτης</span>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Persian_language" title="Old Persian language">Old Persian</a> <i>Miθradāta</i>, "gift of <a href="/wiki/Mithra" title="Mithra">Mithra</a>"; 135–63 BC, also known as <b>Mithradates the Great</b> (<b>Megas</b>) and <b>Eupator Dionysius</b>, was king of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Pontus" title="Kingdom of Pontus">Pontus</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armenia_Minor" title="Armenia Minor">Armenia Minor</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>) from about 120–63 BC. Mithridates is remembered as one of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a>’s most formidable and successful enemies, who engaged three of the prominent generals from the late Roman Republic in the <a href="/wiki/Mithridatic_Wars" title="Mithridatic Wars">Mithridatic Wars</a>: <a href="/wiki/Sulla" title="Sulla">Lucius Cornelius Sulla</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lucullus" title="Lucullus">Lucius Licinius Lucullus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pompey" title="Pompey">Gnaeus Pompey Magnus</a>. He is often considered the greatest ruler of the Kingdom of Pontus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancestry.2C_family_and_early_life">Ancestry, family and early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithridates_VI_of_Pontus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ancestry, family and early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PonticKingdom.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1296" data-file-width="1551" height="292" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/PonticKingdom.png/350px-PonticKingdom.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/PonticKingdom.png/525px-PonticKingdom.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/PonticKingdom.png/700px-PonticKingdom.png 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PonticKingdom.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of the Kingdom of Pontus, Before the reign of Mithridates VI (dark purple), after his conquests (purple), his conquests in the first Mithridatic wars (pink) and Pontus' ally the Kingdom of Armenia (green).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Mithridates VI was a prince of <a href="/wiki/Persian_people" title="Persian people">Persian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek</a> ancestry. He claimed descent from <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great" title="Cyrus the Great">Cyrus the Great</a>, the family of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darius_the_Great" title="Darius the Great">Darius the Great</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Regent" title="Regent">Regent</a> <a href="/wiki/Antipater" title="Antipater">Antipater</a>, the generals of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> as well as the later kings <a href="/wiki/Antigonus_I_Monophthalmus" title="Antigonus I Monophthalmus">Antigonus I Monophthalmus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seleucus_I_Nicator" title="Seleucus I Nicator">Seleucus I Nicator</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iranicaonline.org_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-iranicaonline.org-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mithridates was born in the Pontic city of <a href="/wiki/Sinop,_Turkey" title="Sinop, Turkey">Sinope</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and was raised in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Pontus" title="Kingdom of Pontus">Kingdom of Pontus</a>. He was the first son among the children born to <a href="/wiki/Laodice_VI" title="Laodice VI">Laodice VI</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mithridates_V_of_Pontus" title="Mithridates V of Pontus">Mithridates V of Pontus</a> (reigned 150–120 BC). His father, Mithridates V, was a prince and the son of the former Pontic monarchs <a href="/wiki/Pharnaces_I_of_Pontus" title="Pharnaces I of Pontus">Pharnaces I of Pontus</a> and his wife-cousin <a href="/wiki/Nysa_(wife_of_Pharnaces_I_of_Pontus)" title="Nysa (wife of Pharnaces I of Pontus)">Nysa</a>. His mother, Laodice VI, was a Seleucid princess and the daughter of the Seleucid monarchs <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_IV_Epiphanes" title="Antiochus IV Epiphanes">Antiochus IV Epiphanes</a> and his wife-sister <a href="/wiki/Laodice_IV" title="Laodice IV">Laodice IV</a>.</p>
<p>Mithridates V was assassinated in about 120 BC in <a href="/wiki/Sinop,_Turkey" title="Sinop, Turkey">Sinope</a>, poisoned by unknown persons at a lavish banquet which he held.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He left the kingdom to the joint rule of Mithridates' mother, Laodice VI, Mithridates, and his younger brother, <a href="/wiki/Mithridates_Chrestus" title="Mithridates Chrestus">Mithridates Chrestus</a>. Neither Mithridates nor his younger brother were of age, and their mother retained all power as regent for the time being.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Laodice VI’s regency over Pontus was from 120 BC to 116 BC (even perhaps up to 113 BC) and favored Mithridates Chrestus over Mithridates. During his mother’s regency, he escaped from his mother's plots against him, and went into hiding.</p>
<p>Mithridates emerged from hiding, returning to Pontus between 116 BC and 113 BC and was hailed as king. He removed his mother and brother from the throne, imprisoning both, becoming the sole ruler of Pontus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mayor.2C_p.394_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mayor.2C_p.394-8">[8]</a></sup> Laodice VI died in prison, ostensibly of natural causes. Mithridates Chrestus may have died in prison also, or may have been tried for treason and executed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mayor.2C_p.394_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mayor.2C_p.394-8">[8]</a></sup> Mithridates gave both royal funerals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Mithridates first married his younger sister <a href="/wiki/Laodice_(sister-wife_of_Mithridates_VI_of_Pontus)" title="Laodice (sister-wife of Mithridates VI of Pontus)">Laodice</a>, aged 16.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> His goal was to preserve the purity of their bloodline, solidify his claim to the throne, to co-rule over Pontus, and to ensure the succession to his legitimate children.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_reign">Early reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithridates_VI_of_Pontus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mithridates entertained ambitions of making his state the dominant power in the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>. He first subjugated <a href="/wiki/Colchis" title="Colchis">Colchis</a>, a region east of the Black Sea, and prior to 164 BC, an independent kingdom. He then clashed for supremacy on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pontic_steppe" title="Pontic steppe">Pontic steppe</a> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scythian" title="Scythian">Scythian</a> King <a href="/wiki/Palacus" title="Palacus">Palacus</a>. The most important centres of <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tauric_Chersonesus" title="Tauric Chersonesus">Tauric Chersonesus</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bosporan_Kingdom" title="Bosporan Kingdom">Bosporan Kingdom</a> readily surrendered their independence in return for Mithridates' promises to protect them against the Scythians, their ancient enemies. After several abortive attempts to invade the Crimea, the Scythians and the allied <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhoxolanoi" title="Rhoxolanoi">Rhoxolanoi</a> suffered heavy losses at the hands of the Pontic general <a href="/wiki/Diophantus_(general)" title="Diophantus (general)">Diophantus</a> and accepted Mithridates as their overlord.</p>
<p>The young king then turned his attention to Anatolia, where Roman power was on the rise. He contrived to partition <a href="/wiki/Paphlagonia" title="Paphlagonia">Paphlagonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galatia" title="Galatia">Galatia</a> with King <a href="/wiki/Nicomedes_III_of_Bithynia" title="Nicomedes III of Bithynia">Nicomedes III of Bithynia</a>. It soon became clear to Mithridates that Nicomedes was steering his country into an anti-Pontic alliance with the expanding Roman Republic. When Mithridates fell out with Nicomedes over control of <a href="/wiki/Cappadocia" title="Cappadocia">Cappadocia</a>, and defeated him in a series of battles, the latter was constrained to openly enlist the assistance of Rome. The Romans twice interfered in the conflict on behalf of Nicomedes (95–92 BC), leaving Mithridates, should he wish to continue the expansion of his kingdom, with little choice other than to engage in a future Roman-Pontic war.</p>

<p>The next ruler of Bithynia, <a href="/wiki/Nicomedes_IV_of_Bithynia" title="Nicomedes IV of Bithynia">Nicomedes IV of Bithynia</a>, was a figurehead manipulated by the Romans. Mithridates plotted to overthrow him, but his attempts failed and Nicomedes IV, instigated by his Roman advisors, declared war on Pontus. Rome itself was involved in the <a href="/wiki/Social_War_(91%E2%80%9388_BC)" title="Social War (91–88 BC)">Social War</a>, a civil war with its Italian allies. Thus, in all of Roman Asia Province there were only two legions present in Macedonia. These legions combined with Nicomedes IV's army to invade Mithridates' kingdom of Pontus in 89 BC. Mithridates, however, won a decisive victory, scattering the Roman-led forces. His victorious forces were welcomed throughout Anatolia. The following year, 88 BC, Mithridates orchestrated a massacre of Roman and Italian settlers remaining in several Anatolian cities, essentially wiping out the Roman presence in the region. This episode is known as the <a href="/wiki/Asiatic_Vespers" title="Asiatic Vespers">Asiatic Vespers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Kingdom of Pontus comprised a mixed population in its <a href="/wiki/Ionians" title="Ionians">Ionian Greek</a> and Anatolian cities. The royal family moved the capital from <a href="/wiki/Amasya" title="Amasya">Amasya</a> to the Greek city of <a href="/wiki/Sinop,_Turkey" title="Sinop, Turkey">Sinope</a>. Its rulers tried to fully assimilate the potential of their subjects by showing a Greek face to the Greek world and an Iranian/Anatolian face to the Eastern world. Whenever the gap between the rulers and their Anatolian subjects became greater, they would put emphasis on their Persian origins. In this manner, the royal propaganda claimed heritage both from Persian and Greek rulers, including <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great" title="Cyrus the Great">Cyrus the Great</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darius_I_of_Persia" title="Darius I of Persia">Darius I of Persia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seleucus_I_Nicator" title="Seleucus I Nicator">Seleucus I Nicator</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Mithridates too posed as the champion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic_civilization" title="Hellenistic civilization">Hellenism</a>, but this was mainly to further his political ambitions; it is no proof that he felt a mission to promote its extension within his domains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Whatever his true intentions, the Greek cities (including <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>) defected to the side of Mithridates and welcomed his armies in mainland Greece, while his fleet besieged the Romans at <a href="/wiki/Rhodes" title="Rhodes">Rhodes</a>. Neighboring King of Armenia <a href="/wiki/Tigranes_the_Great" title="Tigranes the Great">Tigranes the Great</a> established an alliance with Mithridates and married one of Mithridates’ daughters, <a href="/wiki/Cleopatra_of_Pontus" title="Cleopatra of Pontus">Cleopatra of Pontus</a>. They would support each other in the coming conflict with Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Badmoutioun_Hayots.2C_Volume_I_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Badmoutioun_Hayots.2C_Volume_I-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Romans responded by organising a large invasion force to defeat him and remove him from power. The <a href="/wiki/First_Mithridatic_War" title="First Mithridatic War">First Mithridatic War</a>, fought between 88 BC and 84 BC, saw <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lucius_Cornelius_Sulla" title="Lucius Cornelius Sulla">Lucius Cornelius Sulla</a> force Mithridates VI out of Greece proper. After victory in several battles, Sulla received news of trouble back in Rome posed by his enemy <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Marius" title="Gaius Marius">Gaius Marius</a> and hurriedly concluded peace talks with Mithridates. As Sulla returned to <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> <a href="/wiki/Lucius_Licinius_Murena" title="Lucius Licinius Murena">Lucius Licinius Murena</a> was left in charge of Roman forces in Anatolia. The lenient peace treaty, which was never ratified by the Senate, allowed Mithridates VI to recoup his forces. Murena attacked Mithridates in 83 BC, provoking the <a href="/wiki/Second_Mithridatic_War" title="Second Mithridatic War">Second Mithridatic War</a> from 83 BC to 81 BC. Mithridates scored a victory over Murena's green forces before peace was again declared by treaty.</p>
<p>When Rome attempted to annex Bithynia (bequested to Rome by its last king) nearly a decade later, Mithridates VI attacked with an even larger army, leading to the <a href="/wiki/Third_Mithridatic_War" title="Third Mithridatic War">Third Mithridatic War</a> from 73 BC to 63 BC. First <a href="/wiki/Lucullus" title="Lucullus">Lucullus</a> and then <a href="/wiki/Pompey" title="Pompey">Pompey</a> were sent against Mithridates VI, who surged back to retake his kingdom of Pontus, but was at last defeated by Pompey. After Pompey defeated him in Pontus in 66 BC, Mithridates VI fled with a small army to Colchis (modern Georgia) and then over the Caucasus Mountains to <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a> and made plans to raise yet another army to take on the Romans. His eldest living son, <a href="/wiki/Machares" title="Machares">Machares</a>, viceroy of Cimmerian Bosporus, was unwilling to aid his father. Mithridates had Machares killed, and Mithridates took the throne of the <a href="/wiki/Bosporan_Kingdom" title="Bosporan Kingdom">Bosporan Kingdom</a>. Mithridates then ordered the conscriptions and preparations for war. In 63 BC, <a href="/wiki/Pharnaces_II_of_Pontus" title="Pharnaces II of Pontus">Pharnaces II of Pontus</a>, one of his sons, led a rebellion against his father, joined by Roman exiles in the core of Mithridates' Pontic army. Mithridates withdrew to the citadel in <a href="/wiki/Panticapaeum" title="Panticapaeum">Panticapaeum</a>, where he committed suicide. Pompey buried Mithridates in the rock-cut tombs of his ancestors in Amasya, the old capital of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pontus" title="Pontus">Pontus</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assassination_conspiracy">Assassination conspiracy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithridates_VI_of_Pontus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Assassination conspiracy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the time of the First Mithridatic War, a group of Mithridates' friends plotted to kill him. These were Mynnio and Philotimus of Smyrna, and Cleisthenes and <a href="/wiki/Asclepiodotus_of_Lesbos" title="Asclepiodotus of Lesbos">Asclepiodotus of Lesbos</a>. Asclepiodotus changed his mind and became an <a href="/wiki/Informant" title="Informant">informant</a>. He arranged to have Mithridates hide under a couch to hear the plot against him. The other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conspiracy_(political)" title="Conspiracy (political)">conspirators</a> were <a href="/wiki/Torture" title="Torture">tortured</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Executed" title="Executed">executed</a>. However, this was not enough for Mithridates, who also killed all of the plotters' families and friends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Representation_of_Power">Representation of Power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithridates_VI_of_Pontus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Representation of Power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CoinOfMithVI.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="483" data-file-width="870" height="122" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/CoinOfMithVI.jpg/220px-CoinOfMithVI.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/CoinOfMithVI.jpg/330px-CoinOfMithVI.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/CoinOfMithVI.jpg/440px-CoinOfMithVI.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CoinOfMithVI.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A coin depicting Mithridates VI</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Where his ancestors pursued <a href="/wiki/Philhellenism" title="Philhellenism">philhellenism</a> as a means of attaining respectability and prestige among the Hellenistic kingdoms, Mithridates VI made use of Hellenism as a political tool. As protector of Greek cities on the Black Sea and in Asia against barbarism, Mithridates VI logically became protector of Greece and Greek culture, and would use this stance in his clashes with Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Strabo mentions that Chersonesus buckled under the pressure of the barbarians and asked Mithridates VI to become its protector (7.4.3. c.308). The most impressive symbol of Mithridates VI's approbation with Greece (Athens in particular) appears at <a href="/wiki/Delos" title="Delos">Delos</a>: a <a href="/wiki/Heroon" title="Heroon">heroon</a> dedicated to the Pontic king in 102/1 by the Athenian Helianax, a priest of Poseidon Aisios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> A dedication at <a href="/wiki/Delos" title="Delos">Delos</a>, by Dicaeus, a priest of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarapis" title="Sarapis">Sarapis</a>, was made in 94/93 BC on behalf of the Athenians, Romans, and "King Mithridates Eupator Dionysus."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Greek styles mixed with Persian elements also abound on official Pontic <a href="/wiki/Numismatics" title="Numismatics">coins</a> – Perseus was favored as an intermediary between both worlds, East and West.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Certainly influenced by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>, Mithridates VI extended his propaganda from "defender" of Greece to the "great liberator" of the Greek world as war with <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a> became inevitable. The Romans were easily translated into "barbarians", in the same sense as the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persian Empire</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Persian_Wars" title="Greco-Persian Wars">war with Persia</a> in the first half of the 5th century BC and during Alexander's campaign. How many Greeks genuinely bought into this claim will never be known. It served its purpose, however. At least partially because of it, Mithridates VI was able to fight the <a href="/wiki/First_Mithridatic_War" title="First Mithridatic War">First War with Rome</a> on Greek soil, and maintain the allegiance of Greece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> His campaign for the allegiance of the Greeks was aided in no small part by his enemy Sulla, who allowed his troops to <a href="/wiki/Delphi#Treasuries" title="Delphi">sack the city of Delphi</a> and plunder many of the city's most famous treasures to help finance his military expenses.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithridates_VI_of_Pontus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After Pompey defeated him in Pontus, Mithridates VI fled to the lands north of the Black Sea in the winter of 66 BC in the hope that he could raise a new army and carry on the war. However, his preparations proved to be too harsh on the local nobles and populace, and they rebelled against his rule. He allegedly attempted <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a> by poison; this attempt failed, however, because of his immunity to the poison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rome-leglay_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-rome-leglay-21">[21]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Appian" title="Appian">Appian's</a> <i>Roman History</i>, he then requested his Gaulish bodyguard and friend, Bituitus, to kill him by the sword:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Mithridates then took out some poison that he always carried next to his sword, and mixed it. There two of his daughters, who were still girls growing up together, named Mithridates and Nysa, who had been betrothed to the kings of [Ptolemaic] Egypt and of Cyprus, asked him to let them have some of the poison first, and insisted strenuously and prevented him from drinking it until they had taken some and swallowed it. The drug took effect on them at once; but upon Mithridates, although he walked around rapidly to hasten its action, it had no effect, because he had accustomed himself to other drugs by continually trying them as a means of protection against poisoners. These are still called the Mithridatic drugs.</p>
<p>Seeing a certain Bituitus there, an officer of the Gauls, he said to him, "I have profited much from your right arm against my enemies. I shall profit from it most of all if you will kill me, and save from the danger of being led in a Roman triumph one who has been an autocrat so many years, and the ruler of so great a kingdom, but who is now unable to die by poison because, like a fool, he has fortified himself against the poison of others. Although I have kept watch and ward against all the poisons that one takes with his food, I have not provided against that domestic poison, always the most dangerous to kings, the treachery of army, children, and friends." Bituitus, thus appealed to, rendered the king the service that he desired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cassius_Dio" title="Cassius Dio">Cassius Dio</a> <i>Roman History</i>, on the other hand, records a different account:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Mithridates had tried to make away with himself, and after first removing his wives and remaining children by poison, he had swallowed all that was left; yet neither by that means nor by the sword was he able to perish by his own hands. For the poison, although deadly, did not prevail over him, since he had inured his constitution to it, taking precautionary antidotes in large doses every day; and the force of the sword blow was lessened on account of the weakness of his hand, caused by his age and present misfortunes, and as a result of taking the poison, whatever it was. When, therefore, he failed to take his life through his own efforts and seemed to linger beyond the proper time, those whom he had sent against his son fell upon him and hastened his end with their swords and spears. Thus Mithridates, who had experienced the most varied and remarkable fortune, had not even an ordinary end to his life. For he desired to die, albeit unwillingly, and though eager to kill himself was unable to do so; but partly by poison and partly by the sword he was at once self-slain and murdered by his foes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>At the behest of Pompey, Mithridates' body was later buried alongside his ancestors (in either <a href="/wiki/Sinop,_Turkey" title="Sinop, Turkey">Sinope</a> or <a href="/wiki/Amasya" title="Amasya">Amaseia</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mount_Mithridat" title="Mount Mithridat">Mount Mithridat</a> in the central <a href="/wiki/Kerch" title="Kerch">Kerch</a> and the town of <a href="/wiki/Yevpatoria" title="Yevpatoria">Yevpatoria</a> in Crimea commemorate his name.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mithridates.27_antidote">Mithridates' antidote</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithridates_VI_of_Pontus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mithridates' antidote">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mithridate" title="Mithridate">Mithridate</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:De_medicina_V00117_00000006.tif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3153" data-file-width="2088" height="332" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/De_medicina_V00117_00000006.tif/lossy-page1-220px-De_medicina_V00117_00000006.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/De_medicina_V00117_00000006.tif/lossy-page1-330px-De_medicina_V00117_00000006.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/De_medicina_V00117_00000006.tif/lossy-page1-440px-De_medicina_V00117_00000006.tif.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:De_medicina_V00117_00000006.tif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>De medicina</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In his youth, after the assassination of his father Mithridates V in 120 BC, Mithridates is said to have lived in the wilderness for seven years, inuring himself to hardship. While there, and after his accession, he cultivated an immunity to poisons by regularly ingesting sub-lethal doses of the same.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> He invented a complex "universal antidote" against poisoning; several versions are described in the literature. <a href="/wiki/Aulus_Cornelius_Celsus" title="Aulus Cornelius Celsus">Aulus Cornelius Celsus</a> gives one in his <a href="/wiki/De_Medicina" title="De Medicina">De Medicina</a> and names it <i>Antidotum Mithridaticum</i>, whence English <i><a href="/wiki/Mithridate" title="Mithridate">mithridate</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Pliny the Elder's version comprised 54 ingredients to be placed in a flask and matured for at least two months. After Mithridates' death in 63 BC, many imperial Roman physicians claimed to possess and improve on the original formula, which they touted as Mithradatium. In keeping with most medical practices of his era, Mithridates' anti-poison routines included a religious component; they were supervised by the <i>Agari</i>, a group of Scythian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shaman" title="Shaman">shamans</a> who never left him. Mithridates was reportedly guarded in his sleep by a horse, a bull, and a stag, which would whinny, bellow, and bleat whenever anyone approached the royal bed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mithridates_as_polyglot">Mithridates as polyglot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithridates_VI_of_Pontus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mithridates as polyglot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>'s account of famous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyglot_(person)" title="Polyglot (person)">polyglots</a>, Mithridates could <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperpolyglot" title="Hyperpolyglot">speak the languages of all</a> the twenty-two nations he governed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> This reputation led to the use of Mithridates' name as title in some later works on comparative linguistics, such as <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Gessner" title="Conrad Gessner">Conrad Gessner</a>'s <i>Mithridates de differentis linguis,</i> (1555), and Adelung and Vater's <i>Mithridates oder allgemeine Sprachenkunde</i> (1806–1817).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wives.2C_mistresses_and_children">Wives, mistresses and children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithridates_VI_of_Pontus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Wives, mistresses and children">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mithridates VI had wives and mistresses, by whom he had various children. The names he gave his children are a representation of his Persian, Greek heritage and of his ancestry.</p>
<ol>
<li>First wife, <a href="/wiki/Laodice_(sister-wife_of_Mithridates_VI_of_Pontus)" title="Laodice (sister-wife of Mithridates VI of Pontus)">his sister Laodice</a>. They were married from 115/113 BC till about 90 BC. Mithridates with Laodice had various children:
<ul>
<li>Sons: Mithridates, <a href="/wiki/Arcathius" title="Arcathius">Arcathius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Machares" title="Machares">Machares</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pharnaces_II_of_Pontus" title="Pharnaces II of Pontus">Pharnaces II of Pontus</a></li>
<li>Daughters: <a href="/wiki/Cleopatra_of_Pontus" title="Cleopatra of Pontus">Cleopatra of Pontus</a> (sometimes called Cleopatra the Elder to distinguish her from her sister of the same name) and <a href="/wiki/Drypetina" title="Drypetina">Drypetina</a> (a diminutive form of "<a href="/wiki/Drypetis" title="Drypetis">Drypetis</a>"). Drypetina was Mithridates VI’s most devoted daughter. Her baby teeth never fell out, so she had a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supernumerary_teeth" title="Supernumerary teeth">double set of teeth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Second wife, the Greek Macedonian Noblewoman, <a href="/wiki/Monime" title="Monime">Monime</a>. They were married from about 89/88 BC till 72/71 BC. By whom, he had:
<ul>
<li>Daughter: <a href="/wiki/Athenais_Philostorgos_II" title="Athenais Philostorgos II">Athenais</a>, who married King <a href="/wiki/Ariobarzanes_II_of_Cappadocia" title="Ariobarzanes II of Cappadocia">Ariobarzanes II of Cappadocia</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Third wife, Greek woman <a href="/wiki/Berenice_of_Chios" title="Berenice of Chios">Berenice of Chios</a>, married from 86–72/71 BC</li>
<li>Fourth wife, Greek woman <a href="/wiki/Stratonice_of_Pontus" title="Stratonice of Pontus">Stratonice of Pontus</a>, married from after 86–63 BC
<ul>
<li>Son: <a href="/wiki/Xiphares" title="Xiphares">Xiphares</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Fifth wife, unknown</li>
<li>Sixth wife, Caucasian woman <a href="/wiki/Hypsicratea" title="Hypsicratea">Hypsicratea</a>, married from an unknown date to 63 BC</li>
</ol>
<p>One of his mistresses was the Galatian Celtic Princess <a href="/wiki/Adobogiona_the_Elder" title="Adobogiona the Elder">Adobogiona the Elder</a>. By Adobogiona, Mithridates had two children: a son called <a href="/wiki/Mithridates_I_of_the_Bosporus" title="Mithridates I of the Bosporus">Mithridates I of the Bosporus</a> and a daughter called <a href="/wiki/Adobogiona_the_Younger" title="Adobogiona the Younger">Adobogiona the Younger</a>.</p>
<p>His sons born from his concubine were Cyrus, Xerxes, Darius, <a href="/wiki/Ariarathes_IX_of_Cappadocia" title="Ariarathes IX of Cappadocia">Ariarathes IX of Cappadocia</a>, Artaphernes, Oxathres, Phoenix (Mithridates’ son by a mistress of Syrian descent) and Exipodras, named after kings of the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persian Empire</a>, which he claimed ancestry from. His daughters born from his concubine were Nysa, Eupatra, Cleopatra the Younger, Mithridates and <a href="/wiki/Orsabaris" title="Orsabaris">Orsabaris</a>. Nysa and Mithridates, were engaged to the <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_dynasty" title="Ptolemaic dynasty">Egyptian Greek Pharaohs</a> <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_XII_Auletes" title="Ptolemy XII Auletes">Ptolemy XII Auletes</a> and his brother <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_of_Cyprus" title="Ptolemy of Cyprus">Ptolemy of Cyprus</a>.</p>
<p>In 63 BC, when the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Pontus" title="Kingdom of Pontus">Kingdom of Pontus</a> was annexed by the Roman general <a href="/wiki/Pompey" title="Pompey">Pompey</a> the remaining sisters, wives, mistresses and children of Mithridates VI in Pontus were put to death. Plutarch writing in his lives (Pompey v.45) states that Mithridates' sister and five of his children took part in Pompey's triumphal procession on this return to Rome in 61 BC.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cappadocia" title="Cappadocia">Cappadocian</a> <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek nobleman</a> and <a href="/wiki/High_priest" title="High priest">high priest</a> of the temple-state of <a href="/wiki/Comana_(Cappadocia)" title="Comana (Cappadocia)">Comana, Cappadocia</a> <a href="/wiki/Archelaus_(high_priest_of_Comana_Cappadocia)" title="Archelaus (high priest of Comana Cappadocia)">Archelaus</a> had descended from Mithridates VI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> He claimed to be a son of Mithridates VI,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> however chronologically Archelaus may have been a maternal grandson of the Pontic king, who his father was Mithridates VI’s favorite general may have married one of the daughters of Mithridates VI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithridates_VI_of_Pontus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The demise of Mithridates VI is detailed in the 1673 play <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mithridate_(play)" title="Mithridate (play)">Mithridate</a></i> written by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Racine" title="Jean Racine">Jean Racine</a>. This play is the basis for several 18th century operas including one of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozart" title="Mozart">Mozart</a>'s earliest, known most commonly by its Italian name, <i><a href="/wiki/Mitridate,_re_di_Ponto" title="Mitridate, re di Ponto">Mitridate, re di Ponto</a></i> (1770).</li>
<li>Mithridates is the subject of the opera <i><a href="/wiki/Mitridate_Eupatore" title="Mitridate Eupatore">Mitridate Eupatore</a></i> (1707) by <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Scarlatti" title="Alessandro Scarlatti">Alessandro Scarlatti</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">Ralph Waldo Emerson</a> included his "Mithridates" in his 1847 "Poems".</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexandre_Dumas,_p%C3%A8re" title="Alexandre Dumas, père">Alexandre Dumas</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Count_of_Monte_Cristo" title="The Count of Monte Cristo">The Count of Monte Cristo</a></i> refers to the potential of a <a href="/wiki/Mithridate" title="Mithridate">mithridate</a> as an instrument both of defense and offence.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/William_Wordsworth" title="William Wordsworth">William Wordsworth</a>, amidst casting about for poetic themes in <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Prelude" title="s:The Prelude">The Prelude</a></i> (Bk i vv 186 ff):</li>
</ul>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Sometimes, more sternly moved, I would relate<br/>
How vanquished Mithridates northward passed,<br/>
And, hidden in the cloud of years, became<br/>
Odin, the Father of a race by whom<br/>
Perished the Roman Empire.</p>
</blockquote>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">James Joyce</a> alludes to Mithridates' immunity to poison in his love poem <i>Though I Thy Mithridates Were</i>.</li>
<li>The poet <a href="/wiki/A._E._Housman" title="A. E. Housman">A. E. Housman</a> alludes to Mithridates' antidote in the final stanza of <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Terence,_This_Is_Stupid_Stuff" title="s:Terence, This Is Stupid Stuff">"Terence, This Is Stupid Stuff"</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/A_Shropshire_Lad" title="A Shropshire Lad">A Shropshire Lad</a></i>:</li>
</ul>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>There was a king reigned in the East:<br/>
There, when kings will sit to feast,<br/>
They get their fill before they think<br/>
With poisoned meat and poisoned drink.<br/>
He gathered all that springs to birth<br/>
From the many-venomed earth;<br/>
First a little, thence to more,<br/>
He sampled all her killing store;<br/>
And easy, smiling, seasoned sound,<br/>
Sate the king when healths went round.<br/>
They put <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> in his meat<br/>
And stared aghast to watch him eat;<br/>
They poured <a href="/wiki/Strychnine" title="Strychnine">strychnine</a> in his cup<br/>
And shook to see him drink it up:<br/>
They shook, they stared as white’s their shirt:<br/>
Them it was their poison hurt.<br/>
–I tell the tale that I heard told.<br/>
Mithridates, he died old.</p>
</blockquote>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dorothy_L._Sayers" title="Dorothy L. Sayers">Dorothy L. Sayers</a>' <a href="/wiki/Detective_fiction" title="Detective fiction">detective novel</a> "<a href="/wiki/Strong_Poison" title="Strong Poison">Strong Poison</a>", from 1929, has the protagonist, <a href="/wiki/Lord_Peter_Wimsey" title="Lord Peter Wimsey">Lord Peter Wimsey</a> solve a case of murder by <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> poisoning, and quotes the last line from Housman's poem.</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Grass_Crown_(novel)" title="The Grass Crown (novel)">The Grass Crown</a></i>, the second in the <i><a href="/wiki/Masters_of_Rome" title="Masters of Rome">Masters of Rome</a></i> series, <a href="/wiki/Colleen_McCullough" title="Colleen McCullough">Colleen McCullough</a> describes in detail the various aspects of his life – the murder of <a href="/wiki/Laodice_(sister-wife_of_Mithridates_VI_of_Pontus)" title="Laodice (sister-wife of Mithridates VI of Pontus)">Laodice</a>, and the Roman Consul who, quite alone and surrounded by the Pontic army, ordered Mithridates to leave Cappadocia immediately and go back to Pontus – which he did.</li>
<li><i>The Last King</i> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical_novel" title="Historical novel">historical novel</a> by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Curtis_Ford" title="Michael Curtis Ford">Michael Curtis Ford</a> about the King and his exploits against the Roman Republic. Mithridates is a major character in <a href="/wiki/Poul_Anderson" title="Poul Anderson">Poul Anderson</a>'s novel <i>The Golden Slave.</i></li>
<li>In the novel <i>Mithridates is Dead</i> (Spanish: <a class="external text" href="http://www.bubok.es/download.php?book=MTkwNzk3LTIwMTAxMjAyLQ==" rel="nofollow"><i>Mitrídates ha muerto</i></a>), <a href="/wiki/Ignasi_Rib%C3%B3" title="Ignasi Ribó">Ignasi Ribó</a> traces parallels between the historical figures of Mithridates and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osama_Bin_Laden" title="Osama Bin Laden">Osama Bin Laden</a>. Within a postmodern narrative of the making and unmaking of history, Ribó suggests that the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a> on the United States closely paralleled the massacre of Roman citizens in 88 B.C. and prompted similar consequences, namely the imperialist overstretch of the American and Roman republics respectively. Furthermore, he suggests that the ensuing <a href="/wiki/Mithridatic_Wars" title="Mithridatic Wars">Mithridatic Wars</a> were one of the key factors in the demise of Rome's republican regime, as well as in the spread of the Christian faith in Asia Minor and eventually throughout the whole Roman Empire. The novel implies that the current events in the world might have similar unforeseen consequences.</li>
<li>In <i>The King's Gambit</i>, the first volume of the <a href="/wiki/SPQR_series" title="SPQR series">SPQR series</a> by <a href="/wiki/John_Maddox_Roberts" title="John Maddox Roberts">John Maddox Roberts</a>, the protagonist, Decius Metellus, becomes aware of a plot between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gnaeus_Pompeius_Magnus" title="Gnaeus Pompeius Magnus">Pompey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Licinius_Crassus" title="Marcus Licinius Crassus">Crassus</a> to relieve Lucullus of command and allow Pompey to lead the final campaign against Mithradates. At the time of this novel, Decius reflects that Mithradates has successfully resisted Roman military campaigns for so long that the public has built him up as some kind of superhuman <a href="/wiki/Bogeyman" title="Bogeyman">bogeyman</a>.</li>
</ul>
