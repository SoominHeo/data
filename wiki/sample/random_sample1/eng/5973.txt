<p><b>Mikhail Leontyevich Mil</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Михаи́л Лео́нтьевич Миль</span>; 22 November 1909 – 31 January 1970) was a Soviet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerospace_engineer" title="Aerospace engineer">aerospace engineer</a>. He founded the <a href="/wiki/Mil_Moscow_Helicopter_Plant" title="Mil Moscow Helicopter Plant">Mil Moscow Helicopter Plant</a>, which is responsible for many well-known Soviet <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopter</a> models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikhail_Mil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mil was born in <a href="/wiki/Irkutsk" title="Irkutsk">Irkutsk</a>. His father was an employee of the <a href="/wiki/Trans-Siberian_Railway" title="Trans-Siberian Railway">Trans-Siberian Railway</a>, and his mother was a dentist. His grandfather was a <a href="/wiki/Cantonist" title="Cantonist">cantonist</a> who had been drafted from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Libava" title="Libava">Libava</a> (today <a href="/wiki/Liep%C4%81ja" title="Liepāja">Liepāja</a>), <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a>, and who settled in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> after 25 years in the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Russian_Navy" title="Imperial Russian Navy">Imperial Russian Navy</a>.</p>
<p>At age 12, Mil won first prize for a model glider competition. In 1926 he entered the <a href="/wiki/Tomsk_Polytechnic_University" title="Tomsk Polytechnic University">Siberian Technological Institute</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tomsk" title="Tomsk">Tomsk</a>; however, since there was no curriculum for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aeronautical_engineering" title="Aeronautical engineering">aeronautical engineering</a>, he transferred in 1928 to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Polytechnical_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Don Polytechnical Institute (page does not exist)">Don Polytechnical Institute</a> in <a href="/wiki/Novocherkassk" title="Novocherkassk">Novocherkassk</a>, where he was able to specialize in aviation. He married a fellow student, P.G. Rudenko, in 1932 and 4 daughters and a son followed.</p>
<p>After graduating from the Institute in 1931, Mil began his career at <a href="/wiki/TsAGI" title="TsAGI">TsAGI</a>, too late to work under its original founder, <a href="/wiki/Nikolay_Yegorovich_Zhukovsky" title="Nikolay Yegorovich Zhukovsky">Nikolay Yegorovich Zhukovsky</a>. He specialized in the design of <a href="/wiki/Autogyro" title="Autogyro">autogyros</a>, and was an assistant to his future rival, <a href="/wiki/Nikolay_Kamov" title="Nikolay Kamov">Nikolay Kamov</a>. With the start of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Mil was drafted into the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a> and fought on the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Front_(World_War_II)" title="Eastern Front (World War II)">Eastern Front</a> in 1941 near <a href="/wiki/Yelnya" title="Yelnya">Yelnya</a>. In 1943 he was called back to continue <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a> in improving the stability and control of combat aircraft. He completed his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dissertation" title="Dissertation">dissertations</a> ("Candidate", 1943, <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Ph.D.</a>, 1945) and in 1947 headed the Helicopter Lab at TsAGI, which was later turned into the Moscow Helicopter Plant.</p>
<p>Mil's creations won many domestic and international awards and set 69 world records. Most notably, the <a href="/wiki/Mil_Mi-4" title="Mil Mi-4">Mil Mi-4</a> won a Gold Medal in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brussels_International_Exhibition" title="Brussels International Exhibition">Brussels International Exhibition</a> in 1958. In 1971, after his death, his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mil_Mi-12" title="Mil Mi-12">Mil Mi-12</a> won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sikorsky_Prize" title="Sikorsky Prize">Sikorsky Prize</a> as the most powerful helicopter in the world. Unlike his Soviet counterpart, Nikolai Kamov, Mil enjoyed great prestige due to his single-rotor helicopters, as Kamov used the co-axial rotor layout, which was more controversial.</p>
<p>Mil died in 1970 in Moscow and was buried in Yudinskoe Cemetery in the outskirts of Moscow.</p>
