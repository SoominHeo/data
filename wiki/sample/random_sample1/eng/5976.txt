<p><b>Mikheil Gelovani</b> (<a href="/wiki/Georgian_language" title="Georgian language">Georgian</a>: <span lang="ka" xml:lang="ka">მიხეილ გელოვანი</span>, <a href="/wiki/Russification" title="Russification">Russified</a> as Михаи́л Гео́ргиевич Гелова́ни, <i>Mikhail Georgievich Gelovani</i>; January 6 [<a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">O.S.</a> December 25, 1892] 1893 – December 21, 1956) was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georgian_people" title="Georgian people">Georgian</a>-<a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> actor, known for his many portrayals of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a> in cinema.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikheil_Gelovani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikheil_Gelovani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mikheil Gelovani was a descendant of the old Georgian princely house of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gelovani" title="Gelovani">Gelovani</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DG_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-DG-1">[1]</a></sup> He made his stage debut in a theater in <a href="/wiki/Batumi" title="Batumi">Batumi</a> during 1913. From 1919 to 1920, he attended the Drama Studio in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tiflis" title="Tiflis">Tiflis</a>. In the two following years, he was a member of the cast in the city's <a href="/wiki/Rustaveli_Theatre" title="Rustaveli Theatre">Rustaveli Theatre</a>. From 1923, he worked as an actor and a director in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georgian_SSR" title="Georgian SSR">Georgian SSR</a>'s <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Goskinprom&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Goskinprom (page does not exist)">Goskinprom</a> film studio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-T_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-T-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1924, he first appeared on screen in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Three_Lives_(film)" title="Three Lives (film)">Three Lives</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He moved to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armenian_SSR" title="Armenian SSR">Armenian SSR</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armenkino" title="Armenkino">Armenkino</a> production unit in 1927. In addition to his cinematic work, Gelovani continued to appear in theater, and performed on stages in <a href="/wiki/Kutaisi" title="Kutaisi">Kutaisi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baku" title="Baku">Baku</a>. In 1936 he returned to the ensemble of the Rustaveli Theatre, and remained there for three years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-P_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-P-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antebellum">Antebellum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikheil_Gelovani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Antebellum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1938, Gelovani first portrayed Stalin in <a href="/wiki/Mikheil_Chiaureli" title="Mikheil Chiaureli">Mikheil Chiaureli</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Great_Dawn" title="The Great Dawn">The Great Dawn</a></i>. His performance won him the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Red_Banner_of_Labour" title="Order of the Red Banner of Labour">Order of the Red Banner of Labour</a> on 1 February 1939 and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stalin_Prize" title="Stalin Prize">Stalin Prize</a> during 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-T_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-T-2">[2]</a></sup> Afterwards, Gelovani "established a monopoly on the role of Stalin", which he continued to portray in twelve other pictures until the premier's death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Gelovani greatly resembled Stalin physically, except in his stature: he was much taller than the latter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Reportedly, he was not the premier's favorite candidate for depicting himself on screen: since he was Georgian, he mimicked Stalin's accent "to perfection". Therefore, the leader personally preferred <a href="/wiki/Aleksei_Dikiy" title="Aleksei Dikiy">Aleksei Dikiy</a>, who used classic Russian pronunciation. However, Gelovani appeared in his role much more than Dikiy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> According to the <i>The Guinness Book of Movie Facts and Feats</i>, Gelovani had probably portrayed the same historical figure more than any other actor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> When the two met, the general secretary told the actor: "you are observing me thoroughly... You do not waste time, do you?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Soviet cinema played an important part in cultivating the leader's <a href="/wiki/Cult_of_personality" title="Cult of personality">cult of personality</a>: from 1937 and onward, in a gradual process, Stalin's reign was legitimized by depicting him as <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Vladimir Lenin</a>'s most devout follower<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and by positively presenting historical autocrats - like in <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Eisenstein" title="Sergei Eisenstein">Sergei Eisenstein</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Ivan_the_Terrible_(film)" title="Ivan the Terrible (film)">Ivan the Terrible</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikheil_Gelovani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to his identification with Stalin, Gelovani was barred from playing other roles in cinema; he was not allowed to depict "mere mortals".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> From 1942 to 1948, he was a member of the cast in the <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Art_Theatre" title="Moscow Art Theatre">Gorky Moscow Art Theatre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-P_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-P-4">[4]</a></sup> During World War II, the personality cult was abandoned in favor of patriotic motifs, but returned already at the war's late stages, and with greater intensity than ever after 1945: Stalin was soon credited as the sole architect of victory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In the postwar films in which he portrayed him – <i><a href="/wiki/The_Vow_(1946_film)" title="The Vow (1946 film)">The Vow</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fall_of_Berlin_(film)" title="The Fall of Berlin (film)">The Fall of Berlin</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Unforgettable_Year_1919" title="The Unforgettable Year 1919">The Unforgettable Year 1919</a></i> – Gelovani presented the leader as "a living god".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The actor was awarded three more Stalin Prizes, all of which were granted for his performances of the premier in film: in 1942 for <i>The Defence of Tsaritsyn</i>, in 1947 for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Vow_(1946_film)" title="The Vow (1946 film)">The Vow</a></i> and in 1950 for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fall_of_Berlin_(film)" title="The Fall of Berlin (film)">The Fall of Berlin</a></i>. On 3 June 1950, he was given the title <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Artist_of_the_USSR" title="People's Artist of the USSR">People's Artist of the USSR</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-T_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-T-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After Stalin's death in 1953, Gelovani was denied new roles in films, since he was <a href="/wiki/Typecasting_(acting)" title="Typecasting (acting)">completely identified with the character</a> of the dead ruler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-C-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> From 1953 until his death in 1956, he acted in Moscow's <a class="extiw" href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%93%D0%BE%D1%81%D1%83%D0%B4%D0%B0%D1%80%D1%81%D1%82%D0%B2%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%BD%D1%8B%D0%B9_%D1%82%D0%B5%D0%B0%D1%82%D1%80_%D0%BA%D0%B8%D0%BD%D0%BE%D0%B0%D0%BA%D1%82%D1%91%D1%80%D0%B0" title="ru:Государственный театр киноактёра">State Theater for Film Actors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-P_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-P-4">[4]</a></sup> Andreas Kilb wrote that he ended his life "a pitiful <i><a href="/wiki/Kagemusha" title="Kagemusha">Kagemusha</a></i>" of Stalin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Gelovani is buried in the <a href="/wiki/Novodevichy_Cemetery" title="Novodevichy Cemetery">Novodevichy Cemetery</a>, alongside his wife Ludmila.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following <a href="/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev" title="Nikita Khrushchev">Nikita Khrushchev</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secret_Speech" title="Secret Speech">Secret Speech</a> in 1956, most of the pictures he appeared in as Stalin were either banned or had the relevant scenes removed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-C-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
