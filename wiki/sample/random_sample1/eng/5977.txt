<p><b>Mikhail Petrovich Kirponos</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Михаи́л Петро́вич Кирпоно́с</span>, <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">Ukrainian</a>: <span lang="uk" xml:lang="uk">Михайло Петрович Кирпонос, <i>Mykhailo Petrovych Kyrponos</i></span>; 12 January 1892 – 20 September 1941) was a <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/Ukrainians" title="Ukrainians">Ukrainian</a> general of the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a>. Being accorded the highest military decoration, the <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Hero of the Soviet Union">Hero of the Soviet Union</a> title, for the skill and courage in commanding a <a href="/wiki/Division_(military)" title="Division (military)">division</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Winter_War" title="Winter War">1939-1940 Finnish campaign</a>, Kirponos is mostly remembered for his crucial role in organizing and leading the defense of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ukrainian_SSR" title="Ukrainian SSR">Soviet Ukraine</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Brody_(1941)" title="Battle of Brody (1941)">Battle of Brody</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Uman" title="Battle of Uman">Battle of Uman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kiev_(1941)" title="Battle of Kiev (1941)">Kiev</a> in the first months of the <a href="/wiki/Operation_Barbarossa" title="Operation Barbarossa">1941 invasion of the Soviet Union</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_powers_of_World_War_II" title="Axis powers of World War II">Axis forces</a> (led by <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>). He was <a href="/wiki/Killed_in_action" title="Killed in action">killed in action</a> in the defense of <a href="/wiki/Kiev" title="Kiev">Kiev</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikhail_Kirponos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kirponos was born in a poor peasant family and worked as a forester. He was conscripted in 1915 and took part in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. In 1917 he joined the Red Army, fought in the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Civil_War" title="Russian Civil War">Russian Civil War</a>, and joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolshevik" title="Bolshevik">Bolshevik</a> party in 1918.</p>
<p>In 1927 he graduated from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_military_academies#Frunze_Military_Academy" title="Soviet military academies">Frunze Military Academy</a>. After graduation he was chief of staff of the 44th Rifle Division, then commandant of the Kazan Military School from 1934 to 1939 on behalf of the Supreme Soviet of the Tatar Autonomous Republic.</p>
<p>On 21 March 1940, he was awarded the title of the <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Hero of the Soviet Union">Hero of the Soviet Union</a> for his actions during the <a href="/wiki/Winter_War" title="Winter War">Soviet-Finnish War</a>. He became commander of the <a href="/wiki/Leningrad_Military_District" title="Leningrad Military District">Leningrad Military District</a> the same year.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikhail_Kirponos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In February 1941 he was assigned commander of the <a href="/wiki/Kiev_Military_District" title="Kiev Military District">Kiev Military District</a>, which was transformed into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southwestern_Front_(Soviet)" title="Southwestern Front (Soviet)">Southwestern Front</a> at the beginning of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German-Soviet_War" title="German-Soviet War">German-Soviet War</a>. On the night of 21 June 1941, the day before the launch of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Barbarossa" title="Operation Barbarossa">Operation Barbarossa</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a>, Mikhail Kirponos disregarded the strict instruction from <a href="/wiki/Stavka" title="Stavka">Stavka</a> to ignore rumors of the pending invasion the next day and spent the night preparing mission orders for his command. That same night, the unfortunate <a href="/wiki/Dmitry_Pavlov_(general)" title="Dmitry Pavlov (general)">D. G. Pavlov</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_II)" title="Western Front (World War II)">Western Front</a> accepted Stavka's assertion that rumors of war were deception at face value and went to the opera in <a href="/wiki/Minsk" title="Minsk">Minsk</a>.</p>
<p>While his front-line units were under the Stavka's general order to treat any German attack as a likely provocation, and not to return fire, just as all other front line units of the Soviet armies of the frontier had been instructed, the armies of the Southwestern Front were alert, and had not been completely stood down. It is possibly because of this wary attitude of Kirponos and his staff that the Southwestern Front was not caught completely flat footed when the Germans attacked.</p>
<p>Disposition of forces for the Southwestern Front, and considerable terrain advantages also favored Kirponos in comparison to his counterparts in <a href="/wiki/Byelorussian_Soviet_Socialist_Republic" title="Byelorussian Soviet Socialist Republic">Byelorussia</a>. In general, his command had more forces to deploy in depth, and von Runsdedt's <a href="/wiki/Army_Group_South" title="Army Group South">Army Group South</a> only attacked with a single Panzer army, as opposed to two available to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Army_Group_Center" title="Army Group Center">Army Group Center</a>. Stavka in fact believed that Kirponos had enough forces under his command to effectively comply with Stavka's General Chief of Staff <a href="/wiki/Georgy_Zhukov" title="Georgy Zhukov">G. K. Zhukov</a>'s "Directive No. 3", which called for a counterattack by the Southwestern Front with the objective of seizing Lublin in German occupied Poland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Kirponos, and his staff were ambivalent about this ambitious proposal.</p>
<p>Shortly thereafter, Zhukov himself showed up at Southwestern Front headquarters at <a href="/wiki/Ternopil" title="Ternopil">Ternopil</a> with <a href="/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev" title="Nikita Khrushchev">Nikita Khrushchev</a> in tow, to personally direct the operation. The result was the ill-fated attack against the flanks of the <a href="/wiki/1st_Panzer_Army" title="1st Panzer Army">1st Panzer Group</a> that was advancing toward Kiev between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/5th_Army_(Soviet_Union)" title="5th Army (Soviet Union)">5th</a> and <a href="/wiki/6th_Army_(Soviet_Union)" title="6th Army (Soviet Union)">6th</a> armies, known as the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Brody_(1941)" title="Battle of Brody (1941)">Battle of Brody</a>.</p>
<p>Severe communications, logistics and coordination problems plagued the operation, and as a consequence, the uncoordinated Mechanized Corps showed up late to their jump-off points in a disorganized fashion, without their complete complement of equipment and entered the battle piecemeal.</p>
<p>The fact that Zhukov and Kirponos were at odds about the offensive deepened these problems with Kirponos issuing a general order to cease the offensive on the 27th of June, because he wanted to make his front line shorter, "so as to prevent the enemy tank groupings from penetrating into the rear of the 6th and 26th Armies", according to H. Baghramyan.* <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This order was quickly countermanded by Zhukov who ordered the attack resumed, an order that was promptly refused on the "personal responsibility" of the commander of the <a href="/wiki/9th_Mechanized_Corps_(Soviet_Union)" title="9th Mechanized Corps (Soviet Union)">9th Mechanized Corps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Rokossovsky" title="Konstantin Rokossovsky">Konstantin Rokossovsky</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERyabyshev2002_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERyabyshev2002-3">[3]</a></sup> leaving the commander of the <a href="/wiki/8th_Mechanized_Corps_(Soviet_Union)" title="8th Mechanized Corps (Soviet Union)">8th Mechanized Corps</a> unaware that he was engaging alone.</p>
<p>Despite these difficulties, and the eventual loss of the great majority of the tanks involved in the fight, the German command was taken off guard.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In the Army Group South sector, heavy fighting continues on the right flank of Panzer Group 1. The Russian 8th Tank Corps has effected a deep penetration of our front and is now in the rear of the 11th Panzer Division. This penetration has seriously disrupted our rear areas between Brody and Dubno. The enemy is threatening Dubno from the southwest ... the enemy also has several separate tank groups acting in the rear of Panzer Group 1, which are managing to cover considerable distances.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— General <a href="/wiki/Franz_Halder" title="Franz Halder">Franz Halder</a>, diary<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERyabyshev2002_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERyabyshev2002-3">[3]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Even though in the balance the Southwestern Front did comparatively better than the other Front commands in the frontier battles, and generally maintained organizational cohesion and some operational initiative, Nikita Khrushchev noted that Zhukov said "I am afraid your commander (Kirponos) here is pretty weak".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Zhukov was soon forced to return to Moscow due to the critical situation developing along the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bia%C5%82ystok%E2%80%93Minsk" title="Battle of Białystok–Minsk">Bialystok-Minsk-Smolensk</a> axis, and the Southwestern Front, and newly formed <a href="/wiki/Southern_Front_(Soviet_Union)" title="Southern Front (Soviet Union)">Southern Front</a> created on the basis of the <a href="/wiki/Odessa_Military_District" title="Odessa Military District">Odessa Military District</a>, were put under the umbrella of the "Southwestern Direction" commanded by <a href="/wiki/Marshal_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Marshal of the Soviet Union">Marshal</a> <a href="/wiki/Semyon_Budyonny" title="Semyon Budyonny">Semyon Budyonny</a>, a long time Stalin associate, in mid July,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> with disastrous results in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Uman" title="Battle of Uman">Battle of Uman</a>.</p>
<p>Afterwards, Kirponos' forces fought in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kiev_(1941)" title="Battle of Kiev (1941)">Battle of Kiev</a>. He was <a href="/wiki/Killed_in_action" title="Killed in action">killed in action</a> during the defense of Kiev, in which the Soviets ended up losing badly due to the overwhelming advantage by the Germans, which was exacerbated by the grave errors of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a> and the high-ranking military leaders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deht_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-deht-6">[6]</a></sup> Nevertheless, more than sixty years later and after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_Soviet_Union_(1985-1991)#Yeltsin_and_the_dissolution_of_the_USSR" title="History of the Soviet Union (1985-1991)">collapse of the Soviet Union</a>, Kirponos remains highly regarded both in <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> for his exemplary military leadership, courage and valour.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
