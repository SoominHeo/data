<p>A <b>mixtape</b> (also <b>mix-tape</b> or <b>mix tape</b>) is a home-made compilation of music (typically copyrighted songs taken from other sources) recorded in a specific order, traditionally onto a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cassette_tape" title="Cassette tape">cassette tape</a>, though later replaced by CD or <a href="/wiki/MP3" title="MP3">MP3</a> <a href="/wiki/Playlist" title="Playlist">playlist</a> formats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The songs can be sequential, or by <a href="/wiki/Beatmatching" title="Beatmatching">beatmatching</a> the songs and creating overlaps and fades between the end of one song and the beginning of another the tape may become a seamless whole. Compilations may include a selection of favorite songs, or music linked by theme or mood, perhaps tailored to the tape's intended recipient.</p>
<p>Writing in 2004, essayist <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_O%27Brien" title="Geoffrey O'Brien">Geoffrey O'Brien</a> called the personal mix tape "perhaps the most widely practiced American art form".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pooter_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-pooter-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Mixtape" is also used in <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop" title="Hip hop">hip hop</a> to describe certain types of recordings, typically self-produced and independently released albums that are issued free of charge to gain publicity and/or circumvent possible copyright infringement.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mixtape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:8trackmixtape.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/8trackmixtape.JPG/220px-8trackmixtape.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/8trackmixtape.JPG/330px-8trackmixtape.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/8trackmixtape.JPG/440px-8trackmixtape.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:8trackmixtape.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An early pirated 8 track mixtape from 1974</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The most common early mix tapes were <a href="/wiki/Bootleg_recording" title="Bootleg recording">bootleg</a> <a href="/wiki/8-track_tape" title="8-track tape">8 track</a> tapes that were sold at flea markets and truck stops in the late 1960s through the early 1980s, with names like "Super 73", "Country Chart Toppers" or "Top Pops 1977".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Homemade mix tapes became common in the 1980s. Although the <a href="/wiki/Compact_Cassette" title="Compact Cassette">compact audio cassette</a> by <a href="/wiki/Philips" title="Philips">Philips</a> appeared at the 1963 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berlin_Radio_Show" title="Berlin Radio Show">Berlin Radio Show</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> the sound quality of cassettes was not good enough to be seriously considered for music recording until further advances in tape formulations, including the advent of chrome and metal tape. Before the introduction of the audio cassette, the creation of a pop music compilation required specialized or cumbersome equipment, such as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reel-to-reel" title="Reel-to-reel">reel-to-reel</a> or <a href="/wiki/8-track_tape" title="8-track tape">8 track</a> recorder, that was often inaccessible to the casual music <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fan_(aficionado)" title="Fan (aficionado)">fan</a>. As cassette tapes and recorders grew in popularity and portability, these technological hurdles were lowered to the point where the only resources required to create a mix were a handful of cassettes and a cassette recorder connected to a source of pre-recorded music, such as a radio or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vinyl_record" title="Vinyl record">LP</a> player. The <a href="/wiki/8-track_tape" title="8-track tape">8-track tape</a> cartridge was more popular for music recording during much of the 1960s, as the cassette was originally only mono and intended for vocal recordings only, such as in office dictation machines. But improvements in fidelity finally allowed the cassette to become a major player. The ready availability of the cassette and higher quality home recording decks to serve the casual home user allowed the cassette to become the dominant tape format, to the point that the <a href="/wiki/8-track_tape" title="8-track tape">8 track</a> tape nearly disappeared shortly after the turn of the 1980s. The growth of the mixtape was also encouraged by improved quality and increased popularity of audio cassette players in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car_audio" title="Car audio">car entertainment systems</a>, and by the introduction of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sony_Walkman" title="Sony Walkman">Sony Walkman</a> in 1979.</p>
<p>A distinction should be drawn between a private mixtape, which is usually intended for a specific listener or private social event, and a public mixtape, or "party tape", usually consisting of a recording of a club performance by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DJ" title="DJ">DJ</a> and intended to be sold to multiple individuals. In the 1970s, such DJs as <a href="/wiki/Grandmaster_Flash" title="Grandmaster Flash">Grandmaster Flash</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Furious_Five" title="Furious Five">Furious Five</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afrika_Bambaataa" title="Afrika Bambaataa">Afrika Bambaataa</a> and the Soulsonic Force, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kool_DJ_Herc" title="Kool DJ Herc">Kool Herc and the Herculoids</a>, and <a href="/wiki/DJ_Hollywood" title="DJ Hollywood">DJ Hollywood</a> would often distribute recordings of their club performances via audio cassette, as well as customized recordings (often prepared at exorbitant prices) for individual tape purchasers. These recordings tended to be of higher technical ability than home-made mixtapes and incorporated techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Beatmatching" title="Beatmatching">beatmatching</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scratching" title="Scratching">scratching</a>. One 12 October 1974 article in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billboard_Magazine" title="Billboard Magazine">Billboard Magazine</a></i> reported, "Tapes were originally <a href="/wiki/Dubbing_(music)" title="Dubbing (music)">dubbed</a> by jockeys to serve as standbys for times when they did not have disco <a href="/wiki/Phonograph" title="Phonograph">turntables</a> to hand. The tapes represent each jockey's concept of programming, placing, and sequencing of record sides. The music is heard without interruption. One- to three-hour programs bring anywhere from $30 to $75 per tape, mostly reel-to-reel, but increasingly on cartridge and cassette." Club proprietors, as well as DJs, would often prepare such tapes for sale.</p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CD-R.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/CD-R" title="CD-R">CD-R</a> disc is currently the most common medium for homemade mixes</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Throughout the 1980s, mixtapes were a highly visible element of <a href="/wiki/Youth_culture" title="Youth culture">youth culture</a>. However, the increased availability of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CD_recorder" title="CD recorder">CD burners</a> and MP3 players and the gradual disappearance of cassette players in cars and households have led to a decline in the popularity of the compact audio cassette as a medium for homemade mixes. The high point of traditional mixtape culture was arguably the publication of <a href="/wiki/Nick_Hornby" title="Nick Hornby">Nick Hornby's</a> novel <i><a href="/wiki/High_Fidelity_(novel)" title="High Fidelity (novel)">High Fidelity</a></i> in 1995. Since then, mixtapes have largely been replaced by mix CDs and shared MP3 playlists, which are more durable, can hold more songs, and require minutes (rather than hours) to prepare, and MP3 players take only seconds compared to CD-Rs. While some mixtape enthusiasts bemoan the obsolescence of the cassette tape, others concede that the greater convenience offered by the mix CD has expanded the possibilities and accessibility of the medium, as indicated by the recent resurgence of mix-swapping clubs that trade mix CDs by regular mail. Some mix enthusiasts also appreciate the potential of the mix CD for extended, continuous mixes and creative album art. MP3 players have further enhanced track accessibility, though ones without a screen defeat that purpose.</p>
<p>Today, websites concerned with electronic music provide mixes in a digital format. These usually consist of recorded DJ sets of live, beat-matched mixes of songs, which are used by DJs seeking to demonstrate their mixing skills to an online audience. Some radio shows worldwide specialize in mix series, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Breezeblock" title="The Breezeblock">The Breezeblock</a></i> on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_1" title="BBC Radio 1">BBC Radio 1</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Solid_Steel" title="Solid Steel">The Solid Steel Show</a></i> (formerly on <a href="/wiki/KISS-FM" title="KISS-FM">KISS-FM</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Eddy_Temple-Morris" title="Eddy Temple-Morris">Eddy Temple-Morris</a>/The Remix on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xfm" title="Xfm">Xfm</a>.</p>
<p>Additionally, DJs such as <a href="/wiki/Grandmaster_Flash" title="Grandmaster Flash">Grandmaster Flash</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DJ_QBert" title="DJ QBert">DJ QBert</a>, <a href="/wiki/DJ_Spooky" title="DJ Spooky">DJ Spooky</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DJ_Z-Trip" title="DJ Z-Trip">DJ Z-Trip</a> or <a href="/wiki/DJ_Shadow" title="DJ Shadow">DJ Shadow</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Avalanches" title="The Avalanches">The Avalanches</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rjd2" title="Rjd2">Rjd2</a> have gained fame for creating new songs by combining fragments of existing songs (which need not necessarily belong to the same genre). The resulting <a href="/wiki/Remix" title="Remix">remix</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mashup_(music)" title="Mashup (music)">mash-up</a> can be seen as an evolution of the mixtape, in that it appropriates existing songs to give them new meanings through their juxtaposition, but does so in a quicker, more integrated style. This practice is heavily derived from the use of song loops as musical backdrops for an MC's rhymes in <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop music</a>, which is also related to <a href="/wiki/Turntablism" title="Turntablism">turntablism</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_issues_in_the_United_States">Legal issues in the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mixtape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legal issues in the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Frank Creighton, a director of anti-copyright infringement efforts for the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a>, considers that "money did not have to be involved for copying to be illegal".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aesthetic">Aesthetic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mixtape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aesthetic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the process of recording a mix onto an audio cassette from LPs or compact discs is technically straightforward, many music fans who create more than one mixtape are eventually compelled to confront some of the practical and aesthetic challenges involved in the mixtape format. From a practical standpoint, such issues as avoiding an excessive amount of blank tape at the end of one side (which requires careful planning of the length of each side of the mix) and reducing the audible click between songs (which requires mastery of the pause button on the cassette recorder) have been identified as part of the shared experience of mixtape aficionados. From an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aesthetic" title="Aesthetic">aesthetic</a> point of view, many enthusiasts believe that because a tape player, unlike a CD player, lacks the ability to skip from song to song, the mixtape needs to be considered in its entirety. This requires the mixtape creator to consider the transitions between songs, the effects caused by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Juxtaposition_(literary)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Juxtaposition (literary) (page does not exist)">juxtaposing</a> a soft song with a loud song, and the overall "<a href="/wiki/Narrative" title="Narrative">narrative arc</a>" of the entire tape. One notable listing of such aesthetic "rules" can be found in a paragraph from <a href="/wiki/Nick_Hornby" title="Nick Hornby">Nick Hornby</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/High_Fidelity_(novel)" title="High Fidelity (novel)">High Fidelity</a></i>:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">To me, making a tape is like writing a letter—there's a lot of erasing and rethinking and starting again. A good compilation tape, like breaking up, is hard to do. You've got to kick off with a corker, to hold the attention (I started with "Got to Get You Off My Mind", but then realized that she might not get any further than track one, side one if I delivered what she wanted straightaway, so I buried it in the middle of side two), and then you've got to up it a notch, or cool it a notch, and you can't have white music and black music together, unless the white music sounds like black music, and you can't have two tracks by the same artist side by side, unless you've done the whole thing in pairs and...oh, there are loads of rules.</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>

<p>Many enthusiasts also devote substantial attention to the packaging of a mix tape intended as a gift, sometimes going so far as to create cover art and customized liner notes. The cover of the original <a href="/wiki/McSweeney%27s" title="McSweeney's">McSweeney's</a> edition of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/31_Songs" title="31 Songs">31 Songs</a></i>, a 2003 essay collection by <a href="/wiki/Nick_Hornby" title="Nick Hornby">Nick Hornby</a>, was intended to suggest the packaging of a homemade mixtape, with the Side A half (of a Maxell cassette J-card) as the front cover and the Side B half on the back cover. It also came with an actual CD featuring ten of the songs discussed in the text. Indeed, the look of mix tapes, featuring hand-written notes on the recording medium manufacturer's supplied labels, has become one of the aesthetic conventions of modern design, a distinct style that designers may attempt to copy or cite. Many have been so widely distributed that the <a href="/wiki/CDDB" title="CDDB">CDDB</a> has logged and can identify <a href="/wiki/ID3" title="ID3">ID3</a> tags when a disc mix tape is inserted into a PC.</p>
<p>From an artistic point of view, many creators of mix tapes seem to regard them as a form of emotional self-expression, although whether a mix tape retains the same web of emotional associations when passed from its creator to the recipient is, at best, debatable. Some argue that in selecting, juxtaposing, or even editing originally unrelated tracks of pop music into a new work of art, the "author" of a mix tape moves from passive listener to <a href="/wiki/Archivist" title="Archivist">archivist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Editing" title="Editing">editor</a>, and finally active <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/participation" title="wikt:participation">participant</a> in the process of musical creation. (Some legitimacy for this viewpoint was provided by <i>Cassette Stories</i>, a 2003 exhibition at the Museum of Communication in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hamburg,_Germany" title="Hamburg, Germany">Hamburg, Germany</a>, which featured stories and submissions from eighty mix tape enthusiasts.) However, this perception of the mix tape as a work of art has been criticized as resulting in a sort of elitism, with creators becoming more concerned with finding arcane and surprising combinations of tracks than with creating a tape that is listenable, enjoyable, or appropriate to its intended recipient. (In <i>High Fidelity</i>, for example, the narrator's girlfriend complains that his mix tapes are too <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Didactic" title="Didactic">didactic</a>.) On a very basic level, the creation of a mix tape can be seen as an expression of the individual compiler's taste in music, often put forward for the implicit approval of the tape's recipient, and in many cases as a tentative step towards building the compiler's personal <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/canon" title="wiktionary:canon">canon</a> of pop music.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mixtape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although a "comprehensive" list of the different genres or categories of mixtapes could be extended indefinitely, creating a taxonomy of mix tapes is a project that many mixtape enthusiasts have intuitively attempted.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The different types of mixtapes identified on community sites, such as FoundTrack (www.FoundTrack.com) and Art of the Mix (www.artofthemix.org; which lists over two hundred genres), indicate the variety of potential categories. Beyond basic genres, such as the simple taping of an entire album, the collection of favourite songs, and the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/snapshot#Noun" title="wiktionary:snapshot">"snapshot"</a> mix of recent favourites, some of the more commonly cited categories include the driving mix, the workout mix, the party mix, the krazy mix (eclectic selections of obscure, rare or otherwise unconventional tracks), the didactic mix (intended to educate the recipient as to the essential works of a particular artist or genre), the concept / theme mix (e.g., a mix of <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> songs, songs about cars, covers of songs by a particular artist or similar), and the mood mix (i.e., a mix of songs intended to sustain a specific mood, stated or unstated—notable subgenres include the romantic mix and the break-up mix). Mixtapes eventually made their way onto television screens by video mix DJs (e.g., Jordan Laws "Fit to Fill Video Mixtape" <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=DJ_SWB&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="DJ SWB (page does not exist)">DJ SWB</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A more detailed citation is required. (November 2012)">full citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_hip_hop">In hip hop</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mixtape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In hip hop">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a>'s earliest days, the music only existed in live form, so performers' music was spread via tapes of parties and shows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shapiro_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shapiro-6">[6]</a></sup> Hip hop mixtapes first appeared in the mid-1970s in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, featuring artists such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kool_Herc" title="Kool Herc">Kool Herc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Afrika_Bambaataa" title="Afrika Bambaataa">Afrika Bambaataa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shapiro_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shapiro-6">[6]</a></sup> As more tapes became available, they were collected and traded by fans. In the late 70's into the early 80's, DJs began recording mixtapes from their homes, referring to them as House Tapes. DJs such as Harold G. (who later became known as Whiz Kid) and DJ Super V would create personalized House Tapes which would eventually circulate throughout New York City.</p>
<p>In the mid-1980s, DJs, such as Brucie B, began recording their live music and selling them as mixtapes, which was soon followed by other DJs, like <a href="/wiki/Kid_Capri" title="Kid Capri">Kid Capri</a> and Doo Wop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shapiro_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Shapiro-6">[6]</a></sup> Ron G moved the mixtape forward in the early 1990s by blending R&amp;B <i>a cappellas</i> with hip hop beats (known as "blends").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shapiro_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Shapiro-6">[6]</a></sup> Blend tapes became increasingly popular by the mid-1990s<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, where fans could find exclusive tracks and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freestyle_rapping" title="Freestyle rapping">freestyles</a>. These were developed to promote one or more new artists, or as a pre-release by more established artists to promote upcoming "official" albums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the hip hop scene, mix tape is often displayed as a single term <i>mixtape</i>. It is now a word that could be used to describe full-length albums released for free. However, there are notable differences between hip hop albums and mixtapes. Because mixtapes and albums have no absolute conditions to divide them, they are instead judged by characteristics of the project, along with what the artist chooses to describe them as. For example, mixtapes can contain original beats with samples in them that have not been cleared. There is generally no legal recourse for those who own the rights to sampled material, because the mixtape is free and so no profit is being earned from the work. Mixtapes can also take other artists' beats and simply add new verses on top, generally labelled as a remix or a freestyle.</p>
<p>Albums, on the other hand, only contain samples that have been cleared in order to avoid legal issues. Albums are also almost always sold, whereas mixtapes are usually free. The modern form of a mixtape that made a popular following by <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a> and his group <a href="/wiki/G-Unit" title="G-Unit">G-Unit</a> in the early 2000s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> sometimes containing all original music, other times composed of <a href="/wiki/Freestyle_rap" title="Freestyle rap">freestyles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Remix" title="Remix">remixes</a> of popular tracks. There are exceptions, such as the <a href="/wiki/Free_Weezy_Album" title="Free Weezy Album">Free Weezy Album</a> by <a href="/wiki/Lil_Wayne" title="Lil Wayne">Lil Wayne</a>, which, as the name suggests, was a free album. Another is <i><a href="/wiki/If_You%27re_Reading_This_It%27s_Too_Late" title="If You're Reading This It's Too Late">If You're Reading This It's Too Late</a></i>, a mixtape by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drake_(rapper)" title="Drake (rapper)">Drake</a> sold for profit. It's rumored Drake was obligated to sell the mixtape to appease his <a href="/wiki/Young_Money_Entertainment" title="Young Money Entertainment">Young Money</a> record contract, which he was trying to evade, at the time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
