<p><b>Liquorice</b>, or <b>licorice</b>, (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ᵊ/ optional 'ə' in 'jewelry'">ᵊ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="/ʃ/ 'sh' in 'shy'">ʃ</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">LIK</span></span></b>-(ə-)rish</i></a></span> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ᵊ/ optional 'ə' in 'jewelry'">ᵊ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">LIK</span></span></b>-(ə-)ris)</i></a></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is the root of <i><b>Glycyrrhiza glabra</b></i> from which a sweet flavour can be extracted. The liquorice plant is a herbaceous perennial <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">legume</a> native to southern Europe and parts of Asia, such as India. It is not botanically related to <a href="/wiki/Anise" title="Anise">anise</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_anise" title="Star anise">star anise</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Fennel" title="Fennel">fennel</a>, which are sources of similar flavouring compounds.</p>
<p>Most liquorice is used as a flavouring agent for tobacco, particularly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US" title="US">US</a> blend cigarettes, to which liquorice lends a natural sweetness and a distinctive flavour and makes it easier to inhale the smoke by creating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronchodilators" title="Bronchodilators">bronchodilators</a>, which open up the lungs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cook_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cook-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boeken_v._Phillip_Morris_Inc_1640_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boeken_v._Phillip_Morris_Inc_1640-4">[4]</a></sup> Liquorice flavours are also used as candies or sweeteners, particularly in some European and Middle Eastern countries. Liquorice extracts have a number of medical uses, and they are also used in herbal and folk medications. Excessive consumption of liquorice (more than 2 mg/kg/day of pure <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glycyrrhizinic_acid" title="Glycyrrhizinic acid">glycyrrhizinic acid</a>, a liquorice component) may result in adverse effects, and overconsumption should be suspected clinically in patients presenting with otherwise unexplained <a href="/wiki/Hypokalemia" title="Hypokalemia">hypokalemia</a> and muscle weakness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAGE_Publications_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-SAGE_Publications-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquorice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>liquorice</i> is derived (via the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i>licoresse</i>) from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> γλυκύρριζα (<i>glukurrhiza</i>), meaning "sweet root",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> from γλυκύς (<i>glukus</i>), "sweet"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> + ῥίζα (<i>rhiza</i>), "root",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> the name provided by <a href="/wiki/Pedanius_Dioscorides" title="Pedanius Dioscorides">Dioscorides</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It is usually spelled <i>liquorice</i> in British usage, but <i>licorice</i> in the United States and Canada.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquorice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbaceous" title="Herbaceous">herbaceous</a> <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennial</a>, growing to 1 m in height, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinnate" title="Pinnate">pinnate</a> <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> about 7–15 cm (2.8–5.9 in) long, with 9–17 leaflets. The <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a> are 0.8–1.2 cm (<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>3</sub></span>–<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> in) long, purple to pale whitish blue, produced in a loose <a href="/wiki/Inflorescence" title="Inflorescence">inflorescence</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> is an oblong <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">pod</a>, 2–3 cm (<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>⁄<sub>4</sub></span>–<span class="frac nowrap">1<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>6</sub></span> in) long, containing several <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhs_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-rhs-11">[11]</a></sup> The roots are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stoloniferous" title="Stoloniferous">stoloniferous</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquorice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Glycyrrhizic_Acid.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="284" data-file-width="512" height="122" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Glycyrrhizic_Acid.svg/220px-Glycyrrhizic_Acid.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Glycyrrhizic_Acid.svg/330px-Glycyrrhizic_Acid.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Glycyrrhizic_Acid.svg/440px-Glycyrrhizic_Acid.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Glycyrrhizic_Acid.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Much of the sweetness in liquorice comes from <a href="/wiki/Glycyrrhizin" title="Glycyrrhizin">glycyrrhizin</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The scent of liquorice root comes from a complex and variable combination of compounds, of which <a href="/wiki/Anethole" title="Anethole">anethole</a> is up to 3% of total volatiles. Much of the sweetness in liquorice comes from <a href="/wiki/Glycyrrhizin" title="Glycyrrhizin">glycyrrhizin</a>, which has a sweet taste, 30–50 times the sweetness of <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>. The sweetness is very different from sugar, being less instant, tart, and lasting longer.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Isoflavene" title="Isoflavene">isoflavene</a> <a href="/wiki/Glabrene" title="Glabrene">glabrene</a> and the isoflavane <a href="/wiki/Glabridin" title="Glabridin">glabridin</a>, found in the roots of liquorice, are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phytoestrogen" title="Phytoestrogen">phytoestrogens</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation_and_uses">Cultivation and uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquorice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cultivation and uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Liquorice, which grows best in well-drained soils in deep valleys with full sun, is harvested in the <a href="/wiki/Autumn" title="Autumn">autumn</a> two to three years after planting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhs_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-rhs-11">[11]</a></sup> Countries producing liquorice include India, Iran, Italy, Afghanistan, the People’s Republic of China, Pakistan, Iraq, Azerbaijan, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan,Turkey, and England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mafco2010_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mafco2010-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The world's leading manufacturer of liquorice products is <a href="/wiki/M%26F_Worldwide" title="M&amp;F Worldwide">M&amp;F Worldwide</a>, which manufactures more than 70% of the worldwide liquorice flavours sold to end users.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tobacco">Tobacco</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquorice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tobacco">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most liquorice is used as a flavouring agent for <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a>. For example, M&amp;F Worldwide reported in 2011 that about 63% of its liquorice product sales are to the worldwide tobacco industry for use as tobacco flavour enhancing and moistening agents in the manufacture of American blend <a href="/wiki/Cigarette" title="Cigarette">cigarettes</a>, moist <a href="/wiki/Snuff_(tobacco)" title="Snuff (tobacco)">snuff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chewing_tobacco" title="Chewing tobacco">chewing tobacco</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pipe_tobacco" title="Pipe tobacco">pipe tobacco</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mafco2010_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mafco2010-15">[15]</a></sup> American blend cigarettes made up a larger portion of worldwide tobacco consumption in earlier years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and the percentage of liquorice products used by the tobacco industry was higher in the past. M&amp;F Worldwide sold approximately 73% of its liquorice products to the tobacco industry in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> A consultant to M&amp;F Worldwide's predecessor company stated in 1975 that it was believed that well over 90% of the total production of liquorice extract and its derivatives found its way into tobacco products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cook_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cook-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Liquorice provides tobacco products with a natural sweetness and a distinctive flavour that blends readily with the natural and imitation flavouring components employed in the tobacco industry. It represses harshness and is not detectable as liquorice by the consumer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cook_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cook-3">[3]</a></sup> Tobacco flavourings such as liquorice also make it easier to inhale the smoke by creating bronchodilators, which open up the lungs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boeken_v._Phillip_Morris_Inc_1640_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Boeken_v._Phillip_Morris_Inc_1640-4">[4]</a></sup> Chewing tobacco requires substantially higher levels of liquorice extract as emphasis on the sweet flavour appears highly desirable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cook_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cook-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_candy">Food and candy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquorice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Food and candy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Liquorice_(confectionery)" title="Liquorice (confectionery)">Liquorice (confectionery)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Liquorice flavour is found in a wide variety of <a href="/wiki/Liquorice_(confectionery)" title="Liquorice (confectionery)">candies or sweets</a>. In most of these candies, the taste is reinforced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aniseed" title="Aniseed">aniseed</a> oil so the actual content of liquorice is very low. Liquorice confections are primarily purchased by consumers in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mafco2010_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mafco2010-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Netherlands, liquorice candy (<i>drop</i>) is one of the most popular forms of sweets. It is sold in many forms. Mixing it with <a href="/wiki/Mentha" title="Mentha">mint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Menthol" title="Menthol">menthol</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aniseed" title="Aniseed">aniseed</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bay_Laurel" title="Bay Laurel">laurel</a> is quite popular. Mixing it with <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a> (<i>salmiak</i>) is also popular. The most popular liquorice, known in the Netherlands as <i>zoute drop</i> (<a href="/wiki/Salty_liquorice" title="Salty liquorice">salty liquorice</a>), actually contains very little salt, i.e., sodium chloride.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The salty taste is probably due to ammonium chloride and the blood pressure-raising effect is due to glycyrrhizin. Strong, <a href="/wiki/Salty_liquorice" title="Salty liquorice">salty</a> sweets are also popular in <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">Nordic countries</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pontefract" title="Pontefract">Pontefract</a> in <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> was the first place where liquorice mixed with sugar began to be used as a sweet in the same way it is in the modern day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_cake" title="Pontefract cake">Pontefract cakes</a> were originally made there. In County Durham, Yorkshire, and Lancashire, it is colloquially known as 'Spanish', supposedly because Spanish monks grew liquorice root at <a href="/wiki/Rievaulx_Abbey" title="Rievaulx Abbey">Rievaulx Abbey</a> near <a href="/wiki/Thirsk" title="Thirsk">Thirsk</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Italy (particularly in the south), Spain, and France, liquorice is popular in its natural form. The root of the plant is simply dug up, washed, dried, and chewed as a mouth freshener. Throughout <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, unsweetened liquorice is consumed in the form of small black pieces made only from 100% pure liquorice extract; the taste is bitter. In <a href="/wiki/Calabria" title="Calabria">Calabria</a> a popular <a href="/wiki/Liqueur" title="Liqueur">liqueur</a> is made from pure liquorice extract.</p>
<p>Liquorice is also very popular in <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, where it is sold as a drink, in shops as well as street vendors. It is used for its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expectorant" title="Expectorant">expectorant</a> qualities in <a href="/wiki/Traditional_medicine" title="Traditional medicine">folk medicine</a> in Egypt.</p>

<p>Dried liquorice root can be chewed as a sweet. Black liquorice contains about 100 <a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">calories</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (July 2015)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Liquorice#Dubious" title="Talk:Liquorice">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> per ounce (15 kJ/g).</p>
<p>Liquorice is used by brewers to flavour and colour porter classes of beers, and the enzymes in the root also stabilize the foam heads produced by beers brewed with it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medicine">Medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquorice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Glycyrrhizin" title="Glycyrrhizin">Glycyrrhizin</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Enoxolone" title="Enoxolone">Enoxolone</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Glycyrrhizin" title="Glycyrrhizin">Glycyrrhizin</a> has also demonstrated antiviral, antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, hepatoprotective, and blood pressure-increasing effects <i>in vitro</i> and <i>in vivo</i>, as is supported by the finding that intravenous glycyrrhizin (as if it is given orally very little of the original drug makes it into circulation) slows the progression of viral and <a href="/wiki/Autoimmune_hepatitis" title="Autoimmune hepatitis">autoimmune hepatitis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In one clinical trial liquorice demonstrated promising activity, when applied topically, against <a href="/wiki/Atopic_dermatitis" title="Atopic dermatitis">atopic dermatitis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Additionally, liquorice may be effective in treating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperlipidaemia" title="Hyperlipidaemia">hyperlipidaemia</a> (a high amount of fats in the blood).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Liquorice has also demonstrated efficacy in treating inflammation-induced skin <a href="/wiki/Hyperpigmentation" title="Hyperpigmentation">hyperpigmentation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Liquorice may also be useful in preventing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurodegenerative_disorders" title="Neurodegenerative disorders">neurodegenerative disorders</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dental_caries" title="Dental caries">dental caries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The antiulcer, laxative, antidiabetic, anti-inflammatory, immunomodulatory, antitumour and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expectorant" title="Expectorant">expectorant</a> properties of liquorice have been investigated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The compound <a href="/wiki/Glycyrrhizin" title="Glycyrrhizin">glycyrrhizin</a> (or glycyrrhizic acid), found in liquorice, has been proposed as being useful for liver protection in <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> therapy, but evidence does not support this use, which may in fact be harmful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liu2008_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Liu2008-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Folk_medicine">Folk medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquorice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Folk medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_medicine" title="Traditional Chinese medicine">traditional Chinese medicine</a>, liquorice (甘草) is believed to "harmonize" the ingredients in a formula and to carry the formula to the 12 <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(Chinese_medicine)" title="Meridian (Chinese medicine)">"regular meridians"</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Liquorice has been traditionally known and used as medicine in Ayurveda for rejuvenation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Liquorice extract is used as a home remedy for skin lightening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquorice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Toxicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Its major dose-limiting toxicities are <a href="/wiki/Corticosteroid" title="Corticosteroid">corticosteroid</a> in nature, because of the inhibitory effect its chief active constituents, glycyrrhizin and <a href="/wiki/Enoxolone" title="Enoxolone">enoxolone</a>, have on <a href="/wiki/Cortisol" title="Cortisol">cortisol</a> degradation and include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oedema" title="Oedema">oedema</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypokalaemia" title="Hypokalaemia">hypokalaemia</a>, weight gain or loss, and <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>The United States <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> believes that foods containing liquorice and its derivatives (including glycyrrhizin) are safe if not consumed excessively. Other jurisdictions have suggested no more than 100 mg to 200 mg of glycyrrhizin per day, the equivalent of about 70 to 150 g (2.5 to 5.3 oz) of liquorice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAGE_Publications_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-SAGE_Publications-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
