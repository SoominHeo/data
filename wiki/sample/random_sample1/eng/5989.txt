<p>A <b>folk wrestling</b> style is any traditional style of <a href="/wiki/Wrestling" title="Wrestling">wrestling</a>, which may or may not be codified as a modern <a href="/wiki/Sport" title="Sport">sport</a>. Most cultures have developed regional forms of <a href="/wiki/Grappling" title="Grappling">grappling</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Folk_wrestling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_martial_arts" title="European martial arts">European martial arts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greco-Roman_wrestling" title="Greco-Roman wrestling">Greco-Roman wrestling</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_Isles">British Isles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Folk_wrestling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: British Isles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Traditionally wrestling has two main centers in Great Britain: the <a href="/wiki/West_Country" title="West Country">West Country</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> styles were developed, and in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_England" title="Northern England">Northern</a> counties, the home of the <a href="/wiki/Cumberland" title="Cumberland">Cumberland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Westmorland" title="Westmorland">Westmorland</a> styles.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="North_Country_styles">North Country styles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Folk_wrestling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: North Country styles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Lancashire_wrestling" title="Lancashire wrestling">Lancashire wrestling</a></b> is a historic wrestling style from <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a> in England known for its "Catch-as-catch-can", or <i>no wrestling holds barred</i>, style.
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Catch_wrestling" title="Catch wrestling">Catch wrestling</a></b>, or <b>Catch-as-catch-can</b>, originated from Lancashire wrestling but was further developed during the traveling <a href="/wiki/Circus" title="Circus">circus</a> phenomenon of the 19th and early 20th century <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>Backhold Wrestling</b>, whose origin is unknown, was practiced in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_England" title="North England">North England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> in the 7th and 8th century but competitions are held in present-day at the Highland and Border Games as well as in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. Styles of Backhold are distinct from Lancashire Wrestling because they enforce rules designed to minimize injury to the participants by disallowing ground fighting.
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Cumberland_and_Westmorland_wrestling" title="Cumberland and Westmorland wrestling">Cumberland and Westmorland wrestling</a></b>, or <b>Cumbrian Wrestling</b>, is practiced in the northern counties of England. It is a form of Backhold Wrestling where the wrestlers put the left arm over the opponents right arm and grip behind the opponent's back. Throws and trips are important since the first wrestler to touch the ground or break hold loses. Competitors often wear stockings (long johns), singlet and trunks.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Scottish_Backhold" title="Scottish Backhold">Scottish Backhold</a></b> is a form of Backhold practiced in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. Almost identical in style to Cumberland &amp; Westmorland style apart from variations in rules. Competitors often wear kilts.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="West_Country_styles">West Country styles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Folk_wrestling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: West Country styles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Cornish_wrestling" title="Cornish wrestling">Cornish wrestling</a></b>, from <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, is a form of <a href="/wiki/Jacket_wrestling" title="Jacket wrestling">jacket wrestling</a>. It does not use groundwork. It is related to Breton Gouren wrestling.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Devon_wrestling" title="Devon wrestling">Devon wrestling</a></b>, or <b>Devonshire wrestling</b>, was a style similar to the Cornish style in that jackets were worn. Devonshire wrestlers, however, also wore heavy clogs and were able to kick the opponents. In matches between Cornish and Devon, Devonshire wrestlers might have worn one only shoe. Unlike Cornish wrestling, the style is generally considered to be extinct.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Folk_wrestling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Collar-and-elbow" title="Collar-and-elbow">Collar-and-elbow</a></b> wrestling is native to <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and can be traced back to the 17th century. It has ties to the <a href="/wiki/Tailteann_Games" title="Tailteann Games">Tailteann Games</a> between 632 BC and 1169 AD.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nordic_countries">Nordic countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Folk_wrestling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nordic countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gl%C3%ADma" title="Glíma">Glíma</a></b>, the national sport of <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, traces its history to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking" title="Viking">Vikings</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a>. It is a standing style with rules similar to Shuai-Jiao and Bukh, and consists of three forms: 1) <i>Hryggtök</i>, the <i>Backhold Grip</i>; 2) <i>Brokartök</i> or the <i>Pant-and-belt Grip</i> that utilises a leather harness around the waist and thighs, which the wrestlers hold (making it a form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belt-wrestling" title="Belt-wrestling">belt-wrestling</a> similar to Swiss Schwingen), and 3) <i><a href="/wiki/Lausat%C3%B6k" title="Lausatök">Lausatök</a></i> or <i>Free-Grip</i> is the most aggressive form of glima and contestants can use the holds they wish. It is practised both outdoors and indoors.</li>
<li><b>Kragkast</b>, type of folk wrestling originating from <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, similar to <a href="/wiki/Freestyle_wrestling" title="Freestyle wrestling">Freestyle wrestling</a></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Continental_Europe">Continental Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Folk_wrestling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Continental Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt>Western Europe</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Gouren" title="Gouren">Gouren</a></b> - traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breton_people" title="Breton people">Breton</a> jacket wrestling. Similar to <i>Cornish wrestling.</i></li>
<li><b>Ranggeln</b> - meaning "to wrangle" in German, Ranggeln is a prominent form of wrestling in <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>. <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mongolian_wrestling">Mongolian wrestling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Folk_wrestling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Mongolian wrestling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B%C3%B6kh" title="Bökh">Bökh</a></b> - (Khalkha bökh, Khalkha wrestling) traditional <a href="/wiki/Khalkha_Mongols" title="Khalkha Mongols">Khalkha</a> <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolian</a> jacket wrestling where touching the ground with anything other than a foot loses the match.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Bökh means "wrestling" or "wrestler" in Mongolian.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Buryats" title="Buryats">Buryat</a> wrestling</b> (Buriad bökh)</li>
<li><b>Bukh noololdoon</b> - <a href="/wiki/Oirats" title="Oirats">Oirat</a> wrestling or Western Mongolian wrestling</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Inner_Mongolia" title="Inner Mongolia">Southern Mongolian</a> wrestling</b> - (<a href="/wiki/%C3%9Czemchin_Mongols" title="Üzemchin Mongols">Üzemchin</a> wrestling) jacket wrestling that wear jacket made of cow leather, long pants with chaps over and boots. Rules and techniques are more similar to <i>Shuai Jiao</i> than to <i>Bokh</i> practised in <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, where wrestlers wear only short, tight, collarless, heavy-duty short-sleeved jacket and small, tight-fitting briefs made of red or blue colored cotton cloth.</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Asia">East Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Folk_wrestling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: East Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Folk_wrestling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shuai_Jiao" title="Shuai Jiao">Shuai Jiao</a> 摔跤</b>: <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> jacket wrestling originating from <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tianjin" title="Tianjin">Tianjin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baoding" title="Baoding">Baoding</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hebei" title="Hebei">Northern Hebei</a> which means "Throw and Trip (at the ankle)". Also known as <i>Guan Jiao 摜跤</i> and <i>Liao Jiao 撩跤</i>, meaning "Continuing Trip (at the ankle)" and "Hold-up and Trip (at the ankle)". In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qing_Dynasty" title="Qing Dynasty">Qing Dynasty</a> time it was also known as "<i>Buku (布庫)</i>", Manchu word for wrestling which has the root as Mongol word Boke. This style of wrestling was the style of martial arts practiced by imperial guards in the Liao, Jin, Yuan, Ming and Qing dynasties. The present techniques were codified by the <a href="/wiki/Kangxi_Emperor" title="Kangxi Emperor">Kangxi Emperor</a> in the 1670s. Traditionally wrestlers wear jacket called "<i>Da Lian</i> (搭褳)" and chaps over their pants, which allow grabbing the chaps for lifting. In addition, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qin_Na" title="Qin Na">Qin Na</a> techniques such as arm bars were allowed in Beijing, and striking and blocking using upper arms were allowed in Tianjin. Rules have since incorporated Shanxi <i>Die Jiao</i> and modern <i>Olympic Wrestlings</i>.</li>
<li><b>Die Jiao 跌跤</b>: Chinese wrestling from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xinzhou,_Shanxi" title="Xinzhou, Shanxi">Xinzhou</a> (忻州), <a href="/wiki/Shanxi" title="Shanxi">Northern Shanxi</a> (山西) Province. Competitors wear only pants, without jacket, belt, or boots. Its primary techniques are the "48 Leg Takedowns", first codified in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" title="Song Dynasty">Song Dynasty</a> circa 1180. This form of wrestling was popular throughout <a href="/wiki/North_China" title="North China">Northern China</a> until the spread of Shuai Jiao, which is considered more advanced in its grappling and tripping techniques. It was colloquially known as "Mo Ni Qiu (摸泥鳅)", literally <i>grabbing the mud Qiu</i>, Qiu being a kind of catfish in Northern China.</li>
<li><b>Qielixi 切里西</b>: Chinese <a href="/wiki/Belt_wrestling" title="Belt wrestling">belt wrestling</a> practiced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uyghur_people" title="Uyghur people">Uyghur</a> Nationality.</li>
<li><b>Gi Ge 几格</b>: Chinese <a href="/wiki/Belt_wrestling" title="Belt wrestling">belt wrestling</a> practiced by <a href="/wiki/Yi_people" title="Yi people">Yi</a> Nationality (彝族) in <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">Sichuan</a> (四川) and <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a> (雲南). "Gi Ge" literally means "Holding Waist". The three main rules are: no tripping using the legs; no grabbing the jacket or pants; no pushing or striking. Rules have now been changed to allow holding the legs.</li>
<li><b>Ndrual Dluad</b>: Chinese wrestling practiced by <a href="/wiki/Miao_people" title="Miao people">Miao</a>/<a href="/wiki/Hmong_people" title="Hmong people">Hmong</a> Nationality throughout <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southwestern_China" title="Southwestern China">Southwestern China</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Belt_wrestling" title="Belt wrestling">belt wrestling</a> style, competitors can wear traditional dress or modern dress but they must hold on to the belt at all times.</li>
<li><b>Beiga 北嘎</b>: Chinese belt wrestling practiced by the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_people" title="Tibetan people">Tibetan</a> people. Also known as "Jiazhe (加哲)" and "Youri (有日)" in <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>, "Xiezhe (寫澤)" in <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">Western Sichuan</a>, and "Jiareze (卡惹則)" in <a href="/wiki/Qinghai" title="Qinghai">Qinghai</a>. It is a form of belt wrestling. Wrestlers compete barefoot and must hold the belt at all times. No tripping is allowed. Leg trips were introduced circa the 13th century, making 2 distinct forms.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_countries">Other countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Folk_wrestling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Other countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Sumo" title="Sumo">Sumo</a></b>: Japanese wrestling based on forcing the opponent out of the ring or to touch the ground with anything other than the soles of the feet. Sumo is notable for allowing slaps and strikes with the open palm. The rules were codified during the Tokugawa Shogunate and were based on Xiang-Pu 相撲, the Chinese wrestling style during the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Jujutsu" title="Jujutsu">Jujutsu</a></b>: Ancient style of Japanese wrestling that focuses on throws, pins, chokes and joint locks. Further refined by samurai during the violent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sengoku_Period" title="Sengoku Period">Sengoku Period</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ssireum" title="Ssireum">Ssireum</a></b>: <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Korea" title="Culture of Korea">Korean</a> belt wrestling contested in a sand pit</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Tegumi" title="Tegumi">Tegumi</a></b> is the folk wrestling practiced in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Okinawa" title="Okinawa">Okinawa</a>.</li>
<li><b>Mariwariwosu</b>, the indigenous style of the Formosan Aboriginal people of <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Paiwan_people" title="Paiwan people">Paiwan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bunun_people" title="Bunun people">Bunun</a> tribes. Performed on a circular sandpit with competitors grabbing hold of their opponents large waist belts before the start of the match it involves many skillful throws and is an important part of the National Aboriginal Games.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Americas">Americas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Folk_wrestling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Americas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Luta_Livre" title="Luta Livre">Luta Livre</a></b>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazilian</a> style of submission wrestling without a belt or jacket.</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lucha_Libre" title="Lucha Libre">Lucha Libre</a></b>, Mexican style of wrestling.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Huka-huka" title="Huka-huka">Huka-huka</a></b>, style of wrestling practiced by the indigenous <a href="/wiki/Yawalapiti" title="Yawalapiti">Yawalapiti</a> people of the Amazon basin.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Folk_wrestling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lucha_canaria" title="Lucha canaria">Lucha canaria</a></b>, a style of wrestling originating from the <a href="/wiki/Guanches" title="Guanches">guanches</a> ethny in the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> and further developed by <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> colonists</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Lutte_Traditionnelle" title="Lutte Traditionnelle">Lutte Traditionnelle</a></b>, (fr. for <i>Traditional Wrestling</i>) related styles of <i>West African wrestling</i>, known as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laamb" title="Laamb">Laamb</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a>, <i>Boreh</i> in <a href="/wiki/The_Gambia" title="The Gambia">The Gambia</a>, <i>Evala</i> in <a href="/wiki/Togo" title="Togo">Togo</a>, and <i>Lutte Traditionnelle</i> in <a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">Niger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Burkina_Faso" title="Burkina Faso">Burkina Faso</a>. International competition takes place during the <a href="/wiki/Jeux_de_la_Francophonie" title="Jeux de la Francophonie">Jeux de la Francophonie</a> and the newly organised <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Championnat_d%27Afrique_de_lutte_traditionnelle" title="fr:Championnat d'Afrique de lutte traditionnelle">Championnat d'Afrique de lutte traditionnelle</a>.</li>
<li><b>Tigel</b> - Traditional wrestling of Ethiopia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Grech" title="Grech">Grech</a></b> - Traditional wrestling practiced in <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceania">Oceania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Folk_wrestling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Oceania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Coreeda" title="Coreeda">Coreeda</a></b>, a modern synthesis that combines traditional Aboriginal dance, mainly in the form of kangaroo mimicry, with a style of wrestling performed around a yellow 4.5m diameter circle with black and red borders similar to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aboriginal_flag" title="Aboriginal flag">Aboriginal flag</a>. Competitors wear knee length pants, a wide sash belt and a jersey that can be grabbed to assist in throws. It is based on similar games that were played in pre-colonial <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and is usually performed during NAIDOC in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Suburbs_(Sydney)" title="Western Suburbs (Sydney)">Western Suburbs</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>. Extinct indigenous Australian styles include <i>turdererin</i> from Southern Victoria, <i>partambelin</i> from Southern NSW, <i>goombooboodoo</i> from Western NSW, <i>ami</i> from Southern QLD and <i>donaman/arungga</i> from Northern QLD.</li>
<li><b>Epoo korio</b>, a friendly style of wrestling done on <a href="/wiki/Kiwai_Island" title="Kiwai Island">Kiwai Island</a> in the Fly River Delta of Western Province of <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>, involved one wrestler who had to defend a small mound of sand which his opponent was trying to destroy.</li>
<li><b>Boumwane</b>, the national style of <a href="/wiki/Kiribati" title="Kiribati">Kiribati</a> with a simple toppling victory performed during National Day celebrations, a similar sport is also played in <a href="/wiki/Nauru" title="Nauru">Nauru</a>.</li>
<li><b>Fagatua</b>, the indigenous style of <a href="/wiki/Tokelau" title="Tokelau">Tokelau</a> used mainly to settle regional disputes between villages.</li>
<li><b>Hokoko</b>, the indigenous style of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kanaka_Maoli" title="Kanaka Maoli">Kanaka Maoli</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hawaii_Islands" title="Hawaii Islands">Hawaii Islands</a>, first recorded by crew members during the HMS Resolution's 1779 visit to the main island as part of the pa'ani'kahiko or 'ancient games', performed during the Makahiki New Year Festival. Along with <i>mokomoko boxing</i> it is a core skill of the bone breaking martial art of lua.</li>
<li><b>Mamau/ringa ringa</b>, the indigenous style of the <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language" title="Māori language">Maori</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, mainly used for warrior training but now occasionally being performed as part of the Matariki New Year Festivals as a recreational activity.</li>
<li><b>Moana</b>, the indigenous style of the Ma'ohi of <a href="/wiki/Tahiti" title="Tahiti">Tahiti</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_Polynesia" title="French Polynesia">French Polynesia</a>; along with <i>teka</i> (spear throwing), <i>motora'a</i> (boxing) and <i>amoraa ofae</i> (heavy stone lifting) will be included as part of the Heiva i Tahiti or traditional sports festival held in <a href="/wiki/Papeete" title="Papeete">Papeete</a> every July. A similar sport is also played in the <a href="/wiki/Cook_Islands" title="Cook Islands">Cook Islands</a> during the Te Maeva Nui national day celebrations.</li>
<li><b>Pi'i tauva</b>, the indigenous style of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Tonga" title="Kingdom of Tonga">Kingdom of Tonga</a> was first seen by Europeans in 1777 in which the artist <a href="/wiki/John_Webber" title="John Webber">John Webber</a> recorded in lithograph. It combined boxing and wrestling, being performed as entertainment for visitors by both men and women.</li>
<li><b>Taupiga</b>, the indigenous style of the <a href="/wiki/Samoan_Islands" title="Samoan Islands">Samoan Islands</a> saw the wrestlers greased up with coconut oil before competitions (similar to Turkish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yagli_gures" title="Yagli gures">yagli gures</a>) and was an important part of the inter-village gatherings.</li>
<li><b>Uma</b>, also known as <b>Kulakula'i</b>, is a hand-wrestling game practiced by the indigenous residents of <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>. The contestants kneel and grasp each other's elbows on the same side. The object is to force one's opponent's arm to the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Malo1903_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Malo1903-4">[4]</a></sup> The game was frequently played by the Hawaiian ruling class (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ali%27i" title="Ali'i">Ali'i</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Veibo</b>, the indigenous style of <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a> was mainly used as a method of warrior training but also occasionally as a form of entertainment. In the early 20th century, indentured labourers were brought from <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> to work the cane fields and their style of wrestling, <a href="/wiki/Kushti" title="Kushti">kushti</a>, was fused with veibo to create a hybrid style similar to freestyle wrestling.</li>
</ul>
