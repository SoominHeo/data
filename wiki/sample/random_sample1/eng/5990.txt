<p><b>Min</b> or <b>Miin</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[a]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans">闽语</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%96%A9" title="wikt:閩">閩</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%AA%9E" title="wikt:語">語</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Mǐn yǔ</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Pe%CC%8Dh-%C5%8De-j%C4%AB" title="Pe̍h-ōe-jī">Pe̍h-ōe-jī</a>: <i><span lang="nan">Bân gú</span></i>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B%C3%A0ng-u%C3%A2-c%C3%AA" title="Bàng-uâ-cê">BUC</a>: Mìng ngṳ̄) is a broad group of <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Chinese" title="Varieties of Chinese">Chinese varieties</a> spoken by over 70 million people in the southeastern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_China" title="Provinces of China">province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fujian" title="Fujian">Fujian</a> as well as by migrants from this province in <a href="/wiki/Guangdong" title="Guangdong">Guangdong</a> (around <a href="/wiki/Chaozhou" title="Chaozhou">Chaozhou</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swatou" title="Swatou">Swatou</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Chaoshan" title="Chaoshan">Chaoshan</a> area, <a href="/wiki/Leizhou" title="Leizhou">Leizhou</a> peninsula and Part of <a href="/wiki/Zhongshan" title="Zhongshan">Zhongshan</a>), <a href="/wiki/Hainan" title="Hainan">Hainan</a>, three counties in southern <a href="/wiki/Zhejiang" title="Zhejiang">Zhejiang</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zhoushan_archipelago" title="Zhoushan archipelago">Zhoushan archipelago</a> off <a href="/wiki/Ningbo" title="Ningbo">Ningbo</a>, some towns in <a href="/wiki/Liyang" title="Liyang">Liyang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jiangyin" title="Jiangyin">Jiangyin</a> City in <a href="/wiki/Jiangsu" title="Jiangsu">Jiangsu</a> province, and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>. The name is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Min_River_(Fujian)" title="Min River (Fujian)">Min River</a> in Fujian. Min varieties are not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutually_intelligible" title="Mutually intelligible">mutually intelligible</a> with any other varieties of Chinese.</p>
<p>There are many Min speakers among <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Chinese" title="Overseas Chinese">overseas Chinese</a> in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>. The most widely spoken variety of Min outside Fujian is <a href="/wiki/Hokkien" title="Hokkien">Hokkien</a> (which includes <a href="/wiki/Taiwanese_Hokkien" title="Taiwanese Hokkien">Taiwanese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amoy_dialect" title="Amoy dialect">Amoy</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Min_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Min homeland of Fujian was opened to Chinese settlement by the defeat of the <a href="/wiki/Minyue" title="Minyue">Minyue</a> state by the armies of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wu_of_Han" title="Emperor Wu of Han">Emperor Wu of Han</a> in 110 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENorman1991328_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENorman1991328-2">[1]</a></sup> The area features rugged mountainous terrain, with short rivers that flow into the <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a>. Most subsequent migration from north to south China passed through the valleys of the <a href="/wiki/Xiang_River" title="Xiang River">Xiang</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gan_River_(Jiangxi)" title="Gan River (Jiangxi)">Gan</a> rivers to the west, so that Min varieties have experienced less northern influence than other southern groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENorman1988210.2C_228_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENorman1988210.2C_228-3">[2]</a></sup> As a result, whereas most <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Chinese" title="Varieties of Chinese">varieties of Chinese</a> can be treated as derived from <a href="/wiki/Middle_Chinese" title="Middle Chinese">Middle Chinese</a>, the language described by <a href="/wiki/Rime_dictionary" title="Rime dictionary">rhyme dictionaries</a> such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Qieyun" title="Qieyun">Qieyun</a></i> (601 AD), Min varieties contain traces of older distinctions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENorman1988228.E2.80.93229_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENorman1988228.E2.80.93229-4">[3]</a></sup> Linguists estimate that the oldest layers of Min dialects diverged from the rest of Chinese around the time of the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETing19839.E2.80.9310_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETing19839.E2.80.9310-5">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaxterSagart201433.2C_79_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaxterSagart201433.2C_79-6">[5]</a></sup> However, significant waves of migration from the <a href="/wiki/North_China_Plain" title="North China Plain">North China Plain</a> occurred:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYan2006120_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYan2006120-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Uprising_of_the_Five_Barbarians" title="Uprising of the Five Barbarians">Uprising of the Five Barbarians</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Jin_dynasty_(265%E2%80%93420)" title="Jin dynasty (265–420)">Jin dynasty</a>, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Disaster_of_Yongjia" title="Disaster of Yongjia">Disaster of Yongjia</a> in 311 AD, caused a tide of immigration to the south.</li>
<li>In 669, <a href="/wiki/Chen_Zheng_(Tang_dynasty)" title="Chen Zheng (Tang dynasty)">Chen Zheng</a> and his son <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chen_Yuanguang" title="Chen Yuanguang">Chen Yuanguang</a> from <a href="/wiki/Gushi_County" title="Gushi County">Gushi County</a> in <a href="/wiki/Henan" title="Henan">Henan</a> set up a regional administration in <a href="/wiki/Fujian" title="Fujian">Fujian</a> to suppress an insurrection by the <a href="/wiki/She_people" title="She people">She people</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wang_Chao_(Tang_dynasty)" title="Wang Chao (Tang dynasty)">Wang Chao</a> was appointed governor of Fujian in 893, near the end of the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a>, and brought tens of thousands of troops from <a href="/wiki/Henan" title="Henan">Henan</a>. In 909, following the fall of the Tang dynasty, his son <a href="/wiki/Wang_Shenzhi" title="Wang Shenzhi">Wang Shenzhi</a> founded the <a href="/wiki/Min_(Ten_Kingdoms)" title="Min (Ten Kingdoms)">Min Kingdom</a>, one of the <a href="/wiki/Ten_Kingdoms" title="Ten Kingdoms">Ten Kingdoms</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Five_Dynasties_and_Ten_Kingdoms_period" title="Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period">Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jerry_Norman_(sinologist)" title="Jerry Norman (sinologist)">Jerry Norman</a> identifies four main layers in the vocabulary of modern Min varieties:</p>
<ol>
<li>A non-Chinese substratum from the original languages of <a href="/wiki/Minyue" title="Minyue">Minyue</a>, which Norman and Mei Tsu-lin believe were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austroasiatic" title="Austroasiatic">Austroasiatic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENormanMei1976_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENormanMei1976-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENorman1991331.E2.80.93332_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENorman1991331.E2.80.93332-9">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>The earliest Chinese layer, brought to Fujian by settlers from <a href="/wiki/Zhejiang" title="Zhejiang">Zhejiang</a> to the north during the Han dynasty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENorman1991334.E2.80.93336_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENorman1991334.E2.80.93336-10">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>A layer from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_and_Southern_Dynasties" title="Northern and Southern Dynasties">Northern and Southern Dynasties</a> period, which is largely consistent with the phonology of the <i>Qieyun</i> dictionary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENorman1991336_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENorman1991336-11">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Literary_and_colloquial_readings_of_Chinese_characters" title="Literary and colloquial readings of Chinese characters">literary layer</a> based on the <a href="/wiki/Koin%C3%A9_language" title="Koiné language">koiné</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chang%27an" title="Chang'an">Chang'an</a>, the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENorman1991337_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENorman1991337-12">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ol>

<p>Min is usually described as one of seven or ten groups of <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Chinese" title="Varieties of Chinese">varieties of Chinese</a> but has greater dialectal diversity than any of the other groups. The varieties used in neighbouring counties, and in the mountains of western Fujian even in adjacent villages, are often mutually unintelligible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENorman1988188_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENorman1988188-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early classifications, such as those of <a href="/wiki/Li_Fang-Kuei" title="Li Fang-Kuei">Li Fang-Kuei</a> in 1937 and <a href="/wiki/Yuan_Jiahua" title="Yuan Jiahua">Yuan Jiahua</a> in 1960, divided Min into Northern and Southern subgroups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKurpaska201049_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKurpaska201049-14">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENorman1988233_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENorman1988233-15">[14]</a></sup> However, in a 1963 report on a survey of Fujian, Pan Maoding and colleagues argued that the primary split was between inland and coastal groups. A key discriminator between the two groups is a group of words that have a <a href="/wiki/Lateral_consonant" title="Lateral consonant">lateral</a> initial <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/l/</span> in coastal varieties, and a voiceless fricative <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/s/</span> or <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʃ/</span> in inland varieties, contrasting with another group having <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/l/</span> in both areas. Norman reconstructs these initials in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Min" title="Proto-Min">Proto-Min</a> as voiceless and voiced laterals that merged in coastal varieties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENorman1988233_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENorman1988233-15">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBranner200098.E2.80.93100_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBranner200098.E2.80.93100-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although coastal varieties can be derived from a proto-language with four series of stops or affricates at each point of articulation (e.g. <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tʰ/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/d/</span>, and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/dʱ/</span>), inland varieties contain traces of two further series, which Norman termed "softened stops" due to their reflexes in some varieties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENorman1973_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENorman1973-17">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENorman1988228.E2.80.93230_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENorman1988228.E2.80.93230-18">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBranner2000100.E2.80.93104_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBranner2000100.E2.80.93104-19">[18]</a></sup> Coastal varieties also feature some uniquely Min vocabulary, including pronouns and negatives, where inland varieties use forms cognate with vocabulary in <a href="/wiki/Hakka_Chinese" title="Hakka Chinese">Hakka</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yue_Chinese" title="Yue Chinese">Yue</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENorman1988233.E2.80.93234_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENorman1988233.E2.80.93234-20">[19]</a></sup> All coastal varieties have complex <a href="/wiki/Tone_sandhi" title="Tone sandhi">tone sandhi</a> systems, whereas inland varieties have little or no tone sandhi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENorman1988239_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENorman1988239-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pan and colleagues divided the coastal varieties into three groups:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKurpaska201052_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKurpaska201052-22">[21]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Southern_Min" title="Southern Min">Southern Min</a> (Min Nan) originates from the south of Fujian and the eastern corner of Guangdong. In common parlance, Southern Min usually refers to <a href="/wiki/Hokkien" title="Hokkien">Hokkien</a>, of which the two major poles are the <a href="/wiki/Amoy_dialect" title="Amoy dialect">Amoy dialect</a> of <a href="/wiki/Xiamen" title="Xiamen">Xiamen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taiwanese_Hokkien" title="Taiwanese Hokkien">Taiwanese Hokkien</a> of <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>. <a href="/wiki/Zhenan_Min" title="Zhenan Min">Zhenan Min</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cangnan_County" title="Cangnan County">Cangnan County</a> in southern <a href="/wiki/Zhejiang" title="Zhejiang">Zhejiang</a> is also of this type. Related Hokkien varieties are spoken in Chinese communities spread across Southeast Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENorman1988232.E2.80.933_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENorman1988232.E2.80.933-23">[22]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Teochew_dialect" title="Teochew dialect">Teochew</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shantou_dialect" title="Shantou dialect">Shantou dialects</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Chaoshan" title="Chaoshan">Chaoshan</a> region of eastern Guangdong have difficult mutual intelligibility with the Amoy dialect. Chaoshan varieties are also spoken by most <a href="/wiki/Thai_Chinese" title="Thai Chinese">Thai Chinese</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENorman1988233_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENorman1988233-15">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pu-Xian_Min" title="Pu-Xian Min">Pu-Xian Min</a> is spoken in the city of <a href="/wiki/Putian" title="Putian">Putian</a> and the county of <a href="/wiki/Xianyou_County" title="Xianyou County">Xianyou County</a>. Li Rulong and Chen Zhangtai examined 214 words, finding 62% shared with <a href="/wiki/Quanzhou_dialect" title="Quanzhou dialect">Quanzhou dialect</a> (Southern Min) and 39% shared with Fuzhou dialect (Eastern Min), and concluded that Pu-Xian was more closely related to Southern Min.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELiChen1991_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELiChen1991-24">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eastern_Min" title="Eastern Min">Eastern Min</a> (Min Dong), centered around the city of <a href="/wiki/Fuzhou" title="Fuzhou">Fuzhou</a>, the capital of Fujian province, with <a href="/wiki/Fuzhou_dialect" title="Fuzhou dialect">Fuzhou dialect</a> as the prestige form.</li>
</ul>
<p>Pan and colleagues divided the inland varieties into two groups:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKurpaska201052_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKurpaska201052-22">[21]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Northern_Min" title="Northern Min">Northern Min</a> (Min Bei) is spoken in <a href="/wiki/Nanping" title="Nanping">Nanping</a> prefecture in Fujian, with <a href="/wiki/Jian%27ou_dialect" title="Jian'ou dialect">Jian'ou dialect</a> taken as typical.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Central_Min" title="Central Min">Central Min</a> (Min Zhong), spoken in <a href="/wiki/Sanming" title="Sanming">Sanming</a> prefecture.</li>
</ul>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Language_Atlas_of_China" title="Language Atlas of China">Language Atlas of China</a></i> (1987) includes three additional groups besides the five identified by Pan et al.:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKurpaska201071_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKurpaska201071-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Shao-Jiang_Min" title="Shao-Jiang Min">Shaojiang Min</a>, spoken in the northwestern Fujian counties of <a href="/wiki/Shaowu" title="Shaowu">Shaowu</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jiangle" title="Jiangle">Jiangle</a>, were classified as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hakka" title="Hakka">Hakka</a> by Pan and his associates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENorman1988233_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENorman1988233-15">[14]</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Norman_(sinologist)" title="Jerry Norman (sinologist)">Jerry Norman</a> suggested that they were inland varieties of Min that had been subject to heavy <a href="/wiki/Gan_Chinese" title="Gan Chinese">Gan</a> or Hakka influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENorman1988235.2C_241_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENorman1988235.2C_241-26">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Leizhou_Min" title="Leizhou Min">Leizhou Min</a>, spoken on the <a href="/wiki/Leizhou_Peninsula" title="Leizhou Peninsula">Leizhou Peninsula</a> in southern Guangdong, had previously been included in Southern Min.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hainanese" title="Hainanese">Hainanese</a>, spoken on the island of <a href="/wiki/Hainan" title="Hainan">Hainan</a>, had also previously been included in Southern Min. These dialects feature drastic changes to the initial consonants, including a series of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Implosive_consonants" title="Implosive consonants">implosives</a>, that have been attributed to contact with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tai-Kadai_languages" title="Tai-Kadai languages">Tai-Kadai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Austroasiatic_languages" title="Austroasiatic languages">Austroasiatic languages</a> spoken on the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELien2015169_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELien2015169-27">[26]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_system">Writing system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Min_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Writing system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Written_Hokkien" title="Written Hokkien">Written Hokkien</a></div>
<p>When using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_character" title="Chinese character">Chinese characters</a> to write a non-Mandarin form, standard practice is to use characters that correspond etymologically to the words being represented, and to invent new characters for words with no evident ancient Chinese etymology or in some cases for alternative pronunciations of existing characters, especially when the meaning is significantly different. <a href="/wiki/Written_Cantonese" title="Written Cantonese">Written Cantonese</a> has carried this process out to the farthest extent of any non-Mandarin variety, to the extent that pure Cantonese vernacular can be unambiguously written using Chinese characters. Contrary to popular belief, a vernacular written in this fashion is not in general comprehensible to a Mandarin speaker, due to significant changes in grammar and vocabulary and the necessary use of large number of non-Mandarin characters.</p>
<p>A similar process has never taken place for any of the Min varieties and there is no standard system for writing Min, although some specialized characters have been created. Given that Min combines the Chinese of several different periods and contains some non-Chinese vocabulary, one may have trouble finding the appropriate Chinese characters for some Min vocabulary. In the case of <a href="/wiki/Taiwanese_Hokkien" title="Taiwanese Hokkien">Taiwanese</a>, there are also indigenous words borrowed from <a href="/wiki/Formosan_languages" title="Formosan languages">Formosan languages</a>, as well as a substantial number of loan words from <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>. The Min spoken in Singapore and Malaysia has borrowed heavily from <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a> and, to a lesser extent, from <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> and other languages. The result is that cases of Min written purely in Chinese characters does not represent actual Min speech, but contains a heavy mixture of Mandarin forms.</p>
<p>Attempts to faithfully represent Min speech necessarily rely on <a href="/wiki/Romanization" title="Romanization">romanization</a>, i.e. representation using Latin characters. Some Min speakers use the Church Romanization (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh">教會羅馬字</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Jiaohui Luomazi</span></i>). For <a href="/wiki/Hokkien" title="Hokkien">Hokkien</a> the romanization is called <a href="/wiki/Pe%CC%8Dh-%C5%8De-j%C4%AB" title="Pe̍h-ōe-jī">Pe̍h-ōe-jī</a> (POJ) and for <a href="/wiki/Fuzhou_dialect" title="Fuzhou dialect">Fuzhou dialect</a> called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B%C3%A0ng-u%C3%A2-c%C3%AA" title="Bàng-uâ-cê">Bàng-uâ-cê</a> (BUC). Both systems were created by foreign missionaries in the 19th century (see <a class="extiw" href="https://zh-min-nan.wikipedia.org/wiki/" title="zh-min-nan:">Min Nan</a> and <a class="extiw" href="https://cdo.wikipedia.org/wiki/" title="cdo:">Min Dong Wikipedia</a>). There are some uncommon publications in mixed writing, using mostly Chinese characters but using the <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin alphabet</a> to represent words that cannot easily be represented by Chinese characters.</p>
