<p>The <b>Migration Period</b> was a time of widespread migrations within or into <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> in the middle of the first millennium AD. It has also been termed the <i><b><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Völkerwanderung</span></b></i> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and, from the Roman and Southern European perspective the <b>Barbarian Invasions</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Many of the migrations were movements of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_people" title="Germanic people">Germanic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slavic_people" title="Slavic people">Slavic</a>, and other peoples into the territory of the then <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, with or without accompanying invasions or war.</p>
<p>The migrants comprised war bands or tribes of 10,000 to 20,000 people,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heather2003_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heather2003-3">[3]</a></sup> but in the course of 100 years, they numbered not more than 750,000 in total, compared to an average 39.9 million population of the Roman Empire at that time. Although immigration was common throughout the time of the Roman Empire,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> the period in question was, in the 19th century, often defined as running from about the 5th to 8th centuries AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The first migrations of peoples were made by <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic tribes</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a> (including the <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ostrogoths" title="Ostrogoths">Ostrogoths</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Vandals" title="Vandals">Vandals</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxons</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">Lombards</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Suebi" title="Suebi">Suebi</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Frisii" title="Frisii">Frisii</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Jutes" title="Jutes">Jutes</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Burgundians" title="Burgundians">Burgundians</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alamanni" title="Alamanni">Alamanni</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Scirii" title="Scirii">Scirii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a>; they were later pushed westwards by the <a href="/wiki/Huns" title="Huns">Huns</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pannonian_Avars" title="Pannonian Avars">Avars</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Slavs" title="Slavs">Slavs</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bulgars" title="Bulgars">Bulgars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later invasions (such as the <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Viking</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Norman</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hungarian_people" title="Hungarian people">Hungarian</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moorish</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Turkic_peoples" title="Turkic peoples">Turkic</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Mongol_invasion_of_Europe" title="Mongol invasion of Europe">Mongol</a>), also had significant effects (especially in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>); however, they are outside the scope of the Migration Period.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_of_Germanic_tribes"><span id="Origins_of_the_Germanic_Tribes"></span>Origins of Germanic tribes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Migration_Period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins of Germanic tribes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" title="Proto-Germanic">Proto-Germanic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Roman_Iron_Age_(Northern_Europe)" title="Pre-Roman Iron Age (Northern Europe)">Pre-Roman Iron Age (Northern Europe)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marcomannic_Wars" title="Marcomannic Wars">Marcomannic Wars</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic peoples</a> moved out of southern <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> to the adjacent lands between the <a href="/wiki/Elbe" title="Elbe">Elbe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oder" title="Oder">Oder</a> after 1000 BC. The first wave moved westward and southward (pushing the resident <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a> west to the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> by about 200 BC), moving into <a href="/wiki/Southern_Germany" title="Southern Germany">southern Germany</a> up to the Roman provinces of <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cisalpine_Gaul" title="Cisalpine Gaul">Cisalpine Gaul</a> by 100 BC, where they were stopped by <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Marius" title="Gaius Marius">Gaius Marius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>. It is this western group which was described by the Roman historian <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> (56–117 AD) and Julius Caesar (100–44 BC). A later wave of Germanic tribes migrated eastward and southward from <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> between 600 and 300 BC to the opposite coast of the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>, moving up the <a href="/wiki/Vistula" title="Vistula">Vistula</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Carpathian_Mountains" title="Carpathian Mountains">Carpathians</a>. During <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a>' era they included lesser known tribes such as the <a href="/wiki/Tencteri_and_Usipetes" title="Tencteri and Usipetes">Tencteri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cherusci" title="Cherusci">Cherusci</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hermunduri" title="Hermunduri">Hermunduri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chatti" title="Chatti">Chatti</a>; however, a period of federation and intermarriage resulted in the familiar groups known as the <a href="/wiki/Alemanni" title="Alemanni">Alemanni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frisians" title="Frisians">Frisians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thuringii" title="Thuringii">Thuringians</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_phase"><span id="The_first_phase"></span>First phase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Migration_Period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: First phase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Iron_Age_(Northern_Europe)" title="Roman Iron Age (Northern Europe)">Roman Iron Age (Northern Europe)</a></div>
<p>The Barbarian Invasions may be divided into two phases. The first phase, occurring between <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">a.d.</span> 300 and 500, is partly documented by Greek and Latin historians but difficult to verify <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeologically</a>. It puts Germanic peoples in control of most areas of what was then the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall51_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-halsall51-11">[11]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tervingi" title="Tervingi">Tervingi</a> entered Roman territory (after a clash with the <a href="/wiki/Huns" title="Huns">Huns</a>) in 376. Some time thereafter in <a href="/wiki/Marcianopolis" title="Marcianopolis">Marcianopolis</a>, the escort to <a href="/wiki/Fritigern" title="Fritigern">Fritigern</a> (their leader) was killed while meeting with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lupicinus_(Roman)" title="Lupicinus (Roman)">Lupicinus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram2001127ff._12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram2001127ff.-12">[12]</a></sup> The Tervingi rebelled, and the <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a>, a group derived either from the Tervingi or from a fusion of mainly Gothic groups, eventually invaded Italy and <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Rome_(410)" title="Sack of Rome (410)">sacked Rome</a> in 410, before settling in Gaul, and then, 50 years later, in Iberia, founding <a href="/wiki/Visigothic_Kingdom" title="Visigothic Kingdom">a kingdom</a> that lasted for 250 years. They were followed into Roman territory first by a confederation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heruli" title="Heruli">Herulian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rugians" title="Rugians">Rugian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scirii" title="Scirii">Scirian</a> warriors, under <a href="/wiki/Odoacer" title="Odoacer">Odoacer</a>, that deposed <a href="/wiki/Romulus_Augustulus" title="Romulus Augustulus">Romulus Augustulus</a> on 4 September AD 476, and later by the <a href="/wiki/Ostrogoths" title="Ostrogoths">Ostrogoths</a>, led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theodoric_the_Great" title="Theodoric the Great">Theodoric the Great</a>, who settled in Italy. In <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>, the Franks (a fusion of western Germanic tribes whose leaders had been aligned with Rome since the third century <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">a.d.</span>) entered Roman lands gradually during the fifth century, and after consolidating power under <a href="/wiki/Childeric_I" title="Childeric I">Childeric</a> and his son <a href="/wiki/Clovis_I" title="Clovis I">Clovis’s</a> decisive victory over <a href="/wiki/Syagrius" title="Syagrius">Syagrius</a> in 486, established themselves as rulers of northern Roman Gaul. Fending off challenges from the Allemanni, Burgundians, and Visigoths, the Frankish kingdom became the nucleus of what would later become France and Germany. The initial <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_settlement_of_Britain" title="Anglo-Saxon settlement of Britain">Anglo-Saxon settlement of Britain</a> occurred during the fifth century, when <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman control of Britain</a> had come to an end.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDumville1990_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDumville1990-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Burgundians" title="Burgundians">Burgundians</a> settled in North Western Italy, Switzerland and Eastern France in the fifth century.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_phase"><span id="The_second_phase"></span>Second phase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Migration_Period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Second phase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_Iron_Age" title="Germanic Iron Age">Germanic Iron Age</a> and <a href="/wiki/Early_Slavs" title="Early Slavs">Early Slavs</a></div>
<p>The second phase took place between 500 and 700 and saw Slavic tribes settling in <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">central</a> and eastern Europe (notably in eastern <a href="/wiki/Germania" title="Germania">Magna Germania</a>), gradually making it predominantly Slavic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kob530_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-kob530-14">[14]</a></sup> Additionally, Turkic tribes such as the <a href="/wiki/Pannonian_Avars" title="Pannonian Avars">Avars</a> became involved in this phase. In 567, the Avars and the Lombards destroyed much of the <a href="/wiki/Gepids" title="Gepids">Gepid Kingdom</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">Lombards</a>, a Germanic people, settled in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> with their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heruli" title="Heruli">Herulian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suebi" title="Suebi">Suebian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gepid" title="Gepid">Gepid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thuringii" title="Thuringii">Thuringian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bulgar" title="Bulgar">Bulgarian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarmatian" title="Sarmatian">Sarmatian</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saxon" title="Saxon">Saxon</a> allies in the 6th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DBI_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-DBI-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> They were later followed by the <a href="/wiki/Bavarian_dynasty" title="Bavarian dynasty">Bavarians</a> and the Franks, who conquered and ruled most of Italy. The <a href="/wiki/Bulgars" title="Bulgars">Bulgars</a>, originally a nomadic group from <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, had occupied the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pontic_steppe" title="Pontic steppe">Pontic steppe</a> north of <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> since the second century, but after, pushed by the <a href="/wiki/Khazars" title="Khazars">Khazars</a>, the majority of them migrated west and dominated <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> territories along the <a href="/wiki/First_Bulgarian_Empire" title="First Bulgarian Empire">lower Danube</a> in the seventh century.</p>
<p>During the early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine%E2%80%93Arab_Wars" title="Byzantine–Arab Wars">Byzantine–Arab Wars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rashidun_army" title="Rashidun army">Arab armies</a> attempted to invade <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">southeast Europe</a> via <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Asia Minor</a> during the late seventh and early eighth centuries, but were defeated at the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Constantinople_(717%E2%80%93718)" title="Siege of Constantinople (717–718)">siege of Constantinople (717–718)</a> by the joint forces of Byzantium and the <a href="/wiki/Bulgars" title="Bulgars">Bulgars</a>. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khazar%E2%80%93Arab_Wars" title="Khazar–Arab Wars">Khazar–Arab Wars</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Khazars" title="Khazars">Khazars</a> stopped the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim_conquests" title="Muslim conquests">Arab expansion</a> into <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> across the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> (7th and 8th centuries). At the same time, the Moors (consisting of Africans like the <a href="/wiki/Haratin" title="Haratin">Haratin</a> Moors of the African country of Mauritania (Mooritania), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab" title="Arab">Arabs</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_people" title="Berber people">Berbers</a>) invaded Europe via <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Umayyad_conquest_of_Hispania" title="Umayyad conquest of Hispania">conquering Hispania</a>—the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>—from the <a href="/wiki/Visigothic_Kingdom" title="Visigothic Kingdom">Visigothic Kingdom</a> in 711), before being halted. These battles broadly demarcated the frontiers between <a href="/wiki/Christendom" title="Christendom">Christendom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muslim_world" title="Muslim world">Islam</a> for the next <a href="/wiki/Millennium" title="Millennium">millennium</a>. The following centuries saw the Muslims successful in <a href="/wiki/History_of_Islam_in_southern_Italy" title="History of Islam in southern Italy">conquering most of Sicily</a> from the Christians by 902.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_conquest_of_the_Carpathian_Basin" title="Hungarian conquest of the Carpathian Basin">Hungarian conquest of the Carpathian Basin</a> from around 895, and the <a href="/wiki/Viking_expansion" title="Viking expansion">Viking expansion</a> from the late 8th century conventionally mark the last large movements of the period. Christianity <a href="/wiki/Christianization#Christianization_of_Europe_.287th-15th_centuries.29" title="Christianization">gradually converted</a> the non-Islamic newcomers and integrated them into the medieval Christian order.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climatic_factors"><span id="Causes"></span>Climatic factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Migration_Period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Climatic factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of contemporary historical references worldwide refer to an extended period of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extreme_weather_events_of_535-536" title="Extreme weather events of 535-536">extreme weather during 535–536</a>. Evidence of this cold period is also found in <a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology" title="Dendrochronology">dendrochronology</a> and ice cores. The consequences of this cold period are debated.</p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Bond_event" title="Bond event">Bond event</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discussions"><span id="Modern_discourse"></span>Discussions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Migration_Period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Discussions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Barbarian_identity">Barbarian identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Migration_Period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Barbarian identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Analysis of barbarian identity and how it was created and expressed during the Barbarian Invasions has elicited discussion among scholars. Herwig Wolfram, a historian of the Goths,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> in discussing the equation of <i>migratio gentium</i> with <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Völkerwanderung</span></i>, observes that Michael Schmidt introduced the equation in his 1778 history of the Germans. Wolfram observed that the significance of <i><a href="/wiki/Gens" title="Gens">gens</a></i> as a biological community was shifting, even during the early Middle Ages and that "to complicate matters, we have no way of devising a terminology that is not derived from the concept of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nationhood" title="Nationhood">nationhood</a> created during the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>".</p>
<p>The "primordialistic"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> paradigm prevailed during the 19th century. Scholars, such as German linguist <a href="/wiki/Johann_Gottfried_Herder" title="Johann Gottfried Herder">Johann Gottfried Herder</a>, viewed tribes as coherent biological (racial) entities, using the term to refer to discrete ethnic groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> He also believed that the <i>Volk</i> were an organic whole, with a core identity and spirit evident in art, literature and language. These characteristics were seen as intrinsic, unaffected by external influences, even conquest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harvtxt.7CKulikowski.7C2007.7Cp_.3D_46_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-harvtxt.7CKulikowski.7C2007.7Cp_.3D_46-20">[20]</a></sup> Language, in particular, was seen as the most important expression of ethnicity. They argued that groups sharing the same (or similar) language possessed a common identity and ancestry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> This was the <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romantic</a> ideal that there once had been a single German, Celtic or Slavic people who originated from a common homeland and spoke a <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">common tongue</a>, helping to provide a <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_framework" title="Conceptual framework">conceptual framework</a> for <a href="/wiki/Political_movement" title="Political movement">political movements</a> of the 18th and 19th centuries such as <a href="/wiki/Pan-Germanism" title="Pan-Germanism">Pan-Germanism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pan-Slavism" title="Pan-Slavism">Pan-Slavism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harvtxt.7CKulikowski.7C2007.7Cp_.3D_46_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-harvtxt.7CKulikowski.7C2007.7Cp_.3D_46-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the 1960s, a reinterpretation of archaeological and historic evidence prompted scholars, such as Goffart and Todd, to propose new models for explaining the construction of barbarian identity. They maintained that no sense of shared identity was perceived by the <i>Germani</i>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halsall_2008_17_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Halsall_2008_17-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> a similar theory having been proposed for Celtic and Slavic groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>A theory states that the primordialist mode of thinking was encouraged by a <i><a href="/wiki/Prima_facie" title="Prima facie">prima facie</a></i> interpretation of <a href="/wiki/Greco-Roman_world" title="Greco-Roman world">Graeco-Roman</a> sources, which grouped together many tribes under such labels as <i>Germani</i>, <i>Keltoi</i> or <i>Sclavenoi</i>, thus encouraging their perception as distinct peoples. Modernists argue that the uniqueness perceived by specific groups was based on common political and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_interest" title="Economic interest">economic interests</a> rather than biological or racial distinctions.</p>
<p>The role of language in constructing and maintaining group identity can be ephemeral since large-scale language shifts occur commonly in history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Modernists propose the idea of "imagined communities"; the barbarian polities in late antiquity were social constructs rather than unchanging lines of blood kinship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The process of forming tribal units was called "<a href="/wiki/Ethnogenesis" title="Ethnogenesis">ethnogenesis</a>", a term coined by <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> scholar <a href="/wiki/Yulian_Bromley" title="Yulian Bromley">Yulian Bromley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The Austrian school (led by Reinhard Wenskus) popularized this idea, which influenced medievalists such as Herwig Wolfram, Walter Pohl and Patrick Geary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halsall_2008_17_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Halsall_2008_17-22">[22]</a></sup> It argues that the stimulus for forming tribal polities was perpetuated by a small nucleus of people, known as the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Traditionskern</span></i> ("kernel of tradition"), who were a military or aristocratic elite. This core group formed a standard for larger units, gathering adherents by employing amalgamative metaphors such as kinship and aboriginal commonality and claiming that they perpetuated an ancient, divinely-sanctioned lineage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harvtxt.7CGeary.7C2003.7Cp_.3D_77_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-harvtxt.7CGeary.7C2003.7Cp_.3D_77-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>"The common, track-filled map of the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Völkerwanderung</span></i> may illustrate such [a] course of events, but it misleads. Unfolded over long periods of time, the changes of position that took place were necessarily irregular ... (with) periods of emphatic discontinuity. For decades and possibly centuries, the tradition bearers idled, and the tradition itself hibernated. There was ample time for forgetfulness to do its work".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Viewpoints">Viewpoints</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Migration_Period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Viewpoints">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Historians have postulated several explanations for the appearance of "barbarians" on the Roman frontier: weather and crops, population pressure, a "primeval urge" to push into the Mediterranean or the "domino effect" of the Huns falling upon the Goths who, in turn, pushed other Germanic tribes before them. Entire barbarian tribes (or nations) flooded into <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">Roman provinces</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> ending classical <a href="/wiki/Urbanism" title="Urbanism">urbanism</a> and beginning new types of rural settlements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fouracre35_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-fouracre35-31">[31]</a></sup> In general, French and Italian scholars have tended to view this as a catastrophic event, the destruction of a civilization and the beginning of a "Dark Age" that set Europe back a millennium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fouracre35_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-fouracre35-31">[31]</a></sup> In contrast, German and English historians have tended to see Roman/Barbarian interaction as the replacement of a "tired, effete and decadent Mediterranean civilization" with a "more virile, martial, Nordic one".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fouracre35_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-fouracre35-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rather than "invasion", German and Slavic scholars speak of "migration" (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Völkerwanderung</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a>: <span lang="cs" xml:lang="cs"><i>Stěhování národů</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>: <span lang="sv" xml:lang="sv"><i>folkvandring</i></span> and <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a>: <span lang="hu" xml:lang="hu"><i>népvándorlás</i></span>), aspiring to the idea of a dynamic and "wandering <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-Germanic</a> people".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The scholar Guy Halsall has seen the barbarian movement as the result of the fall of the Roman Empire, not its cause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fouracre35_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-fouracre35-31">[31]</a></sup> Archaeological finds have confirmed that Germanic and Slavic tribes were settled agriculturalists<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> who were probably merely "drawn into the politics of an empire already falling apart for quite a few other causes". The <a href="/wiki/Crisis_of_the_Third_Century" title="Crisis of the Third Century">Crisis of the Third Century</a> caused significant changes within the Roman Empire in both its western and its eastern portions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In particular, economic fragmentation removed many of the political, cultural and economic forces that had held the empire together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rural population in Roman provinces became distanced from the metropolis, and there was little to differentiate them from other peasants across the Roman frontier. In addition, Rome increasingly used foreign mercenaries to defend itself. That "barbarisation" parallelled changes within <i>barbaricum</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>For example, the Roman Empire played a vital role in building up barbarian groups along its frontier. Propped up with imperial support and gifts, the armies of allied barbarian chieftains served as buffers against other, hostile, barbarian groups. The disintegration of Roman <a href="/wiki/Economic_power" title="Economic power">economic power</a> weakened groups that had come to depend on Roman gifts for the maintenance of their own power. The arrival of the Huns helped prompt many groups to invade the provinces for economic reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>The nature of the barbarian takeover of former Roman provinces varied from region to region. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Aquitaine" title="Aquitaine">Aquitaine</a>, the provincial administration was largely self-reliant. Halsall has argued that local rulers simply "handed over" military rule to the <a href="/wiki/Ostrogoths" title="Ostrogoths">Ostrogoths</a>, acquiring the identity of the newcomers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall51_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-halsall51-11">[11]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>, the collapse of imperial rule resulted in anarchy: the Franks and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alamanni" title="Alamanni">Alemanni</a> were pulled into the ensuing "power vacuum",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> resulting in conflict. In Spain, local aristocrats maintained independent rule for some time, raising their own armies against the <a href="/wiki/Vandals" title="Vandals">Vandals</a>. Meanwhile, the Roman withdrawal from Lowland England resulted in conflict between Saxons and the Brythonic chieftains (whose centres of power retreated westward as a result). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire" title="Eastern Roman Empire">Eastern Roman Empire</a> attempted to maintain control of the Balkan provinces despite a thinly-spread imperial army relyingmainly on local militias and an extensive effort to refortify the Danubian <i>limes</i>. The ambitious fortification efforts collapsed, worsening the impoverished conditions of the local populace and resulting in colonization by Slavic warriors and their families.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Halsall and Noble have argued that such changes stemmed from the breakdown in Roman political control, which exposed the weakness of local Roman rule. Instead of large-scale migrations, there were military takeovers by small groups of warriors and their families, who usually numbered only in the tens of thousands. The process involved active, conscious decision-making by Roman provincial populations.</p>
<p>The collapse of centralized control severely weakened the sense of Roman identity in the provinces, which may explain why the provinces then underwent dramatic cultural changes even though few barbarians settled in them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ultimately, the Germanic groups in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a> were accommodated without "dispossessing or overturning indigenous society", and they maintained a structured and hierarchical (but attenuated) form of Roman administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ironically, they lost their unique identity as a result of such an accommodation and were absorbed into Latinhood. In contrast, in the east, Slavic tribes maintained a more "spartan and egalitarian"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> existence bound to the land "even in times when they took their part in plundering Roman provinces".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Their organizational models were not Roman, and their leaders were not normally dependent on Roman gold for success. Thus they arguably had a greater effect on their region than the Goths, the Franks or the <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxons</a> had on theirs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethnicity">Ethnicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Migration_Period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ethnicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Based on the belief that particular types of artifacts, elements of personal adornment generally found in a funerary context, are thought to indicate the race and/or ethnicity of the person buried, the "Culture-History" school of archaeology assumed that archaeological cultures represent the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Urheimat" title="Urheimat">Urheimat</a></span></i> (homeland) of tribal polities named in historical sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> As a consequence, the shifting extensions of material cultures were interpreted as the expansion of peoples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kulikowski_2007_61_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kulikowski_2007_61-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Influenced by <a href="/wiki/Social_constructionism" title="Social constructionism">constructionism</a>, process-driven archaeologists rejected the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cultural-history_archaeology" title="Cultural-history archaeology">Culture-Historical</a> doctrine<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kulikowski_2007_61_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kulikowski_2007_61-46">[46]</a></sup> and marginalized the discussion of ethnicity altogether and focused on the intragroup dynamics that generated such material remains. Moreover, they argued that adoption of new cultures could occur through trade or internal political developments rather than only military takeovers.</p>
