<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Nation and Destiny</i></h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><i><b>Nation and Destiny</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Chosŏn'gŭl</a>: <span lang="ko-Hang" xml:lang="ko-Hang">민족과 운명</span>; <a href="/wiki/McCune%E2%80%93Reischauer" title="McCune–Reischauer">MR</a>: <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Korean transliteration">Minjokgwa ummyeong</span></i>) is a 62-part <a href="/wiki/North_Korean" title="North Korean">North Korean</a> film series released between 1992 and 2002. It aims to show that the Korean people "can live a glorious life only in the bosom of the <a href="/wiki/Kim_Il-sung" title="Kim Il-sung">Great Leader</a> and socialist fatherland". <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-Il" title="Kim Jong-Il">Kim Jong-Il</a> personally chose the title and was extensively involved in the early episodes. Conceived as the largest film series ever produced in any country, it was the largest investment ever made in the history of <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_North_Korea" title="Cinema of North Korea">North Korean cinema</a>. Initially, the most senior writers, directors and actors were involved in the project and it was heavily promoted and eulogized by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Korean_media" title="North Korean media">North Korean media</a>. The series was projected to reach 100 episodes, but none have been released since 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film is notable for its scenes set in the Western world and <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, and for its portrayal of the "anti-system figure" <a href="/wiki/Han_Sorya" title="Han Sorya">Han Sorya</a>, who was purged in the 1960s by Kim Il-sung, as a hero.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The use of South Korean popular songs was part of a 'mosquito-net strategy', whereby it was hoped the North Korean public would be immunised to the culture of the outside world by gradual exposure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> According to defectors' testimony, North Korean audiences were engrossed by its depiction of First World affluence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synopsis">Synopsis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nation_and_Destiny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Synopsis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Parts 1-4 were based on the life of <a href="/wiki/Choe_Deok-sin" title="Choe Deok-sin">Choe Deok-sin</a></li>
<li>Parts 5, 15-17 were based on the life of <a href="/wiki/Isang_Yun" title="Isang Yun">Isang Yun</a></li>
<li>Parts 6-9 were based on the life of <a href="/wiki/Choi_Hong_Hi" title="Choi Hong Hi">Choi Hong Hi</a></li>
<li>Parts 11-13 were based on the life of <a href="/wiki/Ri_In-mo" title="Ri In-mo">Ri In-mo</a></li>
</ul>
