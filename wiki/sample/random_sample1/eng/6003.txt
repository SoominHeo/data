<p><i><b>Milliyet</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a> for "<i>nationality</i>") is a major <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daily_newspaper" title="Daily newspaper">daily newspaper</a> published in <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_profile">History and profile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milliyet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and profile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Milliyet</i> came to publishing life at the Nuri Akça press in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bab%C4%B1ali" title="Babıali">Babıali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a> as a daily private newspaper on 3 May 1950. Its owner was <a href="/wiki/Ali_Naci_Karacan" title="Ali Naci Karacan">Ali Naci Karacan</a>. After his death in 1955 the paper was published by his son, Encüment Karacan.</p>
<p>For a number of years the person who made his mark on the paper as the editor in chief was <a href="/wiki/Abdi_%C4%B0pek%C3%A7i" title="Abdi İpekçi">Abdi İpekçi</a>. İpekçi managed to raise the standards of the Turkish press by introducing his journalistic criteria. On 1 February 1979, İpekçi was murdered by <a href="/wiki/Mehmet_Ali_A%C4%9Fca" title="Mehmet Ali Ağca">Mehmet Ali Ağca</a>, who would later attempt to assassinate the <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a>.</p>
<p><i>Milliyet</i> is published in <a href="/wiki/Broadsheet" title="Broadsheet">broadsheet format</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adsm_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-adsm-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001 <i>Milliyet</i> had a circulation of 337,000 copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adsm_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-adsm-2">[2]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/ComScore" title="ComScore">comScore</a>, <i>Milliyet</i>'s website is the fifth most visited news website in Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ownership">Ownership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milliyet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ownership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1979 the founding Karacan family sold the paper to <a href="/wiki/Ayd%C4%B1n_Do%C4%9Fan" title="Aydın Doğan">Aydın Doğan</a>. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Erdo%C4%9Fan_Demir%C3%B6ren&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Erdoğan Demirören (page does not exist)">Erdoğan Demirören</a>, who owned 25% of the paper, later also sold his stake to Doğan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In October 1998 the paper was briefly sold to <a href="/wiki/Korkmaz_Yi%C4%9Fit" title="Korkmaz Yiğit">Korkmaz Yiğit</a>, being bought back within weeks when Yiğit's business empire collapsed in the face of unrelated fraud allegations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crisis_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-crisis-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The paper was purchased by a joint venture of the <a href="/wiki/Demir%C3%B6ren_Group" title="Demirören Group">Demirören Group</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Karacan_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Karacan Group (page does not exist)">Karacan Group</a> in May 2011,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> but after legal and financial issues Karacan sold its stake to Demirören in February 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Editorial_line">Editorial line</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milliyet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Editorial line">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 1994, <i>Milliyet</i> has abandoned its stable, "upmarket" journalism established by Abdi İpekçi for a <a href="/wiki/Middle-market_newspaper" title="Middle-market newspaper">middle-market</a> editorial line akin to that of <a href="/wiki/H%C3%BCrriyet" title="Hürriyet">Hürriyet</a>. Internet edition of <i>Milliyet</i> often incorporates sensational material from <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Sun_(newspaper)" title="The Sun (newspaper)">The Sun</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Mail" title="Daily Mail">Daily Mail</a></i> and there is tremendous amount of overlap among the daily coverage, such as identical articles and photographs.</p>
<p><i>Milliyet</i> has been criticised for having <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-censored" title="Self-censored">self-censored</a> a column that was critical of the Prime Minister's reaction to a press leak.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The column was frozen out for two weeks and then blanket-refused for publication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In early 2012 <i>Milliyet</i> fired <a href="/wiki/Ece_Temelkuran" title="Ece Temelkuran">Ece Temelkuran</a> after she had written articles critical of the government's handling of the December 2011 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uludere_massacre" title="Uludere massacre">Uludere massacre</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Nuray_Mert" title="Nuray Mert">Nuray Mert</a> after Turkish Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Recep_Tayyip_Erdo%C4%9Fan" title="Recep Tayyip Erdoğan">Recep Tayyip Erdoğan</a> publicly criticized her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-PM-targets_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-PM-targets-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, <i>Milliyet</i> fired two columnists <a href="/wiki/Hasan_Cemal" title="Hasan Cemal">Hasan Cemal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Can_D%C3%BCndar" title="Can Dündar">Can Dündar</a>, who had taken critical stances against the <a href="/wiki/Justice_and_Development_Party_(Turkey)" title="Justice and Development Party (Turkey)">AKP</a> government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Digital_archives">Digital archives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milliyet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Digital archives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On September 2009, <i>Milliyet</i> opened its digital archive becoming the first Turkish newspaper to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
