<p>The <b>Battle of the Milvian Bridge</b> took place between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Emperor" title="Roman Emperor">Roman Emperors</a> <a href="/wiki/Constantine_the_Great" title="Constantine the Great">Constantine I</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maxentius" title="Maxentius">Maxentius</a> on October 28, 312. It takes its name from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milvian_Bridge" title="Milvian Bridge">Milvian Bridge</a>, an important route over the <a href="/wiki/Tiber" title="Tiber">Tiber</a>. Constantine won the battle and started on the path that led him to end the <a href="/wiki/Tetrarchy" title="Tetrarchy">Tetrarchy</a> and become the sole ruler of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>. Maxentius drowned in the Tiber during the battle and his body was later taken from the river and decapitated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to chroniclers such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eusebius_of_Caesarea" title="Eusebius of Caesarea">Eusebius of Caesarea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lactantius" title="Lactantius">Lactantius</a>, the battle marked the beginning of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_I_and_Christianity" title="Constantine I and Christianity">Constantine's conversion to Christianity</a>. Eusebius of Caesarea recounts that Constantine and his soldiers had a vision sent by the Christian God. This was interpreted as a promise of victory if the sign of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chi-Rho" title="Chi-Rho">Chi-Rho</a>, the first two letters of Christ's name in Greek, was painted on the soldiers' shields. The <a href="/wiki/Arch_of_Constantine" title="Arch of Constantine">Arch of Constantine</a>, erected in celebration of the victory, certainly attributes Constantine's success to divine intervention; however, the monument does not display any overtly Christian symbolism.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_background">Historical background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_the_Milvian_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The underlying causes of the battle were the rivalries inherent in Diocletian's Tetrarchy. After Diocletian stepped down on 1 May 305, his successors began to struggle for control of the Roman Empire almost immediately. Although Constantine was the son of the Western Emperor <a href="/wiki/Constantius_Chlorus" title="Constantius Chlorus">Constantius</a>, the Tetrarchic ideology did not necessarily provide for hereditary succession. When Constantius died on 25 July 306, his father's troops proclaimed Constantine as <a href="/wiki/Augustus_(honorific)" title="Augustus (honorific)">Augustus</a> in <a href="/wiki/Eboracum" title="Eboracum">Eboracum</a> (<a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>). In Rome, the favorite was Maxentius, the son of Constantius' imperial colleague <a href="/wiki/Maximian" title="Maximian">Maximian</a>, who seized the title of emperor on 28 October 306. But whereas Constantine's claim was recognized by <a href="/wiki/Galerius" title="Galerius">Galerius</a>, ruler of the Eastern provinces and the senior emperor in the Empire, Maxentius was treated as a usurper. Galerius, however, recognized Constantine as holding only the lesser imperial rank of Caesar. Galerius ordered his co-Augustus, <a href="/wiki/Flavius_Valerius_Severus" title="Flavius Valerius Severus">Severus</a>, to put Maxentius down in early 307. Once Severus arrived in Italy, however, his army defected to Maxentius. Severus was captured, imprisoned, and executed. Galerius himself marched on Rome in the autumn, but failed to take the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Constantine avoided conflict with both Maxentius and the Eastern emperors for most of this period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 312, however, Constantine and Maxentius were engaged in open hostility with one another, although they were brothers-in‑law through Constantine's marriage to <a href="/wiki/Fausta" title="Fausta">Fausta</a>, sister of Maxentius. In the spring of 312, Constantine gathered his forces and decided to oust Maxentius himself. He easily overran northern Italy, winning two major battles: the first near <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Turin_(312)" title="Battle of Turin (312)">Turin</a>, the second at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Verona_(312)" title="Battle of Verona (312)">Verona</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_prefect" title="Praetorian prefect">praetorian prefect</a> <a href="/wiki/Ruricius_Pompeianus" title="Ruricius Pompeianus">Ruricius Pompeianus</a>, Maxentius' most senior general, was killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vision_of_Constantine">Vision of Constantine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_the_Milvian_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vision of Constantine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Missorium_Kerch.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1609" data-file-width="1600" height="251" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Missorium_Kerch.jpg/250px-Missorium_Kerch.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Missorium_Kerch.jpg/375px-Missorium_Kerch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Missorium_Kerch.jpg/500px-Missorium_Kerch.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Missorium_Kerch.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Missorium depicting Constantine's son Constantius II, accompanied by a guardsman with the <a href="/wiki/Chi_Rho" title="Chi Rho">Chi Rho</a> monogram depicted on his shield</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">"Vision of Constantine" redirects here. For the Bernini sculpture, see <a href="/wiki/The_Vision_of_Constantine_(Bernini)" title="The Vision of Constantine (Bernini)">The Vision of Constantine (Bernini)</a>.</div>
<p>It is commonly understood that on the evening of 27 October with the armies preparing for battle, Constantine had a vision which led him to fight under the protection of the Christian God. Some details of that vision, however, differ between the sources reporting it.</p>
<p>Lactantius states that, in the night before the battle, Constantine was commanded in a dream to "delineate the heavenly sign on the shields of his soldiers" (<i>On the Deaths of the Persecutors</i> 44.5). He followed the commands of his dream and marked the shields with a sign "denoting Christ". Lactantius describes that sign as a "staurogram", or a <a href="/wiki/Christian_cross" title="Christian cross">Latin cross</a> with its upper end rounded in a P-like fashion. There is no certain evidence that Constantine ever used that sign, opposed to the better known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chi-Rho" title="Chi-Rho">Chi-Rho</a> sign described by Eusebius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Constantine_multiple_CdM_Beistegui_233.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="1000" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Constantine_multiple_CdM_Beistegui_233.jpg/220px-Constantine_multiple_CdM_Beistegui_233.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Constantine_multiple_CdM_Beistegui_233.jpg/330px-Constantine_multiple_CdM_Beistegui_233.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Constantine_multiple_CdM_Beistegui_233.jpg/440px-Constantine_multiple_CdM_Beistegui_233.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Constantine_multiple_CdM_Beistegui_233.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A coin struck in 313, depicting Constantine as the companion of a solar deity</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>From Eusebius, two accounts of the battle survive. The first, shorter one in the <i><a href="/wiki/Church_History_(Eusebius)" title="Church History (Eusebius)">Ecclesiastical History</a></i> promotes the belief that God helped Constantine but does not mention any vision. In his later <i>Life of Constantine</i>, Eusebius gives a detailed account of a vision and stresses that he had heard the story from the Emperor himself. According to this version, Constantine with his army was marching (Eusebius does not specify the actual location of the event, but it clearly is not in the camp at Rome), when he looked up to the sun and saw a cross of light above it, and with it the Greek words "Εν Τούτῳ Νίκα", <i>En toutō níka</i>, usually translated into Latin as "<i><a href="/wiki/In_hoc_signo_vinces" title="In hoc signo vinces">in hoc signo vinces</a></i>". Both phrases have the literal meaning "In this sign, [you shall] conquer"; a more free translation would be "Through this sign [you shall] conquer". At first he was unsure of the meaning of the apparition, but in the following night he had a dream in which Christ explained to him that he should use the sign against his enemies. Eusebius then continues to describe the <a href="/wiki/Labarum" title="Labarum">labarum</a>, the military standard used by Constantine in his later wars against <a href="/wiki/Licinius" title="Licinius">Licinius</a>, showing the Chi-Rho sign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The accounts of the two contemporary authors, though not entirely consistent, have been merged into a popular notion of Constantine seeing the Chi-Rho sign on the evening before the battle. Both authors agree that the sign was not widely understandable to denote Christ (although among the Christians, it was already being used in the <a href="/wiki/Chi_Rho#Gallery" title="Chi Rho">catacombs</a> along with other special symbols to mark and/or decorate Christian tombs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Its first imperial appearance is on a Constantinian silver coin from c. 317, which proves that Constantine did use the sign at that time, though not very prominently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He made more extensive use of the Chi-Rho and the Labarum later, during the conflict with Licinius.</p>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fargo_Sundogs_2_18_09.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The description from 28th October 312, "A cross centered on the Sun" fits with modern-day photographs of Sun dogs.</div>

<p>Some<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> have considered the vision in a solar context (e.g. as a <a href="/wiki/Halo_(optical_phenomenon)" title="Halo (optical phenomenon)">solar halo</a> phenomenon called a <a href="/wiki/Sun_dog" title="Sun dog">sun dog</a>), which may have preceded the Christian beliefs later expressed by Constantine. Coins of Constantine depicting him as the companion of a solar deity were minted as late as 313, the year following the battle. The solar deity <a href="/wiki/Sol_Invictus" title="Sol Invictus">Sol Invictus</a> is often pictured with a <a href="/wiki/Halo_(religious_iconography)" title="Halo (religious iconography)">nimbus</a> or halo. Various emperors portrayed Sol Invictus on their official coinage, with a wide range of legends, only a few of which incorporated the epithet <i>invictus</i>, such as the legend <small>SOLI INVICTO COMITI</small>, claiming the Unconquered Sun as a companion to the emperor, used with particular frequency by Constantine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Constantine's official coinage continues to bear images of Sol until 325/6. A <a href="/wiki/Solidus_(coin)" title="Solidus (coin)">solidus</a> of Constantine as well as a gold medallion from his reign depict the Emperor's bust in profile <a href="/wiki/Jugate" title="Jugate">jugate</a> with Sol Invictus, with the legend <small>INVICTUS CONSTANTINUS</small>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The official cults of Sol Invictus and Sol Invictus <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mithras" title="Mithras">Mithras</a> were popular amongst the soldiers of the Roman Army. Statuettes of Sol Invictus, carried by the standard-bearers, appear in three places in reliefs on the <a href="/wiki/Arch_of_Constantine" title="Arch of Constantine">Arch of Constantine</a>. Constantine's triumphal arch was carefully positioned to align with the <a href="/wiki/Colossus_of_Nero" title="Colossus of Nero">colossal statue of Sol</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Colosseum" title="Colosseum">Colosseum</a>, so that Sol formed the dominant backdrop when seen from the direction of the main approach towards the arch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Events_of_the_battle">Events of the battle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_the_Milvian_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Events of the battle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Luk_Konstantyna_6DSCF0032.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Luk_Konstantyna_6DSCF0032.JPG/250px-Luk_Konstantyna_6DSCF0032.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Luk_Konstantyna_6DSCF0032.JPG/375px-Luk_Konstantyna_6DSCF0032.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Luk_Konstantyna_6DSCF0032.JPG/500px-Luk_Konstantyna_6DSCF0032.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Luk_Konstantyna_6DSCF0032.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A contemporary image of the battle from the Arch of Constantine, Rome. In the frieze at the foot of the image Constantine's cavalry drive Maxentius' troops into the waters of the Tiber.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Constantine reached Rome at the end of October 312 approaching along the <a href="/wiki/Via_Flaminia" title="Via Flaminia">Via Flaminia</a>. He camped at the location of Malborghetto near <a href="/wiki/Prima_Porta" title="Prima Porta">Prima Porta</a>, where remains of a Constantinian monument, the <a href="/wiki/Arch_of_Malborghetto" title="Arch of Malborghetto">Arch of Malborghetto</a>, in honour of the occasion are still extant.</p>
<p>It was expected that Maxentius would remain within Rome and endure a siege; he had successfully employed this strategy twice before, during the invasions of Severus and Galerius. Indeed, Maxentius had organised the stockpiling of large amounts of food in the city in preparation for such an event. Surprisingly, he decided otherwise, choosing to meet Constantine in open battle. Ancient sources commenting on these events attribute this decision either to <a href="/wiki/Miracle" title="Miracle">divine intervention</a> (e.g. Lactantius, Eusebius) or superstition (e.g. Zosimus). They also note that the day of the battle was the same as the day of his accession (28 October), which was generally thought to be a good omen. Additionally, Maxentius is reported to have consulted the oracular <a href="/wiki/Sibylline_Books" title="Sibylline Books">Sibylline Books</a>, which stated that "on October 28 an enemy of the Romans would perish". Maxentius interpreted this prophecy as being favourable to himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Lactantius also reports that the populace supported Constantine with acclamations during circus games,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Battle_of_the_Milvian_Bridge_by_Giulio_Romano,_1520-24.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1858" data-file-width="4433" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Battle_of_the_Milvian_Bridge_by_Giulio_Romano%2C_1520-24.jpg/350px-Battle_of_the_Milvian_Bridge_by_Giulio_Romano%2C_1520-24.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Battle_of_the_Milvian_Bridge_by_Giulio_Romano%2C_1520-24.jpg/525px-Battle_of_the_Milvian_Bridge_by_Giulio_Romano%2C_1520-24.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Battle_of_the_Milvian_Bridge_by_Giulio_Romano%2C_1520-24.jpg/700px-Battle_of_the_Milvian_Bridge_by_Giulio_Romano%2C_1520-24.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Battle_of_the_Milvian_Bridge_by_Giulio_Romano,_1520-24.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/The_Battle_of_the_Milvian_Bridge_(Giulio_Romano)" title="The Battle of the Milvian Bridge (Giulio Romano)">The Battle of the Milvian Bridge</a></i> (1520–24) by <a href="/wiki/Giulio_Romano" title="Giulio Romano">Giulio Romano</a>.</div>
</div>

<p>Maxentius chose to make his stand in front of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milvian_Bridge" title="Milvian Bridge">Milvian Bridge</a>, a stone bridge that carries the <a href="/wiki/Via_Flaminia" title="Via Flaminia">Via Flaminia</a> road across the Tiber River into Rome (the bridge stands today at the same site, somewhat remodelled, named in Italian <i>Ponte Milvio</i> or sometimes <i>Ponte Molle</i>, "soft bridge"). Holding it was crucial if Maxentius was to keep his rival out of Rome, where the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Senate" title="Roman Senate">Senate</a> would surely favour whoever held the city. As Maxentius had probably partially destroyed the bridge during his preparations for a siege, he had a wooden or <a href="/wiki/Pontoon_bridge" title="Pontoon bridge">pontoon bridge</a> constructed to get his army across the river. The sources vary as to the nature of the bridge central to the events of the battle. Zosimus mentions it, vaguely, as being constructed in two parts connected by iron fastenings, while others indicate that it was a pontoon bridge; sources are also unclear as to whether the bridge was deliberately constructed as a collapsible trap for Constantine's forces or not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The next day, the two armies clashed, and Constantine won a decisive victory. The dispositions of Maxentius may have been faulty as his troops seem to have been arrayed with the River Tiber too close to their rear, giving them little space to allow re-grouping in the event of their formations being forced to give ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Already known as a skilful general, Constantine first launched his cavalry at the cavalry of Maxentius and broke them. Constantine's infantry<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> then advanced; most of Maxentius's troops fought well but they began to be pushed back toward the Tiber. Maxentius then decided to order a retreat, intending to make another stand at Rome itself. However, there was only one escape route, via the bridge. Constantine's men inflicted heavy losses on the retreating army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Finally, the temporary bridge set up alongside the Milvian Bridge, over which many of the Maxentian troops were escaping, collapsed, and those stranded on the north bank of the Tiber were either taken prisoner or killed. Maxentius' <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_Guard" title="Praetorian Guard">Praetorian Guard</a>, who had originally acclaimed him emperor, seem to have made a stubborn stand on the northern bank of the river; "in despair of pardon they covered with their bodies the place which they had chosen for combat."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Maxentius was among the dead, having drowned in the river while trying to swim across it in an attempt to escape or, alternatively, he is described as having been thrown by his horse into the river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Lactantius describes the death of Maxentius in the following manner: "The bridge in his rear was broken down. At sight of that the battle grew hotter. The hand of the Lord prevailed, and the forces of Maxentius were routed. He fled towards the broken bridge; but the multitude pressing on him, he was driven headlong into the Tiber."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_the_Milvian_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Constantine entered Rome on 29 October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odahl.2C_108_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Odahl.2C_108-23">[23]</a></sup> He staged a grand arrival ceremony in the city (<i><a href="/wiki/Adventus_(ceremony)" title="Adventus (ceremony)">adventus</a>)</i>, and was met with popular jubilation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Maxentius' body was fished out of the Tiber and decapitated. His head was paraded through the streets for all to see.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odahl.2C_108_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Odahl.2C_108-23">[23]</a></sup> After the ceremonies, Maxentius' head was sent to <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a> as proof of his downfall, Africa then offered no further resistance. The battle gave Constantine undisputed control of the western half of the Roman Empire. The descriptions of Constantine's entry into Rome omit mention of him ending his procession at the temple of Capitoline Jupiter, where sacrifice was usually offered. Though often employed to show Constantine's Christian sensibilities, this silence cannot be taken as proof that Constantine was a Christian at this point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> He chose to honour the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Senate" title="Roman Senate">Senatorial</a> <a href="/wiki/Curia_Julia" title="Curia Julia">Curia</a> with a visit,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odahl.2C_109_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Odahl.2C_109-26">[26]</a></sup> where he promised to restore its ancestral privileges and give it a secure role in his reformed government: there would be no revenge against Maxentius' supporters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odahl.2C_109_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Odahl.2C_109-26">[26]</a></sup> Maxentius was condemned to <i><a href="/wiki/Damnatio_memoriae" title="Damnatio memoriae">damnatio memoriae</a></i>, all his legislation was invalidated and Constantine usurped all of Maxentius' considerable building projects within Rome, including the <a href="/wiki/Santi_Cosma_e_Damiano#History" title="Santi Cosma e Damiano">Temple of Romulus</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Maxentius" title="Basilica of Maxentius">Basilica of Maxentius</a>. Maxentius' strongest supporters in the military were neutralized when the <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_Guard" title="Praetorian Guard">Praetorian Guard</a> and Imperial Horse Guard (<i>equites singulares</i>) were disbanded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odahl.2C_109_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-Odahl.2C_109-26">[26]</a></sup> Constantine is thought to have replaced the former imperial guards with a number of cavalry units termed the <a href="/wiki/Scholae_Palatinae" title="Scholae Palatinae">Scholae Palatinae</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Significance">Significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_the_Milvian_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Bishops_of_Rome_under_Constantine_I" title="Bishops of Rome under Constantine I">Bishops of Rome under Constantine I</a></div>
<p>Paul K. Davis writes, "Constantine’s victory gave him total control of the Western Roman Empire paving the way for Christianity to become the dominant religion for the Roman Empire and ultimately for Europe."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The following year, 313, Constantine and <a href="/wiki/Licinius" title="Licinius">Licinius</a> issued the <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Milan" title="Edict of Milan">Edict of Milan</a>, which made Christianity an officially recognised and tolerated religion in the Roman Empire.</p>
