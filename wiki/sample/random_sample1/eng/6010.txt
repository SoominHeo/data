<p>The <b>mille-feuille</b> (<small>French pronunciation:¬†‚Äã</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[mil f≈ìj]</a></span>, "thousand leaves"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[notes 1]</a></sup> <b>vanilla slice, custard slice,</b> also known as the <b>Napoleon</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/French_cuisine" title="French cuisine">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Pastry" title="Pastry">pastry</a> whose exact origin is unknown. Its modern form was influenced by improvements of <a href="/wiki/Marie-Antoine_Car%C3%AAme" title="Marie-Antoine Car√™me">Marie-Antoine Car√™me</a>.</p>
<p>Traditionally, a mille-feuille is made up of three layers of <a href="/wiki/Puff_pastry" title="Puff pastry">puff pastry</a> (<i>p√¢te feuillet√©e</i>), alternating with two layers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pastry_cream" title="Pastry cream">pastry cream</a> (<i>cr√®me p√¢tissi√®re</i>), but sometimes <a href="/wiki/Whipped_cream" title="Whipped cream">whipped cream</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jam" title="Jam">jam</a> are substituted. The top pastry layer is dusted with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Confectioner%27s_sugar" title="Confectioner's sugar">confectioner's sugar</a>, and sometimes cocoa, pastry crumbs, or pulverized seeds (e.g. roasted <a href="/wiki/Almond" title="Almond">almonds</a>). Alternatively the top is glazed with <a href="/wiki/Icing_(food)" title="Icing (food)">icing</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fondant_icing" title="Fondant icing">fondant</a> in alternating white (icing) and <a href="/wiki/Chocolate" title="Chocolate">brown (chocolate)</a> stripes, and <a href="/wiki/Paper_marbling" title="Paper marbling">combed</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mille-feuille&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All the elements of the recipe are present in numerous cookbooks since, at least, the 16th century but the exact origin of the mille-feuille is unknown. The earliest mention of the name Mille-feuille itself appears in 1733 in an English-language cookbook written by French chef <a href="/wiki/Vincent_La_Chapelle" title="Vincent La Chapelle">Vincent La Chapelle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[1]</a></sup> Unlike the modern cake, the 18th-century Mille-feuille is served stuffed with jam and marmalade instead of cream.</p>
<p>In French, the first mention of the Mille-feuille appears a little later, in 1749, in a cookbook by <a href="/wiki/Menon_(cookbook_author)" title="Menon (cookbook author)">Menon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>To make a <i>mille-feuille</i> cake, you take puff pastry, make out of it five cakes of equal size, &amp; of the thickness of two coins, in the last one you shall make a hole in the middle in the shape of a <a href="/wiki/Maltese_cross" title="Maltese cross">Knight's cross</a>, regarding the size you will base yourself on the dish that you will use for service, bake them in the oven. When they are baked &amp; cooled, stack them one on the other, the one with the hole on top, &amp; jams between every cake, [sentence unclear, maybe referring to covering all sides with jam] &amp; ice them everywhere with white icing so that they appear to be a single piece; you can embellish it with some red currant jelly, candied lemon skins &amp; pistachio, you serve them on a plate.</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"><a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Menon" title="Menon">Menon</a>, <i>La science du ma√Ætre d'h√¥tel cuisinier , avec des observations sur la connaissance &amp; propri√©t√©s des alimens</i> , 1749, p.367. Available <a class="external text" href="http://gallica.bnf.fr/ark:/12148/bpt6k54549881/f471.item.r=mille.zoom" rel="nofollow">online</a>.</cite></div>
<p>The word <i>mille-feuille</i> is not used again in the recipe books of the 18th century. However, under the reign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">Napoleon Bonaparte</a>, several of the fanciest Parisian pastry shops appear to have sold the cake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> During the 19th century, all recipes are filled with jam with the exception of the 1876 recipe by <a href="/wiki/Urbain_Dubois" title="Urbain Dubois">Urbain Dubois</a> which is served with <a href="/wiki/Bavarian_cream" title="Bavarian cream">Bavarian cream</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Alan_Davidson_(food_writer)" title="Alan Davidson (food writer)">Alan Davidson</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_Companion_to_Food" title="Oxford Companion to Food">Oxford Companion to Food</a> (p.¬†505), the invention of the form (but not of the pastry itself) is usually attributed to <a href="/wiki/Szeged" title="Szeged">Szeged</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, where a caramel-coated mille-feuille is called <i>Szegedinertorte</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OxCoFo-mille_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-OxCoFo-mille-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mille-feuille&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Traditionally, a mille-feuille is made up of three layers of puff pastry, and two layers of <a href="/wiki/Custard" title="Custard">cr√®me p√¢tissi√®re</a>. The top layer is coated with a sprinkling of powdered sugar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> In later variations, the top is glazed with icing, in alternating white (icing) and brown (chocolate) strips, and then combed. Today, there are also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Savoury_(small_dish)" title="Savoury (small dish)">savory</a> mille-feuille, with <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">cheese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spinach" title="Spinach">spinach</a> or other savory fillings.</p>

<p>According to La Varenne, it was earlier called <i>g√¢teau de mille-feuilles</i> (English: cake of a thousand sheets), referring to the many layers of pastry. Using traditional puff pastry, made with six folds of three layers, it has 729 layers; with some modern recipes it may have as many as 2,048.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The variant name of <i>Napoleon</i> appears to come from <i>napolitain</i>, the French <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjective</a> for the Italian city of <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>, but altered by association with the name of Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" title="Napoleon I of France">Napoleon I of France</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Larousse_Gastronomique" title="Larousse Gastronomique">Larousse Gastronomique</a></i> does not mention the Napol√©on, although a <i>gateau napolitain</i> is listed, with a note that while the cake itself is not often seen, small biscuits known as <i>fonds napolitains</i> are still made, decorated with butter cream or conserves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> There is no evidence to connect the pastry to the emperor himself. In France, a <i>Napol√©on</i> is a particular type of mille-feuille filled with almond flavoured paste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OxCoFo-mille_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-OxCoFo-mille-6">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Russian_cuisine" title="Russian cuisine">Russian</a> literature, a cake named <i>–ù–∞–ø–æ–ª–µ–æ–Ω (Napoleon)</i> is first mentioned as early as in the first half of the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bestuzhev_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bestuzhev-11">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Bestuzhev" title="Alexander Bestuzhev">Alexander Bestuzhev</a> explained the emergence of such names by the <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">romantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Historicism_(art)" title="Historicism (art)">historicist</a> spirit of that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bestuzhev_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bestuzhev-11">[10]</a></sup> The cake has enjoyed an especially great popularity since the centenary celebration of the Russian victory over Napoleon in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriotic_War_of_1812" title="Patriotic War of 1812">Patriotic War of 1812</a>. During the celebrations in 1912, triangular-shape pastries were sold resembling the <a href="/wiki/Bicorne" title="Bicorne">bicorne</a>. The many layers of the cake symbolized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Grande_Arm%C3%A9e" title="La Grande Arm√©e">La Grande Arm√©e</a>. The top is covered by pastry crumbs symbolizing the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Winter" title="Russian Winter">snow of Russia</a> which helped the Russians defeat Napoleon. Later, the cake became a standard dessert in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_cuisine" title="Soviet cuisine">Soviet cuisine</a>. Nowadays, the Napoleon remains one of the most popular cakes in Russia and other post-Soviet countries. It typically has more layers than the French archetype, but the same height.</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Italian_cuisine" title="Italian cuisine">Italy</a>, it is called <i>mille foglie</i> and contains similar fillings. A savory Italian version consists of puff pastry filled with <a href="/wiki/Spinach" title="Spinach">spinach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">cheese</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pesto" title="Pesto">pesto</a>, among other things. Another important distinction of the Italian variety is that it often consists of a layer of puff pastry with layers of sponge cake as well (e.g. from bottom to top, puff pastry, sponge cake strawberries and cream and then puff pastry).</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/British_cuisine" title="British cuisine">United Kingdom</a>, the pastry is most often called a <i>vanilla slice</i>, <i>cream slice</i>, or a <i>custard slice</i>, but can, on occasion, be named <i>mille-feuille</i> or <i>Napoleon</i> on branded products. It is common in the UK to only use two slices of pastry with a single, thick layer of filling between them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Canadian_cuisine" title="Canadian cuisine">Canada</a>, mille-feuille is often named <i>g√¢teaux Napol√©on</i> or <i>Napoleon Slice</i> (in English Canada). It is sold either with custard, whipped cream, or both, between three layers of puff pastry. Almond paste is the most common flavoured variety. There is a French Canadian way where the mille-feuille is done with graham crackers instead of puff pastry, and where pudding replaces the custard layer.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/South_African_cuisine" title="South African cuisine">South Africa</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zimbabwean_cuisine" title="Zimbabwean cuisine">Zimbabwe</a>, it is called a <i>custard slice</i>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Australian_cuisine" title="Australian cuisine">Australia</a> it is called a <i>custard slice</i> or a <i>vanilla slice</i>, using a gelein set cr√®me p√¢tissi√®re, and in many cases, <a href="/wiki/Passiflora_edulis" title="Passiflora edulis">passionfruit</a> icing. <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victorians</a> refer to them as <a href="/wiki/Dried_nasal_mucus" title="Dried nasal mucus">snot</a> blocks.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>In New Zealand it is variously known as a 'custard slice', a 'custard square', a 'vanilla slice', or, with passion-fruit icing, a 'passion-fruit slice'.</p>
<p>In the German speaking part of <a href="/wiki/Swiss_cuisine" title="Swiss cuisine">Switzerland</a> and also in <a href="/wiki/Austrian_cuisine" title="Austrian cuisine">Austria</a> it is called <i>Cr√®meschnitte</i>. In <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> it is known by a variation of that name, <i>kremshnit</i> (◊ß◊®◊û◊©◊†◊ô◊ò).</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Polish_cuisine" title="Polish cuisine">Poland</a>, the local variant of the pastry is called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Krem%C3%B3wka" title="Krem√≥wka">krem√≥wka</a></i>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Krem%C3%B3wka" title="Krem√≥wka"><i>napoleonka</i></a>. It consists of two layers of pastry separated by a thick cream layer. The whole pastry is then covered with powdered sugar. A similar local variety is called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Krempita" title="Krempita">krempita</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Serbian_cuisine" title="Serbian cuisine">Serbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Croatian_cuisine" title="Croatian cuisine">Croatia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina_cuisine" title="Bosnia and Herzegovina cuisine">Bosnia and Herzegovina</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kremna_rezina" title="Kremna rezina">kremna rezina</a></i> or <i>krem≈°nita</i> in <a href="/wiki/Slovenian_cuisine" title="Slovenian cuisine">Slovenia</a>, and <i>kr√©me≈°</i> in <a href="/wiki/Slovak_cuisine" title="Slovak cuisine">Slovakia</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_cuisine" title="Hungarian cuisine">Hungary</a> it is called <i>Kr√©mes</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> Its version <i>francia kr√©mes</i> (French Napoleon) is topped with whipped cream and caramel.</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Swedish_cuisine" title="Swedish cuisine">Sweden</a> as well as in Finland, the <i>Napoleonbakelse</i> (Napoleon pastry) is a mille-feuille filled with whipped cream, custard, and jam. The top of the pastry is glazed with icing and currant jelly. In <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, it is simply called <i>Napoleonskake</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Belgian_cuisine" title="Belgian cuisine">Belgium</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_cuisine" title="Dutch cuisine">Netherlands</a> the <i><a href="/wiki/Tompouce" title="Tompouce">tompouce</a></i> or <i>tompoes</i> is the equivalent pastry. Several variations exist in Belgium, but in the Netherlands it has achieved an almost iconic status and the market allows preciously little variation in form, size, ingredients and colour (always two layers of pastry, nearly always pink glazing, but orange around national festivities). The cartoon character <a href="/wiki/Tom_Puss" title="Tom Puss">Tom Puss</a> by <a href="/wiki/Marten_Toonder" title="Marten Toonder">Marten Toonder</a> is named after the tompouce.</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Greek_cuisine" title="Greek cuisine">Greece</a>, the pastry is called <i>ŒúŒπŒªœÜŒ≠ŒπŒ≥</i>, which is the transcription of the word mille-feuille using Greek letters. The filling between the layers is cream whereas Chantilly cream is used at the top of the pastry. It also has some resemblance to the traditional <a href="/wiki/Greek_cuisine" title="Greek cuisine">Greek</a> dish of <a href="/wiki/Galaktoboureko" title="Galaktoboureko">Galaktoboureko</a> (literally "milk <a href="/wiki/B%C3%B6rek" title="B√∂rek">b√∂rek</a>"), though with this dish, the custard is baked with the pastry, rather than added afterwards.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_cuisine" title="Spanish cuisine">Spanish</a> <a href="/wiki/Milhoja" title="Milhoja">milhojas</a>, the puff pastry is thin and crunchy. They are often far deeper than solely of three layers of the pastry, and reach up to .5 feet (0.15¬†m) tall.</p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_american" title="Latin american">latin american</a> <a href="/wiki/Milhoja" title="Milhoja">milhojas</a>, various layers of puff pastry are layered with <a href="/wiki/Dulce_de_leche" title="Dulce de leche">dulce de leche</a> and confectioner sugar on top. A Colombian version of milhoja has dulce de leche, melted <i><a href="/wiki/Guava_jelly" title="Guava jelly">bocadillo</a></i> between the layers, topped with whipped cream and coconut flakes.</p>

<p>in <a href="/wiki/Philippine_cuisine" title="Philippine cuisine">Philippines</a>, it is called <i>Napoleones</i> (pronunciation: na-pol-yo-nes); and is composed of 2 layers, with pastry cream or custard as filling, topped with sugar glaze. It is a popular specialty in <a href="/wiki/Negros_Occidental" title="Negros Occidental">Negros Occidental</a> and can be bought as <a href="/wiki/Pasalubong" title="Pasalubong">pasalubong</a> by many who visit the province.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Iranian_cuisine" title="Iranian cuisine">Iran</a>, the pastry is called "ÿ¥Ÿäÿ±ŸäŸÜŸâ ŸÜÿßŸæŸÑÿ¶ŸàŸÜŸâ" (<i>Shirini-e N√¢pel'oni</i>, literally "Napoleonic Sweet Pastry"). It consists of thin puff pastry and often topped with powdered sugar.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Moroccan_cuisine" title="Moroccan cuisine">Morocco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algerian_cuisine" title="Algerian cuisine">Algeria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tunisian_cuisine" title="Tunisian cuisine">Tunisia</a>, it is consumed regularly. It is called <i>mille-feuille</i> also. In northern Morocco they call it <i>Milfa</i> which is a combination/portmanteau of the words mille and feuille.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Competitions">Competitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mille-feuille&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Competitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An annual competition for the best vanilla slice baker is the <i>Great Australian Vanilla Slice Triumph</i> held in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ouyen,_Victoria" title="Ouyen, Victoria">Ouyen</a> in western <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>.
