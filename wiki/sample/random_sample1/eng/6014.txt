<p><b>Bagamoyo</b>, <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>, is a town founded at the end of the 18th century. It was (also spelled <i>Bagamojo</i>) the original capital of <a href="/wiki/German_East_Africa" title="German East Africa">German East Africa</a> and was one of the most important trading ports along the <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East African</a> coast. Now, the town has about 30,000 inhabitants and is the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Bagamoyo_District" title="Bagamoyo District">Bagamoyo District</a>, which was recently considered as a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bagamoyo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bagamoyo is located at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Bagamoyo&amp;params=6_26_S_38_54_E_region:TZ_type:city(30,000)"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">6°26′S</span> <span class="longitude">38°54′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">6.433°S 38.900°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-6.433; 38.900</span></span></span></a></span>. It lies 75 kilometres (47 miles) north of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dar-es-Salaam" title="Dar-es-Salaam">Dar-es-Salaam</a> on the coast of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, close to the island of <a href="/wiki/Zanzibar" title="Zanzibar">Zanzibar</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bagamoyo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bagamoyo was the most important trading <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entrepot" title="Entrepot">entrepot</a> of the east central coast of Africa in the late 19th century. Bagamoyo's history has been influenced by Indian and Arab traders, by the German colonial government and by Christian missionaries. About 5 km (3 mi) south of Bagamoyo, the <a href="/wiki/Kaole" title="Kaole">Kaole</a> Ruins with remnants of two <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">mosques</a> and a couple of <a href="/wiki/Tomb" title="Tomb">tombs</a> can be dated back to the 13th century, showing the importance of Islam in those early Bagamoyo times. Until the middle of the 18th century, Bagamoyo was a small and insignificant trading center where most of the population were fishermen and farmers. The main trading goods were fish, salt, and gum, among other things.</p>
<p>In the late 18th century Muslim families settled in Bagamoyo, all of which were relatives of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shamvi_la_Magimba&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shamvi la Magimba (page does not exist)">Shamvi la Magimba</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a>. They made their living by enforcing taxes on the native population and by trading in salt, gathered from the <a href="/wiki/Nunge" title="Nunge">Nunge</a> coast north of Bagamoyo. In the first half of the 19th century, Bagamoyo became a trading port for <a href="/wiki/Ivory_trade" title="Ivory trade">ivory</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave_trade" title="Slave trade">slave trade</a>, with traders coming from the African interior, from places as far as <a href="/wiki/Morogoro" title="Morogoro">Morogoro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tanganyika" title="Lake Tanganyika">Lake Tanganyika</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Usambara" title="Usambara">Usambara</a> on their way to <a href="/wiki/Zanzibar" title="Zanzibar">Zanzibar</a>. This explains the meaning of the word Bagamoyo ("Bwaga-Moyo") which means "Lay down your Heart" in <a href="/wiki/Swahili_language" title="Swahili language">Swahili</a>. It is disputed whether this refers to the slave trade which passed through the town (i.e. "give up all hope") or to the porters who rested in Bagamoyo after carrying 35 lb cargos on their shoulders from the Great Lakes region (i.e. "take the load off and rest"). Since there is little evidence to support that Bagamoyo was a major slave port (Kilwa, much further south, has earned this status<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>), and that tens of thousands of porters arrived at Bagamoyo annually in the latter half of the 19th century, it is more likely that the name of the town derives from the latter interpretation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Synthesis_of_published_material" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag may be based upon an improper synthesis of sources. (February 2014)">improper synthesis?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>The slave trade in East Africa was officially prohibited in the year 1873, but continued surreptitiously to the end of the 19th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1868, Bagamoyo local rulers, known as majumbe, presented the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fathers_of_the_Holy_Ghost" title="Fathers of the Holy Ghost">Fathers of the Holy Ghost</a>" with land for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mission_(Christian)" title="Mission (Christian)">mission</a> north of the town, the first mission in East Africa. This caused resistance by the native <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zaramo" title="Zaramo">Zaramo</a> people which was mediated by representatives of Sultan Majid and, after 1870, by Sultan <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barghash" title="Barghash">Barghash</a>. Originally the mission was intended to house children who were rescued from slavery, but it soon expanded to a church, a school, and some workshops and farming projects.</p>
<p>But Bagamoyo was not only a trade centre for ivory and <a href="/wiki/Copra" title="Copra">copra</a>; it was also a starting point for renowned European explorers. From Bagamoyo they moved out to find the source of the River Nile and explored the African inner lakes. Some of these were <a href="/wiki/Richard_Francis_Burton" title="Richard Francis Burton">Richard Francis Burton</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Hanning_Speke" title="John Hanning Speke">John Hanning Speke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Morton_Stanley" title="Henry Morton Stanley">Henry Morton Stanley</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stanley_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stanley-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:55</sup> and <a href="/wiki/James_Augustus_Grant" title="James Augustus Grant">James Augustus Grant</a>. Although often believed so, <a href="/wiki/David_Livingstone" title="David Livingstone">David Livingstone</a> had never been to Bagamoyo in his lifetime. Only after his death he was laid out in the Old Church's tower (nowadays named Livingston Tower) to wait for the high tide to come in and ship his body to Zanzibar.</p>
<p>Bagamoyo was the first capital of Tanzania while serving as the German headquarters of <a href="/wiki/German_East_Africa" title="German East Africa">German East Africa</a> (first under the auspices of the German East African Company and then the German Imperial Government) between 1886-1891. <a href="/wiki/Dar_es_Salaam" title="Dar es Salaam">Dar es Salaam</a> became the new capital of the colony in 1891. The town was apparently the (1895) birthplace of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS-Oberf%C3%BChrer" title="SS-Oberführer">SS-Oberführer</a> <a href="/wiki/Julian_Scherner" title="Julian Scherner">Julian Scherner</a>. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, on August 15, 1916, a British air attack and naval bombardment was launched on Bagamoyo, the Germans were overrun and the German garrison taken. When the German Empire decided to build a railway from Dar es Salaam into the interior in 1905, Bagamoyo's importance began to decline.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Today">Today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bagamoyo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Today, Bagamoyo is a centre for <a href="/wiki/Dhow" title="Dhow">dhow</a> sailboat building. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Department_of_Antiquities&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Department of Antiquities (page does not exist)">Department of Antiquities</a> in Tanzania is working to maintain the ruins of the colonial era in and around Bagamoyo and to revitalize the town. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bagamoyo_College_of_Arts" title="Bagamoyo College of Arts">Bagamoyo College of Arts</a> (“Chuo cha Sanaa”) has been an internationally famous arts college in Tanzania, teaching traditional Tanzanian painting, sculpture, drama, dancing and drumming. In 2007 based on the college, the <a href="/wiki/Bagamoyo_Arts_and_Cultural_Institute" title="Bagamoyo Arts and Cultural Institute">Bagamoyo Arts and Cultural Institute</a> (TaSUBa) was established.</p>
<p>Because of its varied history and proximity to Dar es Salaam, Bagamoyo is more diverse than some other areas of the country. Bagamoyo is home to many ethnic groups, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wakwere" title="Wakwere">Wakwere</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wazaramo" title="Wazaramo">Wazaramo</a>, Wazigua, <a href="/wiki/Maasai_people" title="Maasai people">Maasai</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waswahili" title="Waswahili">Waswahili</a>. Although Swahili culture dominates, many different cultures coexist in Bagamoyo, including people of Arab descent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
