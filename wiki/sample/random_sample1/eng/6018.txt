<p><b>Baghlan</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pashto_language" title="Pashto language">Pashto</a>/<a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">بغلان</span>‎‎ <i>Baġlān</i>) is one of the thirty-four <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Afghanistan" title="Provinces of Afghanistan">provinces of Afghanistan</a>. It is in the north of the country. As of 2013, the province has a population of about 910,700.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cso_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-cso-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Its capital is <a href="/wiki/Puli_Khumri" title="Puli Khumri">Puli Khumri</a> (Persian:پل خمری), but its name comes from the other major town in the province, <a href="/wiki/Baghlan" title="Baghlan">Baghlan</a>. The ruins of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoroastrian" title="Zoroastrian">Zoroastrian</a> <a href="/wiki/Fire_temple" title="Fire temple">fire temple</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Surkh_Kotal" title="Surkh Kotal">Surkh Kotal</a>, are located in Baghlan. The lead nation of the local <a href="/wiki/Provincial_Reconstruction_Team" title="Provincial Reconstruction Team">Provincial Reconstruction Team</a> (PRT) was <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, which operated from 2006 to 2015.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baghlan_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The name Baghlan is derived from <i>Bagolango</i> or "image-temple", inscribed on the temple of <a href="/wiki/Surkh_Kotal" title="Surkh Kotal">Surkh Kotal</a> during the reign of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kushan" title="Kushan">Kushan</a> emperor, <a href="/wiki/Kanishka" title="Kanishka">Kanishka</a> in the early 2nd century CE. The Chinese Buddhist monk <a href="/wiki/Xuanzang" title="Xuanzang">Xuanzang</a> traveled through Baghlan in the mid-7th Century CE, and referred to it as the "kingdom of <i>Fo-kia-lang</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 13th Century CE, a permanent garrison of Mongol troops was quartered in the Kunduz-Baghlan area, and in 1253 fell under the jurisdiction of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sali_Noyan_Tatar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sali Noyan Tatar (page does not exist)">Sali Noyan Tatar</a>, appointed there by <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6ngke_Khan" title="Möngke Khan">Möngke Khan</a>. Sali Noyan's position was later inherited by his son Uladu, and grandson Baktut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> These Turco-Mongol garrison troops (<i>tamma</i>) formed the <a href="/wiki/Qara%27unas" title="Qara'unas">Qara'unas</a> faction, and by the 14th Century had allied with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chaghataite_Khanate" title="Chaghataite Khanate">Chaghataite Khanate</a>. Under the rule of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timur_Lenk" title="Timur Lenk">Temür</a> the Qara'unas were given to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chek%C3%BC_Barlas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chekü Barlas (page does not exist)">Chekü Barlas</a>, and then to his son Jahānshāh. Forbes Manz notes that these Kunduz-Baghlan forces appear to have remained cohesive and influential throughout the Timurid period, though under different leaders and different names, up until the Uzbek invasion.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (December 2011)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-https_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-https-4">[4]</a></sup> By the Islamic year 900 (1494-1495 CE), the area was noted in the <i><a href="/wiki/Baburnama" title="Baburnama">Baburnama</a></i> as ruled by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qipchaq" title="Qipchaq">Qipchaq</a> emir.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-https_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-https-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baghlan_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the mid-20th Century, as Afghanistan became the target of international development from both the Western and Soviet world, agricultural-industrial projects were initiated in Baghlan. These included factories for the production of sugar from sugar beets (initiated by Czech experts in the 1940s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>) and for vegetable oil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Czech expertise also figured heavily into the development of Baghlans' coal-mining industry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> centred at Baghlan's <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Karkar_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Karkar Valley (page does not exist)">Karkar Valley</a>, the only coal mine in Afghanistan to remain operational up through 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The modern Baghlan Province was created out of the former <a href="/wiki/Qataghan_Province" title="Qataghan Province">Qataghan Province</a> in 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iranica_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Iranica-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Soviet%E2%80%93Afghan_War" title="Soviet–Afghan War">Soviet–Afghan War</a>, the Soviets in 1982 established the Kayan military zone in southern Baghlan. The area was defended by 10,000 Ismaili militiamen, increasing to 18,000 by 1992, who sided with the Soviets due to differences with the Islamist opposition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BhatiaSedra2008_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-BhatiaSedra2008-10">[10]</a></sup> Afghan Ismailis overall were inclined to support the Communists, though a local Ismaili leader, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sayed_Manuchehr&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sayed Manuchehr (page does not exist)">Sayed Manuchehr</a>, lead a partisan movement against the Communists until Ismaili leader <a href="/wiki/Sayed_Mansur_Naderi" title="Sayed Mansur Naderi">Sayed Mansur Naderi</a> accepted Soviet support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Large portions of Baghlan and neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Samangan_Province" title="Samangan Province">Samangan Province</a> were under the sway of the Soviet-aligned Naderi clan, the hereditary Ismaili <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sayed_of_Kayan" title="Sayed of Kayan">Sayeds (spiritual leaders) of Kayan</a>. Under their jurisdiction, was largely quiet and societally functional throughout the 1980s, with hospitals, schools, and administrative services, funded by the communist central government. Despite the Naderi's alliance with the Communists, they also maintained positive relations with the Mujahideen as well, permitting them to move through the area provided they refrained from attacks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the Soviets' three primary bases in Afghanistan, <a href="/wiki/Kiligai" title="Kiligai">Kiligai</a>, was located in Baghlan Province, and served as the "largest military supply and armoury centre of the Soviet troops in Afghanistan."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_history">Recent history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baghlan_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Recent history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2001_Afghan_War" title="2001 Afghan War">2001 Afghan War</a> commenced, Ismaili spiritual leader <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sayed_Mansoor_Naderi" title="Sayed Mansoor Naderi">Sayed Mansoor Naderi</a> attempted to retake Baghlan from the Taliban. Naderi was aligned with <a href="/wiki/Uzbeks" title="Uzbeks">Uzbek</a> warlord <a href="/wiki/Abdul_Rashid_Dostum" title="Abdul Rashid Dostum">Abdul Rashid Dostum</a> and his <a href="/wiki/National_Islamic_Movement_of_Afghanistan" title="National Islamic Movement of Afghanistan">Jumbesh-e Milli</a> party, and the competing <a href="/wiki/Tajiks" title="Tajiks">Tajik</a>-dominated <a href="/wiki/Jamiat-e_Islami" title="Jamiat-e Islami">Jamiat-e Islami</a> party was also keen to seize control of Baghlan as Taliban power eroded. The Jamiat were able to seize the capital of Pul-i Khumri before Naderi, who despite his strong backing among the Afghan <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ismailism" title="Ismailism">Ismailis</a> and Shia <a href="/wiki/Hazaras" title="Hazaras">Hazaras</a>, was unable to rally enough supporters to control the province. Naderi failed to retake the capital in 2001 and 2003, in the latter event he was forced by the dominant <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Andarabi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Andarabi (page does not exist)">Andarabi</a> militias to fall back to the Ismaili bastion of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kayan_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kayan Valley (page does not exist)">Kayan Valley</a>, and then to flee the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 13 June 2012, <a href="/wiki/2012_Afghanistan_earthquakes" title="2012 Afghanistan earthquakes">two earthquakes</a> hit Afghanistan and there was a major landslide in Burka District of Baghlan Province. The village of Sayi Hazara was buried under up to 30 meters of rock, killing an estimated 71 people.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics_and_governance">Politics and governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baghlan_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Politics and governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Afghanistan" title="Politics of Afghanistan">Politics of Afghanistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_governors_of_Baghlan" title="List of governors of Baghlan">List of governors of Baghlan</a></div>
<p>The current <a href="/wiki/Governor" title="Governor">governor</a> of the province is <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sultan_Mohammad_Ebadi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sultan Mohammad Ebadi (page does not exist)">Sultan Mohammad Ebadi</a>. The town of <a href="/wiki/Puli_Khumri" title="Puli Khumri">Puli Khumri</a> serves as the capital of the province. All <a href="/wiki/Law_enforcement_in_Afghanistan" title="Law enforcement in Afghanistan">law enforcement</a> activities throughout the province are handled by the <a href="/wiki/Afghan_National_Police" title="Afghan National Police">Afghan National Police</a> (ANP). The provincial police chief represents the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_the_Interior_(Afghanistan)" title="Ministry of the Interior (Afghanistan)">Ministry of the Interior</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kabul" title="Kabul">Kabul</a>. The ANP is backed by other <a href="/wiki/Afghan_National_Security_Forces" title="Afghan National Security Forces">Afghan National Security Forces</a> (ANSF), including the NATO-led forces.</p>

<p>The population of Baghlan province was reported at 863,700 in the year 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cso_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-cso-1">[1]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tajik_people" title="Tajik people">Tajiks</a> make up 55% of the population, followed by 20% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pashtun_people" title="Pashtun people">Pashtuns</a>, 15% <a href="/wiki/Hazaras" title="Hazaras">Hazaras</a>, 9% <a href="/wiki/Uzbeks" title="Uzbeks">Uzbeks</a>, and the remainder are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tatar" title="Tatar">Tatar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In another source Tajiks along their sub-groups like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aimaks" title="Aimaks">Aimaks</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Sayyid" title="Sayyid">Sayyid</a></i>-Tajiks make more than 70% of the provincial population. In addition, a significant number of Hazaras are also counted as part of the Persian-speaking people which stating Persian language as overwhelming speaking language, followed by Pashhtu-speaking Pashtuns, Uzbeks and some Tatars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Baghlan is also home to a small community of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ismaili" title="Ismaili">Ismaili</a> Muslims led by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sayeds_of_Kayan" title="Sayeds of Kayan">Sayeds of Kayan</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Healthcare">Healthcare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baghlan_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Healthcare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Health_in_Afghanistan" title="Health in Afghanistan">Health in Afghanistan</a></div>
<p>The percentage of households with clean drinking water increased from 19% in 2005 to 25% in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cimicweb.org_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-cimicweb.org-17">[17]</a></sup> The percentage of births attended to by a skilled birth attendant increased from 5.5% in 2005 to 22% in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cimicweb.org_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-cimicweb.org-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baghlan_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Afghanistan" title="Education in Afghanistan">Education in Afghanistan</a></div>
<p>The overall literacy rate (6+ years of age) increased from 21% in 2005 to 24% in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cimicweb.org_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-cimicweb.org-17">[17]</a></sup> The overall net enrolment rate (6–13 years of age) increased from 29% in 2005 to 62% in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cimicweb.org_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-cimicweb.org-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baghlan_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Afghanistan" title="Economy of Afghanistan">Economy of Afghanistan</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baghlan_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Baghlan's primary crops (as of 1974) were cotton and sugar beets, industrial sugar production having begun under Czech supervision in the 1940s. The area also produced grapes, pistachios, and pommegranates. The primary livestock are <a href="/wiki/Karakul_sheep" title="Karakul sheep">Karakul sheep</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.com_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_products">Other products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baghlan_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The province also produces silk, and coal is mined in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Karkar_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Karkar Valley (page does not exist)">Karkar Valley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.com_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
