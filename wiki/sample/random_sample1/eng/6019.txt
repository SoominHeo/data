<p><b>Bananarama</b> are a British female <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop music</a> vocal group formed in London in 1979 by friends <a href="/wiki/Sara_Dallin" title="Sara Dallin">Sara Dallin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siobhan_Fahey" title="Siobhan Fahey">Siobhan Fahey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Keren_Woodward" title="Keren Woodward">Keren Woodward</a>. Their success on both pop and dance charts have earned them a listing in <i>The Guinness Book of Records</i> as the all-female group with the most chart entries in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The group's 10 UK Top 10 hits include "<a href="/wiki/T%27ain%27t_What_You_Do_(It%27s_the_Way_That_You_Do_It)" title="T'ain't What You Do (It's the Way That You Do It)">It Ain't What You Do...</a>" (1982), "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Really_Saying_Something" title="Really Saying Something">Really Saying Something</a>" (1982), "<a href="/wiki/Shy_Boy" title="Shy Boy">Shy Boy</a>" (1982), "<a href="/wiki/Na_Na_Hey_Hey_Kiss_Him_Goodbye" title="Na Na Hey Hey Kiss Him Goodbye">Na Na Hey Hey Kiss Him Goodbye</a>" (1983), "<a href="/wiki/Cruel_Summer_(song)" title="Cruel Summer (song)">Cruel Summer</a>" (1983), "<a href="/wiki/Robert_De_Niro%27s_Waiting..." title="Robert De Niro's Waiting...">Robert De Niro's Waiting...</a>" (1984) and "<a href="/wiki/Love_in_the_First_Degree_(Bananarama_song)" title="Love in the First Degree (Bananarama song)">Love in the First Degree</a>" (1987). In 1986, they had a US number one with another of their UK Top 10 hits, "<a href="/wiki/Venus_(Shocking_Blue_song)" title="Venus (Shocking Blue song)">Venus</a>". In total, they had 11 singles reach the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">Billboard Hot 100</a> (1983-1988), including two more in the top 10, with "Cruel Summer" (1984) and "<a href="/wiki/I_Heard_a_Rumour" title="I Heard a Rumour">I Heard a Rumour</a>" (1987). They are associated with the MTV-driven <a href="/wiki/Second_British_Invasion" title="Second British Invasion">Second British Invasion</a> of the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scripps-Howard_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scripps-Howard-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1988, "Love in the First Degree" earned them a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brit_Award" title="Brit Award">Brit Award</a> nomination for Best British Single,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> while their fourth album <i><a href="/wiki/Wow!_(Bananarama_album)" title="Wow! (Bananarama album)">Wow!</a></i> (1987), topped the Australian <a href="/wiki/ARIA_Charts" title="ARIA Charts">ARIA</a> albums chart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auswow_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-auswow-4">[4]</a></sup> Their 1988 music video for the group's hit cover of <a href="/wiki/The_Supremes" title="The Supremes">The Supremes</a> 1971 smash single "<a href="/wiki/Nathan_Jones_(song)" title="Nathan Jones (song)">Nathan Jones</a>" earned them another Brit Award nomination.</p>
<p>Following Fahey's departure from the group in 1988, <a href="/wiki/Jacquie_O%27Sullivan" title="Jacquie O'Sullivan">Jacquie O'Sullivan</a> joined the group for three years and they had UK Top 10 hits with "<a href="/wiki/I_Want_You_Back_(Bananarama_song)" title="I Want You Back (Bananarama song)">I Want You Back</a>" (1988) and "<a href="/wiki/Help!_(song)" title="Help! (song)">Help!</a>" (1989). In addition to their chart success, the band is also known for their vocal style, generally singing in <a href="/wiki/Unison" title="Unison">unison</a> rather than in harmony like most vocal groups. Since 1992, Woodward and Dallin have continued Bananarama as a duo, with hits including "<a href="/wiki/Movin%27_On_(Bananarama_song)" title="Movin' On (Bananarama song)">Movin' On</a>" (1992) and "<a href="/wiki/More,_More,_More" title="More, More, More">More, More, More</a>" (1993). They returned to the UK Top 20 for the first time since 1991 with the 2005 single "<a href="/wiki/Move_in_My_Direction" title="Move in My Direction">Move in My Direction</a>". Between 1982 and 2009, they had 28 singles reach the Top 50 of the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a>. In December 2016, <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard magazine</a></i> ranked them the 94th most successful dance artist of all-time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bananarama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bananarama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bananarama were founded in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> in 1979 by Fahey, Woodward and Dallin, the latter two having been childhood friends in <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a> since the age of four, and attending St. George's School for Girls together. The pair became a trio when Dallin met Fahey while studying fashion journalism in London. They became friends because they both dressed more radically than the other students. The trio were ardent followers of the <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Post-punk" title="Post-punk">post-punk</a> music scene during the late 1970s and early 1980s and often performed impromptu sets or backing vocals at gigs for such bands as <a href="/wiki/The_Monochrome_Set" title="The Monochrome Set">The Monochrome Set</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iggy_Pop" title="Iggy Pop">Iggy Pop</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Jam" title="The Jam">The Jam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Department_S_(band)" title="Department S (band)">Department S</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Nipple_Erectors" title="The Nipple Erectors">The Nipple Erectors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1981, Bananarama's members were living above the rehearsal room that was used by former <a href="/wiki/Sex_Pistols" title="Sex Pistols">Sex Pistols</a> members <a href="/wiki/Steve_Jones_(musician)" title="Steve Jones (musician)">Steve Jones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Cook" title="Paul Cook">Paul Cook</a>. With their help, Bananarama recorded their first <a href="/wiki/Demo_(music)" title="Demo (music)">demo</a> "<a href="/wiki/Aie_a_Mwana" title="Aie a Mwana">Aie a Mwana</a>" (UK #92, a cover of a song by Black Blood, sung in <a href="/wiki/Swahili_language" title="Swahili language">Swahili</a>). The demo was heard at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demon_Records" title="Demon Records">Demon Records</a>, who consequently offered Bananarama their first deal. The song was an underground hit and Bananarama were signed by <a href="/wiki/Decca_Records" title="Decca Records">Decca</a> (later <a href="/wiki/London_Records" title="London Records">London Records</a>) and remained on the label until 1993. Bananarama's only previous experience in a recording studio at the time was as background vocalists on the <a href="/wiki/Department_S_(band)" title="Department S (band)">Department S</a> B-side, "<a href="/wiki/Solid_Gold_Easy_Action" title="Solid Gold Easy Action">Solid Gold Easy Action</a>," a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T.Rex_(band)" title="T.Rex (band)">T.Rex</a> cover in early 1981.</p>
<p>During this early period Bananarama were approached by <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_McLaren" title="Malcolm McLaren">Malcolm McLaren</a>, who offered to manage the group. McLaren, manager of the <a href="/wiki/Sex_Pistols" title="Sex Pistols">Sex Pistols</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bow_Wow_Wow" title="Bow Wow Wow">Bow Wow Wow</a>, and notorious for generating scandal, proposed some new material that was sexually suggestive, and did not fit with what at the time was the band's tomboyish and straightforward image. Bananarama passed on both the material and McLaren as their manager.</p>
<p>UK fashion magazine <i><a href="/wiki/The_Face_(magazine)" title="The Face (magazine)">The Face</a></i> featured an article on Bananarama after the release of their first single. This caught the attention of ex-<a href="/wiki/The_Specials" title="The Specials">Specials</a> member <a href="/wiki/Terry_Hall_(singer)" title="Terry Hall (singer)">Terry Hall</a>, who invited them to collaborate with his new vocal group <a href="/wiki/Fun_Boy_Three" title="Fun Boy Three">Fun Boy Three</a> on the track "<a href="/wiki/T%27ain%27t_What_You_Do_(It%27s_the_Way_That_You_Do_It)" title="T'ain't What You Do (It's the Way That You Do It)">It Ain't What You Do It's The Way That You Do It</a>". In 1982, the song hit the Top 5 in the UK and gave Bananarama their first significant mainstream success. Fun Boy Three then guest starred on Bananarama's single, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Really_Saying_Something" title="Really Saying Something">Really Saying Something</a>", later that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1982.E2.80.931985:_Deep_Sea_Skiving_and_Bananarama">1982–1985: <i>Deep Sea Skiving</i> and <i>Bananarama</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bananarama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1982–1985: Deep Sea Skiving and Bananarama">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bananarama experienced their greatest success during the period 1982 to 1989, with their first three albums primarily produced and co-written with <a href="/wiki/Jolley_%26_Swain" title="Jolley &amp; Swain">Jolley &amp; Swain</a>. Their debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Deep_Sea_Skiving" title="Deep Sea Skiving">Deep Sea Skiving</a></i> (UK #7, US #63) (1983) contained several hit singles — "Really Saying Something" (UK #5) and "<a href="/wiki/Shy_Boy" title="Shy Boy">Shy Boy</a>" (UK #4) — and included a <a href="/wiki/Cover_version" title="Cover version">cover version</a> of "<a href="/wiki/Na_Na_Hey_Hey_Kiss_Him_Goodbye" title="Na Na Hey Hey Kiss Him Goodbye">Na Na Hey Hey Kiss Him Goodbye</a>" (UK #5). The band recorded a version of the Sex Pistols' song "No Feelings" in late 1982 for the soundtrack of the British film, <i>Party Party</i>.</p>
<p>Although the group was popular in their native UK, their earliest success in America was on a cult/underground basis due to college radio and early MTV exposure. During 1982 and 1983, Bananarama did several promotional US press tours and TV appearances on <i><a href="/wiki/American_Bandstand" title="American Bandstand">American Bandstand</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Solid_Gold_(TV_series)" title="Solid Gold (TV series)">Solid Gold</a></i>. Success in the United States eluded the group until the release of their first top 10 hit "<a href="/wiki/Cruel_Summer_(song)" title="Cruel Summer (song)">Cruel Summer</a>" in mid-1984.</p>
<p>Their second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Bananarama_(album)" title="Bananarama (album)">Bananarama</a></i> (UK #16, US #30) (1984) was a more socially conscious effort. The group wanted to be taken more seriously, so wrote songs that focused on heavier topics: "<a href="/wiki/Hot_Line_to_Heaven" title="Hot Line to Heaven">Hotline To Heaven</a>" (UK#58) is a stand against the drugs-are-cool culture; and "<a href="/wiki/Rough_Justice_(Bananarama_song)" title="Rough Justice (Bananarama song)">Rough Justice</a>" (UK#23) deals with social apathy. The album contained the hit singles, "<a href="/wiki/Robert_De_Niro%27s_Waiting..." title="Robert De Niro's Waiting...">Robert De Niro's Waiting...</a>," (UK#3) and their first US Top 10 hit, "Cruel Summer" (UK#8, US#9) (1983), which was included in the film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Karate_Kid_(1984_film)" title="The Karate Kid (1984 film)">The Karate Kid</a>.</i> The trio also recorded the single, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Wild_Life_(Bananarama_song)" title="The Wild Life (Bananarama song)">The Wild Life</a>" (US#70) for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Wild_Life_(film)" title="The Wild Life (film)">1984 American film of the same name</a>.</p>
<p>Bananarama featured on the <a href="/wiki/Band_Aid_(band)" title="Band Aid (band)">Band Aid</a> single, "<a href="/wiki/Do_They_Know_It%27s_Christmas%3F" title="Do They Know It's Christmas?">Do They Know It's Christmas?</a>," and were the only artists to appear on both the original 1984 Band Aid and the 1989 Band Aid II versions (though Fahey only appeared on the 1984 version).</p>
<p>1985 would be a quiet transitional year for Bananarama. London Records release of "<a href="/wiki/Do_Not_Disturb_(song)" title="Do Not Disturb (song)">Do Not Disturb</a>" (UK #31) would keep the girls' public profile alive.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1986.E2.80.931987:_True_Confessions_and_international_success">1986–1987: <i>True Confessions</i> and international success</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bananarama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1986–1987: True Confessions and international success">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>1986 saw the release of their third album, <i><a href="/wiki/True_Confessions_(album)" title="True Confessions (album)">True Confessions</a></i> (UK #46, US #15) on which some production duties were taken up by <a href="/wiki/Mike_Stock_(musician)" title="Mike Stock (musician)">Mike Stock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matt_Aitken" title="Matt Aitken">Matt Aitken</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pete_Waterman" title="Pete Waterman">Pete Waterman</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Stock_Aitken_Waterman" title="Stock Aitken Waterman">Stock Aitken Waterman</a> (SAW). The move resulted in the international number one hit, "<a href="/wiki/Venus_(Shocking_Blue_song)" title="Venus (Shocking Blue song)">Venus</a>," (a remake of <a href="/wiki/Shocking_Blue" title="Shocking Blue">Shocking Blue</a>'s number one hit from 1970). The dance-oriented beats on the song typified the SAW approach to pop production. Bananarama were said to have been put in contact with SAW after hearing and expressing a fondness for "<a href="/wiki/You_Spin_Me_Round_(Like_a_Record)" title="You Spin Me Round (Like a Record)">You Spin Me Round (Like a Record)</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Dead_or_Alive_(band)" title="Dead or Alive (band)">Dead or Alive</a>. After its worldwide success, "Venus" has become the group's signature song. Also in 1986, Dallin and Woodward were featured as backing vocalists on two songs on <i>Family Album,</i> produced by <a href="/wiki/John_Lydon" title="John Lydon">John Lydon</a>.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a> for "Venus" received heavy airplay on <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a> in the United States. It featured the group in various costumes including a <a href="/wiki/Devil" title="Devil">devil</a>, a French temptress, a <a href="/wiki/Vampire" title="Vampire">vampire</a> and a Greek goddess. The video marked a pivotal shift towards a more glamorous and sexual image, which contrasted with the tomboyish style of their earlier work. It was also the start of their use of toy-boys as backup dancers, which would become a hallmark of their videos.</p>
<p>Follow-up singles "<a href="/wiki/More_Than_Physical" title="More Than Physical">More Than Physical</a>" (UK #41) and "<a href="/wiki/A_Trick_of_the_Night" title="A Trick of the Night">A Trick of the Night</a>" (UK #32) were less successful due to minimal promotions. Woodward was pregnant with her son Thomas and unable to tour or physically promote the parent album or its subsequent singles.</p>
<p>During a press tour in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, the group also recorded a song "Riskin' a Romance" featured in the film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Secret_of_My_Success_(1987_film)" title="The Secret of My Success (1987 film)">The Secret of My Success</a></i>. The track was notable because it featured Fahey taking lead vocals, and <a href="/wiki/Daryl_Hall" title="Daryl Hall">Daryl Hall</a> was the producer. At some point during this trip, the group also re-recorded the vocals for their next UK release "More Than Physical" and "A Trick of the Night".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1987.E2.80.931988:_Wow.21_and_Fahey.27s_departure">1987–1988: <i>Wow!</i> and Fahey's departure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bananarama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1987–1988: Wow! and Fahey's departure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the wake of the success of "Venus," all production and co-writing responsibilities for their fourth album, 1987's <i><a href="/wiki/Wow!_(Bananarama_album)" title="Wow! (Bananarama album)">Wow!</a>,</i> (UK #26, US #44) were transferred from Jolley &amp; Swain to SAW, under whom the group's sound graduated towards dance-oriented <a href="/wiki/Europop" title="Europop">Europop</a>. <i>Wow!</i> topped the Australian <a href="/wiki/ARIA_Charts" title="ARIA Charts">ARIA</a> albums charts for one week in June 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auswow_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-auswow-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/I_Heard_a_Rumour" title="I Heard a Rumour">I Heard a Rumour</a>" (UK #14, US #4) was their strongest performing international hit from this album. "<a href="/wiki/Love_in_the_First_Degree_(Bananarama_song)" title="Love in the First Degree (Bananarama song)">Love in the First Degree</a>" (UK #3) was one of their biggest UK hits, and the <a href="/wiki/Disco" title="Disco">disco</a>-oriented "<a href="/wiki/I_Can%27t_Help_It_(Bananarama_song)" title="I Can't Help It (Bananarama song)">I Can't Help It</a>" (which boasted a semi-controversial video featuring the group in a milk bath filled with fruit and half-naked men) was a minor hit (UK #20).</p>
<p>By the time the third single from <i>Wow!</i> was released in early 1988, Fahey — who had married <a href="/wiki/Eurythmics" title="Eurythmics">Eurythmics</a>' <a href="/wiki/David_A._Stewart" title="David A. Stewart">Dave Stewart</a> — left the group as she had become disillusioned with the direction the band was taking. She was also feeling socially excluded by the other two members, who had 'been best friends a lot longer', and who, she says, were 'no longer inviting her out with them'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Her last performance as a member of the group was at the <a href="/wiki/Brit_Awards" title="Brit Awards">Brit Awards</a> in February 1988, performing "Love in the First Degree". She would later resurface in the BRIT Award-winning pop duo <a href="/wiki/Shakespears_Sister" title="Shakespears Sister">Shakespears Sister</a> with <a href="/wiki/Marcella_Detroit" title="Marcella Detroit">Marcella Detroit</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1988.E2.80.931991:_Second_line-up.2C_Greatest_Hits.2C_Pop_Life_and_world_tour">1988–1991: Second line-up, <i>Greatest Hits</i>, <i>Pop Life</i> and world tour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bananarama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1988–1991: Second line-up, Greatest Hits, Pop Life and world tour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After Fahey's exit, <a href="/wiki/Jacquie_O%27Sullivan" title="Jacquie O'Sullivan">Jacquie O'Sullivan</a> (formerly of the <a href="/wiki/Shillelagh_Sisters" title="Shillelagh Sisters">Shillelagh Sisters</a>) joined the group in March 1988. The single "<a href="/wiki/I_Want_You_Back_(Bananarama_song)" title="I Want You Back (Bananarama song)">I Want You Back</a>" (UK #5) was re-recorded with O'Sullivan, as was <a href="/wiki/The_Supremes" title="The Supremes">The Supremes</a> cover "<a href="/wiki/Nathan_Jones_(song)" title="Nathan Jones (song)">Nathan Jones</a>" (UK #15). "<a href="/wiki/Love,_Truth_and_Honesty" title="Love, Truth and Honesty">Love, Truth and Honesty</a>" (UK #23) was released as a single from their 1988 retrospective compilation, <i><a href="/wiki/Greatest_Hits_Collection_(Bananarama_album)" title="Greatest Hits Collection (Bananarama album)">Greatest Hits Collection</a></i> (UK #3). At the same time, Bananarama entered the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinness_Book_of_World_Records" title="Guinness Book of World Records">Guinness Book of World Records</a></i> as the all-female group who have the most chart entries in history, a record they still hold.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>As a fund-raising charity single for <a href="/wiki/Comic_Relief" title="Comic Relief">Comic Relief</a> in 1989, Bananarama recorded a cover of <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a>' song "<a href="/wiki/Help!_(song)" title="Help! (song)">Help!</a>" with <a href="/wiki/Lananeeneenoonoo" title="Lananeeneenoonoo">Lananeeneenoonoo</a> (UK #3), a mock girl-group created by British female comedy duo <a href="/wiki/French_and_Saunders" title="French and Saunders">French and Saunders</a>, with fellow comedian <a href="/wiki/Kathy_Burke" title="Kathy Burke">Kathy Burke</a>. 1989 also saw the band's first world tour, which hit North America, East Asia and the UK.</p>
<p>Bananarama's 1991 album, <i><a href="/wiki/Pop_Life_(Bananarama_album)" title="Pop Life (Bananarama album)">Pop Life</a></i> (UK #42) saw the group working with a variety of producers including <a href="/wiki/Martin_Glover" title="Martin Glover">Youth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shep_Pettibone" title="Shep Pettibone">Shep Pettibone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Jolley" title="Steve Jolley">Steve Jolley</a>, of Jolley &amp; Swain. They also incorporated a wider range of musical genres including <a href="/wiki/Reggae" title="Reggae">reggae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flamenco" title="Flamenco">flamenco</a> guitar, and <a href="/wiki/Acid_house" title="Acid house">acid house</a>. Despite receiving some of the strongest and most positive reviews of their career, London Records limited promotions and minimal radio airplay resulted in the singles "<a href="/wiki/Only_Your_Love" title="Only Your Love">Only Your Love</a>" (UK #27), "<a href="/wiki/Preacher_Man" title="Preacher Man">Preacher Man</a>" (UK #20) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doobie_Brothers" title="Doobie Brothers">Doobie Brothers</a>' cover of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_Train_Running" title="Long Train Running">Long Train Running</a>" (UK #30) failing to emulate their previous Top 10 successes. After the release of the fourth and final single from the album, "<a href="/wiki/Tripping_on_Your_Love" title="Tripping on Your Love">Tripping on Your Love</a>" (UK #75), band manager Hillary Shaw resigned to manage <a href="/wiki/Kim_Appleby" title="Kim Appleby">Kim Appleby</a>'s career, and O'Sullivan left the band to form the short-lived Slippery Feet.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1992.E2.80.932001:_Duo_re-launch.2C_Please_Yourself.2C_Ultra_Violet_and_Exotica">1992–2001: Duo re-launch, <i>Please Yourself</i>, <i>Ultra Violet</i> and <i>Exotica</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bananarama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1992–2001: Duo re-launch, Please Yourself, Ultra Violet and Exotica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1992, Dallin and Woodward returned as a duo and had a UK top 30 hit with "<a href="/wiki/Movin%27_On_(Bananarama_song)" title="Movin' On (Bananarama song)">Movin' On</a>" (UK #24), which was the first single from the album <i><a href="/wiki/Please_Yourself" title="Please Yourself">Please Yourself</a></i>. Released in 1993, the album marked the return of production duties back to the remaining members of <a href="/wiki/Stock_Aitken_Waterman" title="Stock Aitken Waterman">SAW</a>, Stock and Waterman. Ironically, though the lead single was titled "Movin' On", the album was a regression to their previous <a href="/wiki/Euro_disco" title="Euro disco">Euro disco</a> sound as illustrated by their cover of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andrea_True_Connection" title="Andrea True Connection">Andrea True Connection</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/More,_More,_More" title="More, More, More">More, More, More</a>" (UK #24). Bananarama was losing momentum as subsequent single "<a href="/wiki/Last_Thing_on_My_Mind" title="Last Thing on My Mind">Last Thing on My Mind</a>" (UK #71) struggled to chart. It was to be their last album on <a href="/wiki/London_Records" title="London Records">London Records</a>.</p>
<p>Their next offering was 1995's <i><a href="/wiki/Ultra_Violet_(Bananarama_album)" title="Ultra Violet (Bananarama album)">Ultra Violet</a></i> (titled <i>I Found Love</i> in Japan) on a new label. The album and its three singles "I Found Love", "<a href="/wiki/Every_Shade_of_Blue" title="Every Shade of Blue">Every Shade of Blue</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Take_Me_to_Your_Heart_(Bananarama_song)" title="Take Me to Your Heart (Bananarama song)">Take Me to Your Heart</a>" were only released in some European countries, North America, Japan and Australia, but not in Britain.</p>
<p>In 1998, Dallin, Woodward and Fahey regrouped for the first time in ten years and recorded the track "<a href="/wiki/Waterloo_(ABBA_song)" title="Waterloo (ABBA song)">Waterloo</a>" (a cover of the classic <a href="/wiki/ABBA" title="ABBA">ABBA</a> song) for the <a href="/wiki/Eurovision_Song_Contest" title="Eurovision Song Contest">Eurovision</a> celebration <i>A Song for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eurotrash_(TV_show)" title="Eurotrash (TV show)">Eurotrash</a></i> on <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a>. However, Fahey made it clear that this was a one-off and that she was not formally rejoining the group. In 1999, Dallin, Woodward and Fahey were interviewed together for an episode of the BBC music documentary series <i>Young Guns Go For It</i> dedicated to the group. Jacquie O'Sullivan also took part in the programme.</p>
<p>In 2001, Dallin and Woodward released a new Bananarama album, <i><a href="/wiki/Exotica_(Bananarama_album)" title="Exotica (Bananarama album)">Exotica</a></i>. Collaborators included Pascal Caubet and Dallin's boyfriend Bassey Walker. The album also included Latin and R&amp;B influenced dance songs, as well as reinterpreted versions of their earlier hits. Only two promotional singles would be released from the album, one of which was a cover of <a href="/wiki/Wham!" title="Wham!">Wham!</a>'s ballad "<a href="/wiki/Careless_Whisper" title="Careless Whisper">Careless Whisper</a>" (which was co-written by Woodward's partner <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Ridgeley" title="Andrew Ridgeley">Andrew Ridgeley</a>). The other release "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/If_(Bananarama_song)" title="If (Bananarama song)">If</a>" received positive reviews but failed to chart.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2002.E2.80.932006:_Drama">2002–2006: <i>Drama</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bananarama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2002–2006: Drama">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By 2002, Bananarama had sold 40 million records worldwide.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> That year, they released another greatest hits album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Very_Best_of_Bananarama" title="The Very Best of Bananarama">The Very Best of Bananarama</a></i>, in the UK. They also recorded the song "Love Him, Leave Him, Forget Him" for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Sky_Broadcasting" title="British Sky Broadcasting">Sky TV</a>'s show <i><a href="/wiki/Is_Harry_on_the_Boat%3F" title="Is Harry on the Boat?">Is Harry on the Boat?</a></i> as well as the song "U R My Baby" for a German disco project. The group (with Siobhan Fahey as special guest) celebrated the 20th anniversary of their first hit at the <a href="/wiki/London_Astoria" title="London Astoria">London Astoria</a> in London, with an audience of 3000 people.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>With 1980s retro in vogue, Bananarama made a comeback in the British dance charts in 2005. Solasso remixed their early hit "Really Saying Something" and it was a huge hit with clubbers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A video was filmed with models from <i><a href="/wiki/Britain%27s_Next_Top_Model" title="Britain's Next Top Model">Britain's Next Top Model</a></i> TV show but the record was shelved despite topping the dance charts. A new single, "<a href="/wiki/Move_in_My_Direction" title="Move in My Direction">Move in My Direction</a>", was released in July and hit the UK charts at #14 following playlist support from <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_2" title="BBC Radio 2">Radio 2</a> and many local stations, making this song the group's 24th UK Top 40 hit, and their first Top 20 hit since their 1991 single "<a href="/wiki/Preacher_Man" title="Preacher Man">Preacher Man</a>". Follow-up single "<a href="/wiki/Look_on_the_Floor_(Hypnotic_Tango)" title="Look on the Floor (Hypnotic Tango)">Look on the Floor (Hypnotic Tango)</a>", however, was less successful peaking at #26 which was followed by the <i><a href="/wiki/Drama_(Bananarama_album)" title="Drama (Bananarama album)">Drama</a></i> album which peaked at #169.</p>
<p><i>Drama</i> was released in the U.S. on 16 May and after a fourteen-year absence, Bananarama was back on <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a>'s</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_Dance_Club_Songs" title="Hot Dance Club Songs">Hot Dance Club Songs</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_Dance_Airplay" title="Hot Dance Airplay">Hot Dance Airplay</a> charts with an import version of "<a href="/wiki/Look_on_the_Floor_(Hypnotic_Tango)" title="Look on the Floor (Hypnotic Tango)">Look on the Floor (Hypnotic Tango)</a>" (US#2). "Look on the Floor" became their first U.S. hit since 1992, when they scored with "<a href="/wiki/Tripping_on_Your_Love" title="Tripping on Your Love">Tripping on Your Love</a>" (US#14). "<a href="/wiki/Move_in_My_Direction" title="Move in My Direction">Move in My Direction</a>" (US#14) was released as a second single in the U.S.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2006.E2.80.932011:_Remasters_and_Viva">2006–2011: Remasters and <i>Viva</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bananarama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2006–2011: Remasters and Viva">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Viva_(Bananarama_album)" title="Viva (Bananarama album)">Viva (Bananarama album)</a></div>
<p>Summer 2006 saw the <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Records" title="Warner Bros. Records">Warner Bros. Records</a> release of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Twelve_Inches_of_Bananarama" title="The Twelve Inches of Bananarama">The Twelve Inches of Bananarama</a></i>, a compilation of twelve remixes on CD for the first time. The collection features the rare <a href="/wiki/George_Michael" title="George Michael">George Michael</a> remix of "<a href="/wiki/Tripping_on_Your_Love" title="Tripping on Your Love">Tripping on Your Love</a>", among others.</p>
<p>On 19 March 2007, Bananarama's first six studio albums were reissued by <a href="/wiki/Rhino_Entertainment" title="Rhino Entertainment">Rhino Records</a> on CD with bonus material, including alternative versions, remixes and B-sides. On 7 May 2007, another best-of collection titled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greatest_Hits_and_More_More_More" title="Greatest Hits and More More More">Greatest Hits and More More More</a></i> was released by <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Records" title="Warner Bros. Records">Warner Bros. Records</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dallin and Woodward performed a set along with other 1980s acts at <i>Retro Fest</i> on 1 September 2007 at <a href="/wiki/Culzean_Castle" title="Culzean Castle">Culzean Castle</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ayrshire" title="Ayrshire">Ayrshire</a>, Scotland.</p>
<p>In February 2007, Bananarama's official website announced that they were back in the studio recording new material. A cover version of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voyage_Voyage" title="Voyage Voyage">Voyage Voyage</a>" was performed at a concert in France. Bananarama also confirmed they were contributing vocals as guest artists on the song "Ultra Violet" (not to be confused with Bananarama's seventh album <i><a href="/wiki/Ultra_Violet_(Bananarama_album)" title="Ultra Violet (Bananarama album)">Ultra Violet</a></i>) by new dance act Block Rocker, a teaming up of producers/remixers Digital Dog and Ashiva. However, the song never surfaced.</p>
<p>In October 2007, Bananarama announced that they would be appearing on 2008's <i>Here and Now Tour</i> with other 1980s artists such as <a href="/wiki/Belinda_Carlisle" title="Belinda Carlisle">Belinda Carlisle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Young_(singer_and_guitarist)" title="Paul Young (singer and guitarist)">Paul Young</a>, <a href="/wiki/ABC_(band)" title="ABC (band)">ABC</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rick_Astley" title="Rick Astley">Rick Astley</a>. They also announced plans to record a new album of disco cover versions and new songs.</p>
<p>In August 2008, it was announced that Bananarama was back in the studio recording a track with <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Simmons" title="Joseph Simmons">Rev Run</a> from <a href="/wiki/Run%E2%80%93D.M.C." title="Run–D.M.C.">Run–D.M.C.</a> who had a new album in the pipeline. He wanted to sample "<a href="/wiki/Na_Na_Hey_Hey_Kiss_Him_Goodbye" title="Na Na Hey Hey Kiss Him Goodbye">Na Na Hey Hey Kiss Him Goodbye</a>" for his track but then decided to ask Dallin and Woodward to sing it instead. The title of the track was not confirmed but was to be credited as be "Run-D.M.C. featuring Bananarama".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The song, 'Invincible', credited as Rev Run featuring Bananarama, did not surface until late 2014 on Rev Run's solo album Red Rhythm Rewind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2009, Bananarama performed at the Isle of Wight Festival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In August 2009, they performed at the <a href="/wiki/Rewind_Festival" title="Rewind Festival">80s Rewind Festival</a> in <a href="/wiki/Henley-on-Thames" title="Henley-on-Thames">Henley-on-Thames</a> alongside other 80s acts which included <a href="/wiki/Rick_Astley" title="Rick Astley">Rick Astley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belinda_Carlisle" title="Belinda Carlisle">Belinda Carlisle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kim_Wilde" title="Kim Wilde">Kim Wilde</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The duo also performed at The Manchester Gay Pride Festival over the August Bank Holiday weekend.</p>
<p>Bananarama released a new single entitled "<a href="/wiki/Love_Comes" title="Love Comes">Love Comes</a>" (UK #44), and a new album <i><a href="/wiki/Viva_(Bananarama_album)" title="Viva (Bananarama album)">Viva</a></i> (UK #87) in September 2009. The album was produced entirely by <a href="/wiki/Ian_Masterson" title="Ian Masterson">Ian Masterson</a>, and released through Fascination Records. A second single was released from the album in April 2010, a new remix of the song "<a href="/wiki/Love_Don%27t_Live_Here_(Bananarama_song)" title="Love Don't Live Here (Bananarama song)">Love Don't Live Here</a>" (UK #114) backed by Ian Masterson's 2010 reworking of the 1995 single "<a href="/wiki/Every_Shade_of_Blue" title="Every Shade of Blue">Every Shade of Blue</a>" and "The Runner" (originally recorded by <a href="/wiki/The_Three_Degrees" title="The Three Degrees">The Three Degrees</a>), remixed by Buzz Junkies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Other covers recorded during this period included Bryan Adam's "Run To You", Simon &amp; Garfunkel's "The Sound Of Silence" and Roxy Music's "Tokyo Joe". These were included as B Sides to the released singles and as bonus digital tracks to the parent album.</p>
<p>In September 2010, Bananarama were back in the studio with producer Ian Masterson, and recorded a Christmas song titled "<a href="/wiki/Baby_It%27s_Christmas" title="Baby It's Christmas">Baby It's Christmas</a>" (UK #199). The track was written by Sara Dallin and Ian Masterson and was released on 13 December in the UK and Europe as a digital EP. The track was also included on a US Christmas compilation CD entitled <i>Super Dance Christmas Party, Volume 3</i>. "Baby It's Christmas" reached #19 in the UK Indie Singles chart and #199 in the main UK Singles Chart.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In April 2011, Bananarama appeared on ITV's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benidorm_(TV_Series)" title="Benidorm (TV Series)">Benidorm</a></i> and performed "<a href="/wiki/Love_in_the_First_Degree_(Bananarama_song)" title="Love in the First Degree (Bananarama song)">Love in the First Degree</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Robert_De_Niro%27s_Waiting..." title="Robert De Niro's Waiting...">Robert De Niro's Waiting...</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Movin%27_On_(Bananarama_song)" title="Movin' On (Bananarama song)">Movin' On</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2011, Bananarama performed at <i>Retrolicious 2011</i> in Singapore, together with <a href="/wiki/The_Human_League" title="The Human League">The Human League</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belinda_Carlisle" title="Belinda Carlisle">Belinda Carlisle</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2012.E2.80.93present:_30_Years_of_Bananarama_and_Now_or_Never">2012–present: <i>30 Years of Bananarama</i> and <i>Now or Never</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bananarama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2012–present: 30 Years of Bananarama and Now or Never">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 9 July 2012, Warner Music imprint <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhino_Records" title="Rhino Records">Rhino Records</a> released a greatest hits CD and DVD Collection <i><a href="/wiki/30_Years_of_Bananarama" title="30 Years of Bananarama">30 Years of Bananarama</a></i> (UK #61) to celebrate the band's 30th anniversary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The album charted at 62 on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a>. On 9 August 2012, the band performed at the men's final of the beach volleyball at the London Olympics. They performed a medley of "<a href="/wiki/Cruel_Summer_(song)" title="Cruel Summer (song)">Cruel Summer</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Love_in_the_First_Degree_(Bananarama_song)" title="Love in the First Degree (Bananarama song)">Love in the First Degree</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Venus_(Shocking_Blue_song)" title="Venus (Shocking Blue song)">Venus</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>A four-track digital-only EP <i><a href="/wiki/Now_or_Never_(Bananarama_EP)" title="Now or Never (Bananarama EP)">Now or Never</a></i> was released independently by the group on 21 September 2012 to coincide with the 30th anniversary of the group and the launch of their <a href="/wiki/Hard_Rock_Cafe" title="Hard Rock Cafe">Hard Rock Cafe</a> "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinktober" title="Pinktober">Pinktober</a>" US tour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Apart from two versions of the song "Now or Never" the EP contains two new songs — one entitled "La La Love", the other one being a cover version of <a href="/wiki/Maroon_5" title="Maroon 5">Maroon 5</a>/<a href="/wiki/Christina_Aguilera" title="Christina Aguilera">Christina Aguilera</a>'s "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moves_Like_Jagger" title="Moves Like Jagger">Moves Like Jagger</a>". Via iTunes, "<a href="/wiki/Movin%27_On_(Bananarama_song)" title="Movin' On (Bananarama song)">Movin' On</a> 2012" was available for a limited time as a bonus track.</p>
<p>In March 2013, Dallin posted on <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> that she and Woodward were writing new tracks together. On 11 April, she announced that four tracks were completed and that there were "six to go". Since then, Bananarama have performed at different events in Poland (23 April), Germany (4 May — Kult am Kalkberg) and the UK (18 May - <a href="/wiki/Let%27s_Rock_The_Moor!" title="Let's Rock The Moor!">Let's Rock The Moor!</a>; 25 May — Birmingham Pride).</p>
<p>On 28 October 2013, Bananarama's first six albums <i><a href="/wiki/Deep_Sea_Skiving" title="Deep Sea Skiving">Deep Sea Skiving</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bananarama_(album)" title="Bananarama (album)">Bananarama</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/True_Confessions_(album)" title="True Confessions (album)">True Confessions</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Wow!_(Bananarama_album)" title="Wow! (Bananarama album)">Wow!</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pop_Life_(Bananarama_album)" title="Pop Life (Bananarama album)">Pop Life</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Please_Yourself" title="Please Yourself">Please Yourself</a></i> were reissued by Edsel Records, each of them consisting of Deluxe 2CDs plus DVD. Bananarama are also confirmed to perform in 2014 at Let's Rock Bristol! (7 June), Let's Rock Leeds! (21 June) and Let's Rock Southampton! (12 July). On 9 November 2013, Bananarama revealed on the BBC show <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pointless_Celebrities" title="Pointless Celebrities">Pointless Celebrities</a></i> that they were recording a new album in Nashville and that it would be influenced by country and pop. In March 2015, Edsel Records released <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Megarama&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Megarama (page does not exist)">Megarama</a></i>, a 3 CD collection of remixes that was followed in August with a 33CD singles collection box set entitled <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=In_A_Bunch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="In A Bunch (page does not exist)">In A Bunch</a></i>, which contains all single releases from "<a href="/wiki/Aie_a_Mwana" title="Aie a Mwana">Aie a Mwana</a>" right through to "<a href="/wiki/More,_More,_More" title="More, More, More">More, More, More</a>". In 2016, Bananarama toured Australia in February and showcased their new song "Got to Get Away". On 9 March 2016, Sara confirmed on her Twitter page that Bananarama have signed a new deal with BMG. In November 2016, an excerpt of a song that bears the working title "Looking For Someone" was posted on the official Twitter page of the band.</p>
