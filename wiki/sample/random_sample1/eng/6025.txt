<p><b>TW Hydrae</b> is a <a href="/wiki/T_Tauri_star" title="T Tauri star">T Tauri star</a> approximately 176 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> away in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hydra_(constellation)" title="Hydra (constellation)">Hydra</a> (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_Serpent" title="Sea Serpent">Sea Serpent</a>). The star is the closest such star to the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>. TW Hydrae is about 80% of the <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> of the Sun, but is only about 5-10 million years old. The star appears to be accreting from a face-on <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk" title="Protoplanetary disk">protoplanetary disk</a> of dust and gas, which has been resolved in images from the <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Large_Millimeter_Array" title="Atacama Large Millimeter Array">ALMA observatory</a>. TW Hydrae is accompanied by about twenty other low-mass stars with similar ages and spatial motions, comprising the "<a href="/wiki/TW_Hydrae_association" title="TW Hydrae association">TW Hydrae association</a>" or TWA, one of the closest regions of recent "fossil" star-formation to the Sun.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stellar_characteristics">Stellar characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TW_Hydrae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Stellar characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>TW Hydrae is a <a href="/wiki/Pre-main-sequence_star" title="Pre-main-sequence star">pre-main-sequence star</a> that is approximately 80% the mass of and 111% the radius of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. It has a temperature of 4000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kelvin_scale" title="Kelvin scale">K</a> and is about 8 million years old. In comparison, the Sun is about 4.6 billion years old<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> and has a temperature of 5778 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> The star's luminosity is 28% that of the Sun.</p>
<p>The star's <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a>, or how bright it appears from Earth's perspective, is 11.7. It is too dim to be seen with the naked eye.</p>

<p>The star is known to host one likely exoplanet, <a href="/wiki/TW_Hydrae_b" title="TW Hydrae b">TW Hydrae b</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protoplanetary_disk">Protoplanetary disk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TW_Hydrae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Protoplanetary disk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Previously_disproven_protoplanet">Previously disproven protoplanet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TW_Hydrae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Previously disproven protoplanet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In December 2007, a team led by Johny Setiawan of the <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Institute_for_Astronomy" title="Max Planck Institute for Astronomy">Max Planck Institute for Astronomy</a> in <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> announced discovery of a planet orbiting TW Hydrae, dubbed "TW Hydrae b" with a minimum mass around 1.2 <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_mass" title="Jupiter mass">Jupiter masses</a>, a period of 3.56 days, and an orbital radius of 0.04 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">astronomical units</a> (inside the inner rim of the protoplanetary disk). Assuming it orbits in the same plane as the outer part of the dust disk (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">inclination</a> 7±1°<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-setiawan08_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-setiawan08-9">[8]</a></sup>), it has a true mass of 9.8±3.3 Jupiter masses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-setiawan08_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-setiawan08-9">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newscientist_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-newscientist-10">[9]</a></sup> However, if the inclination is similar to the inner part of the dust disk (4.3±1.0°<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pontoppidan08_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-pontoppidan08-11">[10]</a></sup>), the mass would be 16<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+5<br/>
−3</span> Jupiter masses, making it a <a href="/wiki/Brown_dwarf" title="Brown dwarf">brown dwarf</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pontoppidan08_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-pontoppidan08-11">[10]</a></sup> Since the star itself is so young, it was presumed this is the youngest extrasolar planet yet discovered, and essentially still in formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mpia_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-mpia-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008 a team of Spanish researchers concluded that the planet does not exist: the radial velocity variations were not consistent when observed at different <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a>, which would not occur if the origin of the radial velocity variations was caused by an orbiting planet. Instead, the data was better modelled by <a href="/wiki/Starspot" title="Starspot">starspots</a> on TW Hydrae's surface passing in and out of view as the star rotates. "Results support the spot scenario rather than the presence of a hot Jupiter around TW Hya".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-huelmo_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-huelmo-13">[12]</a></sup> Similar wavelength-dependent radial velocity variations, also caused by starspots, have been detected on other T Tauri stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prato_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-prato-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="New_study_of_more_distant_planet">New study of more distant planet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TW_Hydrae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: New study of more distant planet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>In 2016, <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Large_Millimeter_Array" title="Atacama Large Millimeter Array">ALMA</a> found evidence that a possible Neptune-like planet was forming in its disk, at a distance of around 22 AU.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Detection_of_methanol">Detection of methanol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TW_Hydrae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Detection of methanol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2016, <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, one of the building blocks for life, was detected in the star's protoplanetary disk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
