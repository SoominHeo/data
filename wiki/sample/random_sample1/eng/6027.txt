<p><b>Smelts</b> are a family of small <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, the <b>Osmeridae</b>, found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a> Oceans. They are also known as <b>freshwater smelts</b> or <b>typical smelts</b> to distinguish them from the related <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argentinidae" title="Argentinidae">Argentinidae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bathylagidae" title="Bathylagidae">Bathylagidae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Retropinnidae" title="Retropinnidae">Retropinnidae</a>.</p>
<p>Some smelt species are common in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American_Great_Lakes" title="North American Great Lakes">North American Great Lakes</a>, and in the lakes and seas of the northern part of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, where they run in large <a href="/wiki/Shoaling_and_schooling" title="Shoaling and schooling">schools</a> along the saltwater coastline during spring migration to their spawning streams. In some western parts of the United States, smelt populations have greatly declined in recent decades, leading to their protection under the Endangered Species Act. The <a href="/wiki/Delta_smelt" title="Delta smelt">Delta smelt</a>, <i>Hypomesus transpacificus</i>, found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacramento_Delta" title="Sacramento Delta">Sacramento Delta</a> of California, and the Columbia River smelt, <i>Eulachon</i>, are both protected from harvest.</p>
<p>Some species of smelts are among the few fish that sportsmen have been allowed to net, using hand-held <a href="/wiki/Hand_net" title="Hand net">dip nets</a>, either along the coastline or in streams. Some sportsmen also <a href="/wiki/Ice_fishing" title="Ice fishing">ice fish</a> for smelt. They are often fried and eaten whole.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smelt_(fish)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In size, smelts usually grow to about 20 cm (7.9 in) long, although some species can reach 70 cm (28 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FB_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FB-1">[1]</a></sup> Smelts are a food source for many fish, including salmon, <a href="/wiki/Striped_bass" title="Striped bass">striped bass</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lake_trout" title="Lake trout">lake trout</a>.</p>
<p>Like salmon, many species are <a href="/wiki/Fish_migration" title="Fish migration">anadromous</a>, living most of their lives in the sea, but traveling into fresh water to breed. However, a few exceptions, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surf_smelt" title="Surf smelt">surf smelt</a>, spend their entire lives at sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoF_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoF-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Smelt_dipping">Smelt dipping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smelt_(fish)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Smelt dipping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SmeltDipping.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1728" data-file-width="2304" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/46/SmeltDipping.JPG/220px-SmeltDipping.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/46/SmeltDipping.JPG/330px-SmeltDipping.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/46/SmeltDipping.JPG/440px-SmeltDipping.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

Smelt dipping in the <a href="/wiki/Keweenaw_Peninsula" title="Keweenaw Peninsula">Keweenaw Peninsula</a> in <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a></div>

<p>In the Canadian provinces and U.S. states around the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>, "smelt dipping" is a common group sport in the early spring and when stream waters reach around 4 °C, (40–42 °F). Fish are spotted using a flashlight or headlamp and scooped out of the water using a dip net made of nylon or metal mesh. The smelt are cleaned by removing the head and the entrails. Fins, scales, and bones of all but the largest of smelts are cooked without removal.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On the Maine coast, smelts were also a sign of spring, with the run of these small fish up tiny tidal estuaries. Many of these streams were narrow enough for a person to straddle and get a good catch of smelt by dipping a bucket.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_food">As food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smelt_(fish)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: As food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smelt_(fish)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Smelts are an important winter catch in the saltwater mouths of rivers in <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> and the Maritime Provinces of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Fishermen would historically go to customary locations over the ice using horses and sleighs. Smelt taken out of the cold saltwater were preferred to those taken in warm water. The fish did not command a high price on the market, but provided a source of supplemental income due to their abundance. The smelts were "flash frozen" simply by leaving them on the ice and then sold to fish buyers who came down the rivers.</p>
<p>In present day, smelts are fished both commercially using nets at sea, and for recreation by hand-netting them or spearing or angling them through holes in river ice. They are often the target fish of small 'fishing shack' villages that spring up along frozen rivers. Typical ways of preparing them include pan-frying in flour and butter, or deep-frying in batter, and they are sometimes cooked directly out of the water over small stoves in the shacks.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Asia">East Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smelt_(fish)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: East Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Smelt is popular in Japan, as the species <i>Sprinchus lanceolatus</i>, and is generally served grilled, called <i><a href="/wiki/Shishamo" title="Shishamo">shishamo</a></i>, especially when full of eggs.</p>
<p>Smelt <a href="/wiki/Roe" title="Roe">roe</a>, specifically from <a href="/wiki/Capelin" title="Capelin">capelin</a>, called masago in Japanese, is yellow to orange in color and is often used in <a href="/wiki/Sushi" title="Sushi">sushi</a>.</p>
<p>Smelt is also served in <i><a href="/wiki/Dim_sum" title="Dim sum">dim sum</a></i> restaurants, deep fried with the heads and tails attached, identified as <i>duō chūn yú</i> (多春鱼) or <i>duō luǎn yú</i> (多卵魚), which loosely translates as "fish with many eggs".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Smelt is one of the prime fish species eaten in coastal <a href="/wiki/Karnataka" title="Karnataka">Karnataka</a>, especially in <a href="/wiki/Mangalore" title="Mangalore">Mangalore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Udupi" title="Udupi">Udupi</a> regions, usually fried with heads and tailed removed or in curries. They are called 'Bolingei' (ಬೊಳಂಜೆ) in <a href="/wiki/Kannada" title="Kannada">Kannada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tulu_language" title="Tulu language">Tulu</a> and 'MotiyaLe' in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Konkani" title="Konkani">Konkani</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Festivals">Festivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smelt_(fish)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Festivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In the city of <a href="/wiki/Inje_County" title="Inje County">Inje</a>, South Korea (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gangwon_Province_(South_Korea)" title="Gangwon Province (South Korea)">Gangwon Province</a>), an Ice Fishing Festival is held annually from January 30 to February 2 on Soyang Lake, coinciding with the smelt's yearly run into fresh water to spawn. They are locally known as <i>bing-eo</i> (빙어) and typically eaten alive or deep fried.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.injefestival.co.kr/english/main.asp" rel="nofollow">[1]</a></p>
<p>In Finland, the province of Paltamo has yearly <i>Norssikarnevaali</i> festivals in middle of May.</p>
<p>For some Italians, especially from the region of <a href="/wiki/Calabria" title="Calabria">Calabria</a>, fried smelts are a traditional part of <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> Eve dinner consisting of multiple courses of fish.</p>
<p>In 1956, the Chamber of Commerce in <a href="/wiki/Kelso,_Washington" title="Kelso, Washington">Kelso, Washington</a>, declared Kelso, located on the <a href="/wiki/Cowlitz_River" title="Cowlitz River">Cowlitz River</a>, as the "Smelt Capital of the World". They erected billboards proclaiming this, and held festivals for the annual smelt runs, until the runs dried up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The village of <a href="/wiki/Lewiston,_New_York" title="Lewiston, New York">Lewiston, New York</a>, on the lower portion of the Niagara River, celebrates an annual two-day smelt festival the first weekend in May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> During the festival, approximately 350 pounds of smelt are battered and fried at the Lewiston Waterfront.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The smelt samples are free during the festival and donations are welcome, as they help support programs supported by the Niagara River Anglers. The festival has a motto, which is a play on words: "Lewiston never smelt so good." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lithuania celebrates an annual weekend smelt festival in Palanga "Palangos Stinta" early every January.</p>
<p>At the time of the smelt's spring run in the <a href="/wiki/Neva_River" title="Neva River">Neva River</a> at the head of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Finland" title="Gulf of Finland">Gulf of Finland</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a>, a Smelt Festival (<i>Prazdnik korushki</i>) is celebrated.</p>
