<p><b>Eurypterids</b> (<b>sea scorpions</b>) are an extinct group of <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> related to <a href="/wiki/Arachnid" title="Arachnid">arachnids</a> that include the largest known arthropods to have ever lived. They are members of the extinct order <b>Eurypterida</b> (<a href="/wiki/Chelicerata" title="Chelicerata">Chelicerata</a>); which is the most diverse Paleozoic chelicerate order in terms of species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dunlop2008_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-dunlop2008-1">[1]</a></sup> The name Eurypterida comes from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> words <i><span lang="el" xml:lang="el">eury-</span></i> (meaning "broad" or "wide") and <i><span lang="el" xml:lang="el">pteron</span></i> (meaning "wing").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This name was chosen due to the pair of wide swimming appendages on the first fossil eurypterids discovered. The largest, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Jaekelopterus" title="Jaekelopterus">Jaekelopterus</a></i>, reached 2.5 metres (8 ft 2 in) in length, but most species were less than 20 centimetres (8 in). They were formidable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator">predators</a> that thrived in warm shallow water, in both seas and lakes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> from the mid <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> to late <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a> (<span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=470–248">470 to 248</a> <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">million years ago</a></span>).</p>
<p>Although informally called "sea scorpions", only the earliest ones were marine (later ones lived in brackish or freshwater), and they were not true <a href="/wiki/Scorpion" title="Scorpion">scorpions</a>. According to theory, the move from the sea to fresh water had probably occurred by the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvanian_(geology)" title="Pennsylvanian (geology)">Pennsylvanian</a> subperiod. Eurypterids are believed to have undergone <a href="/wiki/Ecdysis" title="Ecdysis">ecdysis</a>, making their significance in ecosystems difficult to assess, because it can be difficult to tell a fossil <a href="/wiki/Moulting" title="Moulting">moult</a> from a true fossil carcass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> They became extinct during the <a href="/wiki/Permian%E2%80%93Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian–Triassic extinction event">Permian–Triassic extinction event</a> or sometime before the event <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=252.17">252.17</a> <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">million years ago</a></span>. Their fossils have a near global distribution.</p>
<p>About two dozen families of eurypterids are known. Perhaps the best-known genus of eurypterid is <i><a href="/wiki/Eurypterus" title="Eurypterus">Eurypterus</a></i>, of which around 16 fossil species are known. The genus <i>Eurypterus</i> was described in 1825 by <a href="/wiki/James_Ellsworth_De_Kay" title="James Ellsworth De Kay">James Ellsworth De Kay</a>, a zoologist. He recognized the arthropod nature of the first-ever described eurypterid specimen, found by Dr. S. L. Mitchill. In 1984, that species, <i>Eurypterus remipes</i> was named the <a href="/wiki/List_of_U.S._state_fossils" title="List of U.S. state fossils">state fossil</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_of_New_York" title="State of New York">New York</a>.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Pentecopterus" title="Pentecopterus">Pentecopterus decorahensis</a></i>, which lived as early as 467.3 million years ago, is the oldest described eurypterid; with an estimated length of 1.83 metres (6 ft 0 in),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YU-20150831_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-YU-20150831-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SN-20150901_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-SN-20150901-6">[6]</a></sup> it has been described as "the first real big predator".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMC-20150901_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-BMC-20150901-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP-20150831_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP-20150831-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Body_structure">Body structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurypterid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Body structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eurypterus_Smithsonian.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="1536" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Eurypterus_Smithsonian.jpg/220px-Eurypterus_Smithsonian.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Eurypterus_Smithsonian.jpg/330px-Eurypterus_Smithsonian.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Eurypterus_Smithsonian.jpg/440px-Eurypterus_Smithsonian.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eurypterus_Smithsonian.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Eurypterus" title="Eurypterus">Eurypterus</a></i> model, exhibited in the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="National Museum of Natural History">National Museum of Natural History</a>: Hall of Fossils</div>

<p>Eurypterids have been formally described as follows:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Small to very large merostomes with elongate lanceolate, rarely trilobed body; prosoma [head] of moderate size; opisthosoma [body] with 12 moveable segments and styliform to spatulate telson [tail], with division commonly into 7-segmented preabdomen and 5-segmented postabdomen; prosomal [head] appendages 6, comprising 3-jointed chelicerae, walking legs, the last pair commonly transformed into swimming legs. Mouth central, bordered posteriorly by endostoma and metastoma. Operculum with median genital appendage, abdominal appendages plate-shaped with nonlaminate gills. Ordovician-Permian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The typical eurypterid had a large, flat, semicircular <a href="/wiki/Carapace" title="Carapace">carapace</a>, followed by a jointed section, and finally a tapering, flexible tail, most ending with a long spine at the end (<i><a href="/wiki/Pterygotus" title="Pterygotus">Pterygotus</a></i>, though, had a large flat tail, possibly with a smaller spine). Behind the head, eurypterids had twelve body segments, each of which was formed from a dorsal plate (<a href="/wiki/Tergum" title="Tergum">tergite</a>) and a ventral plate (<a href="/wiki/Sternum_(arthropod_anatomy)" title="Sternum (arthropod anatomy)">sternite</a>). The tail, known as the <a href="/wiki/Telson" title="Telson">telson</a>, is spiked in most eurypterids (as in modern <a href="/wiki/Scorpion" title="Scorpion">scorpions</a>) and in some species it may have been used to inject <a href="/wiki/Venom" title="Venom">venom</a>, but there is no evidence that any eurypterids were venomous. Most eurypterids have paddles toward the end of the carapace and beyond, which were used to propel themselves through water. The suborder Stylonurina had walking legs instead of paddles. Some argue that the paddles were also used for digging. Underneath, in addition to the pair of swimming appendages, the creature had four pairs of <a href="/wiki/Arthropod_leg" title="Arthropod leg">jointed legs</a> for walking, and two claws at the front, <a href="/wiki/Chelicerae" title="Chelicerae">chelicerae</a>, which were enlarged in pterygotids. The walking legs had odd hairs, similar to modern-day crabs. Other features, common to ancient and modern arthropods of this type, include one pair of <a href="/wiki/Arthropod_eye" title="Arthropod eye">compound eyes</a> and a pair of smaller eye spots, called <a href="/wiki/Simple_eye_in_invertebrates#Ocelli_or_eye_spots" title="Simple eye in invertebrates"><i>ocelli</i></a>, located between the other, larger, pair of eyes.</p>

<i><a href="/wiki/Megarachne" title="Megarachne">Megarachne</a></i>, a eurypterid closely related to <i><a href="/wiki/Hibbertopterus" title="Hibbertopterus">Hibbertopterus</a></i> from the Late <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a>. It was originally described as a spider - the largest to have ever existed - hence its scientific name, which translates to "great/large spider".</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Many eurypterids had legs large and long enough to do more than allow them to crawl over the sea bottom; a number of species (particularly hibbertopterids) had large stout legs, and were probably capable of terrestrial locomotion (like <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_crab" title="Terrestrial crab">terrestrial crabs</a> today). Studies of what are believed to be <a href="/wiki/Fossil_trackway" title="Fossil trackway">fossil trackways</a> indicate that eurypterids used in-phase, hexapodous (six-legged) and octopodous (eight-legged) gaits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Some species may have been amphibious, emerging onto land for at least part of their life cycle; they may have been capable of breathing both in water and in air. A predatory <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropod</a> known as <i><a href="/wiki/Protichnites" title="Protichnites">Protichnites</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Owen1852_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Owen1852-11">[11]</a></sup> whose traces are found in Cambrian strata dating from <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=510">510</a> <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">million years ago</a></span>, is a possible <a href="/wiki/Crown_group#Stem_groups" title="Crown group">stem group</a> eurypterid, and is among the first evidence of animals on land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burling1917_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burling1917-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The largest sea-scorpion and the largest known arthropod ever to have lived is <i><a href="/wiki/Jaekelopterus" title="Jaekelopterus">Jaekelopterus</a> rhenaniae</i>, supported by the finding of a 46 cm (18 in) long claw in 2007, indicating a body length of 2.5 m (8 ft 2 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships_with_other_groups">Relationships with other groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurypterid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Relationships with other groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eurypterids have traditionally been regarded as close relatives of <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe_crab" title="Horseshoe crab">horseshoe crabs</a> (<a href="/wiki/Xiphosura" title="Xiphosura">Xiphosura</a>), together forming a group called <a href="/wiki/Merostomata" title="Merostomata">Merostomata</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garw_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Garw-14">[14]</a></sup> Subsequent studies placed eurypterids closer to the <a href="/wiki/Arachnid" title="Arachnid">arachnids</a> in a group called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Metastomata&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Metastomata (page does not exist)">Metastomata</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>There has also been a prevailing idea that eurypterids are closely related to <a href="/wiki/Scorpion" title="Scorpion">scorpions</a>, which they resemble.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> This hypothesis is reflected in the common name "sea scorpion". More recently it has been recognised that a little-known, extinct group called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chasmataspida" title="Chasmataspida">Chasmataspida</a> also shares features with Eurypterida,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and the two groups were sometimes confused with one another.</p>
<p>A recent summary of the relationships between arachnids and their relatives recognised Eurypterida, Xiphosura and Arachnida as three major groups, but was not able to resolve the phylogenetic relationship of any shared details between them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Another suggested the eurypterids were sister group to the <a href="/wiki/Chasmataspidida" title="Chasmataspidida">chasmataspids</a>, with these two groups in turn sister group to the horseshoe crabs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garw_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Garw-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phylogeny">Phylogeny</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurypterid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Phylogeny">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Eurypterida_of_New_York_figure_np_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="907" data-file-width="1372" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/The_Eurypterida_of_New_York_figure_np_1.jpg/220px-The_Eurypterida_of_New_York_figure_np_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/The_Eurypterida_of_New_York_figure_np_1.jpg/330px-The_Eurypterida_of_New_York_figure_np_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/The_Eurypterida_of_New_York_figure_np_1.jpg/440px-The_Eurypterida_of_New_York_figure_np_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Eurypterida_of_New_York_figure_np_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Genera found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, illustrated by <a href="/wiki/Charles_R._Knight" title="Charles R. Knight">Charles R. Knight</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cladogram" title="Cladogram">cladogram</a> presented here is simplified from a study by Tetlie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tetlie2007_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-tetlie2007-21">[21]</a></sup> The most important <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylogeny" title="Phylogeny">phylogenetic</a> breakdown is based on the two major innovations that characterise the evolution of the eurypterids. The most important was the transformation of the posteriormost prosomal appendage into a swimming paddle (as found in the clade Eurypterina). The second innovation was the enlargement of the <a href="/wiki/Chelicerae" title="Chelicerae">chelicerae</a>, (as found in the family Pterygotidae), allowing these appendages to be used for active prey capture.</p>
<p>Seventy-five percent of eurypterid species are <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eurypterine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eurypterine (page does not exist)">eurypterines</a>; this represents 99% of specimens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dunlop2008_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-dunlop2008-1">[1]</a></sup> The superfamily <a href="/wiki/Pterygotioidea" title="Pterygotioidea">Pterygotioidea</a> is the most species-rich clade, with 56 species, followed by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Adelophthalmoidea&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Adelophthalmoidea (page does not exist)">Adelophthalmoidea</a> with 43 species; as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sister_taxa" title="Sister taxa">sister taxa</a>, they comprise the most derived eurypterids. <a href="/wiki/Pterygotioidea" title="Pterygotioidea">Pterygotioidea</a> includes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pterygotid" title="Pterygotid">pterygotids</a>, which are the only eurypterids to have a cosmopolitan distribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tetlie2007_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-tetlie2007-21">[21]</a></sup> This clade is one of the best supported within the eurypterids.</p>
<p>It has been suggested that the development of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dermal_armour&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dermal armour (page does not exist)">dermal armour</a> in certain groups of jawless vertebrates (such as the <a href="/wiki/Heterostraci" title="Heterostraci">Heterostraci</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Osteostraci" title="Osteostraci">Osteostraci</a>) is in response to <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predation</a> pressure by increasingly sophisticated eurypterid predators<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> (specifically the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pterygotid" title="Pterygotid">pterygotids</a>) although this has yet to be verified by detailed analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tetliebriggs2009_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-tetliebriggs2009-23">[23]</a></sup> An increase in <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> diversity is tied to a decline in eurypterid diversity in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lower_Devonian" title="Lower Devonian">Lower Devonian</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-briggs2008diversity_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-briggs2008diversity-24">[24]</a></sup> although it is not thought that this represents competitive replacement; in fact, this is rare in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_record" title="Fossil record">fossil record</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ridley_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-ridley-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
