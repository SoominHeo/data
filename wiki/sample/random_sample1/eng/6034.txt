<p>The <b>Badr Organization</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">منظمة بدر</span>‎‎ <i>Munaẓẓama Badr</i>), previously known as the <b>Badr Brigades</b> or <b>Badr Corps</b>, is an <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraqi</a> political party headed by <a href="/wiki/Hadi_Al-Amiri" title="Hadi Al-Amiri">Hadi Al-Amiri</a>. The Badr Brigade was the Iran-officered military wing of the Iran-based Shia Islamic party, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supreme_Council_for_Islamic_Revolution_in_Iraq" title="Supreme Council for Islamic Revolution in Iraq">Supreme Council for Islamic Revolution in Iraq</a> (SCIRI), formed in 1982. Since the <a href="/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">2003 invasion of Iraq</a> most of Badr's fighters have entered the new Iraqi army and police force. Politically, Badr Brigade and SCIRI were considered to be one party since 2003, but have now unofficially separated<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> with the Badr Organization now an official Iraqi political party. Badr Brigade forces, and their Iranian commanders, have come to prominence in 2014 fighting the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_State_of_Iraq_and_the_Levant" title="Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant">Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant</a> (ISIL) in Iraq.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It is a part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Popular_Mobilization_Forces_(Iraq)" title="Popular Mobilization Forces (Iraq)">Popular Mobilization Forces</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Badr_Organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SCIRI">SCIRI</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Badr_Organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: SCIRI">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The organization was formed in Iran in 1982 as the military wing of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supreme_Council_for_Islamic_Revolution_in_Iraq" title="Supreme Council for Islamic Revolution in Iraq">Supreme Council for Islamic Revolution in Iraq</a>. It was based in Iran for two decades during the rule of <a href="/wiki/Saddam_Hussein" title="Saddam Hussein">Saddam Hussein</a> and led by Iranian officers. It consisted of several thousand Iraqi exiles, refugees, and defectors who fought alongside Iranian troops in the <a href="/wiki/Iran%E2%80%93Iraq_War" title="Iran–Iraq War">Iran–Iraq War</a>. The group was armed and directed by Iran.</p>
<p>They briefly returned to Iraq in 1991 during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1991_Iraqi_uprising" title="1991 Iraqi uprising">1991 Iraqi uprising</a> to fight against Saddam Hussein, focusing on the Shia holy cities of <a href="/wiki/Najaf" title="Najaf">Najaf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karbala" title="Karbala">Karbala</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> They retreated back into Iran after the uprising was crushed.</p>
<p>In 1995, during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kurdish_Civil_War" title="Kurdish Civil War">Kurdish Civil War</a>, Iran deployed 5,000 Badr fighters to Iraqi Kurdistan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>Returning to Iraq following the 2003 coalition invasion, the group changed its name from brigade to organization in response to the attempted voluntary disarming of Iraqi militias by the <a href="/wiki/Coalition_Provisional_Authority" title="Coalition Provisional Authority">Coalition Provisional Authority</a>. It is however widely believed the organization is still active as a militia within the security forces and it has been accused of running a secret prison<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and sectarian killings during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_war_in_Iraq_(2006%E2%80%9307)" title="Civil war in Iraq (2006–07)">Iraqi Civil War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-independent2006_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-independent2006-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because of their opposition to Saddam Hussein, the Badr Brigade was seen as a U.S. asset in the fight against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baathist" title="Baathist">Baathist</a> partisans. After the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Baghdad_(2003)" title="Battle of Baghdad (2003)">fall of Baghdad</a>, Badr forces reportedly joined the newly reconstituted army, police and Interior Ministry in significant numbers. The Interior Ministry was controlled by SCIRI and many Badr members became part of the Interior Ministry run <a href="/wiki/Wolf_Brigade_(Iraq)" title="Wolf Brigade (Iraq)">Wolf Brigade</a>. The Iraqi Interior Minister, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bayan_Jabr" title="Bayan Jabr">Bayan Jabr</a>, was a former leader of Badr Brigade militia.</p>
<p>In 2006 the United Nations human rights chief in Iraq, John Pace, said that hundreds of Iraqis were being tortured to death or executed by the Interior Ministry under SCIRI's control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-independent2006_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-independent2006-9">[9]</a></sup> According to a 2006 report by the Independent newspaper:</p>
<p>'Mr Pace said the Ministry of the Interior was "acting as a rogue element within the government". It is controlled by the main Shia party, the Supreme Council for Islamic Revolution in Iraq (Sciri); the Interior Minister, <a href="/wiki/Baqir_Jabr_al-Zubeidi" title="Baqir Jabr al-Zubeidi">Baqir Jabr al-Zubeidi</a>, is a former leader of Sciri's Badr Brigade militia, which is one of the main groups accused of carrying out sectarian killings. Another is the Mehdi Army of the young cleric <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moqtada_al-Sadr" title="Moqtada al-Sadr">Moqtada al-Sadr</a>, who is part of the Shia coalition seeking to form a government after winning the mid-December election. Many of the 110,000 policemen and police commandos under the ministry's control are suspected of being former members of the Badr Brigade. Not only counter-insurgency units such as the Wolf Brigade, the Scorpions and the Tigers, but the commandos and even the highway patrol police have been accused of acting as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Death_squads" title="Death squads">death squads</a>.</p>
<p>The paramilitary commandos, dressed in garish camouflage uniforms and driving around in pick-up trucks, are dreaded in Sunni neighbourhoods. People whom they have openly arrested have frequently been found dead several days later, with their bodies bearing obvious marks of torture.'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-independent2006_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-independent2006-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_action_against_ISIL">Military action against ISIL</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Badr_Organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Military action against ISIL">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Iraqi_Civil_War_(2014%E2%80%93present)" title="Iraqi Civil War (2014–present)">Iraqi Civil War (2014–present)</a></div>
<p>Following <a href="/wiki/Islamic_State_of_Iraq_and_the_Levant" title="Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant">ISIL</a>'s successful <a href="/wiki/Anbar_campaign_(2013%E2%80%9314)" title="Anbar campaign (2013–14)">Anbar campaign</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Iraq_offensive_(June_2014)" title="Northern Iraq offensive (June 2014)">June 2014 offensive</a>, the Badr Organization mobilized and won a series of battles against ISIL, including the <a href="/wiki/Liberation_of_Jurf_Al_Sakhar" title="Liberation of Jurf Al Sakhar">Liberation of Jurf Al Sakhar</a> and lift the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Amirli" title="Siege of Amirli">Lifting of the Siege of Amirli</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-breaking_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-breaking-7">[7]</a></sup> In early February 2015 the group, operating from its base at <a href="/wiki/Camp_Ashraf" title="Camp Ashraf">Camp Ashraf</a>, fought in <a href="/wiki/Diyala_Governorate" title="Diyala Governorate">Diyala Governorate</a> against ISIL. Over 100 militia were killed in the fighting including 25 in <a href="/wiki/Al_Mansouryah" title="Al Mansouryah">Al Mansouryah</a>. Badr's leader, <a href="/wiki/Hadi_Al-Amiri" title="Hadi Al-Amiri">Hadi Al-Amiri</a>, said his militiamen were committed to the safety of Sunnis, but deep mutual suspicions remained in the light of recent sectarian killings and suspicion that some Sunni tribes were allied with IS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTY02715_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NTY02715-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Badr_Organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Badr Corps consists of infantry, armor, artillery, anti-aircraft, and commando units with an estimated strength of between 10,000 and 50,000 men (according to the Badr Organization).</p>
