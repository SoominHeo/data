<p><b>Bárid mac Ímair</b> (also referred to as <b>Barith</b>, <b>Baraid</b>, and <b>Bard</b>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" title="Old Norse language">Old Norse</a>: <span lang="non" xml:lang="non"><i>Bárðr</i></span> or <i>Bárǫðr</i>; d. 881) was a ninth-century <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Dublin" title="Kingdom of Dublin">King of Dublin</a>. He was a son of <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Dmar" title="Ímar">Ímar</a> and a member of the <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_%C3%8Dmair" title="Uí Ímair">Uí Ímair</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1rid_mac_%C3%8Dmair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The earliest mention of Bárid in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_Annals" title="Irish Annals">Irish Annals</a> is in part of a saga embedded within the <i><a href="/wiki/Fragmentary_Annals_of_Ireland" title="Fragmentary Annals of Ireland">Fragmentary Annals of Ireland</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[nb 1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D247_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-D247-3">[2]</a></sup> In this entry, dated 867, Bárid is named as a <a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">Jarl</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lochlann" title="Lochlann">Lochlann</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[nb 2]</a></sup> who, along with a Jarl Háimar was ambushed by men of <a href="/wiki/Connacht" title="Connacht">Connacht</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[4]</a></sup> Bárid is mentioned again by a saga element within the <i>Fragmentary Annals</i> in 872, when he is said to have raided <a href="/wiki/Magh_Luirg" title="Magh Luirg">Moylurg</a> and the islands of <a href="/wiki/Lough_Ree" title="Lough Ree">Lough Ree</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[5]</a></sup> This saga element also says that Bárid fostered a son of <a href="/wiki/%C3%81ed_Findliath" title="Áed Findliath">Áed Findliath</a>, overking of the Northern <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_N%C3%A9ill" title="Uí Néill">Uí Néill</a>. The sagas are usually considered of dubious historical value, but this particular element draws upon earlier written accounts, and there is much evidence for later links between the descendants of Áed Findliath and the <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_%C3%8Dmair" title="Uí Ímair">Uí Ímair</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D24_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-D24-8">[6]</a></sup> Fosterage was used in Ireland as a means of strengthening ties between different ruling families, and it is possible Bárid may have tried to integrate himself with the Irish political elite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D24_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-D24-8">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bárid is mentioned by the <i><a href="/wiki/Annals_of_Inisfallen" title="Annals of Inisfallen">Annals of Inisfallen</a></i> in 873 which say:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">Bárid with a great fleet from <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Áth Cliath</a> [went] by sea westwards, and he plundered <a href="/wiki/Ciarraige" title="Ciarraige">Ciarraige Luachra</a> under ground, i.e. the raiding of the caves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AI873_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-AI873-9">[7]</a></sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p><a href="#Downham">Downham</a> suggests this raid was undertaken as a show of strength; it occurred shortly after the death of <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Dmar" title="Ímar">Ímar</a>, with Bárid probably succeeding him as <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Dublin" title="Kingdom of Dublin">King of Dublin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D24_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-D24-8">[6]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Cogad_G%C3%A1edel_re_Gallaib" title="Cogad Gáedel re Gallaib">Cogad Gáedel re Gallaib</a></i> names a son of Amlaíb, most likely <a href="/wiki/Oistin_mac_Amla%C3%ADb" title="Oistin mac Amlaíb">Oistin</a>, as raiding with him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[nb 3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SB36CGG25_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-SB36CGG25-11">[8]</a></sup> It has been suggested that Bárid and his cousin Oistin ruled together as co-kings following the death of Ímar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the Annals of Ulster, in 875 Oistin was "deceitfully" killed by "Albann", a figure generally agreed to be <a href="/wiki/Halfdan_Ragnarsson" title="Halfdan Ragnarsson">Halfdan Ragnarsson</a>, supposed son of the legendary Viking <a href="/wiki/Ragnar_Lodbrok" title="Ragnar Lodbrok">Ragnar Lodbrok</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[nb 4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[11]</a></sup> Halfdan is sometimes considered a brother of Ímar, and this conflict may have been an attempt by Halfdan to claim Dublin for his own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-C-13">[10]</a></sup> It seems he was not successful in pressing his claim, but he tried to take Dublin again in 877, and he fell in battle against an army of "<a href="/wiki/Dubgaill_and_Finngaill" title="Dubgaill and Finngaill">fair foreigners</a>" at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Strangford_Lough" title="Battle of Strangford Lough">Battle of Strangford Lough</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[12]</a></sup> <i>Cogad Gáedel re Gallaib</i> identifies Bárid as the leader of the "fair foreigners", and as been being wounded "so that he was lame ever after".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SB36CGG25_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-SB36CGG25-11">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The next mention of Bárid in the annals comes in 881, when the <i>Annals of Ulster</i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Annals_of_the_Four_Masters" title="Annals of the Four Masters">Annals of the Four Masters</a></i>, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Chronicon_Scotorum" title="Chronicon Scotorum">Chronicon Scotorum</a></i> describe his death; he was killed and burnt in Dublin shortly after raiding <a href="/wiki/Duleek" title="Duleek">Duleek</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D247_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-D247-3">[2]</a></sup> The annals attribute his death to a miracle of <a href="/wiki/Cian%C3%A1n" title="Cianán">Saint Cianán</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1rid_mac_%C3%8Dmair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bárid's father is identified by the <i>Chronicon Scotorum</i> as Ímar, King of Dublin until his death in 873.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[14]</a></sup> Ímar is sometimes identified with <a href="/wiki/Ivar_the_Boneless" title="Ivar the Boneless">Ivar the Boneless</a>, son of the legendary Viking <a href="/wiki/Ragnar_Lodbrok" title="Ragnar Lodbrok">Ragnar Lodbrok</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[15]</a></sup> The same entry identifies him as "the head of the Northmen". The known brothers of Bárid were <a href="/wiki/Sichfrith_mac_%C3%8Dmair" title="Sichfrith mac Ímair">Sichfrith</a> (died 888) and <a href="/wiki/Sitriuc_mac_%C3%8Dmair" title="Sitriuc mac Ímair">Sitriuc</a> (died 896).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bárid is identified as the father of Uathmarán, who bore an Irish name derived from the Irish word "uathmar", meaning ‘awesome’, perhaps in an attempt to associate with the Irish political elite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D25_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-D25-21">[17]</a></sup> Bárid may also be identified as the father of Eloir mac Báirid (died 891), and the grandfather of the unnamed son of Uathmarán mac Bárid (fl. 921).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[18]</a></sup> This unnamed man may be identical to Sichfrith mac Uathmaráin (fl. 932).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[19]</a></sup> It is uncertain whether Bárid was the father of the unnamed son of Bárid (mac Bárid in the original <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">Old Irish</a>) who plundered <i>Cill Clethi</i> in 937. This unnamed man may be identical to Aric mac Báirith (died 937).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[20]</a></sup> Likewise, it is uncertain if Bárid was the father of <a href="/wiki/Colla_mac_B%C3%A1irid" title="Colla mac Báirid">Colla mac Báirid</a> (fl. 924).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[21]</a></sup> Any or all of the aforementioned Aric, Colla, and the unnamed son of Bárid, could have been sons of <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A1rid_mac_Oitir" title="Bárid mac Oitir">Bárid mac Oitir</a> (died 914), not Bárid mac Ímair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[22]</a></sup></p>
