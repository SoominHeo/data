<p>The <b>University of Warsaw</b> (<a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>: <span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl"><i>Uniwersytet Warszawski</i></span>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Universitas Varsoviensis</i></span>), established in 1816, is the largest<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uw.edu_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-uw.edu-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a> in <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>. It employs over 6,000 staff including over 3,100 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academic" title="Academic">academic</a> educators. It provides graduate courses for 53,000 students (on top of over 9,200 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postgraduate" title="Postgraduate">postgraduate</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doctoral" title="Doctoral">doctoral</a> candidates). The University offers some 37 different fields of study, 18 faculties and over 100 specializations in <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">Humanities</a>, technical as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_Science" title="Natural Science">Natural Sciences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uw.edu_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-uw.edu-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was founded as a Royal University on 19 November 1816, when the <a href="/wiki/Partitions_of_Poland" title="Partitions of Poland">Partitions of Poland</a> separated <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a> from the oldest and most influential <a href="/wiki/Jagiellonian_University" title="Jagiellonian University">Jagiellonian University</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w" title="Kraków">Kraków</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tsar_Alexander_I" title="Tsar Alexander I">Tsar Alexander I</a> granted permission for the establishment of five faculties – <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a> and political science, <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a> and the humanities. The university expanded rapidly, but was closed during <a href="/wiki/November_Uprising" title="November Uprising">November Uprising</a> in 1830. It was reopened in 1857 under the name <i>Akademia Medyko-Chirurgiczna</i> (Academy of Medicine) based in the nearby <a href="/wiki/Staszic_Palace" title="Staszic Palace">Staszic Palace</a> with only medical and pharmaceutical faculties. All Polish-language campuses were closed in 1869 after the failed <a href="/wiki/January_Uprising" title="January Uprising">January Uprising</a>, but the university managed to train 3,000 students, many of whom were important part of the Polish intelligentsia, meanwhile the Main Building was reopened as the Imperial Russian University aimed at training military personnel. Over 70% of students were of Polish nationality, but after the revolution in 1905, the proportion dropped below 10% as a result of the boycott. The university was resurrected during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a> and the number of students in 1918 was estimated at 4,500. After Poland's independence in 1918 the new government focused on improving the university, and in the early 1930s it became the country's largest. New faculties were established and the curriculum was extended. The university was named after Chief-of-State and <a href="/wiki/Marshal_of_Poland" title="Marshal of Poland">Marshal of Poland</a> <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B3zef_Pi%C5%82sudski" title="Józef Piłsudski">Józef Piłsudski</a> after his death in 1935. Following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Planned_destruction_of_Warsaw" title="Planned destruction of Warsaw">devastation of Warsaw</a>, the University successfully reopened in 1945.</p>
<p>Today, the University of Warsaw consists of 126 buildings and educational complexes with over 18 faculties: <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Journalism" title="Journalism">journalism</a> and political science, <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a> and regional studies, <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a>, applied <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a> and Slavic philology, <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philology" title="Philology">philology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pedagogy" title="Pedagogy">pedagogy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish language</a>, <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a> and public administration, <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, applied <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_sciences" title="Social sciences">social sciences</a>, management and <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mechanics" title="Mechanics">mechanics</a>.</p>
<p>The University of Warsaw is one of the top Polish universities. It was ranked by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Media_in_Poland" title="Media in Poland">Perspektywy</a> magazine as best Polish university in 2010, 2011 and 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> International rankings such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ARWU" title="ARWU">ARWU</a> and University Web Ranking <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> rank the university as the best Polish higher level institution. On the list of 100 best European universities compiled by University Web Ranking, the University of Warsaw was placed as 61st. <i><a href="/wiki/QS_World_University_Rankings" title="QS World University Rankings">QS World University Rankings</a></i> positioned the University of Warsaw as the best higher level institution among the world's top 400.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Warsaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginnings_under_Alexander_I">Beginnings under Alexander I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Warsaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Beginnings under Alexander I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1795 the <a href="/wiki/Partitions_of_Poland" title="Partitions of Poland">partitions of Poland</a> left <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a> with access only to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_of_Vilnius" title="Academy of Vilnius">Academy of Vilnius</a>; the oldest and most influential Polish academic center, <a href="/wiki/Jagiellonian_University" title="Jagiellonian University">Jagiellonian University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w" title="Kraków">Kraków</a>, became part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habsburg_Austria" title="Habsburg Austria">Habsburg Austria</a>. In 1815, the newly established autonomous <a href="/wiki/Congress_Poland" title="Congress Poland">Congress Poland</a> de facto belonging to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Russian_Empire" title="The Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a> found itself without a university at all, as Vilnius was incorporated into Russia. The first to be established in Congress Poland were the Law School and the Medical School. In 1816 <a href="/wiki/Tsar" title="Tsar">Tsar</a> <a href="/wiki/Alexander_I_of_Russia" title="Alexander I of Russia">Alexander I</a> permitted the Polish authorities to create a university, comprising five departments: Law and Administration, Medicine, Philosophy, Theology, and Art and Humanities. The university soon grew to 800 students and 50 professors. After most of the students and professors took part in the <a href="/wiki/November_Uprising" title="November Uprising">November 1830 Uprising</a> the university was closed down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> After the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_War" title="Crimean War">Crimean War</a>, Russia entered a brief period of liberalization, and the permission was given to create a Polish medical and surgical academy (<i>Akademia Medyko-Chirurgiczna</i>) in Warsaw. In 1862 departments of Law and Administration, Philology and History, and Mathematics and Physics were opened. The newly established academy gained importance and was soon renamed the "Main School" (<i>Szkoła Główna</i>). However, after the <a href="/wiki/January_Uprising" title="January Uprising">January 1863 Uprising</a> the liberal period ended and all <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish-language</a> schools were closed down again. During its short existence, the Main School educated over 3,000 students, many of whom became part of the backbone of the Polish <a href="/wiki/Intelligentsia" title="Intelligentsia">intelligentsia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Main School was replaced with a Russian-language "Imperial University of Warsaw". Its purpose was to provide education for the Russian military garrison of Warsaw, the majority of students (up to 70% out of an average of 1,500 to 2,000 students) were Poles. The tsarist authorities believed that the Russian university would become a perfect way to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russify" title="Russify">Russify</a> Polish society and spent a significant sum on building a new university campus. However, various underground organizations soon started to grow and the students became their leaders in Warsaw. Most notable of these groups (the supporters of Polish revival and the <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialists</a>) joined the ranks of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1905_Revolution" title="1905 Revolution">1905 Revolution</a>. Afterwards a <a href="/wiki/Boycott" title="Boycott">boycott</a> of Russian educational facilities was proclaimed and the number of Polish students dropped to below 10%. Most of the students who wanted to continue their education left for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galicia_(Central_Europe)" title="Galicia (Central Europe)">Galicia</a> and Western Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uw.edu.pl_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-uw.edu.pl-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the fall of the January Uprising (1863–1864), the Tsarist authorities' decided to convert the Main School into a Russian-language university, which functioned under the name of Imperial University for 46 years. There were two times when the question of moving the university into Russia was considered. During the 1905–1907 revolution, such a proposal was made by some of the professors, in the face of a boycott of the university by Polish students. Talks on that subject were conducted with a number of Russian cities, including <a href="/wiki/Voronezh" title="Voronezh">Voronezh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saratov" title="Saratov">Saratov</a>. The Russian government finally decided to keep a university in Warsaw, but as a result of the boycott, the university was Russian not only in the sense of the language used, but also of the nationality of its professors and students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uw.edu.pl_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-uw.edu.pl-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>For the second time the question emerged during the First World War, when the military and political situation forced the Russian authorities to evacuate. Beginning from the autumn of 1915, there were two Universities of Warsaw: one Polish, in Warsaw, and another Russian, in <a href="/wiki/Rostov-on-Don" title="Rostov-on-Don">Rostov-on-Don</a> which functioned until 1917. On 5 May 1917 the Russian Provisional Government decided to close the University of Warsaw. The decision took effect on 1 July 1917; on the same day, the University of the Don, now called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rostov_State_University" title="Rostov State University">Rostov State University</a> (<a href="/wiki/Southern_Federal_University" title="Southern Federal University">Southern Federal University</a> since 2006), was inaugurated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>During World War I Warsaw was seized by Germany in 1915. In order to win the Poles for their case and secure the Polish area behind the front lines the governments of Germany and <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a> allowed for a certain liberalization of life in Poland. In accordance with the concept of <i><a href="/wiki/Mitteleuropa" title="Mitteleuropa">Mitteleuropa</a></i>, German military authorities permitted several Polish social and educational societies to be recreated. One of these was University of Warsaw. The Polish language was reintroduced, and the professors were allowed to return to work. In order not to let the Polish patriotic movement out of control the number of lecturers was kept low (usually not more than 50), but there were no limits on the number of students. Until 1918 their number rose from a mere 1,000 to over 4,500.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_Polish_Republic">Second Polish Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Warsaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Second Polish Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>After Poland <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independence_of_Poland_Regained#Recovery_of_Statehood" title="Independence of Poland Regained">regained its independence</a> in 1918, the University of Warsaw began to grow very quickly. It was reformed; all the important posts (the <a href="/wiki/Rector_(academia)" title="Rector (academia)">rector</a>, <a href="/wiki/Senate" title="Senate">senate</a>, deans and councils) became democratically elected, and the state spent considerable amounts of money to modernize and equip it. Many professors returned from exile and cooperated in the effort. By the late 1920s the level of education in Warsaw had reached that of western Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the beginning of the 1930s the University of Warsaw had become the largest university in Poland, with over 250 lecturers and 10,000 students. However, the financial problems of the newly reborn state did not allow for free education, and students had to pay a tuition fee for their studies (an average monthly salary, for a year). Also, the number of <a href="/wiki/Scholarship" title="Scholarship">scholarships</a> was very limited, and only approximately 3% of students were able to get one. Despite these economic problems, the University of Warsaw grew rapidly. New <a href="/wiki/Faculty_(division)" title="Faculty (division)">departments</a> were opened, and the main <a href="/wiki/Campus" title="Campus">campus</a> was expanded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-16">[16]</a></sup> After the death of <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B3zef_Pi%C5%82sudski" title="Józef Piłsudski">Józef Piłsudski</a> the senate of the University of Warsaw changed its name to "Józef Piłsudski University of Warsaw" (<i>Uniwersytet Warszawski im. Józefa Piłsudskiego</i>). The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanacja" title="Sanacja">Sanacja</a></i> government proceeded to limit the autonomy of the universities. Professors and students remained divided for the rest of the 1930s as the system of segregated seating for Jewish students, known as <a href="/wiki/Ghetto_benches" title="Ghetto benches">ghetto benches</a>, was implemented customarily, not institutionally, which should be noted; comparable to the era of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_rights_movement" title="Civil rights movement">Civil rights movement</a> in the United States.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Warsaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Poland_(1939)" title="Invasion of Poland (1939)">Polish Defensive War</a> of 1939 the German authorities of the <a href="/wiki/General_Government" title="General Government">General Government</a> closed all the institutions of higher education in Poland. The equipment and most of the laboratories were taken to Germany and divided amongst the <a href="/wiki/List_of_universities_in_Germany" title="List of universities in Germany">German universities</a> while the main campus of the University of Warsaw was turned into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_barracks" title="Military barracks">military barracks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>German racial theories assumed that no education of Poles was needed and the whole nation was to be turned into uneducated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serf" title="Serf">serfs</a> of the German race. Education in Polish was banned and punished with death. However, many professors organized the so-called "Secret University of Warsaw" (<i>Tajny Uniwersytet Warszawski</i>). The lectures were held in small groups in private apartments and the attendants were constantly risking discovery and death. However, the net of underground faculties spread rapidly and by 1944 there were more than 300 lecturers and 3,500 students at various courses.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Many students took part in the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Uprising" title="Warsaw Uprising">Warsaw Uprising</a> as soldiers of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armia_Krajowa" title="Armia Krajowa">Armia Krajowa</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Szare_Szeregi" title="Szare Szeregi">Szare Szeregi</a>. The German-held campus of the University was turned into a fortified area with bunkers and <a href="/wiki/Machine_gun" title="Machine gun">machine gun</a> nests. It was located close to the buildings occupied by the German garrison of Warsaw. Heavy fights for the campus started on the first day of the Uprising, but the partisans were not able to break through the gates. Several assaults were bloodily repelled and the campus remained in German hands until the end of the fights. During the uprising and the occupation 63 professors were killed, either during fights or as an effect of German policy of extermination of Polish <a href="/wiki/Intelligentsia" title="Intelligentsia">intelligentsia</a>. The University lost 60% of its buildings during the fighting in 1944. A large part of the collection of priceless works of art and books donated to the University was either destroyed or transported to Germany, never to return.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_People.27s_Republic">In the People's Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Warsaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In the People's Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>After World War II it was not clear whether the university would be restored or whether Warsaw itself would be rebuilt. However, many professors who had survived the war returned, and began organizing the university from scratch. In December 1945, lectures resumed for almost 4,000 students in the ruins of the campus, and the buildings were gradually rebuilt. Until the late 1940s the university remained relatively independent. However, soon the communist authorities started to impose political controls, and the period of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stalinism_in_Poland" title="Stalinism in Poland">Stalinism</a> started. Many professors were arrested by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urz%C4%85d_Bezpiecze%C5%84stwa" title="Urząd Bezpieczeństwa">Urząd Bezpieczeństwa (Secret Police)</a>, the books were <a href="/wiki/Censorship" title="Censorship">censored</a> and ideological criteria in employment of new lecturers and admission of students were introduced. On the other hand, education in Poland became free of charge and the number of young people to receive the state <a href="/wiki/Scholarship" title="Scholarship">scholarships</a> reached 60% of all the students. After <a href="/wiki/W%C5%82adys%C5%82aw_Gomu%C5%82ka" title="Władysław Gomułka">Władysław Gomułka</a>'s rise to power in 1956, a brief period of liberalization ensued, though communist ideology still played a major role in most faculties (especially in such faculties as history, law, economics and political science). International cooperation was resumed and the level of education rose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>By mid-1960s the government started to suppress freedom of thought, which led to increasing unrest among the students. A political struggle within the communist party prompted <a href="/wiki/Zenon_Kliszko" title="Zenon Kliszko">Zenon Kliszko</a> to ban the production of <i>Dziady</i> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mickiewicz" title="Mickiewicz">Mickiewicz</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Polish_Theatre_in_Warsaw" title="Polish Theatre in Warsaw">Polish Theatre</a>, leading to <a href="/wiki/1968_Polish_political_crisis" title="1968 Polish political crisis">1968 Polish political crisis</a> coupled with anti-Zionist and anti-democratic campaign and the outbreak of student demonstrations in Warsaw, which were brutally crushed – not by police, but by the <a href="/wiki/ORMO" title="ORMO">ORMO</a> <i>reserve militia</i> squads of plain-clothed workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipn.gov-1_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipn.gov-1-19">[19]</a></sup> As a result, a large number of students and professors were expelled from the university. Nonetheless, the University remained the centre of free thought and education. What professors could not say during lectures, they expressed during informal meetings with their students. Many of them became leaders and prominent members of the <a href="/wiki/Solidarity_(Polish_trade_union)" title="Solidarity (Polish trade union)">Solidarity</a> movement and other societies of the democratic opposition which led to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collapse_of_communism" title="Collapse of communism">collapse of communism</a>. The scientists working at the University of Warsaw were also among the most prominent printers of books forbidden by <a href="/wiki/Censorship" title="Censorship">censorship</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>University of Warsaw owns a total of 126 buildings. Further construction and a vigorous renovation program is underway at the main <a href="/wiki/Campus" title="Campus">campus</a>. The university is spread out over the city, though most of the buildings are concentrated in two areas.</p>

<p>The main <a href="/wiki/Campus" title="Campus">campus</a> of the University of Warsaw is in the city center, adjacent to the <a href="/wiki/Krakowskie_Przedmie%C5%9Bcie" title="Krakowskie Przedmieście">Krakowskie Przedmieście</a> street. It comprises several historic <a href="/wiki/Palace" title="Palace">palaces</a>, most of which had been nationalized in the 19th century. The chief buildings include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kazimierzowski_Palace" title="Kazimierzowski Palace">Kazimierzowski Palace</a> (<i>Pałac Kazimierzowski</i>) – the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Rector_(academia)" title="Rector (academia)">rector</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Senate" title="Senate">senate</a>;</li>
<li><a class="extiw" href="https://pl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pa%C5%82ac_Uruskich" title="pl:Pałac Uruskich">Uruski Palace</a> (<i>Pałac Uruskich</i>) – left side of main gate entrance, houses the Department of Geography and Regional Studies</li>
<li>the Old Library (<i>Stary BUW</i>) – since recent refurbishment, a secondary lecture building;</li>
<li>the Main School (<i>Szkoła Główna</i>) – former seat of the Main School until the <a href="/wiki/January_Uprising" title="January Uprising">January 1863 Uprising</a>, later the faculty of biology; now, since its refurbishment, the seat of the institute of archaeology;</li>
<li><i>Auditorium Maximum</i> – the main lecture hall, with seats for several hundred students.</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_University_Library" title="Warsaw University Library">Warsaw University Library</a> building is a short walk downhill from the main campus, in the <a href="/wiki/Powi%C5%9Ble" title="Powiśle">Powiśle</a> neighborhood.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_sciences_campus">Natural sciences campus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Warsaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Natural sciences campus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The second important campus is located near <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Banach" title="Stefan Banach">Banacha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louis_Pasteur" title="Louis Pasteur">Pasteura</a> streets. It is home to the departments of chemistry, physics, biology, mathematics, computer science and geology, and contains several other university buildings such as the Interdisciplinary Centre for Mathematical and Computational Modelling, the Environmental Heavy Ion Laboratory that houses a cyclotron and a facility for the production of <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission_tomography" title="Positron emission tomography">PET</a> radiopharmaceuticals, and a sports facility. Several new buildings have been constructed within this campus in the recent years, and the Department of Physics moved here from its previous location at Hoża street.</p>
<p>Together with buildings of other institutions, such as the Institute of Experimental Biology, Radium Institute and the <a href="/wiki/Medical_University_of_Warsaw" title="Medical University of Warsaw">Medical University of Warsaw</a>, the campus is part of an almost contiguous area of scientific and educational facilities covering approximately 43 hectares (110 acres).</p>
