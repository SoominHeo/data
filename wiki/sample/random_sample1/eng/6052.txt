<p>The <b>Royal Castle in Warsaw</b> (<a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>: <span lang="pl"><i>Zamek Królewski w Warszawie</i></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castle residency</a> that formerly served throughout the centuries as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_palace" title="Royal palace">official residence</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Polish_rulers" title="List of Polish rulers">Polish monarchs</a>. It is located in the <a href="/wiki/Castle_Square,_Warsaw" title="Castle Square, Warsaw">Castle Square</a>, at the entrance to the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Old_Town" title="Warsaw Old Town">Warsaw Old Town</a>. The personal offices of the king and the administrative offices of the Royal Court of Poland were located there from the sixteenth century until the <a href="/wiki/Partitions_of_Poland" title="Partitions of Poland">Partitions of Poland</a>.</p>
<p>Initially the complex served as the residence of the Dukes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Masovia" title="Masovia">Masovia</a>, and since the sixteenth century, the seat of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish-Lithuanian_Commonwealth" title="Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth">Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth</a>: the King and Parliament (Chamber of Deputies and Senate). In its long history the Royal Castle was repeatedly plundered and devastated by the invading Swedish, Brandenburgian, Prussian and Tsarist armies. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitution_of_3_May_1791" title="Constitution of 3 May 1791">Constitution of 3 May 1791</a>, the first of its type in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and the world's second-oldest codified national constitution after the 1789 U.S. Constitution, was drafted here by the <a href="/wiki/Great_Sejm" title="Great Sejm">Four-Year Sejm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In the 19th century, after the collapse of the <a href="/wiki/November_Uprising" title="November Uprising">November Uprising</a>, it was used as an administrative centre by the <a href="/wiki/Tsar" title="Tsar">Tsar</a> and was re-designed for the needs of the Imperial Russian administration. During the course of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> it was the residence of the German Governor-General. In 1920-1922 the Royal Castle was the seat of the Polish Head of State and between 1926 and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> the building was the residence of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish_president" title="Polish president">Polish president</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ignacy_Mo%C5%9Bcicki" title="Ignacy Mościcki">Ignacy Mościcki</a>.</p>
<p>Burned and looted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazis" title="Nazis">Nazi Germans</a> following the <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Poland" title="Invasion of Poland">Invasion of Poland</a> in 1939 and almost completely destroyed in 1944 after the failed <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Uprising" title="Warsaw Uprising">Warsaw Uprising</a>, the Castle was completely rebuilt and reconstructed; in 1965 the surviving fragments of the castle and the Royal Library, the adjacent <a href="/wiki/Copper-Roof_Palace" title="Copper-Roof Palace">Copper-Roof Palace</a> and the Kubicki Arcades were registered as historical monuments by the government. Reconstruction of the castle carried out in 1971-1984 was led by the Civic Committee, responsible for the reconstruction of Warsaw. It was afforded by mainly US donations. In 1980, the Royal Castle, together with the Old Town was registered as a protected <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">UNESCO World Heritage Site</a>. Today it is a historical and national monument, and is listed as a national museum visited by over 500,000 people every year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Royal Castle, due to its iconic appearance and its long history, is one of Warsaw's most recognizable landmarks.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Castle,_Warsaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Castle_in_the_Middle_Ages">Castle in the Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Castle,_Warsaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Castle in the Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VarsaviaCastelloCorteAlaGotica.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/VarsaviaCastelloCorteAlaGotica.jpg/220px-VarsaviaCastelloCorteAlaGotica.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/VarsaviaCastelloCorteAlaGotica.jpg/330px-VarsaviaCastelloCorteAlaGotica.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/VarsaviaCastelloCorteAlaGotica.jpg/440px-VarsaviaCastelloCorteAlaGotica.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VarsaviaCastelloCorteAlaGotica.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Medieval wall of the <i>Curia Maior</i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the 1339 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papal_Legate" title="Papal Legate">Papal Legate</a> in <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suchodolska_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Suchodolska-4">[4]</a></sup> heard a case brought by the King of <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Casimir_III_the_Great" title="Casimir III the Great">Casimir III the Great</a>, against the German <a href="/wiki/Teutonic_Order" title="Teutonic Order">Teutonic Order</a>. He claimed that they had illegally seized a slice of Polish territory — <a href="/wiki/Pomerania" title="Pomerania">Pomerania</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kujawy" title="Kujawy">Kujawy</a> region. The documents in this case are the earliest written testimony to the existence of <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a>. At that time a fortified town surrounded by earthen and wooden <a href="/wiki/Defensive_wall" title="Defensive wall">ramparts</a>, and situated where the Royal Castle now stands, it was the seat of Trojden, <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Masovia" title="Duke of Masovia">Duke of Masovia</a>. At the end of the 13th century, during the Duke's Conrad II of Mazovia reign, the wooden-earthen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gord_(Slavic_settlement)" title="Gord (Slavic settlement)">gord</a> called Smaller Manor (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la"><i>Curia Minor</i></span>) was built. The next duke, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casimir_I_of_Poland" title="Casimir I of Poland">Casimir I</a>, decided to build the first brick building here at the burg-city's area the Great Tower (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la"><i>Turris Magna</i></span>).</p>
<p>In the middle of the 14th century the Castle Tower, whose structure up to the first storey has survived to this day, was built, while during the reign over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Masovia" title="Masovia">Masovia</a> by Duke <a href="/wiki/Janusz_I_of_Warsaw" title="Janusz I of Warsaw">Janusz I the Elder</a>, the <i>Curia Maior</i> (Big Manor) was erected between 1407 and 1410.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suchodolska_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Suchodolska-4">[4]</a></sup> Its facade, which was still standing in 1944, was <a href="/wiki/Planned_destruction_of_Warsaw" title="Planned destruction of Warsaw">knocked down by the Germans</a>, but has been rebuilt since then. The character of the new residence and its size (47.5 m/14.5 m) decided the change of the buildings status, and from 1414 it functioned as a Prince Manor.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance_period">Renaissance period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Castle,_Warsaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Renaissance period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Plan_Castle_Warsaw_17th.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1309" data-file-width="1342" height="215" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Plan_Castle_Warsaw_17th.jpg/220px-Plan_Castle_Warsaw_17th.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Plan_Castle_Warsaw_17th.jpg/330px-Plan_Castle_Warsaw_17th.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Plan_Castle_Warsaw_17th.jpg/440px-Plan_Castle_Warsaw_17th.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Plan_Castle_Warsaw_17th.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Plan of the Royal Castle in Warsaw at the beginning of the 17th century.</div>
</div>

<p>When the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Masovia" title="Masovia">Masovia</a> region was incorporated in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Poland_(1385%E2%80%931569)" title="Kingdom of Poland (1385–1569)">Kingdom of Poland</a> in 1526, the edifice, which until then had been the Castle of the Dukes of Masovia, became one of the royal residences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wrede_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wrede-5">[5]</a></sup> From 1548 onwards Queen <a href="/wiki/Bona_Sforza" title="Bona Sforza">Bona Sforza</a> resided in it with her daughters <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isabella_Jagie%C5%82%C5%82o" title="Isabella Jagiełło">Izabela</a>, who became Queen of <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Jagiellon" title="Catherine Jagiellon">Catherine</a>, later to become Queen of Sweden, and <a href="/wiki/Anna_Jagiellon" title="Anna Jagiellon">Anna Jagiellon</a>, later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen_of_Poland" title="Queen of Poland">Queen of Poland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wrede_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wrede-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1556–1557 and in 1564 the King of Poland, <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_II_Augustus" title="Sigismund II Augustus">Sigismund II Augustus</a>, convened royal parliaments in <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a>. They met in the Castle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gessner_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gessner-6">[6]</a></sup> Following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lublin_Union" title="Lublin Union">Lublin Union</a> (1569), by which the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish_Crown" title="Polish Crown">Polish Crown</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Lithuania" title="Grand Duchy of Lithuania">Grand Duchy of Lithuania</a> - were united as a single country, Warsaw Castle was regularly the place where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sejm_walny" title="Sejm walny">parliament</a> of the Two-Nations State met.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wrede_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wrede-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1569–1572 King <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_II_Augustus" title="Sigismund II Augustus">Sigismund II Augustus</a> started alterations in the Castle, the architects being <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_di_Quadro" title="Giovanni Battista di Quadro">Giovanni Battista di Quadro</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dziedzictwo_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-dziedzictwo-7">[7]</a></sup> and Giacopo Pario.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wrede_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wrede-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Curia Maior</i> was altered so as provide a meeting place for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sejm_walny" title="Sejm walny">Parliament</a>, with premises for the Chamber of Deputies (<i>Sejm</i> - delegates of the gentry) on the ground floor (<i>the Old Chamber of Deputies</i>), and the Senate Chamber (where the Senators debated in the presence of the King) on the first floor. This was one of the first attempts in Europe to create a building that would be used solely for parliamentary purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wrede_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Wrede-5">[5]</a></sup> The parliamentary character of the <i>Curia Maior</i> is stressed by the paintings of the facade — the coats-of-arms of <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, of <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a>, and of the various regions from which the delegates were elected. A new <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>—style building, known as the "Royal House", was erected next to the <i>Curia Maior</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wrede_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Wrede-5">[5]</a></sup> The king resided there when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sejm_walny" title="Sejm walny">parliament</a> was in session.</p>

<p>The next alterations to the Castle were made in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_III_Vasa" title="Sigismund III Vasa">Sigismund III</a>, who transferred the royal residence from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cracow" title="Cracow">Cracow</a> to <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wrede1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wrede1-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1598–1619 the Castle was enlarged. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giovanni_Trevano" title="Giovanni Trevano">Giovanni Trevano</a> was in charge of the reconstruction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wrede1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wrede1-8">[8]</a></sup> His plans were probably amended by the Venetian architect <a href="/wiki/Vincenzo_Scamozzi" title="Vincenzo Scamozzi">Vincenzo Scamozzi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-perelki_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-perelki-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Between 1601–1603 Giacomo Rodondo<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wrede1_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wrede1-8">[8]</a></sup> finished the new northern wing. From 1602 Paolo del Corte<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wrede1_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wrede1-8">[8]</a></sup> was doing stonework. Later after 1614, when Matteo Castelli<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wrede1_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Wrede1-8">[8]</a></sup> took the lead, the western wing was built (from today's <a href="/wiki/Castle_Square,_Warsaw" title="Castle Square, Warsaw">Plac Zamkowy</a> side) as chancelleries and a marshals office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dziedzictwo_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-dziedzictwo-7">[7]</a></sup> The southern wing was built at the end. In that way five-wings in a <a href="/wiki/Mannerist_architecture_and_sculpture_in_Poland" title="Mannerist architecture and sculpture in Poland">mannerist</a>-early baroque style were built. In 1619 the New Royal Tower (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la"><i>Nova Turris Regia</i></span>) also called Sigismund's Tower was finished. It was 60 meters high and was placed in the middle of a newly built west castle 90 meters in length. At the top of the tower, a clock with gilded hands and copper face was placed. The new tower's spire was 13 meters high and had glided knobs and a copper flag at the top.</p>
<p>On 29 October 1611 in the Senator's Chamber, Tsar <a href="/wiki/Vasili_IV_of_Russia" title="Vasili IV of Russia">Vasili IV of Russia</a>, who had been captured by the hetman <a href="/wiki/Stanis%C5%82aw_%C5%BB%C3%B3%C5%82kiewski" title="Stanisław Żółkiewski">Stanisław Żółkiewski</a>, paid homage to the Polish king <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_III_Vasa" title="Sigismund III Vasa">Sigismund III Vasa</a>.</p>
<p>The Polish king <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_III_Vasa" title="Sigismund III Vasa">Sigismund III</a> and his successors of the Vasa dynasty — <a href="/wiki/W%C5%82adys%C5%82aw_IV_Vasa" title="Władysław IV Vasa">Władysław IV Vasa</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_II_Casimir_Vasa" title="John II Casimir Vasa">John II Casimir Vasa</a> — collected many rich works of art in the castle, such as oriental fabrics, tapestries, and numerous paintings by such famous artists as <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Veronese" title="Paolo Veronese">Veronese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacopo_Bassano" title="Jacopo Bassano">Jacopo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leandro_Bassano" title="Leandro Bassano">Leandro Bassano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tintoretto" title="Tintoretto">Tintoretto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palma_il_Giovane" title="Palma il Giovane">Palma il Giovane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Vassilacchi" title="Antonio Vassilacchi">Antonio Vassilacchi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tommaso_Dolabella" title="Tommaso Dolabella">Tommaso Dolabella</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guercino" title="Guercino">Guercino</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tarandaite_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tarandaite-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Guido_Reni" title="Guido Reni">Guido Reni</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tarandaite_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tarandaite-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Heintz_the_Elder" title="Joseph Heintz the Elder">Joseph Heintz</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tarandaite_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tarandaite-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bartholomeus_Spranger" title="Bartholomeus Spranger">Bartholomeus Spranger</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tarandaite_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Tarandaite-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roelant_Savery" title="Roelant Savery">Roelant Savery</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tarandaite_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Tarandaite-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rembrandt" title="Rembrandt">Rembrandt</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tarandaite_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-Tarandaite-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pieter_Soutman" title="Pieter Soutman">Pieter Soutman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Danckerts_de_Rij" title="Peter Danckerts de Rij">Peter Danckerts de Rij</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">Peter Paul Rubens</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tarandaite_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-Tarandaite-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jan_Brueghel_the_Elder" title="Jan Brueghel the Elder">Jan Brueghel the Elder</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tarandaite_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-Tarandaite-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Seghers" title="Daniel Seghers">Daniel Seghers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georg_Daniel_Schultz" title="Georg Daniel Schultz">Georg Daniel Schultz</a> and sculptures by <a href="/wiki/Giambologna" title="Giambologna">Giambologna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Francesco_Susini" title="Giovanni Francesco Susini">Giovanni Francesco Susini</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adriaen_de_Vries" title="Adriaen de Vries">Adriaen de Vries</a>. These splendid works of art were either destroyed or plundered during the invasions of Poland by Sweden and Germans of <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg" title="Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a> during (<a href="/wiki/Deluge_(history)" title="Deluge (history)">The Deluge</a>, in 1655–1657).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wrede1_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-Wrede1-8">[8]</a></sup> The Swedes and Germans took all the priceless pictures, furniture, tapestries, the royal library, the crown archive, numerous sculptures, whole floors and royal flags.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dziedzictwo_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-dziedzictwo-7">[7]</a></sup> In the castle they had a military <i>Lazareth</i> field hospital, which additionally contributed to the devastation of the buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wrede1_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-Wrede1-8">[8]</a></sup> A few months later armies destroyed the rest, plundering most of the copper elements and tearing up the rest of castle's floor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wrede1_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-Wrede1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The majority of the preserved castle furnishings from the Vasa period found its place in the collection of the Visitationist Monastery in Warsaw as donations from the last Vasa, John II Casimir and his wife <a href="/wiki/Marie_Louise_Gonzaga" title="Marie Louise Gonzaga">Marie Louise Gonzaga</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fryz_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fryz-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1628 the first <a href="/wiki/Polish_opera" title="Polish opera">Polish opera</a> - <i>Galatea</i>, was staged at the Castle. The great <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">opera</a> hall (double-storied, over 50m long), which existed at the Royal Castle,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wrede1_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-Wrede1-8">[8]</a></sup> was demolished by Swedes and Germans and rebuilt in the 1660s by King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_II_Casimir" title="John II Casimir">John II Casimir</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-opera_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-opera-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1657 the reconstruction of the castle started, under the Italian architect Izydor Affait's guidance. Because of the lack of money the following Polish king, <a href="/wiki/Micha%C5%82_Korybut_Wi%C5%9Bniowiecki" title="Michał Korybut Wiśniowiecki">Michael I Korybut</a> did not decide on radical rebuilding, just limiting himself to rebuilding destroyed buildings. Because of bad conditions of the residence he had to move to <a href="/wiki/Ujazd%C3%B3w_Castle" title="Ujazdów Castle">Ujazdów Castle</a> in 1669. Until 1696 when the next Polish king, <a href="/wiki/John_III_Sobieski" title="John III Sobieski">John III Sobieski</a> died, no serious works were done. They only limited work to current inspections of the building's condition. Sessions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sejm_walny" title="Sejm walny">Parliament</a> continued to be held in the castle, as well as various State occasions, such as when the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Hohenzollern" title="House of Hohenzollern">Hohenzollern Dukes of Prussia</a> paid homage to the Kings of Poland and occasions when the king received the ambassadors of foreign countries.</p>
<p>After choosing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustus_II" title="Augustus II">Augustus II</a> in an election in 1697, the castle again began to deteriorate. A new conflict with the King of Sweden, <a href="/wiki/Charles_XII_of_Sweden" title="Charles XII of Sweden">Charles XII</a> significantly limited the king's budget. Despite problems, in 1698 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustus_II" title="Augustus II">Augustus II</a> commissioned a residence reconstruction project. In 1700 it was done by Johann Friedrich Karcher, who came from abroad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saxons_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-saxons-13">[13]</a></sup> On 25 May 1702 the Swedes re-seized the Royal Castle in Warsaw, creating a hospital with 500 beds, and into The Chamber of Deputies and ministers’ rooms, they placed a stable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saxons_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-saxons-13">[13]</a></sup> During the Polish army's siege in 1704 the castle was retaken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saxons_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-saxons-13">[13]</a></sup> However, it was soon retaken once more by Sweden's army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saxons_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-saxons-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1707, by virtue of the peace treaty between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustus_II" title="Augustus II">Augustus II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_XII_of_Sweden" title="Charles XII of Sweden">Charles XII of Sweden</a>, Russian allied troops entered Warsaw, and Tsar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_I_of_Russia" title="Peter I of Russia">Peter I of Russia</a> settled in the castle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saxons_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-saxons-13">[13]</a></sup> After two months, Russian forces were removed from Warsaw, taking with them works of art from the castle, including <a href="/wiki/Tommaso_Dolabella" title="Tommaso Dolabella">Tommaso Dolabella</a>'s pictures, which included two that were very important for Russians: <i>The Defense of Smolensk</i> and <i>Russian Tsar Vasili IV compelled to kneel before Polish King Sigismund III of Poland</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saxons_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-saxons-13">[13]</a></sup> The Władysław's Opera Hall was completely devastated and was never restored.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-opera_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-opera-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The reconstruction according to Karcher's plans began from 1713-1715.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saxons_13-6"><a href="#cite_note-saxons-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1717 the Parliament Hall was completely rebuilt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saxons_13-7"><a href="#cite_note-saxons-13">[13]</a></sup> It was used to serve the Saxon rulers as a coronation hall. During the following years, between 1722-1723, the other castle halls were converted-under the direction of architect <a href="/wiki/Joachim_Daniel_von_Jauch" title="Joachim Daniel von Jauch">Joachim Daniel von Jauch</a>, the new Senate Chamber was built, and all the furnishings moved from the old to the new location, including among others: 60 Polish provincial emblems, paneling, mouldings and lizens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saxons_13-8"><a href="#cite_note-saxons-13">[13]</a></sup> On 31 May 1732, a fire broke out in the castle destroying the west elevation and part of the Sigismund's Tower and the exterior façade sculptures, known as <a href="/wiki/Armature_(sculpture)" title="Armature (sculpture)">armature</a>.</p>
<p>The next reconstruction project of the Royal Castle appeared after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustus_III" title="Augustus III">Augustus III</a> took to the Polish throne in 1733. New plans, which were formed in 1734 and developed in 1737 by architect <a href="/wiki/Gaetano_Chiaveri" title="Gaetano Chiaveri">Gaetano Chiaveri</a>, saw among other things, the reconstruction of the castle's façade on the <a href="/wiki/Vistula" title="Vistula">Vistula</a> side in the <a href="/wiki/Rococo" title="Rococo">rococo</a> style, which was meant to form a new so called Saxon elevation and also the conversion of the north-east part with the Altana Tower, where it was planned for 3 two-storey rysalits to be built on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-perelki_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-perelki-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saxons_13-9"><a href="#cite_note-saxons-13">[13]</a></sup> The reconstruction work according to these plans was carried out with various intensity between 1740 and 1752. During the period of 1740-1747 the façade on the Vistula side was reconstructed in the late baroque style (architects: Gaetano Chiaveri, Carl Friedrich Pöppelmann, Jan Krzysztof Knöffel).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saxons_13-10"><a href="#cite_note-saxons-13">[13]</a></sup> One of the best sculptors who did work on the castle in this period was <a href="/wiki/Jan_Jerzy_Plersch" title="Jan Jerzy Plersch">Jan Jerzy Plersch</a>, who made the royal decorative frames, mouldings and statues called the Famous Figures, which held the royal crowns on the top of the middle rysalit, of the Saxon elevation, on the Vistula side. The last reconstruction work of this period was finished by late 1763, after the death of Augustus III, when Plersch made the last sculptures and frames with province emblems for the Parliament Hall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saxons_13-11"><a href="#cite_note-saxons-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Stanis.C5.82aw_II_Augustus_period">The Stanisław II Augustus period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Castle,_Warsaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Stanisław II Augustus period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The most splendid period in the history of the Castle was during the rule of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanis%C5%82aw_II_Augustus" title="Stanisław II Augustus">Stanisław II Augustus</a> (1764–1795). This monarch collected exquisite works of art, many of which have survived to this day. He recruited first-rate architects such as <a href="/wiki/Jakub_Fontana" title="Jakub Fontana">Jakub Fontana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Domenico_Merlini" title="Domenico Merlini">Merlini</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jan_Chrystian_Kamsetzer" title="Jan Chrystian Kamsetzer">Kamsetzer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jakub_Kubicki" title="Jakub Kubicki">Kubicki</a>, to work on the castle, as well as splendid painters such as <a href="/wiki/Marcello_Bacciarelli" title="Marcello Bacciarelli">Marcello Bacciarelli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bernardo_Bellotto" title="Bernardo Bellotto">Bernardo Bellotto</a> (otherwise known as Canaletto), <a href="/wiki/Franciszek_Smuglewicz" title="Franciszek Smuglewicz">Franciszek Smuglewicz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kazimierz_Wojniakowski" title="Kazimierz Wojniakowski">Kazimierz Wojniakowski</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Pillement" title="Jean-Baptiste Pillement">Jean-Baptiste Pillement</a> and eminent sculptors such as André le Brun and Jakub Monaldi, and famous French artists such as the architect <a href="/wiki/Victor_Louis" title="Victor Louis">Victor Louis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dziedzictwo_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-dziedzictwo-7">[7]</a></sup> The total reconstruction of the castle planned by the king did not come to fruition, but the interior was changed to the neoclassical style - although this, known in Poland as the <i>Stanisław Augustus style</i>, was rather different from neo—classicism in the rest of Europe.</p>

<p>During 1766-1785 on the basis of Jakub Fontana's plans,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gessner_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gessner-6">[6]</a></sup> the southern wing of the castle, which was burnt on 15 December 1767 was rebuilt (2 destroyed floors, a new elevation on the south side with three rysalits, the division of the façade by lizens and Jon capital pilasters).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-perelki_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-perelki-9">[9]</a></sup> Between 1774 and 1777 the monarch's private apartments were furnished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-perelki_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-perelki-9">[9]</a></sup> They consisted of the Prospect Room (with landscapes by <a href="/wiki/Bernardo_Bellotto" title="Bernardo Bellotto">Canaletto</a>), the Chapel, the Audience Chamber, and the Bedchamber, while between 1779 and 1786 the Senate Apartments were completed, consisting of the Ballroom, the Knights Hall, the Throne Room, the Marble Room, and the Conference Chamber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dziedzictwo_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-dziedzictwo-7">[7]</a></sup> These rooms contained pictures and sculptures depicting great events in Poland's history, as well as portraits of Polish kings, generals, statesmen and scholars (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copernicus" title="Copernicus">Copernicus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adam_Naruszewicz" title="Adam Naruszewicz">Adam Naruszewicz</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gessner_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gessner-6">[6]</a></sup> The Castle also housed the rich royal collections including 3200 pictures, classical statues, about 100 000 graphics, in addition to medals, coins, and a fine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Library_at_the_Royal_Castle,_Warsaw" title="Library at the Royal Castle, Warsaw">library</a>, to house which a separate building was erected in 1780-1784.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biblioteki_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-biblioteki-14">[14]</a></sup> The new library building housed many books, gems, drawings, coins, maps and plans belonging to the monarch. The Royal Library's book collection amounted to 16 000 volumes of various works, 25,525 drawings, 44,842 etchings in 726 bound volumes, overall a number of 70,000 etchings—fancy dress balls were also held in this hall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biblioteki_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-biblioteki-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Up until 1786 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanis%C5%82aw_II_Augustus" title="Stanisław II Augustus">Stanisław II Augustus</a> tried a few times to change the outside decor of the Castle and to build an architectural castle square, he was not however successful in carrying out these plans.</p>
<p>During this period, the Castle was the place where the ideas of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish_Enlightenment" title="Polish Enlightenment">Polish Enlightenment</a> first flourished. The king held "Thursday lunches" at the Castle, for scientists, scholars, writers and artists. This was where the idea for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Education_Commission" title="National Education Commission">National Education Commission</a>; one of the first secular Ministries of Education in Europe, was mooted. The Castle was the place where the first proposals were made for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Szko%C5%82a_Rycerska" title="Szkoła Rycerska">Knights' School</a>, and for a <a href="/wiki/National_Theatre,_Warsaw" title="National Theatre, Warsaw">national theatre</a>. It was in the Senate Chamber in the Castle that what was known as the "<a href="/wiki/Great_Sejm" title="Great Sejm">Great Sejm</a>" (Great Parliament) passed the famous Polish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitution_of_3rd_May" title="Constitution of 3rd May">Constitution of 3rd May</a>, 1791.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gessner_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Gessner-6">[6]</a></sup> During the ceremony the King was carried out to the nearby church of St. John. In honour of this occasion a marble plaque with <a href="/wiki/Ignacy_Krasicki" title="Ignacy Krasicki">Ignacy Krasicki's</a> text written on it was set into the wall of the Castle.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_partitioned_Poland_and_the_Second_Republic">In partitioned Poland and the Second Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Castle,_Warsaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In partitioned Poland and the Second Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Between 19–20 December 1806 and 1–30 January 1807, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">Napoleon Bonaparte</a>, the French emperor, spent his time at the Castle. Here in 1807 he made the decision to form a <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Warsaw" title="Duchy of Warsaw">Warsaw duchy</a>, which was to be ruled by the Saxon prince <a href="/wiki/Frederick_August_I,_Duke_of_Oldenburg" title="Frederick August I, Duke of Oldenburg">Frederick August I</a>, using the Royal castle as his residence. Prince <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B3zef_Poniatowski" title="Józef Poniatowski">Józef Poniatowski</a>, Commander-in-Chief of the Polish Army and Marshal of France, resided in the <a href="/wiki/Copper-Roof_Palace" title="Copper-Roof Palace">Copper-Roof Palace</a> joined to the Castle. After the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Congress_Poland" title="Congress Poland">constitutional Kingdom of Poland</a> (1815) its parliaments met here at the Castle. As Kings of Poland, the Russian Tsars <a href="/wiki/Alexander_I_of_Russia" title="Alexander I of Russia">Alexander I</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_I_of_Russia" title="Nicholas I of Russia">Nicholas I</a> also resided in the castle when they stayed in <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/November_Uprising" title="November Uprising">November Uprising</a>, on 25 January 1831, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sejm_of_the_Republic_of_Poland" title="Sejm of the Republic of Poland">Sejm</a> debating in the castle dethroned <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_I_of_Russia" title="Nicholas I of Russia">Tsar of Russia, Nicholas I</a> as Polish king.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-castles_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-castles-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Conference_room_without_paintings_19th.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1428" data-file-width="1220" height="258" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Conference_room_without_paintings_19th.JPG/220px-Conference_room_without_paintings_19th.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Conference_room_without_paintings_19th.JPG/330px-Conference_room_without_paintings_19th.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Conference_room_without_paintings_19th.JPG/440px-Conference_room_without_paintings_19th.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Conference_room_without_paintings_19th.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Conference Room at the Castle without paintings that were stolen by the Tsarist army.</div>

<p>In 1836, after abolishing the division into voivodeships in the <a href="/wiki/Congress_Poland" title="Congress Poland">Congress Poland</a>, they were replaced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guberniya" title="Guberniya">guberniyas</a>. During that time, the Royal Castle became the residence of the Tsar's governor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ivan_Paskievich" title="Ivan Paskievich">Ivan Paskievich</a>. Paskievich charged Ludvik Corio – a Russian Colonel and architect – with designing new elevations and facades (the west, south and east parts). However, Russian authorities were not satisfied with the new designs and Corio was told to prepare another design – one that would refer to Kubicki's solutions (and his co-workers Lelewel and Thomas). Finally, Corio rebuilt all the elevations and facades in the <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">neoclassical</a> style, but the Saxon Elevation was left the same. After the death of Paskievich in 1856, all the next governors resided in the Royal Castle's Chamberlain's Room. The Russian officials occupied rooms on both floors of the west and north wings of the castle. The governors were heavily guarded by the Russian army. Unfortunately, the living space that was assigned to these soldiers was the Parliamentary Hall, Library and <a href="/wiki/Barracks" title="Barracks">barracks</a> under the Castle. As a result, these were left devastated.</p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/January_Uprising" title="January Uprising">January Uprising</a> in 1863, the Russian army totally destroyed the Royal garden on the Vistula side (which was transformed into the <a href="/wiki/Military_parade" title="Military parade">military parade</a> square), building a few barracks made of brick for stables and <a href="/wiki/Cossacks" title="Cossacks">Cossacks</a>’ barracks. In 1862-1863 some maintenance work was done in the Royal Castle under the supervision of Jerzy Orłowicz, Ludwik Gosławski and Potolov. In 1890 the Saxon Elevation was rebuilt under the supervision of a builder January Kiślański, when the arcades of both viewing galleries, dating back to the August III period, were deformed. The last repair works, which cost 28 000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubles" title="Rubles">rubles</a>, during the reign of Russia, were in 1902 in the rooms which had been occupied by the Russian army.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a> it was the residence of the German military governor. After Poland regained her independence in 1918, the Castle became the residence of the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Poland" title="President of Poland">President of Poland</a>. It was lovingly restored under the guidance of Kazimierz Skórewicz (1920-1928) and <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Szyszko-Bohusz" title="Adolf Szyszko-Bohusz">Adolf Szyszko-Bohusz</a> (until 1939). Under the terms of the Peace Treaty signed with Soviet Russia at <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Riga" title="Peace of Riga">Riga in 1920</a>, works of art and other precious things, including all the castle furnishings, which had been taken away to Russia, were brought back to Poland. As a result, it was possible to restore the historic rooms to their appearance in the reign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanis%C5%82aw_II_Augustus" title="Stanisław II Augustus">Stanisław II Augustus</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="During_World_War_II">During World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Castle,_Warsaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: During World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tmulti tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:164px;max-width:164px">
<div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:162px;max-width:162px">
<div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Royal_Castle_in_Warsaw_-_burning_17.09.1939.jpg"><img alt="" data-file-height="1590" data-file-width="2000" height="127" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/The_Royal_Castle_in_Warsaw_-_burning_17.09.1939.jpg/160px-The_Royal_Castle_in_Warsaw_-_burning_17.09.1939.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/The_Royal_Castle_in_Warsaw_-_burning_17.09.1939.jpg/240px-The_Royal_Castle_in_Warsaw_-_burning_17.09.1939.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/The_Royal_Castle_in_Warsaw_-_burning_17.09.1939.jpg/320px-The_Royal_Castle_in_Warsaw_-_burning_17.09.1939.jpg 2x" width="160"/></a></div>
<div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">Royal Castle in flames following German bombardment, 17 September 1939.</div>
</div>
<div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:162px;max-width:162px">
<div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Castle_Warsaw_1941.JPG"><img alt="" data-file-height="1080" data-file-width="1605" height="108" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Castle_Warsaw_1941.JPG/160px-Castle_Warsaw_1941.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Castle_Warsaw_1941.JPG/240px-Castle_Warsaw_1941.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Castle_Warsaw_1941.JPG/320px-Castle_Warsaw_1941.JPG 2x" width="160"/></a></div>
<div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">Royal Castle in 1941 without roofs, deliberately removed by the Germans to accelerate the devastation process.</div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 17 September 1939, the Castle was shelled by German artillery. The roof and the turrets were destroyed by fire (they were partly restored by the Castle's staff, but later deliberately removed by the Germans).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-destruction_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-destruction-16">[16]</a></sup> The ceiling of the Ballroom collapsed, resulting in the destruction of <a href="/wiki/Marcello_Bacciarelli" title="Marcello Bacciarelli">Bacciarelli</a>'s ceiling <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">fresco</a> <i>The Creation of the World</i> and other rooms were slightly damaged. But immediately after the seizure of Warsaw by the Germans, their occupation troops set to demolish the castle. The more valuable objects, even including the central heating and ventilation installations, were dismantled and taken away to Germany.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Royal_Castle_Warsaw_1945.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1318" data-file-width="1127" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Royal_Castle_Warsaw_1945.jpg/170px-Royal_Castle_Warsaw_1945.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Royal_Castle_Warsaw_1945.jpg/255px-Royal_Castle_Warsaw_1945.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Royal_Castle_Warsaw_1945.jpg/340px-Royal_Castle_Warsaw_1945.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Royal_Castle_Warsaw_1945.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Pro Fide, Lege et Grege</i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 4 October 1939 in Berlin, <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> issued the order to blow up the Royal Castle. On 10 October 1939, special German units, under the supervision of history and art experts (Dr. <a href="/wiki/Dagobert_Frey" title="Dagobert Frey">Dagobert Frey</a>, an art historian at the University of Wrocław; Gustaw Barth, the director of museums in Wrocław, and Dr. Joseph Mühlmann, an art historian from Vienna) started to demount floor, marbles, sculptures and stone elements such as fireplaces or moulds. The artefacts were taken to Germany or stored in <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w" title="Kraków">Kraków</a>'s warehouses. Many of them were also seized by various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> dignitaries who resided in Warsaw. The Castle was totally emptied. Disobeying German orders, despite the danger of being shot, Polish museum staff and experts in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_restoration" title="Art restoration">art restoration</a> managed to save many of the works of art from the castle, as well as fragments of the <a href="/wiki/Stucco" title="Stucco">stucco</a>-work, the parquet floors, the wood panelling, and more which were later used in the reconstruction. The great service done to Poland by Professor <a href="/wiki/Stanis%C5%82aw_Lorentz" title="Stanisław Lorentz">Stanisław Lorentz</a>, in leading this campaign to save the castle's treasures, is well known. <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a> sappers then bored tens of thousands of holes for dynamite charges in the stripped walls.</p>
<p>In 1944, after the collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Uprising" title="Warsaw Uprising">Warsaw Uprising</a>, when hostilities had already ceased, the Germans blew up the Castle's demolished walls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-destruction_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-destruction-16">[16]</a></sup> Leveling the Royal Castle was only a part of a larger plan – the Pabst Plan – the goal of which was to build a monumental Community Hall (ger. <i>Volkshalle</i>) or an equally sizable Congress Hall of NSDAP (National Socialist German Workers Party - ger. <i>Parteivolkshalle</i>) in the Royal Castle's place and to replace the <a href="/wiki/Sigismund%27s_Column" title="Sigismund's Column">Sigismund's Column</a> with the Germania Monument.</p>
<p>A pile of rubble, surmounted by only two fragments of walls, was all that was left of the six-hundred-year-old edifice. On one of these fragments part of the <a href="/wiki/Stucco" title="Stucco">stucco</a> decoration remained, this was a cartouche with the royal version of the motto of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_White_Eagle_(Poland)" title="Order of the White Eagle (Poland)">Order of the White Eagle</a> — "<a href="/wiki/Pro_Fide,_Lege_et_Rege" title="Pro Fide, Lege et Rege">PRO FIDE, LEGE ET REGE</a>" (for Faith, Law, and the Nation - literally translated, the last word means a <i><a href="/wiki/Herd" title="Herd">herd</a></i>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reconstruction">Reconstruction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Castle,_Warsaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reconstruction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arkady_Kubickiego.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3240" data-file-width="4320" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Arkady_Kubickiego.JPG/250px-Arkady_Kubickiego.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Arkady_Kubickiego.JPG/375px-Arkady_Kubickiego.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Arkady_Kubickiego.JPG/500px-Arkady_Kubickiego.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arkady_Kubickiego.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The castle seen from riverside.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Immediately after the end of war in 1945, work started on rescuing the surviving fragments of the castle's walls, foundations, and cellars as well as the fire-blackened walls of the <a href="/wiki/Copper-Roof_Palace" title="Copper-Roof Palace">Copper-Roof Palace</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Library_at_the_Royal_Castle,_Warsaw" title="Library at the Royal Castle, Warsaw">Royal Library</a> building, from further destruction. In 1949 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sejm_of_the_Republic_of_Poland" title="Sejm of the Republic of Poland">Polish Parliament</a> passed a bill to rebuild the Castle as a monument to Polish history and culture. Meanwhile, special architectural designing offices, under Jan Dąbrowski, Piotr Biegański and <a href="/wiki/Jan_Zachwatowicz" title="Jan Zachwatowicz">Jan Zachwatowicz</a>, drew up blueprints for restoring the framework of the building and furnishing the historical rooms. The decision to start work was postponed several times, but was finally taken on 20 January 1971.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-destruction_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-destruction-16">[16]</a></sup> A Civic Committee was set up. Amid universal applause it was decided to rebuild the castle from voluntary contributions. Both, in Poland and abroad fund-raising committees were set up.</p>
<p>By May 1975 the Fund had already reached the 500 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Z%C5%82oty" title="Złoty">złotys</a>. By the same date more than a thousand valuable works of art had been given to the Castle by numerous Poles resident both in Poland and abroad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-destruction_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-destruction-16">[16]</a></sup> Official representatives of other countries have likewise presented to the Castle works of art of great artistic and historic value.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Castle_today">The Castle today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Castle,_Warsaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The Castle today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The imposing façade, built of <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">brick</a> is 90 m long and faces the <a href="/wiki/Castle_Square,_Warsaw" title="Castle Square, Warsaw">Castle Square</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zamki_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-zamki-17">[17]</a></sup> At each end of the façade stands a square tower with a bulbous <a href="/wiki/Spire" title="Spire">spire</a>. The Sigismund's Tower is located in the centre of the main façade, flanked on both sides by the castle. This huge clock tower of 60 m in height designed in the sixteenth century, has always been a symbol of the Polish capital and source of inspiration for the architects of other buildings in Warsaw. Nowadays, the Castle serves as the Museum and is subordinated to the Ministry of Culture and National Heritage. Many official visits and state meetings are also held in the Royal Castle.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interior">Interior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Castle,_Warsaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Interior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rembrandt_Girl_in_a_Picture_Frame.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="664" height="230" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Rembrandt_Girl_in_a_Picture_Frame.jpg/170px-Rembrandt_Girl_in_a_Picture_Frame.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Rembrandt_Girl_in_a_Picture_Frame.jpg/255px-Rembrandt_Girl_in_a_Picture_Frame.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Rembrandt_Girl_in_a_Picture_Frame.jpg/340px-Rembrandt_Girl_in_a_Picture_Frame.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rembrandt_Girl_in_a_Picture_Frame.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>The Girl in a Picture Frame</i> by <a href="/wiki/Rembrandt" title="Rembrandt">Rembrandt</a>. One of two pieces of that artist at the castle from <a href="/wiki/Stanis%C5%82aw_August_Poniatowski" title="Stanisław August Poniatowski">Stanisław Augustus</a>' collection.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The interior consists of many different rooms, all painstakingly restored with as much original exhibits as possible after the destruction of the Second World War.</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jagiellon" title="Jagiellon">Jagiellonian</a> Rooms</li>
</ul>
<p>These rooms, which belonged to the residence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sigismund_Augustus" title="Sigismund Augustus">Sigismund Augustus</a>, are now host to a number of portraits of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jagiellon_dynasty" title="Jagiellon dynasty">Jagiellon dynasty</a>, a royal dynasty originating in <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a> that reigned in some Central European countries between the 14th and 16th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 2011 the Jagiellonian Rooms were re-arranged to house the modern Gallery of Painting, Sculpture and Decorative Arts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>The Houses of Parliament</li>
</ul>
<p>From the 16th century onwards, Polish <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a> started here.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poselska_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-poselska-20">[20]</a></sup> In 1573, amendments to the constitution of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish-Lithuanian_Commonwealth" title="Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth">Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth</a> were written here, with great religious tolerance. Also, during the <a href="/wiki/Deluge_(history)" title="Deluge (history)">Deluge</a> in 1652, the <a href="/wiki/Liberum_veto" title="Liberum veto">liberum veto</a> was established in these rooms, although not carried out until 1669. In 1791, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/May_Constitution_of_Poland" title="May Constitution of Poland">May Constitution</a>, Europe's first modern codified national <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitution</a> as well as the second-oldest national constitution in the world, was drafted here. The decorations in the room are replicas of the originals by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_di_Quadro" title="Giovanni Battista di Quadro">Giovanni Battista di Quadro</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poselska_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-poselska-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>The Royal Apartments</li>
</ul>
<p>In these apartments, King Stanisław Augustus Poniatowski lived. They consist of the <a href="/wiki/Canaletto" title="Canaletto">Canaletto</a> room, in which several painted views of Warsaw are on display.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dziedzictwo_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-dziedzictwo-7">[7]</a></sup> These were not painted by Canaletto, but rather by his nephew, <a href="/wiki/Bernardo_Bellotto" title="Bernardo Bellotto">Bernardo Bellotto</a> also called <i>il Canaletto</i>. <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Pillement" title="Jean-Baptiste Pillement">Jean-Baptiste Pillement</a> worked between 1765–1767 on one of his largest projects, the wallpaper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dziedzictwo_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-dziedzictwo-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Domenico_Merlini" title="Domenico Merlini">Domenico Merlini</a> designed the adjacent Royal Chapel in 1776.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dziedzictwo_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-dziedzictwo-7">[7]</a></sup> Nowadays, the heart of <a href="/wiki/Tadeusz_Ko%C5%9Bciuszko" title="Tadeusz Kościuszko">Tadeusz Kościuszko</a> is kept here in an urn. The Audience Rooms are also designed by Merlini, with four paintings by <a href="/wiki/Marcello_Bacciarelli" title="Marcello Bacciarelli">Marcello Bacciarelli</a> on display. Andrzej Grzybowski took care of the restoration of the room, that included many original pieces.</p>
<ul>
<li>Lanckoroński Collection</li>
</ul>
<p>In 1994 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Countess_Karolina_Lanckoro%C5%84ska" title="Countess Karolina Lanckorońska">Countess Karolina Lanckorońska</a> donated 37 pictures to the Royal Castle. Collection includes two paintings (portraits) by <a href="/wiki/Rembrandt" title="Rembrandt">Rembrandt</a>: <i>The Father of the Jewish Bride</i> (also known as <i>The Scholar at the Lectern</i>) and <i>The Jewish Bride</i> (also known as <i>The Girl in a Picture Frame</i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> both originally in the Stanisław Augustus Poniatowski collection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Copper-Roof_Palace">Copper-Roof Palace</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Castle,_Warsaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Copper-Roof Palace">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Copper-Roof_Palace" title="Copper-Roof Palace">Copper-Roof Palace</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Warsaw_07-13_img10_Old_town.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3970" data-file-width="3373" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Warsaw_07-13_img10_Old_town.jpg/170px-Warsaw_07-13_img10_Old_town.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Warsaw_07-13_img10_Old_town.jpg/255px-Warsaw_07-13_img10_Old_town.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Warsaw_07-13_img10_Old_town.jpg/340px-Warsaw_07-13_img10_Old_town.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Warsaw_07-13_img10_Old_town.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Copper-Roof_Palace" title="Copper-Roof Palace">Copper-Roof Palace</a> adjacent to the castle.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Copper-Roof Palace has since 1989 been a branch of the Royal Castle Museum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The palace is contiguous with Warsaw's Royal Castle, and down a slope from the <a href="/wiki/Castle_Square,_Warsaw" title="Castle Square, Warsaw">Castle Square</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Old_Town" title="Warsaw Old Town">Old Town</a>. It was originally a patrician house of Wawrzyniec Reffus, it was built 1651-1656.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-warszawa1939_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-warszawa1939-24">[24]</a></sup> After 1657 destruction by the army of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_II_R%C3%A1k%C3%B3czi" title="George II Rákóczi">George II Rákóczi</a>, it was completely remodeled in 1698-1701<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-perelki_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-perelki-9">[9]</a></sup> for <a href="/wiki/Jerzy_Dominik_Lubomirski" title="Jerzy Dominik Lubomirski">Jerzy Dominik Lubomirski</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-warszawa1939_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-warszawa1939-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lubomirski expanded the palace by building a southern wing, perpendicular to the rest of structure, and also expanded the western elevation. Shortly after its construction the palace became known as <i>Palais Martin</i>, after Lubomirski's grandson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiezowce_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-wiezowce-25">[25]</a></sup> In 1720 the palace was rebuilt with an addition of a second northern wing. Additionally the interior was decorated with <a href="/wiki/Rococo" title="Rococo">rococo</a> paintings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiezowce_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-wiezowce-25">[25]</a></sup> After 1777 the palace passed into ownership of Poland's last king, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanis%C5%82aw_II_Augustus" title="Stanisław II Augustus">Stanisław II Augustus</a>, who hired the architect <a href="/wiki/Domenico_Merlini" title="Domenico Merlini">Domenico Merlini</a> to once again redesign the inside rooms of the palace and join the library wing of the Royal Castle to it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiezowce_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-wiezowce-25">[25]</a></sup> The king then made a present of the redecorated place to his nephew Prince <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B3zef_Poniatowski" title="Józef Poniatowski">Józef Poniatowski</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-warszawa1939_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-warszawa1939-24">[24]</a></sup> The younger Poniatowski was a successful commander in the 1794 <a href="/wiki/Ko%C5%9Bciuszko_Uprising" title="Kościuszko Uprising">Kościuszko Uprising</a>, and later one of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">Napoleon Bonaparte</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marshal_of_France" title="Marshal of France">marshals</a>. Under his ownership the palace became a center of Warsaw's high class social scene. When Warsaw became part of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kingdom of Prussia</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Third_Partition_of_Poland" title="Third Partition of Poland">Third Partition of Poland</a> the buildings became the headquarters for the Prussian Ministry of War.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiezowce_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-wiezowce-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Copper-Roof Palace was burned in 1944 and reconstructed, based on paintings of <a href="/wiki/Bernardo_Bellotto" title="Bernardo Bellotto">Bernardo Bellotto</a>, between 1948-1949.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-perelki_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-perelki-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Currently the palace is a museum hosting, <i>inter alia</i>, a permanent exhibition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oriental_carpets" title="Oriental carpets">oriental carpets</a> and other oriental decorative art, donated to the museum by Mrs. Teresa Sahakian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wystawa_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-wystawa-26">[26]</a></sup> The collection comprises 579 items, 562 of which are textiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<li>On 24 May 1829 in the Royal Castle's Senator's Hall, <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_I_of_Russia" title="Nicholas I of Russia">Nicholas I of Russia</a> was crowned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Poland" title="King of Poland">King of Poland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 5 November 1916, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Act_of_5_November" title="Act of 5 November">Act of 5 November</a> was announced in the Grand Hall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 23 April 1935, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/April_Constitution" title="April Constitution">April Constitution</a> was signed in the <i>Knight Hall</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zamki_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-zamki-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>Stanisław Augustus Poniatowski's regalia are kept in the Royal Chapel. These are the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_White_Eagle_(Poland)" title="Order of the White Eagle (Poland)">Order of the White Eagle</a>, the ceremonial sword of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_Stanislaus" title="Order of Saint Stanislaus">Saint Stanisław's Order</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beryl#Aquamarine_and_maxixe" title="Beryl">aquamarine</a> sceptre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dziedzictwo_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-dziedzictwo-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>The insignia of presidential power are also stored in the Castle- the stamp of the President, the Jack of the President of the Republic of Poland and national documents, which <a href="/wiki/Ryszard_Kaczorowski" title="Ryszard Kaczorowski">Ryszard Kaczorowski</a> gave to <a href="/wiki/Lech_Wa%C5%82%C4%99sa" title="Lech Wałęsa">Lech Wałęsa</a> on 22 December 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li>Many of the Polish <a href="/wiki/Legend" title="Legend">legends</a> are connected with the Royal Castle. According to one of them in 1569 the King <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_II_Augustus" title="Sigismund II Augustus">Sigismund Augustus</a>, who was in mourning after death of his beloved wife <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Radziwi%C5%82%C5%82" title="Barbara Radziwiłł">Barbara Radziwiłł</a>, asked the renowned sorcerer <a href="/wiki/Pan_Twardowski" title="Pan Twardowski">Master Twardowski</a> to evoke her ghost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wegow_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-wegow-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kucharczak_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kucharczak-33">[33]</a></sup> The experiment was successful with support of a magic mirror, which today is kept in the <a href="/wiki/W%C4%99gr%C3%B3w" title="Węgrów">Węgrów</a> Cathedral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wegow_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-wegow-32">[32]</a></sup> Despite that some people suspected that it was not the Queen's ghost but closely resembling her king's mistress Barbara Giżanka and the whole event was set up by Giżanka's accomplice Mikołaj Mniszech, king's chamberlain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kucharczak_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kucharczak-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chicago_replica">Chicago replica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Castle,_Warsaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Chicago replica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1979, the historic <a href="/wiki/Gateway_Theatre_(Chicago)" title="Gateway Theatre (Chicago)">Gateway Theatre</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_Park,_Chicago" title="Jefferson Park, Chicago">Jefferson Park</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Community_areas_of_Chicago" title="Community areas of Chicago">community area</a> of Chicago was purchased by the <a href="/wiki/Copernicus_Foundation" title="Copernicus Foundation">Copernicus Foundation</a> with the intention of converting it into the seat of the Polish Cultural and Civic Center. Because of the building's historical significance, its interior was kept intact while the exterior was remodelled and a <a href="/wiki/Baroque_Revival_architecture" title="Baroque Revival architecture">Neo-Baroque</a> clock tower was added to give it the resemblance of the Royal Castle in Warsaw.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> It is a visual tribute to <a href="/wiki/Poles_in_Chicago" title="Poles in Chicago">Chicago's large Polish populace</a>, the largest such presence outside of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Poland" title="Republic of Poland">Republic of Poland</a>.</p>
