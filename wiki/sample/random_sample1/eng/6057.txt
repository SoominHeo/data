<p><b>El Museo del Barrio</b>, often known simply as <b>El Museo</b> (the museum) is a <a href="/wiki/Museum" title="Museum">museum</a> located towards the northern end in the neighborhood of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Museum_Mile,_New_York_City" title="Museum Mile, New York City">Museum Mile</a>, immediately north of the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_the_City_of_New_York" title="Museum of the City of New York">Museum of the City of New York</a> and south of the future <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Museum_for_African_Art" title="Museum for African Art">Museum for African Art</a>. Founded in 1969, El Museo specializes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_of_Latin_America" title="Art of Latin America">Latin American</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_art" title="Caribbean art">Caribbean</a> art, with an emphasis on works from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Art of Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nuyorican" title="Nuyorican">Puerto Rican community in New York City.</a></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collection">Collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Museo_del_Barrio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The museum features an extensive collection of around 8,500<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_York_Times_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-New_York_Times-2">[2]</a></sup> pieces composed of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian" title="Pre-Columbian">pre-Columbian</a> and traditional <a href="/wiki/Artifact_(archaeology)" title="Artifact (archaeology)">artifacts</a>, particularly a large permanent <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno" title="Taíno">Taíno</a> exhibit, as well as 20th century <a href="/wiki/Handicraft" title="Handicraft">arts and crafts</a>, graphics and <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">popular media</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexican_mask-folk_art" title="Mexican mask-folk art">Mexican masks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textiles</a> from <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> and photographs and traditional art from <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> There are often temporary exhibits on Puerto Rican and Latino <a href="/wiki/Modern_art" title="Modern art">modern art</a>. The museum also sponsors numerous festivals and educational programs throughout the year including the annual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_Kings_Day" title="Three Kings Day">Three Kings Day</a> parade. Due to a lack of space prior to their <a href="/wiki/El_Museo_del_Barrio#Expansion" title="El Museo del Barrio">2009-2010 expansion</a>, the museum began to place some of their permanent collection online. This also served as a means of audience development.</p>
<p>Seeking to increase their audience and reach new audiences, El Museo has partnered with a number of organizations for joint exhibits including <i>Nueva York</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Historical_Society" title="New York Historical Society">New York Historical Society</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nuyo_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nuyo-4">[4]</a></sup> and <i>Caribbean Crossroads</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queens_Museum_of_Art" title="Queens Museum of Art">Queens Museum of Art</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Studio_Museum_in_Harlem" title="Studio Museum in Harlem">Studio Museum in Harlem</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ccross_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ccross-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Museo_del_Barrio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg"><img alt="[icon]" data-file-height="31" data-file-width="44" height="14" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" width="20"/></a></td>

<p>Originally, the museum was a Fire Station during the <a href="/wiki/Nuyorican_Movement" title="Nuyorican Movement">Nuyorican Movement</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_Rights_Movement" title="Civil Rights Movement">Civil Rights Movement</a>, where books were burned by radical political figures. Spurred by concerns over a lack of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_diversity" title="Cultural diversity">cultural diversity</a> in <a href="/wiki/Education_in_New_York_City" title="Education in New York City">city educational programs</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elmuseo1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-elmuseo1-1">[1]</a></sup> and educational opportunities in the barrio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reop_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-reop-6">[6]</a></sup> The group also included a number of parents, educators and community activists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nya_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-nya-7">[7]</a></sup> Artist/educator <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raphael_Monta%C3%B1ez_Ort%C3%ADz" title="Raphael Montañez Ortíz">Rafael Montañez Ortíz</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rmo_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-rmo-8">[8]</a></sup> was enlisted to create materials for schools in East and Central <a href="/wiki/Harlem" title="Harlem">Harlem</a> that would highlight Puerto Rican art, history and culture. Ortiz reconceived this project as a community museum that would primarily function as a neighborhood institution serving <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Ricans</a> in East Harlem. The museum also sought to define itself as an educational institution and its original location was a public school classroom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nya_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-nya-7">[7]</a></sup> With the increasing size of the Latino population throughout New York City, of which Puerto Ricans are still the majority, the scope of the museum has expanded, breeding conflict with some artists, scholars, and neighborhood activists anxious to preserve its original mission.</p>
<p>In 1977, El Museo joined the <a href="/wiki/Cultural_Institutions_Group" title="Cultural Institutions Group">Cultural Institutions Group</a> (also known as CIGs) which helped increase and maintain its funding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nya_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-nya-7">[7]</a></sup> Its funding was frozen in the 1980s following a period of mismanagement. However it was able to successfully rebound and grow. A <a href="/wiki/Frida_Kahlo" title="Frida Kahlo">Frida Kahlo</a> exhibit in 2002 brought more attendees than normally visited the museum annually and helped to transform its perception and led to the museum's first non-Puerto Rican Director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miss_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-miss-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 15, 2013, it was announced that Margarita Aguilar left her post as the director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_York_Times_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-New_York_Times-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Museo_del_Barrio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From El Museo's origins in a <a href="/wiki/State_school" title="State school">public school</a> classroom,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nya_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-nya-7">[7]</a></sup> it has called a number of different locations home. Between 1969 and 1976, it operated out of a number of different storefronts on <a href="/wiki/Third_Avenue" title="Third Avenue">Third</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lexington_Avenue_(Manhattan)" title="Lexington Avenue (Manhattan)">Lexington</a> Avenues in the nexus of the <a href="/wiki/East_Harlem" title="East Harlem">community</a> they served.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nya_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-nya-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1977, they moved to their permanent home, the <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">neo-classical</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heckscher_Building" title="Heckscher Building">Heckscher Building</a> (THIS APPEARS TO BE INCORRECT, AS THE ARTICLE ABOUT THE HECKSHER BLDG SAYS IT'S ON 57TH ST., AND THE MUSEO IS NOWHERE NEAR THERE, AND THE PICTURE OF THE HECKSHER LOOKS NOTHING LIKE THE MUSEO BLDG.) on Fifth Avenue and became a founding member of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Museum_Mile,_New_York_City" title="Museum Mile, New York City">Museum Mile</a> when it launched in 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nya_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-nya-7">[7]</a></sup> This location contributed to the museum's growth and audience development, increasing the market share of non Latino visitors to 40% of their audience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nya_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-nya-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion">Expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Museo_del_Barrio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early 2000s the museum experienced a significant increase in visitors, however remained confined to one floor in its building, which it shares with a school and a number of private organizations. A plan was proposed for the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_the_City_of_New_York" title="Museum of the City of New York">Museum of the City of New York</a>, across the street from El Museo, to relocate to the historic <a href="/wiki/Tweed_Courthouse" title="Tweed Courthouse">Tweed Courthouse</a> by City Hall in <a href="/wiki/Lower_Manhattan" title="Lower Manhattan">Lower Manhattan</a>. El Museo would then have moved into the other museum's former building, dramatically expanding its available exhibition space.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, Mayor <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bloomberg" title="Michael Bloomberg">Michael Bloomberg</a> decided to site the new <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Department_of_Education" title="New York City Department of Education">New York City Department of Education</a> in the Tweed Courthouse instead, frustrating the efforts of both museums.</p>
<p>After the failed relocation, El Museo opted to pursue a $15 million project to transform its outdoor courtyard into an open glass lobby, café and performance space, and to provide a suitable public "face" to the street on the model of the renovated <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Museum" title="Brooklyn Museum">Brooklyn Museum</a>. It reopened following the renovation and expansion in October 2009 to mostly positive reviews. The renovation was spearheaded by NYC-based architect Gruzen Samton and completed at a cost of $35 million and added a shop and restaurant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reop_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-reop-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
