<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parishes_of_Barbados&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>They are legally styled as the "Parish of ("Parish name") as opposed to the American naming convention with the name "Parish" coming after the name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The use of the term "parish" derives from the island's religious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglican" title="Anglican">Anglican</a> history under the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parishes_of_Barbados&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This system of parish churches was based on the system that of the Church of England and was the visible expression forming the basis of the parliamentary representation in Barbados. The differing size and shape of each parish were primarily influenced by the large plantation estates of <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugar_cane" title="Sugar cane">sugar cane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> that existed during the <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonial</a> years of Barbados. As various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chaples_of_ease" title="Chaples of ease">chaples of ease</a> were created during the 17th century across the island, some local churches were elevated to <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England_parish_church" title="Church of England parish church">parish church</a> status, leading to the formation of new parishes surrounding those freshly created vestries.</p>
<p>By 1629, the <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> settlers after landing at <a href="/wiki/Holetown" title="Holetown">James Town</a> formed six original parishes on the island which were:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barbados_Parliament_website_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barbados_Parliament_website-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>The Parish of <a href="/wiki/Christ_Church,_Barbados" title="Christ Church, Barbados">Christ Church</a>,</li>
<li>The Parish of <a href="/wiki/Saint_James,_Barbados" title="Saint James, Barbados">St. James</a>,</li>
<li>The Parish of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lucy,_Barbados" title="Saint Lucy, Barbados">St. Lucy</a>,</li>
<li>The Parish of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Michael,_Barbados" title="Saint Michael, Barbados">St. Michael</a>,</li>
<li>The Parish of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter,_Barbados" title="Saint Peter, Barbados">St. Peter</a>, and</li>
<li>The Parish of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas,_Barbados" title="Saint Thomas, Barbados">St. Thomas</a></li>
</ul>
<p>By 1645, the land holding of Barbados increased and the shape of the original six were reconfigured giving way to an additional five parishes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barbados_Parliament_website_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barbados_Parliament_website-3">[3]</a></sup> Some prior churches of the state within the existing parishes were elevated to the level of Parish Church and as a consequence they formed new parishes around those new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vestries" title="Vestries">vestries</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li>The Parish of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Andrew,_Barbados" title="Saint Andrew, Barbados">St. Andrew</a>,</li>
<li>The Parish of <a href="/wiki/Saint_George,_Barbados" title="Saint George, Barbados">St. George</a>,</li>
<li>The Parish of <a href="/wiki/Saint_John,_Barbados" title="Saint John, Barbados">St. John</a>,</li>
<li>The Parish of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Joseph,_Barbados" title="Saint Joseph, Barbados">St. Joseph</a>, and</li>
<li>The Parish of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Philip,_Barbados" title="Saint Philip, Barbados">St. Philip</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Thus Barbados was converted into the current eleven parishes of today. As was common under the British system, each Parish had a single main <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parishional church</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">cathedral</a> in the case of Bridgetown having been elevated to city status), which acted as a sort of capital for each parish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_to_Z_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-A_to_Z-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The parishes each held their own Local Government Councils until these were abolished in 1959, following a brief administrative <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_Barbados" title="Districts of Barbados">districting experiment</a> within Barbados until 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_to_Z_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-A_to_Z-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>The nation's capital <a href="/wiki/Bridgetown" title="Bridgetown">Bridgetown</a>, which is located within the parish of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Michael,_Barbados" title="Saint Michael, Barbados">Saint Michael</a>, may one day be made into its own district.</p>
<p>Within the country, travel is unrestricted to everyone in moving about from parish-to-parish. With the rise of urban sprawl and new construction projects across the country many neighbourhoods and even parishional border-lines today are ill defined.</p>
