<p><b>Bastogne</b> (<small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[bas.tɔɲ]</a></span>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i>Bastenaken</i>, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i>Bastenach</i>, <a href="/wiki/Luxembourgish_language" title="Luxembourgish language">Luxembourgish</a>: <i>Baaschtnech</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">Walloon</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipalities_in_Belgium" title="Municipalities in Belgium">municipality</a> of <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> located in the <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg_(Belgium)" title="Luxembourg (Belgium)">province of Luxembourg</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Ardennes" title="Ardennes">Ardennes</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Municipality" title="Municipality">municipality</a> of Bastogne includes the old <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipalities_in_Belgium" title="Municipalities in Belgium">communes</a> of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Longvilly&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Longvilly (page does not exist)">Longvilly</a>, Noville, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Villers-la-Bonne-Eau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Villers-la-Bonne-Eau (page does not exist)">Villers-la-Bonne-Eau</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wardin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wardin (page does not exist)">Wardin</a>. The town is situated on a ridge in the <a href="/wiki/Ardennes" title="Ardennes">Ardennes</a> at an elevation of 510 metres (1,670 ft).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bastogne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the time of the Roman conquest the region of Bastogne was inhabited by the <a href="/wiki/Treveri" title="Treveri">Treveri</a>, a tribe of <a href="/wiki/Gauls" title="Gauls">Gauls</a>. A form of the name Bastogne was first mentioned only much later, in 634, when the local lord ceded these territories to the <a href="/wiki/St._Maximin%27s_Abbey,_Trier" title="St. Maximin's Abbey, Trier">St Maximin's Abbey</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Trier" title="Trier">Trier</a>. A century later, the Bastogne area went to the nearby <a href="/wiki/Pr%C3%BCm_Abbey" title="Prüm Abbey">Prüm Abbey</a>. The town of Bastogne and its marketplace are again mentioned in an 887 document. By the 13th century, <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry VII, Holy Roman Emperor">Henry VII, Holy Roman Emperor</a> and count of <a href="/wiki/County_of_Luxemburg" title="County of Luxemburg">Luxemburg</a>, was minting coins in Bastogne. In 1332, <a href="/wiki/John_of_Bohemia" title="John of Bohemia">John the Blind</a>, his son, granted the city its charter and had it encircled by defensive walls, part of which, the current <i>Porte de Trèves</i>, still exists. In 1451, the lands of the county of Luxemburg were absorbed into the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Burgundy" title="Duchy of Burgundy">Duchy of Burgundy</a> and as a result, Bastogne became part of the lands of the Spanish Crown when the Burgundian heir <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles</a> became king of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Spain" title="Kingdom of Spain">Spain</a> in 1516.</p>
<p>The city’s walls were quite effective at protecting it during the troubled times that followed. The city’s economy actually flourished thanks to the renown of its agricultural and cattle fairs. The walls repelled a <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> attack successfully in 1602. In 1688, they were dismantled by order of King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_XIV" title="Louis XIV">Louis XIV</a> when the town was occupied by French forces during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nine_Years_War" title="Nine Years War">Nine Years War</a>.</p>
<p>The 19th century and <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Revolution" title="Belgian Revolution">Belgium's independence</a> were favourable to Bastogne, as its forest products and cattle fairs became better known abroad. Several railway lines were built to link it to the neighbouring towns. This all came to an end with the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> occupation during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bastogne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bastogne" title="Battle of Bastogne">Battle of Bastogne</a></div>
<p>Liberated by the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a> in September 1944, Bastogne was attacked by German forces a few months later. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hitler" title="Hitler">Hitler</a>'s idea was to regain control of the <a href="/wiki/Ardennes" title="Ardennes">Ardennes</a>, splitting British from American forces, then advance to and reoccupy the strategic port of <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a> and cut off the key Allied supply line. On December 16, taking advantage of cold and fog, <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">German</a> artillery initiated the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Bulge" title="Battle of the Bulge">Battle of the Bulge</a> attacking the American divisions deployed sparsely around Bastogne. A few days later, <a href="/wiki/Anthony_McAuliffe" title="Anthony McAuliffe">Brigadier General McAuliffe</a> and the <a href="/wiki/101st_Airborne_Division" title="101st Airborne Division">101st Airborne Division</a> along with elements of the <a href="/wiki/10th_Armored_Division_(United_States)" title="10th Armored Division (United States)">10th Armored Division (United States)</a> and the 82nd Airborne Division, arrived to counter-attack but, after heavy fighting, became encircled within the town. On December 22, German emissaries asked for the American surrender, to which the General answered tersely, “Nuts!” The next day the skies cleared, allowing Allied air forces to retaliate and to drop much needed food, medicine, and weaponry to ground forces. On December 26, the Third U.S. Army under the command of <a href="/wiki/George_S._Patton" title="George S. Patton">General Patton</a> arrived and broke the deadlock. The official end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bastogne" title="Battle of Bastogne">Battle of Bastogne</a> occurred three weeks later, when all fighting in the area ceased.</p>
<p>Bastogne is the terminus of the <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Road_(France)" title="Liberty Road (France)">Liberty Road (France)</a>, the commemorative way that marks the path of liberating Allied forces, and of the Third Army that subsequently relieved Bastogne.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bastogne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<ul>
<li>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=101st_Airborne_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="101st Airborne Museum (page does not exist)">101st Airborne Museum</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> includes dioramas and more about the experiences of soldiers and civilians during the siege of the city during World War II.</li>
<li>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bastogne_Barracks&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bastogne Barracks (page does not exist)">Bastogne Barracks</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> museum is free and operated by the Belgian Army. It is located in the barracks used as the headquarters of the U.S. 101st Airborne during the Siege of Bastogne. It features an extensive collection of restored tanks and military vehicles as well as a guided tour of the underground barracks and artifacts from U.S., German and British forces. The base ("caserne") is about 5 blocks from the Place de St. Pierre. The museum includes the basement office where General McAuliffe issued the famous "Nuts!" response to the German demand for surrender. Other rooms display artillery, small arms, radio and medical equipment. Belgian Army specialists guide visitors through the base.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Bastogne_War_Museum" title="Bastogne War Museum">Bastogne War Museum</a> has many war artifacts and videos recreating the experience of the Battle of the Bulge for visitors.</li>
<li>The <i>Porte de Trèves</i>, part of the defensive walls that had been erected in the 14th century by <a href="/wiki/John_of_Bohemia" title="John of Bohemia">John the Blind</a>, can still be seen.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque</a> tower of St Pierre church and its baptismal fonts also date from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Mardasson_Memorial" title="Mardasson Memorial">Mardasson Memorial</a>, was erected near Bastogne in 1950 to honor the memory of American soldiers wounded or killed during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Bulge" title="Battle of the Bulge">Battle of the Bulge</a>.</li>
<li>Monuments to <a href="/wiki/Anthony_McAuliffe" title="Anthony McAuliffe">Brigadier General McAuliffe</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_S._Patton" title="George S. Patton">General Patton</a> and others can be found around town.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Recogne_German_war_cemetery" title="Recogne German war cemetery">Recogne German war cemetery</a>, 6 km to the North. Contains the graves of 6,807 German soldiers.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Folklore">Folklore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bastogne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Folklore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The key character of all legends about Bastogne is the so-called <i>piche-cacaye</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This is pronounced pish-cackay.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bastogne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bastogne originally had an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NMBS/SNCB" title="NMBS/SNCB">NMBS/SNCB</a> railway line connecting it to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Libramont" title="Libramont">Libramont</a> and to <a href="/wiki/Gouvy" title="Gouvy">Gouvy</a>. Passenger trains to Gouvy stopped in 1984 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and in the 1990s the line to Libramont was taken out of service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The two station buildings in Bastogne remain, but are now used for other purposes. Part of the rail line has been converted into a cycle path. However, two bus stations are now open in Bastogne, according to SNCB: Bastogne Nord and Bastonge Sud. The short line that runs is only a rural shuttle line from Bastogne Nord to Libramont stopping only at Bastogne Sud.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bastogne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Bastogne is the halfway point of the <a href="/wiki/Li%C3%A8ge%E2%80%93Bastogne%E2%80%93Li%C3%A8ge" title="Liège–Bastogne–Liège">Liège–Bastogne–Liège</a> <a href="/wiki/Classic_cycle_races" title="Classic cycle races">classic bicycle race</a>, one of the oldest and most prestigious one-day events in the professional <a href="/wiki/Cycling" title="Cycling">cycling</a> calendar, dating back to the late 19th century.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Circuit_des_Ardennes" title="Circuit des Ardennes">Circuit des Ardennes</a></i>, which today refers to <a href="/wiki/Circuit_des_Ardennes_(cycling)" title="Circuit des Ardennes (cycling)">a bicycle race</a>, used to refer to a 600 kilometres (370 mi) long car-racing event in the Bastogne area. The race used to attract famous enthusiasts, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_II,_German_Emperor" title="William II, German Emperor">William II, German Emperor</a>. It was relocated to <a href="/wiki/Circuit_de_Spa-Francorchamps" title="Circuit de Spa-Francorchamps">Francorchamps</a> around the time of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, where it now lives on as the <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Grand_Prix" title="Belgian Grand Prix">Belgian Grand Prix</a>.</li>
</ul>
