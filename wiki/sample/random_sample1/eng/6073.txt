<p><b>Vasily Ivanovich Alekseyev</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Василий Иванович Алексеев</span>; 7 January 1942 – 25 November 2011) was a Soviet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weightlifter" title="Weightlifter">weightlifter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greatath_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-greatath-4">[4]</a></sup> He set 80 world records and 81 Soviet records in weightlifting and won gold medals at the <a href="/wiki/1972_Summer_Olympics" title="1972 Summer Olympics">1972</a> and <a href="/wiki/1976_Summer_Olympics" title="1976 Summer Olympics">1976 Summer Olympics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sr_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-sr-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vasily_Alekseyev&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the age of 18, Alekseyev began practicing weightlifting at Trud <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voluntary_Sports_Societies_of_the_USSR" title="Voluntary Sports Societies of the USSR">Voluntary Sports Society</a>, trained by his coach <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Plyukfelder" title="Rudolf Plyukfelder">Rudolf Plyukfelder</a> until 1968, when he began to train solo. He was not a naturally large man like other super heavyweights but was encouraged to gain strength by adding weight. In January 1970 Alekseyev set his first world record, and during the World Weightlifting Championship in Columbus, Ohio in 1970 he was the first man to clean and jerk 500 pounds (227 kg) in competition. During one of his early world records, Oscar State OBE remarked that the weight of over 460 pounds (209 kg) in the Olympic press looked so easy it could have been a broomstick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greatath_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-greatath-4">[4]</a></sup> This was the beginning of a series of 80 world records Alekseyev set between 1970 and 1977. He received bonus funds every time he set a world record by the Soviet Union; so he made it a point to gradually increase his world records by 1.1 pounds or 0.5 kg. He was unbeaten and held the World Championship and European Championship titles for those eight years. He was the first man to total over 600 kg in the triple event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greatath_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-greatath-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many thought he would be the first to clean and jerk the mythical 600 pounds but it was never to be as his habit of increasing world records by only 1/2 kilo took so long that age caught up to him.</p>
<p>Alekseyev's performance in the <a href="/wiki/1980_Summer_Olympics" title="1980 Summer Olympics">Moscow Olympics</a> of 1980 was a disappointment. He had by then become more of a recluse, training by himself without a coach. In the snatch he set his opening weight too high and was unable to lift it, scoring zero kilograms as the result. He retired from weightlifting after the Moscow Olympics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sr_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-sr-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1987, Alekseyev was elected to represent the <a href="/wiki/Ryazan_Oblast" title="Ryazan Oblast">Ryazan District</a> for the Soviet Union's <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_People%27s_Deputies_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Congress of People's Deputies of the Soviet Union">Congress of People's Deputies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greatath_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-greatath-4">[4]</a></sup> Alekseyev worked as a coach between 1990 and 1992. Under his leadership, the <a href="/wiki/Unified_Team_at_the_Olympics" title="Unified Team at the Olympics">Unified Team</a> earned ten medals in weightlifting at the <a href="/wiki/1992_Summer_Olympics" title="1992 Summer Olympics">1992 Summer Olympics</a>, including five golds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1966 Alekseyev lived in <a href="/wiki/Shakhty" title="Shakhty">Shakhty</a>, where in 1971 he graduated from the branch of the <a href="/wiki/Novocherkassk" title="Novocherkassk">Novocherkassk</a> Polytechnical Institute. He died on 25 November 2011 in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> in a clinic where he had been sent for serious heart problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-3">[3]</a></sup> He was 69. The Russian Weightlifting Federation reported his death and called him a "Soviet sports legend" and "one of the strongest people in the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He was survived by wife Olimpiada and sons Sergey and Dmitry. Dmitry competed nationally in weightlifting, placing fourth at the 1988 Soviet weightlifting championships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sr_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-sr-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_and_awards">Legacy and awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vasily_Alekseyev&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legacy and awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Vysotsky" title="Vladimir Vysotsky">Vladimir Vysotsky</a> devoted his "Song about weightlifter" (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Песня о штангисте</span>, 1971) to Alekseyev.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alekseyev was featured on the cover of <i><a href="/wiki/Sports_Illustrated" title="Sports Illustrated">Sports Illustrated</a></i> 14 April 1975, titled "World's Strongest Man".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1999, in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, Alekseyev was acknowledged as the best sportsman of the 20th century. He was also awarded: <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Lenin" title="Order of Lenin">Order of Lenin</a> (1972), <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Friendship_of_Peoples" title="Order of Friendship of Peoples">Order of Friendship of Peoples</a>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Badge_of_Honour" title="Order of the Badge of Honour">Order of the Badge of Honour</a> (1970), and <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Red_Banner_of_Labour" title="Order of the Red Banner of Labour">Order of the Red Banner of Labour</a> (1972). In 1993 he was elected a member of the <a href="/wiki/International_Weightlifting_Federation" title="International Weightlifting Federation">International Weightlifting Federation</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hall_of_fame" title="Hall of fame">Hall of Fame</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Shakhty, where he lived much of his life, there is a street and park named after him,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> as well as his monument installed in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
