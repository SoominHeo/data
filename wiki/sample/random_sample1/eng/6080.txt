<p>The <b>Bowery</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/aʊ/ 'ou' in 'pout'">aʊ</span><span title="/ᵊ/ optional 'ə' in 'jewelry'">ᵊ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is a street and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neighborhood" title="Neighborhood">neighborhood</a> in the southern portion of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> <a href="/wiki/Borough_(New_York_City)" title="Borough (New York City)">borough</a> of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>. The street runs from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chatham_Square_(Manhattan)" title="Chatham Square (Manhattan)">Chatham Square</a> at <a href="/wiki/Park_Row_(Manhattan)" title="Park Row (Manhattan)">Park Row</a>, <a href="/wiki/Worth_Street" title="Worth Street">Worth Street</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mott_Street" title="Mott Street">Mott Street</a> in the south to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cooper_Square_(Manhattan)" title="Cooper Square (Manhattan)">Cooper Square</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4th_Street" title="4th Street">4th Street</a> in the north,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enc-nyc2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-enc-nyc2-2">[2]</a></sup> while the neighborhood's boundaries are roughly <a href="/wiki/4th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="4th Street (Manhattan)">East 4th Street</a> and the <a href="/wiki/East_Village,_Manhattan" title="East Village, Manhattan">East Village</a> to the north; <a href="/wiki/Canal_Street_(Manhattan)" title="Canal Street (Manhattan)">Canal Street</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinatown,_Manhattan" title="Chinatown, Manhattan">Chinatown</a> to the south; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allen_Street_(Manhattan)" title="Allen Street (Manhattan)">Allen Street</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lower_East_Side,_Manhattan" title="Lower East Side, Manhattan">Lower East Side</a> to the east; and <a href="/wiki/Little_Italy,_Manhattan" title="Little Italy, Manhattan">Little Italy</a> to the west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 17th century, the road branched off <a href="/wiki/Broadway_(Manhattan)" title="Broadway (Manhattan)">Broadway</a> north of <a href="/wiki/Fort_Amsterdam" title="Fort Amsterdam">Fort Amsterdam</a> at the tip of Manhattan to the homestead of <a href="/wiki/Peter_Stuyvesant" title="Peter Stuyvesant">Peter Stuyvesant</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Director-General_of_New_Netherland" title="Director-General of New Netherland">Director-General of New Netherland</a>. The street was known as Bowery Lane prior to 1807.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> "Bowery" is an <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation" title="Anglicisation">anglicization</a> of the Dutch <i>bouwerij</i>, derived from an antiquated <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> word for "<a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farm</a>", as in the 17th century the area contained many large farms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enc-nyc2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-enc-nyc2-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Subway" title="New York City Subway">New York City Subway</a> station named <a href="/wiki/Bowery_(BMT_Nassau_Street_Line)" title="Bowery (BMT Nassau Street Line)">Bowery</a>, serving the <a href="/wiki/BMT_Nassau_Street_Line" title="BMT Nassau Street Line">BMT Nassau Street Line</a> (<span class="nowrap"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J_(New_York_City_Subway_service)" title="J (New York City Subway service)">J</a></span> <span class="nowrap"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Z_(New_York_City_Subway_service)" title="Z (New York City Subway service)">Z</a></span> trains), is located close to the Bowery's intersection with <a href="/wiki/Delancey_Street" title="Delancey Street">Delancey</a> and Kenmare Streets. There is a tunnel under the Bowery once intended for use by <a href="/wiki/Proposed_expansion_of_the_New_York_City_Subway" title="Proposed expansion of the New York City Subway">proposed but never built New York City Subway services</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Second_Avenue_Subway" title="Second Avenue Subway">Second Avenue Subway</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colonial_and_Federal_periods">Colonial and Federal periods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Colonial and Federal periods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Bowery is the oldest thoroughfare on Manhattan Island, preceding European intervention as a <a href="/wiki/Lenape" title="Lenape">Lenape</a> footpath, which spanned roughly the entire length of the island, from north to south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> When the Dutch settled Manhattan island, they named the path <i>Bouwerij road</i>—"bouwerij" being an old Dutch word for "farm"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>—because it connected farmlands and estates on the outskirts to the heart of the city in today's <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a>/<a href="/wiki/Battery_Park" title="Battery Park">Battery Park</a> area.</p>
<p>In 1654, the Bowery’s first residents settled in the area of <a href="/wiki/Chatham_Square" title="Chatham Square">Chatham Square</a>; ten freed slaves and their wives set up cabins and a cattle farm there.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petrus_Stuyvesant" title="Petrus Stuyvesant">Petrus Stuyvesant</a>, the last Dutch Governor of <a href="/wiki/New_Amsterdam" title="New Amsterdam">New Amsterdam</a> before the English took control, retired to his Bowery farm in 1667. After his death in 1672, he was buried in his private chapel. His mansion burned down in 1778 and his great-grandson sold the remaining chapel and graveyard, now the site of the Episcopal church of <a href="/wiki/St._Mark%27s_Church_in-the-Bowery" title="St. Mark's Church in-the-Bowery">St. Mark's Church in-the-Bowery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In her <i>Journal</i> of 1704–05, <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Kemble_Knight" title="Sarah Kemble Knight">Sarah Kemble Knight</a> describes the Bowery as a leisure destination for residents of New York City in December:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Their Diversions in the Winter is Riding <a href="/wiki/Sled" title="Sled">Sleys</a> about three or four Miles out of Town, where they have Houses of entertainment at a place called Bowery, and some go to friends Houses who handsomely treat them. [...] I believe we mett 50 or 60 slays that day—they fly with great swiftness and some are so furious that they'le turn out of the path for none except a Loaden Cart. Nor do they spare for any diversion the place affords, and sociable to a degree, they'r Tables being as free to their Naybours as to themselves.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>By 1766, when <a href="/wiki/John_Montresor" title="John Montresor">John Montresor</a> made his detailed plan of New York,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> "Bowry Lane", which took a more north-tending track at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rope_walk" title="Rope walk">rope walk</a>, was lined for the first few streets with buildings that formed a solid frontage, with <a href="/wiki/Market_garden" title="Market garden">market gardens</a> behind them; when <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_Da_Ponte" title="Lorenzo Da Ponte">Lorenzo Da Ponte</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Librettist" title="Librettist">librettist</a> for <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Mozart</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Don_Giovanni" title="Don Giovanni">Don Giovanni</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Marriage_of_Figaro" title="The Marriage of Figaro">The Marriage of Figaro</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Cos%C3%AC_fan_tutte" title="Così fan tutte">Così fan tutte</a></i>, immigrated to New York City in 1806, he briefly ran one of the shops along the Bowery, a fruit and vegetable store. In 1766, straight lanes led away at right angles to gentlemen's seats, mostly well back from the dusty "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Post_Road" title="Eastern Post Road">Road to Albany and Boston</a>", as it was labeled on Montresor's map; Nicholas Bayard's was planted as an <a href="/wiki/Avenue_(landscape)" title="Avenue (landscape)">avenue</a> of trees. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Delancey" title="James Delancey">James Delancey</a>'s grand house, flanked by matching outbuildings, stood behind a forecourt facing Bowery Lane; behind it was his <a href="/wiki/Parterre" title="Parterre">parterre</a> garden, ending in an <a href="/wiki/Exedra" title="Exedra">exedra</a>, clearly delineated on the map.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bull%27s_Head_Tavern" title="Bull's Head Tavern">Bull's Head Tavern</a> was noted for <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>'s having stopped there for refreshment before riding down to the waterfront to witness the <a href="/wiki/Evacuation_Day_(New_York)" title="Evacuation Day (New York)">departure of British troops</a> in 1783. Leading to the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Post_Road#New_York" title="Boston Post Road">Post Road</a>, the main route to <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, the Bowery rivaled <a href="/wiki/Broadway_(Manhattan)" title="Broadway (Manhattan)">Broadway</a> as a thoroughfare; as late as 1869, when it had gained the "reputation of cheap trade, without being disreputable" it was still "the second principal street of the city".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_of_the_area">Rise of the area</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rise of the area">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the population of New York City continued to grow, its northern boundary continue to move, and by the early 1800s the Bowery was no longer a farming area outside the city. The street gained in respectability and elegance, becoming a broad boulevard, as well-heeled and famous people moved their residences there, including <a href="/wiki/Peter_Cooper" title="Peter Cooper">Peter Cooper</a>, the industrialist and philanthropist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enc-nyc2_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-enc-nyc2-2">[2]</a></sup> The Bowery began to rival <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Avenue" title="Fifth Avenue">Fifth Avenue</a> as an address.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enc-nyc2_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-enc-nyc2-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>When <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lafayette_Street_(Manhattan)" title="Lafayette Street (Manhattan)">Lafayette Street</a> was opened parallel to the Bowery in the 1820s, the <a href="/wiki/Bowery_Theatre" title="Bowery Theatre">Bowery Theatre</a> was founded by rich families on the site of the Red Bull Tavern, which had been purchased by <a href="/wiki/John_Jacob_Astor" title="John Jacob Astor">John Jacob Astor</a>; it opened in 1826 and was the largest auditorium in North America at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enc-nyc2_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-enc-nyc2-2">[2]</a></sup> Across the way the <a href="/wiki/Bowery_Amphitheatre" title="Bowery Amphitheatre">Bowery Amphitheatre</a> was erected in 1833, specializing in the more populist entertainments of <a href="/wiki/Equestrianism" title="Equestrianism">equestrian shows</a> and <a href="/wiki/Circus" title="Circus">circuses</a>. From stylish beginnings, the tone of Bowery Theatre's offerings matched the slide in the social scale of the Bowery itself.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slide_from_respectability">Slide from respectability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Slide from respectability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By the time of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>, the mansions and shops had given way to low-brow concert halls, <a href="/wiki/Brothel" title="Brothel">brothels</a>, German <a href="/wiki/Beer_garden" title="Beer garden">beer gardens</a>, pawn shops, and <a href="/wiki/Flophouse" title="Flophouse">flophouses</a>, like the one at No.15 where the composer <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Foster" title="Stephen Foster">Stephen Foster</a> lived in 1864.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Dreiser" title="Theodore Dreiser">Theodore Dreiser</a> closed his tragedy <i><a href="/wiki/Sister_Carrie" title="Sister Carrie">Sister Carrie</a></i>, set in the 1890s, with the suicide of one of the main characters in a Bowery flophouse. The Bowery, which marked the eastern border of the slum of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Five_Points_(Manhattan)" title="Five Points (Manhattan)">Five Points</a>", had also become the turf of one of America's earliest street gangs, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Know_Nothings" title="Know Nothings">nativist</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bowery_Boys_(gang)" title="Bowery Boys (gang)">Bowery Boys</a>. In the spirit of social reform, the first <a href="/wiki/YMCA" title="YMCA">YMCA</a> opened on the Bowery in 1873;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> another notable religious and social welfare institution established during this period was the <a href="/wiki/Bowery_Mission" title="Bowery Mission">Bowery Mission</a>, which was founded in 1880 at 36 Bowery by Reverend <a href="/wiki/Albert_Gleason_Ruliffson" title="Albert Gleason Ruliffson">Albert Gleason Ruliffson</a>. The mission has relocated along the Bowery throughout its lifetime. From 1909 to the present, the mission has remained at 227–229 Bowery.</p>

<p>By the 1890s, the Bowery was a center for <a href="/wiki/Prostitution" title="Prostitution">prostitution</a> that rivaled the <a href="/wiki/Tenderloin,_Manhattan" title="Tenderloin, Manhattan">Tenderloin</a>, and for bars catering to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gay_community" title="Gay community">gay men and some lesbians</a> at various social levels, from The Slide at 157 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bleecker_Street_(Manhattan)" title="Bleecker Street (Manhattan)">Bleecker Street</a>, New York's "worst dive",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> to Columbia Hall at 5th Street, called Paresis Hall. One investigator in 1899 found six saloons and dance halls, the resorts of "degenerates" and "fairies", on the Bowery alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Gay subculture was more highly visible there and more integrated into working-class male culture than it was to become in the following generations, according to the historian of gay New York, George Chauncey.</p>
<p>From 1878 to 1955 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Avenue_El" title="Third Avenue El">Third Avenue El</a> ran above the Bowery, further darkening its streets, populated largely by men. "It is filled with employment agencies, cheap clothing and knickknack stores, cheap moving-picture shows, cheap lodging-houses, cheap eating-houses, cheap saloons", writers in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Century_Magazine" title="The Century Magazine">The Century Magazine</a></i> found it in 1919. "Here, too, by the thousands come sailors on shore leave,—notice the 'studios' of the tattoo artists,—and here most in evidence are the 'down and outs'".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Prohibition eliminated the Bowery's numerous saloons: One Mile House, the "stately old tavern... replaced by a cheap saloon"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> at the southeast corner of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rivington_Street_(Manhattan)" title="Rivington Street (Manhattan)">Rivington Street</a>, named for the battered milestone across the way,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> where the politicians of the East Side had made informal arrangements for the city's governance, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> was renovated for retail space in 1921, "obliterating all vestiges of its former appearance", <i>The New York Times</i> reported. Restaurant supply stores were among the businesses that had come to the Bowery,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-1921-12-11_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-1921-12-11-21">[21]</a></sup> and many remain to this day.</p>
<p>Pressure for a new name after World War I came to naught<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-1921-12-11_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-1921-12-11-21">[21]</a></sup> and in the 1920s and 1930s, it was an impoverished area. From the 1940s through the 1970s, the Bowery was New York City's "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skid_Row" title="Skid Row">Skid Row</a>," notable for "Bowery Bums" (disaffiliated <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">alcoholics</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeless" title="Homeless">homeless</a> persons).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Among those who wrote about Bowery personalities was <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">New Yorker</a></i> staff member <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Mitchell_(writer)" title="Joseph Mitchell (writer)">Joseph Mitchell</a> (1908–1996). Aside from cheap clothing stores that catered to the derelict and down-and-out population of men, commercial activity along the Bowery became specialized in used restaurant supplies and lighting fixtures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enc-nyc2_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-enc-nyc2-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Revival">Revival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Revival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The vagrant population of the Bowery declined after the 1970s, in part because of the city's effort to disperse it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enc-nyc2_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-enc-nyc2-2">[2]</a></sup> Since the 1990s the entire Lower East Side has been reviving. As of July 2005, <a href="/wiki/Gentrification" title="Gentrification">gentrification</a> is contributing to ongoing change along the Bowery. In particular, the number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-rise" title="High-rise">high-rise</a> <a href="/wiki/Condominium" title="Condominium">condominiums</a> is growing. In 2006, <a href="/wiki/AvalonBay_Communities" title="AvalonBay Communities">AvalonBay Communities</a> opened its first luxury apartment complex on the Bowery, which included an upscale <a href="/wiki/Whole_Foods_Market" title="Whole Foods Market">Whole Foods Market</a>. Avalon Bowery Place was quickly followed with the development of Avalon Bowery Place II in 2007. That same year, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SANAA_(firm)" title="SANAA (firm)">SANAA</a>-designed facility for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Museum_of_Contemporary_Art" title="New Museum of Contemporary Art">New Museum of Contemporary Art</a> opened between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanton_Street_(Manhattan)" title="Stanton Street (Manhattan)">Stanton</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Street" title="Prince Street">Prince Street</a>.</p>
<p>The new development has not come without a social cost. <a href="/wiki/Michael_Dominic" title="Michael Dominic">Michael Dominic's</a> <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">documentary</a> <i>Sunshine Hotel</i> followed the lives of residents of one of the few remaining flophouses.</p>
<p>The Bowery from <a href="/wiki/Houston_Street" title="Houston Street">Houston</a> to <a href="/wiki/Delancey_Street" title="Delancey Street">Delancey Street</a> serves as New York's principal market for restaurant equipment, and from Delancey to <a href="/wiki/Grand_Street_(Manhattan)" title="Grand Street (Manhattan)">Grand</a> for lamps.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Areas">Areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bowery_Historic_District">Bowery Historic District</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Bowery Historic District">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In October 2011, a Bowery Historic District was registered with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_State_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="New York State Register of Historic Places">New York State Register of Historic Places</a> and, because of that, automatically nominated for listing on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>. A grassroots community organization named Bowery Alliance of Neighbors (BAN) in association with the community-based housing organization called the Two Bridges Neighborhood Council led the effort for creation of the historic district. The designation means that property owners will have financial incentives to restore rather than demolish old buildings on the Bowery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clark_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clark-23">[23]</a></sup> BAN was recognized for its preservation efforts with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Village_Award" title="Village Award">Village Award</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> from the <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Village_Society_for_Historic_Preservation" title="Greenwich Village Society for Historic Preservation">Greenwich Village Society for Historic Preservation</a> in 2013.</p>
<p>The historic district runs from <a href="/wiki/Chatham_Square" title="Chatham Square">Chatham Square</a> to <a href="/wiki/Astor_Place" title="Astor Place">Astor Place</a> on both sides of the Bowery.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Little_Saigon">Little Saigon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Little Saigon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>New York's "<a href="/wiki/Little_Saigon" title="Little Saigon">Little Saigon</a>", though not officially designated, exists on the Bowery between Grand Street and Hester Street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/New_York_(magazine)" title="New York (magazine)"><i>New York</i> magazine</a> claims that while this street blends in with neighboring <a href="/wiki/Chinatown,_Manhattan" title="Chinatown, Manhattan">Chinatown</a>, the area is filled with <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_cuisine" title="Vietnamese cuisine">Vietnamese</a> restaurants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amato_Opera">Amato Opera</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Amato Opera">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Amato_Opera" title="Amato Opera">Amato Opera</a></div>
<p>This company, founded in 1948 by Tony Amato and his wife, Sally, found a permanent home at 319 Bowery next to the former <a href="/wiki/CBGB" title="CBGB">CBGB</a> and afforded many young singers the opportunity to hone their craft in full-length productions with a cut-down orchestration. The curtain fell on this well-established NYC opera forum on May 31, 2009, when Tony Amato retired.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bank_buildings">Bank buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Bank buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bowery_Savings_Bank" title="Bowery Savings Bank">Bowery Savings Bank</a> was chartered in May 1834, when the Bowery was an upscale residential street, and grew with the rising prosperity of the city. Its 1893 headquarters building remains a Bowery landmark, as does the 1920s domed Citizens Savings Bank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bowery_Ballroom">Bowery Ballroom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Bowery Ballroom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bowery_Ballroom" title="Bowery Ballroom">Bowery Ballroom</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bowery_Ballroom" title="Bowery Ballroom">Bowery Ballroom</a> is a music venue. The structure, at 6 Delancey Street, was built just before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stock_Market_Crash_of_1929" title="Stock Market Crash of 1929">Stock Market Crash of 1929</a>. It stood vacant until the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, when it became a high-end retail store. The neighborhood subsequently went into decline again, and so did the caliber of businesses occupying the space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In 1997 it was converted into a music venue. It has a capacity of 550 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Directly in front of the venue's entrance is the <a href="/wiki/Bowery_(BMT_Nassau_Street_Line)" title="Bowery (BMT Nassau Street Line)">Bowery Station</a> (<span class="nowrap"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J_(New_York_City_Subway_service)" title="J (New York City Subway service)">J</a></span> <span class="nowrap"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Z_(New_York_City_Subway_service)" title="Z (New York City Subway service)">Z</a></span> trains) of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Subway" title="New York City Subway">New York City Subway</a>.</p>
<p>The club serves as the namesake of at least one recording: <a href="/wiki/Joan_Baez" title="Joan Baez">Joan Baez</a>'s <i>Bowery Songs</i> album, recorded live at a concert at the Bowery Ballroom in November 2004.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bowery_Mural">Bowery Mural</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Bowery Mural">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Bowery Mural is an outdoor exhibition space located on the corner of <a href="/wiki/Houston_Street" title="Houston Street">Houston Street</a> and the Bowery, on a wall owned by Goldman Properties since 1984. Real estate developer <a href="/wiki/Tony_Goldman" title="Tony Goldman">Tony Goldman</a> began the project with Jeffery Deitch and <a href="/wiki/Deitch_Projects" title="Deitch Projects">Deitch Projects</a> in 2008. Goldman’s goal was to use this wall to present the top contemporary artists from around the world, with an emphasis on artists who work on the streets. Seasonal murals have appeared on the wall curated and organized in collaboration with The Hole, NYC, an art gallery in <a href="/wiki/SoHo,_Manhattan" title="SoHo, Manhattan">SoHo</a> run by former Deitch Projects directors Kathy Grayson and Meghan Coleman.</p>
<p>The mural series was initiated from March to December 2008 with a tribute to <a href="/wiki/Keith_Haring" title="Keith Haring">Keith Haring</a>’s noted 1982 Bowery mural. This was followed by a mural by the Brazilian twin-brother duo <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Os_G%C3%AAmeos" title="Os Gêmeos">Os Gêmeos</a>, which they dedicated to artist <a href="/wiki/Dash_Snow" title="Dash Snow">Dash Snow</a>, who had recently died from a drug overdose; this was presented from July 2009 to March 2010. The next mural, by <a href="/wiki/Shepard_Fairey" title="Shepard Fairey">Shepard Fairey</a>, was on exhibit from April through August 2010, and was followed by a mural by <a href="/wiki/Barry_McGee" title="Barry McGee">Barry McGee</a> which celebrated the role of <a href="/wiki/Graffiti#Tag" title="Graffiti">graffiti tagging</a> in the history of New York City street art; it was on display from August to November 2010. This was followed by a tribute to <a href="/wiki/Dash_Snow" title="Dash Snow">Dash Snow</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irak" title="Irak">Irak</a>, which ran from November 24–26, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Other artists to have murals presented include the twins How &amp; Nosm (2012), <a href="/wiki/John_Matos" title="John Matos">Crash</a> (2013), <a href="/wiki/Martha_Cooper" title="Martha Cooper">Martha Cooper</a> (2013), Revok and POSE (2013), <a href="/wiki/Swoon_(artist)" title="Swoon (artist)">Swoon</a> (2014), and <a href="/wiki/Maya_Hayuk" title="Maya Hayuk">Maya Hayuk</a> (whose mural was tag-bombed several times shortly after its completion in 2014).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bowery_Poetry">Bowery Poetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Bowery Poetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bowery_Poetry_Club" title="Bowery Poetry Club">Bowery Poetry Club</a></div>
<p>Bowery Poetry is a performance space at Bowery and Bleecker Street. Originally founded in 2001 as Bowery Poetry Club (BPC) it provided a home base for established and upcoming artists. It was founded by <a href="/wiki/Bob_Holman" title="Bob Holman">Bob Holman</a>, owner of the building and former <a href="/wiki/Nuyorican_Poets_Caf%C3%A9" title="Nuyorican Poets Café">Nuyorican Poets Café</a> Poetry Slam MC (1988–1996). The BPC featured regular shows by <a href="/wiki/Amiri_Baraka" title="Amiri Baraka">Amiri Baraka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anne_Waldman" title="Anne Waldman">Anne Waldman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taylor_Mead" title="Taylor Mead">Taylor Mead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taylor_Mali" title="Taylor Mali">Taylor Mali</a>, along with open mic, <a href="/wiki/Gay" title="Gay">gay</a> poets, a weekly poetry slam, and an <a href="/wiki/Emily_Dickinson" title="Emily Dickinson">Emily Dickinson</a> Marathon, amongst other events. The Club closed in 2012 and reopened in 2013 as a shared performance space under the name "Bowery Poetry". Bowery Arts + Science presents poetry, and Duane Park presents alternative <a href="/wiki/Burlesque" title="Burlesque">burlesque</a> in this space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bowery_Theatre">Bowery Theatre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Bowery Theatre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bowery_Theatre" title="Bowery Theatre">Bowery Theatre</a></div>
<p>The Bowery Theatre was a 19th-century playhouse at 46 Bowery. It was founded in the 1820s by rich families to compete with the upscale <a href="/wiki/Park_Theatre_(Manhattan)" title="Park Theatre (Manhattan)">Park Theatre</a>. By the 1850s, the theatre came to cater to immigrant groups such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_American" title="Irish American">Irish</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_American" title="German American">Germans</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_American" title="Chinese American">Chinese</a>. It burned down four times in 17 years, and a fire in 1929 destroyed it for good.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CBGB">CBGB</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: CBGB">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/CBGB" title="CBGB">CBGB</a></div>
<p>CBGB, a former club initially opened to play country, bluegrass &amp; blues (as the name CBGB stands for), began to book <a href="/wiki/Television_(band)" title="Television (band)">Television</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patti_Smith" title="Patti Smith">Patti Smith</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Ramones" title="Ramones">Ramones</a> as house bands in the mid-1970s. This spawned a full-blown scene of new bands (<a href="/wiki/Talking_Heads" title="Talking Heads">Talking Heads</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blondie_(band)" title="Blondie (band)">Blondie</a>, edgy R&amp;B-influenced <a href="/wiki/Mink_DeVille" title="Mink DeVille">Mink DeVille</a>, rockabilly revivalist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Gordon_(musician)" title="Robert Gordon (musician)">Robert Gordon</a>, and others) performing mostly original material in a mostly raw and often loud and fast attack. The label of <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk rock</a> was applied to the scene even if not all the bands that made their early reputations at the club were punk rockers, strictly speaking, but CBGB became known as the American cradle of punk rock. CBGB closed on October 31, 2006, after a long battle by club owner <a href="/wiki/Hilly_Kristal" title="Hilly Kristal">Hilly Kristal</a> to extend its lease. The space is now a <a href="/wiki/John_Varvatos" title="John Varvatos">John Varvatos</a> boutique.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Museum">New Museum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: New Museum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/New_Museum" title="New Museum">New Museum</a></div>
<p>In December 2007, the New Museum opened the doors of its new location at 235 Bowery, at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Street" title="Prince Street">Prince Street</a>, continuing its focus of exhibiting emerging international artists. This new facility, designed by the Tokyo-based firm Sejima + Nishizawa/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanaa_(firm)" title="Sanaa (firm)">SANAA</a> and the New York-based firm <a href="/wiki/Gensler" title="Gensler">Gensler</a>, has greatly expanded the Museum’s exhibitions and space. In March 2008, the museum's new building was named one of the architectural seven wonders by <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conde_Nast_Traveler" title="Conde Nast Traveler">Conde Nast Traveler</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nl_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-nl-34">[34]</a></sup> The museum has an ongoing <i>Bowery Project</i> honoring artists who lived on the Bowery with taped interviews and archived records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>Literature</b></p>
<ul>
<li>Bowery is the setting for <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Crane" title="Stephen Crane">Stephen Crane</a>'s first novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Maggie:_A_Girl_of_the_Streets" title="Maggie: A Girl of the Streets">Maggie: A Girl of the Streets</a></i> (published in 1893), about a poor family living in the neighborhood.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/New_York_School_(art)" title="New York School (art)">New York School</a> poet <a href="/wiki/Ted_Berrigan" title="Ted Berrigan">Ted Berrigan</a> mentions the Bowery several times in his seminal work "The Sonnets".</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kirby" title="Jack Kirby">Jack Kirby</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stan_Lee" title="Stan Lee">Stan Lee</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Fantastic_Four" title="Fantastic Four">Fantastic Four</a></i> #4 (1962), the <a href="/wiki/Human_Torch" title="Human Torch">Human Torch</a> flees to the Bowery to lose himself "among all the <i>other</i> human derelicts..." In one of the Bowery's flophouses, he discovers the amnesiac 1940s-era character <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Namor_the_Sub-Mariner" title="Namor the Sub-Mariner">Namor the Sub-Mariner</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Wild_Cards" title="Wild Cards">Wild Cards</a></i> series of books sets the Bowery as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jokertown" title="Jokertown">Jokertown</a>, the place where the malformed go to live after the Wild Card Virus is released over New York.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Brenda_Coultas" title="Brenda Coultas">Brenda Coultas</a>' 2003 book of poetry, <i>A Handmade Museum</i>, contains a section called "the Bowery Project" which documents the pre-<a href="/wiki/Gentrification" title="Gentrification">gentrification</a> process.</li>
</ul>

<p><b>Stage</b></p>
<ul>
<li>The phrase "On the Bowery", which has since fallen into disuse, was a generic way to say one was down-and-out. It originated in the song <a href="/wiki/The_Bowery_(song)" title="The Bowery (song)">"The Bowery"</a> from the 1891 musical <i><a href="/wiki/A_Trip_to_Chinatown" title="A Trip to Chinatown">A Trip to Chinatown</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> which included the chorus "The Bow’ry, The Bow’ry! / They say such things, / and they do strange things / on the Bow’ry"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>On the Bowery</i>, an 1894 play starring <a href="/wiki/Steve_Brodie_(bridge_jumper)" title="Steve Brodie (bridge jumper)">Steve Brodie</a>, supposed <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Bridge" title="Brooklyn Bridge">Brooklyn Bridge</a> jumper and Bowery saloonkeeper.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">Disney's</a> "<a href="/wiki/Newsies_(musical)" title="Newsies (musical)">Newsies</a>", the showgirls featured in the song, "I Never Planned On You/ Don't Come A-Knocking" are called the Bowery Beauties.</li>
</ul>

<li><i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Bowery" title="On the Bowery">On the Bowery</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Lionel_Rogosin" title="Lionel Rogosin">Lionel Rogosin</a>'s 1956 film, was nominated for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Documentary" title="Academy Award for Best Documentary">Academy Award for Best Documentary</a>.</li>
<li>In the 2002 film <i><a href="/wiki/Gangs_of_New_York" title="Gangs of New York">Gangs of New York</a></i>, Bowery is a mentioned territory of the <a href="/wiki/Bowery_Boys" title="Bowery Boys">Bowery Boys</a>, a street gang of the late 19th century during the New York Draft Riots.</li>
</ul>
<p><b>Art</b></p>
<ul>
<li><i>The Bowery in Two Inadequate Descriptive Systems,</i> a collection of photographs and poems by <a href="/wiki/Martha_Rosler" title="Martha Rosler">Martha Rosler</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li>"Come Closer: Art Around the Bowery, 1969–1989", "Drawing upon the New Museum’s Bowery Artist Tribute archive and the online archive of Marc H. Miller, 98bowery.com, this exhibition features original artwork, ephemera, and performance documentation by over fifteen artists who lived and worked on or near [the] Bowery in New York."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p><b>Advertising</b></p>
<ul>
<li>In the 1960s, radio and television commercials for the <a href="/wiki/Bowery_Savings_Bank" title="Bowery Savings Bank">Bowery Savings Bank</a> featured a jingle with the lyrics "The Bowery, The Bowery / The Bowery pays a lot / The Bowery pays you 6% / Commercial banks in New York simply do not." The number changed according to the amount of interest available on a <a href="/wiki/Passbook" title="Passbook">passbook savings account</a> offered by the bank.</li>
</ul>
<p><b>Wrestling</b></p>
<ul>
<li>Professional wrestler <a href="/wiki/Raven_(wrestler)" title="Raven (wrestler)">Raven</a> is billed as being from the Bowery despite his being born in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> and residing in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWEBio_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-WWEBio-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
