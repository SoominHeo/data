<p><b>Hyraxes</b> (from the Greek <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ὕραξ</span>, <i>hurax</i>, "shrewmouse"), also called <b>dassies</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> are small, thickset, herbivorous <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a> <b>Hyracoidea</b>. Hyraxes are well-furred, rotund animals with short tails. Typically, they measure between 30 and 70 cm (12 and 28 in) long and weigh between 2 and 5 kg (4.4 and 11 lb). They are superficially similar to <a href="/wiki/Pika" title="Pika">pikas</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a> (especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marmots" title="Marmots">marmots</a>), but are more closely related to <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manatee" title="Manatee">manatees</a>.</p>
<p>Four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extant_taxon" title="Extant taxon">extant</a> species are recognised; the <a href="/wiki/Rock_hyrax" title="Rock hyrax">rock hyrax</a> (<i>Procavia capensis</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Yellow-spotted_rock_hyrax" title="Yellow-spotted rock hyrax">yellow-spotted rock hyrax</a> (<i>Heterohyrax brucei</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Western_tree_hyrax" title="Western tree hyrax">western tree hyrax</a> (<i>Dendrohyrax dorsalis</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Southern_tree_hyrax" title="Southern tree hyrax">southern tree hyrax</a> (<i>Dendrohyrax arboreus</i>). Their distribution is limited to <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyrax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hyraxes retain a number of primitive mammalian characteristics; in particular, they have poorly developed internal temperature regulation,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> which they compensate for by behavioural <a href="/wiki/Thermoregulation" title="Thermoregulation">thermoregulation</a>, such as huddling together and basking in the sun.</p>
<p>Unlike most other browsing and grazing animals, they do not use the <a href="/wiki/Incisor" title="Incisor">incisors</a> at the front of the jaw for slicing off leaves and grass, rather, they use the <a href="/wiki/Molar_(tooth)" title="Molar (tooth)">molar</a> teeth at the side of the jaw. The two upper incisors are large and tusk-like, and grow continuously through life, similar to <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>. The four lower incisors are deeply grooved 'comb teeth'. There is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diastema_(dentistry)" title="Diastema (dentistry)">diastema</a> between the incisors and the cheek teeth. The <a href="/wiki/Dentition" title="Dentition">dental formula</a> for hyraxes is <span class="nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; padding:0 0.1em;" title="upper dentition: Incisors.Canines.Premolars.Molars">1.0.4.3</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; padding:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;" title="lower dentition: Incisors.Canines.Premolars.Molars">2.0.4.3</span></span></span>.</p>

<p>Although not <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">ruminants</a>, hyraxes have complex, multi-chambered stomachs that allow <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">symbiotic</a> bacteria to break down tough plant materials, but their overall ability to digest fibre is lower than that of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ungulates" title="Ungulates">ungulates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Their mandibular motions (see video) are deceptively similar to chewing <a href="/wiki/Cud" title="Cud">cud</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> but the hyrax is physically incapable of <a href="/wiki/Regurgitation_(digestion)" title="Regurgitation (digestion)">regurgitation</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> as in the <a href="/wiki/Even-toed_ungulate" title="Even-toed ungulate">even-toed ungulates</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merycism" title="Merycism">merycism</a> of some of the <a href="/wiki/Macropodidae" title="Macropodidae">macropods</a>. This behaviour is referred to in a passage in the Bible which erroneously describes hyraxes as chewing the cud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It has been suggested that this chewing behaviour is a form of <a href="/wiki/Agonistic_behaviour" title="Agonistic behaviour">agonistic behaviour</a> when the animal feels threatened.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zootorah.com_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-zootorah.com-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hyraxes inhabit rocky terrain across <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>. Their feet have rubbery pads with numerous sweat glands, which may help the animal maintain its grip when quickly moving up steep, rocky surfaces. Hyraxes have stumpy toes with hoof-like nails; there are four toes on each front foot and three on each back foot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> They also have efficient <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidneys</a>, retaining water so that they can better survive in arid environments.</p>
<p>Female hyraxes give birth to up to four young after a <a href="/wiki/Gestation" title="Gestation">gestation</a> period of between seven and eight months, depending on the species. The young are <a href="/wiki/Weaning" title="Weaning">weaned</a> at one to five months of age, and reach sexual maturity at 16 to 17 months.</p>
<p>Hyraxes live in small family groups, with a single male who aggressively defends the territory from rivals. Where there is abundant living space, the male may have sole access to multiple groups of females, each with their own range. The remaining males live solitary lives, often on the periphery of areas controlled by larger males, and mate only with younger females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoM_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoM-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hyraxes have highly charged <a href="/wiki/Myoglobin" title="Myoglobin">myoglobin</a>, which has been inferred to reflect an aquatic ancestry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Similarities_with_elephants_and_sirenia">Similarities with elephants and sirenia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyrax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Similarities with elephants and sirenia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hyraxes share several unusual characteristics with <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sirenia" title="Sirenia">sirenia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Manatee" title="Manatee">manatees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dugong" title="Dugong">dugongs</a>), which have resulted in them all being placed in the taxon <a href="/wiki/Paenungulata" title="Paenungulata">Paenungulata</a>. Male hyraxes lack a scrotum and their testicles remain tucked up in their abdominal cavity next to the kidneys,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carnaby2008_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carnaby2008-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sisson1914_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sisson1914-12">[12]</a></sup> the same as elephants, manatees, and dugongs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Female hyraxes have a pair of teats near their arm pits (<a href="/wiki/Axilla" title="Axilla">axilla</a>), as well as four teats in their <a href="/wiki/Groin" title="Groin">groin</a> (inguinal area); elephants have a pair of teats near their axillae, and dugongs and manatees have a pair of teats, one located close to each of the front flippers. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tusks" title="Tusks">tusks</a> of hyraxes develop from the <a href="/wiki/Incisor" title="Incisor">incisor</a> teeth as do the tusks of elephants; most mammalian tusks develop from the <a href="/wiki/Canine_tooth" title="Canine tooth">canines</a>. Hyraxes, like elephants, have flattened <a href="/wiki/Nail_(anatomy)" title="Nail (anatomy)">nails</a> on the tips of their digits, rather than curved, elongated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claws" title="Claws">claws</a> which are usually seen on mammals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_accounts">Historical accounts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyrax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Historical accounts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Klipdas.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1104" data-file-width="1656" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Klipdas.jpg/220px-Klipdas.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Klipdas.jpg/330px-Klipdas.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Klipdas.jpg/440px-Klipdas.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Klipdas.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Young hyrax on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Kenya" title="Mount Kenya">Mount Kenya</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The words "rabbit", "hare", or "coney" appear as terms for the hyrax in some English translations of the Bible. Early English translators had no knowledge of the hyrax (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> שָּׁפָן <i>shafan</i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and therefore no name for them. There are references to hyraxes in the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> particularly in Leviticus 11, where they are described as lacking a split hoof and therefore not being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kosher" title="Kosher">kosher</a>. It also describes the hyrax as chewing its cud; this is technically inaccurate as the hyrax does not regurgitate its food to chew it again. The Hebrew phrase in question (מַעֲלֵה גֵרָה) means "bringing up cud". Some of the modern translations refer to them as rock badgers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyrax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pachyhyrax_championi.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2931" data-file-width="4683" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Pachyhyrax_championi.JPG/220px-Pachyhyrax_championi.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Pachyhyrax_championi.JPG/330px-Pachyhyrax_championi.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Pachyhyrax_championi.JPG/440px-Pachyhyrax_championi.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pachyhyrax_championi.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Pachyhyrax championi</i>, a large fossil hyrax from the <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> of Rusinga, Kenya (Natural History Museum collection)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>All modern hyraxes are members of the family Procaviidae (the only living family within Hyracoidea) and are found only in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>. In the past, however, hyraxes were more diverse, and widespread. The order first appears in the fossil record at a site in the Middle East in the form of <i><a href="/wiki/Dimaitherium" title="Dimaitherium">Dimaitherium</a></i>, 37 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> For many millions of years, hyraxes were the primary terrestrial herbivore in Africa, just as <a href="/wiki/Odd-toed_ungulate" title="Odd-toed ungulate">odd-toed ungulates</a> were in North America.</p>
<p>Through the middle to late <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a>, there were many different species,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> the largest of them weighing the same as a small horse and the smallest the size of a mouse. During the <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a>, however, competition from the newly developed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bovid" title="Bovid">bovids</a>, which were very efficient grazers and browsers, displaced the hyraxes into marginal niches. Nevertheless, the order remained widespread and diverse as late as the end of the <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a> (about two million years ago) with representatives throughout most of Africa, <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>.</p>
<p>The descendants of the giant 'hyracoids' (common ancestors to the hyraxes, elephants, and sirenians) evolved in different ways. Some became smaller, and evolved to become the modern hyrax family. Others appear to have taken to the water (perhaps like the modern <a href="/wiki/Capybara" title="Capybara">capybara</a>), ultimately giving rise to the elephant family and perhaps also the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sirenian" title="Sirenian">sirenians</a>. <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> evidence supports this hypothesis, and the small modern hyraxes share numerous features with elephants, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toenail" title="Toenail">toenails</a>, excellent hearing, sensitive pads on their feet, small tusks, good memory, higher brain functions compared to other similar mammals, and the shape of some of their <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hyraxes are sometimes described as being the closest living relative to the elephant,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> although whether this is so is disputed. Recent morphological and molecular-based classifications reveal the sirenians to be the closest living relatives of elephants. While hyraxes are closely related, they form a taxonomic <a href="/wiki/Outgroup_(cladistics)" title="Outgroup (cladistics)">outgroup</a> to the assemblage of elephants, sirenians, and extinct orders such as <a href="/wiki/Embrithopoda" title="Embrithopoda">Embrithopoda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Desmostylia" title="Desmostylia">Desmostylia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Asher_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Asher-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extinct" title="Extinct">extinct</a> <a href="/wiki/Meridiungulata" title="Meridiungulata">meridiungulate</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Archaeohyracidae" title="Archaeohyracidae">Archaeohyracidae</a>, consisting of four <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genera</a> of <a href="/wiki/Notoungulata" title="Notoungulata">notoungulate</a> <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> known from the <a href="/wiki/Paleocene" title="Paleocene">Paleocene</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Oligocene" title="Oligocene">Oligocene</a> of <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> is a group unrelated to the true hyraxes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extant_species">Extant species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyrax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Extant species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 2000s, taxonomists <a href="/wiki/Lumpers_and_splitters" title="Lumpers and splitters">reduced</a> the number of recognized species of hyrax. In 1995, there were eleven or more recognized species; in 2013, only four are recognized, with the others now each considered as a subspecies of one of the recognized four. There are over 50 recognized subspecies and species, many of which are considered highly endangered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSW3_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSW3-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
