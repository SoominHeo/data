<p>The <b>picathartes</b>, <b>rockfowl</b> or <b>bald crows</b> are a small genus of two <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> forming the family <b>Picathartidae</b> found in the rain-forests of tropical west and central <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. They have unfeathered heads, and feed on insects and invertebrates picked from damp rocky areas. Both species are totally <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">non-migratory</a>, being dependent on a specialised rocky jungle habitat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW12_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-HBW12-1">[1]</a></sup> Both species are listed as <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">vulnerable</a> to extinction on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Picathartes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The taxonomic position of the <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> and its two species has been confusing. At various times, it has been grouped with the babblers, flycatchers, starlings, crows and others before being placed in a family of its own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/William_Serle" title="William Serle">Serle</a> in 1952 thought it resembled the Asian genus <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eupetes" title="Eupetes">Eupetes</a></i> while <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sibley" title="Charles Sibley">Sibley</a> used egg-albumin protein similarity, determined by <a href="/wiki/Electrophoresis" title="Electrophoresis">electrophoresis</a>, to suggest that it belonged to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timaliidae" title="Timaliidae">Timaliidae</a>. <a href="/wiki/Storrs_L._Olson" title="Storrs L. Olson">Olson</a> revived the idea that it was related to <i>Eupetes</i> in 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> A molecular sequence based study suggests that it may indeed be closely related to the crows and placed somewhere at the boundary between the <a href="/wiki/Passerida" title="Passerida">Passerida</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corvida" title="Corvida">Corvida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>This generic name comes from a combination of the Latin genera <i>pica</i> for "magpie" and <i>cathartes</i> for "vulture".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Species">Species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Picathartes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/White-necked_rockfowl" title="White-necked rockfowl">White-necked rockfowl</a> (<i>Picathartes gymnocephalus</i>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Grey-necked_rockfowl" title="Grey-necked rockfowl">Grey-necked rockfowl</a> (<i>Picathartes oreas</i>)</li>
</ul>
<p>A possible third species may exist in <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>, in the vicinity of the <a href="/wiki/Kazinga_Channel" title="Kazinga Channel">Kazinga Channel</a>, linking <a href="/wiki/Lake_Edward" title="Lake Edward">Lake Edward</a> with <a href="/wiki/Lake_George_(Uganda)" title="Lake George (Uganda)">Lake George</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Picathartes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PicathartesReichenow.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2623" data-file-width="1854" height="241" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/PicathartesReichenow.jpg/170px-PicathartesReichenow.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/PicathartesReichenow.jpg/255px-PicathartesReichenow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/PicathartesReichenow.jpg/340px-PicathartesReichenow.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PicathartesReichenow.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A 1902 illustration of the two species</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The picathartes are large (33–38 centimetres (13–15 in) long) <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerines</a> with <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crow-like</a> black bills, long neck, tail and legs. They weigh between 200–250 grams (7.1–8.8 oz). The strong feet and grey legs are adapted to terrestrial movement, and the family progresses through the forest with long bounds on the ground. The wings are long but are seldom used for long <a href="/wiki/Bird_flight" title="Bird flight">flights</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> is similar between the two species, with white breasts and bellies and darker (grey and grey-black) wings, backs and tails. The neck color varies between the two species, giving them their individual names (grey-necked and white-necked picathartes). They also have bald heads with brightly coloured and patterned skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW12_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-HBW12-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Picathartes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rockfowl are generalised feeders, taking a wide range of invertebrate prey. Prey items include a range of insects, particularly <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ants</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Millipede" title="Millipede">millipedes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Centipede" title="Centipede">centipedes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastropod" title="Gastropod">gastropods</a>. <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">Frogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a> are also taken, but these are mostly fed to their chicks. Prey is taken both by foraging on the ground and in the trees. They will also forage in shallow flowing water for crabs. When foraging on the ground, they move forward with hops and bounds, then pausing to search for prey. The longish bill is used to turn over leaves and seize prey, but the feet are never used for either. Both species will <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ant-follower" title="Ant-follower">follow</a> swarms of ants in order to snatch prey fleeing the ants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW12_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-HBW12-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both species of rockfowl breed seasonally in the <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">wet season</a>. Where an area experiences two wet seasons in a year, they will breed twice in that year. Despite reports of cooperative breeding, it is now thought that they are exclusively <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a>, breeding in pairs. They are also commonly reported to be <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonial</a>, and will breed in colonies of up to seven pairs, but solitary breeders and smaller colonies of just two pairs are more common. The <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a> is made of mud attached to a cave roof or overhanging rock on a cliff. The nest is a cup-like structure of dried leaves, twigs and plant fibres set into dried mud. Two eggs are laid, 24 to 48 hours apart. Both parents participate in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" title="Avian incubation">incubating</a> the eggs, each taking 12-hour shifts before being relieved by their partner. It takes around 20 days for the eggs to hatch. Picathartes hatchlings are <a href="/wiki/Altricial" title="Altricial">altricial</a> at hatching, almost naked (a few feathers are present on the crown and back) and helpless. The chicks take around 25 days to <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW12_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-HBW12-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Picathartes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The rockfowl are distributed in west and western <a href="/wiki/Central_Africa" title="Central Africa">Central Africa</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Guinea" title="Guinea">Guinea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone" title="Sierra Leone">Sierra Leone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">Ivory Coast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a> (<a href="/wiki/White-necked_rockfowl" title="White-necked rockfowl">white-necked rockfowl</a>), <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea">Equatorial Guinea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">Gabon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Republic of the Congo">Republic of the Congo</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Republic" title="Central African Republic">Central African Republic</a> (<a href="/wiki/Grey-necked_rockfowl" title="Grey-necked rockfowl">grey-necked rockfowl</a>). The rockfowl live in lowland rainforest at up to 800 m, in rocky and hilly terrain on the slopes of hills and mountains. These birds require forest litter for foraging, a large enough area to contain army-ant swarms (<a href="/wiki/Dorylinae" title="Dorylinae">Dorylinae</a>), and rocks, cliffs or caves for nesting sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW12_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-HBW12-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
