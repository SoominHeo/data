<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Binaural recording</h1>

<p><b>Binaural recording</b> is a method of <a href="/wiki/Recording" title="Recording">recording</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_recording" title="Sound recording">sound</a> that uses two <a href="/wiki/Microphone" title="Microphone">microphones</a>, arranged with the intent to create a <a href="/wiki/Three-dimensional_space" title="Three-dimensional space">3-D</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereo_sound" title="Stereo sound">stereo sound</a> sensation for the listener of actually being in the room with the performers or instruments. This effect is often created using a technique known as "<a href="/wiki/Dummy_head_recording" title="Dummy head recording">dummy head recording</a>", wherein a <a href="/wiki/Mannequin" title="Mannequin">mannequin</a> head is outfitted with a microphone in each ear. Binaural recording is intended for replay using headphones and will not translate properly over stereo speakers. This idea of a three dimensional or "internal" form of sound has also translated into useful advancement of technology in many things such as stethoscopes creating "in-head" acoustics and IMAX movies being able to create a three dimensional acoustic experience.</p>
<p>The term "binaural" has frequently been confused as a synonym for the word "<a href="/wiki/Stereophonic_sound" title="Stereophonic sound">stereo</a>", and this is partially due to a large amount of misuse in the mid-1950s by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recording_industry" title="Recording industry">recording industry</a>, as a <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">marketing</a> <a href="/wiki/Buzzword" title="Buzzword">buzzword</a>. Conventional stereo recordings do not factor in natural ear spacing or "<a href="/wiki/Head_shadow" title="Head shadow">head shadow</a>" of the head and ears, since these things happen naturally as a person listens, generating their own <a href="/wiki/Interaural_time_difference" title="Interaural time difference">ITDs</a> (interaural time differences) and ILDs (interaural level differences). Because loudspeaker-crosstalk of conventional stereo interferes with binaural reproduction, either headphones are required, or crosstalk cancellation of signals intended for loudspeakers such as <a href="/wiki/Ambiophonics" title="Ambiophonics">Ambiophonics</a>. For listening using conventional speaker-stereo, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_audio_player" title="Digital audio player">mp3 players</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinna_(anatomy)" title="Pinna (anatomy)">pinna</a>-less dummy head may be preferable for quasi-binaural recording, such as the sphere microphone or <a href="/wiki/Ambiophonics" title="Ambiophonics">Ambiophone</a>. As a general rule, for true binaural results, an audio recording and reproduction system chain, from microphone to listener's brain, should contain one and only one set of pinnae (preferably the listener's own) and one head-shadow.</p>

<p>With a simple recording method, two microphones are placed 18 cm (7") apart facing away from each other. This method will not create a real binaural recording. The distance and placement roughly approximates the position of an average human's <a href="/wiki/Ear_canal" title="Ear canal">ear canals</a>, but that is not all that is needed. More elaborate techniques exist in pre-packaged forms. A typical binaural recording unit has two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-fidelity" title="High-fidelity">high-fidelity</a> microphones mounted in a dummy head, inset in ear-shaped <a href="/wiki/Molding_(process)" title="Molding (process)">molds</a> to fully capture all of the <a href="/wiki/Audio_frequency" title="Audio frequency">audio frequency</a> adjustments (known as <a href="/wiki/Head-related_transfer_function" title="Head-related transfer function">head-related transfer functions</a> (HRTFs) in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychoacoustic" title="Psychoacoustic">psychoacoustic</a> research community) that happen naturally as sound wraps around the human head and is "shaped" by the form of the outer and inner <a href="/wiki/Ear" title="Ear">ear</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georg_Neumann_GmbH" title="Georg Neumann GmbH">Neumann</a> KU-81, and KU-100 are the most commonly used binaural packages, especially by musicians. A simplified version of binaural recordings can be achieved using microphones with a separating element, like the <a href="/wiki/Jecklin_Disk" title="Jecklin Disk">Jecklin Disk</a>. Not all cues required for exact localization of the sound sources can be preserved this way, but it also works well for loudspeaker reproduction.</p>
<p>In the late 1960s, <a href="/wiki/Aiwa" title="Aiwa">Aiwa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a> offered headphones with a pair of microphones mounted on the headband around two inches above the ears. These allowed pseudo-binaural recordings to be made.</p>
<p>Miniature binaural "in-ear" or "near-ear" microphones can be linked to a portable Digital Audio Tape (DAT) or MiniDisc recorder, bypassing the need for a dummy head by using the recordist's own head. The first clip-in binaural microphones using the recordist's own head were offered by Sennheiser in 1974. The first clip-on binaural microphones using the recordist's own head were offered by <a href="/wiki/Core_Sound_LLC" title="Core Sound LLC">Core Sound LLC</a> in 1989. The first completely "in-ear" binaural microphones using the recordist's own head were offered by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sound_Professionals&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sound Professionals (page does not exist)">Sound Professionals</a> in 1999. <a href="/wiki/Roland_Corporation" title="Roland Corporation">Roland Corporation</a> also offers its CS-10EM in-ear binaural microphone set.</p>

The key constituents that cause the 3D stereo effect: timing, loudness and <a href="/wiki/Timbre" title="Timbre">timbre</a>. Sound coming from the left arrives first to the left ear and microseconds later to the right ear. Head muffles the sound making the sound louder to the left ear than to the right ear. The head and other parts of the body deflect the sound thus changing the sound's frequency spectrum along its way from the left side to the right side. The human brain interprets these differences and automatically causes a sensation of a certain location for the sound to the listening person.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Once recorded, the binaural effect can be reproduced using headphones or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dipole_stereo" title="Dipole stereo">dipole stereo</a>. It does not work with <a href="/wiki/Monaural" title="Monaural">mono</a> playback; nor does it work while using loudspeaker units, as the acoustics of this arrangement distort the channel separation via natural crosstalk (an approximation can be obtained if the listening environment is carefully designed by employing expensive crosstalk cancellation equipment.)</p>
<p>Any set of headphones that provides good right and left channel isolation is sufficient to hear the immersive effects of the recording, and anyone who has even a cheap set of headphones can enjoy the recordings.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Several high-end head set manufacturers have created some units specifically for the playback of binaural. In addition, a number of headphone amplifier companies have created hardware that takes advantage of these special recordings. However, these in-ear-canal phones tend to suffer from poor externalization i.e. inside-head localization.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It is also found that even normal headphones suffer from poor externalization, especially if the headphone completely blocks the ear from outside. A better design for externalization found in experiments is the open-ear one, where the drivers are sitting in front of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinna_(anatomy)" title="Pinna (anatomy)">pinnae</a> with the ear canal connected to the air. The hypothesis is that when the ear canal is completely blocked, the <a href="/wiki/Radiation_impedance" title="Radiation impedance">radiation impedance</a> seen from the eardrum to the outside has been altered, which negatively affects externalization.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>There are some complications with the playback of binaural recordings through normal headphones. The sound that is picked up by a microphone placed in or at the entrance of the ear channel has a frequency spectrum that is very different from the one that would be picked up by a free-standing microphone. The diffuse-field head-transfer function (HRTF), that is, the frequency response at the ear drum averaged for sounds coming from all possible directions, is quite grotesque, with peaks and dips exceeding 10 <a href="/wiki/Decibel" title="Decibel">dB</a>. Frequencies from around 2 kHz to 5 kHz in particular are strongly amplified as compared to free field presentation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-innerfidelity_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-innerfidelity-2">[2]</a></sup> Being near the ear, headphones bypass this effect so if they had a flat response, they would sound much too 'dull' when used to listen to stereo recordings. For this reason, most headphones are designed to provide the same 2 kHz to 5 kHz boost that the head and ear would provide to natural free-field sounds. This can only approximate a particular listener's ears, meaning that there can be no such thing as a 'flat' pair of headphones – they have to match the listener's ears, unlike speakers which aim to produce a flat free field response. For binaural listening though, the headphones need to have a flat response to the ear-canal entrance (not the eardrum) since it is at the ear-canal entrance that the recording microphones are placed. While it is in theory possible to equalise any headphones for a flat response at the entrance, a better approach is to use headphones designed without the notch in the first place.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>That most headphones are actually designed to have a boost in the 2 kHz to 5 kHz region is not widely understood, and this may have led to errors in the assessment of binaural recordings, since flat headphones are not normally available, and 'high quality' headphones incorporate the boost just like any others, though perhaps with a better approximation to that needed by the average ear. Male and female ears differ in size, and the outer ear gets bigger with increasing age, raising the possibility that most headphones are only really suited to young men. If headphones were designed to be flat, with a choice of equalisations available in headphone amplifiers, then headphone listening could be a more controlled experience, whether in binaural or stereo mode.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Binaural_recording&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The history of binaural recording goes back to 1881. The first binaural unit, the <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9%C3%A2trophone" title="Théâtrophone">Théâtrophone</a>, was an array of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> <a href="/wiki/Telephone" title="Telephone">telephone</a> microphones installed along the front edge of the <a href="/wiki/Palais_Garnier" title="Palais Garnier">Opera Garnier</a>. The signal was sent to subscribers through the <a href="/wiki/Telephone" title="Telephone">telephone</a> system, and required that they wear a special head set, which had a tiny speaker for each ear.</p>
<p>The novelty wore off, and there wasn't significant interest in the <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> until around forty years later when a Connecticut <a href="/wiki/Radio_station" title="Radio station">radio station</a> began to broadcast binaural shows. Stereo radio had not yet been implemented, so the station actually broadcast the left channel on one <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> and the right channel on a second. Listeners would then have to own two radios, and plug the right and left ear pieces of their head sets into each radio. Naturally, the expense of owning two radios was, at the time, too much for a broad audience, and again binaural faded into obscurity.</p>
<p>In 1978, <a href="/wiki/Lou_Reed" title="Lou Reed">Lou Reed</a> released the first commercially produced binaural pop record, <i><a href="/wiki/Street_Hassle" title="Street Hassle">Street Hassle</a></i>, a combination of live and studio recordings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-billboard_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-billboard-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Binaural stayed in the background due to the expensive, specialized equipment required for quality recordings, and the requirement of headphones for proper reproduction. Particularly in pre-<a href="/wiki/Walkman" title="Walkman">Walkman</a> days, most consumers considered headphones an inconvenience, and were only interested in recordings that could be listened to on a home stereo system or in automobiles. Lastly, the types of things that can be recorded do not have a typically high <a href="/wiki/Market_value" title="Market value">market value</a>. Recordings that are done in <a href="/wiki/Studio" title="Studio">studios</a> would have little to benefit from using a binaural set up, beyond natural cross-feed, as the spatial quality of the studio would not be very dynamic and interesting. Recordings that are of interest are live <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orchestral" title="Orchestral">orchestral</a> performances, and ambient "environmental" recordings of city sounds, nature, and other such subject matters.</p>
<p>The modern era has seen a resurgence of interest in binaural, partially due to the widespread availability of headphones, and cheaper methods of recording. A small <a href="/wiki/Grassroots" title="Grassroots">grassroots</a> movement of people building their own recording sets and swapping them on the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> has joined the very few <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CD" title="CD">CDs</a> available for purchase. The <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_sensory_meridian_response" title="Autonomous sensory meridian response">ASMR</a> internet community is another movement of late which has widely employed binaural recordings.</p>
<p>In 2013 <a href="/wiki/David_Cittadini" title="David Cittadini">David Cittadini</a>, along with Andrew Hills, used binaural recording techniques and technologies on the Australian short film <a class="external text" href="http://www.imdb.com/title/tt3262160/" rel="nofollow">The Blind Passenger</a>. On 29 August 2013 and 31 August 2013 they recorded The Metropolitan Orchestra using binaural recording techniques, the first recording of an orchestra in Australia using binaural recording techniques.</p>
