<p>A <b>binaural beat</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Auditory_illusion" title="Auditory illusion">auditory illusion</a> <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perceived</a> when two different pure-tone <a href="/wiki/Sine_wave" title="Sine wave">sine waves</a>, both with <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequencies</a> lower than 1500 Hz, with less than a 40 Hz difference between them, are presented to a <a href="/wiki/Hearing" title="Hearing">listener</a> dichotically (one through each <a href="/wiki/Ear" title="Ear">ear</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> For example, if a 530 Hz pure <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">tone</a> is presented to a subject's right ear, while a 520 Hz pure tone is presented to the subject's left ear, the listener will <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perceive</a> the <a href="/wiki/Auditory_illusion" title="Auditory illusion">auditory illusion</a> of a third tone, in addition to the two pure-tones presented to each ear. The third sound is called a binaural beat, and in this example would have a <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perceived</a> <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">pitch</a> correlating to a frequency of 10 Hz, that being the difference between the 530 Hz and 520 Hz pure tones presented to each ear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Binaural_beats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term 'binaural' literally signifies 'to hear with two ears ', and was introduced in 1859 to signify the practice of listening to the same sound through both ears, or to two discrete sounds, one through each ear. It was not until 1916 that <a href="/wiki/Carl_Stumpf" title="Carl Stumpf">Carl Stumpf</a> (1848-1936), a German <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosopher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychologist</a>, distinguished between dichotic listening, which refers to the stimulation of each ear with a different <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology)" title="Stimulus (physiology)">stimulus</a>, and diotic listening, the simultaneous stimulation of both ears with the same stimulus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later, it would be become apparent that binaural hearing, whether dichotic or diotic, is the means by which the <a href="/wiki/Geolocation" title="Geolocation">geolocation</a> and direction of a <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a> is determined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scientific consideration of binaural hearing began before the phenomenon was so named, with the ideas articulated in 1792 by <a href="/wiki/William_Charles_Wells" title="William Charles Wells">William Charles Wells</a> (1757–1817), a Scottish-American <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">printer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physician</a> at <a href="/wiki/St_Thomas%27_Hospital" title="St Thomas' Hospital">Saint Thomas' Hospital</a>, London. Wells sought to theoretically examine and explain aspects of human <a href="/wiki/Hearing" title="Hearing">hearing</a>, including the way in which listening with two ears rather than one might affect the perception of sound, which proceeded from his research into <a href="/wiki/Binocular_vision" title="Binocular vision">binocular vision</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Subsequently, between 1776 and 1802, <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Venturi" title="Giovanni Battista Venturi">Giovanni Battista Venturi</a> (1746 - 1822), an Italian <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physicist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Savant_syndrome" title="Savant syndrome">savant</a>, man of letters, <a href="/wiki/Diplomat" title="Diplomat">diplomat</a>, and <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">historian</a> of <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>, conducted and described a series of <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">experiments</a> intended to elucidate the nature of binaural <a href="/wiki/Hearing" title="Hearing">hearing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It was in an appendix to a monograph on color that <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Venturi" title="Giovanni Battista Venturi">Venturi</a> described experiments on <a href="/wiki/Auditory_system" title="Auditory system">auditory</a> <a href="/wiki/Geolocation" title="Geolocation">localization</a> using one or two ears, concluding that "the inequality of the two impressions, which are perceived at the same time by both ears, determines the correct direction of the sound."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, none of <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Venturi" title="Giovanni Battista Venturi">Venturi's</a> contemporaries at the end of the eighteenth and beginning of the nineteenth centuries considered his original work worthy of citation or attention, with the exception of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ernst_Florens_Friedrich_Chladni" title="Ernst Florens Friedrich Chladni">Ernst Florens Friedrich Chladni</a> (1756–1827), a German <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physicist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">musician</a>, who is widely cited as the father of acoustics. After investigating the behavior of <a href="/wiki/Vibration" title="Vibration">vibrating</a> strings and plates, and examining the way in which <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a> appeared to be <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perceived</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ernst_Florens_Friedrich_Chladni" title="Ernst Florens Friedrich Chladni">Chladni</a> acknowledged <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Venturi" title="Giovanni Battista Venturi">Venturi's</a> work, agreeing with him that the ability to determine the <a href="/wiki/Geolocation" title="Geolocation">location</a>, and direction of sound depended upon detected differences in a sound between both ears, including <a href="/wiki/Amplitude" title="Amplitude">amplitude</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a>, subsequently denoted by the term 'interaural differences'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other significant historic investigations into binaural hearing include those of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Wheatstone" title="Charles Wheatstone">Charles Wheatstone</a> (1802–1875), an English scientist, whose many inventions included the <a href="/wiki/Concertina" title="Concertina">concertina</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Stereoscope" title="Stereoscope">stereoscope</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Heinrich_Weber" title="Ernst Heinrich Weber">Ernst Heinrich Weber</a> (1795–1878), a German physician cited as one of the founders of <a href="/wiki/Experimental_psychology" title="Experimental psychology">experimental psychology</a>; and <a href="/wiki/August_Seebeck" title="August Seebeck">August Seebeck</a> (1805–1849), a scientist at the <a href="/wiki/Technische_Hochschule" title="Technische Hochschule">Technische Universität</a>, Dresden, remembered for his work on <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hearing" title="Hearing">hearing</a>. Like <a href="/wiki/William_Charles_Wells" title="William Charles Wells">Wells</a>, these researchers attempted to compare and contrast what would become known as binaural hearing with the principles of binocular integration generally, and binocular color mixing specifically. They found that <a href="/wiki/Binocular_vision" title="Binocular vision">binocular vision</a> did not follow the laws of combination of colors from different bands of the <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">spectrum</a>. Rather, it was found that when presenting a different color to each eye, they did not combine, but often competed for <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perceptual</a> <a href="/wiki/Attentional_control" title="Attentional control">attention</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meanwhile, of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Wheatstone" title="Charles Wheatstone">Wheatstone</a> conducted experiments in which he presented a different <a href="/wiki/Tuning_fork" title="Tuning fork">tuning fork</a> to each ear, stating:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>It is well known, that when two consonant sounds are heard together, a third sound results from the coincidences of their vibrations; and that this third sound, which is called the grave harmonic, is always equal to unity, when the two primitive sounds are represented by the lowest integral numbers. This being premised, select two tuning-forks the sounds of which differ by any consonant interval excepting the octave; place the broad sides of their branches, while in vibration, close to one ear, in such a manner that they shall nearly touch at the acoustic axis; the resulting grave harmonic will then be strongly audible, combined with the two other sounds; place afterwards one fork to each ear, and the consonance will be heard much richer in volume, but no audible indications whatever of the third sound will be perceived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Charles_Wheatstone" title="Charles Wheatstone">Wheatstone's</a> reference to the perceptual fusion of harmonically related tones were directly related to the principles examined by <a href="/wiki/William_Charles_Wells" title="William Charles Wells">Wells</a>. However, both their observations were ignored and remained uncited by contemporaraneous and subsequent German researchers of the following decades.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Venturi" title="Giovanni Battista Venturi">Venturi's</a> experiments were repeated and confirmed by <a href="/wiki/John_William_Strutt,_3rd_Baron_Rayleigh" title="John William Strutt, 3rd Baron Rayleigh">Lord Rayleigh</a> (1842–1919), almost seventy-five years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other investigators of the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, who were contemporaries of <a href="/wiki/John_William_Strutt,_3rd_Baron_Rayleigh" title="John William Strutt, 3rd Baron Rayleigh">Lord Rayleigh</a>, also investigated the significance of binaural hearing. These included <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_Trenchard_More&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Louis Trenchard More (page does not exist)">Louis Trenchard More</a> (1870-1944), a <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">professor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Harry_Shipley_Fry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Harry Shipley Fry (page does not exist)">Harry Shipley Fry</a> (1878-1949), a lecturer in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, both at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cincinnati" title="University of Cincinnati">University of Cincinnati</a>; <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=H._A._Wilson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="H. A. Wilson (page does not exist)">H. A. Wilson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Samuel_Myers" title="Charles Samuel Myers">Charles Samuel Myers</a>, both professors of science at <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_London" title="King's College London">King's College London</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Alfred_M._Mayer" title="Alfred M. Mayer">Alfred M. Mayer</a> (1836 - 1897), an American physicist, each of whom conducted experimental investigations with intent to discover the means by which human subjects ascertain the location, origin, and direction of sound, believing this to be in some way dependent on dichotic hearing, that is listening to sound through both ears.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Understanding of how the difference in <a href="/wiki/Sound#Longitudinal_and_transverse_waves" title="Sound">sound signal</a> between two ears contributes to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auditory_processing" title="Auditory processing">auditory processing</a> in such a way as to enable the <a href="/wiki/Geolocation" title="Geolocation">location</a> and direction of sound to be determined was considerably advanced after the invention of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Differential_stethophone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Differential stethophone (page does not exist)">differential stethophone</a> by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Somerville_Scott_Alison&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Somerville Scott Alison (page does not exist)">Somerville Scott Alison</a> in 1859, who coined the term 'binaural'. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Somerville_Scott_Alison&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Somerville Scott Alison (page does not exist)">Alison</a> based his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stethophone" title="Stethophone">stethophone</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Stethoscope" title="Stethoscope">stethoscope</a>, a previous invention of <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Laennec" title="René Laennec">René Théophile Hyacinthe Laennec</a> (1781–1826).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike the <a href="/wiki/Stethoscope" title="Stethoscope">stethoscope</a>, which had only a single sound-source piece placed upon the chest, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Somerville_Scott_Alison&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Somerville Scott Alison (page does not exist)">Alison's</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stethophone" title="Stethophone">stethophone</a> had two separate ones, allowing the user to hear and compare sounds derived from two discrete locations. This allowed a <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physician</a> to identify the source of a sound through the process of binaural hearing. Subsequently, Alison referred to his invention as a 'binaural stethoscope', describing it as:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>…an instrument consisting of two hearing-tubes, or trumpets, or stethoscopes, provided with collecting-cups and ear-knobs, one for each ear respectively. The two tubes are, for convenience, mechanically combined, but may be said to be acoustically separate, as care is taken that the sound, once admitted into one tube, is not communicated to the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Neurophysiology">Neurophysiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Binaural_beats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Neurophysiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cortical_Oscillation_and_Electroencephalography_.28EEG.29">Cortical Oscillation and Electroencephalography (EEG)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Binaural_beats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cortical Oscillation and Electroencephalography (EEG)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The activity of <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neurons</a> generate <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric currents</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Synchronization" title="Synchronization">synchronous</a> action of <a href="/wiki/Neural_ensemble" title="Neural ensemble">neural ensembles</a> in the cerebral cortex, comprising large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neurons</a>, produce macroscopic <a href="/wiki/Neural_oscillation" title="Neural oscillation">oscillations</a>, which can be monitored and graphically documented by an <a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">electroencephalogram</a> (EEG). The <a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">electroencephalographic</a> representations of those <a href="/wiki/Neural_oscillation" title="Neural oscillation">oscillations</a> are typically denoted by the term 'brainwaves' in common parlance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Neural_oscillation" title="Neural oscillation">Neural oscillations</a> are rhythmic or repetitive electrochemical activity in the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a>. Such <a href="/wiki/Oscillation" title="Oscillation">oscillations</a> can be characterized by their <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amplitude" title="Amplitude">amplitude</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phase_(waves)" title="Phase (waves)">phase</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nervous_tissue" title="Nervous tissue">Neural tissue</a> can generate oscillatory activity driven by mechanisms within individual <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neurons</a>, as well as by interactions between them. They may also adjust <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> to <a href="/wiki/Synchronization" title="Synchronization">synchronize</a> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wave_period" title="Wave period">periodicity</a> of an external <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">acoustic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">visual</a> <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology)" title="Stimulus (physiology)">stimuli</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The technique of recording <a href="/wiki/Neural_oscillation" title="Neural oscillation">neural electrical activity</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a> from <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemical</a> readings taken from the <a href="/wiki/Scalp" title="Scalp">scalp</a> originated with the experiments of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Caton" title="Richard Caton">Richard Caton</a> in 1875, whose findings were developed into <a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">electroencephalography</a> (EEG) by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Berger" title="Hans Berger">Hans Berger</a> in the late 1920s.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Frequency_bands_of_cortical_neural_ensembles">Frequency bands of cortical neural ensembles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Binaural_beats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Frequency bands of cortical neural ensembles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The fluctuating <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oscillation" title="Oscillation">oscillations</a> generated by the <a href="/wiki/Synchronization" title="Synchronization">synchronous</a> activity of <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex" title="Cerebral cortex">cortical</a> <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neurons</a>, measurable with an <a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">electroencephalogram</a> (EEG), via <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrodes</a> attached to the <a href="/wiki/Scalp" title="Scalp">scalp</a>, are conveniently categorized into general bands, in order of decreasing frequency, measured in <a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">Hertz</a> (Hz) as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gamma_wave" title="Gamma wave">Gamma</a>, 30 to 50 Hz</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Beta_wave" title="Beta wave">Beta</a>, 14 to 30 Hz</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alpha_wave" title="Alpha wave">Alpha</a>, 8 to 14 Hz</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theta_rhythm" title="Theta rhythm">Theta</a>, 4 to 8 Hz</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Delta_wave" title="Delta wave">Delta</a>, 0.1 to 4 Hz</li>
</ul>
<p>In addition, three further <a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">wave</a> forms are often delineated in <a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">electroencephalographic</a> studies:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mu_wave" title="Mu wave">Mu</a>, 8 to 12 Hz</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sleep_spindle" title="Sleep spindle">Sigma (sleep spindle)</a>, 12 to 14 Hz</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sensorimotor_rhythm" title="Sensorimotor rhythm">SMR (Sensory motor rhythm)</a>, 12.5 to 15.5 Hz<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>It was Berger who first described the <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> bands <a href="/wiki/Delta_wave" title="Delta wave">Delta</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theta_rhythm" title="Theta rhythm">Theta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alpha_wave" title="Alpha wave">Alpha</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Beta_wave" title="Beta wave">Beta</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neurophysiological_origin_of_binaural_beat_perception">Neurophysiological origin of binaural beat perception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Binaural_beats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Neurophysiological origin of binaural beat perception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Binaural-beat <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perception</a> originates in the <a href="/wiki/Inferior_colliculus" title="Inferior colliculus">inferior colliculus</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Midbrain" title="Midbrain">midbrain</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Superior_olivary_complex" title="Superior olivary complex">superior olivary complex</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Brainstem" title="Brainstem">brainstem</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Audio_signal_processing" title="Audio signal processing">auditory signals</a> from each <a href="/wiki/Ear" title="Ear">ear</a> are integrated and precipitate <a href="/wiki/Action_potential" title="Action potential">electrical impulses</a> along <a href="/wiki/Neural_pathway" title="Neural pathway">neural pathways</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Reticular_formation" title="Reticular formation">reticular formation</a> up the <a href="/wiki/Midbrain" title="Midbrain">midbrain</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Thalamus" title="Thalamus">thalamus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Auditory_cortex" title="Auditory cortex">auditory cortex</a>, and other cortical regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Neural_oscillations_and_mental_state">Neural oscillations and mental state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Binaural_beats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Neural oscillations and mental state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the technique of measuring such <a href="/wiki/Neural_oscillation" title="Neural oscillation">brainwaves</a> by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Berger" title="Hans Berger">Berger</a>, there has remained a ubiquitous consensus that <a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">electroencephalogram</a> (EEG) readings depict <a href="/wiki/Neural_oscillation" title="Neural oscillation">brainwave</a> wave form patterns that alter over time, and correlate with the aspects of the subject's mental and <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">emotional state</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mental_status_examination" title="Mental status examination">mental status</a>, and degree of <a href="/wiki/Consciousness" title="Consciousness">consciousness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alertness" title="Alertness">vigilance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> It is therefore now established and accepted that discreet <a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">electroencephalogram</a> (EEG) measurements, including frequency and amplitude of neural oscillations, correlate with different perceptual, motor and cognitive states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Furthermore, <a href="/wiki/Neural_oscillation" title="Neural oscillation">brainwaves</a> alter in response to changes in environmental <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology)" title="Stimulus (physiology)">stimuli</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a> and <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a>; and while the degree and nature of alteration is partially dependent on individual <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perception</a>, such that the same stimulus may precipitate differing changes in neural oscillations and correlating <a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">electroencephalogram</a> (EEG) readings in different subjects, the frequency of cortical neural oscillations, as measured by the EEG, has also been shown to <a href="/wiki/Synchronization" title="Synchronization">synchronize</a> with or entrain to that of an external <a href="/wiki/Entrainment_(biomusicology)" title="Entrainment (biomusicology)">acoustic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">photic</a> stimulus, with accompanying alterations in <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognitive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">emotional</a> state. This process is called neuronal entrainment or <a href="/wiki/Brainwave_entrainment" title="Brainwave entrainment">brainwave entrainment</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Entrainment">Entrainment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Binaural_beats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Entrainment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meaning_and_origin_of_the_term_.27entrainment.27">Meaning and origin of the term 'entrainment'</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Binaural_beats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Meaning and origin of the term 'entrainment'">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Entrainment is a term originally derived from <a href="/wiki/Complex_systems" title="Complex systems">complex systems theory</a>, and denotes the way that two or more independent, autonomous <a href="/wiki/Oscillation" title="Oscillation">oscillators</a> with differing rhythms or <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequencies</a>, when situated in a context and at a proximity where they can interact for long enough, influence each other mutually, to a degree dependent on <a href="/wiki/Couple_(mechanics)" title="Couple (mechanics)">coupling force</a>, such that they adjust until both oscillate with the same frequency. Examples include the <a href="/wiki/Injection_locking#Entrainment" title="Injection locking">mechanical entrainment</a> or cyclic <a href="/wiki/Synchronization" title="Synchronization">synchronization</a> of two electric clothes dryers placed in close proximity, and the biological entrainment evident in the synchronized illumination of <a href="/wiki/Firefly" title="Firefly">fireflies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Entrainment is a concept first identified by the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physicist</a> <a href="/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens" title="Christiaan Huygens">Christiaan Huygens</a> in 1665 who discovered the phenomenon during an experiment with <a href="/wiki/Pendulum" title="Pendulum">pendulum</a> clocks: He set them each in motion and found that when he returned the next day, the sway of their pendulums had all <a href="/wiki/Synchronization" title="Synchronization">synchronized</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>Such entrainment occurs because small amounts of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> are transferred between the two systems when they are out of <a href="/wiki/Phase_(waves)" title="Phase (waves)">phase</a> in such a way as to produce <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback" title="Negative feedback">negative feedback</a>. As they assume a more stable <a href="/wiki/Phase_(waves)" title="Phase (waves)">phase</a> relationship, the amount of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> gradually reduces to zero, with system of greater <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> slowing down, and the other speeding up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Subsequently, the term 'entrainment' has been used to describe a shared tendency of many physical and <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biological</a> systems to <a href="/wiki/Synchronization" title="Synchronization">synchronize</a> their periodicity and <a href="/wiki/Rhythm" title="Rhythm">rhythm</a> through interaction. This tendency has been identified as specifically pertinent to the study of <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a> and <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a> generally, and <a href="/wiki/Acoustics" title="Acoustics">acoustic</a> rhythms specifically. The most ubiquitous and familiar examples of neuromotor <a href="/wiki/Entrainment_(biomusicology)" title="Entrainment (biomusicology)">entrainment to acoustic stimuli</a> is observable in spontaneous foot or finger tapping to the rhythmic beat of a <a href="/wiki/Song" title="Song">song</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exogenous_entrainment">Exogenous entrainment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Binaural_beats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Exogenous entrainment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Exogenous <a href="/wiki/Rhythm" title="Rhythm">rhythmic</a> entrainment, which occurs outside the body, has been identified and documented for a variety of human activities, which include the way people adjust the <a href="/wiki/Rhythm" title="Rhythm">rhythm</a> of their <a href="/wiki/Speech" title="Speech">speech</a> patterns to those of the subject with whom they communicate, and the rhythmic unison of an audience clapping.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>Even among groups of strangers, the rate of <a href="/wiki/Breathing" title="Breathing">breathing</a>, locomotive and subtle expressive <a href="/wiki/Neuromuscular_junction" title="Neuromuscular junction">motor movements</a>, and rhythmic <a href="/wiki/Speech" title="Speech">speech</a> patterns have been observed to <a href="/wiki/Synchronization" title="Synchronization">synchronize</a> and entrain, in response to an <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">auditory stimuli</a>, such as a piece of <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a> with a consistent <a href="/wiki/Rhythm" title="Rhythm">rhythm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Furthermore, <a href="/wiki/Neuromuscular_junction" title="Neuromuscular junction">motor synchronization</a> to repetitive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Touch" title="Touch">tactile</a> <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology)" title="Stimulus (physiology)">stimuli</a> occurs in animals, including <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monkey" title="Monkey">monkeys</a> as well as humans, with accompanying shifts in <a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">electroencephalogram</a> (EEG) readings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Endogenous_entrainment">Endogenous entrainment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Binaural_beats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Endogenous entrainment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Examples of endogenous entrainment, which occurs within the body, include the <a href="/wiki/Entrainment_(chronobiology)" title="Entrainment (chronobiology)">synchronizing</a> of human <a href="/wiki/Circadian_rhythm" title="Circadian rhythm">circadian</a> sleep-wake cycles to the 24-hour cycle of light and dark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Synchronization" title="Synchronization">synchronization</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Heart_rate_variability" title="Heart rate variability">heartbeat</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Artificial_cardiac_pacemaker" title="Artificial cardiac pacemaker">cardiac pacemaker</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brainwave_entrainment">Brainwave entrainment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Binaural_beats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Brainwave entrainment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Brainwave_entrainment" title="Brainwave entrainment">Brainwave entrainment</a></div>
<p>Brainwaves, or <a href="/wiki/Neural_oscillation" title="Neural oscillation">neural oscillations</a>, share the fundamental constituents with <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">acoustic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">optical</a> <a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">wave forms</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amplitude" title="Amplitude">amplitude</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wave_period" title="Wave period">periodicity</a>. Consequently, Huygens' discovery precipitated inquiry into whether or not the <a href="/wiki/Synchronization" title="Synchronization">synchronous</a> <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electrical activity</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex" title="Cerebral cortex">cortical</a> <a href="/wiki/Neural_ensemble" title="Neural ensemble">neural ensembles</a> might not only alter in response to external <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">acoustic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">optical</a> <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology)" title="Stimulus (physiology)">stimuli</a> but also entrain or <a href="/wiki/Synchronization" title="Synchronization">synchronize</a> their <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> to that of a specific <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology)" title="Stimulus (physiology)">stimulus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Brainwave_entrainment" title="Brainwave entrainment">Brainwave entrainment</a> is a colloquialism for such 'neural entrainment', which is a term used to denote the way in which the aggregate <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oscillation" title="Oscillation">oscillations</a> produced by the synchronous electrical activity in ensembles of <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex" title="Cerebral cortex">cortical</a> <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neurons</a> can adjust to <a href="/wiki/Synchronization" title="Synchronization">synchronize</a> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wave_period" title="Wave period">periodicity</a> of an external <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology)" title="Stimulus (physiology)">stimuli</a>, such as a sustained <a href="/wiki/Acoustics" title="Acoustics">acoustic</a> <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> perceived as <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">pitch</a>, a regularly repeating pattern of intermittent sounds, perceived as <a href="/wiki/Rhythm" title="Rhythm">rhythm</a>, or a regularly rhythmically intermittent flashing light.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_frequency_following_response_and_auditory_driving">The frequency following response and auditory driving</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Binaural_beats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: The frequency following response and auditory driving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Frequency_following_response" title="Frequency following response">Frequency following response</a></div>
<p>The hypothesized entrainment of <a href="/wiki/Neural_oscillation" title="Neural oscillation">neural oscillations</a> to the frequency of an <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">acoustic stimulus</a> occurs by way of the <a href="/wiki/Frequency_following_response" title="Frequency following response">Frequency following response</a> (FFR), also referred to as Frequency Following Potential (FFP). The use of sound with intent to influence <a href="/wiki/Neural_oscillation" title="Neural oscillation">brainwave</a> cortical brainwave frequency is called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Auditory_driving&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Auditory driving (page does not exist)">auditory driving</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Auditory_driving&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Auditory driving (page does not exist)">Auditory driving</a> refers to the hypothesized ability for repetitive rhythmic <a href="/wiki/Auditory_system" title="Auditory system">auditory</a> <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology)" title="Stimulus (physiology)">stimuli</a> to 'drive' <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neural</a> <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric</a> activity to entrain with it. By the principles of such hypotheses, it is proposed that, for example, a subject who hears drum rhythms at 8 beats per second, will be influenced such that an <a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">electroencephalogram</a> (EEG) reading will show an increase brainwave activity at 8 Hz range, in the upper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theta_rhythm" title="Theta rhythm">theta</a>, lower <a href="/wiki/Alpha_wave" title="Alpha wave">alpha</a> band.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Binaural_beats_and_neural_entrainment">Binaural beats and neural entrainment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Binaural_beats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Binaural beats and neural entrainment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the problems inherent in any <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific</a> investigation conducted in order to ascertain whether <a href="/wiki/Neural_oscillation" title="Neural oscillation">brainwaves</a> can entrain to the <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">acoustic stimulus</a> is that human subjects rarely hear frequencies below 20 Hz, which is exactly the range of <a href="/wiki/Delta_wave" title="Delta wave">Delta</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theta_rhythm" title="Theta rhythm">Theta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alpha_wave" title="Alpha wave">Alpha</a>, and low to mid <a href="/wiki/Beta_wave" title="Beta wave">Beta</a> <a href="/wiki/Neural_oscillation" title="Neural oscillation">brainwaves</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> Among the methods by which some investigations have sought to overcome this problem is to measure <a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">electroencephalogram</a> (EEG) readings of a subject while he or she listens to binaural beats. Some researchers have published experimental results indicating that such listening precipitates <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Auditory_driving&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Auditory driving (page does not exist)">auditory driving</a> by which ensembles of cortical neurons entrain their <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequencies</a> to that of the binaural beat, with associated changes in self-reported subjective experience of <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">emotional</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognitive</a> state, though the latter results are found only in alternative medicine journals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> It is however possible that the real cause of the observed changes is the headphones, rather than the sound itself, as demonstrated in 2002 during a University of Virginia presentation at the Society for Psychophysiologial Research. It was shown that <a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">EEG</a> changes did not occur when the standard electromagnetic headphones were replaced by air conduction headphones, which were connected to a remote transducer by rubber tubes. This suggests that the basis for the entrainment effects is electromagnetic rather than acoustical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> Additionally, other researchers, such as Vernon et al. found that "broad-band and narrow-band amplitudes, and frequency showed no effect of binaural beat frequency eliciting a frequency following effect in the EEG."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> Similarly, Guruprasath and Gnanavel's 2015 study also found "no significant difference or frequency following effect elicited by either continuous or short burst stimulation was observed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> Yet other studies found both EEG power increases and decreases as well as generally negative effects on synchronization, suggesting that, although entrainment doesn't occur, monaural and binaural beats could potentially be a noninvasive way to affect brain waves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Binaural_beats_and_music">Binaural beats and music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Binaural_beats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Binaural beats and music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many of the aforementioned reports are based on the use of <a href="/wiki/Auditory_system" title="Auditory system">auditory</a> <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology)" title="Stimulus (physiology)">stimuli</a> that combine binaural beats with other sounds, including <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a> and <a href="/wiki/Speech" title="Speech">verbal guidance</a>. This consequently precludes the attribution of any influence on or positive outcome for the listener specifically to the <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perception</a> of the binaural beats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> Very few studies have sought to isolate the effect of binaural beats on listeners. However, initial findings in one experiment suggest that listening to binaural beats may exert an influence on both low frequency and high frequency components of <a href="/wiki/Heart_rate_variability" title="Heart rate variability">heart rate variability</a>, and may increase subjective <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">feelings</a> of <a href="/wiki/Relaxation_(psychology)" title="Relaxation (psychology)">relaxation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup></p>
<p>Notwithstanding this problem, a review of research findings suggest that listening to <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a> can modulate <a href="/wiki/Autonomic_nervous_system" title="Autonomic nervous system">autonomic</a> <a href="/wiki/Arousal" title="Arousal">arousal</a> through <a href="/wiki/Brainwave_entrainment" title="Brainwave entrainment">entrainment</a> of <a href="/wiki/Neural_oscillation" title="Neural oscillation">neural oscillations</a>. Furthermore, <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a> generally, and <a href="/wiki/Rhythm" title="Rhythm">rhythmic patterns</a>, such as those produced by <a href="/wiki/Percussion_instrument" title="Percussion instrument">percussive performance</a> including <a href="/wiki/Drum" title="Drum">drumming</a> specifically, have been shown to influence <a href="/wiki/Arousal" title="Arousal">arousal</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ergotropical&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ergotropical (page does not exist)">ergotropically</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Trophotropical&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Trophotropical (page does not exist)">trophotropically</a>, increasing and decreasing <a href="/wiki/Arousal" title="Arousal">arousal</a> respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> Such <a href="/wiki/Auditory_system" title="Auditory system">auditory stimulation</a> has been demonstrated to improve <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune function</a>, facilitate <a href="/wiki/The_Relaxation_Response" title="The Relaxation Response">relaxation</a>, improve <a href="/wiki/Mood_(psychology)" title="Mood (psychology)">mood</a>, and contribute to the alleviation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(psychology)" title="Stress (psychology)">stress</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meanwhile, the therapeutic benefits of listening to <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a> and <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a>, whether or not the outcome can be attributed to <a href="/wiki/Brainwave_entrainment" title="Brainwave entrainment">neural entrainment</a>, is a well-established principle upon which the practice of receptive <a href="/wiki/Music_therapy" title="Music therapy">music therapy</a> is founded. The term 'receptive music therapy' denotes a process by which patients or participants listen to <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a> with specific intent to therapeutically benefit; and is a term used by therapists to distinguish it from 'active music therapy' by which patients or participants engage in producing <a href="/wiki/Human_voice" title="Human voice">vocal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Musical_instrument" title="Musical instrument">instrumental</a> <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup></p>
<p>Receptive music therapy is an effective adjunctive <a href="/wiki/Mind%E2%80%93body_interventions" title="Mind–body interventions">intervention</a> suitable for treating a range of <a href="/wiki/Human_body" title="Human body">physical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mind" title="Mind">mental</a> conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meanwhile, the evident changes in <a href="/wiki/Brainwave_entrainment" title="Brainwave entrainment">neural oscillations</a> precipitated by listening to music, which are demonstrable through <a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">electroencephalogram</a> (EEG) measurements,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup> have contributed to the development of <a href="/wiki/Neurologic_music_therapy" title="Neurologic music therapy">neurologic music therapy</a>, which uses music and song as an active and receptive intervention, to contribute to the treatment and management of disorders characterized by impairment to parts of the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Traumatic_brain_injury" title="Traumatic brain injury">traumatic brain injury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Parkinson's disease">Parkinson's disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huntington%27s_disease" title="Huntington's disease">Huntington's disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_palsy" title="Cerebral palsy">cerebral palsy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alzheimer%27s_disease" title="Alzheimer's disease">Alzheimer's disease</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Autism" title="Autism">autism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non_ordinary_states_of_consciousness">Non ordinary states of consciousness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Binaural_beats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Non ordinary states of consciousness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Altered_state_of_consciousness" title="Altered state of consciousness">Altered state of consciousness</a></div>
<p>Historically, <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a> generally, and <a href="/wiki/Percussion_instrument" title="Percussion instrument">percussive performance</a> specifically was and remains integral to <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritual</a> <a href="/wiki/Ceremony" title="Ceremony">ceremony</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spiritual_practice" title="Spiritual practice">spiritual practice</a> among <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">early and indigenous peoples</a> and their descendants, where it is often used to induce the <a href="/wiki/Altered_state_of_consciousness" title="Altered state of consciousness">non ordinary state of consciousness</a> (NOSC) believed by participants to be a requisite for communication with <a href="/wiki/Spirit" title="Spirit">spiritual</a> <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energies</a> and entities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup></p>
<p>While there is no scientific evidence for existence of such energy or entities, and consequently no human capacity to communicate with such beings, the findings of some contemporary research suggests that listening to <a href="/wiki/Rhythm" title="Rhythm">rhythmic</a> <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sounds</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Percussion_instrument" title="Percussion instrument">percussion</a>, can induce the subjective experience of a <a href="/wiki/Altered_state_of_consciousness" title="Altered state of consciousness">non ordinary states of consciousness</a>
