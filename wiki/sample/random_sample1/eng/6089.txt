<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <b>vinyl</b> or <b>ethenyl</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is the <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functional group</a> −<a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">C</a><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H</a>=CH<sub>2</sub>, namely the <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC" title="IUPAC">IUPAC</a> ethene) molecule (H<sub>2</sub>C=CH<sub>2</sub>) minus one <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> atom. The name is also used for any compound containing that group, namely R−CH=CH<sub>2</sub> where R is any other group of atoms.</p>
<p>An industrially important example is <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_chloride" title="Vinyl chloride">vinyl chloride</a>, precursor to <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">PVC</a>, a plastic commonly known as <i>vinyl</i>.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rollup_chessboard.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="1844" height="239" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Rollup_chessboard.jpg/220px-Rollup_chessboard.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Rollup_chessboard.jpg/330px-Rollup_chessboard.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Rollup_chessboard.jpg/440px-Rollup_chessboard.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Vinyl is one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkenyl" title="Alkenyl">alkenyl</a> functional groups. On a carbon skeleton, <i>sp</i><sup>2</sup>-hybridized carbons or positions are often called <b>vinylic</b>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allyl" title="Allyl">Allyls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acrylate" title="Acrylate">acrylates</a> and styrenics contain vinyl groups. (A styrenic crosslinker with two vinyl groups is called <i>divinyl benzene</i>.)</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vinyl_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a> of <i>vinyl</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>vinum</i> = "<a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a>", because of its relationship with <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a> (in its original sense of <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethyl alcohol</a>). The term "vinyl" was coined by the German chemist <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Kolbe" title="Hermann Kolbe">Hermann Kolbe</a> in 1851.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vinyl_polymers">Vinyl polymers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vinyl_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vinyl polymers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_polymer" title="Vinyl polymer">Vinyl polymer</a></div>
<p>Vinyl groups can <a href="/wiki/Polymerization" title="Polymerization">polymerize</a> with the aid of a radical initiator or a catalyst, forming <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_polymer" title="Vinyl polymer">vinyl polymers</a>. In these polymers, the double bonds of the vinyl <a href="/wiki/Monomer" title="Monomer">monomers</a> turn into single bonds and the different monomers are joined by single bonds. Vinyl groups do not exist in vinyl polymer; the term refers to the precursor. It is sometimes important to ascertain the absence of unreacted vinyl monomer in the final product when the monomer is toxic or reduces the performance of the plastic. The following table gives some examples of vinyl polymers.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Vinylidene_group" title="Vinylidene group">vinylidene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vinylene_group" title="Vinylene group">vinylene</a> derivatives can polymerize in the same manner.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactivity">Reactivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vinyl_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reactivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vinyl derivatives are <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a>. If activated by an adjacent group, the increased polarization of the bond gives rise to characteristic reactivity, which is termed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vinylogous" title="Vinylogous">vinylogous</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allyl" title="Allyl">allyl</a> compounds, where the next carbon is saturated but substituted once, <a href="/wiki/Allylic_rearrangement" title="Allylic rearrangement">allylic rearrangement</a> and related reactions are observed.
<ul>
<li>Allyl <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grignard_reagent" title="Grignard reagent">Grignard reagents</a> (organomagnesiums) can attack with the vinyl end first.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>If next to an electron-withdrawing group, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conjugate_addition" title="Conjugate addition">conjugate addition</a> (Michael addition) occurs.</li>
</ul>
<p>Vinyl organometallics, e.g. vinyl lithium, participate in coupling reactions such as in <a href="/wiki/Negishi_coupling" title="Negishi coupling">Negishi coupling</a>.</p>
