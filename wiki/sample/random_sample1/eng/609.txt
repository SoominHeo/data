<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Third-hand smoke</h1>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Third-hand_smoke&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>third-hand smoke</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologism</a> coined by a research team from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dana%E2%80%93Farber/Harvard_Cancer_Center" title="Dana–Farber/Harvard Cancer Center">Dana–Farber/Harvard Cancer Center</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scientificamerican_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-scientificamerican-2">[2]</a></sup> The 'third-hand' component of the term is a reference to the remnants on surfaces after "<a href="/wiki/Passive_smoking" title="Passive smoking">second-hand smoke</a>" has cleared out. The term <i><a href="/wiki/Smoke" title="Smoke">first-hand smoke</a></i> refers to what is inhaled into the smoker's own lungs, while <i>second-hand smoke</i> is a mixture of exhaled smoke and other substances leaving the smoldering end of the <a href="/wiki/Cigarette" title="Cigarette">cigarette</a> that enters the atmosphere and can be inhaled by others; third-hand smoke, by that token, is contamination on the surfaces of objects that remains after the second-hand smoke has cleared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scientificamerican_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-scientificamerican-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_harm">Potential harm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Third-hand_smoke&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Potential harm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although "second-hand smoke" dissipates from a room or confined space after a short period of time, nicotine and other components of the smoke tend to coat a space's surfaces including skin, hair and clothing and continue to emit toxins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scientificamerican_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-scientificamerican-2">[2]</a></sup> Third-hand smoke is environmental tobacco smoke that has oxidized with environmental nitrous acid to create carcinogens not seen in cigarette ingredients or tobacco smoke. The carcinogens found in third hand smoke are known as tobacco-specific nitrosamines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> A 2010 study published in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proceedings_of_the_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences">Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences</a> journal found that the residue of the nicotine coating smokers as well as, interior car or room surfaces can react with <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_acid" title="Nitrous acid">nitrous acid</a> present in the air to create <a href="/wiki/Tobacco-specific_nitrosamines" title="Tobacco-specific nitrosamines">tobacco-specific nitrosamines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogens</a> found in tobacco products. This was demonstrated by spraying what was termed a "high but reasonable" level of nitrous acid (about 4–12 times the levels typically found in homes) onto cellulose substrates used to wipe the interior of a vehicle that had been heavily smoked in over time. Similar results were found when cellulose substrates were kept (without wiping) in the same vehicle for three days when smoking occurred. Ensuring ventilation while a cigarette is smoked does not eliminate the deposition of third-hand smoke in an enclosed space, according to the study's authors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msnbc_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-msnbc-5">[5]</a></sup> The study found that eleven <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogenic</a> compounds could be found in third-hand smoke, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactivity" title="Radioactivity">radioactive</a> element <a href="/wiki/Polonium" title="Polonium">polonium-210</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-6">[6]</a></sup> Third-hand smoke is thought to potentially cause the greatest harm to infants and young children, because younger children are more likely to put their hands in their mouths, be cuddled up to a smoker with toxins on their skin and clothes. Infants also crawl on the floor and eat from their hands without washing them first, ingesting the toxins into their systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_awareness_and_implications">Public awareness and implications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Third-hand_smoke&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Public awareness and implications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Third-hand smoke is a relatively newly postulated concept, and public awareness of it is lower than that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second-hand_smoke" title="Second-hand smoke">second-hand smoke</a>. However awareness campaigns are steadily growing in number and primarily focus on the health risks for young children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A 2014 study published in the journal <a href="/wiki/Pediatrics_(journal)" title="Pediatrics (journal)"><i>Pediatrics</i></a> demonstrated that parents are more likely to attempt to quit smoking if they become convinced that third-hand smoke is harmful to children and more likely to have smoke-free home and car policies if they are aware of the dangers of third-hand smoke. It was recommended for parents to safeguard their children by ensuring they have a smoke free zone. One such way recommended for smokers to protect family, friends and others is washing skin and hair and changing clothes before coming in to contact with others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, research has shown that parents who are heavy smokers (&gt; 10 cigarettes per day) are less likely to believe that third-hand smoke is harmful to children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Third-hand smoke is one of the issues promoting indoor smoking bans, especially supported by performers such as musicians, who are forced to bring third-hand smoke contamination into their homes through contaminated instruments and cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
