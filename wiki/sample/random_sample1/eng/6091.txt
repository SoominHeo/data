<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Viroid</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Viroids</b> are the smallest infectious pathogens known. They are solely composed of a short strand of circular, single-stranded, <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a> without protein coat. All known viroids are inhabitants of higher plants, in which most cause diseases, some of which are of slight to catastrophic economic importance.</p>
<p>The first recognized viroid, the pathogenic agent of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Potato_spindle_tuber_disease&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Potato spindle tuber disease (page does not exist)">potato spindle tuber disease</a>, was discovered, initially molecularly characterized, and named by <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Otto_Diener" title="Theodor Otto Diener">Theodor Otto Diener</a>, plant pathologist at the U.S Department of Agriculture's Research Center in Beltsville, Maryland, in 1971.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid5095900_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid5095900-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ARS_timeline_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ARS_timeline-2">[2]</a></sup> This viroid is now called <a href="/wiki/Potato_spindle_tuber_viroid" title="Potato spindle tuber viroid">Potato spindle tuber viroid</a>, abbreviated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PSTVd" title="PSTVd">PSTVd</a>.</p>
<p>Discovery of the viroid triggered the third major extension of the biosphere in history to include smaller lifelike entities—-after the discovery of the “subvisible” microorganisms by Antonie van Leeuwenhoek in 1675 and the “submicroscopic” viruses by Dmitri Iosifovich Ivanovsky in 1892. The unique properties of viroids have been recognized by the International Committee for Virus Taxonomy with the creation of a new order of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subviral_agents" title="Subviral agents">subviral agents</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>As cogently expressed by Flores et al.:“viruses (and viroids) share the most characteristic property of living beings: In an appropriate environment, they are able to generate copies of themselves, in other words, they are endowed with autonomous replication (and evolution). It is in this framework where viroids represent the frontier of life (246 to 467nt), an aspect that should attract the attention of anybody interested in biology.”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although viroids are composed of nucleic acid, they do not code for any <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The viroid's replication mechanism uses <a href="/wiki/RNA_polymerase_II" title="RNA polymerase II">RNA polymerase II</a>, a host cell enzyme normally associated with synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Messenger_RNA" title="Messenger RNA">messenger RNA</a> from DNA, which instead catalyzes "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rolling_circle" title="Rolling circle">rolling circle</a>" synthesis of new RNA using the viroid's RNA as a template. Some viroids are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribozymes" title="Ribozymes">ribozymes</a>, having <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalytic" title="Catalytic">catalytic</a> properties which allow self-cleavage and ligation of unit-size genomes from larger replication intermediates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>With Diener’s 1989 hypothesis<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> that viroids may represent "living relics" from the widely assumed, ancient, and non-cellular <a href="/wiki/RNA_world" title="RNA world">RNA world</a>—-extant before the evolution of DNA or proteins—-viroids have assumed significance beyond plant pathology to evolutionary science, by representing the most plausible RNAs capable of performing crucial steps in <a href="/wiki/Abiogenesis" title="Abiogenesis">abiogenesis</a>, the evolution of life from inanimate matter (see update below)</p>
<p>The human pathogen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hepatitis_D_virus" title="Hepatitis D virus">hepatitis D virus</a> is a "defective" RNA virus similar to a viroid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transmission">Transmission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viroid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Transmission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Viroids-_how_it_do.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="843" data-file-width="1500" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Viroids-_how_it_do.gif/220px-Viroids-_how_it_do.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Viroids-_how_it_do.gif/330px-Viroids-_how_it_do.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Viroids-_how_it_do.gif/440px-Viroids-_how_it_do.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Viroid infections are transmitted by cross contamination following mechanical damage to plants as a result of horticultural or agricultural practices. Some are transmitted by <a href="/wiki/Aphid" title="Aphid">aphids</a>, and they can also be transferred from plant to plant by leaf contact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nt_10-9"><a href="#cite_note-nt-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Replication">Replication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viroid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Replication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Viroids replicate in the nucleus (<i>Pospiviroidae</i>) or chloroplasts (<i>Avsunviroidae</i>) of plant cells in three steps through an RNA-based mechanism. They require <a href="/wiki/RNA_polymerase_II" title="RNA polymerase II">RNA polymerase II</a>, a host cell enzyme normally associated with synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Messenger_RNA" title="Messenger RNA">messenger RNA</a> from DNA, which instead catalyzes "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rolling_circle" title="Rolling circle">rolling circle</a>" synthesis of new RNA using the viroid as template<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22719735_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22719735-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="RNA_silencing">RNA silencing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viroid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: RNA silencing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There has long been uncertainty over how viroids induce <a href="/wiki/Symptom" title="Symptom">symptoms</a> in plants without encoding any <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> products within their sequences. Evidence suggests that <a href="/wiki/RNA_silencing" title="RNA silencing">RNA silencing</a> is involved in the process. First, changes to the viroid <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a> can dramatically alter its <a href="/wiki/Virulence" title="Virulence">virulence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid1546460_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid1546460-14">[14]</a></sup> This reflects the fact that any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SiRNA" title="SiRNA">siRNAs</a> produced would have less complementary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Base_pairing" title="Base pairing">base pairing</a> with target <a href="/wiki/Messenger_RNA" title="Messenger RNA">messenger RNA</a>. Secondly, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SiRNA" title="SiRNA">siRNAs</a> corresponding to sequences from viroid genomes have been isolated from infected plants. Finally, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transgenic" title="Transgenic">transgenic</a> expression of the noninfectious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hairpin_RNA" title="Hairpin RNA">hpRNA</a> of <a href="/wiki/Potato_spindle_tuber_viroid" title="Potato spindle tuber viroid">potato spindle tuber viroid</a> develops all the corresponding viroid-like symptoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This indicates that when viroids replicate via a double stranded intermediate <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a>, they are targeted by a <a href="/wiki/Dicer" title="Dicer">dicer</a> enzyme and cleaved into siRNAs that are then loaded onto the <a href="/wiki/RNA-induced_silencing_complex" title="RNA-induced silencing complex">RNA-induced silencing complex</a>. The viroid siRNAs contain sequences capable of complementary base pairing with the plant's own messenger RNAs, and induction of degradation or inhibition of translation causes the classic viroid symptoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Living_relics_of_the_RNA_world.3F">Living relics of the RNA world?</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viroid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Living relics of the RNA world?">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Diener's 1989 hypothesis<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> had proposed that the unique properties of viroids make them more plausible macromolecules than <a href="/wiki/Intron" title="Intron">introns</a>, or other RNAs considered in the past as possible "living relics" of a hypothetical, pre-cellular <a href="/wiki/RNA_world" title="RNA world">RNA world</a>. If so, viroids have assumed significance beyond plant virology for evolutionary science, because their properties make them more plausible candidates than other RNAs to perform crucial steps in the evolution of life from inanimate matter (abiogenesis). These properties are:</p>
<ol>
<li>viroids' small size, imposed by error-prone replication</li>
<li>their high <a href="/wiki/Guanine" title="Guanine">guanine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cytosine" title="Cytosine">cytosine</a> content, which increases stability and replication fidelity</li>
<li>their circular structure, which assures complete replication without <a href="/wiki/Expressed_sequence_tag" title="Expressed sequence tag">genomic tags</a></li>
<li>existence of structural periodicity, which permits modular assembly into enlarged genomes</li>
<li>their lack of protein-coding ability, consistent with a <a href="/wiki/Ribosome" title="Ribosome">ribosome</a>-free habitat</li>
<li>replication mediated in some by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribozymes" title="Ribozymes">ribozymes</a>—the fingerprint of the RNA world</li>
</ol>
<p>Diener's hypothesis was mostly forgotten until 2014, when it was resurrected in a review article by Flores et al.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flores-2014_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flores-2014-18">[18]</a></sup> in which the authors summarized Diener's evidence supporting his hypothesis (see above). In the same year, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> science writer Carl Zimmer published a popularized piece that mistakenly credited Flores et al. with the hypothesis' original conception.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zimmer_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zimmer-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The presence, in extant cells, of RNAs with molecular properties predicted for RNAs of the RNA World constitutes another powerful argument supporting the RNA World hypothesis.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viroid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1920s, symptoms of a previously unknown potato disease were noticed in New York and New Jersey fields. Because tubers on affected plants become elongated and misshapen, they named it the potato spindle tuber disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The symptoms appeared on plants onto which pieces from affected plants had been budded—indicating that the disease was caused by a transmissible pathogenic agent. However, a fungus or bacterium could not be found consistently associated with symptom-bearing plants, and therefore, it was assumed the disease was caused by a virus. Despite numerous attempts over the years to isolate and purify the assumed virus, using increasingly sophisticated methods, these were unsuccessful when applied to extracts from potato spindle tuber disease-afflicted plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ARS_timeline_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ARS_timeline-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1971 Theodor O. Diener showed that the agent was not a virus, but a totally unexpected novel type of pathogen, one-80th the size of typical viruses, for which he proposed the term "viroid".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid5095900_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid5095900-1">[1]</a></sup> Parallel to agriculture-directed studies, more basic scientific research elucidated many of viroids' physical, chemical, and macromolecular properties. Viroids were shown to consist of short stretches (a few hundred nucleobases) of single-stranded RNA and, unlike viruses, did not have a protein coat. Compared with other infectious plant pathogens, viroids are extremely small in size, ranging from 246 to 467 nucleobases; they thus consist of fewer than 10,000 atoms. In comparison, the genomes of the smallest known viruses capable of causing an infection by themselves are around 2,000 nucleobases long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pommerville_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pommerville-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1976, Sänger et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid1069269_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid1069269-22">[22]</a></sup> presented evidence that potato spindle tuber viroid is a "single-stranded, covalently closed, circular RNA molecule, existing as a highly base-paired rod-like structure"—believed to be the first such molecule described. Circular RNA, unlike linear RNA, forms a covalently closed continuous loop, in which the 3' and 5' ends present in linear RNA molecules have been joined together. Sänger et al. also provided evidence for the true circularity of viroids by finding that the RNA could not be phosphorylated at the 5' terminus. Then, in other tests, they failed to find even one free 3' end, which ruled out the possibility of the molecule having two 3’ ends. Viroids thus are true circular RNAs.</p>
<p>The single-strandedness and circularity of viroids was confirmed by electron microscopy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid4728831_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid4728831-23">[23]</a></sup> and Gross et al. determined the complete nucleotide sequence of potato spindle tuber viroid in 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> PSTVd was the first pathogen of a eukaryotic organism for which the complete molecular structure has been established. Over thirty plant diseases have since been identified as viroid-, not virus-caused, as had been assumed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pommerville_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pommerville-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hammond_2006_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hammond_2006-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
