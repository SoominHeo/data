<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integral_calculus" title="Integral calculus">integral calculus</a>, the <b>tangent half-angle substitution</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Integration_by_substitution" title="Integration by substitution">substitution</a> used for finding <a href="/wiki/Antiderivative" title="Antiderivative">antiderivatives</a>, and hence definite integrals, of <a href="/wiki/Rational_function" title="Rational function">rational functions</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigonometric_function" title="Trigonometric function">trigonometric functions</a>. <a href="/wiki/Without_loss_of_generality" title="Without loss of generality">No generality is lost</a> by taking these to be rational functions of the sine and cosine. <a href="/wiki/Michael_Spivak" title="Michael Spivak">Michael Spivak</a> wrote that "The world's sneakiest substitution is undoubtedly" this technique.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Euler_and_Weierstrass">Euler and Weierstrass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tangent_half-angle_substitution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Euler and Weierstrass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Various books call this the <b>Weierstrass substitution</b>, after <a href="/wiki/Karl_Weierstrass" title="Karl Weierstrass">Karl Weierstrass</a> (1815 – 1897), without citing any occurrence of the substitution in Weierstrass' writings,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> but the technique appears well before Weierstrass was born, in the work of <a href="/wiki/Leonhard_Euler" title="Leonhard Euler">Leonhard Euler</a> (1707 – 1783).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geometry">Geometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tangent_half-angle_substitution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Geometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Weierstrass.substitution.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="783" data-file-width="927" height="338" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d2/Weierstrass.substitution.svg/400px-Weierstrass.substitution.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d2/Weierstrass.substitution.svg/600px-Weierstrass.substitution.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d2/Weierstrass.substitution.svg/800px-Weierstrass.substitution.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Weierstrass.substitution.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Weierstrass substitution parametrizes the <a href="/wiki/Unit_circle" title="Unit circle">unit circle</a> centered at (0, 0). Instead of +∞ and −∞, we have only one ∞, at both ends of the real line. That is often appropriate when dealing with rational functions and with trigonometric functions. (This is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/One-point_compactification" title="One-point compactification">one-point compactification</a> of the line.)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As <i>x</i> varies, the point (cos <i>x</i>, sin <i>x</i>) winds repeatedly around the <a href="/wiki/Unit_circle" title="Unit circle">unit circle</a> centered at (0, 0). The point</p>

<p>goes only once around the circle as <i>t</i> goes from −∞ to +∞, and never reaches the point (−1, 0), which is approached as a limit as <i>t</i> approaches ±∞. As <i>t</i> goes from −∞ to −1, the point determined by <i>t</i> goes through the part of the circle in the third quadrant, from (−1, 0) to (0, −1). As <i>t</i> goes from −1 to 0, the point follows the part of the circle in the fourth quadrant from (0, −1) to (1, 0). As <i>t</i> goes from 0 to 1, the point follows the part of the circle in the first quadrant from (1, 0) to (0, 1). Finally, as <i>t</i> goes from 1 to +∞, the point follows the part of the circle in the second quadrant from (0, 1) to (−1, 0).</p>
<p>Here is another geometric point of view. Draw the unit circle, and let <i>P</i> be the point <span class="nowrap">(−1, 0)</span>. A line through <i>P</i> (except the vertical line) is determined by its slope. Furthermore, each of the lines (except the vertical line) intersects the unit circle in exactly two points, one of which is <i>P</i>. This determines a function from points on the unit circle to slopes. The trigonometric functions determine a function from angles to points on the unit circle, and by combining these two functions we have a function from angles to slopes.</p>
