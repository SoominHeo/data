<p>The <b>Bayon</b> (<a href="/wiki/Khmer_language" title="Khmer language">Khmer</a>: <span lang="km">ប្រាសាទបាយ័ន, Prasat Bayon</span>) is a well-known and richly decorated <a href="/wiki/Khmer_Empire" title="Khmer Empire">Khmer</a> temple at <a href="/wiki/Angkor" title="Angkor">Angkor</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>. Built in the late 12th or early 13th century as the official state temple of the <a href="/wiki/Mahayana" title="Mahayana">Mahayana</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> King <a href="/wiki/Jayavarman_VII" title="Jayavarman VII">Jayavarman VII</a>, the Bayon stands at the centre of Jayavarman's capital, <a href="/wiki/Angkor_Thom" title="Angkor Thom">Angkor Thom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Higham_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Higham-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Higham1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Higham1-2">[2]</a></sup> Following Jayavarman's death, it was modified and augmented by later <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theravada" title="Theravada">Theravada</a> Buddhist kings in accordance with their own religious preferences.</p>
<p>The Bayon's most distinctive feature is the multitude of serene and smiling stone faces on the many towers which jut out from the upper terrace and cluster around its central peak.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freeman_and_Jacques.2C_p.78_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman_and_Jacques.2C_p.78-3">[3]</a></sup> The temple is known also for two impressive sets of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bas-relief" title="Bas-relief">bas-reliefs</a>, which present an unusual combination of <a href="/wiki/Itihasa" title="Itihasa">mythological</a>, <a href="/wiki/Early_history_of_Cambodia#Angkor" title="Early history of Cambodia">historical</a>, and mundane scenes. The current main conservatory body, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Government" title="Japanese Government">Japanese Government</a> Team for the Safeguarding of <a href="/wiki/Angkor" title="Angkor">Angkor</a> (the JSA) has described the temple as "the most striking expression of the baroque style" of <a href="/wiki/Khmer_architecture" title="Khmer architecture">Khmer architecture</a>, as contrasted with the classical style of <a href="/wiki/Angkor_Wat" title="Angkor Wat">Angkor Wat</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Bayon was the last state temple to be built at <a href="/wiki/Angkor" title="Angkor">Angkor</a>, and the only Angkorian state temple to be built primarily as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mahayana_Buddhist" title="Mahayana Buddhist">Mahayana Buddhist</a> shrine dedicated to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddha" title="Buddha">Buddha</a>, though a great number of minor and local deities were also encompassed as representatives of the various districts and cities of the realm. It was the centrepiece of <a href="/wiki/Jayavarman_VII" title="Jayavarman VII">Jayavarman VII</a>'s massive program of monumental construction and public works, which was also responsible for the walls and <a href="/wiki/N%C4%81ga" title="Nāga">nāga</a>-bridges of <a href="/wiki/Angkor_Thom" title="Angkor Thom">Angkor Thom</a> and the temples of <a href="/wiki/Preah_Khan" title="Preah Khan">Preah Khan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ta_Prohm" title="Ta Prohm">Ta Prohm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Banteay_Kdei" title="Banteay Kdei">Banteay Kdei</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coedes_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coedes-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Das_L%C3%A4cheln_von_Angkor.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3872" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Das_L%C3%A4cheln_von_Angkor.jpg/260px-Das_L%C3%A4cheln_von_Angkor.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Das_L%C3%A4cheln_von_Angkor.jpg/390px-Das_L%C3%A4cheln_von_Angkor.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Das_L%C3%A4cheln_von_Angkor.jpg/520px-Das_L%C3%A4cheln_von_Angkor.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Das_L%C3%A4cheln_von_Angkor.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
From the vantage point of the temple's upper terrace, one is struck by "the serenity of the stone faces" occupying many towers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glaize.2C_p.87_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Glaize.2C_p.87-5">[5]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The similarity of the 216 gigantic faces on the temple's towers to other statues of the king has led many scholars to the conclusion that the faces are representations of <a href="/wiki/Jayavarman_VII" title="Jayavarman VII">Jayavarman VII</a> himself. Others have said that the faces belong to the <a href="/wiki/Bodhisattva" title="Bodhisattva">bodhisattva</a> of compassion called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avalokitesvara" title="Avalokitesvara">Avalokitesvara</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lokesvara" title="Lokesvara">Lokesvara</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The two hypotheses need not be regarded as mutually exclusive. Angkor scholar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Coed%C3%A8s" title="George Coedès">George Coedès</a> has theorized that Jayavarman stood squarely in the tradition of the Khmer monarchs in thinking of himself as a "devaraja" (god-king), the salient difference being that while his predecessors were Hindus and regarded themselves as consubstantial with <a href="/wiki/Shiva" title="Shiva">Shiva</a> and his symbol the <a href="/wiki/Lingam" title="Lingam">lingam</a>, Jayavarman as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> identified himself with the <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">Buddha</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bodhisattva" title="Bodhisattva">bodhisattva</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alterations_following_the_death_of_Jayavarman_VII">Alterations following the death of Jayavarman VII</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Alterations following the death of Jayavarman VII">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since the time of <a href="/wiki/Jayavarman_VII" title="Jayavarman VII">Jayavarman VII</a>, the Bayon has undergone numerous additions and alterations at the hands of subsequent monarchs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glaize.2C_p.87_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Glaize.2C_p.87-5">[5]</a></sup> During the reign of <a href="/wiki/Jayavarman_VIII" title="Jayavarman VIII">Jayavarman VIII</a> in the mid-13th century, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khmer_empire" title="Khmer empire">Khmer empire</a> reverted to <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a> and its state temple was altered accordingly. In later centuries, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theravada_Buddhism" title="Theravada Buddhism">Theravada Buddhism</a> became the dominant religion, leading to still further changes, before the temple was eventually abandoned to the jungle. Current features which were not part of the original plan include the terrace to the east of the temple, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Architecture_of_Cambodia#Library" title="Architecture of Cambodia">libraries</a>, the square corners of the inner <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Architecture_of_Cambodia#Gallery" title="Architecture of Cambodia">gallery</a>, and parts of the upper terrace.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_restoration">Modern restoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern restoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the first part of the 20th century, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_Fran%C3%A7aise_d%27Extr%C3%AAme_Orient" title="École Française d'Extrême Orient">École Française d'Extrême Orient</a> took the lead in the conservation of the temple, restoring it in accordance with the technique of <a href="/wiki/Anastylosis" title="Anastylosis">anastylosis</a>. Since 1995 the Japanese Government team for the Safeguarding of Angkor (the JSA) has been the main conservatory body, and has held annual symposia.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_site">The site</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The site">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bayon04.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="538" data-file-width="340" height="348" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Bayon04.svg/220px-Bayon04.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Bayon04.svg/330px-Bayon04.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Bayon04.svg/440px-Bayon04.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bayon04.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Bayon in plan, showing the main structure. The dimensions of the upper terrace are only approximate, due to its irregular shape.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The temple is oriented towards the east, and so its buildings are set back to the west inside enclosures elongated along the east-west axis. Because the temple sits at the exact centre of <a href="/wiki/Angkor_Thom" title="Angkor Thom">Angkor Thom</a>, roads lead to it directly from the gates at each of the city's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_point" title="Cardinal point">cardinal points</a>. The temple itself has no wall or <a href="/wiki/Moat" title="Moat">moats</a>, these being replaced by those of the city itself: the city-temple arrangement, with an area of 9 square kilometres, is much larger than that of <a href="/wiki/Angkor_Wat" title="Angkor Wat">Angkor Wat</a> to the south (2 km²). Within the temple itself, there are two galleried enclosures (the third and second enclosures) and an upper terrace (the first enclosure). All of these elements are crowded against each other with little space between. Unlike <a href="/wiki/Angkor_Wat" title="Angkor Wat">Angkor Wat</a>, which impresses with the grand scale of its architecture and open spaces, the Bayon "gives the impression of being compressed within a frame which is too tight for it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_outer_gallery:_historical_events_and_everyday_life">The outer gallery: historical events and everyday life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The outer gallery: historical events and everyday life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The outer wall of the outer gallery features a series of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bas-reliefs" title="Bas-reliefs">bas-reliefs</a> depicting historical events and scenes from the everyday life of the Angkorian Khmer. Though highly detailed and informative in themselves, the bas-reliefs are not accompanied by any sort of epigraphic text, and for that reason considerable uncertainty remains as to which historical events are portrayed and how, if at all, the different reliefs are related.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> From the east <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gopura" title="Gopura">gopura</a> clockwise, the subjects are:</p>

<p>The outer gallery encloses a courtyard in which there are two libraries (one on either side of the east entrance). Originally the courtyard contained 16 chapels, but these were subsequently demolished by the Hindu restorationist <a href="/wiki/Jayavarman_VIII" title="Jayavarman VIII">Jayavarman VIII</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_inner_gallery:_depictions_of_mythological_events">The inner gallery: depictions of mythological events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The inner gallery: depictions of mythological events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The inner gallery is raised above ground level and has doubled corners, with the original redented cross-shape later filled out to a square. Its bas-reliefs, later additions of Jayavarman VIII, are in stark contrast to those of the outer: rather than set-piece battles and processions, the smaller canvases offered by the inner gallery are decorated for the most part with scenes from <a href="/wiki/Hindu_mythology" title="Hindu mythology">Hindu mythology</a>. Some of the figures depicted are <a href="/wiki/Shiva" title="Shiva">Siva</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">Vishnu</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Brahma" title="Brahma">Brahma</a>, the members of the <a href="/wiki/Trimurti" title="Trimurti">trimurti</a> or threefold godhead of <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apsaras" title="Apsaras">Apsaras</a> or celestial dancers, <a href="/wiki/Ravana" title="Ravana">Ravana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Garuda" title="Garuda">Garuda</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> There is however no certainty as to what some of the panels depict, or as to their relationship with one another. One gallery just north of the eastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Architecture_of_Cambodia#Gopura" title="Architecture of Cambodia">gopura</a>, for example, shows two linked scenes which have been explained as the freeing of a goddess from inside a mountain, or as an act of iconoclasm by Cham invaders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Another series of panels shows a king fighting a gigantic serpent with his bare hands, then having his hands examined by women, and finally lying ill in bed; these images have been connected with the legend of the Leper King, who contracted leprosy from the venom of a serpent with whom he had done battle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Less obscure are depictions of the construction of a <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">Vishnuite</a> temple (south of the western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gopura" title="Gopura">gopura</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Samudra_manthan" title="Samudra manthan">Churning of the Sea of Milk</a> (north of the western gopura).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_upper_terrace:_200_faces_of_Lokesvara">The upper terrace: 200 faces of Lokesvara</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The upper terrace: 200 faces of Lokesvara">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The inner gallery is nearly filled by the upper terrace, raised one level higher again. The lack of space between the inner gallery and the upper terrace has led scholars to conclude that the upper terrace did not figure in the original plan for the temple, but that it was added shortly thereafter following a change in design. Originally, it is believed, the Bayon had been designed as a single-level structure, similar in that respect to the roughly contemporaneous foundations at <a href="/wiki/Ta_Prohm" title="Ta Prohm">Ta Prohm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Banteay_Kdei" title="Banteay Kdei">Banteay Kdei</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The upper terrace is home to the famous "face towers" of the Bayon, each of which supports two, three or (most commonly) four gigantic smiling faces. In addition to the mass of the central tower, smaller towers are located along the inner gallery (at the corners and entrances), and on chapels on the upper terrace. "Wherever one wanders," writes <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Glaize" title="Maurice Glaize">Maurice Glaize</a>, "the faces of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lokesvara" title="Lokesvara">Lokesvara</a> follow and dominate with their multiple presence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Efforts to read some significance into the numbers of towers and faces have run up against the circumstance that these numbers have not remained constant over time, as towers have been added through construction and lost to attrition. At one point, the temple was host to 49 such towers; now only 37 remain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freeman_and_Jacques.2C_p.78_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman_and_Jacques.2C_p.78-3">[3]</a></sup> The number of faces is approximately 200, but since some are only partially preserved there can be no definitive count.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_central_tower_and_sanctuary">The central tower and sanctuary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The central tower and sanctuary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like the inner gallery, the central tower was originally cruciform but was later filled out and made circular. It rises 43 metres above the ground. At the time of the temple's foundation, the principal religious image was a statue of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddha" title="Buddha">Buddha</a>, 3.6 m tall, located in the <a href="/wiki/Sanctuary" title="Sanctuary">sanctuary</a> at the heart of the central tower. The statue depicted the Buddha seated in meditation, shielded from the elements by the flared hood of the serpent king <a href="/wiki/Mucalinda" title="Mucalinda">Mucalinda</a>. During the reign of Hindu restorationist monarch <a href="/wiki/Jayavarman_VIII" title="Jayavarman VIII">Jayavarman VIII</a>, the figure was removed from the <a href="/wiki/Sanctuary" title="Sanctuary">sanctuary</a> and smashed to pieces. After being recovered in 1933 from the bottom of a well, it was pieced back together, and is now on display in a small pavilion at <a href="/wiki/Angkor" title="Angkor">Angkor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
