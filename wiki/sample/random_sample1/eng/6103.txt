<p><b>Viking 1</b> was the first of two spacecraft (along with <a href="/wiki/Viking_2" title="Viking 2">Viking 2</a>) sent to <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> as part of <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Viking_program" title="Viking program">Viking program</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20061218_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20061218-1">[1]</a></sup> On July 20, 1976, it became the first spacecraft to land successfully on Mars and perform its mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Viking 1 held the record for the longest Mars surface mission of 2307 days<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20061218_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20061218-1">[1]</a></sup> or 2245 <a href="/wiki/Timekeeping_on_Mars#Sols" title="Timekeeping on Mars">sols</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20061218_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20061218-1">[1]</a></sup> until that record was broken by <a href="/wiki/Opportunity_(rover)" title="Opportunity (rover)"><i>Opportunity</i></a> on May 19, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission">Mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viking_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following launch using a <a href="/wiki/Titan_IIIE" title="Titan IIIE">Titan</a>/<a href="/wiki/Centaur_(rocket_stage)" title="Centaur (rocket stage)">Centaur</a> launch vehicle on August 20, 1975, and a 10-month cruise to Mars, the orbiter began returning global images of Mars about 5 days before orbit insertion. The Viking 1 Orbiter was inserted into Mars orbit on June 19, 1976, and trimmed to a 1513 x 33,000 km, 24.66 h site certification orbit on June 21. Landing on Mars was planned for July 4, 1976, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Bicentennial" title="United States Bicentennial">United States Bicentennial</a>, but imaging of the primary landing site showed it was too rough for a safe landing. The landing was delayed until a safer site was found. The lander separated from the orbiter on July 20 08:51 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a> and landed at 11:53:06 UTC. It was the first attempt by the United States at landing on Mars.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbiter">Orbiter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viking_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbiter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The instruments of the <a href="/wiki/Orbiter" title="Orbiter">orbiter</a> consisted of two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vidicon" title="Vidicon">vidicon</a> cameras for imaging (VIS), an infrared spectrometer for water vapor mapping (MAWD) and infrared radiometers for thermal mapping (IRTM).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soffen_.26_Snyder_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Soffen_.26_Snyder-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Orbiter" title="Orbiter">orbiter</a> primary mission ended at the beginning of <a href="/wiki/Solar_conjunction" title="Solar conjunction">solar conjunction</a> on November 5, 1976. The extended mission commenced on December 14, 1976, after solar conjunction. Operations included close approaches to <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">Phobos</a> in February 1977. The <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">periapsis</a> was reduced to 300 km on March 11, 1977. Minor orbit adjustments were done occasionally over the course of the mission, primarily to change the walk rate — the rate at which the areocentric longitude changed with each orbit, and the periapsis was raised to 357 km on July 20, 1979. On August 7, 1980, Viking 1 Orbiter was running low on attitude control gas and its orbit was raised from 357 × 33943 km to 320 × 56000 km to prevent impact with Mars and possible contamination until the year 2019. Operations were terminated on August 17, 1980, after 1485 orbits. A 2009 analysis concluded that, while the possibility that Viking 1 had impacted mars could not be ruled out, it was most likely still in orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jefferson_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jefferson-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>The lander and its <a href="/wiki/Aeroshell" title="Aeroshell">aeroshell</a> separated from the orbiter on July 20 at 08:51 UTC. At the time of separation, the lander was orbiting at about 5 kilometres per second (3.1 miles per second). The aeroshell's retrorockets fired to begin the lander de-orbit maneuver. After a few hours at about 300 kilometres (190 miles) altitude, the lander was reoriented for atmospheric entry. The aeroshell with its ablative <a href="/wiki/Heat_shield" title="Heat shield">heat shield</a> slowed the craft as it plunged through the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars" title="Atmosphere of Mars">atmosphere</a>. During this time, entry science experiments were performed by using a retarding potential analyzer, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometer" title="Mass spectrometer">mass spectrometer</a>, as well as pressure, temperature, and density sensors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soffen_.26_Snyder_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Soffen_.26_Snyder-6">[6]</a></sup> At 6 km (3.7 mi) altitude, traveling at about 250 metres per second (820 feet per second), the 16 m diameter lander parachutes deployed. Seven seconds later the aeroshell was jettisoned, and 8 seconds after that the three lander legs were extended. In 45 seconds the <a href="/wiki/Parachute" title="Parachute">parachute</a> had slowed the lander to 60 metres per second (200 feet per second). At 1.5 km (0.93 mi) altitude, retrorockets on the lander itself were ignited and, 40 seconds later at about 2.4 m/s (7.9 ft/s), the lander arrived on Mars with a relatively light jolt. The legs had honeycomb aluminum shock absorbers to soften the landing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soffen_.26_Snyder_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Soffen_.26_Snyder-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The landing rockets used an 18-nozzle design to spread the hydrogen and nitrogen exhaust over a large area. NASA calculated that this approach would mean that the surface would not be heated by more than one 1°C (1.8°F), and that it would move no more than 1 millimetre (0.039 inches) of surface material. Since most of Viking's experiments focused on the surface material a more straightforward design would not have served.</p>
<p>The Viking 1 Lander touched down in western <a href="/wiki/Chryse_Planitia" title="Chryse Planitia">Chryse Planitia</a> ("Golden Plain") at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Viking_1&amp;params=22.697_N_48.222_W_globe:Mars"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">22°41′49″N</span> <span class="longitude">48°13′19″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">22.697°N 48.222°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">22.697; -48.222</span></span></span></a></span> at a reference altitude of −2.69 kilometres (−1.67 mi) relative to a reference ellipsoid with an equatorial radius of 3,397 kilometres (2,111 mi) and a flatness of 0.0105 (22.480° N, 47.967° W planetographic) at 11:53:06 UT (16:13 local Mars time). Approximately 22 kilograms (49 lb) of propellants were left at landing.</p>
<p>Transmission of the first surface image began 25 seconds after landing and took about 4 minutes (see below). During these minutes the lander activated itself. It erected a high-gain antenna pointed toward Earth for direct communication and deployed a meteorology boom mounted with sensors. In the next 7 minutes the second picture of the 300° panoramic scene (displayed below) was taken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> On the day after the landing the first color picture of the surface of Mars (displayed below) was taken. The seismometer failed to uncage, and a sampler arm locking pin was stuck and took five days to shake out. Otherwise, all experiments functioned normally. The lander had two means of returning data to Earth: a relay link up to the orbiter and back, and by using a direct link to Earth. The data capacity of the relay link was about 10 times higher than the direct link.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soffen_.26_Snyder_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Soffen_.26_Snyder-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>The lander had two facsimile cameras, three analyses for metabolism, growth or photosyntheses, a gas chromatograph-mass spectrometer (GCMS), an x-ray fluorescence spectrometer, pressure, temperature and wind velocity sensors, a three-axis seismometer, a magnet on a sampler observed by the cameras, and various engineering sensors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soffen_.26_Snyder_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Soffen_.26_Snyder-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Viking 1 Lander was named the <a href="/wiki/Thomas_A._Mutch" title="Thomas A. Mutch">Thomas Mutch</a> Memorial Station in January 1982 in honor of the leader of the Viking imaging team. The lander operated for 2245 <a href="/wiki/Timekeeping_on_Mars#Sols" title="Timekeeping on Mars">sols</a> (about 2306 Earth days or 6 years) until November 11, 1982, (sol 2600), when a faulty command sent by ground control resulted in loss of contact. The command was intended to uplink new battery charging software to improve the lander's deteriorating battery capacity, but it inadvertently overwrote data used by the antenna pointing software. Attempts to contact the lander during the next four months, based on the presumed antenna position, were unsuccessful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 2006 the Viking 1 lander was imaged on the Martian surface by the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter">Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_results">Mission results</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viking_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mission results">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Search_for_life">Search for life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viking_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Search for life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Viking carried a biology experiment whose purpose was to look for evidence of life. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking_spacecraft_biological_experiments" title="Viking spacecraft biological experiments">Viking spacecraft biological experiments</a> weighed 15.5 kg (34 lbs) and consisted of three subsystems: the <a href="/wiki/Viking_lander_biological_experiments#Pyrolytic_release" title="Viking lander biological experiments">pyrolytic release</a> experiment (PR), the labeled release experiment (LR), and the gas exchange experiment (GEX). In addition, independent of the biology experiments, Viking carried a gas chromatograph/mass spectrometer (GCMS) that could measure the composition and abundance of organic compounds in the martian soil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The results were surprising and interesting: the GCMS gave a negative result; the PR gave a negative result, the GEX gave a negative result, and the LR gave a positive result.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Viking scientist Patricia Straat stated in 2009, "Our (LR) experiment was a definite positive response for life, but a lot of people have claimed that it was a false positive for a variety of reasons."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Most scientists now believe that the data were due to inorganic chemical reactions of the soil; however, this view may be changing after the recent discovery of near-surface ice near the Viking landing zone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Some scientists still believe the results were due to living reactions. No organic chemicals were found in the soil. However, dry areas of <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> do not have detectable organic compounds either, but they have organisms living in the rocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Mars has almost no ozone layer, unlike the Earth, so UV light sterilizes the surface and produces highly reactive chemicals such as peroxides that would oxidize any organic chemicals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phoenix_Lander" title="Phoenix Lander">Phoenix Lander</a> discovered the chemical <a href="/wiki/Perchlorate" title="Perchlorate">perchlorate</a> in the Martian soil. Perchlorate is a strong oxidant so it may have destroyed any organic matter on the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> If it is widespread on Mars, <a href="/wiki/Carbon-based_life" title="Carbon-based life">carbon-based life</a> would be difficult at the soil surface.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Test_of_general_relativity">Test of general relativity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viking_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Test of general relativity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Introduction_to_general_relativity" title="Introduction to general relativity">Introduction to general relativity</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cassini-science-br.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="924" data-file-width="640" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Cassini-science-br.jpg/150px-Cassini-science-br.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Cassini-science-br.jpg/225px-Cassini-science-br.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Cassini-science-br.jpg/300px-Cassini-science-br.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cassini-science-br.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
High-precision test of general relativity by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cassini-Huygens" title="Cassini-Huygens">Cassini</a> space probe (artist's impression)</div>
</div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Gravitational_time_dilation" title="Gravitational time dilation">Gravitational time dilation</a> is a phenomenon predicted by the theory of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Relativity" title="General Relativity">General Relativity</a> whereby time passes more slowly in regions of lower <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_potential" title="Gravitational potential">gravitational potential</a>. Scientists used the lander to test this hypothesis, by sending radio signals to the lander on Mars, and instructing the lander to send back signals, in cases which sometimes included the signal passing close to the Sun. Scientists found that the observed <a href="/wiki/Shapiro_delay" title="Shapiro delay">Shapiro delays</a> of the signals matched the predictions of General Relativity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
