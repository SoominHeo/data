<p>More precisely, let <i>F</i> be the underlying <a href="/wiki/Field_(mathematics)" title="Field (mathematics)">field</a>, and let <i>F</i>[<i>X</i>] be the <a href="/wiki/Polynomial_ring" title="Polynomial ring">ring of polynomials</a> in one variable, <i>X</i>, with coefficients in <i>F</i>. Then each <i>f<sub>i</sub></i> lies in <i>F</i>[<i>X</i>].</p>
<p><i>∂<sub>X</sub></i> is the <a href="/wiki/Derivative" title="Derivative">derivative</a> with respect to <i>X</i>. The algebra is generated by <i>X</i> and <i>∂<sub>X</sub></i> .</p>
<p>The Weyl algebra is an example of a <a href="/wiki/Simple_ring" title="Simple ring">simple ring</a> that is not a <a href="/wiki/Matrix_ring" title="Matrix ring">matrix ring</a> over a <a href="/wiki/Division_ring" title="Division ring">division ring</a>. It is also a noncommutative example of a <a href="/wiki/Domain_(ring_theory)" title="Domain (ring theory)">domain</a>, and an example of an <a href="/wiki/Ore_extension" title="Ore extension">Ore extension</a>.</p>
<p>The Weyl algebra is isomorphic to the <a href="/wiki/Quotient_ring" title="Quotient ring">quotient</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Free_algebra" title="Free algebra">free algebra</a> on two generators, <i>X</i> and <i>Y</i>, by the <a href="/wiki/Ideal_(ring_theory)" title="Ideal (ring theory)">ideal</a> generated by the element</p>

<p>The Weyl algebra is the first in an infinite family of algebras, also known as Weyl algebras. The <b><i>n</i>-th Weyl algebra</b>, <i>A<sub>n</sub></i>, is the ring of differential operators with polynomial coefficients in <i>n</i> variables. It is generated by <i>X<sub>i</sub></i> and <i>∂<sub>X<sub>i</sub></sub></i>, <span class="nowrap"><i>i</i> = 1, ..., <i>n</i></span>.</p>
<p>Weyl algebras are named after <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Weyl" title="Hermann Weyl">Hermann Weyl</a>, who introduced them to study the <a href="/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg" title="Werner Heisenberg">Heisenberg</a> <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty_principle" title="Uncertainty principle">uncertainty principle</a> in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Quotient_ring" title="Quotient ring">quotient</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Universal_enveloping_algebra" title="Universal enveloping algebra">universal enveloping algebra</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heisenberg_algebra" title="Heisenberg algebra">Heisenberg algebra</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lie_algebra" title="Lie algebra">Lie algebra</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Heisenberg_group" title="Heisenberg group">Heisenberg group</a>, by setting the central element of the Heisenberg algebra (namely [<i>X</i>,<i>Y</i>]) equal to the unit of the universal enveloping algebra (called 1 above).</p>
<p>The Weyl algebra is also referred to as the <b>symplectic Clifford algebra</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-helmstetter-2008-p12_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-helmstetter-2008-p12-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ablamowicz-Pxvi_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ablamowicz-Pxvi-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Weyl algebras represent the same structure for symplectic <a href="/wiki/Bilinear_form" title="Bilinear form">bilinear forms</a> that <a href="/wiki/Clifford_algebra" title="Clifford algebra">Clifford algebras</a> represent for non-degenerate symmetric bilinear forms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-helmstetter-2008-p12_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-helmstetter-2008-p12-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quantization">Quantization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weyl_algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Quantization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>If one prefers to have the <i>iħ</i> and work over the complex numbers, one could have instead defined the Weyl algebra above as generated by <i>X</i><sub><i>i</i></sub> and <i>iħ∂<sub>X<sub>i</sub></sub></i> (as per <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> usage).</p>
<p>Thus, the Weyl algebra is a quantization of the symmetric algebra, which is essentially the same as the <a href="/wiki/Moyal_product" title="Moyal product">Moyal quantization</a> (if for the latter one restricts to polynomial functions), but the former is in terms of generators and relations (considered to be differential operators) and the latter is in terms of a deformed multiplication.</p>
<p>In the case of <a href="/wiki/Exterior_algebra" title="Exterior algebra">exterior algebras</a>, the analogous quantization to the Weyl one is the <a href="/wiki/Clifford_algebra" title="Clifford algebra">Clifford algebra</a>, which is also referred to as the <i>orthogonal Clifford algebra</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ablamowicz-Pxvi_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ablamowicz-Pxvi-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_of_the_Weyl_algebra">Properties of the Weyl algebra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weyl_algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Properties of the Weyl algebra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Stone%E2%80%93von_Neumann_theorem" title="Stone–von Neumann theorem">Stone–von Neumann theorem</a></div>
<p>In the case that the ground field <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">F</span> has characteristic zero, the <i>n</i>th Weyl algebra is a <a href="/wiki/Simple_ring" title="Simple ring">simple</a> <a href="/wiki/Noetherian_ring" title="Noetherian ring">Noetherian</a> <a href="/wiki/Domain_(ring_theory)" title="Domain (ring theory)">domain</a>. It has <a href="/wiki/Global_dimension" title="Global dimension">global dimension</a> <i>n</i>, in contrast to the ring it deforms, Sym(<i>V</i>), which has global dimension 2<i>n</i>.</p>
<p>It has no finite-dimensional representations. Although this follows from simplicity, it can be more directly shown by taking the trace σ(<i>X</i>) and σ(<i>Y</i>) for some finite-dimensional representation σ (where <span class="nowrap">[<i>X</i>,<i>Y</i>] = 1</span>).</p>

<p>Since the trace of a commutator is zero, and the trace of the identity is the dimension of the matrix, the representation must be zero dimensional.</p>
<p>In fact, there are stronger statements than the absence of finite-dimensional representations. To any finitely generated <i>A<sub>n</sub></i>-module <i>M</i>, there is a corresponding subvariety <i>Char(M)</i> of <span class="nowrap"><i>V</i> × <i>V</i>*</span> called the 'characteristic variety'<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. <p>An even stronger statement is <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gabber%27s_theorem&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gabber's theorem (page does not exist)">Gabber's theorem</a>, which states that <i>Char(M)</i> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lagrangian_submanifold" title="Lagrangian submanifold">co-isotropic</a> subvariety of <span class="nowrap"><i>V</i> × <i>V</i>*</span> for the natural symplectic form.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Positive_characteristic">Positive characteristic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weyl_algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Positive characteristic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The situation is considerably different in the case of a Weyl algebra over a field of <a href="/wiki/Characteristic_(algebra)" title="Characteristic (algebra)">characteristic</a> <span class="nowrap"><i>p</i> &gt; 0</span>.</p>
<p>In this case, for any element <i>D</i> of the Weyl algebra, the element <i>D<sup>p</sup></i> is central, and so the Weyl algebra has a very large center. In fact, it is a finitely generated module over its center; even more so, it is an <a href="/wiki/Azumaya_algebra" title="Azumaya algebra">Azumaya algebra</a> over its center. As a consequence, there are many finite-dimensional representations which are all built out of simple representations of dimension <i>p</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Generalizations">Generalizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weyl_algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Generalizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For more details about this quantization in the case <i>n</i> = 1 (and an extension using the <a href="/wiki/Fourier_transform" title="Fourier transform">Fourier transform</a> to a class of integrable functions larger than the polynomial functions), see <a href="/wiki/Wigner%E2%80%93Weyl_transform" title="Wigner–Weyl transform">Wigner–Weyl transform</a>.</p>
<p>Weyl algebras and Clifford algebras admit a further structure of a <a href="/wiki/*-algebra" title="*-algebra">*-algebra</a>, and can be unified as even and odd terms of a <a href="/wiki/Superalgebra" title="Superalgebra">superalgebra</a>, as discussed in <a href="/wiki/CCR_and_CAR_algebras" title="CCR and CAR algebras">CCR and CAR algebras</a>.</p>
