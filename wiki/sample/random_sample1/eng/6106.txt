<p><b>Basel</b> was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canton_of_Switzerland" title="Canton of Switzerland">canton of Switzerland</a> that was in existence between 1501 and 1833, when it was split into the two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half-canton" title="Half-canton">half-cantons</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basel-City" title="Basel-City">Basel-City</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basel-Country" title="Basel-Country">Basel-Country</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Basel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Basel" title="History of Basel">History of Basel</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Basel" title="Bishopric of Basel">Bishopric of Basel</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fuerstbistum_Basel_16Jh.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1174" data-file-width="1500" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Fuerstbistum_Basel_16Jh.png/220px-Fuerstbistum_Basel_16Jh.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Fuerstbistum_Basel_16Jh.png/330px-Fuerstbistum_Basel_16Jh.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Fuerstbistum_Basel_16Jh.png/440px-Fuerstbistum_Basel_16Jh.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fuerstbistum_Basel_16Jh.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishopric_of_Basel" title="Prince-Bishopric of Basel">Prince-Bishopric of Basel</a> in the 16th century</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Before the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a>, Basel was ruled by <a href="/wiki/Prince-bishop" title="Prince-bishop">prince-bishops</a> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Basel" title="Bishop of Basel">Bishop of Basel</a>, whose memory is preserved in the <a href="/wiki/Crosier" title="Crosier">crosier</a> shown on the Basel <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a>, as above). In the later 15th century, in the wake of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_of_Basel" title="Council of Basel">Council of Basel</a> (1431–49), the city of Basel grew in wealth and importance. The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Basel" title="University of Basel">University of Basel</a> was established in 1459, and the city became an intellectual center of the <a href="/wiki/German_Renaissance" title="German Renaissance">German Renaissance</a> in the years leading up to the Reformation. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erasmus_of_Rotterdam" title="Erasmus of Rotterdam">Erasmus of Rotterdam</a> taught in Basel, and <a href="/wiki/Incunable" title="Incunable">early printshops</a> were set up by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johannes_Petri" title="Johannes Petri">Johannes Petri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johann_Froben" title="Johann Froben">Johann Froben</a>. In 1495, Basel was incorporated in the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Rhenish_Circle" title="Upper Rhenish Circle">Upper Rhenish</a> <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Circle" title="Imperial Circle">Imperial Circle</a>, the bishop sitting on the Bench of the Ecclesiastical Princes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Establishment">Establishment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Basel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Establishment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a direct consequence of the <a href="/wiki/Swabian_War" title="Swabian War">Swabian War</a>, resolved by the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Basel_(1499)" title="Treaty of Basel (1499)">1499 Treaty of Basel</a>, Basel and the <a href="/wiki/Free_imperial_city" title="Free imperial city">Imperial City</a> of <a href="/wiki/Schaffhausen" title="Schaffhausen">Schaffhausen</a> joined the <a href="/wiki/Old_Swiss_Confederacy" title="Old Swiss Confederacy">Swiss Confederation</a> in 1501, as the confederacy's 11th and 12th states, with <a href="/wiki/Appenzell" title="Appenzell">Appenzell</a> following suit 12 years later to complete the <i><a href="/wiki/Growth_of_the_Old_Swiss_Confederacy#The_Dreizehn_Orte" title="Growth of the Old Swiss Confederacy">Dreizehn Orte</a></i> that made up Switzerland until the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Wars" title="French Revolutionary Wars">French Revolutionary Wars</a>.</p>
<p>The canton of Basel seceded from the prince-bishopric, and the secular rule of the bishops of Basel from this time was limited to territories west of Basel, more or less corresponding to the modern <a href="/wiki/Canton_of_Jura" title="Canton of Jura">canton of Jura</a>. Even though the bishops of Basel no longer held secular authority over the city of Basel, they continued to reside in the city until the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reformation">Reformation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Basel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reformation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1503, the new bishop <a href="/wiki/Christoph_von_Utenheim" title="Christoph von Utenheim">Christoph von Utenheim</a> refused to give Basel a new constitution whereupon, to show its power, the city began the construction of a new city hall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The reformation was brought to Basel by <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Oecolampadius" title="Johannes Oecolampadius">Johannes Oecolampadius</a> cathedral preacher under von Utenheim and co-editor of Erasmus' <a href="/wiki/Novum_Instrumentum_omne" title="Novum Instrumentum omne">first edition of the Greek New Testament</a>. Von Utenheim resigned on 19 February 1527. He was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Philippe_von_Gundelsheim" title="Philippe von Gundelsheim">Philippe von Gundelsheim</a>, canon at Basel Münster since 1510.</p>
<p>In 1529, the city became Protestant under <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Oecolampadius" title="Johannes Oecolampadius">Oecolampadius</a> and the bishop's seat was moved to Porrentruy. In 1530, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laufental" title="Laufental">Laufental</a> rebelled against the bishop, but were suppressed using forces from <a href="/wiki/Solothurn" title="Solothurn">Solothurn</a>. Because of insolvency, the prince-bishopric grew increasingly dependent on the city of Basel, with the city granting him a mortgage on <a href="/wiki/Birseck_Castle" title="Birseck Castle">Birseck Castle</a> in 1542, 1544, and 1545. In 1547, the bishop formally agreed to allow the city to choose its own religion, recognizing that the city had already become Protestant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Basel patriciate (<a href="/wiki/Daig_(Switzerland)" title="Daig (Switzerland)">"Daig"</a>) now played a pivotal role in city affairs as they gradually established themselves as a <i>de facto</i> <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy" title="Aristocracy">city aristocracy</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Bernoulli_family" title="Bernoulli family">Bernoulli family</a>, which included important 17th- and 18th-century mathematicians such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jakob_Bernoulli" title="Jakob Bernoulli">Jakob Bernoulli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johann_Bernoulli" title="Johann Bernoulli">Johann Bernoulli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Bernoulli" title="Daniel Bernoulli">Daniel Bernoulli</a>, were from Basel. The 18th-century mathematician <a href="/wiki/Leonhard_Euler" title="Leonhard Euler">Leonhard Euler</a> was born in Basel and studied under Johann Bernoulli.</p>
<p>Intended as a defence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huguenots" title="Huguenots">Huguenots</a> then persecuted in France, Calvin's <a href="/wiki/Institutes_of_the_Christian_Religion" title="Institutes of the Christian Religion">Institutes</a>, authored in Basel, was a 1536 exposition of Protestant Christian doctrine which later became known as <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinism</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Merian_Basel_1642.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2300" data-file-width="3000" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Merian_Basel_1642.jpg/220px-Merian_Basel_1642.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Merian_Basel_1642.jpg/330px-Merian_Basel_1642.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Merian_Basel_1642.jpg/440px-Merian_Basel_1642.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Merian_Basel_1642.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of Basel in 1642, engraved by <a href="/wiki/Matth%C3%A4us_Merian" title="Matthäus Merian">Matthäus Merian</a>, oriented with SW at the top and NE at the bottom.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first edition of <i>Christianae religionis institutio</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Institutes_of_the_Christian_Religion" title="Institutes of the Christian Religion">Institutes of the Christian Religion</a></i> – <a href="/wiki/John_Calvin" title="John Calvin">John Calvin</a>'s great exposition of <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinist</a> doctrine) was published at Basel in March 1536.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1543, <i><a href="/wiki/De_humani_corporis_fabrica" title="De humani corporis fabrica">De humani corporis fabrica</a></i>, the first book on human anatomy, was published and printed in Basel by <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Vesalius" title="Andreas Vesalius">Andreas Vesalius</a> (1514–1564).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 1648 <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a> finally brought about Imperial recognition of the independence of the <a href="/wiki/Cantons_of_Switzerland" title="Cantons of Switzerland">Swiss cantons</a>, removing the <i>de jure</i> (but not <i>de facto</i>) overlordship of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> rejected by the then–Prince-Bishopric nearly 150 years earlier.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unrest_and_insurrection">Unrest and insurrection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Basel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Unrest and insurrection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Switzerland_in_the_Napoleonic_era" title="Switzerland in the Napoleonic era">Switzerland in the Napoleonic era</a> and <a href="/wiki/Restoration_and_Regeneration_in_Switzerland" title="Restoration and Regeneration in Switzerland">Restoration and Regeneration in Switzerland</a></div>
<p>In 1792, the <a href="/wiki/Rauracian_Republic" title="Rauracian Republic">Rauracian Republic</a>, a revolutionary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_client_republic" title="French client republic">French client republic</a>, was created, lasting for a few months into the following year before being partitioned between a restored canton of Basel, later within the <a href="/wiki/Helvetic_Republic" title="Helvetic Republic">Helvetic Republic</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_French_Republic" title="First French Republic">First French Republic</a>.</p>
<p>Until 1830, Basel was a unified canton, with citizens from both the <a href="/wiki/Basel" title="Basel">city</a> and the municipalities of the countryside sitting in the <i>Kantonsparlament</i>. The cantonal parliament was dominated by members from the city, though its population was less than that of the combined countryside. This had not previously been a source of grievance, but in 1830 the <i>Baselbieter</i>, or citizens from the countryside, grew increasingly distrustful of the city. At a meeting in <a href="/wiki/Bubendorf" title="Bubendorf">Bad Bubendorf</a> on 18 October 1830, 25 <i>Baselbieter</i> wrote to the "esteemed gentlemen and noblemen in Basel", demanding equal rights between city and countryside and a representation in parliament in proportion to their numbers.</p>
<p>When the city rejected this demand, resentment from the countryside region grew still larger to the extent that the city feared an attack. In <a href="/wiki/Liestal" title="Liestal">Liestal</a> a few men of the countryside formed a new provincial government protected by an army of 3,000. The government was however short-lived as on 16 January 1831 a force from Basel occupied Liestal, driving out the new government. A number of villages, such as <a href="/wiki/Gelterkinden" title="Gelterkinden">Gelterkinden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reigoldswil" title="Reigoldswil">Reigoldswil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anwil" title="Anwil">Anwil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bubendorf" title="Bubendorf">Bubendorf</a> remained loyal to Basel, though coming under threat from the rebels. The unrest in the countryside persisted into 1832 and both sides committed injustices upon the other.</p>
<p>On 3 August 1833 over 1200 troops of the city armed with 14 <a href="/wiki/Cannon" title="Cannon">cannons</a> marched on Liestal, but at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_H%C3%BClftenschanz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Battle of Hülftenschanz (page does not exist)">Battle of Hülftenschanz</a>, which took place between <a href="/wiki/Pratteln" title="Pratteln">Pratteln</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frenkendorf" title="Frenkendorf">Frenkendorf</a>, the city's troops were forced back to Basel by the superior numbers of the rebels. Their route back to the city was ambushed and the city forces took heavy losses.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Partition">Partition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Basel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Partition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:219px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Basel.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="264" data-file-width="217" height="264" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8c/Basel.png" width="217"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Basel.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A cantonal coat of arms combining the coats of arms of the two half cantons, was in use from 1833 to 1999 when the "full canton" needed representation, e.g. in the <i><a href="/wiki/Standesscheibe" title="Standesscheibe">Standesscheiben</a></i> shown in the glass dome of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Palace_of_Switzerland" title="Federal Palace of Switzerland">Federal Palace of Switzerland</a>. The same combined coat of arms would be used for the suggested re-united canton.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After this conflict, the highest Swiss authority, the <i><a href="/wiki/Tagsatzung" title="Tagsatzung">Tagsatzung</a></i>, was petitioned on 17 August 1833 to separate the canton of Basel; nine days later, the partition into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half-canton" title="Half-canton">half-cantons</a>, modelled after the precedent of <a href="/wiki/Unterwalden" title="Unterwalden">Unterwalden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Appenzell" title="Appenzell">Appenzell</a>, was effected.</p>
<p>From the country municipalities it allocated only <a href="/wiki/Riehen" title="Riehen">Riehen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bettingen" title="Bettingen">Bettingen</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Basel-Kleinh%C3%BCningen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Basel-Kleinhüningen (page does not exist)">Kleinhüningen</a> — which would otherwise have been an exclave of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basel-Country" title="Basel-Country">Basel-Country</a> between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basel-City" title="Basel-City">Basel-City</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Baden" title="Grand Duchy of Baden">Grand Duchy of Baden</a> — to the new half-canton of Basel-City. The remaining municipalities formed the new canton of Basel-Country.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swiss_Constitution" title="Swiss Constitution">Swiss Constitution</a> of 1844 continued to recognize "Basel" as one of twenty-two "sovereign cantons" of Switzerland, enumerated as <i>Basel (Stadt und Land</i>). In this sense, as a sovereign member of the Swiss Confederacy, the canton of Basel continued to exist until 1999, when the revised constitution recognised the two former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half-canton" title="Half-canton">half-cantons</a> as "cantons" for the first time.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Suggested_re-unification">Suggested re-unification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Basel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Suggested re-unification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several attempts have been made to reunite Basel-City and Basel-Country. In 1969, the citizens of Basel-Country defeated the motion in a referendum. The two cantons have since concluded a number of co-operation agreements, such as joint financing and governance of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Basel" title="University of Basel">University of Basel</a>.</p>
