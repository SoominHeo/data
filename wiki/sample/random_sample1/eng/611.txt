<p><i><b>The 400 Blows</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr"><i>Les Quatre Cents Coups</i></span>) is a 1959 French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drama_film" title="Drama film">drama film</a>, the debut by director <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Truffaut" title="François Truffaut">François Truffaut</a>; it stars <a href="/wiki/Jean-Pierre_L%C3%A9aud" title="Jean-Pierre Léaud">Jean-Pierre Léaud</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albert_R%C3%A9my" title="Albert Rémy">Albert Rémy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Claire_Maurier" title="Claire Maurier">Claire Maurier</a>. One of the defining films of the <a href="/wiki/French_New_Wave" title="French New Wave">French New Wave</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> it displays many of the characteristic traits of the movement. Written by Truffaut and Marcel Moussy, the film is about <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Doinel" title="Antoine Doinel">Antoine Doinel</a>, a misunderstood adolescent in Paris who is thought by his parents and teachers to be a troublemaker. Filmed on location in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Honfleur" title="Honfleur">Honfleur</a>, it is the first in a series of five films in which Léaud plays the semi-autobiographical character.</p>
<p><i>The 400 Blows</i> received numerous awards and nominations, including the <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a> Award for Best Director, the OCIC Award, and a <a href="/wiki/Palme_d%27Or" title="Palme d'Or">Palme d'Or</a> nomination in 1959. The film was also nominated for an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a> for Best Writing in 1960. <i>The 400 Blows</i> had a total of 4,092,970 admissions in France, making it Truffaut's most successful film in his home country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jp_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-jp-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The 400 Blows</i> is widely considered one of the best French films in the history of cinema; in the 2012 <i><a href="/wiki/Sight_%26_Sound" title="Sight &amp; Sound">Sight &amp; Sound</a></i> critics' poll of the greatest films ever made, it was ranked 39th.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_400_Blows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Antoine_Doinel" title="Antoine Doinel">Antoine Doinel</a> (<a href="/wiki/Jean-Pierre_L%C3%A9aud" title="Jean-Pierre Léaud">Jean-Pierre Léaud</a>) is a young boy growing up in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> during the 1950s. Misunderstood at home by his parents for wagging school and stealing things and tormented in school for discipline problems by his teacher (Guy Decomble) (Antoine falsely explains his being away from school was due to his mother's death), Antoine frequently runs away from both places. The boy finally quits school after being caught plagiarizing <a href="/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_de_Balzac" title="Honoré de Balzac">Balzac</a> by his teacher. He steals a typewriter from his stepfather's (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albert_Remy" title="Albert Remy">Albert Remy</a>) work place to finance his plans to leave home, but is apprehended while trying to return it.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Famous_Final_Frame_of_%22Les_Quatre_Cents_Coups%22.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="412" data-file-width="960" height="94" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/47/The_Famous_Final_Frame_of_%22Les_Quatre_Cents_Coups%22.jpg/220px-The_Famous_Final_Frame_of_%22Les_Quatre_Cents_Coups%22.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/47/The_Famous_Final_Frame_of_%22Les_Quatre_Cents_Coups%22.jpg/330px-The_Famous_Final_Frame_of_%22Les_Quatre_Cents_Coups%22.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/47/The_Famous_Final_Frame_of_%22Les_Quatre_Cents_Coups%22.jpg/440px-The_Famous_Final_Frame_of_%22Les_Quatre_Cents_Coups%22.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Antoine Doinel in the final scene</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The stepfather turns Antoine over to the police and Antoine spends the night in jail, sharing a cell with prostitutes and thieves. During an interview with the judge, Antoine’s mother confesses that her husband is not Antoine’s biological father. Antoine is placed in an <a href="/wiki/Youth_detention_center" title="Youth detention center">observation center for troubled youths</a> near the seashore (as per his mother's wishes). A psychologist at the center probes reasons for Antoine's unhappiness, which the youth reveals in a fragmented series of monologues.</p>
<p>One day, while playing football with the other boys, Antoine escapes under a fence and runs away to the ocean, a place he has wanted to visit his entire life. He reaches the shoreline of the sea and runs into it. The film concludes with a freeze-frame of Antoine, and the camera optically zooms in on his face, looking into the camera.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_400_Blows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Title">Title</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_400_Blows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Title">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The English title is a <a href="/wiki/Literal_translation" title="Literal translation">literal translation</a> of the French but misses its meaning, as the French title refers to the idiom <i>"faire les quatre cents coups",</i> which means "to raise hell". On the first prints in the United States, subtitler and dubber <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Noelle_Gilmore&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Noelle Gilmore (page does not exist)">Noelle Gilmore</a> gave the film the title <i>Wild Oats</i>, but the distributor did not like that and reverted it to <i>The 400 Blows.</i> Before seeing it, some people thought the film covered the topic of corporal punishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes">Themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_400_Blows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The semi-autobiographical film reflects events of Truffaut's and his friends' lives. In style, it expresses Truffaut's personal history of French film, with references to other works—most notably a scene borrowed wholesale from <a href="/wiki/Jean_Vigo" title="Jean Vigo">Jean Vigo</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Z%C3%A9ro_de_conduite" title="Zéro de conduite">Zéro de conduite</a></i>. Truffaut dedicated the film to the man who became his spiritual father, <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Bazin" title="André Bazin">André Bazin</a>, who died just as the film was about to be shot.</p>
<p>Besides being a character study, the film is an exposé of the injustices of the treatment of juvenile offenders in France at the time.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_400_Blows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:250px; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"A seminal French New Wave film that offers an honest, sympathetic, and wholly heartbreaking observation of adolescence without trite nostalgia."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"><a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, <i>The 400 Blows (Les Quatre cents coups) (1959)</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rt_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-rt-7">[7]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>The film was widely acclaimed, winning numerous awards, including the <a href="/wiki/Best_Director_Award_(Cannes_Film_Festival)" title="Best Director Award (Cannes Film Festival)">Best Director Award</a> at the <a href="/wiki/1959_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="1959 Cannes Film Festival">1959 Cannes Film Festival</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-festival-cannes.com_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-festival-cannes.com-8">[8]</a></sup> the Critics Award of the 1959 New York Film Critics' Circle and the Best European Film Award at 1960's <a href="/wiki/Bodil_Awards" title="Bodil Awards">Bodil Awards</a>. It was nominated for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Writing_Original_Screenplay" title="Academy Award for Writing Original Screenplay">Best Original Screenplay</a> at the <a href="/wiki/32nd_Academy_Awards" title="32nd Academy Awards">32nd Academy Awards</a>. The film holds a very rare 100% "Certified Fresh" rating on <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> based on 54 reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rt_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-rt-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film is among the top ten of the <a href="/wiki/British_Film_Institute" title="British Film Institute">British Film Institute</a>'s <a href="/wiki/BFI_list_of_the_50_films_you_should_see_by_the_age_of_14" title="BFI list of the 50 films you should see by the age of 14">list of 50 films that should be seen by age 14</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_400_Blows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Truffaut made four other films with Léaud depicting Antoine at later stages of his life. He meets his first love, Colette, in <i><a href="/wiki/Antoine_and_Colette" title="Antoine and Colette">Antoine and Colette</a></i>, which was Truffaut's contribution to the 1962 anthology <i>Love at Twenty</i>. He falls in love with Christine Darbon (<a href="/wiki/Claude_Jade" title="Claude Jade">Claude Jade</a>) in <i><a href="/wiki/Stolen_Kisses" title="Stolen Kisses">Stolen Kisses</a></i>. He marries Christine in <i><a href="/wiki/Bed_and_Board_(1970_film)" title="Bed and Board (1970 film)">Bed and Board</a></i>, but the couple have separated in <i><a href="/wiki/Love_on_the_Run_(1979_film)" title="Love on the Run (1979 film)">Love on the Run</a></i>.</p>
<p>Filmmakers <a href="/wiki/Akira_Kurosawa" title="Akira Kurosawa">Akira Kurosawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luis_Bu%C3%B1uel" title="Luis Buñuel">Luis Buñuel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Satyajit_Ray" title="Satyajit Ray">Satyajit Ray</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Cocteau" title="Jean Cocteau">Jean Cocteau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Theodor_Dreyer" title="Carl Theodor Dreyer">Carl Theodor Dreyer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lester" title="Richard Lester">Richard Lester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norman_Jewison" title="Norman Jewison">Norman Jewison</a> have cited <i>The 400 Blows</i> as one of their favorite movies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Kurosawa called it "one of the most beautiful films that I have ever seen".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film was ranked #29 in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empire_(magazine)" title="Empire (magazine)">Empire</a></i> magazine's list of "The 100 Best Films of World Cinema" in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The festival poster for the <a href="/wiki/71st_Venice_International_Film_Festival" title="71st Venice International Film Festival">71st Venice International Film Festival</a> paid tribute to the film as it featured the character of Antoine Doinel portrayed by Jean-Pierre Léaud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
