<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baklava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word baklava is first attested in English in 1650,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> a borrowing from <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turkish_language" title="Ottoman Turkish language">Ottoman Turkish</a> <span lang="ota" xml:lang="ota"><span class="Nastaliq" style="font-size: 125%; font-family: 'Jameel Noori Nastaleeq', 'Urdu Typesetting', 'Noto Nastaliq Urdu', 'Noto Nastaliq Urdu Draft', 'Hussaini Nastaleeq', 'AlQalam Taj Nastaleeq', IranNastaliq, 'Awami Nastaliq', 'Awami Nastaliq Beta4', 'Awami Nastaliq Beta3', 'Awami Nastaliq Beta2', 'Awami Nastaliq Beta1', 'Nafees Nastaleeq', 'Nafees Nastaleeq v1.01', 'Pak Nastaleeq', 'PDMS_Jauhar', 'Alvi Lahori Nastaleeq';" title="Nastaliq">بقلاوه</span></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/bɑːklɑvɑː/</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dict_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-dict-6">[6]</a></sup> The name baklava is used in many languages with minor phonetic and spelling variations.</p>
<p>Buell argues that the word "baklava" may come from the <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_language" title="Mongolian language">Mongolian</a> root <i><span lang="mn" xml:lang="mn">baγla-</span></i> 'to tie, wrap up, pile up' composed with the <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">Turkic</a> verbal ending <i>-v</i>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buell_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Buell-7">[7]</a></sup> baγla- itself in Mongolian is a Turkic loanword.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> However, a possible Turkish origin is rejected by German Turkologist <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Doerfer" title="Gerhard Doerfer">G. Doerfer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Another form of the word is also recorded in Persian, <span lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">باقلبا</span> (<i><span lang="fa-Latn" xml:lang="fa-Latn">bāqlabā</span></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Though the suffix <i>-vā</i> might suggest a Persian origin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Batmanglij.2C_Najmieh_2007.2C_page_156_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Batmanglij.2C_Najmieh_2007.2C_page_156-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> the <i>baqla-</i> part does not appear to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persian_Language" title="Persian Language">Persian</a> and remains of unknown origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Arabic name <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">بقلاوة</span> <i><span lang="ar-Latn" xml:lang="ar-Latn">baqlāwa</span></i> is doubtless a borrowing from Turkish,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lambraki.2C_p._248-249_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lambraki.2C_p._248-249-14">[14]</a></sup> though a <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">folk etymology</a>, unsupported by <a href="/wiki/Dictionary_of_Modern_Written_Arabic" title="Dictionary of Modern Written Arabic">Wehr's dictionary</a>, connects it to Arabic <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">بقلة</span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/baqlah/</span> 'bean'.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baklava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</div>
<p>Although the history of baklava is not well documented, its current form was probably developed in the imperial kitchens of the <a href="/wiki/Topkap%C4%B1_Palace" title="Topkapı Palace">Topkapı Palace</a> in <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The Sultan presented trays of baklava to the <a href="/wiki/Janissaries" title="Janissaries">Janissaries</a> every 15th of the month of <a href="/wiki/Ramadan" title="Ramadan">Ramadan</a> in a ceremonial procession called the <i><span lang="ota-Latn" xml:lang="ota-Latn">Baklava Alayı</span></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marks_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-marks-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are three proposals for the pre-<a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> roots of baklava: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Asian_cuisine" title="Central Asian cuisine">Central Asian</a> <a href="/wiki/Turkic_peoples" title="Turkic peoples">Turkic</a> tradition of layered breads,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-perry_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-perry-18">[18]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> <a href="/wiki/Placenta_cake" title="Placenta cake">placenta cake</a>, as developed through <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_cuisine" title="Byzantine cuisine">Byzantine cuisine</a>, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faas_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-faas-19">[19]</a></sup> or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persian_cuisine" title="Persian cuisine">Persian</a> lauzinaq.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marks_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-marks-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The oldest (2nd century BCE) recipe that resembles a similar dessert is the honey covered baked layered-dough dessert <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><a href="/wiki/Placenta_cake" title="Placenta cake">placenta</a></span></i> of Roman times, which Patrick Faas identifies as the origin of baklava: "The Greeks and the Turks still argue over which dishes were originally Greek and which Turkish. Baklava, for example, is claimed by both countries. Greek and Turkish cuisine both built upon the cookery of the Byzantine Empire, which was a continuation of the cooking of the Roman Empire. Roman cuisine had borrowed a great deal from the ancient Greeks, but <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">placenta</span></i> (and hence baklava) had a Latin, not a Greek, origin—please note that the conservative, anti-Greek Cato left us this recipe."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faas_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-faas-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Shape the <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">placenta</span></i> as follows: place a single row of <i><span lang="el-Latn" xml:lang="el-Latn">tracta</span></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> along the whole length of the base dough. This is then covered with the mixture [cheese and honey] from the mortar. Place another row of <i><span lang="el-Latn" xml:lang="el-Latn">tracta</span></i> on top and go on doing so until all the cheese and honey have been used up. Finish with a layer of <i><span lang="el-Latn" xml:lang="el-Latn">tracta</span></i>. ... place the placenta in the oven and put a preheated lid on top of it ... When ready, honey is poured over the placenta.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Cato the Elder, <a href="/wiki/De_Agri_Cultura" title="De Agri Cultura">De Agri Cultura</a> 160 BC<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faas_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-faas-19">[19]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Andrew_Dalby" title="Andrew Dalby">Andrew Dalby</a> identifies this, and surrounding dessert recipes in Cato, as coming from a "Greek tradition" and cites <a href="/wiki/Antiphanes_(comic_poet)" title="Antiphanes (comic poet)">Antiphanes</a> (fl. 3rd century BC) as quoted by <a href="/wiki/Athenaeus" title="Athenaeus">Athenaeus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalby21_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dalby21-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalby155_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dalby155-23">[23]</a></sup> Some sources state that this Roman dessert continued to evolve during the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> (Eastern Roman) Empire into modern baklava.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In antiquity the Greek word <i><span lang="el-Latn" xml:lang="el-Latn">plakous</span></i> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">πλακοῦς</span>) was also used for Latin <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">placenta</span></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalby155_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dalby155-23">[23]</a></sup> and the American scholar <a href="/wiki/Speros_Vryonis" title="Speros Vryonis">Speros Vryonis</a> describes one type of plakous, koptoplakous (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine_Greek" title="Byzantine Greek">Byzantine Greek</a>: κοπτοπλακοῦς), as a "Byzantine favorite" and "the same as the Turkish baklava",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Speros_Vryonis_1971.2C_p._482_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Speros_Vryonis_1971.2C_p._482-26">[26]</a></sup> as do other writers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Indeed, the Roman word <i>placenta</i> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">πλατσέντα</span>) is used on the island of <a href="/wiki/Lesbos" title="Lesbos">Lesbos</a> in Greece to describe a baklava-type dessert of layered pastry leaves containing crushed nuts that is baked and then covered in honey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Agia_Paraskevi_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Agia_Paraskevi-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cookbook_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-cookbook-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Muhammad_bin_Hasan_al-Baghdadi" title="Muhammad bin Hasan al-Baghdadi">Muhammad bin Hasan al-Baghdadi</a> was a compiler from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbasid_dynasty" title="Abbasid dynasty">Abbasid period</a> who described lauzinaq, a dessert said by some to have been similar to baklava, though others say it was not like baklava.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perry_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Perry-31">[31]</a></sup> <i>Lauzinaq,</i> which derives from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aramaic" title="Aramaic">Aramaic</a> word for almond, refers to small pieces of <a href="/wiki/Almond_paste" title="Almond paste">almond paste</a> wrapped in very thin pastry ("as thin as grasshoppers' wings") and drenched in syrup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perry2_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Perry2-32">[32]</a></sup> Al-Baghdadi's cookbook, <i><span lang="ar-Latn" xml:lang="ar-Latn">Kitab al-Tabikh</span>,</i> was written in 1226 (in today's <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>) and was based on a collection of 9th century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persian_cuisine" title="Persian cuisine">Persian</a>-inspired recipes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marks_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-marks-16">[16]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Gil_Marks" title="Gil Marks">Gil Marks</a>, Middle Eastern pastry makers developed the process of layering the ingredients; he asserts that "some scholars said they were influenced by Mongols or Turks".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marks_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-marks-16">[16]</a></sup> The only original manuscript of al-Baghdadi's book survives at the <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr">Süleymaniye</span> Library in <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a> (<a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>) and according to <a href="/wiki/Charles_Perry_(food_writer)" title="Charles Perry (food writer)">Charles Perry</a>, "for centuries, it had been the favorite cookbook of the Turks," though Perry also notes that the manuscript has no recipe for baklava.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> A further 260 recipes had been added to the original by Turkish compilers at an unknown date retitling it as <i><span lang="ota-Latn" xml:lang="ota-Latn">Kitâbü’l-Vasfi’l-Et‘ime el-Mu‘tâde</span></i>, and two of its known three copies can be found now at the Topkapı Palace Library in Istanbul. Eventually, Muhammad ibn Mahmud al-Shirwani, the physician of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> Sultan <a href="/wiki/Murad_II" title="Murad II">Murad II</a> prepared a Turkish translation of the book, adding around 70 contemporary recipes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Another recipe for a similar dessert is <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BClla%C3%A7" title="Güllaç">güllaç</a>, a dessert found in the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_cuisine" title="Turkish cuisine">Turkish cuisine</a> and considered by some as the origin of baklava.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Husihui_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Husihui-34">[34]</a></sup> It consists of layers of <a href="/wiki/Filo" title="Filo">filo</a> dough that are put one by one in warmed up milk with sugar. It is served with walnut and fresh pomegranate and generally eaten during <a href="/wiki/Ramadan" title="Ramadan">Ramadan</a>. The first known documentation of <i><span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr">güllaç</span></i> is attested in a food and health manual, written in 1330 that documents <a href="/wiki/Mongol_Empire" title="Mongol Empire">Mongol</a> foods called Yinshan Zhengyao (飮膳正要, <i>Important Principles of Food and Drink</i>), written by <a href="/wiki/Hu_Sihui" title="Hu Sihui">Hu Sihui</a>, an ethnic Mongol court dietitian of the <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">Yuan dynasty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buell_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Buell-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Uzbek_cuisine" title="Uzbek cuisine">Uzbek cuisine</a> has <i><span lang="uz" xml:lang="uz">pakhlava</span></i>, <i><span lang="uz" xml:lang="uz">puskal</span></i> or <i><span lang="uz" xml:lang="uz">yupka</span></i> or in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tatar_people" title="Tatar people">Tatar</a> <i><span lang="tt" xml:lang="tt">yoka</span></i>, which are sweet and salty savories (<i><span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr"><a href="/wiki/B%C3%B6rek" title="Börek">börekler</a></span></i>) prepared with 10–12 layers of dough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lambraki.2C_p._248-249_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lambraki.2C_p._248-249-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are also some similarities between baklava and the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_cuisine" title="Ancient Greek cuisine">Ancient Greek</a> desserts <i><span lang="el-Latn" xml:lang="el-Latn">gastris</span></i> (<span lang="el" xml:lang="el">γάστρις</span>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> <i><span lang="el-Latn" xml:lang="el-Latn">kopte sesamis</span></i> (<span lang="el" xml:lang="el">κοπτὴ σησαμίς</span>), and <i><span lang="el-Latn" xml:lang="el-Latn">kopton</span></i> (<span lang="el" xml:lang="el">κοπτόν</span>) found in book XIV of the <span lang="el-Latn" xml:lang="el-Latn"><a href="/wiki/Deipnosophistae" title="Deipnosophistae">Deipnosophistae</a></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> However, the recipe there is for a filling of nuts and honey, with a top and bottom layer of honey and ground <a href="/wiki/Sesame" title="Sesame">sesame</a> similar to modern <i><span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sesame_candy" title="Sesame candy">pasteli</a></span></i> or <i><span lang="fa-Latn" xml:lang="fa-Latn"><a href="/wiki/Halva" title="Halva">halva</a></span></i>, and no dough, certainly not a flaky dough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baklava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Baklava is normally prepared in large pans. Many layers of <a href="/wiki/Filo" title="Filo">filo</a> dough,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> separated with melted butter and vegetable oil, are laid in the pan. A layer of chopped nuts—typically <a href="/wiki/Walnut" title="Walnut">walnuts</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pistachio" title="Pistachio">pistachios</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Hazelnut" title="Hazelnut">hazelnuts</a> are also sometimes used—is placed on top, then more layers of filo. Most recipes have multiple layers of filo and nuts, though some have only top and bottom pastry.</p>
<p>Before baking (180 °C, 356 °F, 30 minutes), the dough is cut into regular pieces, often parallelograms (lozenge-shaped), triangles, diamonds or rectangles. After baking, a <a href="/wiki/Syrup" title="Syrup">syrup</a>, which may include <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rosewater" title="Rosewater">rosewater</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Orange_flower_water" title="Orange flower water">orange flower water</a> is poured over the cooked baklava and allowed to soak in.</p>
<p>Baklava is usually served at room temperature, often garnished with ground nuts.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_variations">Regional variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baklava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Regional variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, baklava is traditionally made by filling between the layers of dough with pistachios, walnuts, almonds (parts of the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Region" title="Aegean Region">Aegean Region</a>) or a special preparation called <i><span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr">kaymak</span></i> (not to be confused with <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr"><a href="/wiki/Kaymak" title="Kaymak">kaymak</a></span>, the creamy dairy product). In the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea_Region" title="Black Sea Region">Black Sea Region</a> <a href="/wiki/Hazelnut" title="Hazelnut">hazelnuts</a> are commonly used as a filling for baklava.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The city of <a href="/wiki/Gaziantep" title="Gaziantep">Gaziantep</a> in southeast Turkey is famous for its pistachio baklava and it regards itself as the native city for this dish, though it only appears to have been introduced to Gaziantep from Damascus in 1871.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In 2008, the Turkish patent office registered a <a href="/wiki/Geographical_indication" title="Geographical indication">geographical indication</a> for Antep Baklava,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> and in 2013, <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr">Antep Baklavası</span> or <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr">Gaziantep Baklavası</span> was registered as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protected_Geographical_Indication" title="Protected Geographical Indication">Protected Geographical Indication</a> by the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In many parts of Turkey, baklava is often topped with <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr"><a href="/wiki/Kaymak" title="Kaymak">kaymak</a></span> or, in the summer, ice cream (milk cream flavour, called <i><span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr">kaymaklı dondurma</span></i>).</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, baklava is supposed to be made with 33 dough layers, referring to the years of Christ's life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Azerbaijani_pakhlava" title="Azerbaijani pakhlava">Azerbaijani pakhlava</a></i> is made with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walnuts" title="Walnuts">walnuts</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almonds" title="Almonds">almonds</a>. It is usually cut in a rhombus shape. It is traditionally served during the spring holiday of <span lang="az" xml:lang="az"><a href="/wiki/Nowruz" title="Nowruz">Nowruz</a></span>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a>, <i><span lang="hy-Latn" xml:lang="hy-Latn">paklava</span></i> is made with <a href="/wiki/Cinnamon" title="Cinnamon">cinnamon</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cloves" title="Cloves">cloves</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, a drier version of baklava is cooked and presented in smaller diamond-shaped cuts flavored with <a href="/wiki/Rose_water" title="Rose water">rose water</a>. The cities of <span lang="fa-Latn" xml:lang="fa-Latn"><a href="/wiki/Yazd" title="Yazd">Yazd</a></span> and <span lang="fa-Latn" xml:lang="fa-Latn"><a href="/wiki/Qazvin" title="Qazvin">Qazvin</a></span> are famous for their baklava, which is widely distributed in Iran.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Persian baklava uses a combination of chopped almonds and pistachios spiced with cardamom and a rose water-scented syrup and is lighter than other Middle Eastern versions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Batmanglij.2C_Najmieh_2007.2C_page_156_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Batmanglij.2C_Najmieh_2007.2C_page_156-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijani_pakhlava" title="Azerbaijani pakhlava">Azerbaijani pakhlava</a> is widely eaten in Iran, especially in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iranian_Azerbaijan" title="Iranian Azerbaijan">Iranian Azerbaijan</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and <a href="/wiki/State_of_Palestine" title="State of Palestine">Palestine</a>, baklava is prepared from filo dough sheets, butter, walnuts and sugar syrup. It is cut into lozenge pieces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">Maghreb</a>, mainly <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, the pastry was brought along many others by the Ottomans, and is prepared differently depending on the regions and cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina_cuisine" title="Bosnia and Herzegovina cuisine">Bosnia and Herzegovina cuisine</a> the <i>Ruzice</i> is their variant of baklava.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Crimean_Tatar_cuisine" title="Crimean Tatar cuisine">Crimean Tatar cuisine</a> the <i><span lang="tt" xml:lang="tt">pakhlava</span></i> is their variant of baklava.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
