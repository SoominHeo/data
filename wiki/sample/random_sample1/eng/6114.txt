<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Bacchylides</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Bacchylides</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="'z' in 'Zion'">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Βακχυλίδης</span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271878_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271878-1">[1]</a></sup> <i>Bakkhylídēs</i>; <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a href="/wiki/Floruit" title="Floruit">fl.</a> 5th</span> century <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">BC</span></span>) was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_lyric_poet" title="Greek lyric poet">Greek lyric poet</a>. Later Greeks included him in the canonical list of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nine_lyric_poets" title="Nine lyric poets">nine lyric poets</a> which included his uncle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simonides" title="Simonides">Simonides</a>. The elegance and polished style of his lyrics have been noted in Bacchylidean scholarship since at least <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Longinus_(literature)" title="Longinus (literature)">Longinus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Some scholars, however, have characterized these qualities as <a href="/wiki/Superficial_charm" title="Superficial charm">superficial charm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bu3_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-bu3-4">[4]</a></sup> He has often been compared unfavourably with his contemporary, <a href="/wiki/Pindar" title="Pindar">Pindar</a>, as "a kind of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boccherini" title="Boccherini">Boccherini</a> to Pindar's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haydn" title="Haydn">Haydn</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slavitt.2C_p._1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Slavitt.2C_p._1-5">[5]</a></sup> yet the differences in their styles don't allow for easy comparison, with translator <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fagles" title="Robert Fagles">Robert Fagles</a> writing that "to blame Bacchylides for not being Pindar is as childish a judgement as to condemn...<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andrew_Marvel" title="Andrew Marvel">Marvel</a> for missing the grandeur of <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">Milton</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> His career coincided with the ascendency of dramatic styles of poetry, as embodied in the works of <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">Aeschylus</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sophocles" title="Sophocles">Sophocles</a>, and he is in fact considered one of the last poets of major significance within the more ancient tradition of purely lyric poetry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-j27_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-j27-8">[8]</a></sup> The most notable features of his lyrics are their clarity in expression and simplicity of thought,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c415_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-c415-9">[9]</a></sup> making them an ideal introduction to the study of <a href="/wiki/Lyric_poetry" title="Lyric poetry">Greek lyric poetry</a> in general and to Pindar's verse in particular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacchylides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;"><i>One canon is there, one sure way of happiness for mortals – if one can keep a cheerful spirit throughout life.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>This precept, from one of Bacchylides' extant fragments, was considered by his modern editor, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Claverhouse_Jebb" title="Richard Claverhouse Jebb">Richard Claverhouse Jebb</a>, to be typical of the poet's temperament: "If the utterances scattered throughout the poems warrant a conjecture, Bacchylides was of placid temper; amiably tolerant; satisfied with a modest lot; not free from some tinge of that pensive melancholy which was peculiarly Ionian; but with good sense..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bacchylides' lyrics do not seem to have been popular in his own lifetime. Lyrics by his uncle, Simonides, and his rival, Pindar, were known in Athens and were sung at parties, they were parodied by <a href="/wiki/Aristophanes" title="Aristophanes">Aristophanes</a> and quoted by <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>, but no trace of Bacchylides' work can be found until the Hellenistic age, when <a href="/wiki/Callimachus" title="Callimachus">Callimachus</a> began writing some commentaries on them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Like Simonides and Pindar, however, Bacchylides composed lyrics to appeal to the sophisticated tastes of a social elite<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and his patrons, though relatively few in number, covered a wide, geographical area around the Mediterranean, including for example <a href="/wiki/Delos" title="Delos">Delos</a> in The Aegean Sea, <a href="/wiki/Thessaly" title="Thessaly">Thessaly</a> to the north of mainland Greece and Sicily or <a href="/wiki/Magna_Graecia" title="Magna Graecia">Magna Graecia</a> in the west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It has been inferred from the elegance and quiet charm of his lyrics that he only gradually acquired fame towards the end of his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Being drawn from sources compiled long after his death, the details of Bacchylides's life are sketchy and sometimes contradictory. According to <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a>, he was born in <a href="/wiki/Ioulis" title="Ioulis">Ioulis</a>, on the island of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceos" title="Ceos">Ceos</a>, and his mother was the sister of Simonides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Suda" title="Suda">Suda</a>, his father's name was Meidon and his grandfather, also named Bacchylides, was a famous athlete,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> yet according to <a href="/wiki/Etymologicum_Magnum" title="Etymologicum Magnum">Etymologicum Magnum</a> his father's name was Meidylus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> There is an ancient tradition, upheld for example by <a href="/wiki/Eustathius_of_Thessalonica" title="Eustathius of Thessalonica">Eustathius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Magister" title="Thomas Magister">Thomas Magister</a>, that he was younger than Pindar and some modern scholars have endorsed it, such as <a href="/wiki/Richard_Claverhouse_Jebb" title="Richard Claverhouse Jebb">Jebb</a>, who assigns his birth to around 507 BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> whereas <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Bowra" title="Maurice Bowra">Bowra</a>, for example, opted for a much earlier date, around 524–1 BC. Most modern scholars however treat Bacchylides as an exact contemporary of Pindar, placing his birth around 518 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g278_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-g278-21">[21]</a></sup> According to one account, Bacchylides was banished for a time from his native Ceos and spent this period as an exile in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peloponnesus" title="Peloponnesus">Peloponnesus</a>, where his genius ripened and he did the work which established his fame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a> is the only ancient source for this account and yet it is considered credible on the basis of some literary evidence<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> (Pindar wrote a <a href="/wiki/Paean" title="Paean">paean</a> celebrating Ceos, in which he says on behalf of the island "I am renowned for my athletic achievements among Greeks" [Paean 4, epode 1], a circumstance that suggests that Bacchylides himself was unavailable at the time.) Observations by <a href="/wiki/Eusebius_of_Alexandria" title="Eusebius of Alexandria">Eusebius</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georgius_Syncellus" title="Georgius Syncellus">Georgius Syncellus</a> can be taken to indicate that Bacchylides might have been still alive at the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/Peloponnesian_War" title="Peloponnesian War">Peloponnesian War</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-David_Campbell.2C_page_414_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-David_Campbell.2C_page_414-24">[24]</a></sup> but modern scholars have differed widely in estimates of the year of his death – Jebb, for example sets it at 428 BC<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and yet a date around 451 BC is more favoured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g278_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-g278-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceos" title="Ceos">Ceos</a>, where Bacchylides was born and raised, had long had a history of poetical and musical culture, especially in its association with <a href="/wiki/Delos" title="Delos">Delos</a>, the focal point of the <a href="/wiki/Cyclades" title="Cyclades">Cyclades</a> and the principal sanctuary of the <a href="/wiki/Ionians" title="Ionians">Ionian race</a>, where the people of Ceos annually sent choirs to celebrate festivals of Apollo. There was a thriving cult of Apollo on Ceos too, including a temple at Carthaea, a training ground for choruses where, according to <a href="/wiki/Athenaeus" title="Athenaeus">Athenaeus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Bacchylides's uncle, Simonides, had been a teacher in his early years. Ceans had a strong sense of their national identity, characterized by their own exotic legends, national folklore and a successful tradition of athletic competition, especially in running and boxing – making the island a congenial home for a boy of quick imagination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Athletic victories achieved by Ceans in panhellenic festivals were recorded at Ioulis on slabs of stone and thus Bacchylides could readily announce, in an ode celebrating one such victory (Ode 2), a total of twenty-seven victories won by his countrymen at the <a href="/wiki/Isthmian_Games" title="Isthmian Games">Isthmian Games</a>. Ceans had participated in the defeat of the Persians at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Salamis" title="Battle of Salamis">Battle of Salamis</a> and they could take pride in the fact that an elegy composed by Bacchylides's uncle was chosen by Athens to commemorate the Athenians who fell at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Marathon" title="Battle of Marathon">Battle of Marathon</a>. Being only thirteen miles from the Athenian cape <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunium" title="Sunium">Sunium</a>, Ceos was in fact necessarily responsive to Athenian influences.</p>
<p>Bacchylides's career as a poet probably benefitted from the high reputation of his uncle, Simonides, whose patrons, when Bacchylides was born, already included <a href="/wiki/Hipparchus_(son_of_Peisistratos)" title="Hipparchus (son of Peisistratos)">Hipparchus (son of Peisistratos)</a>, brother of <a href="/wiki/Hippias_(tyrant)" title="Hippias (tyrant)">Hippias</a> the tyrant of Athens (527–14 BC) and cultural coordinator of the city at that time. Simonides later introduced his nephew to ruling families in Thessaly and to the Sicilian tyrant, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hieron_of_Syracuse" title="Hieron of Syracuse">Hieron of Syracuse</a>, whose glittering court attracted artists of the calibre of Pindar and <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">Aeschylus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Bacchylides's first notable success came sometime after 500 BC with commissions from Athens for the great Delian festival (Ode 17) and from Macedonia for a song to be sung at a <a href="/wiki/Symposium" title="Symposium">symposium</a> for the young prince, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_I_of_Macedon" title="Alexander I of Macedon">Alexander I</a> (fr. 20B). Soon he was competing with Pindar for commissions from the leading families of <a href="/wiki/Aegina" title="Aegina">Aegina</a> and, in 476 BC, their rivalry seems to have reached the highest levels when Bacchylides composed an ode celebrating Hieron's first victory at the Olympian Games (Ode 5). Pindar celebrated the same victory but used the occasion to advise the tyrant of the need for moderation in one's personal conduct (Pindar's Olympian Ode 1), whereas Bacchylides probably offered his own ode as a free sample of his skill in the hope of attracting future commissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Bacchylides was commissioned by Hieron in 470 BC, this time to celebrate his triumph in the chariot race at the <a href="/wiki/Pythian_Games" title="Pythian Games">Pythian Games</a> (Ode 4). Pindar also composed a celebratory ode for this victory (Pindar's Pythian Ode 1), including however stern, moral advice for the tyrant to rule wisely. Pindar was not commissioned to celebrate Hieron's subsequent victory in the chariot race at the Olympic Games in 468 BC – this, the most prestigious of Hieron's victories, was however celebrated by Bacchylides (Ode 3). The tyrant's apparent preference for Bacchylides over Pindar on this occasion might have been partly due to the Cean poet's simpler language and not just to his less moralizing posture,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c415_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-c415-9">[9]</a></sup> and yet it is also possible that Bacchylides and his uncle were simply better suited to palace politics than was their more high-minded rival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandrian</a> scholars in fact interpreted a number of passages in Pindar as hostile allusions to Bacchylides and Simonides and this interpretation has been endorsed by modern scholars also.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-David_Campbell.2C_page_414_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-David_Campbell.2C_page_414-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a composer of choral lyrics, Bacchylides was probably responsible also for the performance, involving him in frequent travel to venues where musicians and choirs awaited instruction. Ancient authorities testify to his visit to the court of Hieron (478–467)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and this is indeed indicated by his fifth Ode (476 BC), where the word <i>xenos</i> (V.11) implies that he had already been Hieron's guest, (probably accompanied by his uncle).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-David_Campbell.2C_page_414_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-David_Campbell.2C_page_414-24">[24]</a></sup> Verses 15 and 16 of his third ode (468 BC), also for Hieron, indicate that he might have composed that work at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syracuse,_Italy" title="Syracuse, Italy">Syracuse</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacchylides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacchylides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The poems were collected into critical editions sometime in the late 3rd century BC by the Alexandrian scholar, <a href="/wiki/Aristophanes_of_Byzantium" title="Aristophanes of Byzantium">Aristophanes of Byzantium</a>, who probably restored them to their appropriate metres after finding them written in prose form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> They were arranged in nine 'books', exemplifying the following genres<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c415_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-c415-9">[9]</a></sup> (Bacchylides in fact composed in a greater variety of genres than any of the other lyric poets who comprise the canonic nine, with the exception of Pindar, who composed in ten):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Alexandrian grammarian <a href="/wiki/Didymus_Chalcenterus" title="Didymus Chalcenterus">Didymus</a> (circa 30 BC) wrote commentaries on the work of Bacchylides and the poems appear, from the finding of papyri fragments, to have been popular reading in the first three centuries AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campbell_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell-37">[37]</a></sup> Their popularity seems to have continued into the 4th century also: <a href="/wiki/Ammianus_Marcellinus" title="Ammianus Marcellinus">Ammianus Marcellinus</a> (xxv. 4) observed that the emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julian_the_Apostate" title="Julian the Apostate">Julian</a> enjoyed reading Bacchylides, and the largest collection of quotations that survived up until the modern era was assembled by <a href="/wiki/Stobaeus" title="Stobaeus">Stobaeus</a> (early 5th century).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> All that remained of Bacchylides's poetry by 1896, however, were sixty-nine fragments, totalling 107 lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sl3_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-sl3-39">[39]</a></sup> These few remains of his writings were collected by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Fran%C3%A7ois_Philippe_Brunck" title="Richard François Philippe Brunck">Brunck</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wilhelm_Theodor_Bergk&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wilhelm Theodor Bergk (page does not exist)">Bergk</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bland" title="Robert Bland">Bland</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Johann_Adam_Hartung&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Johann Adam Hartung (page does not exist)">Hartung</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Christian_Friedrich_Neue" title="Christian Friedrich Neue">Neue</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271878_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271878-1">[1]</a></sup> The oldest sources on Bacchylides and his work are <a href="/wiki/Scholia" title="Scholia">scholia</a> on <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a>, Pindar, <a href="/wiki/Aristophanes" title="Aristophanes">Aristophanes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollonius_Rhodius" title="Apollonius Rhodius">Apollonius Rhodius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Callimachus" title="Callimachus">Callimachus</a>. <p>Fortunately for Bacchylidean scholarship, a papyrus came to light in Egypt at the end of the 19th century with a text of Greek uncials, which a local claimed to have found in a ransacked tomb, between the feet of a mummy. It was snapped up for a "preposterous" price by the great Egyptologist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wallis_Budge" title="Wallis Budge">Wallis Budge</a>, of the British Museum. Budge's plan to return to the museum with the papyrus was unacceptable to the British Consul and to the Egyptian Service of Antiquities so he resorted to desperate measures. In an elaborate plan involving a crate of oranges, switched trains and covert embarcations, he eventually sailed from the Suez with the papyrus dismembered and disguised as a packet of photographs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sl3_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-sl3-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> He presented his find in 1896 to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frederic_Kenyon" title="Frederic Kenyon">Frederic Kenyon</a> in the British Museum's Department of Manuscripts. Kenyon reassembled 1382 lines, of which 1070 were perfect or easily restored and, the following year, he published an edition of twenty poems, six of them nearly complete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sl3_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-sl3-39">[39]</a></sup> Some more pieces of the Egyptian fragments were fitted together by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Blass" title="Friedrich Blass">Friedrich Blass</a> in Germany and then followed the authoritative edition of Bacchylides' poetry by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Claverhouse_Jebb" title="Richard Claverhouse Jebb">Richard Claverhouse Jebb</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[n 1]</a></sup> – a combination of scholars that inspired one academic to comment: "we almost had the Renaissance back again".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>As noted by Frederic Kenyon,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Google_preview_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Google_preview-47">[46]</a></sup> the papyrus was originally a roll probably about seventeen feet long and about ten inches high, written in the Ptolemaic period, with some Roman characteristics that indicate a transition between styles, somewhere around 50 BC. It reached England in about two hundred torn fragments, the largest about twenty inches in length and containing four and a half columns of writing, the smallest being scraps with barely enough space for one or two letters. The beginning and end sections were missing and the damage done to the roll was not entirely the result of its recent discovery. Kenyon gradually pieced the fragments together, making three independent sections: the first, nine feet long with twenty-two columns of writing; the next section, a little over two feet long with six columns; the third, three and a half feet long with ten columns – a total length of almost fifteen feet and thirty-nine columns. <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Blass" title="Friedrich Blass">Friedrich Blass</a> later pieced together some of the still detached fragments and concluded that two of the poems on the restored roll (Odes vi. and vii., as numbered by Kenyon in the <i><a href="/wiki/Editio_princeps" title="Editio princeps">editio princeps</a></i>) must be parts of a single ode (for Lachon of Ceos) – hence even today the poems can be found numbered differently, with Jebb for example one of those following Blass's lead and numbering the poems differently from Kenyon from poem 8 onwards (Kenyon 9 = Jebb 8 and so on).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campbell_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bacchylides had become, almost overnight, among the best represented poets of the canonic nine, with about half as many extant verses as Pindar, adding about a hundred new words to Greek lexicons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> Ironically, his newly discovered poems sparked a renewed interest in Pindar's work,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup> with whom he was compared so unfavourably that "the students of Pindaric poetry almost succeeded in burying Bacchylides all over again."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bu3_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-bu3-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacchylides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>

<p>Much of Bacchylides's poetry was commissioned by proud and ambitious aristocrats, a dominant force in Greek political and cultural life in the 6th and early part of the 5th centuries, yet such patrons were gradually losing influence in an increasingly democratic Greek world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup> The kind of lofty and stately poetry that celebrated the achievements of these <a href="/wiki/Archaic_Greece" title="Archaic Greece">archaic</a> aristocrats was within the reach of 'The Cean nightingale',<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sl6_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-sl6-52">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-j78_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-j78-53">[52]</a></sup> yet he seems to have been more at home in verses of a humbler and lighter strain, even venturing on folksiness and humour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sl6_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-sl6-52">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-j78_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-j78-53">[52]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The distinctive merits of Bacchylides, his transparent clearness, his gift of narrative, his felicity in detail, the easy flow of his elegant verse, rather fitted him to become a favourite with readers... he was a poet who gave pleasure without demanding effort, a poet with whom the reader could at once feel at home. – Richard Claverhouse Jebb<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Lyric poetry was still a vigorous art-form and its genres were already fully developed when Bacchylides started out on his career. From the time of the <a href="/wiki/Peloponnesian_War" title="Peloponnesian War">Peloponnesian War</a>, around the end of his life, the art-form was in decline, as exemplified by the inferior dithyrambs of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philoxenos_of_Cythera" title="Philoxenos of Cythera">Philoxenos of Cythera</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-j27_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-j27-8">[8]</a></sup> Meanwhile, tragedy, as developed by Athenian dramatists of the calibre of <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">Aeschylus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sophocles" title="Sophocles">Sophocles</a>, had begun to emerge as the leading poetic genre, borrowing the literary dialect, the metres and poetic devices of lyric poetry in general and the dithyramb in particular (Aristotle <i>Poetics</i> IV 1449a). The debt however was mutual and Bacchylides borrowed from tragedy for some of his effects – thus Ode 16, with its myth of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deianeira" title="Deianeira">Deianeira</a>, seems to assume audience knowledge of Sophocles's play, <i>Women of Trachis</i>, and Ode 18 echoes three plays – Aeschylus's <i>Persians</i> and <i>Suppliants</i> and Sophocles's <i>Oedipus Rex</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup> His vocabulary shows the influence of Aeschylus with several words being common to both poets and found nowhere else.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[55]</a></sup> The use of gripping and exciting narrative and the immediacy gained from the frequent use of direct speech are thought to be among Bacchylides's best qualities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c415_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-c415-9">[9]</a></sup> influencing later poets such as <a href="/wiki/Horace" title="Horace">Horace</a> (who imitated him, according to <a href="/wiki/Pomponius_Porphyrion" title="Pomponius Porphyrion">Pomponius Porphyrion</a>, in <i>Carmen</i> I. 15, where <a href="/wiki/Nereus" title="Nereus">Nereus</a> predicts the destruction of <a href="/wiki/Troy" title="Troy">Troy</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[56]</a></sup> These narrative qualities were modelled largely on the work of <a href="/wiki/Stesichorus" title="Stesichorus">Stesichorus</a>, whose lyrical treatment of heroic myth influenced, for instance, Ode 5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[57]</a></sup> Whereas however Stesichorus developed graphic images in his poetry that subsequently became established in vase painting, Bacchylides merely employed images already current in his own day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jebb_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jebb-36">[36]</a></sup></p>

<p>Simonides, the uncle of Bacchylides, was another strong influence on his poetry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[58]</a></sup> as for example in his metrical range, mostly <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dactylo-epitrite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dactylo-epitrite (page does not exist)">dactylo-epitrite</a> in form, with some Aeolic rhythms and a few iambics. The surviving poems in fact are not metrically difficult, with the exception of two odes (Odes XV and XVI, Jebb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[59]</a></sup> He shared Simonides's approach to vocabulary, employing a very mild form of the traditional, literary Doric dialect, with some Aeolic words and some traditional epithets borrowed from epic. Like Simonides, he followed the lyric tradition of coining compound adjectives – a tradition in which the poet was expected to be both innovative and tasteful – but the results are thought by some modern scholars to be uneven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c415_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-c415-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[60]</a></sup> Many of his epithets however serve a thematic and not just a decorative function, as for instance in Ode 3, where the "bronze-walled court" and "well-built halls" of <a href="/wiki/Croesus" title="Croesus">Croesus</a> (Ode 3.30–31 and 3.46) contrast architecturally with the "wooden house" of his funeral pyre (Ode 3.49), in an effect that aims at pathos and which underscores the moral of the ode.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bacchylides is renowned for his use of picturesque detail, giving life and colour to descriptions with small but skilful touches, often demonstrating a keen sense of beauty or splendour in external nature: a radiance, "as of fire," streams from the forms of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nereids" title="Nereids">Nereids</a> (XVI. 103 if. Jebb); an athlete shines out among his fellows like "the bright moon of the mid-month night" among the stars (VIII. 27 if.); the sudden gleam of hope which comes to the <a href="/wiki/Trojan_War" title="Trojan War">Trojans</a> by the withdrawal of <a href="/wiki/Achilles" title="Achilles">Achilles</a> is like a ray of sunshine "from beneath the edge of a storm-cloud" (XII – 105 if.); the shades of the departed, as seen by Heracles on the banks of the Cocytus, resemble countless leaves fluttering in the wind on "the gleaming headlands of Ida" (V. 65 if ). Imagery is employed sparingly but often with impressive and beautiful results,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[62]</a></sup> such as in the simile of the eagle in Ode 5 below.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ode_5">Ode 5</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacchylides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ode 5">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bacchylides has often been compared unflatteringly with Pindar, as for example by the French critic, <a href="/wiki/Henri_Weil" title="Henri Weil">Henri Weil</a>: "There is no doubt that he fails of the elevation, and also of the depth, of Pindar. The soaring wing was refused him, and he should never have compared himself, as he does somewhere, to an eagle."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>The image of the eagle occurs in Ode 5, which was composed for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hieron_of_Syracuse" title="Hieron of Syracuse">Hieron of Syracuse</a> in celebration of his Olympic victory with the race-horse Pherenicus in 476 BC. Pindar's <i>Olympian Ode 1</i> celebrates the same race and the two poems allow for some interesting comparisons. Bacchylides's Ode 5 includes, in addition to a brief reference to the victory itself, a long mythical episode on a related theme, and a gnomic or philosophical reflection – elements that occur also in Pindar's ode and that seem typical of the <a href="/wiki/Epinikion" title="Epinikion">victory ode</a> genre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[64]</a></sup> Whereas however Pindar's ode focuses on the myth of <a href="/wiki/Pelops" title="Pelops">Pelops</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tantalus" title="Tantalus">Tantalus</a> and demonstrates a stern moral about the need for moderation in personal conduct (a reflection on Hieron's political excesses),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[65]</a></sup> Bacchylides's ode focuses on the myths of <a href="/wiki/Meleager" title="Meleager">Meleager</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hercules" title="Hercules">Hercules</a>, demonstrating the moral that nobody is fortunate or happy in all things (possibly a reflection on Hieron's chronic illness).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c423_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-c423-50">[49]</a></sup> This difference in moral posturing was typical of the two poets, with Bacchylides adopting a quieter, simpler and less forceful manner than Pindar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[66]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Frederic_G._Kenyon" title="Frederic G. Kenyon">Frederic G. Kenyon</a>, who edited the papyrus poems, took an unsympathetic view of Bacchylides's treatment of myth in general:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The myths are introduced mechanically, with little attempt to connect them with the subject of the ode. In some cases they appear to have no special appropriateness but to be introduced merely at the poet's pleasure. There is no originality of structure; the poet's art is shown in craftsmanship rather than in invention. – Frederic G. Kenyon<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Google_preview_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-Google_preview-47">[46]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>Bacchylides's image of the poet as an eagle winging across the sea was not original – Pindar had already used it earlier (<i>Nemean Odes</i> 5.20–21). In fact, in the same year that both poets celebrated Pherenicus's Olympic victory, Pindar also composed an ode for <a href="/wiki/Theron_of_Acragas" title="Theron of Acragas">Theron of Acragas</a> (<i>Olympian</i> 2), in which he likens himself to an eagle confronted with chattering ravens – possibly a reference to Bacchylides and his uncle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[70]</a></sup> It is possible in that case that Bacchylides's image of himself as an eagle in Ode 5 was a retort to Pindar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[71]</a></sup> Moreover, Bacchylides's line "So now for me too countless paths extend in all directions" has a close resemblance to lines in one of Pindar's Isthmian Odes (1.1–2), "A thousand ways ... open on every side widespread before me"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[72]</a></sup> but, as the date of Pindar's Isthmian Ode is uncertain, it is not clear in this case who was imitating whom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[73]</a></sup> According to Kenyon, Pindar's idionsyncratic genius entitles him to the benefit of a doubt in all such cases: "... if there be actual imitation at all, it is fairly safe to conclude that it is on the part of Bacchylides."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Google_preview_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-Google_preview-47">[46]</a></sup> In fact one modern scholar<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[74]</a></sup> has observed in Bacchylides a general tendency towards imitation, sometimes approaching the level of quotation: in this case, the eagle simile in Ode 5 may be thought to imitate a passage in the Homeric Hymn to Demeter (375–83), and the countless leaves fluttering in the wind on "the gleaming headlands of Ida", mentioned later in the ode, recall a passage in <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i> (6.146–9). A tendency to imitate other poets is not peculiar to Bacchylides, however – it was common in ancient poetry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[75]</a></sup> as for example in a poem by <a href="/wiki/Alcaeus_of_Mytilene" title="Alcaeus of Mytilene">Alcaeus</a> (fragment 347), which virtually quotes a passage from <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a> (<i>Works and Days</i> 582–8).</p>

<p>Ultimately, however, Bacchylides and Pindar share many of the same goals and techniques – the difference is largely one of temperament:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>They share a common repertory of motifs, images, conventions, diction; and they affirm and celebrate the heroic values of an ancient aristocracy. Both seek to bridge the gap between the fleeting present in its glorious display of beauty and energy and the eternal world of the gods. Pindar however grasps the contrasts between the extremes of mortality and divinity with greater intensity than Bacchylides and for this reason seems the more philosophical and meditative, more concerned with ultimate questions of life and death, transience and permanence. Bacchylides prefers to observe the gentler play of shadow and sadness over the sensuous surface of his brilliant world. – Charles Segal<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[79]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>You, Pindar, holy mouth of the Muses, and you, talkative Siren, Bacchylides ...-anon. in <a href="/wiki/Palatine_Anthology" title="Palatine Anthology">Palatine Anthology</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[80]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ode_13">Ode 13</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacchylides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ode 13">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ode 13 of the Bacchylides is a Nemean ode performed to honor the athlete Pytheas of <a href="/wiki/Aegina" title="Aegina">Aegina</a> for winning the pancration event of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nemean_games" title="Nemean games">Nemean games</a>. Bacchylides begins his ode with the tale of <a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">Heracles</a> fighting the <a href="/wiki/Nemean_lion" title="Nemean lion">Nemean lion</a>, employing the battle to explain why pancration tournaments are now held during the Nemean games. The allusion to Heracles’ fight with the lion is also meant to incite why it is that Pytheas fights for the wreaths of the games: to obtain the undying glory that the heroes of old now possess for their deeds. Bacchylides then sings the praises of Pytheas' home, the island Aegina, and how "her fame excites a dancer’s praise." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-83">[81]</a></sup> Bacchylides continues this dancer allusion in praise of Aegina, and ends it by listing some famous men who were born on the island, namely <a href="/wiki/Peleus" title="Peleus">Peleus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Telamon" title="Telamon">Telamon</a>. Bacchylides then tells of the greatness of these men’s sons, <a href="/wiki/Achilles" title="Achilles">Achilles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ajax_(mythology)" title="Ajax (mythology)">Ajax</a>, alluding to a second myth, the tale of Ajax repelling <a href="/wiki/Hector" title="Hector">Hector</a> on the beaches of Troy, keeping the Trojans from burning the Greek ships. Bacchylides relates how Achilles’ inaction spurred the Trojans to false hope, and how their swollen pride led them to be destroyed at the hands of the men they thought they had vanquished. The ode plays upon the fact that those who are listening to Bacchylides have also read the epics of Homer, and understand the whole story behind this scene that would speak poorly of Achilles if people did not know the role he played in the Trojan war. With this tale complete Bacchylides proclaims once again that the actions he has just told will be forever remembered thanks to the muses, leading once again into his praise of Pytheas and his trainer Menander, who shall be remembered for their great victories in the Pan-Hellenic games, even if an envious rival slights them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_83-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-83">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[82]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ode_15">Ode 15</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacchylides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ode 15">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Sons of <a href="/wiki/Antenor" title="Antenor">Antenor</a>, or Helen Demanded Back, is the first of Bacchylides’s dithyrambs in the text restored in 1896. The opening is incomplete, as part of the papyrus was damaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[83]</a></sup> The dithyramb treats a moment in myth before the Trojan war, when <a href="/wiki/Menelaus" title="Menelaus">Menelaus</a>, Antenor, and Antenor’s sons go to King <a href="/wiki/Priam" title="Priam">Priam</a> to demand the return of Helen. As is often the case with ancient Greek literature, Bacchylides plays of the audience’s knowledge of Homer without repeating a scene told by Homer. He instead describes a scene which is new to the audience, but which is given context by knowledge of the Iliad and <a href="/wiki/Odyssey" title="Odyssey">Odyssey</a>. The story of this embassy was known to Homer, who merely alludes to it at <i>Iliad</i> 3.205ff., but it was fully related in the cyclic epic poem <i><a href="/wiki/Cypria" title="Cypria">Cypria</a></i>, according to the <i><a href="/wiki/Chrestomathy" title="Chrestomathy">Chrestomathy</a></i> of Proclus.</p>
<p>The style also plays off of Homer. Characters are almost always named with their fathers, i.e. Odysseus, son of Laertes (as reconstructed). They are also given epithets, though these are not the traditional Homeric epithets: godly Antenor, upright Justice, reckless Outrage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[84]</a></sup></p>
