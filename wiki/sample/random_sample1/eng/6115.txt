<p>The <b>Báthory</b> family (<a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>: <span lang="pl"><i>Batory</i></span>) was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hungarian_noble" title="Hungarian noble">Hungarian noble</a> family of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gutkeled" title="Gutkeled">Gutkeled</a> clan. The family rose to significant influence in <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a> during the late <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, holding high military, administrative and ecclesiastical positions in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Kingdom of Hungary</a>. In the early modern period, the family brought forth several <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Transylvania" title="Prince of Transylvania">Princes of Transylvania</a> and one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stephen_B%C3%A1thory,_King_of_Poland" title="Stephen Báthory, King of Poland">King of Poland and Grand Duke of Lithuania</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1thory_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Báthory family belonged to the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gutkeled" title="Gutkeled">Gutkeled</a></i>, a clan of <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungarian</a> nobles, which traced its descent to the <a href="/wiki/Swabia" title="Swabia">Swabian</a> brothers <i>Gut</i> and <i>Kelad</i>, who immigrated into Hungary from the castle <i>Stof</i> (probably <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Staufen,_Germany" title="Staufen, Germany">Staufen im Breisgau</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hohenstaufen" title="Hohenstaufen">Hohenstaufen</a> in <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Württemberg">Württemberg</a>) during the reign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_Urseolo_of_Hungary" title="Peter Urseolo of Hungary">King Peter</a> (reigned 1038–1046), who himself was partly of <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venetian</a> descent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gesta_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gesta-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1279, King <a href="/wiki/Ladislaus_IV_of_Hungary" title="Ladislaus IV of Hungary">Ladislaus IV</a> rewarded Andrew's brother Hados<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and Andrew's sons George (d. 1307), Benedict (d. 1321) and <a href="/wiki/Briccius_B%C3%A1thory" title="Briccius Báthory">Briccius</a> (d. 1322) for their military services by granting them <a href="/wiki/Ny%C3%ADrb%C3%A1tor" title="Nyírbátor">Bátor</a> in the county of <a href="/wiki/Szabolcs_County" title="Szabolcs County">Szabolcs</a>. Bátor had been the estate of Vajda son of Lángos, who had married a relative of Andrew but died without issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wertner_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wertner-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1310, Bátor came into the sole possession of Briccius when he reached an agreement with his nephew Michael and his cousin Vid to divide the joint possessions. After this, Briccius and his descendants named themselves "of Bátor" or Báthory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stramberg_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stramberg-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Branches">Branches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1thory_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Branches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The family divided into two major branches, which descended from the sons and grandsons of <a href="/wiki/Briccius_B%C3%A1thory" title="Briccius Báthory">Briccius</a>:</p>
<p>The elder branch of the family, the <b>Báthory of Somlyó</b> were descended from John, Count of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Szatm%C3%A1r" title="Szatmár">Szatmár</a>, the first-born son of Briccius, through his eldest son Ladislaus (died 1373). Ladislaus, Count of <a href="/wiki/Szabolcs_County" title="Szabolcs County">Szabolcs</a>, married Anna Meggyesi and received Somlyó as dowry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Ladislaus' younger brother George II is the ancestor of the <b>Simolin</b> family, later called <b>Báthory of Simolin</b> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B%C3%A1thory#B.C3.A1thory_of_Simolin" title="Báthory">below</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StammtafelSimolin_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-StammtafelSimolin-6">[6]</a></sup> A further division occurred under the great-grandsons of Ladislaus (latter half of the 15th century): John and Stephen dropped the name Báthory and founded the <b>Szaniszlófi</b> family, while Nikolaus continued the Somlyó branch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wertner_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wertner-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The younger branch of the family, the <b>Báthory of Ecsed</b>, were descended from Luke, the youngest son of Briccius. Luke possessed wide estates in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Szatm%C3%A1r" title="Szatmár">Szatmár</a> and was granted by King <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_Hungary" title="Charles I of Hungary">Charles Robert</a> the lordship of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecsed" title="Ecsed">Ecsed</a>, where he built the castle called <i>Hűség</i> (loyalty).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wertner_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wertner-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StammtafelSimolin_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-StammtafelSimolin-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This branch, since they retained the possession of Bátor, are sometimes called <i>of Bátor</i> or, as the younger branch, <i>Nyírbátor</i> (New Bathory).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wertner_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wertner-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legend_and_coats_of_arms">Legend and coats of arms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1thory_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legend and coats of arms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A legendary account, placing the Báthorys' origin in the year 900 (preceding the advent of the Gutkeled clan), related how a god-fearing warrior called Vitus (a namesake of a member of the first generation of the Gutkeled clan) set out to fight a dragon, which loomed in the swamps next to the castle of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecsed" title="Ecsed">Ecsed</a> (actually built only in the 14th century) and harassed the countryside. Vitus killed it with three thrusts of his lance and as a reward received the castle. The grateful people honoured him with the names <i>Báthory</i>, meaning good hero, and "animus magnanimus". In Hungarian the word for <i>brave</i> is <i>bátor</i>.</p>

<p>The Báthory <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a>, granted in 1325 to the sons of Briccius, was styled in reference to this legend: three horizontally placed teeth surrounded by a dragon biting its own tail,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stramberg_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stramberg-4">[4]</a></sup> the surrounding dragon being insignia of <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Dragon" title="Order of the Dragon">Order of the Dragon</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_history">Further history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1thory_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Further history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Ecsed branch first rises to prominence with the sons of Luke's grandson John V. His eldest son Bartholomew I fell in 1432 fighting against the <a href="/wiki/Hussite_Wars" title="Hussite Wars">Hussites</a>. The second son, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_III_B%C3%A1thory" title="Stephen III Báthory">Stephen III</a> rose to become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palatine_(Kingdom_of_Hungary)" title="Palatine (Kingdom of Hungary)">Palatine of Hungary</a> and in 1444 fell in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Varna" title="Battle of Varna">Battle of Varna</a> as flag-bearer of <a href="/wiki/W%C5%82adys%C5%82aw_III_of_Poland" title="Władysław III of Poland">Władysław, King of Poland and Hungary</a>. He had also received the castle Bujak from King <a href="/wiki/Albert_II_of_Germany" title="Albert II of Germany">Albert of Habsburg</a>.</p>
<p>Of Stephen's six sons, Ladislaus V (d. 1474) was supreme count of the counties <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Szatm%C3%A1r" title="Szatmár">Szatmár</a> and Zaránd, the second Andrew III (d. 1495) was confirmed in his possession of Bujak. The third son, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_V_B%C3%A1thory" title="Stephen V Báthory">Stephen V</a> (d. 1493) excelled as a military commander and was made <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Transylvania" title="Prince of Transylvania">Voivod of Transylvania</a>, the first of a long line of Báthory rulers of that country.</p>
<p>The youngest son, Nicolaus III (d. 1506), bishop first of <a href="/wiki/Syrmia" title="Syrmia">Syrmia</a> and after 1474 of <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A1c" title="Vác">Vác</a>, excelled as a <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">renaissance</a> scholar and served as counselor to King <a href="/wiki/Matthias_Corvinus" title="Matthias Corvinus">Matthias Corvinus</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Stephen_VII_B%C3%A1thory" title="Stephen VII Báthory">Stephen VII</a> first proved himself as Count of Temesvár and in 1519 was elected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palatine_(Kingdom_of_Hungary)" title="Palatine (Kingdom of Hungary)">Palatin of Hungary</a>, as which he had to contend with the opposition of the nobility. In 1526, He fought in the disastrous <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Moh%C3%A1cs" title="Battle of Mohács">Battle of Mohács</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottomans</a>, in which King <a href="/wiki/Louis_II_of_Hungary" title="Louis II of Hungary">Louis II</a> fell.</p>
<p>After the harsh and fierce battle, Hungary was torn apart by the conflict between the rival royal claims. The two branches of the family positioned themselves on the opposing sides of the conflict. The Ecsed branch commonly sided with the Habsburgs: Stephen VII, who had escaped the battle, fled with Louis' widow to Pozsony (now <a href="/wiki/Bratislava" title="Bratislava">Bratislava</a>), where he organized the election of <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Ferdinand I, Holy Roman Emperor">Ferdinand of Austria</a> as King of Hungary. In the 1550s, when Ferdinand briefly gained control of Transylvania in 1551, he installed Stephen's nephew <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bonaventura_B%C3%A1thory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bonaventura Báthory (page does not exist)">Bonaventura</a> as his lieutenant to govern the country.</p>
<p>The Somlyó branch, on the other hand, supported <a href="/wiki/John_Z%C3%A1polya" title="John Zápolya">John Zápolya</a>, whom the greater part of the Hungarian nobility had elected King. Zápolya appointed Stephen VIII <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Transylvania" title="Prince of Transylvania">Voivode of Transylvania</a>, which he governed until his death in 1534. Later, the interest of the Zápolyas were represented at the Habsburg court by the Voivod's son <a href="/wiki/Stephen_B%C3%A1thory" title="Stephen Báthory">Stephen IX</a>, who would go on to become <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Transylvania" title="Prince of Transylvania">Prince of Transylvania</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Poland" title="King of Poland">King of Poland</a>.</p>
<p>Impressed by Stephen, George VI Báthory, of the Ecsed branch, was persuaded to change his allegiance from the Habsburgs to Zápolya, for which the Habsburg king deprived him of his castle Bujak. George strengthened his alliance to Stephen by marrying his sister Anna, uniting the branches. Anna Báthory was the widow of the last descendant of the Dragfi family and George now seized the castles of the Dragfi. Since the Dragfi estates were legally due to the crown, the Habsburgs forced George to yield the castles and withdrew to Csitsva in <a href="/wiki/Zempl%C3%A9n_County" title="Zemplén County">Zemplén County</a>.</p>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=George_B%C3%A1thory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="George Báthory (page does not exist)">George</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_B%C3%A1thory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anna Báthory (page does not exist)">Anna Báthory</a> produced the most infamous member of the family, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_B%C3%A1thory" title="Elizabeth Báthory">Elizabeth</a>, who as a widow was imprisoned for allegedly murdering large numbers of young girls. Some scholars have suggested that she served as one of <a href="/wiki/Bram_Stoker" title="Bram Stoker">Bram Stoker</a>'s influences for writing the novel <i><a href="/wiki/Dracula" title="Dracula">Dracula</a></i> but the evidence to support this is slim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<li><b><a href="/wiki/Stephen_VII_B%C3%A1thory" title="Stephen VII Báthory">Stephen VII Báthory</a></b> (died 1530), son of Andrew III, Hungarian commander, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palatine_(Kingdom_of_Hungary)" title="Palatine (Kingdom of Hungary)">Palatin of Hungary</a></li>
<li><b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_IV_B%C3%A1thory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Andrew IV Báthory (page does not exist)">Andrew IV Báthory</a></b>, son of Andrew III, Ban of Belgrad, supreme count of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Szatm%C3%A1r" title="Szatmár">Szatmár</a>, married Catherine of Rozgon, which brought him the castles of Csitsva, Cserép, Rozgon and Thora.</li>
<li><b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bonaventura_B%C3%A1thory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bonaventura Báthory (page does not exist)">Bonaventura Báthory</a></b> (also called Andrew by his soldiers) (d. 1566), first-born son of Andrew IV, supreme count of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Szatm%C3%A1r" title="Szatmár">Szatmár</a> and <a href="/wiki/Szabolcs_County" title="Szabolcs County">Szabolcs</a>, later <i>tabernicorum regalium magister</i> and royal judge. He married twice without having issue.</li>
<li><b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nicolaus_VI_B%C3%A1thory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nicolaus VI Báthory (page does not exist)">Nicolaus VI Báthory</a></b> (died 1585), son of Andrew IV, a royal judge, he is described as "a man of rare honesty, powerful in possessions and influence east of the river Theiss", he died after falling from a chariot.</li>
<li><b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=George_VI_B%C3%A1thory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="George VI Báthory (page does not exist)">George VI Báthory</a></b>, son of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_IV&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Andrew IV (page does not exist)">Andrew IV</a>, turned on the Habsburgs. Father of Elizabeth Báthory.</li>
<li><b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_XII_B%C3%A1thory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stephen XII Báthory (page does not exist)">Stephen XII Báthory</a></b> (died 1605), son of George VI, royal judge in the counties Sümegh and Szatmár and supreme count of Szabolcs, opened the doors of his castle Ecsed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stephen_Bocskay" title="Stephen Bocskay">Stephen Bocskay</a> in his rebellion against the Habsburg, dies shortly afterward.</li>
<li>Gabriel Báthory, the younger brother of Stephen XII, was the last male member of the Ecsed line. It is unclear whether he died before or after his sisters Elizabeth and Sophia.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_B%C3%A1thory" title="Elizabeth Báthory">Elizabeth Báthory</a></b> (died 1614), daughter of George VI and through her mother niece of the Polish King Stephen Báthory, infamous as the "Blood Countess", one of the first known <a href="/wiki/Serial_killer" title="Serial killer">serial killers</a>. Descendant of both the Ecsed and Somlyó branches of the Bathory family.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="B.C3.A1thory_of_Simolin">Báthory of Simolin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1thory_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Báthory of Simolin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Another branch of the family are the <b>Báthory of Simolin</b> family, which was named after their estate <i>Simony</i> (or <i>Simolin</i>). They descended from George II, the younger son of John I, through the George's grandson Michael. In the 15th century, Michael's brothers George and Ladislaus, who both died without issue, had sold their heritage to the then-head of the Somlyó branch, Nicolaus. The Simolin family possessed large estates in Prussia and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Courland" title="Duchy of Courland">Courland</a> and members served the Russian Empresses <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_of_Russia" title="Elizabeth of Russia">Elizabeth</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catherine_II_of_Russia" title="Catherine II of Russia">Catherine</a> as diplomats. In the 19th century, long after the demise of the other branches, the family claimed name and title of <i>Counts Báthory</i>, since their ancestor Michael had never consented to the sale conducted by his brothers, and in 1852 the Russian government confirmed the legitimacy of their claims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StammtafelSimolin_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-StammtafelSimolin-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
