<p><b>Bach flower remedies</b> (BFRs) are solutions of <a href="/wiki/Brandy" title="Brandy">brandy</a> and water—the water containing <a href="/wiki/Homeopathic_dilutions" title="Homeopathic dilutions">extreme dilutions</a> of <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flower</a> material developed by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Bach" title="Edward Bach">Edward Bach</a>, an English <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeopath" title="Homeopath">homeopath</a>, in the 1930s. Bach claimed that dew found on flower petals retain imagined healing properties of that plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Systematic_review" title="Systematic review">Systematic reviews</a> of <a href="/wiki/Clinical_trial" title="Clinical trial">clinical trials</a> of Bach flower solutions have found no efficacy beyond a <a href="/wiki/Placebo" title="Placebo">placebo</a> effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thaler2009_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thaler2009-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ernst2002_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ernst2002-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The solutions contain a 50:50 mix of water and brandy and are called mother tincture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Stock remedies—the solutions sold in shops—are dilutions of mother tincture into other liquid. Most often the liquid used is alcohol, so that the alcohol level by volume in most stock Bach remedies is between 25 and 40%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> (50 to 80 <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_proof" title="Alcohol proof">proof</a>). The solutions do not have a characteristic scent or taste of the plant because of dilution. The dilution process results in the statistical likelihood that little more than a single molecule may remain; it is claimed that the remedies contain <i><a href="/wiki/Energy_(esotericism)" title="Energy (esotericism)">energetic</a></i> or <i>vibrational</i> nature of the flower and that this can be transmitted to the user.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thaler2009_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thaler2009-2">[2]</a></sup> The solutions are described by some as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vibrational_medicine" title="Vibrational medicine">vibrational medicines</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> which implies they rely on the <a href="/wiki/Pseudoscience" title="Pseudoscience">pseudoscientific</a> concept of <a href="/wiki/Water_memory" title="Water memory">water memory</a>. They are often labeled as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeopathic" title="Homeopathic">homeopathic</a> because they are extremely diluted in water, but are not homeopathy as they do not follow other homeopathic ideas such as the <a href="/wiki/Homeopathy#Law_of_similars" title="Homeopathy">law of similars</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effectiveness">Effectiveness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bach_flower_remedies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Effectiveness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a 2002 database review of randomized trials <a href="/wiki/Edzard_Ernst" title="Edzard Ernst">Edzard Ernst</a> concluded:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The hypothesis that flower remedies are associated with effects beyond a placebo response is not supported by data from rigorous clinical trials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ernst2002_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ernst2002-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>All randomized double-blind studies, whether finding for or against the solutions, have suffered from small <a href="/wiki/Cohort_(statistics)" title="Cohort (statistics)">cohort</a> sizes but the studies using the best methods were the ones that found no effect over <a href="/wiki/Placebo" title="Placebo">placebo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WalachRilling_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-WalachRilling-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The most likely means of action for flower remedies is as <a href="/wiki/Placebo" title="Placebo">placebos</a>, enhanced by <a href="/wiki/Introspection" title="Introspection">introspection</a> on the patient's emotional state, or simply being listened to by the practitioner. The act of selecting and taking a remedy may act as a calming <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritual</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ernst2002_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ernst2002-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A systematic review in 2009 concluded:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Most of the available evidence regarding the efficacy and safety of BFRs has a high risk of bias. We conclude that, based on the reported adverse events in these six trials, BFRs are probably safe. Few controlled prospective trials of BFRs for psychological problems and pain exist. Our analysis of the four controlled trials of BFRs for examination anxiety and <a href="/wiki/Attention_deficit_hyperactivity_disorder" title="Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder">ADHD</a> indicates that there is no evidence of benefit compared with a placebo intervention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thaler2009_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Thaler2009-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>A newer systematic review published in 2010 by Ernst concluded</p>
<blockquote>
<p>All placebo-controlled trials failed to demonstrate efficacy. It is concluded that the most reliable clinical trials do not show any differences between flower remedies and placebos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Flower remedies are sometimes promoted as being capable of boosting the immune system, but "there is no scientific evidence to prove that flower remedies can control, cure or prevent any type of disease, including cancer".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use">Use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bach_flower_remedies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Each solution is used alone or in conjunction with other solutions, and each flower is said by advocates to impart specific qualities. The solutions are also used on <a href="/wiki/Pet" title="Pet">pets</a> and domestic animals. Remedies are usually taken orally.</p>
<p>The solutions may be recommended by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naturopath" title="Naturopath">naturopath</a> or by a trained Bach flower practitioner after an interview. Some vendors recommend <a href="/wiki/Dowsing" title="Dowsing">dowsing</a> to select a solution.</p>
<p>The best known solution product is the <i>Rescue Remedy</i> combination,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RescueRemedy_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-RescueRemedy-11">[11]</a></sup> which contains an equal amount each of <a href="/wiki/Rock_rose" title="Rock rose">rock rose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Impatiens" title="Impatiens">impatiens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clematis" title="Clematis">clematis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ornithogalum" title="Ornithogalum">star of Bethlehem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cherry_plum" title="Cherry plum">cherry plum</a> remedies. Rescue Remedy is a trademark and other companies produce the same formula under other names, such as Five Flower Remedy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <i>Rescue Cream</i> contains the same remedies in a cream form, with the addition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crab_apple" title="Crab apple">crab apple</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bach_flower_remedies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bach believed that illness was the result of a conflict between the purposes of the <a href="/wiki/Soul" title="Soul">soul</a> and the personality's actions and outlook. This internal war, according to Bach, leads to negative moods and to "energy blocking", thought to cause a lack of "harmony", thus leading to physical diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Bach_flower_remedies_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Bach_flower_remedies-14">[14]</a></sup><sup>:9–10</sup></p>
<p>Bach derived his solutions <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intuition_(knowledge)" title="Intuition (knowledge)">intuitively</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and based on his perceived <a href="/wiki/Psychic" title="Psychic">psychic</a> connections to the plants, rather than using research based on <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific methods</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vitalism_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vitalism-16">[16]</a></sup><sup>p. 185</sup> If Bach felt a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negative_emotion" title="Negative emotion">negative emotion</a>, he would hold his hand over different plants, and if one alleviated the emotion, he would ascribe the power to heal that emotional problem to that plant. He imagined that early-morning sunlight passing through dew-drops on flower petals transferred the healing power of the flower onto the water,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Xtian_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Xtian-17">[17]</a></sup> so he would collect the dew drops from the plants and preserve the dew with an equal amount of <a href="/wiki/Brandy" title="Brandy">brandy</a> to produce a <a href="/wiki/Homeopathy" title="Homeopathy">mother tincture</a> which would be further diluted before use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Later, he found that the amount of dew he could collect was not sufficient, so he would suspend flowers in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spring_(hydrosphere)" title="Spring (hydrosphere)">spring water</a> and allow the sun's rays to pass through them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Xtian_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Xtian-17">[17]</a></sup> If this was impractical because of lack of sunlight or other reasons, he wrote that the flowers may be boiled. The result of this process Bach termed the "mother tincture", which is then further diluted before sale or use.</p>
<p>Bach was satisfied with the method, because of its simplicity, and because it involved a process of combination of the <a href="/wiki/Classical_element" title="Classical element">four elements</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The earth to nurture the plant, the air from which it feeds, the sun or fire to enable it to impart its power, and water to collect and be enriched with its beneficent magnetic healing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
