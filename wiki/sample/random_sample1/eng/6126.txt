<p><b>Bahira</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">بحيرى</span>‎‎, <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="syc" xml:lang="syc">ܒܚܝܪܐ</span>‎), or "Sergius the Monk" to the Latin West, was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syriac_people" title="Syriac people">Syriac</a> or <a href="/wiki/Arabs" title="Arabs">Arab</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Arianism" title="Arianism">Arian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nestorian" title="Nestorian">Nestorian</a> or possibly <a href="/wiki/Mandaeism" title="Mandaeism">Gnostic Nasorean</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">monk</a> who, according to Islamic tradition, foretold to the adolescent <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a> his future as a <a href="/wiki/Prophet" title="Prophet">prophet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bahira_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bahira-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-watt1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-watt1-4">[4]</a></sup> His name derives from the <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a> <i>bḥīrā</i>, meaning “tested (by God) and approved”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roggema_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roggema-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Islamic_tradition">Islamic tradition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bahira&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Islamic tradition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The story of Muhammad's encounter with Bahira is found in the works of the early <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> historians <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Hisham" title="Ibn Hisham">Ibn Hisham</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibn_Sa%27d_al-Baghdadi" title="Ibn Sa'd al-Baghdadi">Ibn Sa'd al-Baghdadi</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Jarir_al-Tabari" title="Muhammad ibn Jarir al-Tabari">Muhammad ibn Jarir al-Tabari</a>, whose versions differ in some details. When Muhammad was either nine or twelve years old, he met Bahira in the town of <a href="/wiki/Bosra" title="Bosra">Bosra</a> in <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> during his travel with a <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Meccan</a> <a href="/wiki/Camel_train" title="Camel train">caravan</a>, accompanying his uncle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abu_Talib_ibn_%E2%80%98Abd_al-Muttalib" title="Abu Talib ibn ‘Abd al-Muttalib">Abu Talib ibn ‘Abd al-Muttalib</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bahira_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bahira-3">[3]</a></sup> When the caravan was passing by his cell, the monk invited the merchants to a feast. They accepted the invitation, leaving the boy to guard the camel. Bahira, however, insisted that everyone in the caravan should come to him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-watt1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-watt1-4">[4]</a></sup> Then a miraculous occurrence indicated to the monk that Muhammad was to become a prophet.</p>
<p>It was a miraculous movement of a cloud that kept shadowing Muhammad regardless of the time of the day. The monk revealed his visions of Muhammad's future to the boy's uncle (Abu Talib), warning him to preserve the child from the Jews (in Ibn Sa'd's version) or from the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantines</a> (in al-Tabari's version). Both Ibn Sa'd and al-Tabari write that Bahira found the announcement of the coming of Muhammad in the original, unadulterated <a href="/wiki/Gospel" title="Gospel">gospels</a>, which he possessed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bahira_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bahira-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Christian_tradition">Christian tradition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bahira&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Christian tradition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Criticism_of_Muhammad" title="Criticism of Muhammad">Criticism of Muhammad</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mohammed2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="1151" height="294" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Mohammed2.jpg/220px-Mohammed2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Mohammed2.jpg/330px-Mohammed2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Mohammed2.jpg/440px-Mohammed2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mohammed2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Muhammad and the Monk Sergius</i>, <a href="/wiki/Engraving" title="Engraving">engraving</a> of 1508 by <a href="/wiki/Lucas_van_Leyden" title="Lucas van Leyden">Lucas van Leyden</a> (The soldier takes Muhammad's sword. see text).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the Christian tradition Bahira became a <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heretical</a> monk, whose errant views inspired the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">Qur'an</a>. Bahira is at the center of the Apocalypse of Bahira, which exists in Syriac and Arabic which makes the case for an origin of the Qur'an from Christian apocrypha. Certain Arabist authors maintain that Bahira's works formed the basis of those parts of the Qur'an that conform to the principles of Christianity, while the rest was introduced either by subsequent compilers such as Uthman Ibn Affan or contemporary Jews and Arabs. The names and religious affiliations of the monk vary in different Christian sources. For example, <a href="/wiki/John_of_Damascus" title="John of Damascus">John of Damascus</a> (d.749), a Christian writer, states that Muhammad "having chanced upon the Old and New Testaments and likewise, it seems, having conversed with an <a href="/wiki/Arianism" title="Arianism">Arian</a> monk, devised his own heresy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>For <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apology_of_al-Kindy_(book)" title="Apology of al-Kindy (book)">Abd-al-Masih al-Kindi</a>, who calls him Sergius and writes that he later called himself <a href="/wiki/Nestorianism" title="Nestorianism">Nestorius</a>, Bahira was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasorean" title="Nasorean">Nasorean</a>, a group usually conflated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nestorian_Christian" title="Nestorian Christian">Nestorians</a>. After the 9th century, Byzantine polemicists refer to him as Baeira or Pakhyras, both being derivatives of the name Bahira, and describe him as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine_iconoclasm" title="Byzantine iconoclasm">iconoclast</a>. Sometimes Bahira is called a <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Orthodox_Church" title="Syriac Orthodox Church">Jacobite</a> or an <a href="/wiki/Arianism" title="Arianism">Arian</a>. The early Christian polemical biographies of Muhammad share in claiming that any supposed illiteracy of Muhammad did not imply that he received religious instruction solely from the angel Gabriel, and often identified Bahira as a secret, religious teacher to Muhammad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roggema_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Roggema-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a later development of the story Muhammad and Sergius get drunk. While they are sleeping in a stupor a Jew (or in some versions a soldier) takes Muhammad's sword and kills Sergius with it. When he awakes Muhammad sees his bloody sword and is convinced that he killed Sergius in a drunken rage. In shame he bans the use of alcohol among his followers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
