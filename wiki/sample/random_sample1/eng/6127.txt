<p>A <b>mortar</b> is a device that fires projectiles at low velocities and short ranges. The mortar has traditionally been used as a weapon to propel explosive <a href="/wiki/Shell_(projectile)" title="Shell (projectile)">mortar bombs</a> in high-arcing <a href="/wiki/Trajectory_of_a_projectile" title="Trajectory of a projectile">ballistic trajectories</a>. The weapon is typically <a href="/wiki/Muzzleloader" title="Muzzleloader">muzzle-loading</a> with a short, often smooth-bore barrel, generally less than 15 times its <a href="/wiki/Caliber" title="Caliber">caliber</a>. Mortar bombs are often referred to, incorrectly, as "mortars".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mortar_(weapon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Mortars have been used for hundreds of years, originally in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_warfare" title="Siege warfare">siege warfare</a>. Many historians consider the first mortars to have been used at the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Constantinople" title="Fall of Constantinople">1453 siege of Constantinople</a> by <a href="/wiki/Mehmed_the_Conqueror" title="Mehmed the Conqueror">Mehmed the Conqueror</a>. A European account of the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Belgrade_(1456)" title="Siege of Belgrade (1456)">1456 siege of Belgrade</a> by Giovanni da Tagliacozzo said that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turks" title="Ottoman Turks">Ottoman Turks</a> used seven mortars that fired "stone shots one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_mile" title="Italian mile">Italian mile</a> high".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.C3.81goston2005_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-.C3.81goston2005-2">[2]</a></sup> The time of flight of these was apparently long enough that casualties could be avoided by posting observers to give warning of their trajectories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Babinger1992_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Babinger1992-3">[3]</a></sup> However, earlier mortars were used in Korea in a 1413 naval battle when Korean gunsmiths developed the Wan'gu (gourd-shaped mortar) (완구,碗口).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The earliest version of the Wan'gu dates back to 1407.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Choe Hae-san (최해산,崔海山) (1380–1443), the son of <a href="/wiki/Choe_Museon" title="Choe Museon">Choe Mu-seon</a> (1325–1395), is generally credited with inventing the first Wan'gu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>Early mortars, such as the <a href="/wiki/Pumhart_von_Steyr" title="Pumhart von Steyr">Pumhart von Steyr</a>, were also large and heavy, and could not be easily transported. Simply made, these weapons were no more than iron bowls reminiscent of the kitchen and apothecary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mortar_(bowl)" title="Mortar (bowl)">mortars</a> from whence they drew their name. An early transportable mortar was invented by Baron <a href="/wiki/Menno_van_Coehoorn" title="Menno van Coehoorn">Menno van Coehoorn</a> (Siege of Grave, 1673<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>). This mortar fired an exploding shell, which had a fuse lit by the hot gases when fired. This innovation was quickly taken up, necessitating a new form of naval ship, the <a href="/wiki/Bomb_vessel" title="Bomb vessel">bomb vessel</a>. Mortars played a significant role in the Venetian conquest of <a href="/wiki/Morea" title="Morea">Morea</a> and in the course of this campaign an ammunition store in the Parthenon was blown up (see diagram).</p>

</div>
</div>
<p>An early use of these more mobile mortars as field (rather than siege) weapons was by British forces in the suppression of the <a href="/wiki/Jacobite_risings#The_rising_of_1719_.28.22the_Nineteen.22.29" title="Jacobite risings">1719 Jacobite rising</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Glen_Shiel" title="Battle of Glen Shiel">Battle of Glen Shiel</a>. High angle trajectory mortars held a great advantage over standard <a href="/wiki/Field_gun" title="Field gun">field guns</a> in the rough terrain of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Highlands" title="West Highlands">West Highlands</a> of Scotland.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Army" title="US Army">US Army</a> 13-inch mortar "Dictator" was a rail-mounted gun of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>.</div>

<p>Coehorn-type mortars of approximately 180 pounds (82 kg) weight were used by both sides during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>. At the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Vicksburg" title="Siege of Vicksburg">Siege of Vicksburg</a>, General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Grant" title="US Grant">US Grant</a> reported making such mortars "by taking logs of the toughest wood that could be found, boring them out for six- or twelve-pound shells and binding them with stong iron bands. These answered as <a href="/wiki/Coehorn" title="Coehorn">coehorns</a>, and shells were successfully thrown from them into the trenches of the enemy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The mortar had fallen out of general use by the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_era" title="Napoleonic era">Napoleonic era</a> and interest in the weapon was only revived at the beginning of the 20th century. During the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese War</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant-General" title="Lieutenant-General">Lieutenant-General</a> <a href="/wiki/Leonid_Gobyato" title="Leonid Gobyato">Leonid Gobyato</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Russian_Army" title="Imperial Russian Army">Imperial Russian Army</a> applied the principles of <a href="/wiki/Indirect_fire" title="Indirect fire">indirect fire</a> from closed firing positions in the field and, with the collaboration of General <a href="/wiki/Roman_Kondratenko" title="Roman Kondratenko">Roman Kondratenko</a>, he designed the first mortar that fired navy shells.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/German_Army_(German_Empire)" title="German Army (German Empire)">German Army</a> studied the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Port_Arthur" title="Siege of Port Arthur">Siege of Port Arthur</a>, where heavy artillery had been unable to destroy defensive structures like barbed wire and bunkers. The solution they developed was a short-barrelled rifled muzzle-loading mortar called the <i><a href="/wiki/Minenwerfer" title="Minenwerfer">Minenwerfer</a></i>, and was built in three sizes. Recognizing the advantages of the <i>Minenwerfer</i> in trench warfare, production was stepped up and, by 1918, the numbers had increased dramatically to 1,234 heavy, 2,361 medium and 12,329 light versions.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Eprouvette" title="Eprouvette">Eprouvette</a> was a mortar used to test the strength of gunpowder. It fell out of use by the middle of the 19th century.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_portable_mortar">Modern portable mortar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mortar_(weapon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern portable mortar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WilfredStokeswithMortar.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="456" data-file-width="300" height="304" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/WilfredStokeswithMortar.jpg/200px-WilfredStokeswithMortar.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/91/WilfredStokeswithMortar.jpg 1.5x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WilfredStokeswithMortar.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stokes_Mortar" title="Stokes Mortar">Stokes trench mortar</a> was introduced into service in 1915, as the first portable mortar.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It was not until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stokes_Mortar" title="Stokes Mortar">Stokes trench mortar</a> devised by Sir <a href="/wiki/Wilfred_Stokes" title="Wilfred Stokes">Wilfred Stokes</a> in 1915 during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a>, that the modern mortar transportable by one person was born. In the conditions of <a href="/wiki/Trench_warfare" title="Trench warfare">trench warfare</a>, there was a great need for a versatile and easily portable weapon that could be manned by troops undercover in the trenches. Stokes's design was initially rejected in June 1915 because it was unable to use existing stocks of British mortar ammunition, and it took the intervention of <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">David Lloyd George</a> (at that time <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Munitions" title="Minister of Munitions">Minister of Munitions</a>) and Lieutenant-Colonel J. C. Matheson of the Trench Warfare Supply Department (who reported to Lloyd George) to expedite manufacture of the Stokes mortar. The weapon proved to be extremely useful in the muddy trenches of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_I)" title="Western Front (World War I)">Western Front</a>, as a mortar round could be aimed to fall directly into <a href="/wiki/Trench" title="Trench">trenches</a>, where artillery shells, due to their low angle of flight, could not possibly go.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Stokes mortar was a simple weapon, consisting of a <a href="/wiki/Smoothbore" title="Smoothbore">smoothbore</a> metal tube fixed to a base plate (to absorb recoil) with a lightweight <a href="/wiki/Bipod" title="Bipod">bipod</a> mount. When a mortar bomb was dropped into the tube, an impact sensitive <a href="/wiki/Percussion_cap#Primers" title="Percussion cap">primer</a> in the base of the bomb would make contact with a firing pin at the base of the tube, and detonate, firing the bomb towards the target. It could fire as many as 25 bombs per minute and had a maximum range of 800 yards firing the original cylindrical unstabilised projectile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PortugueseLoadingStokesMortarWesternFront.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="293" data-file-width="300" height="215" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/PortugueseLoadingStokesMortarWesternFront.jpg/220px-PortugueseLoadingStokesMortarWesternFront.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5c/PortugueseLoadingStokesMortarWesternFront.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PortugueseLoadingStokesMortarWesternFront.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Expeditionary_Corps" title="Portuguese Expeditionary Corps">Portuguese Expeditionary Corps</a> soldiers loading a Stokes mortar, in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_I)" title="Western Front (World War I)">Western Front</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A modified version of the mortar, which fired a modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fin_stabiliser" title="Fin stabiliser">fin-stabilised</a> streamlined <a href="/wiki/Projectile" title="Projectile">projectile</a> and had a booster charge for longer range, was developed after World War I;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> this was in effect a new weapon. By <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, it could fire as many as 30 bombs per minute, and had a range of over 2,500 yards with some shell types.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The French developed an improved version of the Stokes mortar as the <a href="/wiki/Brandt_Mle_27/31" title="Brandt Mle 27/31">Brandt Mle 27</a>, further refined as the <a href="/wiki/Brandt_Mle_27/31" title="Brandt Mle 27/31">Brandt Mle 31</a>; this design was widely copied with and without license.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bishop2002_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bishop2002-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norris2002_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Norris2002-15">[15]</a></sup> About 700 Stokes mortars were acquired by Poland between 1923 and 1926. These weapons were the prototypes for all subsequent light mortar developments around the world.</p>
<p>Mortars today, while substantially similar in design to the Stokes mortar, are greatly improved versions; these offer a weapon that is light, adaptable, easy to operate, and yet possesses enough accuracy and firepower to provide <a href="/wiki/Infantry" title="Infantry">infantry</a> with quality close fire support against soft and hard targets more quickly than any other means.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Largest_mortars">Largest mortars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mortar_(weapon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Largest mortars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mortier_monstre_-_Li%C3%A8ge_-_Anvers_1832.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1370" data-file-width="1708" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Mortier_monstre_-_Li%C3%A8ge_-_Anvers_1832.jpg/220px-Mortier_monstre_-_Li%C3%A8ge_-_Anvers_1832.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Mortier_monstre_-_Li%C3%A8ge_-_Anvers_1832.jpg/330px-Mortier_monstre_-_Li%C3%A8ge_-_Anvers_1832.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Mortier_monstre_-_Li%C3%A8ge_-_Anvers_1832.jpg/440px-Mortier_monstre_-_Li%C3%A8ge_-_Anvers_1832.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mortier_monstre_-_Li%C3%A8ge_-_Anvers_1832.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An 1832 "Monster Mortar" invented by <a href="/wiki/Henri-Joseph_Paixhans" title="Henri-Joseph Paixhans">Henri-Joseph Paixhans</a>.</div>

<p>The largest mortars ever developed were the Belgian "<a href="/wiki/Monster_Mortar" title="Monster Mortar">Monster Mortar</a>" (24 inches; 610 mm; developed by <a href="/wiki/Henri-Joseph_Paixhans" title="Henri-Joseph Paixhans">Henri-Joseph Paixhans</a> in 1832), <a href="/wiki/Mallet%27s_Mortar" title="Mallet's Mortar">Mallet's Mortar</a> (36 inches; 910 mm; designed by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Mallet" title="Robert Mallet">Robert Mallet</a> and tested by the Woolwich Arsenal, London, in 1857) and the "<a href="/wiki/Little_David" title="Little David">Little David</a>" (36 inches; 914.4 mm; developed in the United States for use in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>).</p>
<p>Although the latter two had a <a href="/wiki/Caliber" title="Caliber">caliber</a> of 36 inches, only the "Monster Mortar" was used in combat (at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Antwerp_(1832)" title="Battle of Antwerp (1832)">Battle of Antwerp</a> in 1832).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guiness_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guiness-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The largest mortars to see active use in modern warfare were the German 60 cm (23.6 inch) <a href="/wiki/Karl-Ger%C3%A4t" title="Karl-Gerät">Karl-Gerät</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Improvised_mortars">Improvised mortars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mortar_(weapon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Improvised mortars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Improvised, or "home-made", mortars have been used by <a href="/wiki/Insurgency" title="Insurgency">insurgent</a> groups, usually to attack fortified military installations or to terrorize civilians. The <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Provisional Irish Republican Army">Provisional Irish Republican Army</a> used some of the best-known examples during the 1970s, 1980s and 1990s. The largest types came to be known as "<a href="/wiki/Barrack_buster" title="Barrack buster">barracks busters</a>" and were usually constructed from heavy steel piping mounted on a steel frame. The largest had a calibre of 320 millimetres (13 in) and fired home-made rounds carrying from 80 to 100 kilograms (180 to 220 lb) of explosive.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Barrack_buster_feb_2010.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3184" data-file-width="3584" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Barrack_buster_feb_2010.jpg/220px-Barrack_buster_feb_2010.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Barrack_buster_feb_2010.jpg/330px-Barrack_buster_feb_2010.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Barrack_buster_feb_2010.jpg/440px-Barrack_buster_feb_2010.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Barrack_buster_feb_2010.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army" title="Irish Republican Army">IRA</a> improvised mortar tube.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As each tube fired only one round, mortars were usually deployed as a battery of four or six welded onto a steel frame. This was often concealed inside a van, such as a <a href="/wiki/Ford_Transit" title="Ford Transit">Ford Transit</a>. The vehicle would be parked, pointing roughly at the target. A timer fired the propellant charges after a delay – this allowed the mortar gunner time to escape. After firing, a timer-operated incendiary device could set the vehicle on fire in order to destroy any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forensic_evidence" title="Forensic evidence">forensic evidence</a> it contained.</p>
<p>Well-known incidents using these weapons include the <a href="/wiki/1985_Newry_mortar_attack" title="1985 Newry mortar attack">1985 Newry mortar attack</a>, when nine members of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Ulster_Constabulary" title="Royal Ulster Constabulary">Royal Ulster Constabulary</a> were killed, and the <a href="/wiki/Downing_Street_mortar_attack" title="Downing Street mortar attack">Downing Street mortar attack</a> in 1991. In the latter, the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Provisional Irish Republican Army">IRA</a> launched three bombs at <a href="/wiki/10_Downing_Street" title="10 Downing Street">10 Downing Street</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">British Prime Minister</a>'s residence, during a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabinet_meeting" title="Cabinet meeting">cabinet meeting</a> there. The only bomb that detonated landed in the building's back garden, and shattered the rear windows. Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/John_Major" title="John Major">John Major</a> had to move to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Admiralty_House_(London)" title="Admiralty House (London)">Admiralty House</a> while repairs were effected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mortar_(weapon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mortier_81_LLR_03.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1664" data-file-width="2496" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Mortier_81_LLR_03.jpg/220px-Mortier_81_LLR_03.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Mortier_81_LLR_03.jpg/330px-Mortier_81_LLR_03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Mortier_81_LLR_03.jpg/440px-Mortier_81_LLR_03.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mortier_81_LLR_03.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/LLR_81mm" title="LLR 81mm">LLR 81mm</a> mortar of the <a href="/wiki/2nd_Foreign_Infantry_Regiment" title="2nd Foreign Infantry Regiment">2e REI</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The mortar is a relatively simple and easy <a href="/wiki/Weapon" title="Weapon">weapon</a> to operate. A modern mortar consists of a tube into which assistant gunners drop a purpose-designed <a href="/wiki/Bomb" title="Bomb">bomb</a>. The tube is generally set at an angle of between 45 and 85 degrees to the ground, with the higher angle giving shorter firing distances. The bomb has a small baseline charge (primary charge) and no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartridge_case" title="Cartridge case">cartridge case</a>; for extra range, propellant rings (augmentation charges) are attached to the bomb's fins. When it reaches the base of the tube, the bomb hits a fixed <a href="/wiki/Firing_pin" title="Firing pin">firing pin</a>, which detonates the baseline charge and fires the projectile. Some mortars have a moving firing pin, operated by a <a href="/wiki/Lanyard" title="Lanyard">lanyard</a>; others may be fired by a <a href="/wiki/Trigger_(firearms)" title="Trigger (firearms)">trigger</a>.</p>
<p>The augmentation charges (propellant rings) are usually easy to remove, because they have a major influence on the speed and thus the range of the bomb. Some mortar rounds can be fired even without any augmentation charges, e.g., the British <a href="/wiki/L16_81mm_mortar" title="L16 81mm mortar">81mm L16 mortar</a>.</p>

<p>These attributes contrast with the mortar's larger siblings, <a href="/wiki/Howitzer" title="Howitzer">howitzers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Field_gun" title="Field gun">field guns</a>, that fire <a href="/wiki/Shell_(projectile)" title="Shell (projectile)">shells</a> at higher <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velocities" title="Velocities">velocities</a>, longer ranges, flatter arcs, sometimes using <a href="/wiki/Direct_fire" title="Direct fire">direct fire</a>. These <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weapons" title="Weapons">weapons</a> are also <a href="/wiki/Breech-loading_weapon" title="Breech-loading weapon">breech-loaded</a>, while most mortars are muzzle-loaded.</p>
<p>From the 17th to the early 20th century, very heavy, relatively immobile <a href="/wiki/Siege" title="Siege">siege</a> mortars were used, of up to one metre <a href="/wiki/Caliber" title="Caliber">calibre</a>, often made of cast iron and with an outside barrel diameter many times that of the bore diameter. An early example was <a href="/wiki/Roaring_Meg_(cannon)" title="Roaring Meg (cannon)">Roaring Meg</a> used during The <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>. Smaller and more portable designs were introduced during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a>, primarily for <a href="/wiki/Trench_warfare" title="Trench warfare">trench warfare</a>, which took place at relatively close ranges. Mortars continue to be in used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Militaries" title="Militaries">militaries</a> to the present day.</p>

<p>Light and medium mortars are portable, and usually used by <a href="/wiki/Infantry" title="Infantry">infantry</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_unit" title="Military unit">units</a>. The chief advantage a mortar section has over an <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">artillery</a> battery is the flexibility of small numbers, mobility and the ability to engage targets in cover with plunging fire. Mortars are able to fire from the protection of a <a href="/wiki/Trench" title="Trench">trench</a> or other type of cover. In these aspects, the mortar is an excellent <a href="/wiki/Infantry" title="Infantry">infantry</a> <a href="/wiki/Weapon" title="Weapon">weapon</a>, as it can be transported over any terrain and is not burdened by the <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">logistical support</a> needed for <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">artillery</a>.</p>
<p>Heavy mortars are typically between 120- and 300-mm calibre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> These weapons are usually towed or vehicle-mounted, sometimes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breech-loaded" title="Breech-loaded">breech-loaded</a>, and normally employed by infantry units attached to <a href="/wiki/Battalion" title="Battalion">battalion</a> through <a href="/wiki/Division_(military)" title="Division (military)">division</a> level. Even at this size, mortars are simpler to operate and less expensive than comparable <a href="/wiki/Howitzer" title="Howitzer">howitzers</a> or field guns.</p>
<p>A mortar can be carried by one or more people (larger mortars can usually be broken down into components), or transported in a vehicle. An infantry mortar can usually also be mounted and fired from a <a href="/wiki/Self-propelled_artillery" title="Self-propelled artillery">mortar-carrier</a>, a purpose-built or modified <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armoured_vehicle" title="Armoured vehicle">armoured vehicle</a> with a large roof-hatch. Heavy mortars can be mounted on a towed carriage, or permanently vehicle-mounted as a self-propelled mortar. Twin-barrelled self-loading mortars—such as the Patria <a href="/wiki/AMOS" title="AMOS">AMOS</a> PT1—are the latest evolution of these heavy mortars and are mounted on platforms such as armoured personnel carriers, tank chassis, and coastal patrol boats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> A mortar can also be a launcher for <a href="/wiki/Fireworks" title="Fireworks">fireworks</a>, a hand-held<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="these are called signal pistols or rocket signals (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> or vehicle-mounted projector for smoke shells or flares, or a large <a href="/wiki/Grenade_launcher" title="Grenade launcher">grenade launcher</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="these don't work the same way and aren't used the same way as mortars (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mortar_(weapon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:L16_baseplate.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1080" data-file-width="1920" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/L16_baseplate.png/220px-L16_baseplate.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/L16_baseplate.png/330px-L16_baseplate.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/L16_baseplate.png/440px-L16_baseplate.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

Picture of the base plate and bipod attached to the barrel</div>

<p>Most modern mortar systems consist of three main components: a barrel, a base plate, and a bipod. Modern mortars normally range in <a href="/wiki/Caliber" title="Caliber">calibre</a> from 60 mm (2.36 in) to 120 mm (4.72 in). However, mortars both larger and smaller than these specifications have been produced. An example of the smaller scale is the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L9A1_51_mm_Light_Mortar" title="L9A1 51 mm Light Mortar">51 mm light mortar</a>, which is carried by an individual and consists of only a tube and a base plate. Conversely, a large example is the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/2S4_Tyulpan" title="2S4 Tyulpan">2S4 M1975 <i>Tyulpan</i></a> (<a href="/wiki/Tulip" title="Tulip">tulip</a> flower) 240 mm self-propelled mortar.</p>
<p>Smaller mortars (up to 82 mm) are commonly used and transported by infantry based mortar sections as a substitute for, or in addition to, artillery.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ammunition" title="Ammunition">Ammunition</a> for mortars generally comes in two main varieties: fin-stabilized and spin-stabilized. Examples of the former have short fins on their posterior portion, which control the path of the bomb in flight. Spin-stabilized mortar bombs rotate as they travel along and leave the mortar tube, which stabilizes them in much the same way as a rifle bullet. Both types of rounds can be either <a href="/wiki/Shell_(projectile)#Illumination" title="Shell (projectile)">illumination</a> (infra-red or visible illumination), <a href="/wiki/Shell_(projectile)#Smoke" title="Shell (projectile)">smoke</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Shell_(projectile)#High-explosive" title="Shell (projectile)">high explosive</a>. There are also training rounds and precision-guided rounds, such as the <a href="/wiki/Strix_mortar_round" title="Strix mortar round">Strix mortar round</a>.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mallet%27s_mortar.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Mallet%27s_mortar.JPG/220px-Mallet%27s_mortar.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Mallet%27s_mortar.JPG/330px-Mallet%27s_mortar.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Mallet%27s_mortar.JPG/440px-Mallet%27s_mortar.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Operators may fire spin-stabilized rounds from either a smoothbore or a rifled barrel. Rifled-mortars are more accurate, but slower to load. Since mortars are generally muzzle-loaded, mortar bombs for rifled barrels usually have a pre-engraved band, called an obturator, that engages with the rifling of the barrel. Exceptions to this were the U.S. <a href="/wiki/M2_4.2_inch_mortar" title="M2 4.2 inch mortar">M2 4.2 inch mortar</a> and <a href="/wiki/M30_mortar" title="M30 mortar">M30 mortar</a>, whose ammunition had a sub-caliber expandable ring that enlarged when fired. This allows the projectile to slide down the barrel freely, but grip the rifling when fired. The system resembles the <a href="/wiki/Mini%C3%A9_ball" title="Minié ball">Minié ball</a> for muzzle-loading rifles.</p>
<p>Mortars are made in a range of calibres. The French 81 mm mortar became standard for many countries, while the Soviet bloc standardized on the 82mm mortar.</p>
<p>Mortars suffer from instability when used on snow or soft ground, because the recoil pushes them into the ground or snow unevenly. A <a href="/wiki/Raschen_Bag" title="Raschen Bag">Raschen Bag</a> addresses this problem.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distinctive_features_of_mortars">Distinctive features of mortars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mortar_(weapon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Distinctive features of mortars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Modern mortars and their <a href="/wiki/Ammunition" title="Ammunition">ammunition</a> are generally much smaller and lighter than artillery, such as guns and howitzers, which allows light and medium (typically, 60mm and 81/82mm) mortars to be considered light weapons; i.e. capable of transport by personnel without vehicle assistance. They are short-range weapons, and often more effective than artillery for many purposes within their shorter range. In particular, due its high, <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_trajectory" title="Parabolic trajectory">parabolic trajectory</a> with a near vertical descent, the mortar can land bombs on nearby targets, including those behind obstacles or in <a href="/wiki/Fortification" title="Fortification">fortifications</a>, such as light vehicles behind hills or structures, or infantry in <a href="/wiki/Trench" title="Trench">trenches</a> or <a href="/wiki/Spider_hole" title="Spider hole">spider holes</a>. This also makes it possible to launch attacks from positions lower than the target of the attack. (E.g., long-range artillery could not shell a target 1 km away and 30 metres (100 ft) higher; a target easily accessible to a mortar.)</p>
<p>Mortars are also highly effective when used from concealed positions, such as the natural escarpments on hillsides or from woods, especially if <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forward_observer" title="Forward observer">forward observers</a> are being employed in strategic positions to direct fire; an arrangement where the mortar is in relatively close proximity both to its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forward_observer" title="Forward observer">FO</a> and its target, allowing for fire to be quickly and accurately delivered to lethal effect.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fin_stabiliser" title="Fin stabiliser">Fin-stabilised</a> mortar bombs do not have to withstand the <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">rotational forces</a> placed upon them by <a href="/wiki/Rifling" title="Rifling">rifling</a> or greater pressures, and can therefore carry a higher <a href="/wiki/Payload" title="Payload">payload</a> in a thinner skin than rifled artillery ammunition. Due to the difference in available volume, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smooth-bore" title="Smooth-bore">smooth-bore</a> mortar of a given diameter will have a greater explosive yield than a similarly sized artillery shell. For example, a 120 mm mortar bomb has about the same explosive capability as a 155 mm artillery shell. Also, fin-stabilised munitions fired from a smooth-bore, which do not rely on the spin imparted by a rifled-bore for greater accuracy, do not have the drawback of veering in the direction of the spin.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spigot_mortar">Spigot mortar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mortar_(weapon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Spigot mortar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Spigot mortars, a particular type of mortar, consist mainly of a solid rod or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spigot" title="Spigot">spigot</a>, onto which a hollow tube in the projectile fits—inverting the normal tube-mortar arrangement. At the top of the tube in the projectile, a cavity contains <a href="/wiki/Propellant" title="Propellant">propellant</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Cordite" title="Cordite">cordite</a>. There is usually a trigger mechanism built into the base of the spigot, with a long firing pin running up the length of the spigot activating a <a href="/wiki/Percussion_cap" title="Percussion cap">primer</a> inside the projectile and firing the propellant charge.</p>
<p>The advantage of a spigot mortar is that the firing unit (baseplate and spigot) is smaller and lighter than a conventional tube mortar of equivalent payload and range. It is also somewhat simpler to manufacture. Further, most spigot mortars have no barrel in the conventional sense, which means ammunition of almost any weight and diameter can be fired from the same mortar.</p>
<p>The disadvantage is that while most mortar bombs have a streamlined shape towards the back that fits a spigot mortar application well, using that space for the spigot mortar tube takes volume and mass away from the payload of the projectile. If a soldier is carrying only a few projectiles, the projectile weight disadvantage is not significant. However, the weight of a large quantity of the heavier and more complex spigot projectiles offsets the weight saved due to the spigot mortar being lighter than a conventional mortar.</p>
<p>A near-silent mortar can operate using the spigot principle. Each round has a close-fitting sliding plug in the tube that fits over the spigot. When the round is fired, the projectile is pushed off the spigot, but before the plug clears the spigot it is caught by a constriction at the base of the tube. This traps the gases from the propelling charge and hence the sound of the firing. After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> the Belgium Fly-K silent spigot mortar was accepted into French service as the TN-8111.</p>
<p>Spigot mortars are generally out of favor in modern usage, replaced by small conventional mortars. Military applications of spigot mortars include</p>
<ul>
<li>The 290 mm petard mortar used on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Churchill_AVRE" title="Churchill AVRE">Churchill AVRE</a> by Britain in World War II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/320_mm_Type_98_mortar" title="320 mm Type 98 mortar">320 mm Type 98 mortar</a> used by Japan in World War II to some psychological effect in the battles of <a href="/wiki/Iwo_Jima" title="Iwo Jima">Iwo Jima</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Okinawa" title="Okinawa">Okinawa</a></li>
<li>Anti-tank launchers
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Blacker_Bombard" title="Blacker Bombard">Blacker Bombard</a> and <a href="/wiki/PIAT" title="PIAT">PIAT</a> anti-tank launcher used by Britain in World War II utilised a spigot mortar type of launcher.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Anti-submarine launchers
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Hedgehog_(weapon)" title="Hedgehog (weapon)">Hedgehog</a> launcher, used from the deck of a ship, used 24 spigot mortars which fired a diamond pattern of anti-submarine projectiles into the sea ahead of the ship. A sinking projectile detonated if it struck a submarine, and the pattern was such that any submarine partly in the landing zone of the projectiles would be struck one or more times.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Non-military applications include the use of small-calibre spigot mortars to launch lightweight, low-velocity foam dummy targets used for training <a href="/wiki/Retriever" title="Retriever">retriever</a> dogs for bird hunting. Extremely simple launchers use a separate small primer cap as the sole propellant (similar or identical to the cartridges used in industrial <a href="/wiki/Nail_gun" title="Nail gun">nail guns</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gun-mortars">Gun-mortars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mortar_(weapon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Gun-mortars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gun-mortars are hybrid weapons, the archetype being the <a href="/wiki/Brandt_Mle_CM60A1" title="Brandt Mle CM60A1">Brandt Mle CM60A1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brandt_60_mm_LR_Gun-mortar" title="Brandt 60 mm LR Gun-mortar">Brandt 60 mm LR</a>, which combine features of modern infantry mortars together with those of modern cannon. Such weapons are smoothbore, firing fin stabilised rounds, using relatively small propellant charges in comparison to projectile weight, they have short barrels in comparison to guns and are much more lightly built than guns of a similar calibre-all characteristics of infantry mortars. However, they also have gun like features, such as vehicle mountings (or mounting on a gun carriage) together with sighting systems that allows direct fire, breech loading and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_British_ordnance_terms#Recuperator" title="List of British ordnance terms">recuperation</a>; some such as the <a href="/wiki/2B9_Vasilek" title="2B9 Vasilek">2B9 Vasilek</a> are autoloading. This produces a weapon capable of engaging area targets with indirect high angle fire, and also specific targets such as vehicles and bunkers with direct fire. Such hybrids are a lot heavier and more complicated than infantry mortars, superior to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket_propelled_grenade" title="Rocket propelled grenade">rocket propelled grenades</a> in the anti armour and bunker busting role, but have a reduced range compared to modern gun-howitzers and inferior anti-tank capability compared to modern anti tank guided weapons. However, they do have a niche in, for example, providing a multi-role anti-personnel, anti-armour capability in light mobile formations.</p>
