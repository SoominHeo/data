<p><b>Crepuscular</b> <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> are those that are active primarily during <a href="/wiki/Twilight" title="Twilight">twilight</a> (i.e., the period immediately after <a href="/wiki/Dawn" title="Dawn">dawn</a> and that immediately before <a href="/wiki/Dusk" title="Dusk">dusk</a>). It is thus to be distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Diurnality" title="Diurnality">diurnal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nocturnality" title="Nocturnality">nocturnal</a> behavior where an animal is active during the hours of daylight or the hours of darkness respectively. The term is not precise, however, as some crepuscular animals may also be active on a moonlit night or during an overcast day. The term <a href="/wiki/Matutinal" title="Matutinal">matutinal</a> is used for animals that are only active before dawn, and <a href="/wiki/Vespertine_(biology)" title="Vespertine (biology)">vespertine</a> for those only active after sunset.</p>
<p>The time of day an animal is active depends on a number of factors. <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">Predators</a> need to link their activities to times of day at which their prey is available, and prey try to avoid the times when their principal predators are at large. The temperature at midday may be too high or at night too low, so for many varied reasons, crepuscular activity may best meet their requirements by compromise. Some creatures may adjust their activities depending on local competition.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_usage">Etymology and usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crepuscular&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>crepuscular</i> derives from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>crepusculum</i> ("twilight").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Winn_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Winn-1">[1]</a></sup> Its sense accordingly differs from <a href="/wiki/Diurnality" title="Diurnality">diurnal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nocturnality" title="Nocturnality">nocturnal</a> behavior, which respectively peak during hours of daylight and darkness. The distinction is not absolute however, because crepuscular animals may also be active on a bright moonlit night or on a dull day. Some animals casually described as nocturnal are in fact crepuscular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Special classes of crepuscular behaviour include <a href="/wiki/Matutinal" title="Matutinal">matutinal</a> (or "matinal") and <a href="/wiki/Vespertine_(biology)" title="Vespertine (biology)">vespertine</a>, denoting species active only in the dawn or only in the dusk, respectively. Those that are active during both morning and evening twilight are said to have a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bimodal_activity_pattern&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bimodal activity pattern (page does not exist)">bimodal activity pattern</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptive_relevance">Adaptive relevance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crepuscular&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Adaptive relevance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GluehwuermchenImWald.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2311" data-file-width="3471" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/GluehwuermchenImWald.jpg/220px-GluehwuermchenImWald.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/GluehwuermchenImWald.jpg/330px-GluehwuermchenImWald.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/GluehwuermchenImWald.jpg/440px-GluehwuermchenImWald.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GluehwuermchenImWald.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Fireflies at twilight, long exposure</div>
</div>

<p>The various patterns of activity are thought to be mainly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antipredator_adaptation" title="Antipredator adaptation">antipredator adaptations</a>, though some could equally well be predatory <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adaptations</a>. Many predators <a href="/wiki/Foraging" title="Foraging">forage</a> most intensively at night, whereas others are active at midday and see best in full sun. Thus, the crepuscular habit may both reduce predation pressure, thereby increasing the crepuscular populations, and in consequence offer better foraging opportunities to predators that increasingly focus their attention on crepuscular prey until a new balance is struck. Such shifting states of balance are often found in ecology.</p>
<p>Some predatory species adjust their habits in response to competition from other predators. For example, the subspecies of <a href="/wiki/Short-eared_owl" title="Short-eared owl">short-eared owl</a> that lives on the <a href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos_Islands" title="Galápagos Islands">Galápagos Islands</a> is normally active during the day, but on islands like <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz_Island_(Gal%C3%A1pagos)" title="Santa Cruz Island (Galápagos)">Santa Cruz</a> that are home to the <a href="/wiki/Galapagos_hawk" title="Galapagos hawk">Galapagos hawk</a>, it is crepuscular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Apart from the relevance to predation, crepuscular activity in hot regions also may be the most effective way of avoiding heat stress while capitalizing on available light.</p>

<p>Many familiar <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammal</a> species are crepuscular, including some bats, <a href="/wiki/Hamster" title="Hamster">hamsters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">housecats</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stray_dog" title="Stray dog">stray dogs</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ferret" title="Ferret">ferrets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guinea_pig" title="Guinea pig">guinea pigs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a>. Other crepuscular mammals include <a href="/wiki/Jaguar" title="Jaguar">jaguars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ocelot" title="Ocelot">ocelots</a>, <a href="/wiki/Strepsirrhini" title="Strepsirrhini">strepsirrhines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red_panda" title="Red panda">red pandas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bear" title="Bear">bears</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moose" title="Moose">moose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinchilla" title="Chinchilla">chinchillas</a>, the common <a href="/wiki/Mouse" title="Mouse">mouse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skunk" title="Skunk">skunks</a>, Australian <a href="/wiki/Wombat" title="Wombat">wombats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wallaby" title="Wallaby">wallabies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quoll" title="Quoll">quolls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phalangeriformes" title="Phalangeriformes">possums</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sugar_glider" title="Sugar glider">marsupial gliders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spotted_hyena" title="Spotted hyena">spotted hyenas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bobcat" title="Bobcat">bobcats</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tenrecidae" title="Tenrecidae">tenrecidae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capybara" title="Capybara">capybaras</a>, <a href="/wiki/African_wild_dog" title="African wild dog">African wild dogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sitatunga" title="Sitatunga">sitatunga</a>. Crepuscular birds include the <a href="/wiki/Common_nighthawk" title="Common nighthawk">common nighthawk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Owlet-nightjar" title="Owlet-nightjar">owlet-nightjar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chimney_swift" title="Chimney swift">chimney swift</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_woodcock" title="American woodcock">American woodcock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spotted_crake" title="Spotted crake">spotted crake</a>.</p>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> are crepuscular and <a href="/wiki/Vespertine_(biology)" title="Vespertine (biology)">vespertine</a>.</p>
