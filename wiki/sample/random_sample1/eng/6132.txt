<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Park_Bum-shin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Park Bum Shin was born in <a href="/wiki/Nonsan" title="Nonsan">Nonsan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chungcheongnam-do" title="Chungcheongnam-do">Chungcheongnam-do</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-return_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-return-3">[3]</a></sup> He graduated from <a href="/wiki/Jeonju_National_University_of_Education" title="Jeonju National University of Education">Jeonju National University of Education</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wonkwang_University" title="Wonkwang University">Wonkwang University</a> and <a href="/wiki/Korea_University" title="Korea University">Korea University</a>. While working as a Korean language teacher at a middle school, he made his literary debut in 1973 with the short story <i>Remains of the Summer</i> (<i>Yeoreum ui janhae</i>), which won him the <a href="/wiki/JoongAng_Ilbo" title="JoongAng Ilbo">JoongAng Ilbo</a>'s New Year's Literary Contest. In the same year, along with the poets Kim Seung-hui and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeong_Ho-seung" title="Jeong Ho-seung">Jeong Ho-seung</a>, Park founded a literary group called the <i>73 Group</i>.</p>
<p>After 28 years of teaching in <a href="/wiki/Myongji_University" title="Myongji University">Myongji University</a>'s creative program, he retired in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rather_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-rather-5">[5]</a></sup> Upon his retirement from the academe and the release of his 39th novel <i>My Hand Turns into a Horseshoe</i>, Park moved back to his hometown, where he concentrates only on writing. He also writes his diaries, which he plans to publish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-return_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-return-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Park_Bum-shin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1979, Park began serializing his first novel <i>Lie Like a Leaf of Grass</i> (<i>Pullipcheoreom nupda</i>) in the <a href="/wiki/JoongAng_Ilbo" title="JoongAng Ilbo">JoongAng Ilbo</a>, which would become known as his signature work. For the work's sensitive, even poetic, descriptions of the losses sustained by the Korean people in the period of rapid urbanization, Park received the 1981 Korean Literature Prize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lti_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-lti-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>More serialized novels followed, which exhibited Park's lyrical but realistic style, which details the dreams and frustrations of average citizens adrift in a world of base materialism and brutal opportunism. Of special note are <i>Country of Fire</i> (<i>Bur ui nara</i>) and <i>Country of Water</i> (<i>Mul ui nara</i>), which appeared in <a href="/wiki/The_Dong-a_Ilbo" title="The Dong-a Ilbo">The Dong-a Ilbo</a> in the early and mid-1980s and won critical recognition. The stories are satirical portrayals of the upsets, ambitions and disappointments of two country boys Baek Chan-gyu and Han Gil-su, who move to Seoul as it rushes toward industrialization and urbanization. The novels reflect the author's own experience of urban life as a young man.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lti_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-lti-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1979, Park began serializing his first novel <i>Lie Like a Leaf of Grass</i> (<i>Pullipcheoreom nupda</i>) in the <a href="/wiki/JoongAng_Ilbo" title="JoongAng Ilbo">JoongAng Ilbo</a>, which would become known as his signature work. For the work's sensitive, even poetic, descriptions of the losses sustained by the Korean people in the period of rapid urbanization, Park received the 1981 Korean Literature Prize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lti_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-lti-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to his two hit novels, Park also wrote other works set against the period of Korea's economic rise, such as <i>The Forest Never Sleeps</i> (<i>Supeun jamdeulji anneunda</i>) and <i>I Listen to Mozart on Wednesdays</i> (<i>Suyoil ein Mochareuteu leul deudneunda</i>). These works describe the dreams of city dwellers frustrated by powers beyond their control, but are narrated in a style close to that of popular romantic or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detective_novel" title="Detective novel">detective novel</a>. The more popular his novels became, however, the more Park resented being characterized as an author catering to public taste. In 1993, with over 20 years of best-selling literature to his name, Park suddenly announced in a newspaper article that he was unable to continue writing his novel, then in progress. The author, whose two suicide attempts as a youth speaks to deep thirst for communication with the world, could not endure criticism and discontinued writing for three years until 1996, when he published <i>The Cart Pulled by the White Cow</i> (<i>Heuin so ga ggeuneun sure</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lti_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-lti-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Park's return to novel writing led to more highly developed artistic works, including re-workings of some of his older novels. More recently, he has written penetrating recollections of his own life as an author, as well as passionate works about nature and life based on his travels and experiences in foreign cultures and spaces. In <i>A Filthy Desk</i> (<i>Deoreoun chaeksang</i>, co-winner of the Manhae Prize for Literature in 2003<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>), <i>Empty Room</i> (<i>Bin bang</i>) and <i>Namaste</i> (<i>Namseute</i>), Park confirmed his position as a veteran author who incorporates both artistic value and popular elements into his writing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Amid the crisis of publishing in a <a href="/wiki/Post-industrial_society" title="Post-industrial society">post-industrial society</a>, in 2008 Park became the first Korean veteran novelist to post his novel on the Internet before publishing it in print. His novel <i>Cholatse</i> was serialized in top internet portal <a href="/wiki/Naver" title="Naver">Naver</a> for five months where it received 1 million hits. Through the new technology, Park said it was a great pleasure for him to learn the readers' responses to his work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The popularity of his novels led to 20 subsequent film and television adaptations, which made Park a household name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Among them, Park said he was most excited about the 2012 film <i><a href="/wiki/A_Muse" title="A Muse">A Muse</a></i>. Adapted from his provocative 2010 novel <i>Eun-gyo</i> in which a poet in his 70s who falls for a high school girl, Park said it reflected his own personal thoughts on aging, human psychology and desire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>"I’ve always gone for the second best options in my life," Park told reporters at a press meeting held in central Seoul in 2011. "It was the only way to compromise. I had too many things to take care of at once. But from now on, I want writing to be my top priority, nothing else."</p>
