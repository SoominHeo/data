<p><b>Pak Pong-ju</b> (born October 24, 1939 or April 10, 1939)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is the current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Premier_of_North_Korea" title="Premier of North Korea">Premier of North Korea</a>, serving in that office since April 2013. He served a previous term from September 3, 2003 until April 11, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pak began his career in 1962 as manager of the Yongchon food factory in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Pyong%27an_Province" title="North Pyong'an Province">North Pyong'an Province</a>. He became an alternative member of the ruling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_Workers%27_Party" title="Korean Workers' Party">Korean Workers' Party</a> (KWP) Central Committee in October 1980, and chief of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Namhung&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Namhung (page does not exist)">Namhung</a> Youth Chemical Combine Committee in July 1983. In May 1993, he became vice director of the KWP's Light Industries Department, and in March 1994, he was the vice director of the party's Economic Policy Supervisory Department. In July of that year, Pak ranked 188th out of 273 members on the funeral committee of the late leader <a href="/wiki/Kim_Il-sung" title="Kim Il-sung">Kim Il-sung</a>, indicating that he was on the periphery of the elite hierarchy. However, in September 1998, he was appointed to the chemical-industries portfolio under premier <a href="/wiki/Hong_Song-nam" title="Hong Song-nam">Hong Song-nam</a>, and replaced him five years later.</p>
<p>In 2005, in a plenary session of the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_People%27s_Assembly" title="Supreme People's Assembly">Supreme People's Assembly</a> Pak spoke regarding the reintroduction of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Public_distribution_system_(North_Korea)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Public distribution system (North Korea) (page does not exist)">Public distribution system</a>. Pak proposed an administrative solution to food distribution and labeled it the party’s position: "By all means, we must reach this year’s grain production targets by thoroughly implementing the party’s policy of agricultural revolution by fully concentrating and mobilizing the entire country’s efforts into the agricultural front".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 11, 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Central_News_Agency" title="Korean Central News Agency">Korean Central News Agency</a> reported that during the 5th session of the 11th Supreme People's Assembly of the DPRK, Pak Pong-ju was "relieved … of premiership" and <a href="/wiki/Kim_Yong-il" title="Kim Yong-il">Kim Yong-il</a> elected the new premier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He had not been seen in public since May 2006. It is rumoured that he was removed from office because he misused oil funds to be used for the farming sector,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> or that he was too heavily focused on economic development suggestions from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>, instead of home-grown ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>As Premier, Pak Pong-ju is the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_government" title="Head of government">head of government</a> in the DPRK, and formed the top executive leadership of the DPRK with other executive officials. The other branch of the executive government is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Defense_Commission_of_North_Korea" title="National Defense Commission of North Korea">National Defense Commission of North Korea</a>, led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_National_Defence_Commission_of_North_Korea" title="Chairman of the National Defence Commission of North Korea">Chairman of the National Defence Commission</a> <a href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-un" title="Kim Jong-un">Kim Jong-un</a>. As premier, he is responsible for organizing the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_North_Korea" title="Cabinet of North Korea">cabinet</a> and appoints ministers and vice-premiers upon confirmation by the Supreme People's Assembly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Prior to becoming Premier, Pak had served as Chemical Industry Minister. He serves as part of a committee heading the executive branch of the North Korean government, along with Kim Jong-un and SPA Presidium chairman <a href="/wiki/Kim_Yong-nam" title="Kim Yong-nam">Kim Yong-nam</a>. Each man nominally holds one-third of the powers held by a president in most presidential systems. Pak handles domestic affairs, Kim Yong-nam conducts foreign relations and Kim Jong-un commands the armed forces. On 23 August 2010, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a> reported that Pak Pong-ju "resurfaced at a state function in the capital, Pyongyang, on Saturday, carrying the title of first deputy director of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Committee_of_the_Workers%27_Party_of_Korea" title="Central Committee of the Workers' Party of Korea">Central Committee</a> of the ruling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Workers%E2%80%99_Party_of_Korea" title="Workers’ Party of Korea">Workers’ Party of Korea</a>, according to the North’s state-run <a href="/wiki/Korean_Central_Television" title="Korean Central Television">Korean Central Television</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He effectively replaced Kim Jong-il's sister <a href="/wiki/Kim_Kyong-hui" title="Kim Kyong-hui">Kim Kyong-hui</a> as director of the Party Light Industry Department in 2012 (he was its vice-director from 1992–1998 and 2010–2012).</p>
<p>He was reputed close to <a href="/wiki/Jang_Sung-taek" title="Jang Sung-taek">Jang Sung-taek</a> and part of the current shifting of the government's attention to the consumer economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> On March 31, 2013 he was elected <a href="/wiki/Politburo_of_the_Workers%27_Party_of_Korea" title="Politburo of the Workers' Party of Korea">Politburo</a> member and replaced by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Paek_Kye-ryong&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Paek Kye-ryong (page does not exist)">Paek Kye-ryong</a> as department director. On April 1, he replaced <a href="/wiki/Choe_Yong-rim" title="Choe Yong-rim">Choe Yong-rim</a> for a second term as Premier. On April 22, he chaired the first full session of the cabinet which included a discussion of the "byungjin line" of co-developing the economy and nuclear weapons, as well as budgetary issues for the People’s Economy in the first and second quarters of 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In July, it was announced that Pak's cabinet had taken full authority over economic measures by calling to "unconditionally executing the cabinet’s decisions and instructions".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
