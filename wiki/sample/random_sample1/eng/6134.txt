<p><b>Park Sang-myun</b> (born January 27, 1968) is a South Korean actor. He is best known for his comic roles, notably in <i><a href="/wiki/My_Wife_Is_a_Gangster" title="My Wife Is a Gangster">My Wife Is a Gangster</a></i> (2001).</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Park_Sang-myun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Park Sang-myun graduated in 1987 with a Theater degree from <a href="/wiki/Seoul_Institute_of_the_Arts" title="Seoul Institute of the Arts">Seoul Institute of the Arts</a>. He made his acting debut in 1993 in a Korean staging of the <a href="/wiki/Musical_theatre" title="Musical theatre">musical</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Guys_and_Dolls" title="Guys and Dolls">Guys and Dolls</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retro_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-retro-1">[1]</a></sup> Park first broke into the film industry with minor roles such as "Ashtray" in the hit 1997 comedy <i><a href="/wiki/No._3" title="No. 3">No. 3</a></i>, but his strong acting talent soon captured the attention of audiences and filmmakers. His first major success came in 2000 via the wrestling comedy <i><a href="/wiki/The_Foul_King" title="The Foul King">The Foul King</a></i>, followed by a memorable role in firefighting drama <i><a href="/wiki/Libera_Me_(2000_film)" title="Libera Me (2000 film)">Libera Me</a></i>. Park's TV <a href="/wiki/Sitcom" title="Sitcom">sitcom</a> <i>Three Friends</i> further cemented his popularity as a <a href="/wiki/Character_actor" title="Character actor">character actor</a>, and he became a common sight on TV programs and advertisements as well as on film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kfilmorg_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-kfilmorg-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late 2001, Park scored his biggest hit with the comedy <i><a href="/wiki/My_Wife_Is_a_Gangster" title="My Wife Is a Gangster">My Wife Is a Gangster</a></i>, which attracted over 5 million viewers nationwide. As the "<a href="/wiki/Straight_man_(stock_character)" title="Straight man (stock character)">straight man</a>," he played a mild-mannered government clerk who doesn't realize that his wife is a fearsome gang boss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Hi!_Dharma!" title="Hi! Dharma!">Hi! Dharma!</a></i>, released a couple months later, also became a runaway hit with audiences for its comic showdown between <a href="/wiki/Kkangpae" title="Kkangpae">gangsters</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> monks.</p>
<p>The year 2002 was less kind, however, with comedies <i>Can't Live Without Robbery</i> and <i>Baby Alone</i> both bombing at the box-office, effectively ending Park's career as a leading actor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kfilmorg_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-kfilmorg-3">[3]</a></sup> Since then, he returned to <a href="/wiki/Supporting_actor" title="Supporting actor">supporting roles</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Korean_drama" title="Korean drama">television dramas</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Seoul_1945" title="Seoul 1945">Seoul 1945</a></i> (2006) and <i><a href="/wiki/King_of_Baking,_Kim_Takgu" title="King of Baking, Kim Takgu">King of Baking, Kim Takgu</a></i> (2010).</p>
<p>Aside from his prolific film and TV career, Park also appears in small-scale stage plays and musicals, notably <i>How Are You, Sister?</i> about a soldier and a nun who meet during the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Kim_Sang-jin_(film_director)" title="Kim Sang-jin (film director)">Kim Sang-jin</a>'s debut as a <a href="/wiki/Theatre_director" title="Theatre director">theatre director</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and <i>Really Really Like You</i>, a 1970s-set nostalgic romance between an English teacher and a high school baseball coach (adapted from the same-titled 1977 film).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retro_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-retro-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
