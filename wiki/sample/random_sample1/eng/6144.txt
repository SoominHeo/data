<p><b>Jan Janse de Weltevree</b> (1595 – not known) was a Dutch sailor and probably the first Dutchman to visit <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>. His adventures were recorded in the report by <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_India_Company" title="Dutch East India Company">Dutch East India Company</a> accountant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hendrik_Hamel" title="Hendrik Hamel">Hendrik Hamel</a>. Hamel stayed in Korea from 1653 to 1666.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jan_Jansz._Weltevree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jan Janse de Weltevree was born around 1595, according to Hendrik Hamel in <a href="/wiki/De_Rijp" title="De Rijp">De Rijp</a>, though other sources speak of <a href="/wiki/Vlaardingen" title="Vlaardingen">Vlaardingen</a>. He signed on the ship 'Hollandia' and went on March 17, 1626 to <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies" title="Dutch East Indies">Dutch East Indies</a>. He arrived in 1627 from <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a> on the ship 'Ouwerkerck'. On July 16, 1627, the 'Ouwerkerck' with its captain Jan Janse de Weltevree captured a Chinese <a href="/wiki/Junk_(ship)" title="Junk (ship)">junk</a> and its 150-man crew bound for the port of Amoy, China. Seventy Chinese were brought aboard the Ouwerkerck. Jan Janse de Weltevree, Dirk Gijsbertsz from De Rijp, and Jan Pieterse Verbaest from Amsterdam, all from Holland, along with thirteen other Dutch crewmen went aboard the junk to sail the vessel to <a href="/wiki/Tainan" title="Tainan">Tainan</a>, Formosa. The Ouwerkerck reached safe harbor after battling a fierce summer storm that swept the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The storm-tossed Chinese junk carrying the hapless Dutch and Chinese ended up on the shores of an island off Korea's west coast, during the reign of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a>. Although the details of what happened next are unclear, the Chinese, with a five-to-one advantage, overpowered the Dutch survivors, captured Jan Janse de Weltevree, Dirk Gijsbertsz and Jan Verbaest, and handed them over to the Korean Joseon authorities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Joseon Dynasty of that time enforced an isolation policy so the captured privateers could not leave the country. Jan Janse de Weltevree took the name <b>Pak Yǒn</b> (박연, <b>Pak</b> is a Korean surname.) and became an important government official. He married a Korean woman with whom he had two children.</p>
<p>According to Jan Janse de Weltevree, the two other captives from the Ouwerkerck were killed in 1636 during a <a href="/wiki/Second_Manchu_invasion_of_Korea" title="Second Manchu invasion of Korea">raid of the Manchu</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> They would have fought in the Korean army.</p>
<p>In 1653 the ship 'De Sperwer' was wrecked en route from Jakarta to <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Hendrick_Hamel" title="Hendrick Hamel">Hendrick Hamel</a> on board, and Jan Janse de Weltevree acted as a translator and adviser. This group of 36 Dutchmen stayed in Korea for 13 years, working as military advisors to the Joseon Army, until 8 of them escaped to Nagasaki in 1666. Hendrick Hamel authored the accounts of his stay in Korea, from which we hear about Jan Janse de Weltevree.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jan_Jansz._Weltevree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Besides the Great Church in De Rijp is a statue of Jan Jansz. A replica of this was erected in 1991 in <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>.</p>
