<p><b>Park Jin-hee</b> (born January 8, 1978) is a South Korean actress. She is best known for her leading roles in the television series <i><a href="/wiki/Please_Come_Back,_Soon-ae" title="Please Come Back, Soon-ae">Please Come Back, Soon-ae</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/War_of_Money" title="War of Money">War of Money</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Giant_(TV_series)" title="Giant (TV series)">Giant</a></i>, as well as for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Shadows_in_the_Palace" title="Shadows in the Palace">Shadows in the Palace</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Park_Jin-hee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Park made her acting debut in 1996 teen drama <i>Start</i>, and rose to stardom in the 1998 <a href="/wiki/Horror_film" title="Horror film">horror film</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Whispering_Corridors" title="Whispering Corridors">Whispering Corridors</a></i>. She was praised for her emotional performance as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single_mother" title="Single mother">single mother</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Broadcasting_System" title="Korean Broadcasting System">KBS</a> television series <i>Stock Flower</i> in 2001, but her other <a href="/wiki/Korean_drama" title="Korean drama">TV dramas</a> were deemed forgettable. She starred in a string of commercial fare on the big screen, namely, <i>Promenade</i> with <a href="/wiki/Kim_Sang-joong" title="Kim Sang-joong">Kim Sang-joong</a>, <i>Just Do It!</i> with Park Sang-myun, <i>Star</i> with <a href="/wiki/Yu_Oh-seong" title="Yu Oh-seong">Yoo Oh-sung</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Love_in_Magic" title="Love in Magic">Love in Magic</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Yeon_Jung-hoon" title="Yeon Jung-hoon">Yeon Jung-hoon</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Independent_film" title="Independent film">indie</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Love_Talk" title="Love Talk">Love Talk</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Bae_Jong-ok" title="Bae Jong-ok">Bae Jong-ok</a> and <a href="/wiki/Park_Hee-soon" title="Park Hee-soon">Park Hee-soon</a> in 2005,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> but none of her films achieved critical or box office success. Park then stopped working for a year and a half, deciding to take some time off for a little introspection. The hiatus paid off in 2006 when she returned to the small screen in <i><a href="/wiki/Please_Come_Back,_Soon-ae" title="Please Come Back, Soon-ae">Please Come Back, Soon-ae</a></i>, playing a 40-something <a href="/wiki/Ajumma" title="Ajumma">ajumma</a> trapped in the body of a sexy young 20-something (with the reverse played by <a href="/wiki/Shim_Hye-jin" title="Shim Hye-jin">Shim Hye-jin</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Body_swap" title="Body swap">body swap</a> comedy was a hit, with average ratings of 25%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paycheck_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-paycheck-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Her popularity continued in 2007 when her drama with <a href="/wiki/Park_Shin-yang" title="Park Shin-yang">Park Shin-yang</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/War_of_Money" title="War of Money">War of Money</a></i> reached ratings of over 30%. As a righteous woman facing a slew of financial hardships,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paycheck_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-paycheck-4">[4]</a></sup> Park's acting was recognized at the <a href="/wiki/Seoul_Broadcasting_System" title="Seoul Broadcasting System">SBS</a> Drama Awards. She then drew laughs as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narcissistic" title="Narcissistic">narcissistic</a> character in the film <i>Underground Rendezvous</i>, a 1980s-set comedy about the <a href="/wiki/Division_of_Korea" title="Division of Korea">North-South divide</a> co-starring <a href="/wiki/Im_Chang-jung" title="Im Chang-jung">Im Chang-jung</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> But her next film would be even more high-profile: <i><a href="/wiki/Shadows_in_the_Palace" title="Shadows in the Palace">Shadows in the Palace</a></i>, a mystery thriller set during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Jeongjo_of_Joseon" title="Jeongjo of Joseon">King Jeongjo</a> (1752-1800). Playing a court medic who investigates a murder within the ranks of the royal harem and household (the film's Korean title translates to <a href="/wiki/Gungnyeo" title="Gungnyeo">gungnyeo</a>, or "palace women"), Park received critical acclaim for her performance, with one review calling it "the peak of her 10-year acting career."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> She won Best Actress at the Golden Cinematography Awards and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fantasia_Festival" title="Fantasia Festival">Fantasia Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the 2008 film <i>Sweet Lie</i> (also known as <i>Lost and Found</i>) in which her character pretends to have <a href="/wiki/Amnesia" title="Amnesia">amnesia</a> to snag her dream guy, Park continued to explore the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romantic_comedy" title="Romantic comedy">romantic comedy</a> genre in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Woman_Who_Still_Wants_to_Marry" title="The Woman Who Still Wants to Marry">The Woman Who Still Wants to Marry</a></i> (also known as <i>Still, Marry Me</i>). In the low-rated 2010 series, she played a career woman in her 30s who falls for a musician ten years her junior (played by <a href="/wiki/Kim_Bum" title="Kim Bum">Kim Bum</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Park then joined the epic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Period_drama" title="Period drama">period drama</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Giant_(TV_series)" title="Giant (TV series)">Giant</a></i>, set during the economic boom of 1970-80s Korea, and co-starring <a href="/wiki/Lee_Beom-soo" title="Lee Beom-soo">Lee Beom-soo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Park_Sang-min_(actor)" title="Park Sang-min (actor)">Park Sang-min</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The series was a hit with TV critics and audiences, with a ratings peak of 40%. And Park's portrayal of a vengeance-driven, ambitious woman who finds success as a <a href="/wiki/Loan_shark" title="Loan shark">loan shark</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> was again lauded at the <a href="/wiki/2010_SBS_Drama_Awards" title="2010 SBS Drama Awards">SBS Drama Awards</a>.</p>
<p>She and <a href="/wiki/Kim_Hae-sook" title="Kim Hae-sook">Kim Hae-sook</a> played mother and daughter in the melodrama <i>A Long Visit</i> (also known as <i>My Mom</i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> followed by Park's <a href="/wiki/Cameo_appearance" title="Cameo appearance">cameo appearance</a> in <a href="/wiki/War_film" title="War film">war film</a> <i><a href="/wiki/71:_Into_the_Fire" title="71: Into the Fire">71: Into the Fire</a></i>.</p>
<p>Park reunited with <i>Stock Flower</i> director Park Chan-hong and writer Kim Ji-woo in her next television series <i><a href="/wiki/Fermentation_Family" title="Fermentation Family">Fermentation Family</a></i>, which aired on cable channel <a href="/wiki/JTBC" title="JTBC">jTBC</a> in 2011. She played a woman who takes over her father's <a href="/wiki/Kimchi" title="Kimchi">kimchi</a> restaurant and rediscovers her love for cooking and family, as she fights with and falls for a gangster played by <a href="/wiki/Song_Il-gook" title="Song Il-gook">Song Il-gook</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, she and <a href="/wiki/John_Park_(musician)" title="John Park (musician)">John Park</a> appeared in <i>Music and Lyrics</i>, an <a href="/wiki/MBC_Music" title="MBC Music">MBC Music</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reality_show" title="Reality show">reality show</a> in which an actress and a male singer are paired together as lyricist and composer, respectively, to create a song.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The song they composed, <i>Maybe, Maybe</i>, was recorded by <a href="/wiki/Alex_Chu" title="Alex Chu">Alex Chu</a> and Horan of <a href="/wiki/Clazziquai" title="Clazziquai">Clazziquai</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Later that year, her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthouse_film" title="Arthouse film">arthouse film</a> with <a href="/wiki/Park_Ji-yoon" title="Park Ji-yoon">Park Ji-yoon</a> was released. Part-mystery, part-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coming-of-age_film" title="Coming-of-age film">coming-of-age film</a>, <i>Grape Candy</i> explored the nostalgia and tension at the reunion of two old high school friends who share a painful past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Park joined the cast of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hur_Jun,_the_Original_Story" title="Hur Jun, the Original Story">Hur Jun, the Original Story</a></i> (in Korean, <i>Gu-am Heo Jun</i>), about the <a href="/wiki/Joseon" title="Joseon">Joseon</a>-era royal physician <a href="/wiki/Heo_Jun" title="Heo Jun">Heo Jun</a> (played by <a href="/wiki/Kim_Joo-hyuk" title="Kim Joo-hyuk">Kim Joo-hyuk</a>). The historical drama aired daily on <a href="/wiki/Munhwa_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Munhwa Broadcasting Corporation">MBC</a> in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_activities">Other activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Park_Jin-hee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Other activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2008, Park appeared in a Coreana cosmetics commercial dressed in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> uniform. Amid protests from Jewish and international groups, the company apologized for their ill-conceived concept and pulled out the commercial after two days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Park was one of the 40,000 volunteers who helped clean up after the <a href="/wiki/2007_South_Korea_oil_spill" title="2007 South Korea oil spill">2007 MT Hebei Spirit oil spill</a> in <a href="/wiki/Taean_County" title="Taean County">Taean County</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Chungcheong_Province" title="South Chungcheong Province">South Chungcheong Province</a>, the worst oil spill in Korean history. She came as part of the volunteer group Information Network Village (of which she was spokesperson), which aims to reduce the digital gap between rural and urban areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>To promote the 2009 Green Film Festival in Seoul, she appeared in the <a href="/wiki/Short_film" title="Short film">short film</a> <i>Take Action, Now or Never!</i>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Kim_Tae-yong" title="Kim Tae-yong">Kim Tae-yong</a>. In the short, Park advocates riding bicycles to reduce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas emissions">greenhouse gas emissions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> She was the 2012 honorary ambassador for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NGO" title="NGO">NGO</a> Korea Green Foundation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>For her 2010 <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master's degree">master's degree</a> <a href="/wiki/Thesis" title="Thesis">thesis</a> for <a href="/wiki/Yonsei_University" title="Yonsei University">Yonsei University</a>, "Studies on Depression and Suicidal Urges Among Actors," Park interviewed 260 actors with incomes ranging from ₩10 million-per-episode to less than ₩1 million a month. According to her findings, 40 percent were suffering from <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a>, and 20 percent have actually purchased pills or toxic agents and "devices" for <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a>. Park said in the paper that the extreme stress to stay young and attractive and to remain in the limelight, or the job instability for lesser-known actors, drives them to mental anguish; and despite the perception of glamor and public adoration, actors are "some of the most lonesome and troubled."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Park_Jin-hee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Park married her boyfriend, a lawyer, on May 11, 2014 at the <a href="/wiki/Hotel_Shilla" title="Hotel Shilla">Hotel Shilla</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> She gave birth to her first child in December 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
