<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Spur</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>A <b>spur</b> is a metal tool designed to be worn in pairs on the heels of <a href="/wiki/Riding_boot" title="Riding boot">riding boots</a> for the purpose of directing a <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horse</a> to move forward or laterally while <a href="/wiki/Equestrianism" title="Equestrianism">riding</a>. It is usually used to refine the <a href="/wiki/Riding_aids" title="Riding aids">riding aids</a> (commands) and to back up the natural aids (the leg, seat, hands and voice). The spur is used in many equestrian disciplines. There are rules in most equestrian organizations about spur design, use and penalties for using spurs in any manner that constitutes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_abuse" title="Animal abuse">animal abuse</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This very old word derives from Anglo-Saxon <i>spura, spora</i>, related to <i>spornan, spurnan</i>, to kick, <i>spurn</i>; cf. Medieval High German <i>Sporn</i>, modern German <i>Sporn</i>, Dutch <i>spoor</i>, Frisian <i>spoar</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The generalized sense of "anything that urges on, stimulus" is recorded in English from circa 1390.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SpurDiagram.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="130" data-file-width="350" height="82" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/de/SpurDiagram.svg/220px-SpurDiagram.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/de/SpurDiagram.svg/330px-SpurDiagram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/de/SpurDiagram.svg/440px-SpurDiagram.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SpurDiagram.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Parts of a simple spur</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The parts of a spur include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The "yoke", "branch", or "heel band", which wraps around the heel of the boot.</li>
<li>The "shank" or "neck", which extends from the back of the yoke and is the area that touches the horse.</li>
<li>The rowel, seen on some spurs, a revolving <a href="/wiki/Wheel" title="Wheel">wheel</a> or disk with radiating "points" at the end attached to the shank.</li>
</ul>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Spur.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="384" data-file-width="512" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Spur.png/220px-Spur.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Spur.png/330px-Spur.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Spur.png/440px-Spur.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Spur.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Spur straps on an English "Prince of Wales" spur</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Spurs are usually held on by a leather or leather-like strap, called a spur strap, that goes over the arch of the foot and under the sole in front of the boot heel. Some western designs have a leather strap that goes only over the top, with a heel chain or a rubber "tiedown" instead of a strap under the boot. There are also styles with no straps where the heel band is simply very tight and slips on wedged between the sole and heel of the boot. Some spur designs have a slot for running the spur strap through, others have "buttons," sometimes on the heel band itself and sometimes attached to the heel band by hinges, that allow a strap with buttonholes to be attached.</p>
<p>When used in military ranks, senior officers, and officers of all ranks in <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">cavalry</a> and other formerly mounted units of some armies, wear a form of spur in certain orders of dress which is known as the box spur, having no spur strap but a long metal prong opposite the neck, extending between the arms of the heel band, which is inserted into a specially fitted recess or "box" in the base of the boot heel. Due to the prong, such spurs can only be worn with appropriately equipped boots. This construction is shown in the illustrations of the swan neck and Waterford spurs below.</p>
<p>Spurs seen in <a href="/wiki/Western_riding" title="Western riding">western riding</a> may also have small curved-up hooks on the shank in front of the rowel, called "chap guards," that were originally used to prevent the rider's <a href="/wiki/Chaps" title="Chaps">chaps</a> from interfering with the rowels of the spur. The shank angle from the yoke can vary from "full". to "one half", to "one quarter" to "straight". Some <a href="/wiki/Cowboy" title="Cowboy">cowboys</a> also added small metal <i>Pajados,</i> also known as <i>Jingo Bobs</i> or <i>Jingle Bobs,</i> near the rowel, to create a jingling sound whenever the foot moved. Rowels can vary in size and number of points.</p>
<p>In the history of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veterinary_science" title="Veterinary science">veterinary science</a>, the word "rowel" described a small disk of <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a> or other material that was used as a <a href="/wiki/Seton_stitch" title="Seton stitch">seton stitch</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Spur_rowel_detail.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="542" data-file-width="605" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Spur_rowel_detail.jpg/220px-Spur_rowel_detail.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Spur_rowel_detail.jpg/330px-Spur_rowel_detail.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Spur_rowel_detail.jpg/440px-Spur_rowel_detail.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Spur_rowel_detail.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Western spur rowel with jingo bobs</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The spur was used by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celtic_people" title="Celtic people">Celts</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_T%C3%A8ne" title="La T√®ne">La T√®ne</a> period (which began in the 5th century BC), and is also mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Xenophon" title="Xenophon">Xenophon</a> (c. 430 - 354 BC.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Iron or bronze spurs were also used throughout the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The spur also existed in the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age" title="Islamic Golden Age">medieval Arab world</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Early spurs had a neck that ended in a point, called a prick, riveted to the heel band. Prick spurs had straight necks in the 11th century and bent ones in the 12th. The earliest form of the horseman's spur armed the heel with a single prick. In England, the rowel spur is shown upon the first seal of <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_England" title="Henry III of England">Henry III</a> and on <a href="/wiki/Monument" title="Monument">monuments</a> of the 13th century, but it does not come into general use until the 14th century. The earliest rowels probably did not revolve but were fixed.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Medieval_spurs.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="307" data-file-width="646" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Medieval_spurs.JPG/280px-Medieval_spurs.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Medieval_spurs.JPG/420px-Medieval_spurs.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Medieval_spurs.JPG/560px-Medieval_spurs.JPG 2x" width="280"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Medieval_spurs.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An iron prick-spur (13th‚Äì14th century) and an iron spur with eight-pointed rowel (15th century)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The spurs of medieval <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knights</a> were <a href="/wiki/Gilding" title="Gilding">gilt</a> and those of <a href="/wiki/Squire" title="Squire">squires</a> were silvered<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. "To win his spurs" meant to gain knighthood, as gilded spurs were reckoned the badge of knighthood<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. In the rare cases of <a href="/wiki/Cashiering" title="Cashiering">ceremonious degradation</a>, the spurs were hacked from the disgraced knight's heels with the cook's chopper<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. After the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Golden_Spurs" title="Battle of the Golden Spurs">battle of the Golden Spurs</a> in 1302, where the French chivalry suffered a humbling defeat, the victors hung up bushels of knights' gilt spurs in the churches of <a href="/wiki/Kortrijk" title="Kortrijk">Kortrijk</a> as trophies of what is still remembered by the Flemings as the <i>Guldensporenslag</i> (the battle of the golden spurs). The English named the French <a href="/wiki/Rout" title="Rout">rout</a> from <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9rouanne" title="Th√©rouanne">Th√©rouanne</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Spurs" title="Battle of the Spurs">Battle of the Spurs</a>, due to the rapidity of the French cavalry's flight.</p>
<p>Prick spurs were the standard form until the 14th century, when the rowel began to become more common. The prick design never died out entirely, but instead became a thicker, shorter neck with a dulled end, such as the modern "Prince of Wales" design commonly seen in <a href="/wiki/English_riding" title="English riding">English riding</a>.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Victorias_st%C3%B6vel_med_sporre_-_Livrustkammaren_-_6630.tif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3328" data-file-width="4992" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Victorias_st%C3%B6vel_med_sporre_-_Livrustkammaren_-_6630.tif/lossy-page1-250px-Victorias_st%C3%B6vel_med_sporre_-_Livrustkammaren_-_6630.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Victorias_st%C3%B6vel_med_sporre_-_Livrustkammaren_-_6630.tif/lossy-page1-375px-Victorias_st%C3%B6vel_med_sporre_-_Livrustkammaren_-_6630.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Victorias_st%C3%B6vel_med_sporre_-_Livrustkammaren_-_6630.tif/lossy-page1-500px-Victorias_st%C3%B6vel_med_sporre_-_Livrustkammaren_-_6630.tif.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

Boot with spur, 19th century</div>

<p>Though often decorated throughout history, in the 15th century, spurs became an art form in both decoration and design, with elaborate engraving, very long shanks and large rowels. Though sometimes it has been claimed that the design changes were used because of <a href="/wiki/Barding" title="Barding">barding</a>, the use of barding had fallen out of fashion by the time the most elaborate spur designs were created. More likely, the elaborate designs reflected the increased abundance of precious metals, particularly <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>, that followed the European exploration of the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> that began in 1492. Spur designs in Spain and colonial <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> were particularly elaborate. For example, the spurs of the Spanish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conquistadors" title="Conquistadors">Conquistadors</a> were sometimes called <i>Espuela Grande,</i> the "Grand Spur," and could have rowels as large as six inches around.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In northern Europe, the spur became less elaborate after the 16th century, particularly following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stuart_Restoration" title="Stuart Restoration">Stuart Restoration</a>, but elaborate spur designs persisted, particularly in the Americas, descendants of which are still seen today, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> and the western <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, where the spur has become an integral part of the <a href="/wiki/Vaquero" title="Vaquero">vaquero</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cowboy" title="Cowboy">cowboy</a> traditions. The spur as an art form as well as a tool is still seen in <a href="/wiki/Western_riding" title="Western riding">western riding</a>, where spurs with engraving and other artistic elements, often handmade and utilizing <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> or other precious metals are still worn.</p>
<p>Collecting of particularly beautiful <a href="/wiki/Antique" title="Antique">antique</a> spurs is a popular pastime for some individuals, particularly aficionados of western history and cowboy culture.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spurs_as_modern_honours">Spurs as modern honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spurs as modern honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Just as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knight</a> was said to have "earned his spurs," the awarding of spurs has continued in the modern era as an honour bestowed upon individuals in organizations with military heritages, and among motorcycle riders. Members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papal_Orders_of_Knighthood" title="Papal Orders of Knighthood">Papal Orders of Knighthood</a> receive gilt spurs directly from the hands of the <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a>; members of the British <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Garter" title="Order of the Garter">Order of the Garter</a> similarly receive gilt spurs from the Monarch. Inductees into the American <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Spur" title="Order of the Spur">Order of the Spur</a> receive gold-coloured (usually brass) spurs if they have earned their membership through combat, or silver-coloured (usually nickel) spurs if they have not seen combat, but complete a <a href="/wiki/Rite_of_passage" title="Rite of passage">rite of passage</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_designs_and_wear">Basic designs and wear</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Basic designs and wear">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Spurs are worn with the tip of the neck pointed downward, sitting on the spur rest of the <a href="/wiki/Riding_boot" title="Riding boot">riding boot</a>, if there is one, with the buckle of the spur strap worn on the outside of the foot.</p>
<p>Spur styles differ between disciplines. Spurs for <a href="/wiki/Western_riding" title="Western riding">western riding</a> tend to be heavier, often decorated, and have rowels that rotate. The neck of western spurs is usually longer and the rowel wide in diameter, to accommodate the leg position of the Western-style rider, where the <a href="/wiki/Stirrup" title="Stirrup">stirrup</a> is adjusted long, and the heavy leather used for the <a href="/wiki/Saddle" title="Saddle">saddle</a>'s fenders and stirrups places the rider's leg a bit farther from the horse.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:112px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Spur2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="166" data-file-width="110" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/96/Spur2.jpg" width="110"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Spur2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
English riding spur</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Spurs in <a href="/wiki/English_riding" title="English riding">English riding</a> tend to be very sleek, slim and conservative in design, with a shorter neck, as the saddle and leg position is closer to the horse. They usually have a rounded or blunt end. Rowels are not as popular as the plain blunt end, although there are types that include a rowel or smooth disk on the end. When used in sports requiring finesse, such as <a href="/wiki/Dressage" title="Dressage">dressage</a>, the spur's purpose is not to speed up a horse, but to give accurate and precise <a href="/wiki/Riding_aids" title="Riding aids">aids</a> in lateral and complex movements, such as pirouettes, <i>travers</i> and <i>renvers</i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Airs_above_the_ground" title="Airs above the ground">airs above the ground</a>. Dressage riders tend to ride in "Waterford" style spurs with a rounded knob at the end. Conversely, <a href="/wiki/Show_hunter" title="Show hunter">show hunter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Show_jumping" title="Show jumping">jumper</a> riders may use a flatter end to encourage forward movement, such as the Prince of Wales design.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Motorcycle_spurs.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="532" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/43/Motorcycle_spurs.jpg/180px-Motorcycle_spurs.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/43/Motorcycle_spurs.jpg/270px-Motorcycle_spurs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/43/Motorcycle_spurs.jpg/360px-Motorcycle_spurs.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Motorcycle_spurs.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Motorcycle Spurs from Loop Spurs</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Another type of modern spur is those used on <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle" title="Motorcycle">motorcycles</a>. They are characterized by rowels worn as foot jewelry, hung off of boots. They can be similar in appearance to spurs worn by equestrians. Their bright material attracts motor vehicle drivers to the presence of motorcyclists, especially to their feet where riders are most vulnerable when stopped in traffic. Their owners may further customize them by adding miniature strobing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LED" title="LED">LED</a> lights. They help traffic light sensors detect their presence in intersections where inductive loops are used.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> They are also awarded by motorcycle clubs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equestrian_riding_technique">Equestrian riding technique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Equestrian riding technique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The spur is a refined tool, designed to allow the rider to transmit very subtle signals to the horse that are nearly invisible to any other observer. No matter the discipline, it is important that a rider has a correct position before using spurs, with a deep seat, legs lengthened to the extent allowed by the <a href="/wiki/Stirrup" title="Stirrup">stirrups</a>, heels down, with knees and thighs rolled in so that the rider has a solid base of support. A swinging or unstable leg may inadvertently jab the horse with the spur as the rider sits, thus irritating, distracting, or frightening the animal, and chronic misuse may deaden the horse to the leg aids. Improper use may also provoke dangerous or undesirable behaviors such as <a href="/wiki/Bucking" title="Bucking">bucking</a> or running away.</p>
<p>Spurs are rarely used in sports such as <a href="/wiki/Horse_racing" title="Horse racing">horse racing</a>, where the rider's leg is not significantly in contact with the horse.</p>
<p>Most spurs are activated by the rider flexing the heel slightly up and in. A roweled spur permits an additional type of action; a rider can roll the spur lightly against the side of the horse rather than being limited to simply pressing inward.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rodeo_spurring">Rodeo spurring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rodeo spurring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Barrel_racing_spur_pair.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1174" data-file-width="1566" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Barrel_racing_spur_pair.jpg/220px-Barrel_racing_spur_pair.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Barrel_racing_spur_pair.jpg/330px-Barrel_racing_spur_pair.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Barrel_racing_spur_pair.jpg/440px-Barrel_racing_spur_pair.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Barrel_racing_spur_pair.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A pair of barrel racing spurs with unique non-rowel design</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The exception to the use of spurs in a subtle fashion is in the <a href="/wiki/Rodeo" title="Rodeo">rodeo</a> events of <a href="/wiki/Bull_riding" title="Bull riding">bull riding</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saddle_bronc_and_bareback_riding" title="Saddle bronc and bareback riding">saddle bronc and bareback riding</a>, where the rider is required to spur in an elaborate, stylized fashion, touching the horse or bull at every stride. This requirement is designed to resemble the behavior of old-time horse-breakers who would deliberately provoke a horse to buck. In modern times, riders are required to use spurs in a manner that is merely encouraging an animal that is already predisposed to buck; they are not to produce pain. Spur design and use is strictly defined by rodeo rules, spurs are dull and rowels must turn freely. In fact, the way spurs are to be used in bucking events generally makes it harder for the rider to stay on: in bareback bronc competition, the spurs must be above the point of the horse's shoulder at the first jump and remain forward at all times, deliberately creating a very awkward position for the rider that requires both strength and coordination to stay on the horse. In saddle bronc competition, the rider must make a full sweep with the spurs from shoulder to flank with each jump, requiring great concentration and any error in balance putting the rider in a position to be quickly unseated. Bull riders are allowed a position that is the closest to that of classic equestrianism, they are not required to spur the bull, but if they choose to spur, may do so with their legs down in a style that resembles a normal riding position.</p>

<p>Spurs are divided into Men's, Women's, and Children's, according to width (which must fit on the heel of the rider's boot). Spurs are further divided according to the length of the neck, with <span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>‚ÅÑ<sub>4</sub></span>¬†in (0.6¬†cm) being relatively small (and a common size in children's spurs), with some being 2‚Äì3¬†in (5‚Äì7.5¬†cm) long. Many competition rules limit the length of the neck.</p>
<ul>
<li>Round end: end is a metal ball about the size of a small marble, making it one of the milder spurs.</li>
<li>Knob end: end of the spur is squared off but blunted at the edges.</li>
<li>Prince of Wales: has a flat end, making it slightly sharper. This is a popular spur.</li>
<li>Rowelled spur: the end of the spur has a toothed wheel which spins. This is the most common western-style spur, although it is seen on some English-style spurs. Teeth are dulled at the points. A rowel with many small teeth is milder than one with only a few, larger teeth. Most rowels have at least eight teeth on each wheel. Other variations, more common in English riding, include:
<ul>
<li>Disc: the end has a small rowel-like rolling disc without teeth, which allows the spur to roll on the horse's side when applied, decreasing chance of spur marks. Popular in dressage. Severity depends on thickness of disc.</li>
<li>Roller spur: end of the neck has a plastic "roller," which moves as the horse's side is touched. This spur tends to reduce spur-rubs on sensitive horses. It is considered very mild.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Swan-neck: the neck of the spur goes upward at an angle, before leveling off, looking similar to the neck of a swan. This is commonly seen in <a href="/wiki/Dressage" title="Dressage">dressage</a>.</li>
<li>Waterford: the end of the neck has a large, round metal ball, making the spur softer and less likely to cause spur rubs.</li>
<li>Le spur (English) or Barrel Racing Spur (Western): a spur with small "teeth" or ridges on the inside of the heel band, instead of a neck. For use, the rider does not have to turn in the heel. A quicker and more subtle design, but also more apt to be accidentally used when not intended.</li>
<li>Half Mounted: The spur is decorated on one side only with silver, copper or bronze decals, logos or coverings.</li>
<li>Full or Double Mounted: The spur is decorated on both sides (in and out) with precious metals, images and designs.</li>
</ul>
