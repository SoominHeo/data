<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Vietnamese_cuisine">In Vietnamese cuisine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1nh_cu%E1%BB%91n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In Vietnamese cuisine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Bánh cuốn</i> is made from a thin, wide sheet of steamed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermented</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> <a href="/wiki/Batter_(cooking)" title="Batter (cooking)">batter</a> filled with a mixture of cooked seasoned ground pork, minced <a href="/wiki/Auricularia_auricula-judae" title="Auricularia auricula-judae">wood ear mushroom</a>, and minced shallots. Sides for this dish usually consist of <i><a href="/wiki/Ch%E1%BA%A3_l%E1%BB%A5a" title="Chả lụa">chả lụa</a></i> (Vietnamese pork sausage), sliced cucumber, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mung_bean_sprouts" title="Mung bean sprouts">bean sprouts</a>, with the dipping sauce which is fish sauce called <i><a href="/wiki/N%C6%B0%E1%BB%9Bc_ch%E1%BA%A5m" title="Nước chấm">nước chấm</a></i>. Sometimes, a drop of <i><a href="/wiki/Lethocerus_indicus" title="Lethocerus indicus">cà cuống</a></i>, which is the essence of a <a href="/wiki/Belostomatidae" title="Belostomatidae">giant water bug</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Lethocerus_indicus" title="Lethocerus indicus">Lethocerus indicus</a></i>, is added to the <i>nước chấm</i> for extra flavor, although this ingredient is scarce and quite expensive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rice sheet in <i>bánh cuốn</i> is extremely thin and delicate. It is made by steaming a slightly fermented rice batter on a cloth that is stretched over a pot of boiling water. It is a light dish, and is generally eaten for breakfast everywhere in Vietnam. A different version of <i>bánh cuốn</i>, called <i>bánh cuốn Thanh Trì</i> and <i>bánh cuốn làng Kênh</i>, may be found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thanh_Tri,_Hanoi" title="Thanh Tri, Hanoi">Thanh Trì</a>, a southern district of <a href="/wiki/Hanoi" title="Hanoi">Hanoi</a> and Kênh village of Nam Định, an ancient village in the centre of Nam Định city.<i>Bánh cuốn Thanh Trì</i> or <i>Bánh cuốn làng Kênh</i> are not rolls, but just rice sheets eaten with <i><a href="/wiki/Ch%E1%BA%A3_l%E1%BB%A5a" title="Chả lụa">chả lụa</a></i>, fried shallots, or prawns.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_other_countries">In other countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1nh_cu%E1%BB%91n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In other countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This type of <a href="/wiki/Rice_noodle_roll" title="Rice noodle roll">rice noodle roll</a> is known in <a href="/wiki/Thai_cuisine" title="Thai cuisine">Thai cuisine</a> as <i>khao phan</i> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th" xml:lang="th">ข้าวพันผัก</span>; lit. "rice wrap"). It is regarded a speciality of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uttaradit_province" title="Uttaradit province">Uttaradit province</a> where it is eaten freshly made in many variations, but also sun-dried. The dried versions often have spices added to them and are popularly used as a <a href="/wiki/Wrap_(sandwich)" title="Wrap (sandwich)">wrap</a> for a spicy salad made with rice noodle and minced pork. <i>Khao phan</i> is not easily found in the rest of Thailand.</p>
