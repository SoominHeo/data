<p><b>Ban Chiang</b> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th" xml:lang="th">แหล่งโบราณคดี บ้านเชียง</span>) is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archeology" title="Archeology">archeological</a> site in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoe_Nong_Han" title="Amphoe Nong Han">Nong Han District</a>, <a href="/wiki/Udon_Thani_Province" title="Udon Thani Province">Udon Thani Province</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>. It has been on the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_heritage" title="World heritage">world heritage</a> list since 1992. Discovered in 1966, the site attracted enormous publicity due to its attractive red painted <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ban_Chiang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Villagers had uncovered some of the pottery in prior years without insight into its age or historical importance. In August 1966 Steve Young, an anthropology and government student at Harvard College, was living in the village conducting interviews for his senior honors thesis. Young, a speaker of Thai, was familiar with the work of Wilhelm G. Solheim and his theory of possible ancient origins of civilization in Southeast Asia. One day while walking down a path in Ban Chiang with his assistant, an art teacher in the village school, Young tripped over a root of a <a href="/wiki/Kapok_tree" title="Kapok tree">kapok tree</a> and fell on his face in the dirt path. Under him were the exposed tops of pottery jars of small and medium sizes. Young recognized that the firing techniques used to make the pots were very rudimentary, but that the designs applied to the surface of the vessels were unique and wonderful. He took samples of pots to Princess Phanthip <a href="/wiki/Chumbhotbongs_Paribatra" title="Chumbhotbongs Paribatra">Chumbote</a> who had the private museum of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suan_Pakkad" title="Suan Pakkad">Suan Pakkad</a> in Bangkok and to Chin Yu Di of the Thai Government's Fine Arts Department<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Later, Elisabeth Lyons, an art historian on the staff of the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Foundation" title="Ford Foundation">Ford Foundation</a>, sent <a href="/wiki/Sherd" title="Sherd">sherds</a> from Ban Chiang to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a> for dating.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeology">Archaeology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ban_Chiang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Archaeology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the first formal scientific excavation in 1967, several skeletons, together with <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> grave gifts, were unearthed. <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">Rice</a> fragments have also been found, leading to the belief that the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> settlers were probably farmers. The site's oldest graves do not include bronze artifacts and are therefore from a <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> culture; the most recent graves date to the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a>. Pots and sherds from the site are now found in museums across the world, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Museum_f%C3%BCr_Indische_Kunst" title="Museum für Indische Kunst">Museum für Indische Kunst</a> in Berlin and the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a> in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>This site has often been called "the cemetery site," but recent research has suggested that the deceased were actually buried next to or beneath dwellings. This practice is called residential burial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dating_the_artifacts">Dating the artifacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ban_Chiang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dating the artifacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first datings of the artifacts using the <a href="/wiki/Thermoluminescence_dating" title="Thermoluminescence dating">thermoluminescence technique</a> resulted in a range from <a href="/wiki/5th_millennium_BC" title="5th millennium BC">4420 BCE</a> to 3400 BCE, which would have made the site the earliest Bronze Age culture in the world. However, with the 1974–1975 excavation, sufficient material became available for <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon dating</a>, which resulted in more recent dates. The earliest grave was about 2100 BCE, the latest about 200 CE. Bronze making began circa 2000 BCE, as evidenced by crucibles and bronze fragments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Bronze objects include bracelets, rings, anklets, wires and rods, spearheads, axes and adzes, hooks, blades, and little bells.</p>
<p>A date of 2100 BCE was obtained from rice phytoliths from inside a grave vessel of the lowest grave. A dating program for this site has involved dating the bones from the people who lived at Ban Chiang and the bones of animals interred with them. The resulting determinations have been analysed using the Bayesian statistic OxCal 4.0, and the results suggested that the initial settlement of Ban Chiang took place by about 1500 BCE, with the transition to the Bronze Age about 1000 BCE.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Museum_and_World_Heritage_Status">Museum and World Heritage Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ban_Chiang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Museum and World Heritage Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Ban Chiang museum is located by the protected excavation site and provides general information for the public about the site and its importance for human history. Staff there act both to inform visiting members of the public about the site, as well as the monitoring of the condition of the site and providing resources for academics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The site itself was declared a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1992<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> under criteria iii, which describes a site that "bear[s] a unique or at least exceptional testimony to a cultural tradition or to a civilization which is living or has disappeared."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Included in the museum's collection is the traveling exhibit curated by <a href="/wiki/Joyce_White" title="Joyce White">Dr. Joyce White</a>, titled <i>Ban Chiang, Discovery of a Lost Bronze Age</i>, which toured the U.S. and international sites following Penn Museum excavations and became part of the Ban Chiang Museum permanent exhibit in 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The museum also includes "displays and information that highlights the three main periods and six sub-periods" as well as the site's general and excavation history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">[8]</a></sup> The site and museum have been well-reviewed by several travel publications, including CNN,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">[8]</a></sup> TripAdvisor,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and the official tourism site of Thailand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="US_legal_case">US legal case</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ban_Chiang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: US legal case">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The site made headlines in January 2008 when thousands of artifacts from the Ban Chiang cultural tradition and other prehistoric traditions of Thailand were found to be illegally in several California museums and other locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The plot involved smuggling the items out of Thailand into the US and then donating them to museums in order to claim large tax <a href="/wiki/Write-off" title="Write-off">write-offs</a>. There were said to be more items in museums than at the site itself. This was brought to light during high profile raids conducted by the police after a <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a> agent had posed as a private collector. Some of the cases have been resolved and some of the museums have returned artifacts to Thailand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>NOTE: The excavation at Ban Chiang in 1974/75 was followed by an article by Chester Gorman and Pisit Charoenwongsa, claiming evidence for the earliest dates in the world for bronze casting and iron working. This led to an at times acrimonious debate, between those who accepted these dates, and those who did not. Subsequent excavations, including that at <a href="/wiki/Ban_Non_Wat" title="Ban Non Wat">Ban Non Wat</a>, have now shown that the proposed early dates for Ban Chiang are unlikely. However, the early claims are still repeated in the secondary literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>After Dr. Gorman's death in 1981, Dr. Joyce White continued research and publications as Director of the Ban Chiang Project at the University of Pennsylvania Museum of Archaeology and Anthropology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Dr. White's research endeavors have included analysis and publication of Penn's excavations at Ban Chiang in Thailand in the mid-1970s; ecological field research at Ban Chiang in 1978–1981 including investigations of how local people identified and used plants; lake coring and ecological mapping for palaeoenvironmental research in several parts of Thailand during the 1990s; and since 2001, survey and excavation in northern Laos, especially in <a href="/wiki/Luang_Prabang_Province" title="Luang Prabang Province">Luang Prabang Province</a>.</p>
