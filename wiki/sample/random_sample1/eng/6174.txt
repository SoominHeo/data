<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semisimple_Lie_algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Root_system" title="Root system">Root system</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Connected_Dynkin_Diagrams.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="625" data-file-width="375" height="367" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Connected_Dynkin_Diagrams.svg/220px-Connected_Dynkin_Diagrams.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Connected_Dynkin_Diagrams.svg/330px-Connected_Dynkin_Diagrams.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Connected_Dynkin_Diagrams.svg/440px-Connected_Dynkin_Diagrams.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Connected_Dynkin_Diagrams.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The simple Lie algebras are classified by the connected <a href="/wiki/Dynkin_diagram" title="Dynkin diagram">Dynkin diagrams</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Every semisimple Lie algebra over an algebraically closed field of characteristic 0 is a <a href="/wiki/Direct_sum" title="Direct sum">direct sum</a> of simple Lie algebras (by definition), and the finite-dimensional simple Lie algebras fall in four families – A<sub>n</sub>, B<sub>n</sub>, C<sub>n</sub>, and D<sub>n</sub> – with five exceptions E<sub>6</sub>, E<sub>7</sub>, E<sub>8</sub>, F<sub>4</sub>, and G<sub>2</sub>. Simple Lie algebras are classified by the connected <a href="/wiki/Dynkin_diagram" title="Dynkin diagram">Dynkin diagrams</a>, shown on the right, while semisimple Lie algebras correspond to not necessarily connected Dynkin diagrams, where each component of the diagram corresponds to a summand of the decomposition of the semisimple Lie algebra into simple Lie algebras.</p>
<p>The classification proceeds by considering a <a href="/wiki/Cartan_subalgebra" title="Cartan subalgebra">Cartan subalgebra</a> (maximal abelian Lie algebra; corresponds to a <a href="/wiki/Maximal_torus" title="Maximal torus">maximal torus</a> in a Lie group) and the <a href="/wiki/Adjoint_representation_of_a_Lie_algebra" title="Adjoint representation of a Lie algebra">adjoint action</a> of the Lie algebra on this subalgebra. The <a href="/wiki/Root_system" title="Root system">root system</a> of the action then both determines the original Lie algebra and must have a very constrained form, which can be classified by the Dynkin diagrams.</p>
<p>The classification is widely considered one of the most elegant results in mathematics – a brief list of axioms yields, via a relatively short proof, a complete but non-trivial classification with surprising structure. This should be compared to the <a href="/wiki/Classification_of_finite_simple_groups" title="Classification of finite simple groups">classification of finite simple groups</a>, which is significantly more complicated.</p>

For example, to classify simple real Lie algebras, one classifies real Lie algebras with a given complexification, which are known as <a href="/wiki/Real_form_(Lie_theory)" title="Real form (Lie theory)">real forms</a> of the complex Lie algebra; this can be done by <a href="/wiki/Satake_diagram" title="Satake diagram">Satake diagrams</a>, which are Dynkin diagrams with additional data ("decorations").</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semisimple_Lie_algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The semisimple Lie algebras over the complex numbers were first classified by <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Killing" title="Wilhelm Killing">Wilhelm Killing</a> (1888–90), though his proof lacked rigor. His proof was made rigorous by <a href="/wiki/%C3%89lie_Cartan" title="Élie Cartan">Élie Cartan</a> (1894) in his Ph.D. thesis, who also classified semisimple real Lie algebras. This was subsequently refined, and the present classification by Dynkin diagrams was given by then 22-year-old <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Dynkin" title="Eugene Dynkin">Eugene Dynkin</a> in 1947. Some minor modifications have been made (notably by J. P. Serre), but the proof is unchanged in its essentials and can be found in any standard reference, such as (<a href="#CITEREFHumphreys1972">Humphreys 1972</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semisimple_Lie_algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Complete_reducibility">Complete reducibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semisimple_Lie_algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Complete reducibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A consequence of semisimplicity is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weyl%27s_completely_reducibility_theorem" title="Weyl's completely reducibility theorem">theorem due to Weyl</a>: every finite-dimensional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Representation_of_a_Lie_algebra" title="Representation of a Lie algebra">representation</a> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Completely_reducible" title="Completely reducible">completely reducible</a>; that is for every invariant subspace of the representation there is an invariant complement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Infinite-dimensional representations of semisimple Lie algebras are not in general completely reducible.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Linear">Linear</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semisimple_Lie_algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Linear">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The adjoint representation is injective, and so a semisimple Lie algebra is also a <a href="/wiki/Linear_Lie_algebra" title="Linear Lie algebra">linear Lie algebra</a> under the adjoint representation. This may lead to some ambiguity, as every Lie algebra is already linear with respect to some other vector space (<a href="/wiki/Ado%27s_theorem" title="Ado's theorem">Ado's theorem</a>), although not necessarily via the adjoint representation. But in practice, such ambiguity rarely occurs.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rank">Rank</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semisimple_Lie_algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Rank">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <b>rank</b> of a complex semisimple Lie algebra is the dimension of any of its <a href="/wiki/Cartan_subalgebra" title="Cartan subalgebra">Cartan subalgebras</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Significance">Significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semisimple_Lie_algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The significance of semisimplicity comes firstly from the <a href="/wiki/Levi_decomposition" title="Levi decomposition">Levi decomposition</a>, which states that every finite dimensional Lie algebra is the semidirect product of a solvable ideal (its radical) and a semisimple algebra. In particular, there is no nonzero Lie algebra that is both solvable and semisimple.</p>
<p>Semisimple Lie algebras have a very elegant classification, in stark contrast to solvable Lie algebras. Semisimple Lie algebras over an algebraically closed field are completely classified by their <a href="/wiki/Root_system" title="Root system">root system</a>, which are in turn classified by <a href="/wiki/Dynkin_diagram" title="Dynkin diagram">Dynkin diagrams</a>. Semisimple algebras over non-algebraically closed fields can be understood in terms of those over the algebraic closure, though the classification is somewhat more intricate; see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_form" title="Real form">real form</a> for the case of real semisimple Lie algebras, which were classified by <a href="/wiki/%C3%89lie_Cartan" title="Élie Cartan">Élie Cartan</a>.</p>
<p>Further, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classification_of_finite-dimensional_representations_of_semisimple_Lie_algebras" title="Classification of finite-dimensional representations of semisimple Lie algebras">representation theory of semisimple Lie algebras</a> is much cleaner than that for general Lie algebras. For example, the Jordan decomposition in a semisimple Lie algebra coincides with the Jordan decomposition in its representation; this is not the case for Lie algebras in general.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Generalizations">Generalizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semisimple_Lie_algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Generalizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Reductive_Lie_algebra" title="Reductive Lie algebra">Reductive Lie algebra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Split_Lie_algebra" title="Split Lie algebra">Split Lie algebra</a></div>
<p>Semisimple Lie algebras admit certain generalizations. Firstly, many statements that are true for semisimple Lie algebras are true more generally for <a href="/wiki/Reductive_Lie_algebra" title="Reductive Lie algebra">reductive Lie algebras</a>. Abstractly, a reductive Lie algebra is one whose adjoint representation is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Completely_reducible" title="Completely reducible">completely reducible</a>, while concretely, a reductive Lie algebra is a direct sum of a semisimple Lie algebra and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abelian_Lie_algebra" title="Abelian Lie algebra">abelian Lie algebra</a>; for example, <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<msub>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">

</mrow>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mi>n</mi>
</mrow>
</msub>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle {\mathfrak {sl}}_{n}}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="{\mathfrak {sl}}_{n}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7c75cbc0e014c41c8cd1e7a40afbadf87eb79de9" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; margin-left: -0.03ex; width:2.959ex; height:2.509ex;"/></span> is semisimple, and <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

Many properties of semisimple Lie algebras depend only on reducibility.</p>
<p>Many properties of complex semisimple/reductive Lie algebras are true not only for semisimple/reductive Lie algebras over algebraically closed fields, but more generally for <a href="/wiki/Split_Lie_algebra" title="Split Lie algebra">split semisimple/reductive Lie algebras</a> over other fields: semisimple/reductive Lie algebras over algebraically closed fields are always split, but over other fields this is not always the case. Split Lie algebras have essentially the same representation theory as semsimple Lie algebras over algebraically closed fields, for instance, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Splitting_Cartan_subalgebra" title="Splitting Cartan subalgebra">splitting Cartan subalgebra</a> playing the same role as the <a href="/wiki/Cartan_subalgebra" title="Cartan subalgebra">Cartan subalgebra</a> plays over algebraically closed fields. This is the approach followed in (<a href="#CITEREFBourbaki2005">Bourbaki 2005</a>), for instance, which classifies representations of split semisimple/reductive Lie algebras.</p>
