<p><b>Anti-German sentiment</b> (or <b>Germanophobia</b>) is defined as an opposition to or fear of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, its <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">inhabitants</a>, its culture and the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German language</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Its opposite is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanophilia" title="Germanophilia">Germanophilia</a>.</p>
<p>As a political phenomenon, anti-German sentiment became significant especially from the mid-19th Century, parallel with the <a href="/wiki/Unification_of_Germany" title="Unification of Germany">Unification of Germany</a> and its rise as a world power.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia">Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1860s Russia experienced an outbreak of Germanophobia, mainly restricted to a small group of writers in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Petersburg" title="St. Petersburg">St. Petersburg</a> who had united around a right wing newspaper. It began in 1864 with the publication of an article by a writer (using the pseudonym "Shedoferotti") who proposed that <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> be given autonomy and that the privileges of the German barons in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltic_republics" title="Baltic republics">Baltic republics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> be preserved. <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Katkov" title="Mikhail Katkov">Mikhail Katkov</a> published a harsh criticism of the article in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moscow_News" title="Moscow News">Moscow News</a></i>, which in turn caused a flood of angry articles in which Russian writers expressed their irritation with Europeans, some of which featured direct attacks on Germans.</p>
<p>The following year, 1865, the 100th anniversary of the death of <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Lomonosov" title="Mikhail Lomonosov">Mikhail Lomonosov</a> was marked throughout the Russian empire. Articles were published mentioning the difficulties Lomonosov had encountered from the foreign members of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Russian Academy of Sciences">Russian Academy of Sciences</a>, most of whom had been of German descent. The authors then criticized contemporary German scholars for their neglect of the Russian language and for printing articles in foreign languages while receiving funds from the Russian people. It was further suggested by some writers that Russian citizens of German origin who did not speak Russian and follow the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Orthodox faith</a> should be considered foreigners. It was also proposed that people of German descent be forbidden from holding diplomatic posts as they might not have "solidarity with respect to Russia".</p>
<p>Despite the press campaign against Germans, Germanophobic feelings did not develop in Russia to any widespread extent, and died out, due to the Imperial family's German roots and the presence of many German names in the Russian political elite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Britain">Great Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Great Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Negative comments about Germany had begun to appear in Britain in the 1870s, following the Prussian victory in the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">Franco-Prussian War</a> in 1870–71.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Criticisms were expressed in the press and in the birth of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invasion_novel" title="Invasion novel">invasion novel</a> (e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Battle_of_Dorking" title="The Battle of Dorking">The Battle of Dorking</a></i>), many of which focused on the idea that Britain might be Germany's next victim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1887, the label <i><a href="/wiki/Made_in_Germany" title="Made in Germany">Made in Germany</a></i> was introduced, to get British buyers to adhere to the concept of "buying British". After suffering slight losses, German manufacturers soon found the label to be of good use.</p>
<p>In the 1890s there was widespread hostility towards foreigners in Britain, mainly directed against eastern European Jews but also including Germans. Joseph Bannister believed that German residents in Britain were mostly "gambling-house keepers, hotel-porters, barbers, 'bullies', runaway conscripts, bath-attendants, street musicians, criminals, bakers, socialists, cheap clerks, etc". Interviewees for the Royal Commission on Alien Immigration believed that Germans were not of the white race and involved in prostitution and burglary. Many people viewed Germans working in Britain as non whites and as threatening the livelihood of Britons by being willing to work for longer hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anti-German hostility deepened since early 1896 after the <a href="/wiki/Kruger_telegram" title="Kruger telegram">Kruger telegram</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaiser_Wilhelm_II" title="Kaiser Wilhelm II">Kaiser Wilhelm II</a>, in which he congratulated <a href="/wiki/Paul_Kruger" title="Paul Kruger">President Kruger</a> of the <a href="/wiki/South_African_Republic" title="South African Republic">Transvaal</a> on resisting the British <a href="/wiki/Jameson_Raid" title="Jameson Raid">Jameson Raid</a>. Attacks on Germans in London were reported in the German press at the time but do not appear to have actually occurred. The <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Review_(London)" title="Saturday Review (London)">Saturday Review (London)</a></i> suggested "be ready to fight Germany, as <i>Germania delenda est</i>" ("Germany is to be destroyed", a reference to the coda against the Carthaginians adopted by Cato's speeches in the second Roman Republic). The Kaiser's reputation was further degraded by his angry tirade and the <a href="/wiki/Daily_Telegraph_Affair" title="Daily Telegraph Affair">Daily Telegraph Affair</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_20th_century">Early 20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the signing of the <a href="/wiki/Entente_Cordiale" title="Entente Cordiale">Entente Cordiale</a> in 1904 between Britain and France, official relationships cooled as did popular attitudes towards Germany and German residents in Britain. A fear of German militarism replaced a previous admiration for German culture and literature. At the same time, journalists produced a stream of articles on the threat posed by Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Daily_Telegraph_Affair" title="Daily Telegraph Affair">Daily Telegraph Affair</a> of 1908-1909, the Kaiser humiliated himself and further soured relations by his intemperate attacks on Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1894 <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Harmsworth,_1st_Viscount_Northcliffe" title="Alfred Harmsworth, 1st Viscount Northcliffe">Alfred Harmsworth</a> commissioned author <a href="/wiki/William_Le_Queux" title="William Le Queux">William Le Queux</a> to write the serial novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Great_War_in_England_in_1897" title="The Great War in England in 1897">The Great War in England in 1897</a></i>, which featured Germany, France and Russia combining forces to crush Britain. Twelve years later Harmsworth asked him to repeat this, promising the full support of his formidable advertising capabilities. The result was the bestselling <i><a href="/wiki/The_Invasion_of_1910" title="The Invasion of 1910">The Invasion of 1910</a></i>, which originally appeared in serial form in the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Mail" title="Daily Mail">Daily Mail</a></i> in 1906 and has been referred to by historians as inducing an atmosphere of paranoia, mass hysteria and Germanophobia that would climax in the Naval Scare of 1908–09.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Articles of the <i>Daily Mail</i> regularly advocated anti-German sentiments throughout the 20th century, telling British readers to refuse service at restaurants by Austrian or German waiters on the claim that they were spies and told them that if a German-sounding waiter claimed to be Swiss that they should demand to see the waiter's passport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the same time conspiracy theories were concocted combining Germanophobia with <a href="/wiki/Antisemitism" title="Antisemitism">antisemitism</a>, concerning the supposed foreign control of Britain, some of which blamed Britain's entry into the Boer War on international financiers "chiefly German in origin and Jewish in race".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Most of these ideas about German-Jewish conspiracies originated from right-wing figures such as <a href="/wiki/Arnold_White" title="Arnold White">Arnold White</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hilaire_Belloc" title="Hilaire Belloc">Hilaire Belloc</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leo_Maxse" title="Leo Maxse">Leo Maxse</a>, the latter using his publication the <i><a href="/wiki/National_Review_(London)" title="National Review (London)">National Review</a></i> to spread them.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>In 1914 when the German army invaded neutral Belgium and northern France many thousands of Belgian and French civilians were accused of being <a href="/wiki/Francs-tireurs" title="Francs-tireurs">francs-tireurs</a> and executed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/German_war_crimes#World_War_I" title="German war crimes">These acts</a> were used to encourage anti-German feelings and the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_I" title="Allies of World War I">Allied Powers</a> produced propaganda depicting Germans as <i><a href="/wiki/List_of_terms_used_for_Germans" title="List of terms used for Germans">Huns</a></i> capable of infinite cruelty and violence.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United Kingdom, anti-German feeling led to infrequent rioting, assaults on suspected Germans and the looting of stores owned by people with German-sounding names, occasionally even taking on an antisemitic tone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Increasing anti-German hysteria even threw suspicion upon the British monarchy and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_V_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George V of the United Kingdom">King George V</a> was persuaded to change his German name of <a href="/wiki/Saxe-Coburg_and_Gotha" title="Saxe-Coburg and Gotha">Saxe-Coburg and Gotha</a> to <a href="/wiki/House_of_Windsor" title="House of Windsor">Windsor</a> and relinquish all German titles and styles on behalf of his relatives who were British subjects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the UK, the <a href="/wiki/German_Shepherd" title="German Shepherd">German Shepherd</a> breed of dog was renamed to the euphemistic "Alsatian"; the <a href="/wiki/The_Kennel_Club" title="The Kennel Club">English Kennel Club</a> only re-authorised the use of 'German Shepherd' as an official name in 1977.</p>
<p>Attitudes to Germany were not entirely negative among British troops fighting on the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_I)" title="Western Front (World War I)">Western Front</a>; the British writer <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Shakespeare" title="Nicholas Shakespeare">Nicholas Shakespeare</a> quotes this statement from a letter written by his grandfather during the First World War in which he says he would rather fight the French and describes German bravery:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Personally, my opinion is that our fellows get on much best [sic] with the Germans, and would very much rather be fighting the French! ... It was a fine sight to see the Germans coming on in solid formation, in front of our machine guns ... they were generally led by one officer in front who came along to certain death as cool as a cucumber, with his sword held straight up in front of him at the salute.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Nicholas Shakespeare, <i>The first casualty of war</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shakespeare_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shakespeare-15">[15]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Graves" title="Robert Graves">Robert Graves</a> who like the King also had German relatives, wrote shortly after the war during his time at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_University" title="Oxford University">Oxford University</a> as an undergraduate that:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The eighteenth century owed its unpopularity largely to its Frenchness. Anti-French feeling among most ex-soldiers amounted almost to an obsession. Edmund, shaking with nerves, used to say at this time: "No more wars for me at any price! Except against the French. If ever there is a war against them, I'll go like a shot." Pro-German feeling had been increasing. With the war over and the German armies beaten, we could give the German soldier credit for being the most efficient fighting man in Europe ... Some undergraduates even insisted that we had been fighting on the wrong side: our natural enemies were the French.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Robert Graves <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goodbye_to_All_That" title="Goodbye to All That">Goodbye to All That</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Canada, <a href="/wiki/Berlin_to_Kitchener_name_change" title="Berlin to Kitchener name change">the Ontario city of Berlin changed its name</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kitchener,_Ontario" title="Kitchener, Ontario">Kitchener</a>, after <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Kitchener,_1st_Earl_Kitchener" title="Herbert Kitchener, 1st Earl Kitchener">Lord Kitchener</a>, famously pictured on the "<a href="/wiki/Lord_Kitchener_Wants_You" title="Lord Kitchener Wants You">Lord Kitchener Wants You</a>" recruiting posters. Several streets in Toronto that had previously been named for Liszt, Humboldt, Schiller, Bismarck, etc., were changed to names with strong British associations, such as Balmoral. There were anti-German riots in Victoria and Calgary in the first years of the war.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AGLbadge.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="290" height="228" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/AGLbadge.jpg/220px-AGLbadge.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9c/AGLbadge.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AGLbadge.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A 1915 Australian badge reflecting the Anti-German sentiment at the time.</div>
</div>

<p>In Australia, an official proclamation of August 10, 1914 required all German citizens to register their domiciles at the nearest police station and to notify authorities of any change of address. Under the later Aliens Restriction Order of May 27, 1915, enemy aliens who had not been interned had to report to the police once a week and could only change address with official permission. An amendment to the Restriction Order in July 1915 prohibited enemy aliens and naturalized subjects from changing their name or the name of any business they ran. Under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_Precautions_Act" title="War Precautions Act">War Precautions Act</a> of 1914 (which survived the First World War), publication of German language material was prohibited and schools attached to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutheran" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a> churches were forced to abandon German as the language of teaching or were closed by the authorities. German clubs and associations were also closed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jupp_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-jupp-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The original German names of settlements and streets were officially changed. In <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a>, <i>Grunthal</i> became <i><a href="/wiki/Verdun,_South_Australia" title="Verdun, South Australia">Verdun</a></i> and <i>Krichauff</i> became <i>Beatty</i>. In <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> <i>Germantown</i> became <i><a href="/wiki/Holbrook,_New_South_Wales" title="Holbrook, New South Wales">Holbrook</a></i> after the submarine commander <a href="/wiki/Norman_Douglas_Holbrook" title="Norman Douglas Holbrook">Norman Douglas Holbrook</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jupp_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-jupp-17">[17]</a></sup> This pressure was strongest in <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a> where 69 towns changed their names, including Pertersburg, South Australia, which became <a href="/wiki/Peterborough,_South_Australia" title="Peterborough, South Australia">Peterborough</a> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_place_names_changed_from_German_names" title="Australian place names changed from German names">Australian place names changed from German names</a>).</p>
<p>Most of the anti-German feeling was created by the press that tried to create the idea that all those of German birth or descent supported Germany uncritically. A booklet circulated widely in 1915 claimed that "there were over 3,000 German spies scattered throughout the states". Anti-German propaganda was also inspired by local and British companies who were keen to take the opportunity to eliminate Germany as a competitor in the Australian market. Germans in Australia were increasingly portrayed as evil by the very nature of their origins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jupp_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-jupp-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p>After the revelation of the <a href="/wiki/Zimmermann_Telegram" title="Zimmermann Telegram">Zimmermann Telegram</a> partly sparked the American declaration of war against Imperial Germany in April 1917, German Americans were sometimes accused of being too sympathetic to Germany. Former president <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> denounced "<a href="/wiki/Hyphenated_American" title="Hyphenated American">hyphenated Americanism</a>", insisting that dual loyalties were impossible in wartime. A small minority came out for Germany, or ridiculed the British (as did <a href="/wiki/H._L._Mencken" title="H. L. Mencken">H. L. Mencken</a>). Similarly, Harvard psychology professor <a href="/wiki/Hugo_M%C3%BCnsterberg" title="Hugo Münsterberg">Hugo Münsterberg</a> dropped his efforts to mediate between America and Germany, and threw his efforts behind the German cause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Justice Department attempted to prepare a list of all German aliens, counting approximately 480,000 of them, more than 4,000 of whom were imprisoned in 1917-18. The allegations included spying for Germany, or endorsing the German war effort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Thousands were forced to buy war bonds to show their loyalty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/American_Red_Cross" title="American Red Cross">Red Cross</a> barred individuals with German last names from joining in fear of sabotage. One person was killed by a mob; in <a href="/wiki/Collinsville,_Illinois" title="Collinsville, Illinois">Collinsville, Illinois</a>, German-born <a href="/wiki/Robert_Prager" title="Robert Prager">Robert Prager</a> was dragged from jail as a suspected spy and lynched.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hickey_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hickey-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>When the United States entered the war in 1917, some German Americans were looked upon with suspicion and attacked regarding their loyalty. Some aliens were convicted and imprisoned on charges of sedition, for refusing to swear allegiance to the United States war effort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Chicago, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Stock" title="Frederick Stock">Frederick Stock</a> was forced to step down as conductor of the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Symphony_Orchestra" title="Chicago Symphony Orchestra">Chicago Symphony Orchestra</a> until he finalized his naturalization papers. Orchestras replaced music by German composer <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Wagner</a> with French composer <a href="/wiki/Hector_Berlioz" title="Hector Berlioz">Berlioz</a>.</p>
<p>The town, Berlin, Michigan, was changed to <a href="/wiki/Marne,_Michigan" title="Marne, Michigan">Marne, Michigan</a> (honoring those who fought in the Battle of Marne). The town of Berlin, Shelby County Ohio changed its name to its original name of <a href="/wiki/Fort_Loramie,_Ohio" title="Fort Loramie, Ohio">Fort Loramie, Ohio</a>. The city of <a href="/wiki/Germantown,_Tennessee" title="Germantown, Tennessee">Germantown</a> in Shelby County <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a> changed its name to Neshoba temporarily during the war.</p>
<p>German street names in many cities were changed. German and Berlin streets in Cincinnati became English and Woodward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cincy_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-cincy-23">[23]</a></sup> In Chicago Lubeck, Frankfort, and Hamburg streets were renamed Dickens, Charleston, and Shakespeare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-simpson_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-simpson-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In New Orleans, Berlin Street was renamed for <a href="/wiki/John_J._Pershing" title="John J. Pershing">General Pershing</a>, head of the American Expeditionary Force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-murrin_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-murrin-26">[26]</a></sup> In Indianapolis, <a href="/wiki/Otto_von_Bismarck" title="Otto von Bismarck">Bismarck</a> Avenue and <a href="/wiki/Germania" title="Germania">Germania</a> Street were renamed to Pershing Avenue and Belleview Street, respectively in 1917,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUPUI_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-IUPUI-27">[27]</a></sup> Brooklyn’s Hamburg Avenue was changed to Wilson Avenue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many businesses changed their names. In Chicago, German Hospital became Grant Hospital; likewise the German Dispensary and the German Hospital in New York City were renamed to <a href="/wiki/Lenox_Hill_Hospital" title="Lenox Hill Hospital">Lenox Hill Hospital</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wyckoff_Heights_Hospital&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wyckoff Heights Hospital (page does not exist)">Wyckoff Heights Hospital</a> respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-simpson_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-simpson-24">[24]</a></sup> In New York, the giant Germania Life Insurance Company became <a href="/wiki/Guardian_Life_Insurance_Company_of_America" title="Guardian Life Insurance Company of America">Guardian</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> At the US Customs House in Lower Manhattan, the word “Germany” that was on a shield that one of the building’s many figures was holding was chiseled over.</p>
<p>Many schools stopped teaching German-language classes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cincy_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-cincy-23">[23]</a></sup> The City College of New York continued teaching German courses, but reduced the number of credits students could receive for them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Books published in German were removed from libraries or even burned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cincy_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-cincy-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati</a>, the public library was asked to withdraw all German books from its shelves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In Iowa, in the 1918 <a href="/wiki/Babel_Proclamation" title="Babel Proclamation">Babel Proclamation</a>, the governor prohibited all foreign languages in schools and public places. Nebraska banned instruction in any language except English, but the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Supreme_Court" title="U.S. Supreme Court">U.S. Supreme Court</a> ruled the ban illegal in 1923 (<i><a href="/wiki/Meyer_v._Nebraska" title="Meyer v. Nebraska">Meyer v. Nebraska</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some words of German origin were changed, at least temporarily. <a href="/wiki/Sauerkraut" title="Sauerkraut">Sauerkraut</a> came to be called "liberty cabbage",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-murrin_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-murrin-26">[26]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_measles" title="German measles">German measles</a> became "liberty measles", <a href="/wiki/Hamburger" title="Hamburger">hamburgers</a> became "liberty sandwiches"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-murrin_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-murrin-26">[26]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Dachshund" title="Dachshund">dachshunds</a> became "liberty pups".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ford_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-ford-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In parallel with these changes, many German Americans elected to Americanize names (e.g. Schmidt to Smith, Müller to Miller) and to limit the use of the German language in public places, especially churches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ethnic German Medal of Honor winners were American <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Service" title="United States Army Air Service">USAAS</a> <a href="/wiki/Flying_ace" title="Flying ace">ace pilots</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_Rickenbacker" title="Edward Rickenbacker">Edward Rickenbacker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frank_Luke" title="Frank Luke">Frank Luke</a>; German-ethnicity DSC winners who also served with the USAAS in Europe included <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Frank_Wehner" title="Joseph Frank Wehner">Joseph Frank Wehner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karl_John_Schoen" title="Karl John Schoen">Karl John Schoen</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_World_War">Second World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Second World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Anti-fascism" title="Anti-fascism">Anti-fascism</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Britain_2">Great Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Great Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1940 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister_of_Information" title="Minister of Information">Ministry of Information</a> launched an "Anger Campaign" to instill "personal anger ... against the German people and Germany", because the British were "harbouring little sense of real personal animus against the average German". This was done to strengthen British resolve against the Germans. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Vansittart,_1st_Baron_Vansittart" title="Robert Vansittart, 1st Baron Vansittart">Sir Robert Vansittart</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreign_Office" title="Foreign Office">Foreign Office</a>'s chief diplomatic advisor until 1941, gave a series of radio broadcasts in which he said that Germany was a nation raised on "envy, self-pity and cruelty" whose historical development had <a href="/wiki/Sonderweg" title="Sonderweg">"prepared the ground for Nazism"</a> and that it was Nazism that had "finally given expression to the blackness of the German soul".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The British Institute of Public Opinion (BIPO) tracked the evolution of anti-German/anti-Nazi feeling in Britain, asking the public, via a series of opinion polls conducted from 1939 to 1943, whether "the chief enemy of Britain was the German people or the Nazi government". In 1939 only 6% of respondents held the German people responsible; however, following <a href="/wiki/The_Blitz" title="The Blitz">the Blitz</a> and the "Anger Campaign" in 1940, this increased to 50%. This subsequently declined to 41% by 1943. It also was reported by Home Intelligence in 1942 that there was some criticism of the official attitude of hatred towards Germany on the grounds that "England ought to be a civilising influence" and that such hatred might hinder the possibility of a reasonable settlement following the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fox_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-fox-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>This attitude was expanded upon by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.R.R._Tolkien" title="J.R.R. Tolkien">J.R.R. Tolkien</a>. In 1944, he wrote in a letter to his son <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Tolkien" title="Christopher Tolkien">Christopher</a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>[I]t is distressing to see the press grovelling in the gutter as low as <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Goebbels" title="Joseph Goebbels">Goebbels</a> in his prime, shrieking that any German commander who holds out in a desperate situation (when, too, the military needs of his side clearly benefit) is a drunkard, and a besotted fanatic. ... There was a solemn article in the local paper seriously advocating systematic exterminating of the entire German nation as the only proper course after military victory: because, if you please, they are <a href="/wiki/Rattlesnake" title="Rattlesnake">rattlesnakes</a>, and don't know the difference between good and evil! (What of the writer?) The Germans have just as much right to declare the <a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">Poles</a> and Jews exterminable vermin, <a href="/wiki/Untermensch" title="Untermensch">subhuman</a>, as we have to select the Germans: in other words, no right, whatever they have done.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In the same year <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_Observation" title="Mass Observation">Mass Observation</a> asked its observers to analyse British private opinion of the German people and found that 54% of opinion was "pro-German", in that it expressed sympathy and "not their fault". This tolerance of the German people as opposed to the Nazi regime increased as the war progressed. Mass Observation established in 1943 that up to 60% of people maintained a distinction between Germans and Nazis, with only 20% or so expressing any "hatred, vindictiveness, or need for retribution". British film propaganda of the period similarly maintained the division between Nazi supporters and the German people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fox_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-fox-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_2">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Internment_of_German_Americans" title="Internment of German Americans">Internment of German Americans</a></div>
<p>Between 1931 and 1940, 114,000 Germans and thousands of Austrians moved to the United States, many of whom — including, e.g., Nobel prize winner <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lion_Feuchtwanger" title="Lion Feuchtwanger">Lion Feuchtwanger</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bertold_Brecht" title="Bertold Brecht">Bertold Brecht</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arnold_Sch%C3%B6nberg" title="Arnold Schönberg">Arnold Schönberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hanns_Eisler" title="Hanns Eisler">Hanns Eisler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Mann" title="Thomas Mann">Thomas Mann</a> — were either <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Germany" title="History of the Jews in Germany">Jewish Germans</a> or <a href="/wiki/Anti-fascism" title="Anti-fascism">anti-Nazis</a> fleeing Nazi oppression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> About 25,000 people became paying members of the pro-Nazi <a href="/wiki/German_American_Bund" title="German American Bund">German American Bund</a> during the years before the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> German aliens were the subject of suspicion and discrimination during the war, although prejudice and sheer numbers meant they suffered as a group generally less than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_American" title="Japanese American">Japanese Americans</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Smith_Act" title="Smith Act">Alien Registration Act of 1940</a> required 300,000 German-born resident aliens who had German citizenship to register with the Federal government and restricted their travel and property ownership rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Under the still active <a href="/wiki/Alien_and_Sedition_Acts" title="Alien and Sedition Acts">Alien Enemy Act of 1798</a>, the United States government <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_American_internment" title="German American internment">interned nearly 11,000 German citizens</a> between 1940 and 1948. Civil rights violations occurred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-real_people_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-real_people-43">[43]</a></sup> An unknown number of "voluntary internees" joined their spouses and parents in the camps and were not permitted to leave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> sought out Americans of German ancestry for top war jobs, including General <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a>, Admiral <a href="/wiki/Chester_W._Nimitz" title="Chester W. Nimitz">Chester W. Nimitz</a>, and General <a href="/wiki/Carl_Andrew_Spaatz" title="Carl Andrew Spaatz">Carl Andrew Spaatz</a>. He appointed Republican <a href="/wiki/Wendell_Willkie" title="Wendell Willkie">Wendell Willkie</a> as a personal representative. German Americans who had fluent German language skills were an important asset to wartime intelligence, and they served as translators and as spies for the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The war evoked strong pro-American patriotic sentiments among German Americans, few of whom by then had contacts with distant relatives in the old country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>The October 1939 seizure by the German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pocket_battleship" title="Pocket battleship">pocket battleship</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DKM_Deutschland" title="DKM Deutschland">Deutschland</a></i> of the US freighter <a href="/wiki/SS_City_of_Flint_(1919)" title="SS City of Flint (1919)">SS <i>City of Flint</i></a>, as it had 4000 tons of oil for Britain on board, provoked much anti-German sentiment in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following its entry into the Second World War, the US Government <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_American_internment" title="German American internment">interned at least 11,000 American citizens</a> of German ancestry. The last to be released, a German-American, remained imprisoned until 1948 at Ellis Island,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> three and a half years after the cessation of hostilities against Germany.</p>
<p>In 1944, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Morgenthau,_Jr." title="Henry Morgenthau, Jr.">Henry Morgenthau, Jr.</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_the_Treasury" title="United States Secretary of the Treasury">United States Secretary of the Treasury</a>, put forward the strongest proposal for punishing Germany to the <a href="/wiki/Second_Quebec_Conference" title="Second Quebec Conference">Second Quebec Conference</a>. It became known as the <a href="/wiki/Morgenthau_Plan" title="Morgenthau Plan">Morgenthau Plan</a>, and was intended to prevent Germany from having the industrial base to start another world war. However this plan was shelved quickly, the Western Allies did not seek reparations for war damage, and the United States implemented the <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Plan" title="Marshall Plan">Marshall Plan</a> that was intended to and did help <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany's</a> <a href="/wiki/Wirtschaftswunder" title="Wirtschaftswunder">post war recovery</a> to its former position as a pre-eminent industrial nation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After Brazil's entry into the war on the Allied side in 1942, anti-German riots broke out in nearly every city in Brazil in which Germans were not the majority population. German factories, including the Suerdick cigar factory in <a href="/wiki/Salvador,_Bahia" title="Salvador, Bahia">Bahia</a>, shops, and hotels were destroyed by mobs. The largest demonstrations took place in <a href="/wiki/Porto_Alegre" title="Porto Alegre">Porto Alegre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande_do_Sul" title="Rio Grande do Sul">Rio Grande do Sul</a>. Brazilian police persecuted and interned "subjects of the Axis powers" in internment camps similar to those used by the US to intern Japanese-Americans. Following the war, German schools were not reopened, the German-language press disappeared completely, and use of the German language became restricted to the home and the older generation of immigrants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soviet_Union">Soviet Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Soviet Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forced_labour_of_Germans_in_the_Soviet_Union" title="Forced labour of Germans in the Soviet Union">Forced labour of Germans in the Soviet Union</a></div>
<p>In 1937 <a href="/wiki/NKVD_Order_No._00439" title="NKVD Order No. 00439">NKVD Order No. 00439</a> arrested all German citizens and former citizens in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fore_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-fore-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later during the war Germans were suggested to be used for forced labour. The Soviet Union began deporting ethnic Germans in their territories and using them for forced labour. By 1950 most deportees had returned home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>

<p>American General <a href="/wiki/George_S._Patton" title="George S. Patton">George S. Patton</a> complained that the U.S. policy of <a href="/wiki/Denazification" title="Denazification">denazification</a> following Germany's surrender harmed American interests and was motivated simply by hatred of the defeated German people.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_and_expulsion_of_Germans">Flight and expulsion of Germans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Flight and expulsion of Germans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Flight_and_expulsion_of_Germans_(1944%E2%80%9350)" title="Flight and expulsion of Germans (1944–50)">Flight and expulsion of Germans (1944–50)</a></div>
<p>After WWII ended about 11 million to at least 12 million<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weber2_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weber2-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kacowicz100_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kacowicz100-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuck156_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schuck156-60">[60]</a></sup> Germans fled or were expelled from Germany's former eastern provinces or migrated from other countries to what remained of Germany, the largest <a href="/wiki/Population_transfer" title="Population transfer">transfer</a> of a single European population in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_Era" title="Modern Era">modern history</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weber2_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weber2-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kacowicz100_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kacowicz100-59">[59]</a></sup> Estimates of the total number of dead range from 500,000 to 2,000,000, where the higher figures include "unsolved cases" of persons reported as missing and presumed dead. Many German civilians were sent to internment and labor camps, where they died. Rummel estimated that 1,585,000 Germans were killed in Poland and 197,000 were killed in Czechoslovakia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The German-Czech Historians Commission, on the other hand, established a death toll for Czechoslovakia of 15,000-30,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> The events are usually classified as <a href="/wiki/Population_transfer" title="Population transfer">population transfer</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFrank2008_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrank2008-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> or as <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_cleansing" title="Ethnic cleansing">ethnic cleansing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERubinstein2004.27.27.5Bhttps:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DnMMAk4VwLLwC_Anti-German_sentiment.5D.27.27_at_.5B.5BGoogle_Books.5D.5D_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERubinstein2004.27.27.5Bhttps:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DnMMAk4VwLLwC_Anti-German_sentiment.5D.27.27_at_.5B.5BGoogle_Books.5D.5D-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Felix_Ermacora" title="Felix Ermacora">Felix Ermacora</a>, among a minority of legal scholars, equated ethnic cleansing with genocide,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> and stated that the expulsion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sudeten_German" title="Sudeten German">Sudeten Germans</a> therefore constituted genocide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forced_labor_of_Germans">Forced labor of Germans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Forced labor of Germans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Forced_labor_of_Germans_after_World_War_II" title="Forced labor of Germans after World War II">Forced labor of Germans after World War II</a></div>
<p>In many Allied countries Germans were used as forced labor after 1945. Some of the laborers, depending on the country, were prisoners of war or just general ethnic Germans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Israel">In Israel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: In Israel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before the rise of Hitler and the NSDAP (1920s to 1933), many Central and east European Jews tended to be pro-German but many not or not so much.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> German Jews were deeply integrated in the country's culture, and many of them fought with distinction in the First World War German Army. Jews in <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czech</a> lands tended to adopt the German language and culture in preference to Slavic languages (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kafka" title="Kafka">Kafka</a> being a conspicuous example), and a similar phenomenon was evident in other parts of the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian" title="Austro-Hungarian">Austro-Hungarian</a> Empire but not all. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theodore_Herzl" title="Theodore Herzl">Theodore Herzl</a> , the founding father of <a href="/wiki/Zionism" title="Zionism">Zionism</a>, himself spoke and wrote German and in his utopian book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altneuland" title="Altneuland">Altneuland</a></i> actually depicted the future Jewish state as German-speaking. However, throughout Eastern Europe, Jews spoke <a href="/wiki/Yiddish" title="Yiddish">Yiddish</a>, and wrote it in Hebrew script, thus keeping their distance from German culture.</p>
<p>Pro-German attitudes suffered an extremely painful rupture and complete reversal with the persecutions and atrocities committed by Nazi Germany, culminating with the systematic genocide of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holocaust" title="Holocaust">Holocaust</a>. In the first decades of Israel's existence, anti-German feelings were strong and dominant in Israeli society. There was a widespread cultural and commercial boycott of all things German (and often, Austrian as well) and a determination "never to set foot on German soil".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> German Jews in Israel, themselves refugees from the Nazi persecutions, came under strong social pressure to cease using German, their mother language.</p>
<p>During the 1940s, the words "German" and "Nazi" were used interchangeably.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> (Until the late 1990s, the <a href="/wiki/Sign_language" title="Sign language">sign language</a> of Israeli <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deaf_community" title="Deaf community">deaf communities</a> used the <a href="/wiki/Swastika" title="Swastika">Swastika</a> as the sign for "German".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>) There was a widespread scepticism about the possibility of "another Germany" ever emerging; specifically, there was suspicion about <a href="/wiki/Konrad_Adenauer" title="Konrad Adenauer">Konrad Adenauer</a>'s claim to be involved in the creation of a new, democratic Germany. Many Israelis took up the Soviet claims made in the early years of the Cold War that <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> was a "fascist state" in which ex-Nazis held key positions.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Reparations_Agreement_between_Israel_and_West_Germany" title="Reparations Agreement between Israel and West Germany">Reparations Agreement</a> with Germany, signed by the <a href="/wiki/David_Ben-Gurion" title="David Ben-Gurion">David Ben-Gurion</a> government in 1952, was the focus of intense political controversy, and the protest demonstrations led by then opposition leader <a href="/wiki/Menachem_Begin" title="Menachem Begin">Menachem Begin</a> turned into pitched battles with the police. In the early 1960s, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eichmann_Trial" title="Eichmann Trial">Eichmann Trial</a> brought the horrors and traumas of the Holocaust to the center of public consciousness. The establishment of diplomatic relations between Israel and West Germany in 1966 entailed a new wave of protests and demonstrations, though less violent than those of 1952.</p>
<p>Since the late 1960s, there has been a clear, though gradual, process of rapprochement between Israelis and Germans in all spheres: diplomatic, commercial and cultural. The 1967 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Six_Day_War" title="Six Day War">Six Day War</a> realigned Israeli politics, with the issue of occupied territories henceforth defining what is "right wing" and "left wing"; one result was that militant Israeli nationalism tended to be anti-Arab rather than anti-German.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> When Begin became Israel's Prime Minister in 1977, he had little option but to take up the maintenance of already very extensive ties with Germany, to whose creation he had been fiercely opposed as an opposition leader.</p>
<p>A momentary flare-up of anti-German feeling occurred during the 1991 Gulf War, when Israel was the subject of missile attack by <a href="/wiki/Saddam_Hussein" title="Saddam Hussein">Saddam Hussein</a>'s Iraq. Some Israeli columnists and politicians combined the revelations of German corporations helping the Iraqi arms industry and the strong anti-war movement in Germany and tied both with the German Nazi past.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The German government of the time managed, however, to assuage Israeli feelings by providing the Israeli Navy with three advanced <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarines</a>, which, according to repeated reports in the international press, were used to mount nuclear missiles and provide Israel with a <a href="/wiki/Second_strike" title="Second strike">second strike</a> capacity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> These <a href="/wiki/Dolphin-class_submarine" title="Dolphin-class submarine">Dolphin class submarines</a> replaced the obsolete 1970s <a href="/wiki/Gal-class_submarine" title="Gal-class submarine">Gal-class submarines</a>, and are some of the most modern submarines in the world. Two submarines were given outright by Germany, and Israel paid for half of the third.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the 21st century, the long debate about whether the <a href="/wiki/Israel_Philharmonic_Orchestra" title="Israel Philharmonic Orchestra">Israel Philharmonic Orchestra</a> should play the works of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a> is mostly considered as a remnant of the past. In March 2008, German Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Angela_Merkel" title="Angela Merkel">Angela Merkel</a> became the first foreign head of government invited to deliver a speech in the Israeli parliament, which she gave in the German language. Several members of parliament did leave in protest during the speech, claiming the need to create a collective memory that "will create a kind of electric wave when Jews will hear the sounds of the German language, they'll remember the Holocaust".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kibbutz <a href="/wiki/Lohamei_HaGeta%27ot" title="Lohamei HaGeta'ot">Lohamei HaGeta'ot</a> (English: "Fighters of the Ghettos")–which included among its founders survivors of the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Ghetto_Uprising" title="Warsaw Ghetto Uprising">Warsaw Ghetto Uprising</a>–reversed a long-standing ban and permitted a delegation from its <a href="/wiki/Ghetto_Fighters%27_House" title="Ghetto Fighters' House">Ghetto Fighters' House</a> museum to accept an invitation and visit Germany. The explanation was given, "When German babies born on the day of Hitler's death are now sixty-three years old, it is ridiculous to continue to demand a collective responsibility."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In one October 2008 interview, researcher Hanan Bar (חנן בר) summed up the ambiguous Israeli attitude to Germany: "If the average Israeli happens to see a football match between Germany and Holland [sic], he would automatically root for the Dutch. But the same person, when buying a washing machine, would prefer a German model, considering it to be the best".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_Europe">Contemporary Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Contemporary Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the separation into two countries following the Second World War; <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> generally had good relationships with its western neighboring states, while <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a> to some degree had similar relationships with its eastern neighbors. Many of these relationships continued after the end of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> with the unified Germany. West Germany was a co-founder of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> and the reunified Germany continues as a leading member. During the process of European unification, Germany and France forged a strong relationship, ending the long-standing <a href="/wiki/French%E2%80%93German_enmity" title="French–German enmity">French–German enmity</a>, which had peaked during and after the First World War.</p>
<p>Much present-day anti-German sentiment has been particularly strong in East European countries occupied by Germany during the war, and those that were at war with Germany and its allies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although views fluctuate somewhat in response to geopolitical issues (such as the invasion of Iraq), Americans regard modern Germany as an ally<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> and few hold anti-German sentiments. Occasionally, German people are stereotyped as Nazis (goose-stepping, shouting "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sieg_Heil" title="Sieg Heil">Sieg Heil</a>!", sporting a "Hitler moustache"), being "<a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Germany" title="Economy of Germany">ruthlessly efficient</a>" and having no <a href="/wiki/German_humour" title="German humour">sense of humour</a> in some parts of American media, as well as in the UK and other countries. <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a>'s music was not performed in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> until 1995 (radio) and 2001 (concert) and was for many years unpopular in Poland. This can be explained, at least partially, due to Richard Wagner's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-semitism" title="Anti-semitism">anti-semitism</a> and the Nazi appropriation of Wagner's music based on Hitler's personal affection for his operas.</p>
<p>In a poll carried out in 2008 for the <a href="/wiki/BBC_World_Service" title="BBC World Service">BBC World Service</a>, in which people in 34 countries were asked about the positive and negative influence of 13 countries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-influential_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-influential-80">[a]</a></sup> Germany was the most popular, ahead of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a>; across all countries surveyed, only 18 percent across all countries surveyed thought Germany had a mainly negative influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCpoll_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBCpoll-81">[80]</a></sup> Mainly negative views were most widespread in Turkey (47 percent) and Egypt (43 percent).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCpoll_81-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBCpoll-81">[80]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/BBC_World_Service" title="BBC World Service">BBC World Service</a> "Country Rating Poll" for 2014 surveyed opinion in 24 participating countries concerning the influence of 16 countries<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-influential_80-1"><a href="#cite_note-influential-80">[a]</a></sup> and the European Union; 12 of the influential countries participated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[82]</a></sup> Results were released at the end of May. The table shows the "Views of Germany's Influence" overall (line 1) and by country. "Germany has kept its position of the most favourably viewed nation in 2014."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[83]</a></sup> That is, Germany is the one whose influence is most commonly (60%) viewed positively; among the 17 Germany stands second to Canada as the ones least commonly (18%) viewed negatively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the first ten polls, annual from 2005, Germany had been the country with world influence most commonly viewed positively at least in 2008<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCpoll_81-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBCpoll-81">[80]</a></sup> as well as 2013 and 2014.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Britain">Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Germans sometimes complain of stereotypical associations of them with acts and a regime of more than sixty years ago, such as the use of anti-German sentiment in headlines by parts of the British press, which were often used in the build-up to football matches between England and Germany. A more recent example arose when German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_(Catholicism)" title="Cardinal (Catholicism)">Cardinal</a> Joseph Ratzinger became <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a> <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">Benedict XVI</a>.</p>
<p>In fact, according to a recent poll, the British people have a rather positive image of Germany, with 62 percent believing that Germany has a mainly positive influence in the world and only 20 percent believing that Germany's influence is mainly negative, slightly better than Germans' views of Britain (60 percent and 27 percent, respectively).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCpoll_81-3"><a href="#cite_note-BBCpoll-81">[80]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poland">Poland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Poland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many Poles perceive Germans as their long-time oppressors, dating back to the times of <a href="/wiki/Teutonic_Order" title="Teutonic Order">Teutonic Order</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This notion is based on a long history of conflict with ethnic Poles, first by the German-language and culture Prussians then by the united German state, starting with <a href="/wiki/Partitions_of_Poland" title="Partitions of Poland">three partitions of Poland</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanization" title="Germanization">germanization</a> in the 19th and 20th centuries, and culminating in the Nazi Germany's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Poland_(1939)" title="Invasion of Poland (1939)">invasion of Poland</a> in 1939 and the genocide of six million Polish citizens, most of whom were Jews<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, that ensued during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_War_II_crimes_in_Poland" title="World War II crimes in Poland">the German occupation 1939–1945</a>.</p>
<p>Several issues have also strained recent Polish-German relations, although Poland and post-reunification Germany overall have had mostly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German%E2%80%93Polish_relations" title="German–Polish relations">positive relations</a>. The Poles are suspicious of the campaign led by <a href="/wiki/Erika_Steinbach" title="Erika Steinbach">Erika Steinbach</a>, a daughter of a German officer in the German forces occupying Poland, made famous for her voting against <a href="/wiki/Oder%E2%80%93Neisse_line" title="Oder–Neisse line">Oder–Neisse line</a> borders,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[85]</a></sup> who seeks reparations for the property lost by Germans expelled by Poland following the Second World War and to create the <a href="/wiki/Centre_Against_Expulsions" title="Centre Against Expulsions">Centre Against Expulsions</a>. In addition, the proposed Russo-German pipeline through the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic</a> <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">Sea</a> is seen by Poles as aimed at cutting off Poland's natural gas supplies from <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> without damaging supply to Germany, and was even compared to the ignominious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molotov-Ribbentrop_pact" title="Molotov-Ribbentrop pact">Molotov-Ribbentrop pact</a> by <a href="/wiki/Rados%C5%82aw_Sikorski" title="Radosław Sikorski">Radosław Sikorski</a>, Polish foreign minister.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[86]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Netherlands">Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Germany%E2%80%93Netherlands_football_rivalry" title="Germany–Netherlands football rivalry">Germany–Netherlands football rivalry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany%E2%80%93Netherlands_relations" title="Germany–Netherlands relations">Germany–Netherlands relations</a></div>
<p>Anti-German sentiment was already prevalent in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> centuries before the <a href="/wiki/Unification_of_Germany" title="Unification of Germany">unification of Germany</a> and establishment of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_German_Empire" title="Second German Empire">Second German Empire</a>, completed 1871. The Dutch are thought to have developed a low opinion of Germans during the 17th century, also known in the Netherlands as the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Golden_Age" title="Dutch Golden Age"><i>Gouden Eeuw</i></a> (literally: "Golden Century"), when the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">Dutch Republic</a> was one of the world's most advanced and powerful countries while modern Germany was still a patchwork of warring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiefdom" title="Fiefdom">fiefs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-itsok_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-itsok-88">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the first half of the 17th century, the Republic saw a major spike in German immigrants including common laborers, <a href="/wiki/Religious_persecution" title="Religious persecution">persecuted</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutheran" title="Lutheran">Lutherans</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jews</a>, and all sorts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_refugee" title="War refugee">war refugees</a> fleeing the violence of the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Cultural_conflict" title="Cultural conflict">culture clash</a> soon followed, and German immigrants were often discriminated against by the native Dutch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-itsok_88-1"><a href="#cite_note-itsok-88">[87]</a></sup> It was likely around this time that the earliest variations of the word <a href="/wiki/List_of_terms_used_for_Germans#Netherlands" title="List of terms used for Germans">mof</a> were first used to refer to lower class German migrants. There are known <a href="/wiki/Joke" title="Joke">joke books</a> in which these Germans are featured prominently and stereotypically as dumb, arrogant and filthy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[88]</a></sup></p>
<p>During <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">WWI</a> (in which the Netherlands was neutral), the so-called <a href="/wiki/Wire_of_Death" title="Wire of Death">Wire of Death</a>, a lethal 2000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volts" title="Volts">volts</a> electric fence built along the southern Dutch border by the <a href="/wiki/Belgium_in_World_War_I" title="Belgium in World War I">Germans occupying Belgium</a> caused a large number of fatalities among the Dutch people, renewing anti-German sentiment in the Netherlands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[89]</a></sup> This sentiment was reborn as hatred when, in 1940, <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> launched its <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Netherlands" title="Battle of the Netherlands">invasion of the Netherlands</a> despite earlier promises from Germany to respect <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_neutrality&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dutch neutrality (page does not exist)">Dutch neutrality</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[91]</a></sup> More than 100,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_Jews" title="Dutch Jews">Dutch Jews</a> were <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Holocaust_in_the_Netherlands&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Holocaust in the Netherlands (page does not exist)">deported to their deaths</a> during the subsequent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi_occupation_of_the_Netherlands" title="Nazi occupation of the Netherlands">Nazi occupation</a>, and starvation afflicted much of the country during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hongerwinter" title="Hongerwinter">"Hunger Winter"</a> of 1944-45. Most elderly Dutch people remember these events including the <a href="/wiki/Rotterdam_Blitz" title="Rotterdam Blitz">Rotterdam Blitz</a> bitterly, and some still refuse to set foot on German soil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-itsok_88-2"><a href="#cite_note-itsok-88">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>A sociological study from 1998 showed that still two generations after it had ended, World War II remained influential, and "present-day parents and young people are negatively biased against Germany."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[92]</a></sup> Aspeslagh and Dekker reported in 1998 that "more than half of the cohort born after 1950 answered 'sometimes' or 'often' when asked whether they harbored anti-German feelings".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[93]</a></sup> Reviewing three large-scale academic studies from the 1990s, they concluded:</p>
<dl>
<dd>The emotional feelings regarding Germany and Germans revealed by these studies are defined by the Second World War. The annual commemorations of World War II, the way history lessons deal with Germany and the continual, casually negative remarks by adults reproduce the negative emotions about Germany and Germans, particularly among the young.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[94]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Newer studies also consistently show that Dutch anti-German sentiment has been falling steadily for years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[95]</a></sup> and that most Dutch people today show a positive view towards both Germany and the German people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[97]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Switzerland">Switzerland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Switzerland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rapid increase of <a href="/wiki/German_immigration_to_Switzerland" title="German immigration to Switzerland">German immigration to Switzerland</a> during the 2000s (decade) has given rise to "Germanophobia" in <a href="/wiki/German-speaking_Switzerland" title="German-speaking Switzerland">German-speaking Switzerland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[98]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greece_and_Italy">Greece and Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-German_sentiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Greece and Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Reich#The_European_Union" title="Fourth Reich">Fourth Reich § The European Union</a></div>
<p>The recent <a href="/wiki/Greek_government-debt_crisis" title="Greek government-debt crisis">Greek government-debt crisis</a> and EU-driven austerity measures imposed on the country have revived anti-German sentiments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[99]</a></sup> The Greek media have been making comments critical of the German policy, often mentioning, and drawing parallels with the <a href="/wiki/Axis_occupation_of_Greece" title="Axis occupation of Greece">Axis occupation of Greece</a>, with some commentators emphasizing a genetic heritage from "<a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a>" or "<a href="/wiki/Huns#20th_century_use_in_reference_to_Germans" title="Huns">Huns</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[100]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[101]</a></sup> A poll by VPRC noted the existence of an anti-German sentiment in Greece, and that the majority of the respondents nowadays connect Germany with negative notions such as "Hitler", "Nazism" and "3rd Reich".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[102]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Popular_Fighters_Group" title="Popular Fighters Group">Popular Fighters Group</a> formed in response to the debt crisis is overtly anti-German and has targeted German organizations in Greece.</p>
<p>In August 2012, Italian Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Mario_Monti" title="Mario Monti">Mario Monti</a> warned that escalating arguments over how to resolve the Euro debt crisis are turning countries against each other and threatening to rip Europe apart. Resentment in Italy is growing against Germany, the European Union and chancellor Merkel, he said, adding that "the pressures already bear the traits of a psychological break-up of Europe".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aldrick2012_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aldrick2012-104">[103]</a></sup></p>
