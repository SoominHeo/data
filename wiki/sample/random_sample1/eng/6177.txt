<p>The <b>bandurria</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Plectrum" title="Plectrum">plucked</a> <a href="/wiki/Chordophone" title="Chordophone">chordophone</a> from <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, similar to the <a href="/wiki/Mandolin" title="Mandolin">mandolin</a>, primarily used in <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Spain" title="Music of Spain">Spanish folk music</a>, but also found in former Spanish colonies.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Instrument_development">Instrument development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bandurria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Instrument development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to the 18th century, the bandurria had a round back, similar or related to the <a href="/wiki/Mandore_(instrument)" title="Mandore (instrument)">mandore</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tyler-52_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tyler-52-1">[1]</a></sup> It had become a flat-backed instrument by the 18th century, with five double courses of strings, tuned in fourths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tyler-52_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tyler-52-1">[1]</a></sup> The original bandurrias of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">Medieval</a> period had three strings. During the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> they gained a fourth string. During the <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> period the bandurria had 10 strings (5 pairs).</p>
<p>The modern bandurria has 12 strings (6 pairs). The strings are tuned in unison pairs, going up in fourths from the low G#. The lowest four strings are a major-third above those of a standard guitar and the highest two strings are a fourth above a standard guitar, i.e. G<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">♯</span>, c<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">♯</span>, f<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">♯</span>, b, e and a.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SID_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-SID-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations_and_uses_in_different_parts_of_the_world">Variations and uses in different parts of the world</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bandurria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Variations and uses in different parts of the world">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spain">Spain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bandurria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Spain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Estudiantina_pro.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2135" data-file-width="2811" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Estudiantina_pro.jpg/250px-Estudiantina_pro.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Estudiantina_pro.jpg/375px-Estudiantina_pro.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Estudiantina_pro.jpg/500px-Estudiantina_pro.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Estudiantina_pro.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Spanish Students, who were first brought to the United States by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Eugene_Abbey" title="Henry Eugene Abbey">Henry Eugene Abbey</a>'s firm in 1880.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abbey_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-abbey-3">[3]</a></sup> They played bandurrias, but confusion in the United States led people to believe the instruments were mandolins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sparks23-29_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sparks23-29-4">[4]</a></sup> This poster was for a Manhattan performance at the <a href="/wiki/Booth%27s_Theatre" title="Booth's Theatre">Booth's Theatre</a> on the corner of 6th Avenue and 23rd Street in Manhattan.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Juan Ruiz first mentioned the term "mandurria" in the 14th century in his "<i>Libro De Buen Amor.</i>" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grove_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grove-5">[5]</a></sup> After that, Juan Bermudo gave the description of the bandurria in his "<i>Comiença el libro llamado declaraciõ de instrumentos</i>" as a three-string instrument in 1555, but he also mentioned other types with four or even five strings. In the early 1870s, a child's wake was accompanied with the bandurria music in Jijona, Alicante Province. The zapateo, a dance derived from the Spanish zapateado and introduced by tobacco cultivators from the Canary Islands, is accompanied with bandurria and other instruments before 1900.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippines">Philippines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bandurria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Philippines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Philippine harp bandurria is a 14-string bandurria used in many Philippine folkloric songs, with 16 frets and shorter neck than the 12 string bandurria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SID_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-SID-2">[2]</a></sup> This instrument probably evolved in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> during the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Philippines_(1521%E2%80%931898)" title="History of the Philippines (1521–1898)">Spanish period</a>, from 1521 to 1898. The Filipino bandurria is used in an orchestra of plucked string instruments called <a href="/wiki/Rondalla" title="Rondalla">rondalla</a>. It is tuned a step lower than the Spanish version, that is, low to high: F# B E A D G.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_America">South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bandurria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are also many different varieties of Bandurria in South America, especially Peru and Bolivia. They have 4 courses, unlike the traditional Spanish 6 courses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SID_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-SID-2">[2]</a></sup> The 4 courses are double, triple or quadruple, and the tuning is guitar-like, rather than the fourths tuning used on the Spanish type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In Lima, Peru, harp and bandurria duos were common in the early 20th century. Nowadays people there still play bandurria accompanying with the popular vals peruano, or vals criollo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grove_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grove-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bandurria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although generally little known in the UK, the bandurria was used by Roy Williamson of the Scottish folk-group The Corrie Folk Trio. When this group later (after the loss of one member) became the immensely popular <a href="/wiki/The_Corries" title="The Corries">Corries</a>, Williamson incorporated a bandurria into one of the two multi-instrument "combolins" that he constructed for himself and his partner.</p>
