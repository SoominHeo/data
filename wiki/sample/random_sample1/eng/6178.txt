<p>The <b>Peramelidae</b> family of <a href="/wiki/Marsupial" title="Marsupial">marsupials</a> contains all of the extant <a href="/wiki/Bandicoot" title="Bandicoot">bandicoots</a>. One known extinct species of bandicoot, the <a href="/wiki/Pig-footed_bandicoot" title="Pig-footed bandicoot">pig-footed bandicoot</a>, was so different from the other species, it was recently moved into its own family. Four fossil peramelids are described. They are found throughout <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>, with at least some species living in every available habitat, from rainforest to desert.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peramelidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Peramelids are small marsupials, ranging in size from the <a href="/wiki/Mouse_bandicoot" title="Mouse bandicoot">mouse bandicoot</a>, which is 15-17.5 cm long, to the <a href="/wiki/Giant_bandicoot" title="Giant bandicoot">giant bandicoot</a>, which at 39-56 cm in length and up 4.7 kg in weight, is about the size of a <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbit</a>. They have short limbs and tails, smallish, mouse-like ears, and a long, pointed snout.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoM_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoM-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Peramelids are <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivorous</a>, with soil-dwelling invertebrates forming the major part of their diet; they also eat seeds, fruit, and fungi. Their teeth are correspondingly unspecialised, with most species having the <a href="/wiki/Dentition#Dental_formula" title="Dentition">dental formula</a> <span class="nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; padding:0 0.1em;" title="upper dentition: Incisors.Canines.Premolars.Molars">5.1.3.4</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; padding:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;" title="lower dentition: Incisors.Canines.Premolars.Molars">3.1.3.4</span></span></span></p>
<p>Female peramelids have a <a href="/wiki/Pouch_(marsupial)" title="Pouch (marsupial)">pouch</a> that opens to the rear, and contains eight teats. The maximum litter size is, therefore, eight, since marsupial young are attached to the teat during development, although two to four young per litter is a more typical number. The <a href="/wiki/Gestation" title="Gestation">gestation</a> period of peramelids is the shortest among mammals, at just 12.5 days, the young are <a href="/wiki/Weaning" title="Weaning">weaned</a> around two months of age, and reach sexual maturity at just three months. This allows a given female to produce more than one litter per breeding season, and gives peramelids an unusually high reproductive rate compared with other marsupials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoM_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-EoM-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
