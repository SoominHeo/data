<p><i><b>42</b></i> is a 2013 American <a href="/wiki/Biographical_film" title="Biographical film">biographical</a> <a href="/wiki/Sports_film" title="Sports film">sports film</a> written and directed by <a href="/wiki/Brian_Helgeland" title="Brian Helgeland">Brian Helgeland</a> about the <a href="/wiki/Racial_integration" title="Racial integration">racial integration</a> of <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Professional_baseball" title="Professional baseball">professional baseball</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baseball_player" title="Baseball player">player</a> <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Robinson" title="Jackie Robinson">Jackie Robinson</a>, who wore jersey number 42 through his <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League</a> career. The film stars <a href="/wiki/Chadwick_Boseman" title="Chadwick Boseman">Chadwick Boseman</a> as Robinson, and <a href="/wiki/Harrison_Ford" title="Harrison Ford">Harrison Ford</a> as <a href="/wiki/Branch_Rickey" title="Branch Rickey">Branch Rickey</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Alan_Tudyk" title="Alan Tudyk">Alan Tudyk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicole_Beharie" title="Nicole Beharie">Nicole Beharie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Meloni" title="Christopher Meloni">Christopher Meloni</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andre_Holland" title="Andre Holland">Andre Holland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lucas_Black" title="Lucas Black">Lucas Black</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hamish_Linklater" title="Hamish Linklater">Hamish Linklater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Merriman" title="Ryan Merriman">Ryan Merriman</a> appearing in supporting roles.</p>
<p>The film received generally positive reviews and grossed over $97 million on a $40 million budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <i>42</i> was released in North America on April 12, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=42_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film tells the story of <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Robinson" title="Jackie Robinson">Jackie Robinson</a> and, under the guidance of team executive <a href="/wiki/Branch_Rickey" title="Branch Rickey">Branch Rickey</a>, Robinson's signing with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Dodgers" title="Brooklyn Dodgers">Brooklyn Dodgers</a> to become the <a href="/wiki/List_of_African-American_firsts" title="List of African-American firsts">first African-American player</a> to break the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baseball_color_barrier" title="Baseball color barrier">baseball color barrier</a>. The story focuses mostly on the <a href="/wiki/1947_Brooklyn_Dodgers_season" title="1947 Brooklyn Dodgers season">1947 Brooklyn Dodgers season</a> and somewhat on Robinson's 1946 season with the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Royals" title="Montreal Royals">Montreal Royals</a>, which emphasize his battles with racism.</p>
<p>In 1945, after sportswriter <a href="/wiki/Wendell_Smith" title="Wendell Smith">Wendell Smith</a> suggests that Rickey consider Robinson as the black ballplayer Rickey is looking for, Robinson and his team, the <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_Monarchs" title="Kansas City Monarchs">Kansas City Monarchs</a>, stop by a gas station. When the attendant refuses Robinson entry to the washroom, Robinson says they will find another station at which to fill up the team bus, and the attendant relents. As Robinson steps out, a scout for the Dodgers sent by Rickey approaches him and invites him to Brooklyn. He is offered a $600 per month contract and a $3,500 signing bonus, which Robinson accepts after being warned by Rickey that he must control his temper if he wants to play. Robinson proposes to his girlfriend, <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Robinson" title="Rachel Robinson">Rachel</a>, by phone and she accepts.</p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/Spring_training" title="Spring training">spring training</a>, Robinson earns a roster spot with the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Royals" title="Montreal Royals">Montreal Royals</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Triple-A_(baseball)" title="Triple-A (baseball)">AAA</a> affiliate of the Brooklyn <a href="/wiki/Farm_team" title="Farm team">farm system</a>. After a great season there and spring training in Panama, he advances to the Dodgers. Most of the team soon signs a petition, stating they refuse to play with Robinson, but manager <a href="/wiki/Leo_Durocher" title="Leo Durocher">Leo Durocher</a> insists Robinson will play with the main team. When Durocher is suspended by <a href="/wiki/Happy_Chandler" title="Happy Chandler">Happy Chandler</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Commissioner_of_Baseball" title="Commissioner of Baseball">Commissioner of Baseball</a>, for actions in his personal life, leaving the Dodgers without a manager to start the regular season, <a href="/wiki/Burt_Shotton" title="Burt Shotton">Burt Shotton</a> agrees to manage the team.</p>
<p>In a game against the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a>, manager <a href="/wiki/Ben_Chapman_(baseball)" title="Ben Chapman (baseball)">Ben Chapman</a> taunts Robinson, causing him to go back to the dugout and smash his bat out of frustration. With encouragement from Rickey, Robinson then returns to the field and hits a single, steals second base and advances to third on a throwing error, and scores the winning run. When Chapman's behavior toward Robinson generates bad press for the team, the Phillies' owner requires him to pose with Robinson for newspapers and magazine photos.</p>
<p>Later, Robinson's teammate <a href="/wiki/Pee_Wee_Reese" title="Pee Wee Reese">Pee Wee Reese</a> comes to understand what kind of pressure Robinson is facing, and makes a public show of solidarity, standing with his arm around Robinson's shoulders before a hostile crowd at <a href="/wiki/Crosley_Field" title="Crosley Field">Crosley Field</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati</a>, silencing them.</p>
<p>In a game against the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Enos_Slaughter" title="Enos Slaughter">Enos Slaughter</a> "accidentally" spikes Robinson on the back of the leg with his cleats while running the bases.</p>
<p>Robinson's home run against <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a> pitcher <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Ostermueller" title="Fritz Ostermueller">Fritz Ostermueller</a>, who had earlier hit him in the head, helps clinch the National League pennant for the Dodgers, sending them to the <a href="/wiki/1947_World_Series" title="1947 World Series">World Series</a>, which they would lose to the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a>. A concluding postscript describes how Rickey, Robinson, and many of his teammates went on to have distinguished careers, including inductions into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" title="Baseball Hall of Fame">Baseball Hall of Fame</a>, besides Chapman, who was fired and forbidden to join an MLB team forever due to his racism. The notes also describe the entrance of other African Americans into the Major Leagues, beginning with the season after Robinson's debut.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=42_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Spike_Lee" title="Spike Lee">Spike Lee</a> planned to write and direct a film based on the life of Jackie Robinson and had it set up at <a href="/wiki/Turner_Entertainment" title="Turner Entertainment">Turner Pictures</a> under his <a href="/wiki/40_Acres_and_a_Mule_Filmworks" title="40 Acres and a Mule Filmworks">40 Acres and a Mule Filmworks</a> in 1995. The studio wanted to release it in 1997 to coincide with the 50th anniversary of Robinson's breaking of the color barrier, and courted <a href="/wiki/Denzel_Washington" title="Denzel Washington">Denzel Washington</a> to star,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> but the project fell apart in 1996 over creative differences. In March 1997 Lee found favor with Columbia Pictures, who signed him to a three-year first-look deal. Columbia President <a href="/wiki/Amy_Pascal" title="Amy Pascal">Amy Pascal</a> reflected that it would bring "enormous potential for Spike to reach audiences that are not traditionally associated with Spike Lee movies."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The project eventually fell apart, but in 2004 <a href="/wiki/Robert_Redford" title="Robert Redford">Robert Redford</a> set up a Jackie Robinson biopic as producer with Deep River Productions, as well as his own production company, Wildwood Productions. Redford also intended to co-star as Branch Rickey,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Howard_Baldwin" title="Howard Baldwin">Howard Baldwin</a> joined as producer the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In June 2011 it was announced that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legendary_Pictures" title="Legendary Pictures">Legendary Pictures</a> would develop and produce a Jackie Robinson biopic with <a href="/wiki/Brian_Helgeland" title="Brian Helgeland">Brian Helgeland</a> on board to write and direct, under a distribution deal with Warner Bros. Legendary collaborated with Robinson’s widow, Rachel Robinson, to ensure the authenticity of her husband’s story. She had previously been involved with Redford's project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>42</i> was filmed primarily in <a href="/wiki/Macon,_Georgia" title="Macon, Georgia">Macon, Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham, Alabama</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chattanooga,_Tennessee" title="Chattanooga, Tennessee">Chattanooga, Tennessee</a>. Some interior scenes were shot at Atlanta Film Studios Paulding County in <a href="/wiki/Hiram,_Georgia" title="Hiram, Georgia">Hiram, Georgia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReelGA_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReelGA-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of the interior stadium shots were filmed at <a href="/wiki/Engel_Stadium" title="Engel Stadium">Engel Stadium</a> in Chattanooga, Tennessee. Utilizing old photographs and stadium blueprints, <a href="/wiki/Ebbets_Field" title="Ebbets Field">Ebbets Field</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shibe_Park" title="Shibe Park">Shibe Park</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Polo_Grounds" title="The Polo Grounds">The Polo Grounds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crosley_Field" title="Crosley Field">Crosley Field</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Forbes_Field" title="Forbes Field">Forbes Field</a> were recreated for the film using <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">digital imagery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Film_wizard_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Film_wizard-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=42_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=42_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>42</i> received generally positive reviews from critics. On <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, the film has a rating of 79%, based on 178 reviews, with an average rating of 6.8/10. The site's critical consensus reads, "<i>42</i> is an earnest, inspirational, and respectfully told biography of an influential American sports icon, though it might be a little too safe and old-fashioned for some." On <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, the film holds a score of 62 out of 100, based on 39 critics, indicating "generally favorable reviews".</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Richard_Roeper" title="Richard Roeper">Richard Roeper</a> stated, "This is a competent but mostly unexceptional film about a most extraordinary man."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Lisa Kennedy, of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denver_Post" title="Denver Post">Denver Post</a>, lauded the film, saying "This story inspires and entertains with a vital chapter in this nation's history."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Conversely, Peter Rainer, of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Christian_Science_Monitor" title="The Christian Science Monitor">The Christian Science Monitor</a></i>, criticized the film as "TV-movie-of-the-week dull and Robinson's ordeal is hammered home to the exclusion of virtually everything else in his life."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film's actors were generally praised, with <a href="/wiki/Owen_Gleiberman" title="Owen Gleiberman">Owen Gleiberman</a> saying of Ford, "He gives an ingeniously stylized cartoon performance, his eyes atwinkle, his mouth a rubbery grin, his voice all wily Southern music, though with that growl of Fordian anger just beneath it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a> commented that Boseman "has the necessary appeal, proves convincing as an athlete and is expressive in spite of the fact that the man he's playing must mostly keep his true feelings bottled up."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jackie Robinson's widow, <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Robinson" title="Rachel Robinson">Rachel Robinson</a>, was involved in the production of the film and has praised the end result, saying, "It was important to me because I wanted it to be an authentic piece. I wanted to get it right. I didn’t want them to make him an angry black man or some stereotype, so it was important for me to be in there . . . I love the movie. I’m pleased with it. It’s authentic and it’s also very powerful."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=42_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film earned $27.3 million for its opening weekend, the best premiere for a baseball-themed film in Hollywood history. Only released in the US and Canada, the film went on to gross a total of $95,020,213.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=42_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>42</i> was released on <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray</a> on July 16, 2013 in America, and on Feb. 3rd, 2014 in the U.K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_inaccuracies">Historical inaccuracies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=42_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Historical inaccuracies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The scene of Robinson breaking his bat in the dugout tunnel is not based in fact. Both Rachel Robinson and <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Branca" title="Ralph Branca">Ralph Branca</a>, film consultant and Dodger pitcher in the dugout that day, say it did not happen. Director Helgeland concurs, explaining that his justification for including the scene was that he felt "there was no way Robinson could have withstood all that abuse without cracking at least once, even if it was in private."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Red_Barber" title="Red Barber">Red Barber</a> would not have broadcast Dodger away games from the opposing team's ballpark in Philadelphia and Cincinnati, as shown in the film. Radio broadcasts of away games in this era were recreated back at the studio from a pitch by pitch summary transmitted over telegraph wire from the stadium where the game was being played.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wendell_Smith" title="Wendell Smith">Wendell Smith</a> is said to have been the first black member of the Baseball Writers Association of America; <a href="/wiki/Sam_Lacy" title="Sam Lacy">Sam Lacy</a> was the first, having joined in 1948.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pirates pitcher <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Ostermueller" title="Fritz Ostermueller">Fritz Ostermueller</a> threw left-handed, not right-handed as in the film. His first inning pitch hit Robinson on the left wrist, not his head, and he claimed it was a routine <a href="/wiki/Brushback_pitch" title="Brushback pitch">brushback pitch</a> without racist intent. There was no fight on the mound afterwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jopglo130505_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-jopglo130505-28">[28]</a></sup> The climactic scene in which Robinson hit a home run to clinch the National League pennant for the Dodgers came in the top of the fourth inning of the game, did not clinch the victory (it made the score 1–0, and the Dodgers eventually won 4–2), and did not clinch the pennant; the Dodgers clinched at least a tie for the pennant on that day before clinching the pennant the next day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
