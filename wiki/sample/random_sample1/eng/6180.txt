<p><b>Rebellion</b>, <b>uprising</b>, or <b>insurrection</b> is a refusal of obedience or order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It refers to the open resistance against the orders of an established <a href="/wiki/Authority" title="Authority">authority</a>. The term comes from the Latin verb <i>rebellō,</i> "I renew war" (from <i>re-</i> ("again") + <i>bellō</i> ("I wage war/I revolt"). The rebel is the individual that partakes in rebellion or rebellious activities, particularly when armed. Thus, the term <i>rebellion</i> also refers to the ensemble of rebels in a state of revolt.</p>
<p>A rebellion originates from a sentiment of indignation and disapproval of a situation and then manifests itself by the refusal to submit or to obey the authority responsible for this situation. Rebellion can be individual or collective, <a href="/wiki/Peace" title="Peace">peaceful</a> (<a href="/wiki/Civil_disobedience" title="Civil disobedience">civil disobedience</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_resistance" title="Civil resistance">civil resistance</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nonviolent_resistance" title="Nonviolent resistance">nonviolent resistance</a>) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Violent" title="Violent">violent</a> (<a href="/wiki/Terrorism" title="Terrorism">terrorism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sabotage" title="Sabotage">sabotage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_warfare" title="Guerrilla warfare">guerrilla warfare</a>.)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In political terms, rebellion and revolt are often distinguished by their different aims. If rebellion generally seeks to evade an oppressive power, a revolt seeks to overthrow and destroy that power, as well as its accompanying laws. The goal of rebellion is resistance while a <b>revolt</b> seeks a revolution.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As power shifts relative to the external adversary, or power shifts within a mixed <a href="/wiki/Coalition" title="Coalition">coalition</a>, or positions harden or soften on either side, an insurrection may seesaw between the two forms.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes_of_rebellion">Causes of rebellion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Causes of rebellion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Macro_approach">Macro approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Macro approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following theories broadly build on the Marxist interpretation of rebellion. They explore the causes of rebellion from a wide lens perspective.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Rebellion is studied, in Theda Skocpol's words, by analyzing "objective relationships and conflicts among variously situated groups and nations, rather than the interests, outlooks, or ideologies of particular actors in revolutions".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESkocpol1979291_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESkocpol1979291-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Marxist_insight">Marxist insight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marxist insight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a>'s analysis of revolutions sees such expression of <a href="/wiki/Political_violence" title="Political violence">political violence</a> not as anomic, episodic outbursts of discontents but rather the symptomatic expression of a particular set of objective but fundamentally contradicting class-based relations of power. Indeed, the central tenet of Marxist philosophy, as expressed in <a href="/wiki/Capital:_Critique_of_Political_Economy" title="Capital: Critique of Political Economy">Capital</a>, is the analysis of society's mode of production (technology and labor) concomitant with the ownership of productive institutions and the division of profit. Marx writes about "the hidden structure of society" that must be elucidated through an examination of "the direct relationship of the owners of the conditions of production to the direct producers". The mismatch, between one mode of production, between the social forces and the social ownership of the production, is at the origin of the revolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESkocpol19797_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESkocpol19797-3">[3]</a></sup> The inner imbalance within these modes of production is derived from the conflicting modes of organization, such as capitalism within feudalism, or more appropriately socialism within capitalism. The dynamics engineered by these class frictions help class consciousness root itself in the collective imaginary. For example, the development of the bourgeoisie class went from oppressed merchant class to urban independence, eventually gaining enough power to represent the state as a whole. Social movements, thus, are determined by an exogenous set of circumstances. The proletariat must also, according to Marx, go through the same process of self-determination which can only be achieved by friction against the bourgeoisie. In Marx's theory revolutions are the "locomotives of history", it is because rebellion has for ultimate goal to overthrow the ruling class and its antiquated mode of production. Later, rebellion attempts to replace it with a new system of political economy, one that is better suited to the new ruling class, thus enabling societal progress. The cycle of rebellion, thus, replaces one mode of production by another through the constant class friction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESkocpol19798_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESkocpol19798-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ted_Gurr:_Roots_of_political_violence">Ted Gurr: Roots of political violence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ted Gurr: Roots of political violence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In his book <i>Why Men Rebel</i>, <a href="/wiki/Ted_Robert_Gurr" title="Ted Robert Gurr">Ted Gurr</a> looks at the roots of political violence itself applied to a rebellion framework. He defines political violence as: "all collective attacks within a political community against the political <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_regime" title="Political regime">regime</a>, its actors [...] or its policies. The concept represents a set of events, a common property of which is the actual or threatened use of violence".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGurr19703_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGurr19703-5">[5]</a></sup> Gurr sees in violence a voice of anger that manifests itself against the established order. More precisely, individuals become angry when they feel what Gurr labels as <a href="/wiki/Relative_deprivation_thesis" title="Relative deprivation thesis">relative deprivation</a>, meaning the feeling of getting less that one is entitled to. He labels it formally as the "perceived discrepancy between value expectations and value capabilities".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGurr197037_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGurr197037-6">[6]</a></sup> Gurr differentiates between three types of relative deprivation:</p>
<ol>
<li><i>Decremental deprivation</i>: one's capacities' decrease when expectations remain high. One example of this is the proliferation and thus depreciation of the value of higher education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGurr197047_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGurr197047-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Aspirational Deprivation</i>: one's capacities stay the same when expectations rise. An example would be a first generation college student lacking the contacts and network to obtain a higher paying job while watching her better-prepared colleagues bypass her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGurr197052_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGurr197052-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Progressive deprivation</i>: expectation and capabilities increase but the former cannot keep up. A good example would be an automotive worker being increasingly marginalized by the automatisation of the assembly line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGurr197053_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGurr197053-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>Anger is thus comparative. One of his key insight is that "The potential for collective violence varies strongly with the intensity and scope of relative deprivation among members of a collectivity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGurr197024_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGurr197024-10">[10]</a></sup> This means that different individuals within society will have different propensities to rebel based on their particular internalization of their situation. As such, Gurr differentiates between three types of political violence:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGurr197011_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGurr197011-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li><i>Turmoil</i> when only the mass population encounters relative deprivation;</li>
<li><i>Conspiracy</i> when the population but especially the elite encounters relative deprivation;</li>
<li><i>Internal War</i>, which includes revolution. In this case, the degree of organization is much higher than turmoil, and the revolution is intrinsically spread to all sections of society, unlike the conspiracy.</li>
</ol>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Charles_Tilly:_Centrality_of_collective_action">Charles Tilly: Centrality of collective action</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Charles Tilly: Centrality of collective action">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <i>From Mobilization to Revolution</i>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Tilly" title="Charles Tilly">Charles Tilly</a> argues that political violence is a normal and endogenous reaction to competition for power between different groups within society. "Collective violence", Tilly writes, "is the product of just normal processes of competition among groups in order to obtain the power and implicitly to fulfill their desires”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETilly197854_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETilly197854-12">[12]</a></sup> He proposes two models to analyze political violence:</p>
<ol>
<li>The <i>polity</i> model takes into account government and groups jockeying for control over power. Thus, both the organizations holding power and the ones challenging them are included.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETilly1978ch3_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETilly1978ch3-13">[13]</a></sup> Tilly labels those two groups "members" and "challengers".</li>
<li>The <i>mobilization</i> model aims to describe the behavior of one single party to the political struggle for power. Tilly further divides the model in two sub-categories, one that deals with the internal dynamics of the group, and the other that is concerned with the "external relations" of the entity with other organizations and/or the government. According to Tilly, the cohesiveness of a group mainly relies on the strength of common interests and the degree of organization. Thus, to answer Gurr, anger alone does not automatically create political violence. Political action is contingent on the capacity to organize and unite. It is far from irrational and spontaneous.</li>
</ol>
<p>Revolutions are included in this theory, although they remain for Tilly particularly extreme since the challenger(s) aim for nothing less than full control over power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETilly1978ch7_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETilly1978ch7-14">[14]</a></sup> The "revolutionary moment occurs when the population needs to choose to obey either the government or an alternative body who is engaged with the government in a zero sum game. This is what Tilly calls "multiple sovereignty".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETilly1978213_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETilly1978213-15">[15]</a></sup> The success of a revolutionary movement hinges on "the formation of coalitions between members of the polity and the contenders advancing exclusive alternative claims to control over Government.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETilly1978213_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETilly1978213-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Chalmers_Johnson_and_societal_values">Chalmers Johnson and societal values</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chalmers Johnson and societal values">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>For Chalmers Johnson, rebellions are not so much the product of political violence or collective action but in "the analysis of viable, functioning societies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJohnson19663_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJohnson19663-16">[16]</a></sup> In a quasi-biological manner, Johnson sees revolutions as symptoms of pathologies within the societal fabric. A healthy society, meaning a "value-coordinated social system"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJohnson196636_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJohnson196636-17">[17]</a></sup> does not experience political violence. Johnson's equilibrium is at the intersection between the need for society adapt to changes but at the same time firmly grounded in selective fundamental values. The legitimacy of a political order, he posits, relies exclusively on its compliance with these societal values and in its capacity to integrate and adapt to any change. Rigidity is, in other words, inadmissible. Johnson writes "to make a revolution is to accept violence for the purpose of causing the system to change; more exactly, it is the purposive implementation of a strategy of violence in order to effect a change in social structure".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJohnson196657_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJohnson196657-18">[18]</a></sup> The aim of a revolution is to re-align a political order on new societal values introduced by an externality that the system itself has not been able to process. Rebellions automatically must face a certain amount of coercion because by becoming "de-synchronized", the now illegitimate political order will have to use coercion to maintain its position. A simplified example would be the French Revolution when the Parisian Bourgeoisie did not recognize the core values and outlook of the King as synchronized with its own orientations. More than the King itself, what really sparked the violence was the uncompromising intransigence of the ruling class. Johnson emphasizes "the necessity of investigating a system's value structure and its problems in order to conceptualize the revolutionary situation in any meaningful way".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJohnson196632_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJohnson196632-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Theda_Skocpol_and_the_Autonomy_of_the_State">Theda Skocpol and the Autonomy of the State</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Theda Skocpol and the Autonomy of the State">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Skocpol introduces the concept of the social revolution, to be contrasted with a political revolution. While the later aims to change the polity, the former is "rapid, basic transformations of a society's state and class structures; and they are accompanied and in part carried through by class-based revolts from below".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESkocpol19794_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESkocpol19794-20">[20]</a></sup> Social revolutions are grassroots movement by nature because they do more than change the modalities of power, they aim to transform the fundamental social structure of society. As a corollary, this means that some "revolutions" may cosmetically change the organization of the monopoly over power without engineering any true change in the social fabric of society. Her analysis is limited to studying the French, Russian, and Chinese revolutions. Skocpol identifies three stages of the revolution in these cases (which she believes can be extrapolated and generalized), each accordingly accompanied by specific structural factors which in turn influence the social results of the political action.</p>
<ol>
<li><i>The Collapse of the Old-Regime State</i>: this is an automatic consequence of certain structural conditions. She highlights the importance of international military and economic competition as well as the pressure of the misfunctioning of domestic affairs. More precisely, she sees the breakdown of the governing structures of society influenced by two theoretical actors, the "landed upper class" and the "imperial state".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESkocpol197949_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESkocpol197949-21">[21]</a></sup> Both could be considered as "partners in exploitation" but in reality competed for resources: the state (monarchs) seek to build up military and economic power to ascertain their geopolitical influence. The upper class works in a logic of profit maximization, meaning preventing as much as possible the state to extract resources. All three revolutions occurred, Skocpol argues, because states failed to be able to "mobilize extraordinary resources from the society and implement in the process reforms requiring structural transformations".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESkocpol197950_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESkocpol197950-22">[22]</a></sup> The apparently contradicting policies were mandated by a unique set of geopolitical competition and modernization. "Revolutionary political crises occurred because of the unsuccessful attempts of the Bourbon, Romanov, and Manchu regimes to cope with foreign pressures."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESkocpol197950_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESkocpol197950-22">[22]</a></sup> Skocpol further concludes "the upshot was the disintegration of centralized administrative and military machineries that had theretofore provided the sole unified bulwark of social and political order".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESkocpol197951_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESkocpol197951-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Peasant Uprisings</i>: more than simply a challenge by the landed upper class in a difficult context, the state needs to be challenged by mass peasant uprisings in order to fall. These uprisings must be aimed not at the political structures <i>per se</i> but at the upper class itself, so that the political revolution becomes a social one as well. Skocpol quotes Barrington Moore who famously wrote: "peasants [...] provided the dynamite to bring down the old building".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESkocpol1979112_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESkocpol1979112-24">[24]</a></sup> Peasant uprisings are more effective depending on two given structural socioeconomic conditions: the level of autonomy (from both an economic and political point of view) peasant communities enjoy, and the degree of direct control the upper class on local politics. In other words, peasants must be able to have some degree of agency in order to be able to rebel. If the coercive structures of the state and/or the landowners keep a very close check on peasant activity, then there is no space to forment dissent.</li>
<li><i>Societal Transformation</i>: this is the third and decisive step after the state organization has been seriously weakened and peasant revolts become widespread against landlords. The paradox of the three revolutions Skocpol studies is that stronger centralized and bureaucratic states emerge after the revolts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESkocpol1979162_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESkocpol1979162-25">[25]</a></sup> The exact parameters depend, again, on structural factors as opposed to voluntarist factors: in Russia, the new state found most support in the industrial base, rooting itself in cities. In China, most of the support for the revolt had been in the countryside, thus the new polity was grounded in rural areas. In France, the peasantry was not organized enough, and the urban centers not potent enough so that the new state was not firmly grounded in anything, partially explaining its artificiality.</li>
</ol>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beyond_Structural_Causes">Beyond Structural Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Beyond Structural Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Stathis N. Kalyvas, a political science professor at Yale University, argues that political violence is heavily influenced by hyperlocal socio-economic factors, from the mundane traditional family rivalries to repressed grudges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003476_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003476-27">[27]</a></sup> Rebellion, or any sort of political violence, are not binary conflicts but must be understood as interactions between public and private identities and actions. The "convergence of local motives and supralocal imperatives" make studying and theorizing rebellion a very complex affair, at the intersection between the political and the private, the collective and the individual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003475_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003475-28">[28]</a></sup> Kalyvas argues that we often try to group political conflicts according to two structural paradigms:</p>
<ol>
<li>The idea that political violence, and more specifically rebellion, is characterized by a complete breakdown of authority and an anarchic state. This is inspired by Thomas Hobbes' views. The approach sees rebellion as being motivated by greed and loot, using violence to break down the power structures of society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003476_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003476-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li>The idea that all political violence is inherently motivated by an abstract group of loyalties and beliefs, "whereby the political enemy becomes a private adversary only by virtue of prior collective and impersonal enmity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003476_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003476-27">[27]</a></sup> Violence is thus not a "man to man" affair as much as a :state to state" struggle, if not an "idea vs idea" conflict.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003476_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003476-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>Kalyvas' key insight is that the central vs periphery dynamic is fundamental in political conflicts. Any individual actor, Kalyvas posits, enters into a calculated <i>alliance</i> with the collective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003486_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003486-29">[29]</a></sup> Rebellions thus cannot be analyzed in molar categories, nor should we assume that individuals are automatically in line with the rest of the actors simply by virtue of ideological, religious, ethnic, or class cleavage. The agency is located both within the collective and in the individual, in the universal and the local.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003486_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003486-29">[29]</a></sup> Kalyvas writes: "Alliance entails a <i>transaction</i> between supralocal and local actors, whereby the former supply the later with external muscle, thus allowing them to win decisive local advantage, in exchange the former rely on local conflicts to recruit and motivate supporters and obtain local control, resources, and information- even when their ideological agenda is opposed to localism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003486_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003486-29">[29]</a></sup> Individuals will thus aim to use the rebellion in order to gain some sort of local advantage, while the collective actors will aim to gain power. Violence is a mean as opposed to a goal, according to Kalyvas.</p>
<p>The greater takeaway from this central/local analytical lens is that violence is not an anarchic tactic or a manipulation by an ideology, but a conversation between the two. Rebellions are "concatenations of multiple and often disparate local cleavages, more or less loosely arranged around the master cleavage".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003486_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003486-29">[29]</a></sup> Any pre-conceived explanation or theory of a conflict must not be placated on a situation, lest one will construct a reality that adapts itself to his pre-conceived idea. Kalyvas thus argues that political conflict is not always political in the sense that they cannot be reduced to a certain discourse, decisions, or ideologies from the "center" of collective action. Instead, the focus must be on "local cleavages and intracommunity dynamics".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003487_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003487-30">[30]</a></sup> Furthermore, rebellion is not "a mere mechanism that opens up the floodgates to random and anarchical private violence".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003487_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKalyvas2003487-30">[30]</a></sup> Rather, it is the result of a careful and precarious alliance between local motivations and collective vectors to help the individual cause.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microfoundational_Evidence_on_the_Causes_of_Rebellion">Microfoundational Evidence on the Causes of Rebellion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Microfoundational Evidence on the Causes of Rebellion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following theories are all based on <a href="/wiki/Mancur_Olson" title="Mancur Olson">Mancur Olson</a>'s work in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Logic_of_Collective_Action" title="The Logic of Collective Action">The Logic of Collective Action</a></i>,a 1965 book that conceptualizes the inherent <a href="/wiki/Tragedy_of_the_commons" title="Tragedy of the commons">problem</a> with an activity that has concentrated costs and diffuse benefits. In this case, the benefits of rebellion are seen as a <a href="/wiki/Public_good" title="Public good">public good</a>, meaning one that is non-excludable and non-rivalrous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOlson19659_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOlson19659-31">[31]</a></sup> Indeed, the political benefits are generally shared by all in society if a rebellion is successful, not just the individuals that have partaken in the rebellion itself. Olson thus challenges the assumption that simple interests in common are all that is necessary for <a href="/wiki/Collective_action_theory" title="Collective action theory">collective action</a>. In fact, he argues the "<a href="/wiki/Free_rider_problem" title="Free rider problem">free rider</a>" possibility, a term that means to reap the benefits without paying the price, will deter rational individuals from collective action. That is, unless there is a clear benefit, a rebellion will not happen en masse. Thus, Olson shows that "selective incentives", only made accessible to individuals participating in the collective effort, can solve the free rider problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOlson196576_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOlson196576-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Rational_Individual_:_the_Political_Economy_Argument">The Rational Individual : the Political Economy Argument</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The Rational Individual : the Political Economy Argument">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Rational_Peasant:_The_Political_Economy_of_Rural_Society_in_Vietnam" title="The Rational Peasant: The Political Economy of Rural Society in Vietnam">The Rational Peasant: The Political Economy of Rural Society in Vietnam</a></div>
<p>Samuel L. Popkin builds on Olson's argument in <i>The Rational Peasant: The Political Economy of Rural Society in Vietnam.</i> His theory is based on the figure of a hyper rational peasant that bases his decision to join (or not) a rebellion uniquely on a cost-benefit analysis. This formalist view of the collective action problem stresses the importance of individual economic rationality and self-interest: a peasant, according to Popkin, will disregard the ideological dimension of a social movement and focus instead on whether or not it will bring any practical benefit to him. According to Popkin, peasant society is based on a precarious structure of economic instability. Social norms, he writes, are "malleable, renegotiated, and shifting in accord with considerations of power and strategic interaction among individuals"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPopkin197922_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPopkin197922-33">[33]</a></sup> Indeed, the constant insecurity and inherent risk to the peasant condition, due to the peculiar nature of the patron-client relationship that binds the peasant to his landowner, forces the peasant to look inwards when he has a choice to make. Popkin argues that peasants rely on their "private, family investment for their long run security and that they will be interested in short term gain vis-à-vis the village. They will attempt to improve their long-run security by moving to a position with higher income and less variance".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPopkin197923_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPopkin197923-34">[34]</a></sup> Popkin stresses this "investor logic" that one may not expect in agrarian societies, usually seen as pre-capitalist communities where traditional social and power structures prevent the accumulation of capital. Yet, the selfish determinants of collective action are, according to Popkin, a direct product of the inherent instability of peasant life. The goal of a laborer, for example, will be to move to a tenant position, then smallholder, then landlord; where there is less variance and more income. Voluntarism is thus non-existent in such communities.</p>
<p>Popkin singles out four variables that impact individual participation:</p>
<ol>
<li>Contribution to the expenditure of resources: collective action has a cost in terms of contribution, and especially if it fails (an important consideration with regards to rebellion)</li>
<li>Rewards : the direct (more income) and indirect (less oppressive central state) rewards for collective action</li>
<li>Marginal impact of the peasant's contribution to the success of collective action</li>
<li>Leadership "viability and trust" : to what extent the resources pooled will be effectively used.</li>
</ol>
<p>Without any moral commitment to the community, this situation will engineer free riders. Popkin argues that selective incentives are necessary to overcome this problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPopkin197934_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPopkin197934-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="The_opportunity_cost_of_rebellion">The opportunity cost of rebellion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The opportunity cost of rebellion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Political Scientist Christopher Blattman and World Bank economist Laura Alston identify rebellious activity as an "occupational choice".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlattman_and_Rason201522_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlattman_and_Rason201522-36">[36]</a></sup> They draw a parallel between criminal activity and rebellion, arguing that the risks and potential payoffs an individual must calculate when making the decision to join such a movement remains similar between the two activities. In both cases, only a selected few reap important benefits, while most of the members of the group do not receive similar payoffs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlattman_and_Rason201523_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlattman_and_Rason201523-37">[37]</a></sup> The choice to rebel is inherently linked with its opportunity cost, namely what an individual is ready to give up in order to rebel. Thus, the available options beside rebellious or criminal activity matter just as much as the rebellion itself when the individual makes the decision. Blattman and Alston, however, recognize that "a poor person's best strategy" might be both rebellion illicit and legitimate activities at the same time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlattman_and_Rason201523_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlattman_and_Rason201523-37">[37]</a></sup> Individuals, they argue, can often have a varied "portofolio" of activities, suggesting that they all operate on a rational, profit maximizing logic. The authors conclude that the best way to fight rebellion is to increase its opportunity cost, both by more enforcement but also by minimizing the potential material gains of a rebellion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlattman_and_Rason201523_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlattman_and_Rason201523-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Selective_incentives_based_on_group_membership">Selective incentives based on group membership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Selective incentives based on group membership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The decision to join a rebellion can be based on the prestige and social status associated with membership in the rebellious group. More than material incentives for the individual, rebellions offer their members <a href="/wiki/Club_good" title="Club good">club goods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Public_good" title="Public good">public goods</a> that are reserved only for the members inside that group. Economist <a href="/wiki/Eli_Berman" title="Eli Berman">Eli Berman</a> and Political Scientist David D. Laitin's study of radical religious groups show that the appeal of club goods can help explain individual membership. Berman and Laitin discuss <a href="/wiki/Suicide_attack" title="Suicide attack">suicide</a> operations, meaning acts that have the highest cost for an individual. They find that in such a framework, the real danger to an organization is not volunteering but preventing defection. Furthermore, the decision to enroll in such high stakes organization can be rationalized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerman_and_Laitin20081965_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerman_and_Laitin20081965-38">[38]</a></sup> Berman and Laitin show that religious organizations supplant the state when it fails to provide an acceptable quality of public goods such a public safety, basic infrastructure, access to utilities, or schooling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerman_and_Laitin20081944_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerman_and_Laitin20081944-39">[39]</a></sup> Suicide operations "can be explained as a costly signal of “commitment” to the community".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerman_and_Laitin20081943_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerman_and_Laitin20081943-40">[40]</a></sup> They further note "Groups less adept at extracting signals of commitment (sacrifices) may not be able to consistently enforce incentive compatibility."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerman_and_Laitin20081954_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerman_and_Laitin20081954-41">[41]</a></sup> Thus, rebellious groups can organize themselves to <i>ask</i> of members proof of commitment to the cause. Club goods serve not so much to coax individuals into joining but to prevent defection.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="The_greed_vs_grievance_model">The greed vs grievance model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: The greed vs grievance model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Greed_versus_grievance" title="Greed versus grievance">Greed versus grievance</a></div>
<p>World Bank economists <a href="/wiki/Paul_Collier" title="Paul Collier">Paul Collier</a> and Anke Hoeffler compare two dimensions of incentives:</p>
<ol>
<li><a href="/wiki/Greed" title="Greed">Greed</a> rebellion: "motivated by predation of the rents from primary commodity exports, subject to an economic calculus of costs and a military survival constraint".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollier_and_Hoeffler200226_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollier_and_Hoeffler200226-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Grievance" title="Grievance">Grievance</a> rebellion: "motivated by hatreds which might be intrinsic to ethnic and religious differences, or reflected objective resentments such as domination by an <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnic</a> majority, political repression, or <a href="/wiki/Economic_inequality" title="Economic inequality">economic inequality</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollier_and_Hoeffler200226_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollier_and_Hoeffler200226-42">[42]</a></sup> The two main sources of grievance are political exclusion and inequality.</li>
</ol>
<p>Vollier and Hoeffler find that the model based on grievance variables systematically fails to predict past conflicts, while the model based on greed performs well. The authors posit that the high cost of risk to society is not taken into account seriously by the grievance model: individuals are fundamentally risk-adverse. However, they allow that conflicts create grievances, which in turn can become risk factors. Contrary to established beliefs, they also find that a multiplicity of ethnic communities make society safer, since individuals will be automatically more cautious, at the opposite of the grievance model predictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollier_and_Hoeffler200226_42-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollier_and_Hoeffler200226-42">[42]</a></sup> Finally, the authors also note that the grievances expressed by members of the <a href="/wiki/Diaspora" title="Diaspora">diaspora</a> of a community in turmoil has an important on the continuation of violence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollier_and_Hoeffler200227_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollier_and_Hoeffler200227-43">[43]</a></sup> Both greed and grievance thus need to be included in the reflection.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Moral_Individual:_the_Moral_Economy_Argument">The Moral Individual: the Moral Economy Argument</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: The Moral Individual: the Moral Economy Argument">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Moral_Economy_of_the_Peasant:_Rebellion_and_Subsistence_in_Southeast_Asia" title="The Moral Economy of the Peasant: Rebellion and Subsistence in Southeast Asia">The Moral Economy of the Peasant: Rebellion and Subsistence in Southeast Asia</a></div>
<p>Spearheaded by political scientist and anthropologist <a href="/wiki/James_C._Scott" title="James C. Scott">James C. Scott</a> in his book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Moral_Economy_of_the_Peasant:_Rebellion_and_Subsistence_in_Southeast_Asia" title="The Moral Economy of the Peasant: Rebellion and Subsistence in Southeast Asia">The Moral Economy of the Peasant</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Moral_economy" title="Moral economy">moral economy</a> school considers moral variables such as social norms, moral values, interpretation of justice, and conception of duty to the community as the prime influencers of the decision to rebel. This perspective still adheres to Olson's framework, but it considers different variables to enter the cost/benefit analysis: the individual is still believed to be rational, albeit not on material but moral grounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEScott19766_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEScott19766-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_conceptualization:_E._P._Thompson_and_bread_riots_in_England">Early conceptualization: E. P. Thompson and bread riots in England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Early conceptualization: E. P. Thompson and bread riots in England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Before being fully conceptualized by Scott, British historian <a href="/wiki/E._P._Thompson" title="E. P. Thompson">E.P. Thompson</a> was the first to use the term "moral economy" in <i>Moral Economy of the English Crowd in the Eighteenth Century</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> In this work, he discussed English bread riots, regular, localized form of rebellion by English peasants all through the 18th century. Such events, Thompson argues, have been routinely dismissed as "riotous", with the connotation of being disorganized, spontaneous, undirected, and undisciplined. In other words, anecdotal. The reality, he suggests, was otherwise: such riots involved a coordinated peasant action, from the pillaging of food convoys to the seizure of grain shops. Here, while a scholar such as Popkin would have argued that the peasants were trying to gain material benefits (crudely: more food), Thompson sees a legitimization factor, meaning "a belief that [the peasants] were defending traditional rights and customs". Thompson goes on to write: "[the riots were] legitimized by the assumptions of an older moral economy, which taught the immorality of any unfair method of forcing up the price of provisions by profiteering upon the necessities of the people". Later, reflecting on this work, Thompson would also write: "My object of analysis was the <i>mentalité</i>, or, as I would prefer, the political culture, the expectations, traditions, and indeed, superstitions of the working population most frequently involved in actions in the market".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The opposition between a traditional, paternalist, and the communitarian set of values clashing with the inverse liberal, capitalist, and market-derived ethics is central to explain rebellion.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="James_C._Scott_and_the_formalization_of_the_moral_economy_argument">James C. Scott and the formalization of the moral economy argument</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: James C. Scott and the formalization of the moral economy argument">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Moral_Economy_of_the_Peasant:_Rebellion_and_Subsistence_in_Southeast_Asia" title="The Moral Economy of the Peasant: Rebellion and Subsistence in Southeast Asia">The Moral Economy of Peasant: Rebellion and Subsistence in Southeast Asia</a>, James C. Scott looks at the impact of exogenous economic and political shocks on peasant communities in Southeast Asia. Scott finds that peasants are mostly in the business of surviving and producing enough to subsist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEScott197615_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEScott197615-47">[47]</a></sup> Therefore, any extractive regime needs to respect this careful equilibrium. He labels this phenomenon the "subsistence ethic".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEScott197613_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEScott197613-48">[48]</a></sup> A landowner operating in such communities is seen to have the moral duty to prioritize the peasant's subsistence over his constant benefit. According to Scott, the powerful colonial state accompanied by market capitalism did not respect this fundamental hidden law in peasant societies. Rebellious movements occurred as the reaction to an emotional grief, a moral outrage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEScott1976193_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEScott1976193-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_non_material_incentives">Other non material incentives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Other non material incentives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chris_Blattman" title="Chris Blattman">Blattman</a> and Ralston recognize the importance of immaterial selective incentives, such as anger, outrage, and injustice ("grievance") in the roots of rebellions. These variable,s they argue, are far from being irrational, as they are sometimes presented. They identify three main types of grievance arguments:</p>
<ol>
<li><i>Intrinsic <a href="/wiki/Incentive" title="Incentive">incentives</a></i> holds that "<a href="/wiki/Injustice" title="Injustice">injustice</a> or perceived transgression generates an intrinsic willingness to punish or seek retribution".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlattman_and_Rason201524_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlattman_and_Rason201524-50">[50]</a></sup> More than material rewards, individuals are naturally and automatically prompted to fight for justice if they feel they have been wronged. The <a href="/wiki/Ultimatum_game" title="Ultimatum game">ultimatum game</a> is an excellent illustration: player one receives $10 and must split it with another player who doesn't get the chance to determine how much he receives, but only if the deal is made or not (if he refuses, everyone looses their money). Rationally, player 2 should take whatever the deal is because it is better in absolute term ($1 more remains $1 more). However, player 2 is most likely unwilling to accept less than 2 or 2 dollars, meaning that they are willing to pay a-$2 for justice to be respected. This game, according to Blattman and Ralston, represents "the expressive pleasure people gain from punishing an injustice".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlattman_and_Rason201524_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlattman_and_Rason201524-50">[50]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Loss_aversion" title="Loss aversion">Loss aversion</a></i> holds that "people tend to evaluate their satisfaction relative to a reference point, and that they are 'loss adverse".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlattman_and_Rason201525_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlattman_and_Rason201525-51">[51]</a></sup> Individuals prefer not losing over the risky strategy of making gains. There is a substantial <a href="/wiki/Subjectivity" title="Subjectivity">subjective</a> part to this, however, as some may realize alone and decide that they are comparatively less well off than a neighbor, for example. To "fix" this gap, individuals will in turn be ready to take great risks so as to not enshrine a loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlattman_and_Rason201525_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlattman_and_Rason201525-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Frustration-aggression</i>: this model holds that the immediate emotional reactions to highly stressful environments do not obey to any "direct utility benefit but rather a more impulsive and emotional response to a threat".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlattman_and_Rason201525_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlattman_and_Rason201525-51">[51]</a></sup> There are limits to this theory: violent action is to a large extent a product of goals by an individual which are in turn determined by a set of <a href="/wiki/Preference" title="Preference">preferences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlattman_and_Rason201526_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlattman_and_Rason201526-52">[52]</a></sup> Yet, this approach shows that contextual elements like economic precarity have a non-negligible impact on the conditions of the decisions to rebel at minimum.</li>
</ol>

<p>An armed but limited rebellion is an insurrection,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> and if the established government does not recognize the rebels as <a href="/wiki/Belligerent" title="Belligerent">belligerents</a> then they are insurgents and the revolt is an <a href="/wiki/Insurgency" title="Insurgency">insurgency</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> In a larger conflict the rebels may be recognized as <a href="/wiki/Belligerent" title="Belligerent">belligerents</a> without their government being recognized by the established government, in which case the conflict becomes a <a href="/wiki/Civil_war" title="Civil war">civil war</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Civil_resistance" title="Civil resistance">Civil resistance</a> movements have often aimed at, and brought about, the fall of a government or head of state, and in these cases could be considered a form of rebellion. In many of these cases the opposition movement saw itself not only as nonviolent, but also as upholding their country's constitutional system against a government that was unlawful, for example if it had refused to acknowledge its defeat in an election. Thus the term "rebel" does not always capture the element in some of these movements of acting as a defender of legality and constitutionalism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are a number of terms that are associated with <i>rebel</i> and <i>rebellion</i>. They range from those with positive connotations to those with pejorative connotations. Examples include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Civil_resistance" title="Civil resistance">Civil resistance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_disobedience" title="Civil disobedience">civil disobedience</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nonviolent_resistance" title="Nonviolent resistance">nonviolent resistance</a> which do not include violence or paramilitary force .<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Who defined this term in this way=April 2016 (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mutiny" title="Mutiny">Mutiny</a>, which is carried out by military or security forces against their commanders<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Who defined this term in this way=April 2016 (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Armed <a href="/wiki/Resistance_movement" title="Resistance movement">resistance movement</a>, which is carried out by <a href="/wiki/Resistance_movement#Freedom_fighter" title="Resistance movement">freedom fighters</a>, often against an occupying foreign power<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Who defined this term in this way=April 2016 (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Revolt, a term that is sometimes used for a more localized rebellions rather than a general uprising<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Who defined this term in this way=April 2016 (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Revolution" title="Revolution">Revolution</a>, which is carried out by radicals, usually meant to overthrow the current <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Who defined this term in this way=April 2016 (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Riot" title="Riot">Riot</a>, a form of civil disorder involving violent public disturbance</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Subversion" title="Subversion">Subversion</a>, which are non-overt attempts at sabotaging a government, carried out by spies or other subversives<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Who defined this term in this way=April 2016 (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Terrorism" title="Terrorism">Terrorism</a>, which is carried out by different kinds of political, economic or religious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Militant_(word)" title="Militant (word)">militant</a> individuals or groups<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Who defined this term in this way=April 2016 (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
