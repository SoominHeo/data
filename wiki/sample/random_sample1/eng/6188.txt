<p>In <a href="/wiki/Anatomy" title="Anatomy">anatomy</a>, a <b>meniscus</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BC%CE%B7%CE%BD%CE%AF%CF%83%CE%BA%CE%BF%CF%82" title="wikt:μηνίσκος">μηνίσκος</a> <i>meniskos</i>, "crescent"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) is a crescent-shaped <a href="/wiki/Fibrocartilage" title="Fibrocartilage">fibrocartilaginous</a> structure that, in contrast to an <a href="/wiki/Articular_disk" title="Articular disk">articular disk</a>, only partly divides a <a href="/wiki/Synovial_joint#Structure" title="Synovial joint">joint cavity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Platzer-208_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Platzer-208-2">[2]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a> they are present in the <a href="/wiki/Knee" title="Knee">knee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wrist" title="Wrist">wrist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acromioclavicular_joint" title="Acromioclavicular joint">acromioclavicular</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sternoclavicular_joint" title="Sternoclavicular joint">sternoclavicular</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Temporomandibular_joint" title="Temporomandibular joint">temporomandibular joints</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> in other animals they may be present in other joints.</p>
<p>Generally, the term 'meniscus' is used to refer to the <a href="/wiki/Cartilage" title="Cartilage">cartilage</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Knee" title="Knee">knee</a>, either to the <a href="/wiki/Lateral_meniscus" title="Lateral meniscus">lateral</a> or <a href="/wiki/Medial_meniscus" title="Medial meniscus">medial meniscus</a>. Both are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartilaginous#Fibrocartilage" title="Cartilaginous">cartilaginous</a> tissues that provide structural integrity to the knee when it undergoes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tension_(mechanics)" title="Tension (mechanics)">tension</a> and <a href="/wiki/Torsion_(mechanics)" title="Torsion (mechanics)">torsion</a>. The menisci are also known as "semi-lunar" cartilages — referring to their half-moon, crescent shape.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meniscus_(anatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The menisci of the knee are two pads of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartilaginous#Fibrocartilage" title="Cartilaginous">fibrocartilaginous</a> tissue which serve to disperse friction in the <a href="/wiki/Knee" title="Knee">knee</a> joint between the lower leg (<a href="/wiki/Tibia" title="Tibia">tibia</a>) and the thigh (<a href="/wiki/Femur" title="Femur">femur</a>). They are <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/concave" title="wiktionary:concave">concave</a> on the top and flat on the bottom, articulating with the tibia. They are attached to the small depressions (<a href="/wiki/Fossa_(anatomy)" title="Fossa (anatomy)">fossae</a>) between the <a href="/wiki/Condyle_(anatomy)" title="Condyle (anatomy)">condyles</a> of the tibia (intercondyloid fossa), and towards the center they are unattached and their shape narrows to a thin shelf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gray-7b_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gray-7b-4">[4]</a></sup> The blood flow of the meniscus is from the periphery (outside) to the central meniscus. Blood flow decreases with age and the central meniscus is avascular by adulthood, leading to very poor healing rates.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meniscus_(anatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The menisci act to disperse the weight of the body and reduce friction during movement. Since the condyles of the femur and tibia meet at one point (which changes during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flexion" title="Flexion">flexion</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_anatomical_terms#Types_of_movement" title="Human anatomical terms">extension</a>), the menisci spread the load of the body's weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This differs from <a href="/wiki/Sesamoid_bone" title="Sesamoid bone">sesamoid bones</a>, which are made of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osseous_tissue" title="Osseous tissue">osseous tissue</a> and whose function primarily is to protect the nearby tendon and to increase its mechanical effect.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meniscus_(anatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Clinical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Injury">Injury</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meniscus_(anatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Injury">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tear_of_meniscus" title="Tear of meniscus">Tear of meniscus</a></div>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Sport" title="Sport">sports</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orthopedic_surgery" title="Orthopedic surgery">orthopedics</a>, people will sometimes speak of "torn cartilage" and actually be referring to an injury to one of the menisci. There are two general types of meniscus injuries, acute tears that are often the result of trauma or a sports injury and chronic or wear-and-tear type tears. Acute tears have many different shapes (vertical, horizontal, radial, oblique, complex) and sizes. They are often treated with surgical repair depending upon the patient's age as they rarely heal on their own. Chronic tears are treated symptomatically: physical therapy with or without the addition of injections and anti-inflammatory medications. If the tear causes continued pain, swelling, or knee dysfunction, then the tear can be removed or repaired surgically. The <a href="/wiki/Unhappy_triad" title="Unhappy triad">unhappy triad</a> is a set of commonly co-occurring knee injuries which includes injury to the medial meniscus.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-surgical_treatment">Non-surgical treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meniscus_(anatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Non-surgical treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Non-surgical treatment is often considered first for a smaller or chronic tear that does not appear amenable to surgical repair. It consists of activity modification, physical therapy for strengthening and range of motion, electro-acupuncture.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surgical_treatment">Surgical treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meniscus_(anatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Surgical treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two surgeries of the meniscus are most common. Depending on the type and location of the tear, the patient's age, and physician's preference, injured menisci are usually either repaired or removed, in part or completely (meniscectomy). Each has its advantages and disadvantages. Many studies show the meniscus serves a purpose and therefore doctors will attempt to repair when possible. However, the meniscus has poor blood supply, and, therefore, healing can be difficult. Traditionally it was thought that if there is no chance of healing, then it is best to remove the damaged and non-functional meniscus. This has been found to be wrong in some cases in at least one study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
