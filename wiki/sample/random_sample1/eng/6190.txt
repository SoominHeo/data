<p><b>Anti-racism</b> includes beliefs, actions, movements, and policies adopted or developed to oppose <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a>. In general, anti-racism is intended to promote an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egalitarian" title="Egalitarian">egalitarian</a> society in which people do not face <a href="/wiki/Discrimination" title="Discrimination">discrimination</a> on the basis of their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(classification_of_human_beings)" title="Race (classification of human beings)">race</a>, however defined.</p>
<p>By its nature, anti-racism tends to promote the view that racism in a particular society is both pernicious and socially pervasive, and that particular changes in political, economic, and/or social life are required to eliminate it.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="European_origins">European origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-racism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: European origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European discovery of the Americas by Christopher Columbus did not occur until 1492. However, two <a href="/wiki/Papal_bull" title="Papal bull">Papal bulls</a> announced several decades before that event were designed to help ward off increasing Muslim invasions into Europe, which they believed would have an effect on the New World.</p>
<p>When Islam presented a serious military threat to Italy and Central Europe during the mid-15th century around the time of the fall of Constantinople, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Nicholas_V" title="Pope Nicholas V">Pope Nicholas V</a> tried to unite <a href="/wiki/Christendom" title="Christendom">Christendom</a> against them but failed. He then granted Portugal the right to subdue and even enslave Muslims whether white or any other race, pagans and other non-Christians in the papal bull <i><a href="/wiki/Dum_Diversas" title="Dum Diversas">Dum Diversas</a></i> (1452). While this bull preceded the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">Atlantic slave trade</a> by several decades, slavery and the slave trade were part of African societies and tribes which supplied the Arab world with slaves long before the arrival of the Europeans.</p>
<p>Increasingly, the Italian merchants from the wealthiest states in Italy, especially Genoa and Venice joined in the lucrative trade and some members sported exotic lackeys and few domestic or workshop slaves whereas before slavery was forbidden in Christendom and only formerly in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim_Spain" title="Muslim Spain">Muslim Spain</a> and Sicily and their buffer border marches were seen and legally allowed. Racial views of Superiority started developing and became more acute about these slaves, social views imported from the Court of Granada where they were highly stratified and classified.</p>
<p>The following year saw the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Constantinople" title="Fall of Constantinople">Fall of Constantinople</a> to Muslim conquerors of the ever-growing <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> which left the pope as the undoubted contested leader of Christendom when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthodox_Church" title="Orthodox Church">Orthodox Church</a> leadership became under submission. Several decades later, European explorers and missionaries spread Christianity to the Americas, Asia, Africa and Oceania. <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_VI" title="Pope Alexander VI">Pope Alexander VI</a> had awarded colonial rights over most of the newly discovered lands by the Iberian Kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Castile" title="Kingdom of Castile">Castile</a> and Portugal. Under their <i>patronato</i> system, however, Royal authorities, not the Vatican, controlled as in Europe all clerical appointments in the new colonies. Thus, the 1455 Papal bull <i><a href="/wiki/Romanus_Pontifex" title="Romanus Pontifex">Romanus Pontifex</a></i> granted the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">Portuguese</a> all lands behind <a href="/wiki/Cape_Bojador" title="Cape Bojador">Cape Bojador</a> "allowing to reduce pagans and other enemies of Christ to perpetual servitude."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Later, the 1481 Papal bull <i><a href="/wiki/Aeterni_regis" title="Aeterni regis">Aeterni regis</a></i> granted all lands south of the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> to the Portuguese Empire, while in May 1493 the Aragonese-born Pope Alexander VI decreed in the Bull <i><a href="/wiki/Inter_caetera" title="Inter caetera">Inter caetera</a></i> that all lands west of a meridian only 100 leagues west of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> Islands should belong to the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a> while new lands discovered east of that line would belong to Portugal. These arrangements were later confirmed in the 1494 <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Tordesillas" title="Treaty of Tordesillas">Treaty of Tordesillas</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="American_origins">American origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-racism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: American origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European origins of racism spread to the Americas alongside the Europeans, but establishment views were questioned when applied to indigenous peoples. After the discovery of the New World many of the clergy sent to the New World, educated in the new Humane values of the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> blooming but still new in Europe and not ratified by the Vatican, began to criticize Spain and their own Church's treatment and views of indigenous peoples and slaves.</p>
<p>In December 1511, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_de_Montesinos" title="Antonio de Montesinos">Antonio de Montesinos</a>, a Dominican friar, was the first man to openly rebuke the Spanish authorities and administrators of <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a> for their "cruelty and tyranny" in dealing with the American natives and those forced to labor as slaves. <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">King Ferdinand</a> enacted the <i><a href="/wiki/Laws_of_Burgos" title="Laws of Burgos">Laws of Burgos</a></i> and <i>Valladolid</i> in response. However enforcement was lax, and the <a href="/wiki/New_Laws" title="New Laws">New Laws</a> of 1542 have to be made to take a stronger line. Because some people like Fray <a href="/wiki/Bartolom%C3%A9_de_las_Casas" title="Bartolomé de las Casas">Bartolomé de las Casas</a> questioned not only the Crown but the Papacy at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valladolid_Controversy" title="Valladolid Controversy">Valladolid Controversy</a> whether the Indians were truly men who deserved baptism, Pope Paul III in the papal bull <i>Veritas Ipsa</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Sublimis_Deus" title="Sublimis Deus">Sublimis Deus</a></i> (1537) confirmed that the Indians and other races were deserving men, <i>so long as they became baptized</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johansen110_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johansen110-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koschorke290_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Koschorke290-2">[2]</a></sup> Afterward, their Christian conversion effort gained momentum along social rights, while leaving the same status recognition unanswered for Africans of Black Race, and legal social racism prevailed towards the Indians or Asians. However, by then the last schism of the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a> had taken place in Europe in those few decades along political lines, and the different views on the Value of human lives of different races were not corrected in the lands of Northern Europe, which would join the <a href="/wiki/History_of_colonialism" title="History of colonialism">Colonial race</a> at the end of the century and over the next, as the Portuguese and Spanish Empires waned. It would take another century, with the influence of the <a href="/wiki/French_colonial_empire" title="French colonial empire">French Empire</a> at its height, and its consequent <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> developed at the highest circles of its Court, to return these previously inconclusive issues to the forefront of the political discourse championed by many intellectual men since <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Rousseau" title="Jean-Jacques Rousseau">Rousseau</a>. These issues gradually permeated to the lower social levels, where they were a reality lived by men and women of different races from the European racial majority.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quaker_initiatives">Quaker initiatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-racism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Quaker initiatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1867_JohnBrowns_Blessing_byTNoble_NYHistoricalSociety.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="324" data-file-width="227" height="243" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/1867_JohnBrowns_Blessing_byTNoble_NYHistoricalSociety.png/170px-1867_JohnBrowns_Blessing_byTNoble_NYHistoricalSociety.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/80/1867_JohnBrowns_Blessing_byTNoble_NYHistoricalSociety.png 1.5x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1867_JohnBrowns_Blessing_byTNoble_NYHistoricalSociety.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/John_Brown_(abolitionist)" title="John Brown (abolitionist)">John Brown</a>'s blessing</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Prior to the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>, a small group of <a href="/wiki/Quakers" title="Quakers">Quakers</a> including <a href="/wiki/John_Woolman" title="John Woolman">John Woolman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Benezet" title="Anthony Benezet">Anthony Benezet</a> successfully persuaded their fellow members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religious_Society_of_Friends" title="Religious Society of Friends">Religious Society of Friends</a> to free their slaves, divest from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave_trade" title="Slave trade">slave trade</a>, and create unified Quaker policies against slavery. This afforded their tiny religious denomination some moral authority to help begin the <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism_in_the_United_States" title="Abolitionism in the United States">Abolitionist</a> movement on both sides of the Atlantic. <a href="/wiki/John_Woolman" title="John Woolman">Woolman</a> died of smallpox in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> in 1775, shortly after crossing the Atlantic to bring his anti-slavery message to the Quakers of the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>.</p>
<p>During and after the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>, Quaker ministrations and preachings against slavery began to spread beyond their movement. In 1783, 300 Quakers, chiefly from the London area, presented the British Parliament with their signatures on the first petition against the slave trade. In 1785, Englishman Thomas Clarkson, enrolled at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambridge_University" title="Cambridge University">Cambridge</a>, and in the course of writing an essay in Latin (<i>Anne liceat invitos in servitutem dare</i> (Is it lawful to enslave the unconsenting?), read the works of <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Benezet" title="Anthony Benezet">Benezet</a>, and began a lifelong effort to outlaw the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave_trade" title="Slave trade">slave trade</a> in England. In 1787, sympathizers formed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Committee_for_the_Abolition_of_the_Slave_Trade" title="Committee for the Abolition of the Slave Trade">Committee for the Abolition of the Slave Trade</a>, a small non-denominational group that could lobby more successfully by incorporating Anglicans, who, unlike the Quakers, could lawfully sit in Parliament. The twelve founding members included nine Quakers, and three pioneering Anglicans: Granville Sharp, Thomas Clarkson, and William Wilberforce — all evangelical Christians.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_successes">Later successes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-racism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later successes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Later successes in opposing racism were won by the abolitionist movement, both in <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Abolitionism in the United Kingdom">England</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism_in_the_United_States" title="Abolitionism in the United States">United States</a>. Though many Abolitionists did not regard blacks or <a href="/wiki/Mulatto" title="Mulatto">mulattos</a> as equal to whites, they did in general believe in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freedom_(political)" title="Freedom (political)">freedom</a> and often even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equal_protection" title="Equal protection">equality of treatment for all people</a>. A few, like <a href="/wiki/John_Brown_(abolitionist)" title="John Brown (abolitionist)">John Brown</a>, went further. Brown was willing to die on behalf of, as he said, "millions in this slave country whose rights are disregarded by wicked, cruel, and unjust enactments ..." Many black Abolitionists, such as <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Douglass" title="Frederick Douglass">Frederick Douglass</a>, explicitly argued for the humanity of blacks and mulattoes, and for the equality of all people.</p>
<p>Prior to and during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, racial egalitarianism in the North became much stronger and more generally disseminated. The success of <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Soldier" title="Buffalo Soldier">black troops</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Union_Army" title="Union Army">Union Army</a> had a dramatic impact on Northern sentiment. The <a href="/wiki/Emancipation_Proclamation" title="Emancipation Proclamation">Emancipation Proclamation</a> was a notable example of this shift in political attitudes, although it notably did not completely extinguish legal slavery in several states. After the war, the <a href="/wiki/Reconstruction_Era" title="Reconstruction Era">Reconstruction</a> government passed the <a href="/wiki/Fourteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Fourteenth Amendment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fifteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Fifteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Fifteenth Amendments</a> to the Constitution to guarantee the rights of blacks and mulattoes. Many ex-slaves had access to education for the first time. Blacks and mulattoes were also allowed to vote, which meant that African-Americans were elected to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congress_of_the_United_States_of_America" title="Congress of the United States of America">Congress</a> in numbers not equaled until the Voting Rights Act and the Warren Court helped re-enfranchise black Americans.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Due to resistance in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">South</a>, however, and a general collapse of idealism in the North, Reconstruction ended, and gave way to the <a href="/wiki/Nadir_of_American_race_relations" title="Nadir of American race relations">nadir of American race relations</a>. The period from about 1890 to 1920 saw the re-establishment of <a href="/wiki/Jim_Crow_laws" title="Jim Crow laws">Jim Crow laws</a>. President <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a>, who regarded Reconstruction as a disaster, segregated the federal government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Ku_Klux_Klan" title="Ku Klux Klan">Ku Klux Klan</a> grew to its greatest peak of popularity and strength. <a href="/wiki/D._W._Griffith" title="D. W. Griffith">D. W. Griffith</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Birth_of_a_Nation" title="The Birth of a Nation">The Birth of a Nation</a></i> was a movie sensation.</p>
<p>In 1911 the <a href="/wiki/First_Universal_Races_Congress" title="First Universal Races Congress">First Universal Races Congress</a> met in London, at which distinguished speakers from many countries for four days discussed race problems and ways to improve interracial relations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_anti-racism">Scientific anti-racism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-racism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Scientific anti-racism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Tiedemann" title="Friedrich Tiedemann">Friedrich Tiedemann</a> was one of the first people scientifically to contest racism. In 1836, using craniometric and brain measurements (taken by him from Europeans and black people from different parts of the world), he refuted the belief of many contemporary naturalists and anatomists that black people have smaller brains and are thus intellectually inferior to white people, saying it was scientifically unfounded and based merely on the prejudiced opinions of travelers and explorers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the start of the 20th Century, the work of anthropologists trying to end the <a href="/wiki/Paradigm" title="Paradigm">paradigms</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_evolutionism" title="Cultural evolutionism">cultural evolutionism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_Darwinism" title="Social Darwinism">social Darwinism</a> within social sciences—anthropologists like <a href="/wiki/Franz_Boas" title="Franz Boas">Franz Boas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Mauss" title="Marcel Mauss">Marcel Mauss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bronis%C5%82aw_Malinowski" title="Bronisław Malinowski">Bronisław Malinowski</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Clastres" title="Pierre Clastres">Pierre Clastres</a> and <a href="/wiki/Claude_L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="Claude Lévi-Strauss">Claude Lévi-Strauss</a>—began the initiative to the end of racism in human sciences and establish <a href="/wiki/Cultural_relativism" title="Cultural relativism">cultural relativism</a> as the new dominant paradigm.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Racial_equality:_Paris_1919">Racial equality: Paris 1919</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-racism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Racial equality: Paris 1919">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Racial_Equality_Proposal" title="Racial Equality Proposal">Racial Equality Proposal</a></div>
<p>Japan first proposed articles dedicated to the elimination of racial discrimination to be added to the rules of the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a>. This was the first proposal concerning the international elimination of racial discrimination in the world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Although the proposal received a majority (11 out of 16) of votes, the chairman, U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a>, overturned it saying that important issues should be unanimously approved. <a href="/wiki/Billy_Hughes" title="Billy Hughes">Billy Hughes</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adb_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-adb-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Cook" title="Joseph Cook">Joseph Cook</a> vigorously opposed it as it undermined the <a href="/wiki/White_Australia_policy" title="White Australia policy">White Australia policy</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Revival_in_the_United_States">Revival in the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-racism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Revival in the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Harlem_Renaissance" title="Harlem Renaissance">Harlem Renaissance</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African-American_Civil_Rights_Movement_(1954%E2%80%9368)" title="African-American Civil Rights Movement (1954–68)">African-American Civil Rights Movement (1954–68)</a></div>
<p>Opposition to racism revived in the 1920s and 1930s. At that time, <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropologists</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Franz_Boas" title="Franz Boas">Franz Boas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Benedict" title="Ruth Benedict">Ruth Benedict</a>, <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Mead" title="Margaret Mead">Margaret Mead</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ashley_Montagu" title="Ashley Montagu">Ashley Montagu</a> argued for the equality of humans across races and cultures. <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Roosevelt" title="Eleanor Roosevelt">Eleanor Roosevelt</a> was a very visible advocate for minority rights during this period. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-capitalist" title="Anti-capitalist">Anti-capitalist</a> organizations like the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Workers_of_the_World" title="Industrial Workers of the World">Industrial Workers of the World</a>, which gained popularity during 1905–1926, were explicitly egalitarian.</p>
<p>Beginning with the <a href="/wiki/Harlem_Renaissance" title="Harlem Renaissance">Harlem Renaissance</a> and continuing into the 1960s, many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African-American" title="African-American">African-American</a> writers argued forcefully against racism.</p>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African-American_Civil_Rights_Movement_(1954%E2%80%9368)" title="African-American Civil Rights Movement (1954–68)">U.S. Civil Rights Movement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Crow_laws" title="Jim Crow laws">Jim Crow laws</a> were repealed in the South and blacks finally re-won the right to vote in Southern states. Dr. <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King_Jr." title="Martin Luther King Jr.">Martin Luther King Jr.</a> was an influential force, and his "<a href="/wiki/I_Have_a_Dream" title="I Have a Dream">I Have a Dream</a>" speech is an exemplary condensation of his egalitarian ideology.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-racism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Crowd rallying at a demonstration in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> against manifestations of racism and discrimination.</div>

<p>Egalitarianism has been a catalyst for <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminism</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-war" title="Anti-war">anti-war</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anti-imperialism" title="Anti-imperialism">anti-imperialist</a> movements. <a href="/wiki/Henry_David_Thoreau" title="Henry David Thoreau">Henry David Thoreau</a>'s opposition to the <a href="/wiki/Mexican%E2%80%93American_War" title="Mexican–American War">Mexican–American War</a>, for example, was based in part on his fear that the U.S. was using the war as an excuse to expand <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_slavery_in_the_United_States" title="History of slavery in the United States">American slavery</a> into new territories. Thoreau's response was chronicled in his famous essay "<a href="/wiki/Civil_Disobedience_(Thoreau)" title="Civil Disobedience (Thoreau)">Civil Disobedience</a>", which in turn helped ignite <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gandhi" title="Gandhi">Gandhi</a>'s successful campaign against the British in India.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Gandhi's example in turn inspired the American civil rights movement.</p>
<p>As <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Loewen" title="James Loewen">James Loewen</a> notes in <i><a href="/wiki/Lies_My_Teacher_Told_Me" title="Lies My Teacher Told Me">Lies My Teacher Told Me</a></i>: "Throughout the world, from Africa to <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, movements of oppressed people continue to use tactics and words borrowed from our abolitionist and civil rights movements." In <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a>, revolutionary <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tiananmen_Square" title="Tiananmen Square">Tiananmen Square</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_South_Africa_in_the_apartheid_era" title="History of South Africa in the apartheid era">South Africa</a>, images, words, and tactics developed by human rights supporters have been used regularly and repeatedly.</p>
<p>Many of these uses have been controversial. For example, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pro-life" title="Pro-life">pro-life</a> movement often draws connections between its goals and the goals of abolitionism. In <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Mugabe" title="Robert Mugabe">Robert Mugabe</a> has used anti-racist rhetoric to promote a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_distribution" title="Land distribution">land distribution</a> scheme whereby privately held land is confiscated from white Rhodesians and distributed to blacks, which has resulted in widespread starvation (see <a href="/wiki/Land_reform_in_Zimbabwe" title="Land reform in Zimbabwe">Land reform in Zimbabwe</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sentamu_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-sentamu-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="White_genocide_conspiracy_theory">White genocide conspiracy theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-racism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: White genocide conspiracy theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
