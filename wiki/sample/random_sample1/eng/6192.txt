<a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">electronic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ambient_music" title="Ambient href="#cite_note-Hischak-2">[2]</a></sup> He is best known for his <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Academy Award</a>-winning score for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Chariots_of_Fire" title="Chariots of Fire">Chariots of Fire</a></i>, composing scores for the films <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cosmos:_A_Personal_Voyage" title="Cosmos: A Personal Voyage">Cosmos: A Personal Voyage</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">Carl Sagan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hischak_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hischak-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After having taken piano lessons, Vangelis began his professional musical career working with several popular bands of the 1960s such as the Forminx and <a href="/wiki/Aphrodite%27s_Child" title="Aphrodite's Child">Aphrodite's Child</a>, with the latter's album <i><a href="/wiki/666_(Aphrodite%27s_Child_album)" title="666 (Aphrodite's Child album)">666</a></i> going on to be recognized as a <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_music" title="Psychedelic music">psychedelic</a> classic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hischak_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hischak-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Throughout the 1970s, Vangelis composed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Music_scores" title="Music scores">music scores</a> for several animal documentaries, including <i><span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><a href="/wiki/L%27Apocalypse_des_animaux_(album)" title="L'Apocalypse des animaux (album)">L'Apocalypse des Animaux</a></span></i>, <i><span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><a href="/wiki/La_F%C3%AAte_sauvage" title="La FÃªte sauvage">La FÃªte sauvage</a></span></i> and <i><span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><a href="/wiki/Op%C3%A9ra_sauvage" title="OpÃ©ra sauvage">OpÃ©ra sauvage</a></span></i>; the success of these scores brought him into the film scoring mainstream. In the early 1980s, Vangelis formed a musical partnership with <a href="/wiki/Jon_Anderson" title="Jon Anderson">Jon Anderson</a>, the lead singer of progressive rock band <a href="/wiki/Yes_(band)" title="Yes (band)">Yes</a>, and the duo went on to release several albums together as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jon_%26_Vangelis" title="Jon &amp; Vangelis">Jon &amp; Vangelis</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1981, he composed the score for the Oscar-winning film <i><a href="/wiki/Chariots_of_Fire" title="Chariots of Fire">Chariots of Fire</a></i>, for which he won an <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Original_Score" title="Academy Award for Best Original Score">Academy Award for Best Original Score</a>. <a href="/wiki/Chariots_of_Fire_(album)" title="Chariots of Fire (album)">The soundtrack's</a> single, the film's "<a href="/wiki/Chariots_of_Fire_(instrumental)" title="Chariots of Fire (instrumental)">Titles</a>" theme, also reached the top of the American <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> chart and was used as the background music at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_2012_Olympics" title="London 2012 Olympics">London 2012 Olympics</a> winners' medal presentation ceremonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hischak_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hischak-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Having had a career in music spanning over 50 years and having composed and performed more than 50 albums, Vangelis is considered to be one of the most important figures in the history of <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">electronic music</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_UK_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph_UK-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Journey_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Journey-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vangelis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formative_years">Formative years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vangelis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formative years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Vangelis was born 29 March 1943, in <a href="/wiki/Agria" title="Agria">Agria</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Volos" title="Volos">Volos</a>, Greece. Largely a self-taught musician, he reportedly began composing at the age of four.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hischak_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hischak-2">[2]</a></sup> His earliest memory is "playing piano, some percussion and whatever else that was available that made a noise. Right from the start, I was only interested in playing my own music".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldstein_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstein-7">[7]</a></sup> He refused to take traditional piano lessons, and throughout his career did not have substantial knowledge of reading or writing musical notation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Musician_guide_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Musician_guide-8">[8]</a></sup> When he was six, Vangelis's parents enrolled him at a specialist music school in <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hischak_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hischak-2">[2]</a></sup> He recalls "I was lucky not to go because music schools close doors rather than open them".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_UK_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph_UK-4">[4]</a></sup> He studied painting, an art he still practices, at the <a href="/wiki/Athens_School_of_Fine_Arts" title="Athens School of Fine Arts">Athens School of Fine Arts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Vangelis said in an interview with <i><a href="/wiki/Life_(magazine)" title="Life (magazine)">Life</a></i>, when asked about his lack of ability to read music:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>When the teachers asked me to play something, I would pretend that I was reading it and play from memory. I didn't fool them, but I didn't care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Musician_guide_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Musician_guide-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Work_in_bands_and_Aphrodite.27s_Child">Work in bands and Aphrodite's Child</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vangelis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Work in bands and Aphrodite's Child">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When Vangelis was twelve years he became interested in the <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> music, and with the social movement to <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock and roll</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bigot1984_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bigot1984-10">[10]</a></sup> At fifteen years old he started to form early school bands, not to cover other musicians, but to have fun,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lake1974_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lake1974-11">[11]</a></sup> resulting in the early 1960s being one of the founders of pop rock group The Forminx (or the Formynx), which became popular in Greece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theforminx_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-theforminx-12">[12]</a></sup> Based in Athens, the five-piece band played a mixture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cover_(music)" title="Cover (music)">cover versions</a> and their own material, the latter written mostly by Vangelis (with lyrics by DJ and record producer Nico Mastorakis) but still sung in English. The Forminx released nine hit singles and a Christmas EP before disbanding in 1966 at the peak of their success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theforminx_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-theforminx-12">[12]</a></sup> A film being made about them at the time, which was initially directed by Theo Angelopoulos, was never fully completed, and the songs, composed for the movie, were never released. Vangelis spent the next two years mostly studio-bound, writing and producing for other Greek artists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Musician_guide_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Musician_guide-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blue_Point_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blue_Point-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Around the time of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1968_student_riots" title="1968 student riots">student riots in 1968</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_UK_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph_UK-4">[4]</a></sup> Vangelis founded <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Aphrodite%27s_Child" title="Aphrodite's Child">Aphrodite's Child</a> together with <a href="/wiki/Demis_Roussos" title="Demis Roussos">Demis Roussos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Loukas_Sideras" title="Loukas Sideras">Loukas Sideras</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Silver_Koulouris" title="Silver Koulouris">Anargyros "Silver" Koulouris</a>. After an unsuccessful attempt to enter the UK, they found a home in Paris where they recorded their first single, a hit across much of Europe called <i>Rain and Tears</i>. Other singles followed, including two albums, which, in total, sold over 20 million copies. The record sales led the record company to request a third album, and Vangelis went on to conceive the double-album <i><a href="/wiki/666_(Aphrodite%27s_Child_album)" title="666 (Aphrodite's Child album)">666</a></i>, based on <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">Revelation</a>, the last book in the Bible. It is often listed as one of the best progressive rock albums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_UK_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph_UK-4">[4]</a></sup> One of the many remarkable features of this album is <a href="/wiki/Irene_Papas" title="Irene Papas">Irene Papas</a>'s guest participation (vocal on "Infinity"). Tensions between members during the recording of <i>666</i> eventually caused the split of the band in 1971, but the album was still released in 1972. Despite the split, Vangelis has since produced several albums and singles for Demis Roussos, who, in turn, contributed vocals to the <a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner_(soundtrack)" title="Blade Runner (soundtrack)"><i>Blade Runner</i> soundtrack</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blue_Point_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blue_Point-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elsewhere_Oor_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Elsewhere_Oor-15">[15]</a></sup> He often recalls on music industry:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I moved into the record industry. I was under the impression that in order to be alive and to be able to create what I had in mind I had to become successful. I realised that success and pure creativity are not very compatible... Instead of being able to move forward freely and do what you really wish, you find yourself stuck and obliged to repeat yourself and your previous success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheGuardian2012_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-TheGuardian2012-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_solo_works">Early solo works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vangelis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early solo works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While still in Aphrodite's Child, Vangelis had already been involved in other projects. In the 1960s he scored music for three Greek films <i>My Brother, the Traffic Policeman</i> (1963) directed by Filippos Fylaktos, <i>5,000 Lies</i> (1966) by <a href="/wiki/Giorgos_Konstantinou" title="Giorgos Konstantinou">Giorgos Konstantinou</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vortex_(film)" title="Vortex (film)">To Prosopo tis Medousas</a></i> (1967) by <a href="/wiki/Nikos_Koundouros" title="Nikos Koundouros">Nikos Koundouros</a>. In 1970 composed the score for <i>Sex-Power</i> directed by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Chapier" title="Henry Chapier">Henry Chapier</a>, as well again for <i>Salut, Jerusalem</i> (1972) and <i>Amore</i> (1974).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hischak_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Hischak-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1971, some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jam_sessions" title="Jam sessions">jam sessions</a> with a group of musicians in London had resulted in two albums' worth of material, unofficially released without Vangelis' permission in 1978, titled <i><a href="/wiki/Hypothesis_(album)" title="Hypothesis (album)">Hypothesis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dragon_(Vangelis_album)" title="The Dragon (Vangelis album)">The Dragon</a></i>. Vangelis succeeded in taking legal action to have them withdrawn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spin_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spin-17">[17]</a></sup> A more successful project was his scoring of wildlife documentary films in the early 1970s made by French <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">filmmaker</a> <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Rossif" title="FrÃ©dÃ©ric Rossif">FrÃ©dÃ©ric Rossif</a>. The first soundtrack <a href="/wiki/L%27Apocalypse_des_animaux_(album)" title="L'Apocalypse des animaux (album)"><i>L'Apocalypse des animaux</i></a> was released in 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo1_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo1-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1972, the student riots of 1968 provided the inspiration for an album titled <i><a href="/wiki/Fais_que_ton_r%C3%AAve_soit_plus_long_que_la_nuit" title="Fais que ton rÃªve soit plus long que la nuit">Fais que ton rÃªve soit plus long que la nuit</a></i> (Make Your Dream Last Longer Than the Night), comprising musical passages mixed with news snippets and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protest_songs" title="Protest songs">protest songs</a>; some lyrics were based on graffiti daubed on walls during the riots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo1_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo1-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> He also did music for the 1973 <a href="/wiki/Henry_Chapier" title="Henry Chapier">Henry Chapier</a> film <i>Amore</i>.</p>
<p>In 1973 Vangelis' solo career began in earnest. His second solo album was <i><a href="/wiki/Earth_(Vangelis_album)" title="Earth (Vangelis album)">Earth</a></i>. It was a percussive-orientated album with Byzantine undertones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo1_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo1-18">[18]</a></sup> It featured a group of musicians including ex-Aphrodite's Child guitarist <a href="/wiki/Silver_Koulouris" title="Silver Koulouris">Silver Koulouris</a> and also vocalist and songwriter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Fitoussi" title="Robert Fitoussi">Robert Fitoussi</a> (better known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F.R._David" title="F.R. David">F.R. David</a> of "<a href="/wiki/Words_(F._R._David_song)" title="Words (F. R. David song)">Words</a>" fame).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> This line-up, later briefly going out under the name "Odyssey", released a single in 1974 titled "Who", but that was Vangelis' last involvement with them. Later in 1974, Vangelis was widely tipped to join another prog-rock band, <a href="/wiki/Yes_(band)" title="Yes (band)">Yes</a>, following the departure of <a href="/wiki/Rick_Wakeman" title="Rick Wakeman">Rick Wakeman</a>. After a couple of weeks of rehearsals Vangelis wavered on the option of joining Yes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spin_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spin-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and the band had to detour and hire Swiss keyboard player <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Moraz" title="Patrick Moraz">Patrick Moraz</a> instead, who later joined <a href="/wiki/The_Moody_Blues" title="The Moody Blues">the Moody Blues</a>. Vangelis did, however, become friends with Yes' lead vocalist <a href="/wiki/Jon_Anderson" title="Jon Anderson">Jon Anderson</a>, and later worked with him on several occasions, including as the duo <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jon_%26_Vangelis" title="Jon &amp; Vangelis">Jon &amp; Vangelis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solo_career_breakthrough">Solo career breakthrough</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vangelis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Solo career breakthrough">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After moving to London in 1975, Vangelis signed with <a href="/wiki/RCA_Records" title="RCA Records">RCA Records</a>, set up his own studio, <a href="/wiki/Nemo_Studios" title="Nemo Studios">Nemo Studios</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and began recording a string of electronic albums,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hischak_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Hischak-2">[2]</a></sup> such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heaven_and_Hell_(1975_album)" title="Heaven and Hell (1975 album)">Heaven and Hell</a></i> (1975), <i><a href="/wiki/Albedo_0.39" title="Albedo 0.39">Albedo 0.39</a></i> (1976), <i><a href="/wiki/Spiral_(Vangelis_album)" title="Spiral (Vangelis album)">Spiral</a></i> (1977), <i><a href="/wiki/Beaubourg_(album)" title="Beaubourg (album)">Beaubourg</a></i> (1978), and <i><a href="/wiki/China_(album)" title="China (album)">China</a></i> (1979). The <i>Heaven and Hell</i> was premiered at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Royal_Albert_Hall" title="The Royal Albert Hall">The Royal Albert Hall</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo2_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo2-24">[24]</a></sup> Each of the albums had special thematic inspiration; <i><a href="/wiki/Heaven" title="Heaven">Heaven</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hell" title="Hell">Hell</a></i> the homonymous mythological places, <i>Albedo 0.39</i> the universe, <i>Spiral</i> the <a href="/wiki/Tao" title="Tao">Tao</a> philosophy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo2_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo2-24">[24]</a></sup> <i>Beaubourg</i> the visit to the <a href="/wiki/Centre_Georges_Pompidou" title="Centre Georges Pompidou">Centre Georges Pompidou</a>, while <i>China</i> the Chinese cultural and musical traditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo3_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo3-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1977 was re-released (originally released in 1975) the film score of <i><a href="/wiki/Do_You_Hear_the_Dogs_Barking%3F" title="Do You Hear the Dogs Barking?">Do You Hear the Dogs Barking?</a></i> directed by <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Reichenbach" title="FranÃ§ois Reichenbach">FranÃ§ois Reichenbach</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo3_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo3-25">[25]</a></sup> Vangelis also contributed as a producer and keyboard player to the album <i>Phos</i>, by the Greek rock band <a href="/wiki/Socrates_Drank_the_Conium" title="Socrates Drank the Conium">Socrates Drank the Conium</a> (later known simply as <i>Socrates</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1976 was released the second soundtrack for Rossif animal documentary, <i><a href="/wiki/La_F%C3%AAte_sauvage" title="La FÃªte sauvage">La FÃªte sauvage</a></i>, which combined African rhythms with Western music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo3_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo3-25">[25]</a></sup> In 1979 was released the third score album for Rossif, <i><a href="/wiki/Op%C3%A9ra_sauvage" title="OpÃ©ra sauvage">OpÃ©ra sauvage</a></i>. Almost as well known as <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Apocalypse_des_animaux_(album)" title="L'Apocalypse des animaux (album)">L'Apocalypse des animaux</a></i>, the resulting soundtrack would bring him to the attention of some of the world's top filmmakers. The music itself would be re-used in other films (most notably the track "L'Enfant" in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Year_of_Living_Dangerously_(film)" title="The Year of Living Dangerously (film)">The Year of Living Dangerously</a></i> (1982) by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Weir" title="Peter Weir">Peter Weir</a>; the melody of same (in marching band format) can also be heard at the beginning of the 1924 Summer Olympics opening ceremonies scene in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Chariots_of_Fire" title="Chariots of Fire">Chariots of Fire</a></i> and television commercials (the track "Hymne", used in <a href="/wiki/Barilla_Group" title="Barilla Group">Barilla</a> pasta commercials in Italy and <a href="/wiki/E_%26_J_Gallo_Winery" title="E &amp; J Gallo Winery">Ernest &amp; Julio Gallo</a> wine ads in the US).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo8_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo8-29">[29]</a></sup> They again collaborated for <i>Sauvage et Beau</i> (1984),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo7_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo7-30">[30]</a></sup> <i>De Nuremburg Ã  Nuremburg</i> (1989).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo9_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo9-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1979 was released album <i><a href="/wiki/Odes_(album)" title="Odes (album)">Odes</a></i>, which included Greek folk songs performed by Vangelis and actress <a href="/wiki/Irene_Papas" title="Irene Papas">Irene Papas</a>. It was instant success in Greece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo3_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo3-25">[25]</a></sup> It was followed by second collaboration album <i>Rapsodies</i> in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo8_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo8-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1980s were released five solo albums; the experimental and satirical <i><a href="/wiki/See_You_Later" title="See You Later">See You Later</a></i> (1980) included "Memories of Green" which featured in the upcoming 1982 film <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a></i>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo4_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo4-32">[32]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Soil_Festivities" title="Soil Festivities">Soil Festivities</a></i> was thematically inspired by the interaction between nature and its microscopic living creatures;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo7_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo7-30">[30]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Invisible_Connections" title="Invisible Connections">Invisible Connections</a></i> (1985) by the world of elementary particles invisible to the naked eye;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo7_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo7-30">[30]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Mask_(Vangelis_album)" title="Mask (Vangelis album)">Mask</a></i> (1985) by the theme of the mask, an obsolete artefact which was used in ancient times for concealment or amusement;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo7_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo7-30">[30]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Direct_(Vangelis_album)" title="Direct (Vangelis album)">Direct</a></i> (1988), the first album to be recorded in the post-Nemo Studios era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo9_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo9-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1990s were also released five solo albums; <i><a href="/wiki/The_City_(Vangelis_album)" title="The City (Vangelis album)">The City</a></i> (1990) was recorded during his stay in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> in 1989, and reflected a day of bustling city life, from dawn until dusk;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo9_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo9-31">[31]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Voices_(Vangelis_album)" title="Voices (Vangelis album)">Voices</a></i> (1995) featured sensual songs filled with nocturnal orchestrations; <i><a href="/wiki/Oceanic_(Vangelis_album)" title="Oceanic (Vangelis album)">Oceanic</a></i> (1996) thematically explored the mystery of underwater worlds and sea sailing;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo11_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo11-33">[33]</a></sup> two classical albums about <a href="/wiki/El_Greco" title="El Greco">El Greco</a> - <i><a href="/wiki/Foros_Timis_Ston_Greco" title="Foros Timis Ston Greco">Foros Timis Ston Greco</a></i> (1995), which had limited release, and <i><a href="/wiki/El_Greco_(album)" title="El Greco (album)">El Greco</a></i> (1998), which was an expansion of the former.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo12_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo12-34">[34]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1981, Vangelis wrote the score for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Chariots_of_Fire" title="Chariots of Fire">Chariots of Fire</a></i>, set at the <a href="/wiki/1924_Summer_Olympics" title="1924 Summer Olympics">1924 Summer Olympics</a>. The choice of music was unorthodox as most period films featured traditional orchestral scores, whereas Vangelis' music was modern and synthesizer-heavy. The movie won the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Picture" title="Academy Award for Best Picture">Academy Award for Best Picture</a> and Vangelis won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Original_Music_Score" title="Academy Award for Best Original Music Score">Academy Award for Best Original Music Score</a>. The opening theme of the film was released as a single in 1982, topping the American <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billboard_magazine" title="Billboard magazine">Billboard</a></i> chart for one week after climbing steadily for five months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> It was used at the <a href="/wiki/1984_Winter_Olympics" title="1984 Winter Olympics">1984 Winter Olympics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_UK_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph_UK-4">[4]</a></sup> Vangelis commented that the " main inspiration was the story itself. The rest I did instinctively, without thinking about anything else, other than to express my feelings with the technological means available to me at the time".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheGuardian2012_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-TheGuardian2012-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Blade_Runner"><i>Blade Runner</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vangelis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Blade Runner">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner_(soundtrack)" title="Blade Runner (soundtrack)">Blade Runner (soundtrack)</a></div>
<p>In 1982, Vangelis collaborated with director <a href="/wiki/Ridley_Scott" title="Ridley Scott">Ridley Scott</a>, to write the score for the science fiction film <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Critics have written that capturing the isolation and melancholy of <a href="/wiki/Harrison_Ford" title="Harrison Ford">Harrison Ford</a>'s character, Rick Deckard, the Vangelis score is as much a part of the dystopian environment as the decaying buildings and ever-present rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The music score was nominated for a <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Film_Music#1982" title="BAFTA Award for Best Film Music">BAFTA</a> and <a href="/wiki/40th_Golden_Globe_Awards" title="40th Golden Globe Awards">Golden Globe</a> award.</p>
<p>A disagreement led to Vangelis withholding permission for his performance of the music from <i>Blade Runner</i> to be released, and the studio instead hired a group of musicians dubbed "The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_American_Orchestra" title="New American Orchestra">New American Orchestra</a>" to record the official <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LP_album" title="LP album">LP</a> released at the time. It took 12 years before the disagreement was resolved and Vangelis's own work was released in the United States, in 1994. The soundtrack was still incomplete, as the film contained some non-Vangelis tracks as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Intuitive_Music_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Intuitive_Music-38">[38]</a></sup> Over the years a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bootleg_recordings" title="Bootleg recordings">bootleg recordings</a> of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blade_Runner_(soundtracks)#Bootlegs" title="Blade Runner (soundtracks)">Blade Runner soundtrack</a></i> from unknown sources have been released, mostly targeted to collectors as "private releases", that contain most of the music cues (including the <a href="/wiki/The_Ladd_Company" title="The Ladd Company">Ladd Company</a> <a href="/wiki/Production_logo" title="Production logo">logo</a> theme).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> An official three-disc <a href="/wiki/Box_set" title="Box set">box set</a> was released in late 2007 to commemorate the film's 25th anniversary: it contained the original 1994 album, a second disc containing some more of the missing music cues and a third disc of new Vangelis material inspired by <i>Blade Runner</i>. The 2007 release still lacks some incidental music, most notably the background music from the Taffey Lewis bar scene featuring vocals by <a href="/wiki/Demis_Roussos" title="Demis Roussos">Demis Roussos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1492:_Conquest_of_Paradise"><i>1492: Conquest of Paradise</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vangelis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 1492: Conquest of Paradise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1492:_Conquest_of_Paradise_(album)" title="1492: Conquest of Paradise (album)">1492: Conquest of Paradise (album)</a></div>
<p>In 1992, <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a> released the film <i><a href="/wiki/1492:_Conquest_of_Paradise" title="1492: Conquest of Paradise">1492: Conquest of Paradise</a></i>, also directed by <a href="/wiki/Ridley_Scott" title="Ridley Scott">Ridley Scott</a>, as a 500th anniversary commemoration of <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>' voyage to the New World. Vangelis's score was nominated as "Best Original Score â Motion Picture" at the 1993 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Globe" title="Golden Globe">Golden Globe</a> awards, but was not nominated for an Academy Award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> However, due to its success Vangelis won <a href="/wiki/Echo_Music_Prize" title="Echo Music Prize">Echo Award</a> as "International Artist Of The Year", and RTL Golden Lion Award for the "Best Title Theme for a TV Film or a Series" in 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Finder_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Finder-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_works">Other works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vangelis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">Carl Sagan</a>'s TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Cosmos:_A_Personal_Voyage" title="Cosmos: A Personal Voyage">Cosmos: A Personal Voyage</a></i> (1980),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo4_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo4-32">[32]</a></sup> uses several pieces composed by Vangelis during the 1970s, including the series' opening theme, <i>Heaven and Hell</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s 3rd movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo4_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo4-32">[32]</a></sup> In 1986, Vangelis was actively involved in the composition of new music for the special edition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo8_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo8-29">[29]</a></sup> Vangelis recalls he was sent by Sagan some sounds collected by satellites, which were exactly what he heard as a child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_UK_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph_UK-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>During 1980, six years after Vangelis decided against joining <a href="/wiki/Yes_(band)" title="Yes (band)">Yes</a>, he and <a href="/wiki/Jon_Anderson" title="Jon Anderson">Jon Anderson</a>, the lead singer of Yes, released their first album together, <i><a href="/wiki/Short_Stories_(Jon_and_Vangelis_album)" title="Short Stories (Jon and Vangelis album)">Short Stories</a></i>, under the band name of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jon_%26_Vangelis" title="Jon &amp; Vangelis">Jon &amp; Vangelis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo4_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo4-32">[32]</a></sup> They would eventually go on to release three more afterwards; <i><a href="/wiki/The_Friends_of_Mr_Cairo" title="The Friends of Mr Cairo">The Friends of Mr Cairo</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Private_Collection_(Jon_and_Vangelis_album)" title="Private Collection (Jon and Vangelis album)">Private Collection</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Page_of_Life" title="Page of Life">Page of Life</a></i> released in 1981, 1983, and 1991 respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo5_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo5-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo6_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo6-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo10_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo10-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>

It was the third score by Vangelis as previous were scored for documentaries about <a href="/wiki/Georges_Mathieu" title="Georges Mathieu">Georges Mathieu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Georges_Braque" title="Georges Braque">Georges Braque</a>. In 1982 composed the film score of <i><a href="/wiki/Missing_(film)" title="Missing (film)">Missing</a></i> directed by <a href="/wiki/Costa-Gavras" title="Costa-Gavras">Costa-Gavras</a>, and which was awarded <a href="/wiki/Palme_d%27Or" title="Palme d'Or">Palme d'Or</a> as well Vangelis was nominated in <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Film_Music#1982" title="BAFTA Award for Best Film Music">BAFTA Award for Best Film Music</a> category.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo5_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo5-43">[43]</a></sup> Other notable Vangelis film soundtracks were <i><a href="/wiki/Antarctica_(Vangelis_album)" title="Antarctica (Vangelis album)">Antarctica</a></i> for the film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nankyoku_Monogatari" title="Nankyoku Monogatari">Nankyoku Monogatari</a></i> in 1983, one of the highest-grossing movies in Japanâs film history,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo6_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo6-44">[44]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bounty" title="The Bounty">The Bounty</a></i> in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo7_30-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo7-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Vangelis also collaborated in 1981 and 1986 with Italian singer <a href="/wiki/Milva" title="Milva">Milva</a>, achieving success especially in Germany with the albums <i>Ich hab' keine Angst</i> and <i>Geheimnisse</i> (<i>I have no fear</i> and <i>Secrets</i>). <p>In the early 1980s he also began composing for ballet and theatre stage plays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo6_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo6-44">[44]</a></sup> In 1983 Vangelis wrote the music for <a href="/wiki/Michael_Cacoyannis" title="Michael Cacoyannis">Michael Cacoyannis</a>' staging of the Greek <a href="/wiki/Tragedy" title="Tragedy">tragedy</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electra_(Euripides)" title="Electra (Euripides)">Elektra</a></i> which was performed with <a href="/wiki/Irene_Papas" title="Irene Papas">Irene Papas</a> at the open-air amphitheater at Epidavros in Greece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo6_44-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo6-44">[44]</a></sup> The same year Vangelis composed his first score for a ballet by <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Eagling" title="Wayne Eagling">Wayne Eagling</a>. It was originally performed by Lesley Colier and Wayne Eagling himself at an <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a> gala in <a href="/wiki/Drury_Lane" title="Drury Lane">Drury Lane</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo6_44-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo6-44">[44]</a></sup> but in 1984 the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Ballet_School" title="Royal Ballet School">Royal Ballet School</a> presented it again at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sadler%27s_Wells" title="Sadler's Wells">Sadler's Wells</a> theatre. <p>Vangelis wrote the film score for the 1992 film <i><a href="/wiki/Bitter_Moon" title="Bitter Moon">Bitter Moon</a></i> directed by <a href="/wiki/Roman_Polanski" title="Roman Polanski">Roman Polanski</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Plague_(1992_film)" title="The Plague (1992 film)">The Plague</a></i> by film director <a href="/wiki/Luis_Puenzo" title="Luis Puenzo">Luis Puenzo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo10_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo10-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> In the 90s, Vangelis scored a number of undersea documentaries for French ecologist and filmmaker, <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Cousteau" title="Jacques Cousteau">Jacques Cousteau</a>, one of which was shown at the <a href="/wiki/Earth_Summit" title="Earth Summit">Earth Summit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo10_45-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo10-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The music score of the film <i>Cavafy</i> (1996) directed by <a href="/wiki/Yannis_Smaragdis" title="Yannis Smaragdis">Yannis Smaragdis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo10_45-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo10-45">[45]</a></sup> was awarded at the <a href="/wiki/Flanders_International_Film_Festival_Ghent" title="Flanders International Film Festival Ghent">Flanders International Film Festival Ghent</a> and Valencia International Film Festival<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Finder_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-Finder-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="World_and_Olympic_Games">World and Olympic Games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vangelis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: World and Olympic Games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sport_Aid" title="Sport Aid">Sport Aid</a> (1986) TV broadcast was set to music specially composed by Vangelis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo8_29-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo8-29">[29]</a></sup> He conceived and staged the ceremony of the <a href="/wiki/1997_World_Championships_in_Athletics" title="1997 World Championships in Athletics">1997 World Championships in Athletics</a> which were held in Greece. He also composed the music as well designed and directed the artistic Olympic flag relay portion, "Handover to <a href="/wiki/2004_Summer_Olympics" title="2004 Summer Olympics">Athens</a>", of the closing ceremonies of the <a href="/wiki/2000_Summer_Olympics" title="2000 Summer Olympics">2000 Summer Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> While no official recording of Vangelis' composition for the 2000 Sydney Games exists, the music can be heard accompanying the presentation of the emblem of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2004_Athens_Games" title="2004 Athens Games">2004 Athens Games</a>. In 2002, Vangelis created the official <i><a href="/wiki/Anthem_(The_2002_FIFA_World_Cup_Official_Anthem)" title="Anthem (The 2002 FIFA World Cup Official Anthem)">Anthem</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/2002_FIFA_World_Cup" title="2002 FIFA World Cup">2002 FIFA World Cup</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> His work from <i><a href="/wiki/Chariots_of_Fire" title="Chariots of Fire">Chariots of Fire</a></i> was heard during the <a href="/wiki/2012_Summer_Olympics_opening_ceremony" title="2012 Summer Olympics opening ceremony">2012 Summer Olympics opening ceremony</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2001, Vangelis performed live and released choral symphony <i><a href="/wiki/Mythodea" title="Mythodea">Mythodea</a></i>, a predominantly orchestral rather than electronic piece that was originally written in 1993, and used by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> as the theme for the <a href="/wiki/2001_Mars_Odyssey" title="2001 Mars Odyssey">Mars Odyssey</a> mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> In 2004, Vangelis released the score for <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Stone" title="Oliver Stone">Oliver Stone</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Alexander_(soundtrack)" title="Alexander (soundtrack)">Alexander</a></i>, continuing his involvement with projects related to Greece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_UK_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph_UK-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Vangelis released 2 albums in 2007; the first was a 3-CD set for the 25th anniversary of <i>Blade Runner</i>, titled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blade_Runner_(soundtracks)#2007_release" title="Blade Runner (soundtracks)">Blade Runner Trilogy</a></i> and second was the soundtrack for the Greek movie, <i><a href="/wiki/El_Greco_(2007_film)" title="El Greco (2007 film)">El Greco</a></i> directed by <a href="/wiki/Yannis_Smaragdis" title="Yannis Smaragdis">Yannis Smaragdis</a>, titled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Greco_Original_Motion_Picture_Soundtrack" title="El Greco Original Motion Picture Soundtrack">El Greco Original Motion Picture Soundtrack</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo15_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo15-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>On December 11, 2011, Vangelis was invited by <a href="/wiki/Katara_(cultural_village)" title="Katara (cultural village)">Katara's Cultural Village</a> in the state of Qatar to conceive, design, direct, and compose music for the opening of its world-class outdoor amphitheater. The event was witnessed by a number of world leaders and dignitaries participating in the 4th Forum of the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_of_Civilizations" title="Alliance of Civilizations">United Nations Alliance of Civilizations</a> held in the city of <a href="/wiki/Doha" title="Doha">Doha</a>. British actor <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Irons" title="Jeremy Irons">Jeremy Irons</a> performed in the role of ceremony master, and the event featured a light show by German artist Gert Hof. It was filmed for a future video release by Oscar-winning British filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Hudson" title="Hugh Hudson">Hugh Hudson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheGuardian2012_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-TheGuardian2012-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, Vangelis re-tooled and added new pieces to his iconic <i>Chariots of Fire</i> soundtrack, for use in the <i><a href="/wiki/Chariots_of_Fire_(play)" title="Chariots of Fire (play)">same-titled</a></i> stage adaptation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheGuardian2012_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-TheGuardian2012-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> He composed the soundtrack of the environmental documentary film <i><a href="/wiki/Trashed_(film)" title="Trashed (film)">Trashed</a></i> (2012) directed by Candida Brady, in which starred <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Irons" title="Jeremy Irons">Jeremy Irons</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> as well scored the music for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Twilight_of_Shadows" title="Twilight of Shadows">Twilight of Shadows</a></i> (2014) directed by <a href="/wiki/Mohammed_Lakhdar-Hamina" title="Mohammed Lakhdar-Hamina">Mohammed Lakhdar-Hamina</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> In 2013 was released documentary film <i>Vangelis And The Journey to Ithaka</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Journey_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Journey-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>For the November 12, 2014 landing of the <a href="/wiki/Philae_(spacecraft)" title="Philae (spacecraft)">Philae</a> lander on <a href="/wiki/67P/Churyumov%E2%80%93Gerasimenko" title="67P/ChuryumovâGerasimenko">Comet 67P</a> (part of the <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Rosetta_(spacecraft)" title="Rosetta (spacecraft)">Rosetta</a> mission), Vangelis composed three short pieces titled "Arrival", "Rosetta's Waltz", and "Philae's Journey". The pieces were released online as videos accompanied by images and animations from the <a href="/wiki/Rosetta_(spacecraft)" title="Rosetta (spacecraft)">Rosetta</a> mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> He was quoted by ESA as saying, "Mythology, science and space exploration are subjects that have fascinated me since my early childhood. And they were always connected somehow with the music I write". In September 2016, the works will be released as part of a new studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Rosetta_(Vangelis_album)" title="Rosetta (Vangelis album)">Rosetta</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vangelis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For an artist of his stature, very little is known about Vangelis' personal life and he rarely gives official interviews to journalists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spin_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Spin-17">[17]</a></sup> However, in a 2005 interview with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i>, Vangelis talked openly about various parts of his life. He stated in the interview that he was "never interested" in the "decadent lifestyle" of his band days, choosing not to use <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Drug" title="Drug">drugs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_UK_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph_UK-4">[4]</a></sup> At the time of the <i>Telegraph</i> interview, Vangelis was involved in his third <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long-term_relationship" title="Long-term relationship">long-term relationship</a>. When asked why he had not had children, Vangelis replied:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>â¦Because of the amount of travelling I do and the nonsense of the music business, I couldn't take care of a child in the way I think it should be taken care of.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_UK_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph_UK-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>It is not known where Vangelis generally resides; he has stated that he "travels around", rather than settling down in one specific place or country for long periods of time. As a hobby, Vangelis enjoys painting; his first art exhibition of 70 paintings was held at Almudin in <a href="/wiki/Valencia" title="Valencia">Valencia</a>, Spain in 2003 and then toured South America until the end of 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_UK_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph_UK-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheGuardian2012_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-TheGuardian2012-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemo14_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nemo14-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>Excerpts from other interviews mention that Vangelis has been married twice before. In a 1976 interview with Dutch music magazine <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oor" title="Oor">Oor</a></i>, the author wrote that Vangelis had a wife named Veronique Skawinska, a photographer who had done some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Album_art" title="Album art">album art</a> work for Vangelis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elsewhere_Oor_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Elsewhere_Oor-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> An <a href="/wiki/Interview" title="Interview">interview</a> in 1982 with <i>Backstage</i> music magazine suggests that Vangelis had previously been married to a singer named Vana Verouti,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> who had performed vocals on some of his records, performing for the first time with him on <i><a href="/wiki/La_F%C3%AAte_sauvage" title="La FÃªte sauvage">La FÃªte sauvage</a></i> and later on <i><a href="/wiki/Heaven_and_Hell_(Vangelis_album)" title="Heaven and Hell (Vangelis album)">Heaven and Hell</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_style_and_compositional_process">Musical style and compositional process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vangelis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Musical style and compositional process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The musical style of Vangelis is diverse; although he primarily uses <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_music_instrument" title="Electronic music instrument">electronic music instruments</a>, which characterize <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">electronic music</a>, his music has been described as a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Electronica" title="Electronica">electronica</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical</a> (his music is often <a href="/wiki/Symphony" title="Symphony">symphonic</a>), <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive rock</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-71">[71]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jazz_music" title="Jazz music">jazz</a> (improvisations),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spin_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-Spin-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ambient_music" title="Ambient music">ambient</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde_music" title="Avant-garde music">avant-garde</a>/<a href="/wiki/Experimental_music" title="Experimental music">experimental</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG_71-2"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pitchfork_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pitchfork-73">[73]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/World_music" title="World music">world</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spin_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-Spin-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/New-age_music" title="New-age music">new-age</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pitchfork_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pitchfork-73">[73]</a></sup> Vangelis is often categorized as a New-age composer, but some consider it an erroneous classification. Vangelis considers it a style which "gave the opportunity for untalented people to make very boring music".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_UK_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph_UK-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a musician who has always composed and played primarily on keyboards, Vangelis relies heavily on synthesizers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> and other electronic approaches to music. However, he also plays and uses many acoustic instruments (including <a href="/wiki/Folk_instrument" title="Folk instrument">folk</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hischak_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-Hischak-2">[2]</a></sup>) and choirs:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I don't always play synthesizers. I play acoustic instruments with the same pleasure. I'm happy when I have unlimited choice; in order to do that, you need everything from simple acoustic sounds to electronic sounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spin_17-5"><a href="#cite_note-Spin-17">[17]</a></sup> Sound is sound and vibration is vibration, whether from an electronic source or an acoustic instrument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheGuardian2012_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-TheGuardian2012-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><i>Synthtopia</i>, an electronic music review website, stated that Vangelis' music could be referred to as "symphonic electronica"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hischak_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-Hischak-2">[2]</a></sup> because of his use of synthesizers in an <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">orchestral</a> fashion. The site went on to describe his music as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melodic" title="Melodic">melodic</a>: "drawing on the melodies of <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a>, especially the Greek music of his homeland".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Vangelis' music and compositions have also been described as "...a distinctive sound with simple, repetitive yet memorable tunes against evocative rhythms and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chord_progressions" title="Chord progressions">chord progressions</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> His first electric instrument was a <a href="/wiki/Hammond_organ#Console_organs" title="Hammond organ">Hammond B3 organ</a>, while first synthesizer a <a href="/wiki/Korg" title="Korg">Korg</a> 700 monophonic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldstein_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstein-7">[7]</a></sup> He has often used <a href="/wiki/Vibrato" title="Vibrato">vibrato</a> on his synthesizers, which was carried out in a distinctive way on his <a href="/wiki/Yamaha_CS-80" title="Yamaha CS-80">Yamaha CS-80</a> polyphonic synthesizer â varying the pressure exerted on the key to produce the expressive vibrato sound. In a 1984 interview Vangelis described the CS-80 as "The most important synthesizer in my career â and for me the best analogue synthesizer design there has ever been."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an interview with <i>Soundtrack</i>, a music and film website, Vangelis talked about his compositional processes. For films, Vangelis stated that he would begin composing a <a href="/wiki/Film_score" title="Film score">score</a> for a feature as soon as he sees a rough cut of the footage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> In addition to working with synthesizers and other electronic instruments, Vangelis also works with and conducts orchestras. For example, in the <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Stone" title="Oliver Stone">Oliver Stone</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Alexander_(2004_film)" title="Alexander (2004 film)">Alexander</a></i>, Vangelis conducted an orchestra that consisted of various <a href="/wiki/Orchestra#Instrumentation" title="Orchestra">classical instruments</a> including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sitars" title="Sitars">sitars</a>, percussion, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finger_cymbals" title="Finger cymbals">finger cymbals</a>, harps, and <a href="/wiki/Duduk" title="Duduk">duduks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<p>Vangelis uses a technique of <a href="/wiki/Multitrack_recording" title="Multitrack recording">recording all tracks simultaneously</a> on tape, using a Zyklus MIDI Performance System.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> He named the album <i><a href="/wiki/Direct_(Vangelis_album)" title="Direct (Vangelis album)">Direct</a></i> after the successor to the Zyklus was specially manufactured for him by the Zyklus MPS designers, Bill Marshall, Pete Kellock, and Mike Crisp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>He explains his customary method of approach. As soon as the musical idea is there, as many keyboards as possible are connected to the control-desk, which in turn are directly connected to the applicable tracks of the multi-track machine. The idea now is to play as many keyboards as possible at the same time. That way, as broad a basis as possible develops, which only needs fine-tuning. After that it's a question of adding things or leaving out things.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Vangelis once used digital sampling keyboard <a href="/wiki/E-mu_Emulator" title="E-mu Emulator">E-mu Emulator</a>, and tried to use computer software for recording, but concluded its "the worst thing that has happened for the performing musician".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldstein_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstein-7">[7]</a></sup> He deems it "insufficient and slow" for immediate and spontaneous creation and all his efforts "are to stay away from that beast".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheGuardian2012_16-6"><a href="#cite_note-TheGuardian2012-16">[16]</a></sup> He considers that the contemporary civilization is living in a cultural "dark age" of "musical pollution". He considers musical composing a science rather than an art, similar to <a href="/wiki/Pythagoreanism#Music_and_harmony" title="Pythagoreanism">Pythagoreanism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_UK_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph_UK-4">[4]</a></sup> He has a mystical viewpoint on music as "one of the greatest forces in the universe",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheGuardian2012_16-7"><a href="#cite_note-TheGuardian2012-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> that the "music exists before we exist".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_UK_4-12"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph_UK-4">[4]</a></sup> Some consider that his experience of music is kind of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synaesthesia" title="Synaesthesia">synaesthesia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_UK_4-13"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph_UK-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honours_and_legacy">Honours and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vangelis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Honours and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1989 received Max Steiner Award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Finder_42-2"><a href="#cite_note-Finder-42">[42]</a></sup> France made Vangelis a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chevalier_des_Arts_et_des_Lettres" title="Chevalier des Arts et des Lettres">Knight of the Order of Arts and Letters</a> in 1992, and <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_Honour" title="Legion of Honour">Chevalier de la Legion dâ Honneur</a> in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> In 1993 received music award Apollo by Friends of the Athens National Opera Society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Finder_42-3"><a href="#cite_note-Finder-42">[42]</a></sup> In 1995, Vangelis had a <a href="/wiki/Minor_planet" title="Minor planet">minor planet</a> named after him (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/6354_Vangelis" title="6354 Vangelis">6354 Vangelis</a></i>) by the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a>'s Minor Planet Center (MPC) at the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Astrophysical_Observatory" title="Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory">Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory</a>; the name was proposed by the MPC's co-director, <a href="/wiki/Gareth_V._Williams" title="Gareth V. Williams">Gareth V. Williams</a>, rather than by the object's original discoverer, <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Joseph_Delporte" title="EugÃ¨ne Joseph Delporte">EugÃ¨ne Joseph Delporte</a>, who died in 1955, long before the 1934 discovery could be confirmed by observations made in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> In 1996 and 1997 was awarded at <a href="/wiki/World_Music_Awards" title="World Music Awards">World Music Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Finder_42-4"><a href="#cite_note-Finder-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> conferred their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Service_Medal_(NASA)" title="Public Service Medal (NASA)">Public Service Medal</a> to Vangelis in 2003. The award is the highest honour the space agency presents to an individual not involved with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_government" title="American government">American government</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> Five years later, in 2008, the board of the <a href="/wiki/National_and_Kapodistrian_University_of_Athens" title="National and Kapodistrian University of Athens">National and Kapodistrian University of Athens</a> voted to make Vangelis an Honorary Doctor, making him <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Professor_Emeritus" title="Professor Emeritus">Professor Emeritus</a> at their Faculty of Primary Education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> In June 2008, the <a href="/wiki/American_Hellenic_Institute" title="American Hellenic Institute">American Hellenic Institute</a> honoured Vangelis with an AHI Hellenic Heritage Achievement Award for his "exceptional artistic achievements" as a pioneer in electronic music and for his lifelong dedication to the promotion of <a href="/wiki/Hellenization" title="Hellenization">Hellenism</a> through the arts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> On 16 September 2013, he received the honour of appearing on the Greek 80 cent postage stamp, as part of a series of six distinguished living personalities of the Greek Diaspora.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
