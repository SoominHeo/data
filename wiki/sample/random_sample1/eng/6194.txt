<p><b>Barnsley Football Club</b> is a professional <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">association football</a> club based in the town of <a href="/wiki/Barnsley" title="Barnsley">Barnsley</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Yorkshire" title="South Yorkshire">South Yorkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. The team play in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Football_League_Championship" title="Football League Championship">Championship</a>, the second tier of the <a href="/wiki/English_football_league_system" title="English football league system">English football league system</a>. Nicknamed "the Tykes", they were founded in 1887 by <a href="/wiki/Tiverton_Preedy" title="Tiverton Preedy">Reverend Tiverton Preedy</a> under the name <b>Barnsley St. Peter's</b>. The club colours are red and white, and their home ground since 1888 has been <a href="/wiki/Oakwell" title="Oakwell">Oakwell</a>. In 2016, Barnsley won 2 Trophies at <a href="/wiki/Wembley_Stadium" title="Wembley Stadium">Wembley Stadium</a> – the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Football_League_Trophy" title="Football League Trophy">Football League Trophy</a>, beating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_United" title="Oxford United">Oxford United</a> 3–2 in the <a href="/wiki/2016_Football_League_Trophy_Final" title="2016 Football League Trophy Final">final</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/2016_Football_League_play-offs" title="2016 Football League play-offs">2016 Football League play-offs</a>, beating <a href="/wiki/Millwall_F.C." title="Millwall F.C.">Millwall F.C.</a> 3–1 in the <a href="/wiki/2016_Football_League_One_play-off_Final" title="2016 Football League One play-off Final">final</a>. Barnsley became the second club to secure both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Football_League_Trophy_(and_predecessors)" title="Football League Trophy (and predecessors)">Football League Trophy</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Football_League" title="The Football League">Football League</a> promotion via playoff finals in the same season, after <a href="/wiki/Grimsby_Town_F.C." title="Grimsby Town F.C.">Grimsby Town F.C.</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnsley_F.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Barnsley_F.C." title="History of Barnsley F.C.">History of Barnsley F.C.</a></div>
<p>Barnsley have spent more seasons in the second tier of English football than any other club in history<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Second_Tier_Historical_Stats_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Second_Tier_Historical_Stats-2">[2]</a></sup> and have produced some notable talents over the years who have gone on to be successful at other clubs. One example is <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Taylor" title="Tommy Taylor">Tommy Taylor</a>, who was a prolific goalscorer for Barnsley in the early 1950s and went on to win two league titles with <a href="/wiki/Manchester_United_F.C." title="Manchester United F.C.">Manchester United</a> (as well as scoring 16 times in 19 England internationals) before losing his life in the <a href="/wiki/Munich_air_disaster" title="Munich air disaster">Munich air disaster</a>. Taylor's move to <a href="/wiki/Manchester_United_F.C." title="Manchester United F.C.">Manchester United</a> was for a fee of £29,999 – one of the highest fees in England at the time. Taylor broke into the Barnsley team just after the sale of wing-half <a href="/wiki/Danny_Blanchflower" title="Danny Blanchflower">Danny Blanchflower</a> to <a href="/wiki/Aston_Villa_F.C." title="Aston Villa F.C.">Aston Villa</a>. Blanchflower would go on to sign for <a href="/wiki/Tottenham_Hotspur_F.C." title="Tottenham Hotspur F.C.">Tottenham Hotspur</a> and be voted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FWA_Player_of_the_Year" title="FWA Player of the Year">FWA Player of the Year</a> twice as well as captaining the <a href="/wiki/North_London" title="North London">North London</a> club to the first <a href="/wiki/Double_(association_football)" title="Double (association football)">league and cup double</a> of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginnings_and_FA_Cup_glory">Beginnings and FA Cup glory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnsley_F.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Beginnings and FA Cup glory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Barnsley FC was established in 1887 by a <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergyman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tiverton_Preedy" title="Tiverton Preedy">Tiverton Preedy</a>, and played in the Sheffield and District League from 1890 and then in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midland_League_(1889)" title="Midland League (1889)">Midland League</a> from 1895. They joined <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Football_League" title="The Football League">the Football League</a> in 1898, and struggled in the <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Second_Division" title="Football League Second Division">Second Division</a> for the first decade, due in part to ongoing financial difficulties. In 1910 the club reached the <a href="/wiki/FA_Cup" title="FA Cup">FA Cup</a> final, where they lost out to <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_United_F.C." title="Newcastle United F.C.">Newcastle United</a> in a replay match. However, they would then reach the <a href="/wiki/1912_FA_Cup_Final" title="1912 FA Cup Final">1912 FA Cup Final</a> where they would defeat <a href="/wiki/West_Bromwich_Albion_F.C." title="West Bromwich Albion F.C.">West Bromwich Albion</a> 1–0 in a replay to win the trophy for the first and only time in their history.</p>

<p>When the league restarted after <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the 1919–20 season brought some significant changes to the league. The principal difference was that the <a href="/wiki/Football_League_First_Division" title="Football League First Division">First Division</a> would be increased from 20 teams to 22. The bottom team from the previous season was <a href="/wiki/Tottenham_Hotspur_F.C." title="Tottenham Hotspur F.C.">Tottenham Hotspur</a> and they were duly relegated. The first extra place in the <a href="/wiki/Football_League_First_Division" title="Football League First Division">First Division</a> went to <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_F.C." title="Chelsea F.C.">Chelsea</a>, who retained their place despite finishing 2nd bottom and therefore in the relegation places. <a href="/wiki/Derby_County_F.C." title="Derby County F.C.">Derby County</a> and <a href="/wiki/Preston_North_End_F.C." title="Preston North End F.C.">Preston North End</a> were rightly promoted from the <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Second_Division" title="Football League Second Division">Second Division</a> which left one place to be filled. Having finished the previous season's <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Second_Division" title="Football League Second Division">Second Division</a> in 3rd place (1914–15), Barnsley expected to achieve <a href="/wiki/Football_League_First_Division" title="Football League First Division">First Division</a> status for the first time, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Football_League" title="The Football League">The Football League</a> instead chose to call a ballot of the clubs. Henry Norris, the then <a href="/wiki/Arsenal_F.C." title="Arsenal F.C.">Arsenal</a> chairman, had recently moved <a href="/wiki/Arsenal_F.C." title="Arsenal F.C.">Woolwich Arsenal</a> north of the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> to <a href="/wiki/Highbury" title="Highbury">Highbury</a>, and needed <a href="/wiki/Football_League_First_Division" title="Football League First Division">First Division</a> football to attract fans to their new home. He was later to admit some underhand dealings, allegedly including the bribing of some member clubs to vote for <a href="/wiki/Arsenal_F.C." title="Arsenal F.C.">Arsenal's</a> inclusion. They duly won the vote and Barnsley were consigned to the second tier of English football for another 8 decades.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prewar_and_postwar_era">Prewar and postwar era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnsley_F.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prewar and postwar era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The club did however come close to reaching the top division in the early years. In 1922, they missed out on promotion by a single goal. During the years preceding and following <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the club found themselves sliding between the <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Second_Division" title="Football League Second Division">Second</a> and <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Third_Division" title="Football League Third Division">Third Division</a>.</p>
<p>In 1949 the club signed a 23-year-old wing-half called <a href="/wiki/Danny_Blanchflower" title="Danny Blanchflower">Danny Blanchflower</a> from <a href="/wiki/Glentoran_F.C." title="Glentoran F.C.">Glentoran</a>, and he so impressed at Oakwell that two years later he was signed by <a href="/wiki/Football_League_First_Division" title="Football League First Division">First Division</a> side <a href="/wiki/Aston_Villa_F.C." title="Aston Villa F.C.">Aston Villa</a>, later signing for <a href="/wiki/Tottenham_Hotspur_F.C." title="Tottenham Hotspur F.C.">Tottenham Hotspur</a> and being voted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FWA_Player_of_the_Year" title="FWA Player of the Year">FWA Player of the Year</a> twice, as well as being the captain of the 20th century's first league and cup double winning team in 1961.</p>
<p>Around the time of <a href="/wiki/Danny_Blanchflower" title="Danny Blanchflower">Blanchflower's</a> departure, a young centre-forward called <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Taylor" title="Tommy Taylor">Tommy Taylor</a> broke into the Barnsley team, scoring 26 goals in 44 games for Barnsley. In April 1953, he became one of the most expensive players in English football at the time when <a href="/wiki/Matt_Busby" title="Matt Busby">Matt Busby</a> signed him for <a href="/wiki/Manchester_United_F.C." title="Manchester United F.C.">Manchester United</a> for a fee of £29,999. <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Taylor" title="Tommy Taylor">Taylor</a> went on to be a prolific goalscorer at the highest level over the next five years, winning two league titles and also scoring 16 times in 19 appearances for the <a href="/wiki/England_national_football_team" title="England national football team">England national football team</a>, before losing his life in the <a href="/wiki/Munich_air_disaster" title="Munich air disaster">Munich air disaster</a> in February 1958.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fourth_Division_era">Fourth Division era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnsley_F.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fourth Division era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When the Northern and Southern sections of the <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Third_Division" title="Football League Third Division">Third Division</a> were replaced by national <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Third_Division" title="Football League Third Division">Third</a> and <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Fourth_Division" title="Football League Fourth Division">Fourth Divisions</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1958-59_in_English_football" title="1958-59 in English football">1958–59 season</a>, Barnsley were still in the <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Second_Division" title="Football League Second Division">Second Division</a>, but went down to the <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Third_Division" title="Football League Third Division">Third Division</a> at the end of that season.</p>
<p>In 1965, Barnsley were relegated to the <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Fourth_Division" title="Football League Fourth Division">Football League Fourth Division</a> for the first time, winning promotion three years later. They went down to the <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Fourth_Division" title="Football League Fourth Division">Fourth Division</a> again in 1972, and this time stayed down for seven seasons, finally returning to the <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Third_Division" title="Football League Third Division">Third Division</a> in 1979.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Revival_in_the_1980s">Revival in the 1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnsley_F.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Revival in the 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two years later, they went up again and quickly established themselves as a decent <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Second_Division" title="Football League Second Division">Second Division</a> side throughout the 1980s, although they still failed to clinch that elusive <a href="/wiki/Football_League_First_Division" title="Football League First Division">First Division</a> place, despite the introduction of the playoffs in the second half of the decade, which gave teams finishing as low as fifth and eventually sixth the chance of winning promotion.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Division_One_and_the_Premier_League">Division One and the Premier League</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnsley_F.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Division One and the Premier League">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Robbie_Savage.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5219" data-file-width="5400" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Robbie_Savage.jpg/220px-Robbie_Savage.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Robbie_Savage.jpg/330px-Robbie_Savage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Robbie_Savage.jpg/440px-Robbie_Savage.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>At the time of the creation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FA_Premier_League" title="FA Premier League">FA Premier League</a> in 1992, Barnsley had been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Football_League" title="Football League">Football League</a> members for 94 years but had still not reached the top flight. They were, at least, in a decent position to make that breakthrough, as members of the new <a href="/wiki/Football_League_First_Division" title="Football League First Division">Division One</a> (as the old <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Second_Division" title="Football League Second Division">Second Division</a> was now called). In December 1989, they turned to <a href="/wiki/Mel_Machin" title="Mel Machin">Mel Machin</a>, manager of <a href="/wiki/Manchester_City_F.C." title="Manchester City F.C.">Manchester City's</a> promotion-winning side the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1988-89_in_English_football" title="1988-89 in English football">previous campaign</a>, to guide them into the top flight, but he left nearly four years later with promotion still to be achieved. Machin's successor <a href="/wiki/Viv_Anderson" title="Viv Anderson">Viv Anderson</a> spent just one season in charge before quitting to become <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Robson" title="Bryan Robson">Bryan Robson</a>'s assistant at <a href="/wiki/Middlesbrough_F.C." title="Middlesbrough F.C.">Middlesbrough</a>, and for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1994-95_in_English_football" title="1994-95 in English football">1994–95 season</a> Barnsley turned to veteran midfielder <a href="/wiki/Danny_Wilson_(footballer,_born_1960)" title="Danny Wilson (footballer, born 1960)">Danny Wilson</a> to manage the club.</p>
<p>Wilson's first season brought a sixth-place finish in <a href="/wiki/Football_League_First_Division" title="Football League First Division">Division One</a>, which would normally have meant a playoff place, but a restructuring of the league meant that they missed out. They finished 10th a year later before finally emerging as serious promotion contenders in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1996-97_in_English_football" title="1996-97 in English football">1996–97 season</a>, finally clinching runners-up spot and automatic promotion and gaining the top flight place that they had spent 99 years trying to win.</p>
<p>Barnsley lasted just one season in the <a href="/wiki/Premier_League" title="Premier League">Premier League</a> but did not go down without a brave fight, and they did reach the quarter-finals of the <a href="/wiki/FA_Cup" title="FA Cup">FA Cup</a>, famously defeating <a href="/wiki/Manchester_United_F.C." title="Manchester United F.C.">Manchester United</a> in the fifth round. Wilson then departed to take over at <a href="/wiki/Sheffield_Wednesday_F.C." title="Sheffield Wednesday F.C.">Sheffield Wednesday</a>, being succeeded as Barnsley manager by veteran striker <a href="/wiki/John_Hendrie_(Scottish_footballer)" title="John Hendrie (Scottish footballer)">John Hendrie</a>, who had been a key player in the promotion-winning team.</p>
<p>Barnsley were the only team from outside the <a href="/wiki/Premier_League" title="Premier League">Premier League</a> to reach the quarter-finals of the FA Cup in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1998-99_in_English_football" title="1998-99 in English football">1998–99 season</a>, but had a disappointing season in <a href="/wiki/Football_League_First_Division" title="Football League First Division">Division One</a>, never really looking like winning promotion and eventually finishing a dismal 13th in the final table. Hendrie was then replaced as manager by <a href="/wiki/Dave_Bassett" title="Dave Bassett">Dave Bassett</a>, who rejuvenated the team and took them to fourth place in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1999-2000_in_English_football" title="1999-2000 in English football">1999–2000</a>, but they lost in the playoff final to <a href="/wiki/Ipswich_Town_F.C." title="Ipswich Town F.C.">Ipswich Town</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mixed_fortunes_in_the_21st_century">Mixed fortunes in the 21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnsley_F.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mixed fortunes in the 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the following years Barnsley were not as successful, with relegation to <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Second_Division" title="Football League Second Division">Division Two</a> in 2002 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Administration_(insolvency)" title="Administration (insolvency)">administration</a> both threatening the existence of the club. Barnsley suffered greatly due to the <a href="/wiki/ITV_Digital" title="ITV Digital">ITV Digital</a> crisis. A late purchase by Barnsley's then Mayor, Peter Doyle, saved the club from folding. Doyle has since left the club, leaving Gordon Shepherd and local businessman Patrick Cryne in control. A regular turnover of managers did the club's stability no favours, either.</p>
<p>Barnsley had the distinction of playing in the last play-off final at <a href="/wiki/Wembley_Stadium_(1923)" title="Wembley Stadium (1923)">Wembley</a> before the stadium was closed for redevelopment,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and in 2006 won in a play-off final at the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Stadium" title="Millennium Stadium">Millennium Stadium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a>, where they beat <a href="/wiki/Swansea_City_A.F.C." title="Swansea City A.F.C.">Swansea City</a> 4–3 on penalties (2–2 after extra-time) to earn promotion to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Football_League_Championship" title="Football League Championship">Championship</a>. The manager at this time was <a href="/wiki/Andy_Ritchie_(English_footballer)" title="Andy Ritchie (English footballer)">Andy Ritchie</a>, who was in his first season in charge after replacing <a href="/wiki/Paul_Hart" title="Paul Hart">Paul Hart</a>.</p>
<p>The team struggled in their first season back in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Football_League_Championship" title="Football League Championship">Championship</a>. In November 2006, with Barnsley in the relegation zone, Ritchie was sacked in favour of <a href="/wiki/Simon_Davey" title="Simon Davey">Simon Davey</a>. Davey managed to steer the team away from relegation in the second half of the season, and the eventually finished 20th. The following season, a much-changed Barnsley side managed a historic <a href="/wiki/FA_Cup" title="FA Cup">FA Cup</a> run, beating <a href="/wiki/Premier_League" title="Premier League">Premier League</a> giants <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_F.C." title="Liverpool F.C.">Liverpool</a> 2–1 at <a href="/wiki/Anfield" title="Anfield">Anfield</a> and defending champions <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_F.C." title="Chelsea F.C.">Chelsea</a> 1–0 to reach the semi-finals for the first time since <a href="/wiki/1912_FA_Cup_Final" title="1912 FA Cup Final">1912</a>, where they narrowly lost out 1–0 to fellow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Football_League_Championship" title="Football League Championship">Championship</a> side <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_City_F.C." title="Cardiff City F.C.">Cardiff City</a> at <a href="/wiki/Wembley_Stadium" title="Wembley Stadium">Wembley</a>.</p>
<p>Barnsley narrowly avoided relegation from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Football_League_Championship" title="Football League Championship">Championship</a> that season, and after a disappointing start to the 2009–10 season <a href="/wiki/Simon_Davey" title="Simon Davey">Simon Davey</a>. was sacked in favour of former <a href="/wiki/Rotherham_United_F.C." title="Rotherham United F.C.">Rotherham United</a> boss <a href="/wiki/Mark_Robins" title="Mark Robins">Mark Robins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2011, after a difficult <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_Barnsley_F.C._season" title="2010–11 Barnsley F.C. season">2010–11</a> season, Robins resigned as manager due to a dispute over the budget for the following season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He was replaced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rochdale_A.F.C" title="Rochdale A.F.C">Rochdale</a> manager <a href="/wiki/Keith_Hill_(footballer)" title="Keith Hill (footballer)">Keith Hill</a> and his assistant <a href="/wiki/David_Flitcroft" title="David Flitcroft">David Flitcroft</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Barnsley ended the 2011–12 season as one of only two football clubs to turn a profit in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Football_League_Championship" title="Football League Championship">Championship</a>; ironically they stayed up only because <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth_F.C." title="Portsmouth F.C.">Portsmouth</a> were given a 10-point deduction for going into administration. The club's form failed to improve the following season, and <a href="/wiki/Keith_Hill_(footballer)" title="Keith Hill (footballer)">Keith Hill</a> was sacked as manager shortly before the turn of the year. <a href="/wiki/David_Flitcroft" title="David Flitcroft">David Flitcroft</a> took over initially as caretaker manager, and after an improved run of results (combined with <a href="/wiki/Sean_O%27Driscoll" title="Sean O'Driscoll">Sean O'Driscoll</a> and <a href="/wiki/Terry_Butcher" title="Terry Butcher">Terry Butcher</a> turning down the chance to manage the club) earned the job on a permanent basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On Sunday 3 April 2016, Barnsley won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Football_League_Trophy" title="Football League Trophy">Football League Trophy</a> after a <a href="/wiki/2016_Football_League_Trophy_Final" title="2016 Football League Trophy Final">3–2 win</a> over <a href="/wiki/Oxford_United_F.C." title="Oxford United F.C.">Oxford United</a> of League Two at <a href="/wiki/Wembley_Stadium" title="Wembley Stadium">Wembley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 19 May, after a 6–1 aggregate win over <a href="/wiki/Walsall_F.C." title="Walsall F.C.">Walsall</a>, Barnsley booked their place in the Play Off Final.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> On 29 May, they gained promotion back to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Football_League_Championship" title="Football League Championship">Football League Championship</a> with a 3–1 win over <a href="/wiki/Millwall" title="Millwall">Millwall</a> at Wembley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_season">Current season</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnsley_F.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Current season">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2016%E2%80%9317_Barnsley_F.C._season" title="2016–17 Barnsley F.C. season">2016–17 Barnsley F.C. season</a></div>
<p>In September 2016, Barnsley were caught up in <a href="/wiki/2016_English_football_scandal" title="2016 English football scandal">an ongoing scandal</a> in English football, with assistant manager <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Wright_(footballer,_born_1966)" title="Tommy Wright (footballer, born 1966)">Tommy Wright</a> alleged to have accepted "bungs" in exchange for working as an ambassador for a third-party player ownership consortium. Wright was initially suspended before being sacked by Barnsley on 28 September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the 13 January 2017, Barnsley released an statement to announce that the club's chief executive, <a href="/wiki/Linton_Brown" title="Linton Brown">Linton Brown</a>, had left the club.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>Barnsley have spent more seasons at the second level of English football than any other team and on 3 January 2011 became the first club to achieve 1,000 wins in the second level of English football with a 2–1 home victory over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coventry_City" title="Coventry City">Coventry City</a>. Barnsley are also the first club to play 3,000 games in second-level league football (W1028, D747, L1224).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stadium">Stadium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnsley_F.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Stadium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Oakwell" title="Oakwell">Oakwell</a></div>
<p>The name, Oakwell, originates from the well and oak tree that were on the stadium site when first built. Oakwell is a multi-purpose sports development in Barnsley, South Yorkshire, used primarily by Barnsley Football Club for playing their home fixtures, and their reserves. While the name 'Oakwell' generally refers to the main stadium, it also includes several neighbouring venues which form the facilities of the Barnsley F.C. academy – an indoor training pitch, a smaller stadium with seating on the south and west sides for around 2,200 spectators, and several training pitches used by the different Barnsley FC squads. Until 2003 the stadium and the vast amount of land that surrounds it was owned by Barnsley Football Club themselves; however, after falling into administration in 2002 the council purchased the main Oakwell Stadium to allow the club to pay its creditors and remain participants in The Football League.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_strip">Home strip</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnsley_F.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Home strip">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Barnsley_FC_Home_Shirt_97-98.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/3f/Barnsley_FC_Home_Shirt_97-98.png/220px-Barnsley_FC_Home_Shirt_97-98.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/3f/Barnsley_FC_Home_Shirt_97-98.png/330px-Barnsley_FC_Home_Shirt_97-98.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/3f/Barnsley_FC_Home_Shirt_97-98.png/440px-Barnsley_FC_Home_Shirt_97-98.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Barnsley_FC_Home_Shirt_97-98.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Barnsleys home shirt in the 1997–98 <a href="/wiki/Premier_League" title="Premier League">Premier League</a> season</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Barnsley have played their home games in red shirts for most of their history. The only exception to this is the period 1887–1901, where it is speculated that the team first wore blue shirts with purple/claret arms, then circa 1890 the team wore chocolate and white stripes, before moving on to blue and white stripes around 1898. The team first wore their now traditional red shirts in 1901.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnsley_.E2.80.93_Historical_Football_Kits_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barnsley_.E2.80.93_Historical_Football_Kits-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since this time, the team has worn red shirts often with a white trim. In more recent times a black trim has sometimes been used. As with most football clubs the shirt design varies from season to season. One particular design that stands out is the <a href="/wiki/1989%E2%80%9390_in_English_football" title="1989–90 in English football">1989–90</a> season shirt which featured white stars on a red background and has been named as one of the worst shirts ever.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Room_101-_The_Worst_Football_Kits_Ever_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Room_101-_The_Worst_Football_Kits_Ever-16">[16]</a></sup> However, the kit is fondly remembered by some fans. <a href="/wiki/Sponsor_(commercial)" title="Sponsor (commercial)">Sponsors</a> names and logos were first worn in the <a href="/wiki/1980%E2%80%9381_in_English_football" title="1980–81 in English football">1980–81</a> season and the club has had 12 different sponsors on the shirt in total. Since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manufacturers" title="Manufacturers">manufacturers</a> logos were added to the shirt in the <a href="/wiki/1976%E2%80%9377_in_English_football" title="1976–77 in English football">1976–77</a> season, the club has 12 different kit manufacturers.</p>
<p>Traditionally, the team has worn white shorts (sometimes with red and/or black trim) for their home games with the only recent exceptions coming in the early years of the 20th century. One other notable exception came in the 2000 Division One Playoff Final against <a href="/wiki/Ipswich_Town_F.C." title="Ipswich Town F.C.">Ipswich Town</a>, where the team wore red shorts, thus having an all-red strip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stewart_pays_tribute_to_keeper_Wright_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stewart_pays_tribute_to_keeper_Wright-17">[17]</a></sup> The Reds have also worn red shorts in their 1988–89 season.</p>
<p>Apart from the club's early years and the period 1921–1934 where the team wore black, the team has worn red and/or white socks for its home games. Again, the design changes from season to season.</p>
<p>For 2010–11 the kit was the traditional red, with white trim. It featured a shield style club badge to the left, with kit sponsors Lotto's logo on the opposite side. The main design was the Barnsley Building Society eagle logo, a return to the design from 2006–07.</p>
<p>In the 2015–16 Play Off Final, Barnsley wore the new home kit for the 2016–17 season, but with black shorts.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Away_strip">Away strip</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnsley_F.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Away strip">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Barnsley_FC_Away_Shirt_98-99.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a2/Barnsley_FC_Away_Shirt_98-99.png/220px-Barnsley_FC_Away_Shirt_98-99.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a2/Barnsley_FC_Away_Shirt_98-99.png/330px-Barnsley_FC_Away_Shirt_98-99.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a2/Barnsley_FC_Away_Shirt_98-99.png/440px-Barnsley_FC_Away_Shirt_98-99.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Barnsley_FC_Away_Shirt_98-99.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Barnsley's away shirt in the 1998–99 season</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The club's away strip (used for away or cup fixtures where there is a clash of colours) differs from season to season but usually follows the design of the season's home strip with a variation on the colours. The most common colour for the away shirt has been white but many others have been used, including blue, yellow, black, <a href="/wiki/Ecru" title="Ecru">ecru</a>, dark green and even black and blue stripes. One notable away strip was the 2001–02 "Its just like watching Brazil" kit, where the team wore the colours of the 5-time <a href="/wiki/FIFA_World_Cup" title="FIFA World Cup">World Cup</a> winners <a href="/wiki/Brazil_national_football_team" title="Brazil national football team">Brazil</a> for their away games.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_strip">Current strip</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnsley_F.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Current strip">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The current strip is manufactured by Puma and the main sponsor is CK Beckett with a rear shirt sponsor of Palmer Construction LTD. The shorts sponsor is BAPP for Bolts.</p>
<p>Barnsley also announced and unveil their third kit for the 2016-2017 season on the club's official website on the 19 October 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The home shirt is the traditional red, with a white Puma logo and club badge on the chest area. There is a white Puma logo on each shoulder. The shorts are all white with a red Puma logo and club badge on the front. The socks are red and white hooped with a Puma logo on the knee/shin area. The socks of the upcoming 2016–17 season are very much like the socks that Barnsley wore when they were promoted to the Premier League.</p>
<p>The away shirt is navy blue, with a gold Puma logo and club badge on the chest area. There is a gold Puma logo on each shoulder. The shorts are all navy blue with a gold Puma logo and club badge on the front. The socks are navy blue, with a gold stripe at either side, front and back. There is a gold Puma logo on the knee/shin area. The letters 'BFC' are woven into the calf area of both socks, in gold.</p>
<p>The third kit is a white shirt with a v-neck collar, with a red trim on the sleeves, and the club badge on the front of the shirt. The shirt also includes the club's sponsors; CK Beckett logo is written on the front of the shirt in red. The Palmer logo is on the back of the shirt, and the Puma logo appears on the chest and sleeves of the shirt. The shorts are red with a white trim, which includes the sponsor logo, Bapp For Bolts, at the back of the shorts. The socks are white, the players were seen wearing white socks, when they were wearing the third kit, during a match against Cardiff in December 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Club_records">Club records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnsley_F.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Club records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><b>Record League Victory:</b> 9–0 v Loughborough Town, <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Second_Division" title="Football League Second Division">Second Division</a>, 28 January 1899</li>
<li><b>Record Cup Victory:</b> 6–0 v <a href="/wiki/Blackpool_F.C." title="Blackpool F.C.">Blackpool</a>, <a href="/wiki/FA_Cup" title="FA Cup">FA Cup</a> First round, 20 January 1910</li>
<li><b>Record League Defeat:</b> 0–9 v <a href="/wiki/Notts_County_F.C." title="Notts County F.C.">Notts County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Second_Division" title="Football League Second Division">Second Division</a>, 19 January 1927</li>
<li><b>Record Cup Defeat:</b> 1–8 v <a href="/wiki/Derby_County_F.C." title="Derby County F.C.">Derby County</a>, <a href="/wiki/FA_Cup" title="FA Cup">FA Cup</a> First round, 30 January 1897</li>
<li><b>Highest League Scorer in Season:</b> Cecil McCormack, 33, 1950–51</li>
<li><b>Most League Goals in Total Aggregate:</b> <a href="/wiki/Ernie_Hine" title="Ernie Hine">Ernie Hine</a>, 131<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Most Internationals Caps:</b> <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Taggart" title="Gerry Taggart">Gerry Taggart</a>, 35, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_national_football_team" title="Northern Ireland national football team">Northern Ireland</a></li>
<li><b>Most League Appearances:</b> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barry_Murphy_(footballer_born_1940)" title="Barry Murphy (footballer born 1940)">Barry Murphy</a>, 514, 1962–78</li>
<li><b>Record Transfer Fee Received:</b> £5,000,000 from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swansea_City_F.C." title="Swansea City F.C.">Swansea City</a> for <a href="/wiki/Alfie_Mawson" title="Alfie Mawson">Alfie Mawson</a>, August 2016</li>
<li><b>Record Transfer Fee Paid:</b> £1,500,000 to <a href="/wiki/FK_Partizan" title="FK Partizan">Partizan Belgrade</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C7%B4or%C7%B5i_Hristov" title="Ǵorǵi Hristov">Georgi Hristov</a> (1997) and £1,500,000 to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queens_Park_Rangers" title="Queens Park Rangers">QPR</a> for <a href="/wiki/Mike_Sheron" title="Mike Sheron">Mike Sheron</a> (1999)</li>
<li><b>Record Attendance:</b> 40,255 v Stoke City, FA Cup 5th round, 15 February 1936</li>
<li><b>Youngest ever Football League player:</b> <a href="/wiki/Reuben_Noble-Lazarus" title="Reuben Noble-Lazarus">Reuben Noble-Lazarus</a> 15 years and 45 days<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Oldest player:</b> <a href="/wiki/Mike_Pollitt" title="Mike Pollitt">Mike Pollitt</a> 41 years, 5 months and 30 days<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Most Goals scored in a single Game:</b> <a href="/wiki/Craig_Davies_(footballer)" title="Craig Davies (footballer)">Craig Davies</a> 4 vs Birmingham City F.C. Away, 0–5</li>
</ul>
