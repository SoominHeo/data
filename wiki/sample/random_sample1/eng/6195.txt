<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ring (jewellery)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>ring</b> is a round band, usually of <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>, worn as an ornamental piece of <a href="/wiki/Jewellery" title="Jewellery">jewellery</a> around the finger, or sometimes the toe; it is the most common current meaning of the word "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ring" title="wiktionary:ring">ring</a>". Strictly speaking a normal ring is a <b>finger ring</b> (which may be hyphenated); other types of rings worn as ornaments are <a href="/wiki/Earring" title="Earring">earrings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bracelet" title="Bracelet">bracelets</a> for the wrist, armlets or <a href="/wiki/Arm_ring" title="Arm ring">arm rings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toe_ring" title="Toe ring">toe rings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Torc" title="Torc">torc</a> or neck rings, but except perhaps for toe rings, the plain term "ring" is not normally used to refer to these.</p>
<p>Rings are most often made of metal but can be of almost any material: <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">stone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Gemstone" title="Gemstone">gemstone</a>. They may be set with a stone or stones, often a gemstone such as <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ruby" title="Ruby">ruby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sapphire" title="Sapphire">sapphire</a> or <a href="/wiki/Emerald" title="Emerald">emerald</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ring_(jewellery)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ring_parts.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="330" data-file-width="295" height="246" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Ring_parts.svg/220px-Ring_parts.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Ring_parts.svg/330px-Ring_parts.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Ring_parts.svg/440px-Ring_parts.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ring_parts.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The parts of a ring. 1: Hoop. 2: Shoulder. 3: <a href="/wiki/Bezel_(jewellery)" title="Bezel (jewellery)">Bezel</a>. 4: Stone or gem in setting/mounting.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Although tracing the exact history is difficult, the custom of giving and receiving finger rings dates back over approximately 6,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Near_East">Ancient Near East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ring_(jewellery)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient Near East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hittites" title="Hittites">Hittite civilization</a> produced rings, including signet rings, only a few of which have been discovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor31_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor31-2">[2]</a></sup> People in <a href="/wiki/Old_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="Old Kingdom of Egypt">Old Kingdom Egypt</a> wore a variety of finger rings, of which a few examples have been found, including the famous scarab design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor31_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor31-2">[2]</a></sup> Rings became more common during the Egyptian middle kingdom, with increasingly complex designs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor31_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor31-2">[2]</a></sup> Egyptians made metal rings but also made rings from <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_faience" title="Egyptian faience">faience</a> some of which were used as new year gifts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ogden60_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ogden60-3">[3]</a></sup> Native styles were superseded by Greek and Roman fashions during the Ptolemaic dynasty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor31_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor31-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaic_and_classical_Greek">Archaic and classical Greek</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ring_(jewellery)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Archaic and classical Greek">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Archaic Greek rings were to some extent influenced by Egyptian rings, although they tended to be less substantial and apparently weren't for the most part used as working signet rings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor33_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor33-4">[4]</a></sup> A lack of locally available gold meant that rings made in the eastern colonies tended to be made from silver and bronze while Etruria used gold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor33_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor33-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The classical period showed a shift away from bronze to wider adoption of silver and gold. The most typical design of the period involved a lozenge bezel mounting an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intaglio_(jewellery)" title="Intaglio (jewellery)">intaglio</a> device.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor34_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor34-5">[5]</a></sup> Over time the bezel moved towards a more circular form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor34_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor34-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_Rings">Roman Rings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ring_(jewellery)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Roman Rings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rings_from_the_snettisham_jewellers_hoard.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1666" data-file-width="2090" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Rings_from_the_snettisham_jewellers_hoard.JPG/220px-Rings_from_the_snettisham_jewellers_hoard.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Rings_from_the_snettisham_jewellers_hoard.JPG/330px-Rings_from_the_snettisham_jewellers_hoard.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Rings_from_the_snettisham_jewellers_hoard.JPG/440px-Rings_from_the_snettisham_jewellers_hoard.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rings_from_the_snettisham_jewellers_hoard.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Henig II rings from the <a href="/wiki/Snettisham_Jeweller%27s_Hoard" title="Snettisham Jeweller's Hoard">Snettisham Jeweller's Hoard</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During the early and middle <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">imperial era</a> (first two centuries AD) the closest there is to a typical Roman ring consisted of a thick hoop that tapered directly into a slightly wider bezel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johns42_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-johns42-6">[6]</a></sup> An engraved oval gem would be embedded within the bezel with the top of the gem only rising slightly above the surrounding ring material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johns42_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-johns42-6">[6]</a></sup> Such rings are referred to Henig II and III/Guiraud 2 in formal academic parlance or simply as Roman rings by modern jewellers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johns42_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-johns42-6">[6]</a></sup> In general Roman rings became more elaborate in the third and fourth centuries AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johns48_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-johns48-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Finger-ring_wearing.2C_choice_of_fingers">Finger-ring wearing, choice of fingers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ring_(jewellery)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Finger-ring wearing, choice of fingers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The fourth digit or <a href="/wiki/Ring_finger" title="Ring finger">ring finger</a> of the left hand has become the customary place to wear a <a href="/wiki/Wedding_ring" title="Wedding ring">wedding ring</a> in much of the world, though in certain countries the right hand finger is used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This custom was practically established as the norm during World War II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The use of the fourth finger of the left hand (the 'ring finger') is associated with an old belief that the left hand's ring finger is connected by a vein directly to the heart: the <i><a href="/wiki/Vena_amoris" title="Vena amoris">vena amoris</a></i> or vein of love. This idea was known in 16th and 17th century England, when <a href="/wiki/Henry_Swinburne_(lawyer)" title="Henry Swinburne (lawyer)">Henry Swinburne</a> referred to it in his book about marriage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It can be traced back to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Rome</a>, when <a href="/wiki/Aulus_Gellius" title="Aulus Gellius">Aulus Gellius</a> cited <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Appianus" title="Appianus">Appianus</a> as saying the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egyptians</a> had found a fine nerve linking that particular finger to the heart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Occasionally rings have been re-purposed to hang from bracelets or necklaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Church55_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Church55-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Size">Size</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ring_(jewellery)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Size">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ring_size" title="Ring size">Ring size</a></div>
<p>While the <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a> standard defines ring size in terms of the inner circumference in millimeters various countries have traditional sizing systems that are still used.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Styles">Styles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ring_(jewellery)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Styles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<td>Five Metals ring</td>
<td></td>
<td>Five Metals ring (a.k.a. "<a href="/wiki/Kabbalah" title="Kabbalah">Kabbalah</a> ring") is suggested by the <i>Talmud</i> as a talisman for luck, and is considered a Kabbalistic appurtenance. Five metals mined from the earth (gold, silver, copper, tin and lead) are to be joined but not alloyed into a ring. It can only be manufactured once a month, "when the moon is in Jupiter", over a five-hour period from 1 p.m. to 6 p.m. and only upon the conclusion of the Sabbath (Saturday night). The Five Metals ring has commonly taken the form of a sterling silver ring with tablets of gold, tin, lead and copper set into the bezel. The <i>Talmud</i> states, "Bear [the ring] upon thee, and thou shalt see miracles." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></td>

<td><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ringrosary.JPG"><img alt="Ringrosary.JPG" data-file-height="623" data-file-width="481" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Ringrosary.JPG/120px-Ringrosary.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Ringrosary.JPG/180px-Ringrosary.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Ringrosary.JPG/240px-Ringrosary.JPG 2x" width="120"/></a></td>
<td>Also known as a <b>Decade ring</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ash1_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-ash1-21">[21]</a></sup> Ring worn around the finger with 10 indentations (or protrusions) and a cross as a bezel, representing one decade of a rosary. The rings are used to keep track of place in the prayer by rotating the ring on a finger and feeling the marks.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signet_ring" title="Signet ring">Signet ring</a></td>
<td><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Baronnet-signet-ring.JPG"><img alt="Baronnet-signet-ring.JPG" data-file-height="718" data-file-width="976" height="88" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Baronnet-signet-ring.JPG/120px-Baronnet-signet-ring.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Baronnet-signet-ring.JPG/180px-Baronnet-signet-ring.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Baronnet-signet-ring.JPG/240px-Baronnet-signet-ring.JPG 2x" width="120"/></a></td>
<td>An emblematic ring, often bearing a family coat of arms, some of which are fit for use to imprint a wax <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seal_(device)" title="Seal (device)">seal</a>. In the event a seal or at least a representation of a seal is on the ring, it is called a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seal_(device)" title="Seal (device)">seal</a> ring". The signet may bear anything from a custom-designed <a href="/wiki/Escutcheon_(heraldry)" title="Escutcheon (heraldry)">escutcheon</a> to simple initials, in which case it is known as an <i>initial ring</i>.</td>
</tr>

<td>Sovereign ring</td>
<td></td>
<td>A large solid gold ring set with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gold_sovereign" title="Gold sovereign">gold sovereign</a>.</td>
</tr>

<td><a href="/wiki/SS-Ehrenring" title="SS-Ehrenring">SS-Ehrenring</a></td>
<td></td>
<td>Nazi "honour ring" or "ring of honour". A plain silver band decorated with a death's head. Awarded to members of the Nazi SS (Schutzstaffel). A similar ring (in the form of a death's head) was also favoured by the SS-SD (Schutzstaffel-Sicherheitsdienst), and was very secretive in design. There were in fact several different award rings during the Third Reich.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></td>
</tr>

<td><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WLA_vanda_jade_thumb_ring.jpg"><img alt="WLA vanda jade thumb ring.jpg" data-file-height="1901" data-file-width="2385" height="96" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/WLA_vanda_jade_thumb_ring.jpg/120px-WLA_vanda_jade_thumb_ring.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/WLA_vanda_jade_thumb_ring.jpg/180px-WLA_vanda_jade_thumb_ring.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/WLA_vanda_jade_thumb_ring.jpg/240px-WLA_vanda_jade_thumb_ring.jpg 2x" width="120"/></a></td>
<td>Originally thumb rings were used as an <a href="/wiki/Archery" title="Archery">archery</a> implement mainly in eastern styles of archery. Thumb rings are an extremely ancient custom.</td>

</tr>

<td>Watch ring</td>
<td><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RingWatch.png"><img alt="RingWatch.png" data-file-height="1528" data-file-width="1828" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/RingWatch.png/120px-RingWatch.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/RingWatch.png/180px-RingWatch.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/RingWatch.png/240px-RingWatch.png 2x" width="120"/></a></td>
