<p>In classical <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a>, a <b>radius</b> of a <a href="/wiki/Circle" title="Circle">circle</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">sphere</a> is any of the <a href="/wiki/Line_segment" title="Line segment">line segments</a> from its <a href="/wiki/Centre_(geometry)" title="Centre (geometry)">center</a> to its <a href="/wiki/Perimeter" title="Perimeter">perimeter</a>, and in more modern usage, it is the length of any of them. The name comes from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>r</i><i>adius</i>, meaning "ray" but also the spoke of a chariot wheel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radic_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-radic-1">[1]</a></sup> The plural of <i>radius</i> can be either <i>radii</i> (from the Latin plural) or the conventional English plural <i>radiuses</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The typical abbreviation and <a href="/wiki/Variable_(mathematics)" title="Variable (mathematics)">mathematic variable</a> name for "radius" is <b>r</b>. By extension, the <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">diameter</a> <b>d</b> is defined as twice the radius:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwd1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-mwd1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>If an object does not have a center, the term may refer to its <b>circumradius</b>, the radius of its <a href="/wiki/Circumscribed_circle" title="Circumscribed circle">circumscribed circle</a> or <a href="/wiki/Circumscribed_sphere" title="Circumscribed sphere">circumscribed sphere</a>. In either case, the radius may be more than half the diameter, which is usually defined as the maximum distance between any two points of the figure. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inradius" title="Inradius">inradius</a> of a geometric figure is usually the radius of the largest circle or sphere contained in it. The inner radius of a ring, tube or other hollow object is the radius of its cavity.</p>
<p>For regular polygons, the radius is the same as its circumradius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schaum_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-schaum-4">[4]</a></sup> The inradius of a regular polygon is also called <a href="/wiki/Apothem" title="Apothem">apothem</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Graph_theory" title="Graph theory">graph theory</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radius_(graph_theory)" title="Radius (graph theory)">radius of a graph</a> is the minimum over all vertices <i>u</i> of the maximum distance from <i>u</i> to any other vertex of the graph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yel_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-yel-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_coordinate_systems">Use in coordinate systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Use in coordinate systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polar_coordinates">Polar coordinates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Polar coordinates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Polar_coordinate_system" title="Polar coordinate system">Polar coordinate system</a></div>
<p>The polar coordinate system is a <a href="/wiki/Two-dimensional_space" title="Two-dimensional space">two</a>-<a href="/wiki/Dimension" title="Dimension">dimensional</a> <a href="/wiki/Coordinate_system" title="Coordinate system">coordinate system</a> in which each <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point_(mathematics)" title="Point (mathematics)">point</a> on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plane_(mathematics)" title="Plane (mathematics)">plane</a> is determined by a <a href="/wiki/Distance" title="Distance">distance</a> from a fixed point and an <a href="/wiki/Angle" title="Angle">angle</a> from a fixed direction.</p>
<p>The fixed point (analogous to the origin of a <a href="/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system" title="Cartesian coordinate system">Cartesian system</a>) is called the <i>pole</i>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ray_(geometry)" title="Ray (geometry)">ray</a> from the pole in the fixed direction is the <i>polar axis</i>. The distance from the pole is called the <i>radial coordinate</i> or <i>radius</i>, and the angle is the <i>angular coordinate</i>, <i>polar angle</i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Azimuth" title="Azimuth">azimuth</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-brown-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cylindrical_coordinates">Cylindrical coordinates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cylindrical coordinates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cylindrical_coordinate_system" title="Cylindrical coordinate system">Cylindrical coordinate system</a></div>
<p>In the cylindrical coordinate system, there is a chosen reference axis and a chosen reference plane perpendicular to that axis. The <i>origin</i> of the system is the point where all three coordinates can be given as zero. This is the intersection between the reference plane and the axis.</p>
<p>The axis is variously called the <i>cylindrical</i> or <i>longitudinal</i> axis, to differentiate it from the <i>polar axis</i>, which is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Line_(mathematics)#Ray" title="Line (mathematics)">ray</a> that lies in the reference plane, starting at the origin and pointing in the reference direction.</p>
<p>The distance from the axis may be called the <i>radial distance</i> or <i>radius</i>, while the angular coordinate is sometimes referred to as the <i>angular position</i> or as the <i>azimuth</i>. The radius and the azimuth are together called the <i>polar coordinates</i>, as they correspond to a two-dimensional polar coordinate system in the plane through the point, parallel to the reference plane. The third coordinate may be called the <i>height</i> or <i>altitude</i> (if the reference plane is considered horizontal), <i>longitudinal position</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> or <i>axial position</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spherical_coordinates">Spherical coordinates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Spherical coordinates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system" title="Spherical coordinate system">Spherical coordinate system</a></div>
<p>In a spherical coordinate system, the radius describes the distance of a point from a fixed origin. Its position if further defined by the polar angle measured between the radial direction and a fixed zenith direction, and the azimuth angle, that is, the angle between the orthogonal projection of the radial direction on a reference plane that passes through the origin and is orthogonal to the zenith, and a fixed reference direction in that plane.</p>
