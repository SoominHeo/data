<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, specifically in <a href="/wiki/Group_theory" title="Group theory">group theory</a>, the concept of a <b>semidirect product</b> is a generalization of a <a href="/wiki/Direct_product_of_groups" title="Direct product of groups">direct product</a>. There are two closely related concepts of semidirect product: an <i>inner</i> semidirect product is a particular way in which a <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">group</a> can be constructed from two <a href="/wiki/Subgroup" title="Subgroup">subgroups</a>, one of which is a <a href="/wiki/Normal_subgroup" title="Normal subgroup">normal subgroup</a>, while an <i>outer</i> semidirect product is a <a href="/wiki/Cartesian_product" title="Cartesian product">Cartesian product</a> as a set, but with a particular multiplication operation. As with direct products, there is a natural equivalence between inner and outer semidirect products, and both are commonly referred to simply as <i>semidirect products</i>.</p>
<p>For <a href="/wiki/Finite_group" title="Finite group">finite groups</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Schur%E2%80%93Zassenhaus_theorem" title="Schur–Zassenhaus theorem">Schur–Zassenhaus theorem</a> provides a sufficient condition for the existence of a decomposition as a semidirect product (a.k.a. <a href="/wiki/Group_extension#Classifying_split_extensions" title="Group extension">split[ting] extension</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inner_semidirect_product_definitions">Inner semidirect product definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semidirect_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Inner semidirect product definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Given a group <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i></span> with <a href="/wiki/Identity_element" title="Identity element">identity element</a> <span class="texhtml"><i>e</i></span>, a <a href="/wiki/Subgroup" title="Subgroup">subgroup</a> <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span>, and a <a href="/wiki/Normal_subgroup" title="Normal subgroup">normal subgroup</a> <span class="nowrap"><span class="texhtml"><i>N</i> ◁ <i>G</i></span></span>; then the following statements are equivalent:</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="texhtml"><i>G</i></span> is the product of subgroups, <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i> = <i>NH</i></span>, where the subgroups have trivial intersection, <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i> ∩ <i>H</i> = {<i>e</i></span>}.</li>
<li>For every <span class="texhtml"><i>g</i></span> ∈ <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i></span>, there are unique <span class="nowrap"><span class="texhtml"><i>n</i> ∈ <i>N</i></span></span> and <span class="nowrap"><span class="texhtml"><i>h</i> ∈ <i>H</i></span></span>, such that <span class="texhtml"><i>g</i> = <i>nh</i></span>.</li>
<li>For every <span class="texhtml"><i>g</i></span> ∈ <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i></span>, there are unique <span class="nowrap"><span class="texhtml"><i>h</i> ∈ <i>H</i></span></span> and <span class="nowrap"><span class="texhtml"><i>n</i> ∈ <i>N</i></span></span>, such that <span class="texhtml"><i>g</i> = <i>hn</i></span>.</li>
<li>The composition <span class="texhtml"><i>π</i> ∘ <i>i</i></span> of the natural embedding <span class="nowrap"><span class="texhtml"><i>i</i>: <i>H</i> → <i>G</i></span></span> and the natural projection <span class="nowrap"><span class="texhtml">π: <i>G</i> → <i>G</i> / <i>N</i></span></span> is an <a href="/wiki/Group_isomorphism" title="Group isomorphism">isomorphism</a> between <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span> and the <a href="/wiki/Quotient_group" title="Quotient group">quotient group</a> <span class="nowrap"><span class="texhtml"><i>G</i> / <i>N</i></span></span>.</li>
<li>There exists a <a href="/wiki/Group_homomorphism" title="Group homomorphism">homomorphism</a> <span class="nowrap"><span class="texhtml"><i>G</i> → <i>H</i></span></span> that is the identity on <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span> and whose <a href="/wiki/Kernel_(algebra)" title="Kernel (algebra)">kernel</a> is <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span>.</li>
</ul>
<p>If these statements hold, we say <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i></span> is the <b>semidirect product</b> of <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span>, written</p>

</dl>
<p>or that <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i></span> <i>splits</i> over <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span>; one also says that <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i></span> is a <b>semidirect</b> product of <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span> acting on <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span>, or even a semidirect product of <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span>. To avoid ambiguity, it is advisable to specify which is the normal subgroup.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outer_semidirect_products">Outer semidirect products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semidirect_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Outer semidirect products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Let <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i></span> be a semidirect product of the normal subgroup <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span> and the subgroup <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span>. Let <span class="texhtml">Aut(<i>N</i>)</span> denote the group of all <a href="/wiki/Automorphism" title="Automorphism">automorphisms</a> of <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span>. The map <span class="texhtml"><i>φ</i>: <i>H</i> → Aut(<i>N</i>)</span> defined by <span class="texhtml"><i>φ</i>(<i>h</i>) = <i>φ</i><sub><i>h</i></sub></span>, conjugation by <span class="texhtml"><i>h</i></span>, where <span class="texhtml"><i>φ</i>(<i>h</i>)(<i>n</i>) = <i>φ</i><sub><i>h</i></sub>(<i>n</i>) = <i>hnh</i><sup>−1</sup></span> for all <span class="texhtml"><i>h</i></span> in <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>n</i></span> in <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span>, is a <a href="/wiki/Group_homomorphism" title="Group homomorphism">group homomorphism</a>. (Note that <span class="texhtml"><i>hnh</i><sup>−1</sup> ∈ <i>N</i></span> since <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span> is normal in <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i></span>.) Together <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span>, <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span>, and <span class="texhtml"><i>φ</i></span> determine <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i></span> <a href="/wiki/Up_to" title="Up to">up to</a> isomorphism, as we show now.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_group" title="Euclidean group">Euclidean group</a> of all rigid motions (<a href="/wiki/Isometry" title="Isometry">isometries</a>) of the plane (maps <span class="texhtml"><i>f</i>: ℝ<sup>2</sup> → ℝ<sup>2</sup></span> such that the Euclidean distance between <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>y</i></span> equals the distance between <span class="texhtml"><i>f</i>(<i>x</i>)</span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>f</i>(<i>y</i>)</span> for all <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>y</i></span> in <span class="texhtml">ℝ<sup>2</sup></span>) is isomorphic to a semidirect product of the abelian group <span class="texhtml">ℝ<sup>2</sup></span> (which describes translations) and the group <span class="texhtml">O(2)</span> of <a href="/wiki/Orthogonal_matrix" title="Orthogonal matrix">orthogonal</a> <span class="texhtml">2 × 2</span> matrices (which describes rotations and reflections that keep the origin fixed). Applying a translation and then a rotation or reflection has the same effect as applying the rotation or reflection first and then a translation by the rotated or reflected translation vector (i.e. applying the <a href="/wiki/Conjugation_of_isometries_in_Euclidean_space" title="Conjugation of isometries in Euclidean space">conjugate</a> of the original translation). This shows that the group of translations is a normal subgroup of the Euclidean group, that the Euclidean group is a semidirect product of the translation group and <span class="texhtml">O(2)</span>, and that the corresponding homomorphism <span class="texhtml"><i>φ</i>: O(2) → Aut(ℝ<sup>2</sup>)</span> is given by <a href="/wiki/Matrix_multiplication" title="Matrix multiplication">matrix multiplication</a>: <span class="texhtml"><i>φ</i>(<i>h</i>)(<i>n</i>) = <i>hn</i></span>.</p>

If we represent <span class="texhtml">C<sub>2</sub></span> as the multiplicative group of matrices <span class="texhtml">{<i>I</i>, <i>R</i>}</span>, where <span class="texhtml"><i>R</i></span> is a reflection of <span class="texhtml"><i>n</i></span>-dimensional space that keeps the origin fixed (i.e., an orthogonal matrix with determinant <span class="texhtml">–1</span> representing an <a href="/wiki/Involution_(mathematics)" title="Involution (mathematics)">involution</a>), then <span class="texhtml"><i>φ</i>: C<sub>2</sub> → Aut(SO(<i>n</i>))</span> is given by <span class="texhtml"><i>φ</i>(<i>H</i>)(<i>N</i>) = <i>HNH</i><sup>−1</sup></span> for all <i>H</i> in <span class="texhtml">C<sub>2</sub></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span> in <span class="texhtml">SO(<i>n</i>)</span>. In the non-trivial case (<span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span> is not the identity) this means that <span class="texhtml"><i>φ</i>(<i>H</i>)</span> is conjugation of operations by the reflection (a rotation axis and the direction of rotation are replaced by their "mirror image").</p>
<p>The group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semilinear_transformation" title="Semilinear transformation">semilinear transformations</a> on a vector space <span class="texhtml"><i>V</i></span> over a field <span class="texhtml">𝕂</span>, often denoted <span class="texhtml">ΓL(<i>V</i>)</span>, is isomorphic to a semidirect product of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear_group" title="Linear group">linear group</a> <span class="texhtml">GL(<i>V</i>)</span> (a <a href="/wiki/Normal_subgroup" title="Normal subgroup">normal subgroup</a> of <span class="texhtml">ΓL(<i>V</i>)</span>), and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automorphism_group" title="Automorphism group">automorphism group</a> of <span class="texhtml">𝕂</span>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semidirect_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a consequence of <a href="/wiki/Lagrange%27s_theorem_(group_theory)" title="Lagrange's theorem (group theory)">Lagrange's theorem</a>, if <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i></span> is the semidirect product of the normal subgroup <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span> and the subgroup <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span>, and both <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span> are finite, then the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Order_of_a_group" title="Order of a group">order</a> of <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i></span> equals the product of the orders of <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_direct_products">Relation to direct products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semidirect_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relation to direct products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Suppose <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i></span> is a semidirect product of the normal subgroup <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span> and the subgroup <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span>. If <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span> is also normal in <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i></span>, or equivalently, if there exists a homomorphism <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i> → <i>N</i></span> that is the identity on <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span>, then <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i></span> is the <a href="/wiki/Direct_product_of_groups" title="Direct product of groups">direct product</a> of <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span>.</p>
<p>The direct product of two groups <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span> can be thought of as the semidirect product of <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span> with respect to <span class="texhtml"><i>φ</i>(<i>h</i>) = id<sub><i>N</i></sub></span> for all <span class="texhtml"><i>h</i></span> in <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span>.</p>
<p>Note that in a direct product, the order of the factors is not important, since <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i> × <i>H</i></span> is isomorphic to <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i> × <i>N</i></span>. This is not the case for semidirect products, as the two factors play different roles.</p>
<p>Furthermore, the result of a (proper) semidirect product by means of a non-trivial homomorphism is never an <a href="/wiki/Abelian_group" title="Abelian group">abelian group</a>, even if the factor groups are abelian.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-uniqueness_of_semidirect_products_.28and_further_examples.29">Non-uniqueness of semidirect products (and further examples)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semidirect_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Non-uniqueness of semidirect products (and further examples)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As opposed to the case with the <a href="/wiki/Direct_product_of_groups" title="Direct product of groups">direct product</a>, a semidirect product of two groups is not, in general, unique; if <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>G′</i></span> are two groups which both contain isomorphic copies of <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span> as a normal subgroup and <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span> as a subgroup, and both are a semidirect product of <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span>, then it does <i>not</i> follow that <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>G′</i></span> are <a href="/wiki/Group_isomorphism" title="Group isomorphism">isomorphic</a> because the semidirect product also depends on the choice of an action of <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span> on <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span>.</p>
<p>For example, there are four non-isomorphic groups of order 16 that are [semi]direct product of <span class="texhtml">C<sub>8</sub></span> and <span class="texhtml">C<sub>2</sub></span>; <span class="texhtml">C<sub>8</sub></span> is necessarily a normal subgroup in this case because it has index 2 in a group of order 16. One of these four [semi]direct products is the direct product, while the other three are non-abelian groups:</p>
<ul>
<li>the dihedral group of order 16</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Quasidihedral_group" title="Quasidihedral group">quasidihedral group</a> of order 16</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Iwasawa_group" title="Iwasawa group">Iwasawa group</a> of order 16 (<a class="external text" href="http://groupprops.subwiki.org/wiki/M16" rel="nofollow">M16</a>)</li>
</ul>
<p>For comparative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cayley_diagram" title="Cayley diagram">Cayley diagrams</a> of these see Example 3 in <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.weddslist.com/groups/building/sdp.html" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>.</p>
<p>If a given group is a semidirect product, then there is no guarantee that this decomposition is unique. For example, there is a group of order 24 (the only one containing six elements of order 4 and six elements of order 6) that can be expressed as semidirect product in the following ways: <span class="texhtml">(D<sub>8</sub> ⋉ C<sub>3</sub>) ≃ (C<sub>2</sub> ⋉ <a href="/wiki/Dicyclic_group" title="Dicyclic group">Q<sub>12</sub></a>) ≃ (C<sub>2</sub> ⋉ D<sub>12</sub>) ≃ (D<sub>6</sub> ⋉ <a href="/wiki/Klein_four-group" title="Klein four-group">V</a>)</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rose2009_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rose2009-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Existence">Existence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semidirect_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Existence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Schur%E2%80%93Zassenhaus_theorem" title="Schur–Zassenhaus theorem">Schur–Zassenhaus theorem</a></div>
<p>In general, there's no known characterization (necessary and sufficient condition) for the existence of semidirect products in groups. However, some sufficient conditions are known, which guarantee existence in certain cases. For finite groups, the <a href="/wiki/Schur%E2%80%93Zassenhaus_theorem" title="Schur–Zassenhaus theorem">Schur–Zassenhaus theorem</a> guarantees existence of a semidirect product when the <a href="/wiki/Order_(group_theory)" title="Order (group theory)">order</a> of the normal subgroup is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coprime" title="Coprime">coprime</a> to the order of the <a href="/wiki/Quotient_group" title="Quotient group">quotient group</a>.</p>
<p>For example, the Schur–Zassenhaus theorem implies the existence of a semi-direct product among groups of order 6; there are two such products, one of which is a direct product, and the other a dihedral group. In contrast, the Schur–Zassenhaus theorem does not say anything about groups of order 4 or groups of order 8 for instance.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Generalizations">Generalizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semidirect_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Generalizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The construction of semidirect products can be pushed much further. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zappa%E2%80%93Szep_product" title="Zappa–Szep product">Zappa–Szep product</a> of groups is a generalization which, in its internal version, does not assume that either subgroup is normal. There is also a construction in <a href="/wiki/Ring_theory" title="Ring theory">ring theory</a>, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Crossed_product_of_rings&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Crossed product of rings (page does not exist)">crossed product of rings</a>. This is seen naturally as soon as one constructs a <a href="/wiki/Group_ring" title="Group ring">group ring</a> for a semidirect product of groups. There is also the semidirect sum of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lie_algebras" title="Lie algebras">Lie algebras</a>. Given a <a href="/wiki/Group_action" title="Group action">group action</a> on a <a href="/wiki/Topological_space" title="Topological space">topological space</a>, there is a corresponding crossed product which will in general be non-commutative even if the group is abelian. This kind of ring (see <a href="/wiki/Crossed_product" title="Crossed product">crossed product</a> for a related construction) can play the role of the <i>space of orbits</i> of the group action, in cases where that space cannot be approached by conventional topological techniques – for example in the work of <a href="/wiki/Alain_Connes" title="Alain Connes">Alain Connes</a> (cf. <a href="/wiki/Noncommutative_geometry" title="Noncommutative geometry">noncommutative geometry</a>).</p>
<p>There are also far-reaching generalisations in <a href="/wiki/Category_theory" title="Category theory">category theory</a>. They show how to construct <i><a href="/wiki/Fibred_category" title="Fibred category">fibred categories</a></i> from <i><a href="/wiki/Indexed_category" title="Indexed category">indexed categories</a></i>. This is an abstract form of the outer semidirect product construction.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Groupoids">Groupoids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semidirect_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Groupoids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another generalization is for groupoids. This occurs in topology because if a group <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i></span> acts on a space <span class="texhtml"><i>X</i></span> it also acts on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_groupoid" title="Fundamental groupoid">fundamental groupoid</a> <span class="texhtml"><i>π</i><sub>1</sub>(<i>X</i>)</span> of the space. The semidirect product <span class="texhtml"><i>π</i><sub>1</sub>(<i>X</i>) ⋊ <i>G</i></span> is then relevant to finding the fundamental groupoid of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbit_space" title="Orbit space">orbit space</a> <span class="texhtml"><i>X/G</i></span>. For full details see Chapter 11 of the book referenced below, and also some details in semidirect product<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/NLab" title="NLab">ncatlab</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Abelian_categories">Abelian categories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semidirect_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Abelian categories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Non-trivial semidirect products do <i>not</i> arise in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abelian_categories" title="Abelian categories">abelian categories</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Category_of_modules" title="Category of modules">category of modules</a>. In this case, the <a href="/wiki/Splitting_lemma" title="Splitting lemma">splitting lemma</a> shows that every semidirect product is a direct product. Thus the existence of semidirect products reflects a failure of the category to be abelian.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notation">Notation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semidirect_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Notation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Usually the semidirect product of a group <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i></span> acting on a group <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span> (in most cases by conjugation as subgroups of a common group) is denoted by <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i> ⋊ <i>H</i></span> or <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i> ⋉ <i>N</i></span>. However, some sources may use this symbol with the opposite meaning. In case the action <span class="texhtml"><i>φ</i>: <i>H</i> → Aut(<i>N</i>)</span> should be made explicit, one also writes <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i> ⋊<sub><i>φ</i></sub> <i>H</i></span>. One way of thinking about the <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i> ⋊ <i>H</i></span> symbol is as a combination of the symbol for normal subgroup (<span class="texhtml">◁</span>) and the symbol for the product (<span class="texhtml">×</span>).</p>
