<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Twinkling</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Twinkle_(disambiguation)" title="Twinkle (disambiguation)">Twinkle (disambiguation)</a> and <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Scintillation_(disambiguation)" title="Scintillation (disambiguation)">Scintillation</a>.</div>
<p><b>Twinkling</b>, or <b>scintillation</b>, is a generic term for variations in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apparent_brightness" title="Apparent brightness">apparent brightness</a> or position of a distant luminous object viewed through a medium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intech_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-intech-1">[1]</a></sup> If the object lies outside the Earth's atmosphere, as in the case of stars and planets, the phenomenon is termed <i>astronomical scintillation;</i> within the atmosphere, the phenomenon is termed <i>terrestrial scintillation.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> As one of the three principal factors governing <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_seeing" title="Astronomical seeing">astronomical seeing</a>, atmospheric twinkling is defined as variations in <a href="/wiki/Illuminance" title="Illuminance">illuminance</a> only.</p>
<p>In simple terms, twinkling of stars is caused by the passing of light through different layers of a turbulent atmosphere. Most scintillation effects are caused by anomalous <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refraction</a> caused by small-scale fluctuations in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_density" title="Air density">air density</a> usually related to temperature gradients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Scintillation effects are always much more pronounced near the horizon than near the <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">zenith</a> (straight up),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> since the light near the horizon passes through a thicker layer of atmosphere. Atmospheric twinkling is measured quantitatively using a <a href="/wiki/Scintillometer" title="Scintillometer">scintillometer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The effects of twinkling are reduced by using a larger receiver <a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">aperture</a>. This effect is known as <i>aperture averaging</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>While light from planets, stars and other astronomical objects are likely to twinkle,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> twinkling does not cause images of planets to flicker appreciably.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stars twinkle because they are so far from Earth that they appear as point sources of light easily disturbed by Earth's atmospheric turbulence which acts like lenses and prisms <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">diverting</a> the light's path. Large astronomical objects closer to Earth, like the Moon and other planets, encompass many points in space and can be resolved as objects with observable diameters. With multiple observed points of light traversing the atmosphere, their light's deviations average out and the viewer perceives less variation in light coming from them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
