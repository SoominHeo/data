<p><i><b>Banchan</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>: <span lang="ko-Hang">반찬</span>; <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">Hanja</a>: <span lang="ko-Hani">飯饌</span>; <span class="nowrap"><small>English pronunciation:</small> <span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/tʃ/ 'ch' in 'china'">tʃ</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>; also spelled <i><b>panch'an</b></i>) refers to small dishes of food served along with cooked rice in <a href="/wiki/Korean_cuisine" title="Korean cuisine">Korean cuisine</a>. This word is used both in the singular and plural. The word Banchan translated into English means side dish.</p>
<p>The basic table setting for a meal called <i>bansang</i> (반상) usually consists of <i><a href="/wiki/Bap_(food)" title="Bap (food)">bap</a></i> (밥, cooked rice), <i><a href="/wiki/Guk" title="Guk">guk</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Guk" title="Guk">tang</a></i> (soup), <i><a href="/wiki/Gochujang" title="Gochujang">gochujang</a></i> or <a href="/wiki/Soy_sauce" title="Soy sauce"><i>ganjang</i></a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Jjigae" title="Jjigae">jjigae</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Kimchi" title="Kimchi">kimchi</a></i>. According to the number of <i>banchan</i> added, the table setting is called <i>3 cheop</i> (삼첩), <i>5 cheop</i> (오첩), <i>7 cheop</i> (칠첩), <i>9 cheop</i> (구첩), <i>12 cheop</i> (십이첩) <i>bansang</i>, with the <i>12 cheop</i> used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_royal_cuisine" title="Korean royal cuisine">Korean royal cuisine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Banchan</i> are set in the middle of the table to be shared. At the center of the table is the secondary main course, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Galbi" title="Galbi">galbi</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Bulgogi" title="Bulgogi">bulgogi</a></i>, and a shared pot of <i><a href="/wiki/Jjigae" title="Jjigae">jjigae</a></i>. Bowls of cooked rice and <i>guk</i> (soup) are set individually. <i>Banchan</i> are served in small portions, meant to be finished at each meal and are replenished during the meal if not enough. Usually, the more formal the meals are, the more <i>banchan</i> there will be. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeolla" title="Jeolla">Jeolla</a> province is particularly famous for serving many different varieties of <i>banchan</i> in a single meal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Banchan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Banchan</i> is thought to be a result of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhism_in_Korea" title="Buddhism in Korea">Buddhist</a> influence at around the mid-<a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms_of_Korea" title="Three Kingdoms of Korea">Three Kingdoms</a> period and the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_vegetarianism" title="Buddhist vegetarianism">proscription against eating meat</a> by the monarchies of these kingdoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hannaone_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hannaone-3">[3]</a></sup> Thus, with the ban on meat-containing dishes, vegetable-based dishes rose in prominence and became the centrepoint of Korean cuisine;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hannaone_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hannaone-3">[3]</a></sup> court kitchens developed various methods for cooking, preparing and presenting these dishes, while less-affluent commoners produced smaller, simpler arrays of these vegetable-based dishes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hannaone_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hannaone-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the <a href="/wiki/Mongol_invasions_of_Korea" title="Mongol invasions of Korea">Mongol invasions of Korea</a> ended the ban on meat-containing dishes, as well as meat offerings for rituals such as <i><a href="/wiki/Jesa" title="Jesa">jesa</a></i>, approximately six centuries of vegetable-based cuisine in the form of <i>banchan</i> had imprinted itself into Korean cuisine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hannaone_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hannaone-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kimchi">Kimchi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Banchan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Kimchi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kimchi" title="Kimchi">Kimchi</a></div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Kimchi" title="Kimchi">Kimchi</a> is fermented vegetables, usually <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baechu" title="Baechu">baechu</a></i> (Napa cabbage), seasoned with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chili_peppers" title="Chili peppers">chili peppers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a>. This is the essential <i>banchan</i> of a standard Korean meal. Some Koreans do not consider a meal complete without kimchi. Kimchi can be made with other vegetables as well, including <a href="/wiki/Scallion" title="Scallion">scallions</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Brassica_juncea" title="Brassica juncea">gat</a></i> (갓), and <a href="/wiki/Korean_radish" title="Korean radish">radish</a> (무; <i>mu</i>).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bokkeum">Bokkeum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Banchan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bokkeum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bokkeum" title="Bokkeum">Bokkeum</a> (볶음) is a dish stir-fried with sauce.</p>
<ul>
<li>Kimchi bokkeum (김치볶음) - Stir-fried kimchi, often with pork<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lk_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-lk-15">[15]</a></sup> (similar to <i>jeyook bokkeum</i>).</li>
<li>Jeyook bokkeum (제육볶음) - Stir-fried pork with <a href="/wiki/Gochujang" title="Gochujang">gochujang</a> (chili pepper paste) sauce and onions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ojingeochae_bokkeum" title="Ojingeochae bokkeum">Ojingeochae bokkeum</a> (오징어채볶음) — Stir-fried <a href="/wiki/Dried_shredded_squid" title="Dried shredded squid">dried shredded squid</a> seasoned with a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Gochujang" title="Gochujang">gochujang</a> (chili pepper paste), garlic, and <i>mullyeot</i> (syrup-like condiment).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nakji_bokkeum" title="Nakji bokkeum">Nakji bokkeum</a> (낙지볶음) - Stir-fried baby octopus in spicy <a href="/wiki/Gochujang" title="Gochujang">gochujang</a> sauce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lk_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-lk-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>Buseot bokkeum (버섯볶음) - Stir-fried mushrooms such as <a href="/wiki/Shiitake" title="Shiitake"><i>pyogo</i></a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oyster_mushrooms" title="Oyster mushrooms">oyster mushrooms</a>, pine mushrooms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lk_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-lk-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jorim">Jorim</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Banchan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Jorim">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jorim" title="Jorim">Jorim</a> is a dish simmered in a seasoned broth.</p>
<ul>
<li>Dubu-jorim (두부조림) — <a href="/wiki/Tofu" title="Tofu">Tofu</a> simmered in diluted soy sauce, a little bit of sesame oil, minced garlic, and chopped green onion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>Jang-jorim (장조림) — Beef simmered in soy sauce, optionally with hard-boiled eggs or hard-boiled quail eggs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jjim">Jjim</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Banchan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Jjim">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jjim" title="Jjim">Jjim</a> is a steamed dish.</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gyeran_jjim" title="Gyeran jjim">Gyeran jjim</a> (계란찜) — Mixed and seasoned eggs steamed in a hot pot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>Saengseon jjim (생선찜)- Steamed fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jeon">Jeon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Banchan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Jeon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Jeon_(food)" title="Jeon (food)">Jeon</a></i> denotes a variety of pan-fried, <a href="/wiki/Pancake" title="Pancake">pancake</a>-like dishes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pancake_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-pancake-22">[22]</a></sup> <i>Buchimgae</i> is a near <a href="/wiki/Synonym" title="Synonym">synonym</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pajeon" title="Pajeon">Pajeon</a> (파전) — Thin pancakes with <a href="/wiki/Scallion" title="Scallion">scallions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pancake_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-pancake-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kimchijeon" title="Kimchijeon">Kimchijeon</a> (김치전) — Thin pancakes with old (ripe) Kimchi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pancake_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-pancake-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gamjajeon" title="Gamjajeon">Gamjajeon</a> (감자전) — Korean-style <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potato_pancakes" title="Potato pancakes">potato pancakes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Saengseon_jeon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Saengseon jeon (page does not exist)">Saengseon jeon</a> (생선전) — Small portions of fish coated with eggs and pan-fried.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Donggeurang_ttaeng&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Donggeurang ttaeng (page does not exist)">Donggeurang ttaeng</a> (동그랑땡) — Patty made with tofu, meat and vegetables, coated with eggs and pan-fried.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Banchan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Japchae" title="Japchae">Japchae</a> (잡채) — A stand-alone dish in its own right, <i>japchae</i> can also be eaten as banchan. <i>Japchae</i> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass_noodles" title="Glass noodles">glass noodles</a> accompanied with a variety of vegetables and beef in a slightly-sweet garlic sauce.</li>
<li>Korean-style potato salad (감자 샐러드) with apples and carrots.</li>
</ul>
