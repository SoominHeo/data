<p><b>Banpresto Co., Ltd.</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">株式会社バンプレスト</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Kabushiki-gaisha Banpuresuto</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> (<a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Stock_Exchange" title="Tokyo Stock Exchange">TYO</a>: <a class="external text" href="http://www2.tse.or.jp/tseHpFront/StockSearch.do?callJorEFlg=1&amp;method=&amp;topSearchStr=7854" rel="nofollow">7854</a>) is a Japanese toy company, best known in America for game development and publishing, headquartered in the Shinagawa Seaside West Building in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shinagawa,_Tokyo" title="Shinagawa, Tokyo">Shinagawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It was founded April 1977 as <b>Hoei Sangyo, Co. Ltd.</b> The company was renamed <b>Coreland</b> in 1982, and during the 1980s it worked mainly as a subcontractor for <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">Sega</a> and its arcade division.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It has been partially owned by <a href="/wiki/Bandai" title="Bandai">Bandai</a> since 1989, when it gained its current name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It was purchased by and became a wholly owned subsidiary of <i><a href="/wiki/Bandai_Namco_Holdings" title="Bandai Namco Holdings">Bandai Namco Holdings</a></i> in March 2006.</p>
<p>Banpresto has created a variety of Japan-only video games, most notably the <i><a href="/wiki/Super_Robot_Wars" title="Super Robot Wars">Super Robot Wars</a></i> series, which gained such a fanbase that three games of the series from its Original Generations sub-series, were translated and released to the US market.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Other projects include anime tie-in games such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Slam_Dunk_(manga)" title="Slam Dunk (manga)">Slam Dunk</a></i> basketball games and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tenchi_Muyou!_Game_Hen" title="Tenchi Muyou! Game Hen">Tenchi Muyou! Game Hen</a></i>. Banpresto also made the <i><a href="/wiki/Another_Century%27s_Episode" title="Another Century's Episode">Another Century's Episode</a></i> series, a collaboration with <a href="/wiki/FromSoftware" title="FromSoftware">FromSoftware</a>, and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compati_Hero" title="Compati Hero">Compati Hero</a></i> series of games which crosses over the <i><a href="/wiki/Ultra_Series" title="Ultra Series">Ultraman</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Kamen_Rider_Series" title="Kamen Rider Series">Kamen Rider</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Gundam" title="Gundam">Gundam</a></i> franchises. Banpresto also makes several varieties of mechanical prize-winning games. As Coreland, the company was responsible for the well-known classic arcade game, <i><a href="/wiki/Pengo_(video_game)" title="Pengo (video game)">Pengo</a></i>, published in 1982 by Sega. Banpresto's video game operations were absorbed into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bandai_Namco_Games" title="Bandai Namco Games">Bandai Namco Games</a> on April 1, 2008. As part of the merger, two of Banpresto's subsidiaries, Pleasure Cast Co. Ltd and Hanayashiki Co. Ltd, became <a href="/wiki/Namco" title="Namco">Namco</a> subsidiaries. Banpresto itself now focuses on its prize machines business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
