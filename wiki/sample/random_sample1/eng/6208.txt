<p>Walras' law is named for the economist <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Walras" title="Léon Walras">Léon Walras</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Lausanne" title="University of Lausanne">University of Lausanne</a> who formulated the concept in his <i>Elements of Pure Economics</i> of 1874.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-invdef_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-invdef-2">[2]</a></sup> Although the concept was expressed earlier but in a less mathematically rigorous fashion by <a href="/wiki/John_Stuart_Mill" title="John Stuart Mill">John Stuart Mill</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Essays_on_Some_Unsettled_Questions_of_Political_Economy" title="Essays on Some Unsettled Questions of Political Economy">Essays on Some Unsettled Questions of Political Economy</a></i> (1844),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Walras noted the mathematically equivalent proposition that when considering any particular market, if all other markets in an economy are in equilibrium, then that specific market must also be in equilibrium. The term "Walras' law" was coined by <a href="/wiki/Oskar_R._Lange" title="Oskar R. Lange">Oskar Lange</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> to distinguish it from <a href="/wiki/Say%27s_law" title="Say's law">Say's law</a>. Some economic theorists<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> also use the term to refer to the weaker proposition that the total value of excess demand cannot exceed the total values of excess supply.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walras%27_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>A market for a particular commodity is in <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Market_equilibrium" title="Market equilibrium">partial equilibrium</a></b> if, at the current prices of all commodities, the quantity of the commodity demanded by potential buyers equals the quantity supplied by potential sellers. For example, suppose the current market price of cherries is $1 per pound. If all cherry farmers summed together are willing to sell a total of 500 pounds of cherries per week at $1 per pound, and if all potential customers summed together are willing to buy 500 pounds of cherries in total per week when faced with a price of $1 per pound, then the market for cherries is in equilibrium because neither <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_shortage" title="Economic shortage">shortages</a> nor surpluses of cherries exist.</li>
<li>An economy is in <b>general equilibrium</b> if every market in the economy is in partial equilibrium. Not only must the market for cherries <a href="/wiki/Market_clearing" title="Market clearing">clear</a>, but so too must all markets for all commodities (apples, automobiles, etc.) and for all resources (labor and economic capital) and for all financial assets, including stocks, bonds, and money.</li>
<li>'Excess demand' refers to a situation in which a market is not in equilibrium at a specific price because the number of units of an item demanded exceeds the quantity of that item supplied at that specific price. Excess demand yields an economic shortage. A negative excess demand is synonymous with an excess supply, in which case there will be an economic surplus of the good or resource. 'Excess demand' may be used more generally to refer to the algebraic value of quantity demanded minus quantity supplied, whether positive or negative.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Walras.27_law">Walras' law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walras%27_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Walras' law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Walras' law implies that the sum of the values of excess demands across all markets must equal zero, whether or not the economy is in a general equilibrium. This implies that if positive excess demand exists in one market, negative excess demand must exist in some other market. Thus, if all markets but one are in equilibrium, then that last market must also be in equilibrium.</p>
<p>This last implication is often applied in formal general equilibrium models. In particular, to characterize general equilibrium in a model with m agents and n commodities, a modeler may impose market clearing for n - 1 commodities and "drop the n-th market-clearing condition." In this case, the modeler should include the budget constraints of all m agents (with equality). Imposing the budget constraints for all m agents ensures that Walras' law holds, rendering the n-th market-clearing condition redundant.</p>
<p>In the former example, suppose that the only commodities in the economy are cherries and apples, and that no other markets exist. This is an <a href="/wiki/Exchange_economy" title="Exchange economy">exchange economy</a> with no money, so cherries are traded for apples and vice versa. If excess demand for cherries is zero, then by Walras' law, excess demand for apples is also zero. If there is excess demand for cherries, then there will be a surplus (excess supply, or negative excess demand) for apples; and the market value of the excess demand for cherries will equal the market value of the excess supply of apples.</p>
<p>Walras' law is ensured if every agent's budget constraint holds with equality. An agent's budget constraint is an equation stating that the total market value of the agent's planned expenditures, including saving for future consumption, must be less than or equal to the total market value of the agent's expected revenue, including sales of financial assets such as bonds or money. When an agent's budget constraint holds with equality, the agent neither plans to acquire goods for free (e.g., by stealing), nor does the agent plan to give away any goods for free. If every agent's budget constraint holds with equality, then the total market value of <i>all</i> agents' planned outlays for <i>all</i> commodities (including saving, which represents future purchases) must equal the total market value of all agents' planned sales of all commodities and assets. It follows that the market value of total value of excess demand in the economy must be zero, which is an the statement of Walras' law. Walras' law implies that if there are <i>n</i> markets and <i>n</i>-1 of these are in equilibrium then the last market must also be in equilibrium, a property which is essential in the proof of the existence of equilibrium.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implications">Implications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walras%27_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Implications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Labor_market">Labor market</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walras%27_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Labor market">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_economics" title="Neoclassical economics">Neoclassical macroeconomic reasoning</a> concludes that because of Walras' law, if all markets for goods are in equilibrium, the market for labor must also be in equilibrium. Thus, by neoclassical reasoning, Walras' law contradicts the <a href="/wiki/Keynesian_economics" title="Keynesian economics">Keynesian</a> conclusion that negative excess demand and consequently, involuntary unemployment, may exist in the labor market, even when all markets for goods are in equilibrium. The Keynesian rebuttal is that this neoclassical perspective ignores financial markets, which may experience excess demand (such as a Keynesian <i><a href="/wiki/Liquidity_trap" title="Liquidity trap">liquidity trap</a></i>) that permits an excess supply of labor and consequently, temporary involuntary unemployment, even if markets for goods are in equilibrium.</p>
