<p><i><b>Balaur bondoc</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theropod" title="Theropod">theropod</a> <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaur</a> which lived in what is now <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> during the late <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> period. It is the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monotypic" title="Monotypic">monotypic</a> <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i>Balaur</i> which was described by scientists in August 2010, and was named after the <i><a href="/wiki/Balaur" title="Balaur">balaur</a></i> (<small>Romanian pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Romanian" title="Help:IPA for Romanian">[baˈla.ur]</a></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>), a dragon of <a href="/wiki/Folklore_of_Romania" title="Folklore of Romania">Romanian folklore</a>. The specific name <i>bondoc</i> means "stocky", so <i>Balaur bondoc</i> means "stocky dragon" in Romanian. This name refers to the greater musculature that <i>Balaur</i> had compared to its relatives. The genus is known from two partial skeletons including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_specimen" title="Type specimen">type specimen</a>.</p>
<p>Seventy million years ago, world sea levels were higher, and the location where its fossils are found was a part of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=European_Archipelago&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="European Archipelago (page does not exist)">European Archipelago</a> called <a href="/wiki/Ha%C8%9Beg_Island" title="Hațeg Island">Hațeg Island</a> which is also referred to as the "Island of the Dwarf Dinosaurs". Unlike other early members of the group <a href="/wiki/Paraves" title="Paraves">Paraves</a>, which includes <i><a href="/wiki/Velociraptor" title="Velociraptor">Velociraptor</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Troodon" title="Troodon">Troodon</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Archaeopteryx" title="Archaeopteryx">Archaeopteryx</a></i>, this theropod had not just one but two large, retractable, sickle-shaped claws on each foot, and its limbs were proportionally shorter and heavier than those of its relatives. As with other dinosaurs from Hațeg, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Magyarosaurus" title="Magyarosaurus">Magyarosaurus</a></i>, a dwarf <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sauropod" title="Sauropod">sauropod</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Magy_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Magy-2">[2]</a></sup> its strange features have been argued to show the effects of its island habitat on its evolution.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balaur_bondoc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Balaur_bondoc_scalechart_2015.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="560" data-file-width="875" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Balaur_bondoc_scalechart_2015.png/220px-Balaur_bondoc_scalechart_2015.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Balaur_bondoc_scalechart_2015.png/330px-Balaur_bondoc_scalechart_2015.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Balaur_bondoc_scalechart_2015.png/440px-Balaur_bondoc_scalechart_2015.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p><i>Balaur</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theropod" title="Theropod">theropod</a> dinosaurs estimated to have lived about <span class="nowrap">70 million</span> years ago in the late Cretaceous (<a href="/wiki/Maastrichtian" title="Maastrichtian">Maastrichtian</a>), and contains the single species <i>B. bondoc</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PNAS_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-PNAS-3">[3]</a></sup> The bones of this species were shorter and heavier than those of other basal paravians. While the feet of most early paravians bore a single, large "sickle claw" on the second toe which was held retracted off the ground, <i>Balaur</i> had large retractable sickle claws on both the first and second toes of each foot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PNAS_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-PNAS-3">[3]</a></sup> In addition to its strange feet, the type specimen of <i>Balaur</i> is unique for its status of being the most complete theropod fossil from the late Cretaceous of Europe. It also possesses a great number of additional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autapomorphies" title="Autapomorphies">autapomorphies</a>, including a reduced and presumably nonfunctional third finger, consisting of only one rudimentary phalanx.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PNAS_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-PNAS-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The partial skeleton was collected from the red floodplain <a href="/wiki/Mudstone" title="Mudstone">mudstone</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Sebe%C8%99" title="Sebeș">Sebeș</a> Formation of Romania. It consists of a variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertebrae" title="Vertebrae">vertebrae</a>, as well as much of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pectoral_girdle" title="Pectoral girdle">pectoral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pelvis" title="Pelvis">pelvic girdles</a>, and a large part of the limbs. It is the first reasonably complete and well-preserved theropod from the Late Cretaceous of Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PNAS_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-PNAS-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

</div>
</div>
<p>It is similar in size to <i><a href="/wiki/Velociraptor" title="Velociraptor">Velociraptor</a></i>, with <i>Balaur'</i>s recovered skeletal elements suggesting an overall length of around 1.8–2.1 metres (5.9–6.9 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PNAS_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-PNAS-3">[3]</a></sup> <i>Balaur</i> had re-evolved a functional first toe used to support its weight, which bore a large claw that could be hyperextended. It had short and stocky feet and legs, and large muscle attachment areas on the pelvis which indicate that it was adapted for strength rather than speed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pys_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-pys-5">[5]</a></sup> Csiki <i>et al.</i> describe this "novel body plan" as "a dramatic example of aberrant morphology developed in island-dwelling taxa."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PNAS_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-PNAS-3">[3]</a></sup> The stocky feet are exemplified by the length of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metatarsus" title="Metatarsus">metatarsus</a> being only two times its width. It is 1.5 times wider than the lower leg. Both traits are unique in the <a href="/wiki/Theropoda" title="Theropoda">Theropoda</a>. The skeleton of <i>Balaur</i> also shows extensive fusion of limb bones. Wrist bones and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metacarpals" title="Metacarpals">metacarpals</a> are fused into a carpometacarpus. The pelvic bones are fused. The shinbone, calf bone and the upper ankle bones have been fused into a tibiotarsus and the lower ankle bones and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metatarsals" title="Metatarsals">metatarsals</a> into a tarsometatarsus. The degree of fusion is typical for the <a href="/wiki/Avialae" title="Avialae">Avialae</a>, the evolutionary branch of the birds and their direct relatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brussate2013_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brussate2013-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_naming">Discovery and naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balaur_bondoc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery and naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Balaur.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2771" data-file-width="8566" height="71" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Balaur.png/220px-Balaur.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Balaur.png/330px-Balaur.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Balaur.png/440px-Balaur.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Balaur.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Skeletal reconstruction showing known remains in white</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first small bones belonging to <i>Balaur bondoc</i> consisted of six elements of the front limbs. Named specimens FGGUB R. 1580–1585, these were discovered in 1997 in <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Grigorescu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dan Grigorescu (page does not exist)">Dan Grigorescu</a>, but the morphology of the arm was so unusual that scientists could not correctly combine them,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pys_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-pys-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Time-7">[7]</a></sup> mistaking them for the remains of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oviraptorosaur" title="Oviraptorosaur">oviraptorosaur</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The first partial skeleton was discovered in September 2009 in Romania, approximately 2.5 kilometers north of Sebeș, along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sebe%C8%99_river" title="Sebeș river">Sebeș river</a> in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sebe%C8%99_Formation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sebeș Formation (page does not exist)">Sebeș Formation</a> dating from the early <a href="/wiki/Maastrichtian" title="Maastrichtian">Maastrichtian</a>, and was given the preliminary field number <i>SbG/A-Sk1</i>. Later it received the <a href="/wiki/Holotype" title="Holotype">holotype</a> inventory number EME VP.313. The discovery was made by the <a href="/wiki/Geologist" title="Geologist">geologist</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleontologist" title="Paleontologist">paleontologist</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%A1ty%C3%A1s_Vremir&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mátyás Vremir (page does not exist)">Mátyás Vremir</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_Transylvanian_History" title="National Museum of Transylvanian History">Transylvanian Museum Society of Cluj Napoca</a> who sent them for analysis to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zolt%C3%A1n_Csiki&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zoltán Csiki (page does not exist)">Zoltán Csiki</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bucharest" title="University of Bucharest">University of Bucharest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The findings were described on August 31, 2010, in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proceedings_of_the_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences">Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PNAS_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-PNAS-3">[3]</a></sup> The 1997 specimens indicate an individual about 45% longer than the holotype; they were also found in a younger <a href="/wiki/Stratum" title="Stratum">stratum</a>.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Name_of_a_biological_genus" title="Name of a biological genus">generic name</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Balaur" title="Balaur">Balaur</a></i> (three syllables, stressed on the second /a/) is from the <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a> word for a dragon of <a href="/wiki/Folklore_of_Romania" title="Folklore of Romania">Romanian folklore</a>, while the <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">specific epithet</a> <i>bondoc</i> (pronounced like "boned oak", meaning "a squat, chubby individual") refers to the small, robust shape of the animal. As the <i>balaur</i> is a winged dragon, the name additionally hints at the close relation of <i>Balaur</i> to the birds within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panaves" title="Panaves">Panaves</a>. <i>Bondoc</i> was chosen by the discoverers also because it is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a> <i>bunduk</i>, "small ball", thus alluding to the probable Asian origin of the ancestors of <i>Balaur</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Csiki2010_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Csiki2010-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balaur_bondoc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Balaur_bondoc_foot.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1332" data-file-width="1702" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Balaur_bondoc_foot.jpg/220px-Balaur_bondoc_foot.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Balaur_bondoc_foot.jpg/330px-Balaur_bondoc_foot.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Balaur_bondoc_foot.jpg/440px-Balaur_bondoc_foot.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Balaur_bondoc_foot.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Left foot of the holotype</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The position of <i>Balaur</i> relative to other bird-like dinosaurs and early birds has been difficult to determine. The initial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylogenetic" title="Phylogenetic">phylogenetic</a> analysis placed <i>Balaur bondoc</i> closest to the Asiatic mainland <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dromaeosaurid" title="Dromaeosaurid">dromaeosaurid</a> species <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velociraptor_mongoliensis" title="Velociraptor mongoliensis">Velociraptor mongoliensis</a></i>. A 2013 study by Brusatte and colleagues, using a modified version of the same data, found it in an unresolved close relationship with the dromaeosaurids <i><a href="/wiki/Deinonychus" title="Deinonychus">Deinonychus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Adasaurus" title="Adasaurus">Adasaurus</a></i>, with some possible alternative trees suggesting it branched off before the common ancestor of <i><a href="/wiki/Deinonychus" title="Deinonychus">Deinonychus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Velociraptor" title="Velociraptor">Velociraptor</a></i>, while others maintained it as the closest relative of <i><a href="/wiki/Velociraptor" title="Velociraptor">Velociraptor</a></i>, with <i><a href="/wiki/Adasaurus" title="Adasaurus">Adasaurus</a></i> as their next closest relative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brussate2013_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brussate2013-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>More recent analyses using different sets of anatomical data have since cast doubt on a dromaeosaurid classification for <i>Balaur</i>. In 2013, a larger analysis containing a wide variety of coelurosaurs found that <i>Balaur</i> was not a dromaeosaurid at all, but a basal <a href="/wiki/Avialae" title="Avialae">avialan</a>, more closely related to modern birds than to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeholornithiformes" title="Jeholornithiformes">Jeholornithiformes</a> but more basal than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omnivoropterygiformes" title="Omnivoropterygiformes">Omnivoropterygiformes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AurornisNature_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-AurornisNature-11">[11]</a></sup> A study published in 2014 found <i>Balaur</i> to be sister to <a href="/wiki/Pygostylia" title="Pygostylia">Pygostylia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> An independent analysis using an expanded version of the original data set (the one that found <i>Balaur</i> to be a dromaeosaurid) drew a similar conclusion in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fothetal2014_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-fothetal2014-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2015, researchers Andrea Cau, Tom Brougham, and Darren Naish published a study which specifically attempted to clarify which theropods were close relatives of <i>Balaur</i>. While their analysis could not completely rule out the possibility that <i>B. bondoc</i> was a dromaeosaurid, they concluded that this result was less likely than the classification of <i>Balaur</i> as a non-<a href="/wiki/Pygostylia" title="Pygostylia">pygostylian</a> avialan based on several important bird-like features. Many of the presumed unique traits would in fact have been normal for a member of the Avialae. Typical bird features included the degree of fusion of the limb bones, the functional first toe, the first toe claw not being smaller than the second claw, a long penultimate phalanx of the third toe, a small fourth toe claw and a long fifth metatarsal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cauetal2015_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-cauetal2015-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleobiology">Paleobiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balaur_bondoc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Paleobiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet_and_lifestyle">Diet and lifestyle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balaur_bondoc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diet and lifestyle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Balaur_bondoc.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1129" data-file-width="1231" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Balaur_bondoc.jpg/220px-Balaur_bondoc.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Balaur_bondoc.jpg/330px-Balaur_bondoc.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Balaur_bondoc.jpg/440px-Balaur_bondoc.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Balaur_bondoc.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Life restoration, depicted employing a kicking motion as originally proposed</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Little is known about the behavior of <i>Balaur</i>. Because of the lack of skull material, it is impossible to determine by the shape of the teeth whether <i>Balaur</i> was a carnivore or a herbivore. The original description assumed it was carnivorous because it had been found that it was closely related to <i>Velociraptor</i>. Csiki speculated in 2010 that it may have been one of the <a href="/wiki/Apex_predator" title="Apex predator">apex predators</a> in its limited <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Island_ecosystem" title="Island ecosystem">island ecosystem</a>, because of larger theropods not even the teeth have ever been found in Romania. He also believed that it likely used its double sickle claws for slashing prey, and that the atrophied state of its hands indicates that it probably did not use them to hunt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> One of the original discoverers indicated that it "was probably more of a kickboxer than a sprinter" compared to <i>Velociraptor</i>, and was probably able to hunt larger animals than itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> However, more recent studies by Denver Fowler and others have shown that the foot anatomy of paravians like <i>Balaur</i> indicate that they used their large claws to grip and pin prey to the ground while flapping with their proto-wings to stay on top of their victim. Once it was worn out, they might have proceeded to feast while it was still alive as some modern birds of prey still do. Due to the shape of the claws, they would not have been effective in slashing attacks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fowleretal2011_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-fowleretal2011-17">[17]</a></sup> The very short, fused metatarsus of <i>Balaur</i> and enlarged first claw, strange even by dromaeosaur standards, are thought to be consistent with these newer studies, lending further support to the idea that <i>Balaur</i> was a predator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Italian paleontologist Andrea Cau has speculated that the aberrant features present in <i>Balaur</i> may have been a result of this theropod being <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivorous</a> or <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivorous</a> rather than carnivorous like most non-avian theropods. The lack of the third finger may be a sign of reduced predatory behavior, and the robust first toe could be interpreted as a weight-supporting adaptation rather than a weapon. These characteristics are consistent with the relatively short, stocky limbs and wide, swept-back pubis, which may indicate enlarged intestines for digesting vegetation as well as reduced speed. Cau referred to this as the "Dodoraptor" model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> However, in light of the research done by Fowler et al., Cau has remarked that the anatomy of <i>Balaur</i> may be more congruent with the hypothesis that <i>Balaur</i> was predatory after all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015, Cau et al. reconsidered the ecology of <i>Balaur</i> again in their reevaluation of its phylogenetic position, arguing that if <i>Balaur</i> was an avialan, it would be <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic_bracketing" title="Phylogenetic bracketing">phylogenetically bracketed</a> by taxa known to have been herbivorous, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Sapeornis" title="Sapeornis">Sapeornis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Jeholornis" title="Jeholornis">Jeholornis</a></i>. This suggests a non-hypercarnivorous lifestyle to be a more parsimonious conclusion and supports Cau's initial interpretations of its specializations. This is also indicated by the reduced third finger, the lack of a gynglymoid lower articulation of the second metatarsal and the rather small and moderately recurved second toe claw. <i>Balaur</i> had a broad pelvis, a broad foot, a large first toe, and broad lower ends of the metatarsals relative to the articulation surfaces; such a combination can in the remainder of the Theropoda only be found with the herbivorous <a href="/wiki/Therizinosauridae" title="Therizinosauridae">Therizinosauridae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cauetal2015_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-cauetal2015-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Island_effects">Island effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balaur_bondoc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Island effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Balaur_in_Hateg_environment.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="966" data-file-width="1200" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Balaur_in_Hateg_environment.jpg/220px-Balaur_in_Hateg_environment.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Balaur_in_Hateg_environment.jpg/330px-Balaur_in_Hateg_environment.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Balaur_in_Hateg_environment.jpg/440px-Balaur_in_Hateg_environment.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Balaur_in_Hateg_environment.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Pair of <i>Balaur bondoc</i> in their native Hațeg island environment of what is now Romania</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Maastrichtian" title="Maastrichtian">Maastrichtian</a> age, much of Europe was fragmented into islands, and a number of the bizarre features of <i>Balaur</i> are thought to be a result of an "island effect", the relatively isolated conditions imposed on many of the populations in this area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Magy_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Magy-2">[2]</a></sup> Species which are isolated on islands can be subject to the effects of <a href="/wiki/Genetic_drift" title="Genetic drift">genetic drift</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Founder_effect" title="Founder effect">founder effect</a> which can magnify the effect of mutations which might be diluted in a larger population. Other island effects such as <a href="/wiki/Foster%27s_rule" title="Foster's rule">Foster's rule</a>, which describes how small mainland species become larger and large mainland species become smaller, can take effect. Cretaceous Romania is known, for example, for its dwarf <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sauropod" title="Sauropod">sauropods</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Magy_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Magy-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2010, this island effect was assumed to consist in rapid changes in a relatively recently arrived invading species and not seen as the result of an isolated development of an endemic form during tens of millions of years. The assumed close relationship to <i>Velociraptor</i> would have implied that the isolation of Haţeg Island must have been relative and intermittent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Csiki2010_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Csiki2010-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, the increased robustness of <i>Balaur</i> was compared to parallel changes seen in isolated herbivorous mammals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Csiki2010_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Csiki2010-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2013, it was claimed that <i>Balaur</i> was the only vertebrate predatory island species known to have become more robust and it was suggested that its broad feet had evolved to improve stability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brussate2013_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Brussate2013-6">[6]</a></sup> The 2015 interpretation of <i>Balaur</i> as an omnivorous member of the Avialae, suggested it was the descendant of a flying species that had developed a larger size similar to the development in several other island herbivores. This would then be a rare instance of secondary flightlessness in a paravian looking like a dromaeosaurid, as predicted by <a href="/wiki/Gregory_S._Paul" title="Gregory S. Paul">Gregory S. Paul</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cauetal2015_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-cauetal2015-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
