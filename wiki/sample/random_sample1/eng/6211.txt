<p><b>Valenciennes</b> (<small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[valɑ̃sjɛn]</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i>Valencijn</i>, <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>: <i>Valentianae</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Communes_of_France" title="Communes of France">commune</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Nord_(French_department)" title="Nord (French department)">Nord</a> <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_France" title="Departments of France">department</a> in northern France.</p>
<p>It lies on the <a href="/wiki/Scheldt" title="Scheldt">Scheldt</a> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Escaut</i></span>) river. Although the city and region experienced a steady population decline between 1975 and 1990, it has since rebounded. The 1999 census recorded that the population of the commune of Valenciennes was 41,278, and that of the metropolitan area was 399,677.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Before_1500">Before 1500</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valenciennes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Before 1500">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Valenciennes is first mentioned in 693 in a legal document written by <a href="/wiki/Clovis_II" title="Clovis II">Clovis II</a> (<i>Valentiana</i>). In the 843 <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Verdun" title="Treaty of Verdun">Treaty of Verdun</a>, it was made a neutral city between <a href="/wiki/Neustria" title="Neustria">Neustria</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Austrasia" title="Austrasia">Austrasia</a>. Later in the 9th century the region was overrun by the <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a>, and in 881 the town passed to them. In 923 it passed to the Duchy of Lower <a href="/wiki/Lotharingia" title="Lotharingia">Lotharingia</a> dependent on the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>. Once the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empire_of_the_Franks" title="Empire of the Franks">Empire of the Franks</a> was established, the city began to develop, though the archaeological record has still not revealed all it has to reveal about this period. Under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottonian" title="Ottonian">Ottonian</a> emperors, Valenciennes became the centre of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marches" title="Marches">marches</a> on the border of the Empire.</p>
<p>In 1008, a terrible <a href="/wiki/Famine" title="Famine">famine</a> brought the <a href="/wiki/Bubonic_plague" title="Bubonic plague">Plague</a>. According to the local tradition, the Virgin Mary held a cordon around the city which, miraculously, has since protected its people from the disease. Since then, every year at that time, the Valenciennois used to walk around the 14 kilometres (9 miles) road round the town, in what is called the tour of the Holy Cordon. Many Counts succeeded, first as Margraves of Valenciennes and from 1070 as <a href="/wiki/County_of_Hainaut" title="County of Hainaut">counts of Hainaut</a>.</p>
<p>In 1259 Valencienne was the site of a General Chapter of the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominican Order</a> at which <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a> together with masters Bonushomo Britto,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Florentius,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albert_the_Great" title="Albert the Great">Albert</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_V" title="Pope Innocent V">Peter</a> took part in establishing a <i>ratio studiorum</i> or program of studies for the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominican Order</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> that featured the study of philosophy as an innovation for those not sufficiently trained to study theology. This innovation initiated the tradition of Dominican scholastic philosophy put into practice, for example, in 1265 at the Order's <i>studium provinciale</i> at the convent of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Sabina" title="Santa Sabina">Santa Sabina</a> in Rome, out of which would develop the <a href="/wiki/Pontifical_University_of_Saint_Thomas_Aquinas" title="Pontifical University of Saint Thomas Aquinas">Pontifical University of Saint Thomas Aquinas, <i>Angelicum</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1285, the currency of Hainaut was replaced by the currency of France: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_ecu" title="French ecu">French ecu</a>. Valenciennes was full of activity, with numerous corporations, and outside its walls a large number of convents developed, like that of the Dominicans (whose church was excavated by the Valenciennes Archaeological Service in 1989 and 1990).</p>
<p>In the 14th century, the Tower of Dodenne was built by <a href="/wiki/Albert_I,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="Albert I, Duke of Bavaria">Albert of Bavaria</a>, where even today, the bell is rung in honour of Our Lady of the Holy Cordon. In the 15th century, the <a href="/wiki/County_of_Hainaut" title="County of Hainaut">County of Hainault</a>, of which Valenciennes is part, was re-attached to <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Burgundy" title="Duchy of Burgundy">Burgundy</a>, losing its autonomy. Valenciennes in this period, however, had several famous sons — the chronicler <a href="/wiki/Georges_Chastellain" title="Georges Chastellain">Georges Chastellain</a>, the poet <a href="/wiki/Jean_Molinet" title="Jean Molinet">Jean Molinet</a>, the miniaturist <a href="/wiki/Simon_Marmion" title="Simon Marmion">Simon Marmion</a>, the sculptor <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pierre_du_Pr%C3%A9au&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pierre du Préau (page does not exist)">Pierre du Préau</a> and the goldsmith <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_de_Moyenneville&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jérôme de Moyenneville (page does not exist)">Jérôme de Moyenneville</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1500.E2.80.931793">1500–1793</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valenciennes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1500–1793">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 1524, <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor</a>, arrived at Valenciennes, and — even when <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_France" title="Henry II of France">Henry II of France</a> allied with him against the Protestants in 1552 — Valenciennes became (c. 1560) an early center of <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinism</a> and in 1562 was location of the first act of resistance against persecution of Protestants in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Netherlands" title="Spanish Netherlands">Spanish Netherlands</a>. On the "Journée des Mals Brûlés" (Bad Burnings Day) in 1562, a mob freed some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestants</a> condemned to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Execution_by_burning" title="Execution by burning">die at the stake</a>. In the wave of <a href="/wiki/Iconoclasm" title="Iconoclasm">iconoclastic</a> attacks called the <a href="/wiki/Beeldenstorm" title="Beeldenstorm">Beeldenstorm</a> that swept the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a> in the summer of 1566, the city was the furthest south to see an attack. Following the "<a href="/wiki/Dutch_Revolt#1566_.E2.80.94_Iconoclasm_and_repression" title="Dutch Revolt">révolte des gueux</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Capture_of_Brielle" title="Capture of Brielle">victory at Brielle</a>, the army of <a href="/wiki/Louis_of_Nassau" title="Louis of Nassau">Louis of Nassau</a>, one of the major commanders of the Dutch rebel forces and supported by the <a href="/wiki/Huguenot" title="Huguenot">Huguenot</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Gaspard_II_de_Coligny" title="Gaspard II de Coligny">Gaspard de Coligny</a>, invaded the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Netherlands" title="Spanish Netherlands">Spanish Netherlands</a> with an army composed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_people" title="German people">German</a>, <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people" title="Scottish people">Scottish</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> soldiers, and took Valenciennes on 21 May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Eighty_Years_War_.281568-1648.29_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Eighty_Years_War_.281568-1648.29-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tracy_p._82_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tracy_p._82-6">[6]</a></sup> However, Louis went on to <a href="/wiki/Mons" title="Mons">Mons</a>, and the Protestant garrison left behind offered only a feeble defence to the Duke of Alba, at the head of the bulk of the Spanish army, who recaptured Valenciennes in early June <a href="/wiki/Huguenot" title="Huguenot">French allies</a>, of one of their main bases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duffy-1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Duffy-1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1576, when for a time the Southern Netherlands joined the revolt, the <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Spanish</a> forces massed at the porte d'Anzin (in a fortress known as "La Redoute") were besieged by Valenciennes. However, in 1580, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Farnese,_Duke_of_Parma" title="Alexander Farnese, Duke of Parma">Alexander Farnese, Duke of Parma</a> took Valenciennes and Protestantism was eradicated there. Hereafter, Valenciennes remained under Spanish protection, no longer directly involved in later fighting of the <a href="/wiki/Eighty_Years%27_War" title="Eighty Years' War">Eighty Years' War</a>. With its manufacturers of wool and fine <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">linens</a>, the city was able to become economically independent.</p>
<p>In 1591, the Jesuits built a school and then the foundations of a church of Sainte-Croix. In 1611, the façade of the town hall was completely rebuilt in magnificent Renaissance style. In the seventeenth century the Scheldt was channelled between Cambrai and Valenciennes, benefitting Valenciennes' wool, fabric and fine arts. To use up flax yarn, women began to make the famous <a href="/wiki/Valenciennes_lace" title="Valenciennes lace">Valenciennes lace</a>.</p>
<p>The French army laid siege to the city in 1656 (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vauban" title="Vauban">Vauban</a> participated in this siege without a command<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>). Defending the city, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_de_Merode&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Albert de Merode (page does not exist)">Albert de Merode</a>, marquis de Trélon was injured during a sortie on horseback, died as a result of his injuries and was buried in the Church of St. Paul (his tomb was found during the archaeological campaign in 1990). The Spanish victory in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Valenciennes_(1656)" title="Battle of Valenciennes (1656)">Battle of Valenciennes</a> (16 July 1656) lifted the French siege.</p>
<p>In 1677, the armies of <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV of France</a> (this time led by Vauban<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>) captured the city and in 1678 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Nijmegen" title="Treaty of Nijmegen">Treaty of Nijmegen</a> gave the French control of Valenciennes (1678) and the surrounding southern part of <a href="/wiki/County_of_Hainaut" title="County of Hainaut">Hainault</a>, roughly cutting the former <a href="/wiki/County" title="County">county</a> in half. The city became one of the main strongholds of northern France, and was fortified by Vauban, who personally visited the town for that purpose shortly after the Treaty.</p>
<p>During the Enlightenment era, the economic situation of Valenciennes was in decline until the discovery of coal. The first pit was dug in Fresnes in 1718 and the discovery of burnable coal in 1734 at the porte d'Anzin led to the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Compagnie_des_mines_d%27Anzin" title="Compagnie des mines d'Anzin">Compagnie des mines d'Anzin</a>. In the eighteenth century, the city was equally renowned for its porcelain — indeed, it was the porcelain furnaces' demand for coal that led to the mining enterprises. Despite their quality of production, the company failed to be sustainable. Valenciennes, rich in artistic talent, became known as the <a href="/wiki/Fifth-century_Athens" title="Fifth-century Athens">Athens</a> of the North (i.e. North of France), underlining its artistic influence.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1793.E2.80.931914">1793–1914</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valenciennes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1793–1914">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The city was besieged by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Coalition" title="First Coalition">First Coalition</a> against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Revolutionary_France" title="Revolutionary France">Revolutionary France</a> in 1793.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Following a protracted <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Valenciennes_(1793)" title="Siege of Valenciennes (1793)">Siege of Valenciennes</a> the city was captured and occupied in July by Anglo-Austrian forces under the <a href="/wiki/Prince_Frederick,_Duke_of_York_and_Albany" title="Prince Frederick, Duke of York and Albany">Duke of York</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Josias_of_Saxe-Coburg" title="Prince Josias of Saxe-Coburg">Prince of Saxe-Coburg</a>, and only retaken by the French Revolutionary armies in August 1794. In July 1795, one year after the execution of Robespierre put an end to the <a href="/wiki/Reign_of_Terror" title="Reign of Terror">Reign of Terror</a>, the Republicans of Valenciennes tortured, and guillotined five <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ursulines_of_Valenciennes" title="Ursulines of Valenciennes">Ursuline</a> nuns; by some accounts, the nuns were raped before being executed. After the Napoleonic era, Valenciennes gave itself up to the <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_Restoration" title="Bourbon Restoration">Bourbons</a> in 1815 for 5 years. After that, the town's sugar-refining and coal industries once more started to expand.</p>
<p>In 1824 Valenciennes became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sous-pr%C3%A9fecture" title="Sous-préfecture">sous-préfecture</a>. In the 19th century, thanks to coal, Valenciennes became a great industrial centre and the capital of Northern France's steel industry.</p>
<p>On 6 August 1890, a law downgraded the town's fortified status, and so from 1891 to 1893, its fortifications were demolished. The town was granted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%C3%A9gion_d%27honneur" title="Légion d'honneur">Légion d'honneur</a> in 1900.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_World_War">First World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valenciennes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: First World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> the German army occupied the town in 1914.</p>
<p>Another wartime personality of Valenciennes was <a href="/wiki/Louise_de_Bettignies" title="Louise de Bettignies">Louise de Bettignies</a> (born in Saint-Amand-les-Eaux), a pupil of the Ursulines in Valenciennes from 1890 to 1896. Fluent in four languages (including German), in 1915 she created and directed the main British intelligence network behind enemy lines, nearly 60 km (37 mi) from the front around <a href="/wiki/Lille" title="Lille">Lille</a>. Arrested at the end of September 1915, and imprisoned in Germany, she died of mistreatment in September 1918 two months before the Armistice. It is estimated that she saved the lives of nearly a thousand British soldiers by the remarkably precise information she obtained. For example, it enabled the British to conduct the first aerial bombing of a train (that of Kaiser Wilhelm II, who came to visit the front at Lille), though both aircraft were not equipped with suitable viewfinders and so the raid narrowly missed its target. The German High Command, based in Brussels, then put all its efforts into neutralising the accursed network that allowed the British to see everything and know everything about this part of the front. Louise's arrest was associated with the escape of Szeck Alexandre, a young Austrian radio operator got out of Brussels in August 1915, allowing the British to get their hands on the secret German diplomatic code. This code was exploited by Secret Service Room 40 ("Room 40"), under the supervision of Sir Reginald Hall, and in January 1917 allowed the decipherment of the famous <a href="/wiki/Zimmermann_Telegram" title="Zimmermann Telegram">Zimmermann Telegram</a>, which triggered the United States' entry into the war in April 1917.</p>
<p>Valenciennes was retaken after bitter fighting in 1918, by British and Canadian troops (one of whose soldiers, a recipient of the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Cross" title="Victoria Cross">Victoria Cross</a> Sergeant <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Cairns_(VC)" title="Hugh Cairns (VC)">Hugh Cairns</a>, was honoured in 1936 when the city named an avenue after him).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_World_War">Second World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valenciennes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Second World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On May 10, 1940, the town's inhabitants fled by road and it was abandoned to looters from the French army.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A huge fire devoured the heart of the town, fuelled in particular by a fuel depot. German troops then occupied the ruined city on May 27. On September 2, 1944, after bloody fighting, American troops entered Valenciennes and liberated the city.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1945_to_present">1945 to present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valenciennes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1945 to present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The town's first antenna was set up in Lille in 1964, then the Centre universitaire was set up in 1970, becoming independent in 1979 as the University of Valenciennes and Hainaut-Cambrésis.</p>
<p>In 2005, a local resident, <a href="/wiki/Isabelle_Dinoire" title="Isabelle Dinoire">Isabelle Dinoire</a>, became the first person to have a partial <a href="/wiki/Face_transplant" title="Face transplant">face transplant</a>.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hindenburg_Line" title="Hindenburg Line">Hindenburg Line</a> ran through Valenciennes during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, leading to extensive destruction. Valenciennes was again almost completely destroyed during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, and has since been rebuilt in concrete.</p>
<p>A few surviving monuments are: 1) The façade of the town hall, which managed to survive the bombardments of the war; 2) Notre-Dame du Saint-Cordon, to which there is an annual pilgrimage; 3) La Maison Espagnole, the remains of the Spanish occupation, which ended in 1678. The building is now used as the town's tourist information office; 4) The Dodenne Tower, the remaining part of the medieval fortifications after Charles V ordered them reduced; 5) Théâtre le Phenix, a theatre and performing arts venue constructed in 1998; 6) The "<a href="/wiki/Bell_tower" title="Bell tower">Beffroi</a>", a large, pin-like monument 45 metres (148 feet) in height, built in 2007 on the site of the former belfry.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valenciennes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Valenciennes is historically renowned for its <a href="/wiki/Valenciennes_lace" title="Valenciennes lace">lace</a>. Until the 1970s, the main <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industries</a> were <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textiles</a>. Since their decline, reconversion attempts focus mainly on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automobile</a> production. In 2001, <a href="/wiki/Toyota" title="Toyota">Toyota</a> built its Western European <a href="/wiki/Assembly_line" title="Assembly line">assembly line</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Yaris" title="Toyota Yaris">Toyota Yaris</a> in Valenciennes. Because of this and other changes, the average unemployment in the region is now lower than the national average.</p>
<p>On 15 July 2004 the Administrative Board of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Railway_Agency" title="European Railway Agency">Railway Agency</a> held its first meeting in Phénix, with representatives of the 25 Member States and <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Lamoureux" title="François Lamoureux">François Lamoureux</a>, those days Director General for Energy and Transportation at the European Commission. Valenciennes was picked as the European Railway Agency headquarters in December 2003. International conferences are held in <a href="/wiki/Lille" title="Lille">Lille</a>.</p>

<p>Line #1 of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tramway_de_Valenciennes" title="Tramway de Valenciennes">Tramway de Valenciennes</a> was put into service on 3 July 2006. 9.5 km (5.9 mi) long, this tramway crosses the five communes in the Valenciennois Metropolitan area, at a cost of 242.75 million <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">Euros</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gare_de_Valenciennes" title="Gare de Valenciennes">Gare de Valenciennes</a> railway station offers connections with Lille, Paris and several regional destinations.</p>
