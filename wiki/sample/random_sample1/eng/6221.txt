<p><b>Valentinian I</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Flavius Valentinianus Augustus</i></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>3 July 321 – 17 November 375), also known as <b>Valentinian the Great</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> was <a href="/wiki/Roman_emperor" title="Roman emperor">Roman emperor</a> from 364 to 375. Upon becoming emperor he made his brother <a href="/wiki/Valens" title="Valens">Valens</a> his co-emperor, giving him rule of the eastern provinces while Valentinian retained the west.</p>
<p>During his reign, Valentinian fought successfully against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alamanni" title="Alamanni">Alamanni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quadi" title="Quadi">Quadi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sarmatians" title="Sarmatians">Sarmatians</a>. Most notable was his victory over the Alamanni in 367 at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Solicinium" title="Battle of Solicinium">Battle of Solicinium</a>. His brilliant general <a href="/wiki/Count_Theodosius" title="Count Theodosius">Count Theodosius</a> defeated a revolt in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Africa" title="Roman Africa">Africa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Conspiracy" title="Great Conspiracy">Great Conspiracy</a>, a coordinated assault on <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a> by <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people" title="Scottish people">Scots</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxons</a>. Valentinian was also the last emperor to conduct campaigns across both the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> rivers. Valentinian rebuilt and improved the fortifications along the frontiers, even building fortresses in enemy territory.</p>
<p>Due to the successful nature of his reign and the rapid decline of the empire after his death, he is often considered to be the "last great western emperor". He founded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valentinian_Dynasty" title="Valentinian Dynasty">Valentinian Dynasty</a>, with his sons Gratian and Valentinian II succeeding him in the western half of the empire.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valentinian_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solidus_de_Valentinien_MAN.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1103" data-file-width="1102" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Solidus_de_Valentinien_MAN.jpg/300px-Solidus_de_Valentinien_MAN.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Solidus_de_Valentinien_MAN.jpg/450px-Solidus_de_Valentinien_MAN.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Solidus_de_Valentinien_MAN.jpg/600px-Solidus_de_Valentinien_MAN.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>

<p>Valentinian was born in 321<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cibalae" title="Cibalae">Cibalae</a> in southern <a href="/wiki/Pannonia" title="Pannonia">Pannonia</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Vinkovci" title="Vinkovci">Vinkovci</a> in <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lenski2002_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lenski2002-7">[7]</a></sup> Valentinian and his younger brother <a href="/wiki/Valens" title="Valens">Valens</a> were the sons of <a href="/wiki/Gratian_the_Elder" title="Gratian the Elder">Gratianus Major</a>, a prominent commander during the reigns of emperors <a href="/wiki/Constantine_the_Great" title="Constantine the Great">Constantine I</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constans_I" title="Constans I">Constans I</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomlinp2_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-tomlinp2-8">[8]</a></sup> He and his brother grew up on the family estate where they were educated in a variety of subjects, including painting and sculpting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gratian the Elder was promoted to <i><a href="/wiki/Comes" title="Comes">Comes</a> Africae</i> in the late 320s or early 330s, and the young Valentinian accompanied his father to Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomlinp4_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-tomlinp4-10">[10]</a></sup> However, Gratian was soon accused of embezzlement and was forced to retire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomlinp4_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-tomlinp4-10">[10]</a></sup> Valentinian joined the army in the late 330s and later probably acquired the position of <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Protector_domesticus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Protector domesticus (page does not exist)">protector domesticus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomlinp4_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-tomlinp4-10">[10]</a></sup> Gratian was later recalled during the early 340s and was made <i>comes</i> of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Britannia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomlinp4_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-tomlinp4-10">[10]</a></sup> After holding this post, Gratianus retired to the family estate in Cibalae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 350, Constans I was assassinated by agents of the <a href="/wiki/Usurper" title="Usurper">usurper</a> <a href="/wiki/Magnentius" title="Magnentius">Magnentius</a>, a commander in <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a> proclaimed emperor by his soldiers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canduci.2C_pg._131_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canduci.2C_pg._131-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Constantius_II" title="Constantius II">Constantius II</a>, older brother of Constans and emperor in the East, promptly set forth towards Magnentius with a large army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zosimus.2C_New_History_II.60_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zosimus.2C_New_History_II.60-13">[13]</a></sup> The following year the two emperors met in Pannonia. The ensuing <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Mursa_Major" title="Battle of Mursa Major">Battle of Mursa Major</a> resulted in a costly victory for Constantius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Two years later he defeated Magnentius again in southern Gaul at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Mons_Seleucus" title="Battle of Mons Seleucus">Battle of Mons Seleucus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Magnentius, now realizing the futility of continuing his revolt, committed suicide in August that year; making Constantius sole ruler of the empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It was around this time that Constantius confiscated Gratianus' property, for supposedly showing hospitality to Magnentius when he was in Pannonia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-11">[11]</a></sup> Despite his father's fall from favor, Valentinian does not seem to have been adversely affected at this time, making it unlikely he ever fought for the usurper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomlinp13_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-tomlinp13-17">[17]</a></sup> It is known that Valentinian was in the region during the conflict, but what involvement he had in the war, if any, is unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomlinp13_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-tomlinp13-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Service_under_Constantius_and_Julian">Service under Constantius and Julian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valentinian_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Service under Constantius and Julian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The conflict between Magnentius and Constantius had allowed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alamanni" title="Alamanni">Alamanni</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a> to take advantage of the confusion and cross the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>, attacking several important settlements and fortifications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomlinp4_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-tomlinp4-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomlinp13_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-tomlinp13-17">[17]</a></sup> In 355, after deposing his cousin Gallus but still feeling the crises of the empire too much for one emperor to handle, Constantius raised his cousin <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julian_the_Apostate" title="Julian the Apostate">Julian</a> to the rank of <i>Caesar</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomlinp4_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-tomlinp4-10">[10]</a></sup> With the situation in Gaul rapidly deteriorating, Julian was made at least nominal commander of one of the two main armies in Gaul, <a href="/wiki/Barbatio" title="Barbatio">Barbatio</a> being commander of the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomlinp4_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-tomlinp4-10">[10]</a></sup> Constantius devised a strategy where Julian and Barbatio would operate in a pincer movement against the Alamanni.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomlinp13_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-tomlinp13-17">[17]</a></sup> However, a band of Alamanni slipped past Julian and Barbatio and attacked <a href="/wiki/Lugdunum" title="Lugdunum">Lugdunum</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a>). Julian sent the tribunes Valentinian and <a href="/wiki/Bainobaudes" title="Bainobaudes">Bainobaudes</a> to watch the road the raiders would have to return by. However, their efforts were hindered by Barbatio and his tribune <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cella_(soldier)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cella (soldier) (page does not exist)">Cella</a>. The Alamann king <a href="/wiki/Chnodomarius" title="Chnodomarius">Chnodomarius</a> took advantage of the situation and attacked the Romans in detail, inflicting heavy losses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomlinp13_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-tomlinp13-17">[17]</a></sup> Barbatio complained to Constantius and the debacle was blamed on Valentinian and Bainobaudes, who were cashiered from the army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomlinp13_17-5"><a href="#cite_note-tomlinp13-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>With his career in ruins, Valentinian returned to his new family estate in <a href="/wiki/Sirmium" title="Sirmium">Sirmium</a>. Two years later his first son <a href="/wiki/Gratian" title="Gratian">Gratian</a> was born by his wife <a href="/wiki/Marina_Severa" title="Marina Severa">Marina Severa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomlinp14_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-tomlinp14-18">[18]</a></sup> Valentinian's actions become uncertain around this time, but he may have been exiled for refusing to do sacrifice to Julian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_to_power">Rise to power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valentinian_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rise to power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the news of Julian's death on a campaign against the Sassanids, the army hastily declared a commander, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jovian_(Emperor)" title="Jovian (Emperor)">Jovian</a>, emperor. The army still found itself beleaguered by Persian attacks, forcing Jovian to accept humiliating peace terms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomlinp16_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-tomlinp16-20">[20]</a></sup> Jovian's authority within the empire was still insecure, so he sent a notary <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Procopius_(notary)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Procopius (notary) (page does not exist)">Procopius</a> and the tribune <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Memoridus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Memoridus (page does not exist)">Memoridus</a> west to announce his accession.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomlinp16_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-tomlinp16-20">[20]</a></sup> During Jovian's reign Valentinian was promoted to tribune of a <i>Scutarii</i> (elite infantry) regiment, and was dispatched to Ancyra. Jovian's rule would be short – only eight months – and before he could even consolidate his position in <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> he died en route between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancyra" title="Ancyra">Ancyra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nicaea" title="Nicaea">Nicaea</a>. His death was attributed to either assassination by poisoning or accidental carbon monoxide poisoning. Jovian is remembered mostly for restoring Christianity to its previous favored status under Constantine and his sons.</p>
<p>The army marched to Nicaea, and a meeting of civil and military officials was convened to choose a new emperor. Two names were proposed: Aequitius, a tribune of the first Scutarii, and Januarius, a relative of Jovian’s in charge of military supplies in Illyricum. Both were rejected; Aequitius as too rough and boorish,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Januarius because he was too far away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> As a man well qualified and at hand, the assembly finally agreed upon Valentinian and sent messengers to inform him in Ancyra.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emperor">Emperor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valentinian_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Emperor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Valentinian accepted the acclamation on 26 February 364. As he prepared to make his accession speech the soldiers threatened to riot, apparently uncertain as to where his loyalties lay. Valentinian reassured them that the army was his greatest priority. According to Ammianus the soldiers were astounded by Valentinian’s bold demeanor and his willingness to assume the imperial authority. To further prevent a succession crisis he agreed to pick a co-Augustus. His decision to elect a fellow-emperor could also be construed as a move to appease any opposition among the civilian officials in the eastern portion of the Empire. By agreeing to appoint a co-ruler, he assured the eastern officials that someone with imperial authority would remain in the east to protect their interests.</p>
<p>Valentinian selected his brother Valens as co-Augustus at Constantinople on 28 March 364. This was done over the objections of Dagalaifus, the <i><a href="/wiki/Magister_equitum" title="Magister equitum">magister equitum</a></i>. Ammianus makes it clear that Valens was subordinate to his brother. The remainder of 364 was spent delegating administrative duties and military commands. Valentinian retained the services of Dagalaifus and promoted Aequitius to <i>Comes Illyricum</i>. Valens was given the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prefecture_of_Oriens" title="Prefecture of Oriens">Prefecture of Oriens</a>, governed by <a href="/wiki/Prefect" title="Prefect">prefect</a> <a href="/wiki/Salutius" title="Salutius">Salutius</a>. Valentinian gained control of <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_prefecture_of_Italy" title="Praetorian prefecture of Italy">Italy</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prefecture_of_Gaul" title="Prefecture of Gaul">Gaul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_prefecture_of_Africa" title="Praetorian prefecture of Africa">Africa</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prefecture_of_Illyricum" title="Prefecture of Illyricum">Illyricum</a>. Valens resided in Constantinople, while Valentinian’s court was at Milan.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Campaigns_in_Gaul_and_Germania">Campaigns in Gaul and Germania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valentinian_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Campaigns in Gaul and Germania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Colosso-de-barletta.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1170" data-file-width="625" height="562" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Colosso-de-barletta.jpg/300px-Colosso-de-barletta.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Colosso-de-barletta.jpg/450px-Colosso-de-barletta.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Colosso-de-barletta.jpg/600px-Colosso-de-barletta.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>

<p>In 365 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alamanni" title="Alamanni">Alamanni</a> crossed the Rhine and invaded Gaul. Simultaneously, <a href="/wiki/Procopius_(usurper)" title="Procopius (usurper)">Procopius</a> – the last scion of the <a href="/wiki/Constantinian_dynasty" title="Constantinian dynasty">Constantinian dynasty</a> – began his revolt against Valens in the east. According to Ammianus, Valentinian received news of both the Alamanni and Procopius' revolt on 1 November while on his way to <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>. He initially sent Dagalaifus to fight the Alamanni<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> while he himself made preparations to march east and help Valens. After receiving counsel from his court and deputations from the leading Gallic cities begging him to stay and protect Gaul, he decided to remain in Gaul and fight the Alamanni.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Valentinian advanced to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Durocortorum" title="Durocortorum">Durocortorum</a> and sent two generals, Charietto and Severianus, against the invaders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Both generals were promptly defeated and killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-27">[27]</a></sup> In 366, Dagalaifus was sent against the Alamanni but he was also ineffective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Late in the campaigning season Dagalaifus was replaced by Jovinus, a general from the court of Valentinian. After several battles Jovinus pushed the Alamanni out of Gaul and was awarded the consulate the following year for his efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In early 367 Valentinian was distracted from launching a punitive expedition against the Alamanni due to crises in Britain and northern Gaul. The Alamanni promptly re-crossed the Rhine and plundered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moguntiacum" title="Moguntiacum">Moguntiacum</a>. Valentinian succeeded in arranging the assassination of Vithicabius, an Alamannic leader, but Valentinian was more determined to bring the Alamanni under Roman hegemony. Valentinian spent the entire winter of 367 gathering a massive army for a spring offensive. He summoned the <i>Comes Italiae</i> Sebastianus, with the Italian and Illyrian legions, to join Jovinus and Severus, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magister_peditum" title="Magister peditum">magister peditum</a></i>. In the spring of 368 Valentinian, his eight-year-old son Gratian and the army crossed the Rhine and <a href="/wiki/Main_river" title="Main river">Main river</a> into Alamannic territory. They did not encounter any resistance initially – burning any dwellings or food stores they found along the way. Finally, Valentinian fought the Alamanni in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Solicinium" title="Battle of Solicinium">Battle of Solicinium</a>; the Romans were victorious<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> but suffered heavy casualties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> A temporary peace was reached and Valentinian returned to <a href="/wiki/Trier" title="Trier">Trier</a> for the winter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> During 369, Valentinian ordered new defensive works to be constructed and old structures refurbished along the length of the Rhine’s west bank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Boldly, he ordered the construction of a fortress across the Rhine in the mountains near modern <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The Alamanni sent envoys to protest, but they were dismissed. The Alamanni attacked the fortress while it was still under construction and destroyed it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 370 the Saxons renewed their attacks on northern Gaul. Nannienus, the <i>comes</i> in charge of the troops in northern Gaul, urged Severus to come to his aid. After several modest successes, a truce was called and the Saxons handed over to the Romans young men fit for duty in the Roman military – in exchange for free passage back to their homeland. The Romans ambushed them and destroyed the entire invading force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Valentinian meanwhile tried to persuade the <a href="/wiki/Burgundians" title="Burgundians">Burgundians</a> – bitter enemies of the Alamanni – to attack <a href="/wiki/Macrian" title="Macrian">Macrian</a>, a powerful Alamannic chieftain. If the Alamanni tried to flee, Valentinian would be waiting for them with his army. Negotiations with the Burgundians broke down when Valentinian, in his usual high-handed manner, refused to meet with the Burgundian envoys and personally assure them of Roman support. Nevertheless, rumors of a Roman alliance with the Burgundians did have the effect of scattering the Alamanni through fear of an imminent attack from their enemies. This event allowed the <i><a href="/wiki/Magister_equitum" title="Magister equitum">magister equitum</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Count_Theodosius" title="Count Theodosius">Theodosius</a> to attack the Alamanni through Raetia – taking many Alamannic prisoners. These captured Alamanni were settled in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Po_river" title="Po river">Po river</a> valley in Italy, where they were still settled at the time Ammianus wrote his history.</p>
<p>Valentinian campaigned unsuccessfully for four more years to defeat Macrian who in 372 barely escaped capture by Theodosius. Meanwhile, Valentinian continued to recruit heavily from Alamanni friendly to Rome. He sent the Alamannic king Fraomarius, along with Alamannic troops commanded by Bitheridius and Hortarius, to Britain in order to replenish troops there. Valentinian’s Alamannic campaigns, however, were hampered by troubles first in Africa, and later on the Danube river. In 374 Valentinian was forced to make peace with Macrian because the Emperor's presence was needed to counter an invasion of Illyricum by the Quadi and Sarmatians.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Great_Conspiracy">The Great Conspiracy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valentinian_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Great Conspiracy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 367, Valentinian received reports from Britain that a combined force of <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Attacotti" title="Attacotti">Attacotti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scoti" title="Scoti">Scots</a> had killed the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comes_litoris_Saxonici" title="Comes litoris Saxonici">Comes litoris Saxonici</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Nectaridus" title="Nectaridus">Nectaridus</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Dux_Britanniarum" title="Dux Britanniarum">Dux Britanniarum</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Fullofaudes" title="Fullofaudes">Fullofaudes</a>. At the same time, Frankish and Saxon forces were raiding the coastal areas of northern Gaul. The empire was in the midst of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Conspiracy" title="Great Conspiracy">Great Conspiracy</a> – and was in danger of losing control of Britain altogether. Valentinian set out for Britain, sending <i>Comes domesticorum</i> Severus ahead of him to investigate. Severus was not able to correct the situation and returned to Gaul, meeting Valentinian at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samarobriva" title="Samarobriva">Samarobriva</a>. Valentinian then sent Jovinus to Britain and promoted Severus to magister peditum. It was at this time that Valentinian fell ill and a battle for succession broke out between Severus, a representative of the army, and Rusticus Julianus, <i>magister memoriae</i> and a representative of the Gallic nobility. Valentinian soon recovered however and appointed his son Gratian as his co-Augustus in the west. Ammianus remarks that such an action was unprecedented. Jovinus quickly returned saying that he needed more men to take care of the situation. In 368 Valentinian appointed Theodosius as the new <i><a href="/wiki/Comes_Britanniarum" title="Comes Britanniarum">Comes Britanniarum</a></i> with instructions to return Britain to Roman rule. Meanwhile, Severus and Jovinus were to accompany the emperor on his campaign against the Alamanni.</p>
<p>Theodosius arrived in 368 with the <a href="/wiki/Batavi_(Germanic_tribe)" title="Batavi (Germanic tribe)">Batavi</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heruli" title="Heruli">Heruli</a>, Jovii and Victores legions. Landing at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rutupi%C3%A6" title="Rutupiæ">Rutupiæ</a>, he proceeded to <a href="/wiki/Londinium" title="Londinium">Londinium</a> restoring order to southern Britain. Later, he rallied the remaining garrison which was originally stationed in Britain; it was apparent the units had lost their cohesiveness when Fullofaudes and Nectaridus had been defeated. Theodosius sent for <a href="/wiki/Civilis_(vicarius)" title="Civilis (vicarius)">Civilis</a> to be installed as the new <a href="/wiki/Vicarius" title="Vicarius">vicarius</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">diocese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dulcitius" title="Dulcitius">Dulcitius</a> as an additional general. In 369, Theodosius set about reconquering the areas north of <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>; putting down the revolt of <a href="/wiki/Valentinus_(rebel)" title="Valentinus (rebel)">Valentinus</a>, the brother-in-law of a <a href="/wiki/Vicarius" title="Vicarius">vicarius</a> <a href="/wiki/Maximinus_(praetorian_prefect)" title="Maximinus (praetorian prefect)">Maximinus</a>. Subsequently, Theodosius restored the rest of Britain to the empire and rebuilt many fortifications – renaming northern Britain '<a href="/wiki/Valentia_(Roman_Britain)" title="Valentia (Roman Britain)">Valentia</a>'. After his return in 369, Valentinian promoted Theodosius to <i>magister equitum</i> in place of Jovinus.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Revolt_in_Africa_and_crises_on_the_Danube">Revolt in Africa and crises on the Danube</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valentinian_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Revolt in Africa and crises on the Danube">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In 372, the rebellion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firmus_(4th_century_usurper)" title="Firmus (4th century usurper)">Firmus</a> broke out in the still-devastated African provinces. This rebellion was driven by the corruption of the <i>comes</i> Romanus. Romanus took sides in the murderous disputes among the legitimate and illegitimate children of Nubel, a Moorish prince and leading Roman client in Africa. Resentment of Romanus' peculations and his failure to defend the province from desert nomads caused some of the provincials to revolt. Valentinian sent in Theodosius to restore imperial control. Over the following two years Theodosius uncovered Romanus' crimes, arrested him and his supporters, and defeated Firmus.</p>
<p>In 373, hostilities erupted with the <a href="/wiki/Quadi" title="Quadi">Quadi</a>, a group of Germanic-speaking people living on the upper Danube. Like the Alamanni, the Quadi were outraged that Valentinian was building fortifications in their territory. They complained and sent deputations that were ignored by the <i>magister armorum per Illyricum</i> Aequitius. However, by 373 the construction of these forts was behind schedule. Maximinus, now praetorian prefect of Gaul, arranged with Aequitius to promote his son Marcellianus and put him in charge of finishing the project. The protests of Quadic leaders continued to delay the project, and in a fit of frustration Marcellianus murdered the Quadic king <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gabinius_(king)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gabinius (king) (page does not exist)">Gabinius</a> at a banquet ostensibly arranged for peaceful negotiations. This roused the Quadi to war; along with their allies the Sarmatians. During the fall, they crossed the Danube and began ravaging the province of <a href="/wiki/Pannonia_Valeria" title="Pannonia Valeria">Pannonia Valeria</a>. The marauders could not penetrate the fortified cities, but they heavily damaged the unprotected countryside. Two legions were sent in but failed to coordinate and were routed by the Sarmatians. Meanwhile, another group of Sarmatians invaded <a href="/wiki/Moesia" title="Moesia">Moesia</a>, but were driven back by the son of Theodosius, <i>Dux Moesiae</i> and later emperor <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">Theodosius</a>.</p>
<p>Valentinian did not receive news of these crises until late 374. The following spring he set out from Trier and arrived at <a href="/wiki/Carnuntum" title="Carnuntum">Carnuntum</a>, which was deserted. There he was met by Sarmatian envoys who begged forgiveness for their actions. Valentinian replied that he would investigate what had happened and act accordingly. Valentinian ignored Marcellianus’ treacherous actions and decided to punish the Quadi. He was accompanied by Sebastianus and <a href="/wiki/Merobaudes_(general)" title="Merobaudes (general)">Merobaudes</a>, and spent the summer months preparing for the campaign. In the fall he crossed the Danube at <a href="/wiki/Aquincum" title="Aquincum">Aquincum</a> into Quadi territory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> After pillaging Quadi lands without opposition, he retired to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sabaria" title="Sabaria">Savaria</a> to winter quarters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valentinian_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the spring he decided to continue campaigning and moved from Savaria to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Szony" title="Szony">Brigetio</a>. Once he arrived on 17 November, he received a deputation from the Quadi. In return for supplying fresh recruits to the Roman army, the Quadi were to be allowed to leave in peace. However, before the envoys left they were granted an audience with Valentinian. The envoys insisted that the conflict was caused by the building of Roman forts in their lands; furthermore individual bands of Quadi were not necessarily bound to the rule of the chiefs who had made treaties with the Romans – and thus might attack the Romans at any time. The attitude of the envoys so enraged Valentinian that he suffered a burst blood vessel in the skull while angrily yelling at them, provoking his death<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> on November 14, 375.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reputation">Reputation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valentinian_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reputation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A.H.M._Jones" title="A.H.M. Jones">A.H.M. Jones</a> writes that although Valentinian I was "less of a boor" than his chief rival for election to the imperial throne, "he was of a violent and brutal temper, and not only uncultivated himself, but hostile to cultivated persons", as <a href="/wiki/Ammianus_Marcellinus" title="Ammianus Marcellinus">Ammianus</a> tells us, 'he hated the well-dressed and educated and wealthy and well-born'. He was, however, an able soldier and a conscientious administrator, and took an interest in the welfare of the humbler classes, from which his father had risen. Unfortunately his good intentions were often frustrated by a bad choice of ministers, and an obstinate belief in their merits despite all evidence to the contrary."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition" title="Encyclopædia Britannica Eleventh Edition">Encyclopædia Britannica 1911</a>, he was a founder of schools, and provided medical attendance for the poor of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, by appointing a physician for each of the fourteen districts of the city.</p>
<p>Valentinian was a <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> but permitted liberal religious freedom to all his subjects, proscribing only some forms of rituals such as particular types of sacrifices, and banning the practice of magic. Against all abuses, both civil and ecclesiastical (excepting, of course, his own excesses), Valentinian steadily set his face, even against the increasing wealth and worldliness of the clergy. His chief flaw was his temper, which at times was frightful, and showed itself in its full fierceness in the punishment of persons accused of witchcraft, some kinds of fortune-telling or magical practices."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Socrates_of_Constantinople" title="Socrates of Constantinople">Socrates Scholasticus</a> gives an interesting account in his Historia Ecclesiastica of Valentinian's marriages, that has inspired some to call this emperor <a href="/wiki/Polygamy_in_Christianity" title="Polygamy in Christianity">polygamous</a>. According to the text: the empress <a href="/wiki/Justina_(empress)" title="Justina (empress)">Justina</a> "became known to <a href="/wiki/Marina_Severa" title="Marina Severa">Marina Severa</a>, wife of the emperor Valentinian, and had frequent dialogue with the empress, until their intimacy at length grew to such an extent that they were accustomed to bathe together. When Severa saw Justina in the bath she was greatly struck with the beauty of the virgin, and spoke of her to the emperor; saying that the daughter of Justus was so lovely a creature, and possessed of such symmetry of form, that she herself, though a woman, was altogether charmed with her. The emperor, treasuring this description by his wife in his own mind, considered with himself how he could espouse Justina, without repudiating Severa, as she had borne him Gratian, whom he had created Augustus a little while before. He accordingly framed a law, and caused it to be published throughout all the cities, by which any man was permitted to have two lawful wives. The law was promulgated and he married Justina, by whom he had Valentinian the younger." (Book IV, chapt. 31.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> This story is only known to Socrates. There is no trace of any edict allowing polygamy in the laws passed by Valentinian I, his predecessors or his successors. This practice is unknown in all other sources of Classical Antiquity. Valentinian I may have divorced Severa according to Roman Law, which allowed for divorce (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Women_in_Ancient_Rome" title="Women in Ancient Rome">Women in Ancient Rome</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> But since divorce was not acknowledged by Christians,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Socrates contemptuously describes him as a bigamist. It is also possible that Socrates, who was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Novatianist" title="Novatianist">Novatianist</a> attempted to accuse Justina, who was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arianist" title="Arianist">Arianist</a>, of fornication, a common aspersion against other cults. Gibbon maintains that the marriages of Valentinian were conducted successively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> According to the Antique sources of <a href="/wiki/John_Malalas" title="John Malalas">John Malalas</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Chronicon_Paschale" title="Chronicon Paschale">Chronicon Paschale</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_of_Nikiu" title="John of Nikiu">John of Nikiu</a> the empress Severa was banished by Valentinian I for conducting an illegal transaction, before he consorted with Justina. Barnes believes this story to be an attempt to justify the divorce of Valentinian I without accusing the emperor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
