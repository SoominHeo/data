<p><b>Emanationism</b> is an idea in the <a href="/wiki/Cosmology" title="Cosmology">cosmology</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cosmogony" title="Cosmogony">cosmogony</a> of certain <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religious</a> or <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophical</a> systems. Emanation, from the Latin <i>emanare</i> meaning "to flow from" or "to pour forth or out of", is the mode by which all things are derived from the first reality, or principle. All things are derived from the first reality or perfect God by steps of degradation to lesser degrees of the first reality or God, and at every step the emanating beings are less pure, less perfect, less divine. Emanationism is a transcendent principle from which everything is derived, and is opposed to both <a href="/wiki/Creationism" title="Creationism">creationism</a> (wherein the universe is created by a sentient God who is separate from creation) and <a href="/wiki/Materialism" title="Materialism">materialism</a> (which posits no underlying subjective and/or ontological nature behind phenomena being immanent).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emanationism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Emanationism is a cosmological theory which asserts that all things "flow" from an underlying principle or reality, usually called the <a href="/wiki/Absolute_(philosophy)" title="Absolute (philosophy)">Absolute</a> or <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">Godhead</a>. Any teachings which involve emanation are usually in opposition to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creation_ex_nihilo" title="Creation ex nihilo">creation ex nihilo</a> as emanation advocates that everything has always existed and has not been "created" from nothing.</p>
<p>Kleinham (2007) writes:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Underlying the worldview of traditional cosmology is the idea that the universe is an emanation of a unitary divine principle. Although this idea has been blended with the revealed creationist doctrines of the major monotheistic religions, orthodox theologians have generally regarded it with suspicion. They have relegated it to the shadowy spheres of mysticism, pantheism, and the occult, which have always been at odds with orthodoxy. The traditional view is summed in the doctrine of emanation formulated by Plotinus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The primary classical exponent of emanationism was <a href="/wiki/Plotinus" title="Plotinus">Plotinus</a>, wherein his work, the <i><a href="/wiki/Enneads" title="Enneads">Enneads</a></i>, all things phenomenal and otherwise were an emanation from the One (<i>hen</i>). In Ennead 5.1.6, emanationism is compared to a diffusion from the One, of which there are three primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypostases" title="Hypostases">hypostases</a>, the One (ἕν, <i>hen</i>), the Intellect/Will (νοῦς, <i><a href="/wiki/Nous" title="Nous">nous</a></i>), and the Soul (ψυχὴ τοῦ παντός, <i><a href="/wiki/Soul#Socrates_and_Plato" title="Soul">psyche</a> tou pantos</i>). For Plotinus, emanation, or the "soul's descent", is a result of the Indefinite <a href="/wiki/Dyad_(Greek_philosophy)" title="Dyad (Greek philosophy)">Dyad</a>, the primordial agnosis inherent to and within the Absolute, the Godhead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another advocate of emanationism was <a href="/wiki/Michael_Servetus" title="Michael Servetus">Michael Servetus</a>, who was burned at the stake for his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nontrinitarian" title="Nontrinitarian">nontrinitarian</a> cosmology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occultism">Occultism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emanationism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Occultism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Emanationism is a common teaching found in <a href="/wiki/Occult" title="Occult">occult</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esoteric" title="Esoteric">esoteric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysical</a> writings. According to Owen (2005):</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Theosophy draws on Neoplatonic emanationism, in particular the concept of separation from and return to the Absolute, and reworks the Eastern concepts of karma and reincarnation to provide an evolutionary theory of both humankind and the universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Theosophy" title="Theosophy">Theosophy</a> teaches that human beings and all organisms including animals and all matter "flow" from a pure spiritual formation in the absolute to a material one over time to become materialised but later will return to the absolute after the cosmic cycle of life.</p>
<p>As Morgan summarises: "The Secret Doctrine laid out an emanationist view of the development of the physical universe, a process of ebb and flow in which spirit gradually unfolded itself in matter, attaining consciousness, and returning to spirit in a higher and more realised form."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> According to the emanationist cosmology of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madame_Blavatsky" title="Madame Blavatsky">Madame Blavatsky</a> all <a href="/wiki/Monad_(philosophy)" title="Monad (philosophy)">monads</a> emerge from divine unity at the beginning of a cosmic cycle and return to this source at its close.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Blavatsky in her book <i>The Key to Theosophy</i> (1889) wrote that: "We believe in a universal divine principle, the root of all, from which all proceeds, and within which all shall be at the end of the great cycle of being."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Samael_Aun_Weor" title="Samael Aun Weor">Samael Aun Weor</a> had taught emanationism from his studies with the <a href="/wiki/Kabbalah" title="Kabbalah">Kabbalah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gnosticism" title="Gnosticism">Gnosticism</a>. He mapped out a complex <a href="/wiki/Esoteric_cosmology" title="Esoteric cosmology">esoteric cosmology</a> with matter flowing from different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planes_of_existence" title="Planes of existence">planes of existence</a> all existing in the <a href="/wiki/Absolute_(philosophy)" title="Absolute (philosophy)">absolute</a>. As Dawson (2007) comments:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>As with esoteric thought in general, Weor holds that the universe originated in the ordering activity of the absolute upon chaotic primordial matter, giving rise to (emanating) the subsequent planes of the created order (Pleroma).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
