<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Balkan League</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Balkan League</b><sup class="reference" id="ref_a_1"><a href="#cnote_a">[a]</a></sup> was an alliance formed by a series of bilateral treaties concluded in 1912 between the Balkan states of <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Montenegro" title="Montenegro">Montenegro</a>, and directed against the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OnWar-first_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-OnWar-first-1">[1]</a></sup> which at the time still controlled much of the Balkan peninsula. The Balkans had been in a state of turmoil since the early 1900s, with years of <a href="/wiki/Macedonian_Struggle" title="Macedonian Struggle">guerrilla warfare in Macedonia</a> followed by the <a href="/wiki/Young_Turk_Revolution" title="Young Turk Revolution">Young Turk Revolution</a> and the protracted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bosnian_Crisis" title="Bosnian Crisis">Bosnian Crisis</a>. The outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/Italo-Turkish_War" title="Italo-Turkish War">Italo-Turkish War</a> in 1911 had further weakened the Ottomans and emboldened the Balkan states. Under <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian</a> influence, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Serbia" title="Kingdom of Serbia">Serbia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bulgaria" title="Kingdom of Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a> settled their differences and signed an alliance, originally directed against <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a> on 13 March 1912,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crampton62_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crampton62-2">[2]</a></sup> but by adding a secret chapter to it essentially redirected the alliance against the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balkan_Crises_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Balkan_Crises-3">[3]</a></sup> Serbia then signed a mutual alliance with <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Montenegro" title="Kingdom of Montenegro">Montenegro</a>, while Bulgaria did the same with <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Greece" title="Kingdom of Greece">Greece</a>. The League was victorious in the <a href="/wiki/First_Balkan_War" title="First Balkan War">First Balkan War</a> which broke out in October 1912, where it successfully wrestled control of almost all European Ottoman territories. Following this victory however, the old differences between the allies re-emerged over the division of the spoils, particularly <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(region)" title="Macedonia (region)">Macedonia</a>, leading to the effective break-up of the League, and soon after, on 16 June 1913, Bulgaria attacked her erstwhile allies, beginning the <a href="/wiki/Second_Balkan_War" title="Second Balkan War">Second Balkan War</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balkan_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bosnian_Crisis_1908.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4965" data-file-width="3420" height="363" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Bosnian_Crisis_1908.jpg/250px-Bosnian_Crisis_1908.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Bosnian_Crisis_1908.jpg/375px-Bosnian_Crisis_1908.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Bosnian_Crisis_1908.jpg/500px-Bosnian_Crisis_1908.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bosnian_Crisis" title="Bosnian Crisis">Bosnian Crisis</a> of 1908 altered the balance of power in the Balkans and precipitated events that would lead to the formation of the Balkan League. <p>After the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_War" title="Crimean War">Crimean War</a> (1853–1856), Russia realized that the other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Powers" title="Great Powers">Great Powers</a> would spare no effort to prevent her from gaining access to the Mediterranean. As a consequence she started engineering an ambitious plan for indirect expansion through the creation of friendly and closely allied states under Russian patronage in the Balkan peninsula. Instrumental to this policy was the emerging <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panslavism" title="Panslavism">Panslavic</a> movement, which henceforth formed the basis of Russian foreign policy up until the end of the <a href="/wiki/Tsar" title="Tsar">Tsarist</a> regime in 1917.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tuminez1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tuminez1-4">[4]</a></sup> Working in this direction, following the victorious <a href="/wiki/Russo-Turkish_War_(1877%E2%80%931878)" title="Russo-Turkish War (1877–1878)">Russo-Turkish War of 1877–1878</a>, Russia managed to establish an autonomous <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Bulgaria" title="Principality of Bulgaria">Bulgarian state</a>. Similarly, after saving Serbia from annihilation at the Turks' hands in 1876, Russia forced the Ottomans to accept a full independent and expanded Serbia two years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frucht1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frucht1-5">[5]</a></sup> However, although both states acknowledged Russian patronage and protection, their conflicted national aspirations soon led to a series of hostile actions before and after the <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Bulgarian_War" title="Serbo-Bulgarian War">short war</a> between them. With the antagonism of the European powers mounting, and smarting from her humiliation by the Austrians at the <a href="/wiki/Bosnian_crisis" title="Bosnian crisis">Bosnian crisis</a>, Russia sought to gain the upper hand by creating a Russophile "Slavic block" in the Balkans, directed both against <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a> and the Ottomans. Consequently, Russian diplomacy began pressuring the two countries, Serbia and Bulgaria, to reach a compromise and form an alliance.</p>
<p>Apart from the Russian pressure upon Bulgaria and Serbia, another issue that triggered the formation of the League was the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_De%C3%A7iq" title="Battle of Deçiq">Albanian Uprising</a> of 1911. The timetable of the negotiations between Serbia and Bulgaria indicates that progress paralleled the success of the Albanian revolt. In May 1912 the Albanians succeeded in taking <a href="/wiki/Skopje" title="Skopje">Skopje</a> and continued towards <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monastir,_Macedonia" title="Monastir, Macedonia">Monastir</a>, forcing the Ottomans to recognize the autonomy of <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a> in June 1912. For Serbia this was considered catastrophic; after her hopes of expansion to the north were thwarted due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_annexation_of_Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Austro-Hungarian annexation of Bosnia and Herzegovina">Austro-Hungarian annexation of Bosnia and Herzegovina</a> in October 1908, Serbia now found the last direction of possible expansion, the south, also closing due to the creation of an <a href="/wiki/Albanian_Vilayet" title="Albanian Vilayet">Albanian Vilayet</a>. The Serbs now had to struggle against time to avoid the establishment of the Albanian state. On the other hand, Bulgaria used this Serbian anxiety in order to force Serbia to agree to significant concessions in regard to <a href="/wiki/Vardar_Macedonia" title="Vardar Macedonia">Vardar Macedonia</a>. Thus the final agreement between the two countries stipulated that, in the event of a victorious war against the Ottomans, Bulgaria would receive all of Macedonia south of the <a href="/wiki/Kriva_Palanka" title="Kriva Palanka">Kriva Palanka</a>-<a href="/wiki/Ohrid" title="Ohrid">Ohrid</a> line. Serbia's expansion was to be to the north of this line, including Kosovo, and west to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adriatic" title="Adriatic">Adriatic</a> coast, a territory including the northern half of modern Albania, giving Serbia access to the sea. In essence, Serbia was forced to exchange Macedonia for Albania, an issue that would play a key role in the eventual dissolution of the League in the spring of 1913, when the Great Powers insisted upon the creation of the Albanian state and denied Serbia her territorial gains in that direction.</p>

<p>Bulgaria, on her part, had held a long-term policy regarding the Ottomans since restoring her independence during the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Turkish_War_(1877%E2%80%931878)" title="Russo-Turkish War (1877–1878)">Russo-Turkish War</a>. After the successful <a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d'état">coup d'état</a> for the incorporation of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Rumelia" title="Eastern Rumelia">Eastern Rumelia</a>, she had orchestrated a methodical scenario of indirect expansion through the creation, in the multi-ethnic Ottoman-held Macedonia (for many centuries an administrative rather than a nationistic name), of a united, liberating and revolutionary organization, the <a href="/wiki/Internal_Macedonian_Revolutionary_Organization" title="Internal Macedonian Revolutionary Organization">IMRO</a>, allegedly without national color. IMRO's rhetoric claimed to be speaking generally for liberation on behalf of the “Macedonian People”, declaring its anti-<a href="/wiki/Chauvinism" title="Chauvinism">chauvinism</a>. In fact, it was a Bulgarian-backed organization created with the secret agenda of facilitating the incorporation of <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a> (Eastern and Western) and Macedonia (Aegean and the Vardar) into a new autonomous state, as an intermediate step before unification with Bulgaria could take place in the same way as with Eastern Rumelia. After initial success, Serbia and especially Greece realized the true purpose of IMRO and consequently a vicious guerrilla war, the so-called <a href="/wiki/Macedonian_Struggle" title="Macedonian Struggle">Macedonian Struggle</a> broke out between Bulgarian and Greek backed armed groups within Ottoman Macedonia. The conflict ended only when the <a href="/wiki/Young_Turks" title="Young Turks">Young Turks</a> movement came to power, promising reforms and equality of all Ottoman subjects regardless of religion or nationality. Bulgaria then turned to the more direct method of expansion through winning a war, building a large army for that purpose and started to see itself as the "<a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> of the Balkans".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> But even so, it was clear that Bulgaria could not win a war against the Ottomans alone, and an alliance was necessary. By signing the military appendix to the original agreement Bulgaria aimed to use the Serbian army to seize most of Macedonia while concentrating her own army for the operations against Thrace with its major cities of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adrianople" title="Adrianople">Adrianople</a> and <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>.</p>
<p>In Greece, army officers had <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goudi_Revolt" title="Goudi Revolt">revolted</a> in August 1909 and secured the appointment of a progressive government under <a href="/wiki/Eleftherios_Venizelos" title="Eleftherios Venizelos">Eleftherios Venizelos</a>, which they hoped would resolve the <a href="/wiki/Cretan_State" title="Cretan State">Cretan</a> issue in Greece's favour and reverse their <a href="/wiki/Greco-Turkish_War_(1897)" title="Greco-Turkish War (1897)">defeat</a> of 1897 at the hands of the Ottomans. In the discussions that led Greece to join the League, Bulgaria refused to commit to any agreement on the distribution of territorial gains, unlike the deal with Serbia over <a href="/wiki/Vardar_Macedonia" title="Vardar Macedonia">Vardar Macedonia</a>. The reason was Bulgaria's diplomatic policy of pushing Serbia into an agreement limiting her access to Macedonia, while at the same time refusing any such agreement with Greece. Having a low regard for the Greek Army's military effectiveness, the Bulgarian leadership estimated that, according to the military plans, their limited forces that had been deployed to the Macedonian theatre would be able to occupy the larger part of the region and the important port city of <a href="/wiki/Thessaloniki" title="Thessaloniki">Thessaloniki</a> before the Greeks. The entry of Greece in the League however was essential for the allies, since Greece, alone among the Balkan states in possessing a major fleet, could preclude the mass transfer of Ottoman reinforcements from Asia directly into Europe by sea. As the Greek ambassador to <a href="/wiki/Sofia" title="Sofia">Sofia</a> had put it during the negotiations that led to Greece's entry in the League: "Greece can provide 600,000 men for the war effort. 200,000 men in the field, and the fleet will be able to stop 400,000 men being landed by Turkey between Salonika and <a href="/wiki/Gallipoli" title="Gallipoli">Gallipoli</a>."</p>
<p>Montenegro, a relatively small country but a close ally of Serbia was considered a second class participant. It took the invitation to the insistence of Serbia more as a favour, having limited local aspirations over the <a href="/wiki/Sanjak" title="Sanjak">Sanjak</a> and the north Albanian city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shkodra" title="Shkodra">Shkodra</a>.</p>
<p>Another fact that helped the formation of the League was the evident inefficiency of the Ottoman army. The Ottomans were at <a href="/wiki/Italo-Turkish_War" title="Italo-Turkish War">war</a> with Italy for a year (29 September 1911 to 18 October 1912) over <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a> after Italy had launched an invasion of <a href="/wiki/Tripolitania" title="Tripolitania">Tripolitania</a>. Although the Italians made little progress and Ottoman resistance, aided by the Libyans, proved stiffer than expected, the war exhausted the Ottoman state. In addition, the Italian occupation of the Greek-inhabited <a href="/wiki/Dodecanese" title="Dodecanese">Dodecanese</a> Islands served as a warning for Greece of the consequences of staying out from a future war against the Ottomans.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_of_the_Great_Powers">Reaction of the Great Powers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balkan_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reaction of the Great Powers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</div>
</div>
<p>These developments did not go unnoticed by the Great Powers, but although there was an official consensus between the <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> Powers over the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Question" title="Eastern Question">territorial integrity</a> of the Ottoman Empire, which led to a stern warning to the Balkan states, unofficially each of them took a different diplomatic approach due to their conflicting interests in the area. As a result, any possible preventative effect of the common official warning was canceled by the mixed unofficial signals, and failed to prevent the outbreak of hostilities:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russia</a> was a prime mover in the establishment of the League and saw it as an essential tool in case of a future war against her rival, the Austro-Hungarian Empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stowell1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stowell1-7">[7]</a></sup> But she was unaware of the Bulgarian plans over Thrace and Constantinople, territories on which she had long-held ambitions, and on which she had just secured a secret agreement of expansion from her allies France and Britain, as a reward in participating in the upcoming <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Central_Powers" title="Central Powers">Central Powers</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/French_Third_Republic" title="French Third Republic">France</a>, not feeling ready for a war against Germany in 1912, took a totally negative position against the League, firmly informing her ally Russia that she would not take part in a potential conflict between Russia and Austro-Hungary if it resulted from the actions of the Balkan League. The French however failed to achieve British participation in a common intervention to stop the upcoming Balkan conflict.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>, although officially a staunch supporter of the Ottoman Empire's integrity, took secret diplomatic steps encouraging the Greek entry into the League in order to counteract Russian influence. At the same time she encouraged the Bulgarian aspirations over Thrace, preferring a Bulgarian Thrace to a Russian one, despite the assurances she had given to the Russians in regard of their expansion there.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a>, struggling for an exit from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adriatic" title="Adriatic">Adriatic</a> and seeking ways for expansion in the south at the expense of the Ottoman Empire, was totally opposed to any other nation's expansion in the area. At the same time, the Habsburg empire had its own internal problems with the significant Slav populations that campaigned against the German-Hungarian control of the multinational state. Serbia, whose aspirations in the direction of Bosnia were no secret, was considered an enemy and the main tool of Russian machinations that were behind the agitation of Austria's Slav subjects.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">Germany</a>, already heavily involved in the internal Ottoman politics, officially opposed a war against the Empire, but in her effort to win Bulgaria for the <a href="/wiki/Central_Powers" title="Central Powers">Central Powers</a>, and seeing the inevitability of Ottoman disintegration, was playing with the idea to replace the Balkan positions of the Ottomans with a friendly Greater Bulgaria in her <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_San_Stefano" title="Treaty of San Stefano">San Stefano</a> borders, an idea that was based on the German origin of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferdinand_of_Bulgaria" title="Ferdinand of Bulgaria">Bulgarian King</a> and his anti-Russian sentiments.</li>
</ul>
<p>For the Balkan League the opportunity was too good to be missed, as the Ottoman Empire was weak and riddled with internal strife. The allied governments intensified their military and diplomatic preparations. During the last days of September, the Balkan states and the Ottoman Empire mobilized their armies. The first state to declare war was Montenegro, on October 8, 1912, starting the <a href="/wiki/First_Balkan_War" title="First Balkan War">First Balkan War</a>. The other three states, after issuing an ultimatum to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottoman_Porte" title="Ottoman Porte">Porte</a> on October 13, declared war on Turkey on October 17.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balkan_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</div>
<p>In the resulting war, the combined Balkan armies effectively destroyed Ottoman power in Europe in a series of victories. However, the League's triumph was short-lived. The antagonisms between the Balkan states still persisted, and after the successful conclusion of the <a href="/wiki/First_Balkan_War" title="First Balkan War">First Balkan War</a>, they resurfaced, especially over the partition of <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(region)" title="Macedonia (region)">Macedonia</a>. Mounting tensions effectively tore the League apart, and the <a href="/wiki/Second_Balkan_War" title="Second Balkan War">Second Balkan War</a> broke out when Bulgaria, confident of a quick victory, attacked her former allies Serbia and Greece. The Serbian and Greek armies repulsed the Bulgarian offensive and counterattacked penetrating into Bulgaria. The Ottoman Empire and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Romania" title="Kingdom of Romania">Romania</a> took advantage of the situation and invaded Bulgaria too. The subsequent peace left Bulgaria with gains in territory, but led to the loss of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Thrace" title="Eastern Thrace">Eastern Thrace</a> to the Ottomans and most of Macedonia to the Greeks. Defeat turned Bulgaria into her participation in the First World War on the side of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Powers" title="Central Powers">Central Powers</a>, since her Balkan enemies (Serbia, Greece and Romania) were involved in the war on the side of the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_I" title="Allies of World War I">Entente</a>.</p>
<p>During the war, the <a href="/wiki/George_I_of_Greece" title="George I of Greece">Greek king</a> was assassinated in Thessaloniki under yet unresolved conditions. That generated a shift in the Greek foreign policy from clearly pro-Entente to neutrality, since the <a href="/wiki/Constantine_I_of_Greece" title="Constantine I of Greece">new King</a>, unlike his father and his popular <a href="/wiki/Eleftherios_Venizelos" title="Eleftherios Venizelos">Prime Minister</a>, was pro-German and tried to keep the country neutral in the upcoming World War. With the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and the Entente's intervention in Macedonia, the conflict between king and first minister steadily deteriorated, leading eventually to the <a href="/wiki/National_Schism" title="National Schism">National Schism</a>, that greatly contributed to the loss of the next <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Turkish_War_(1919%E2%80%931922)" title="Greco-Turkish War (1919–1922)">war</a> against <a href="/wiki/Mustafa_Kemal_Atat%C3%BCrk" title="Mustafa Kemal Atatürk">Kemalist</a> <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a>, and dominated Greek politics for over a half of a century.</p>
<p>The outcome of the Balkan Wars caused a permanent break-up of the Russo-Bulgarian alliance, and left Serbia and Montenegro as the only allies of Russia in this critical region.</p>
