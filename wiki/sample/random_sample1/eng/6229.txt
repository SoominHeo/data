<p>The <b>Balkan <a href="/wiki/Mountain_range" title="Mountain range">mountain range</a></b> (<a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serbian_Cyrillic_language" title="Serbian Cyrillic language">Serbian Cyrillic</a>: <span lang="sr" xml:lang="sr"><span class="Unicode">Стара планина</span></span>, <a href="/wiki/Gaj%27s_Latin_alphabet" title="Gaj's Latin alphabet">Latin Serbian</a> <i><b>Stara planina</b></i>, "Old Mountain"; <small><a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a> pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Bulgarian_and_Macedonian" title="Help:IPA for Bulgarian and Macedonian">[ˈstarɐ pɫɐniˈna]</a></span>; <small>Serbian pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Serbo-Croatian" title="Help:IPA for Serbo-Croatian">[stâːraː planǐna]</a></span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heamus_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-heamus-1">[1]</a></sup> is a mountain range in the eastern part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balkan_Peninsula" title="Balkan Peninsula">Balkan Peninsula</a>. The Balkan range runs 560 km from the <a href="/wiki/Vrashka_Chuka" title="Vrashka Chuka">Vrashka Chuka</a> Peak on the border between <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a> eastward through central Bulgaria to <a href="/wiki/Cape_Emine" title="Cape Emine">Cape Emine</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>. The highest peaks of the Balkan Mountains are in central Bulgaria. The highest peak is <a href="/wiki/Botev_Peak" title="Botev Peak">Botev</a> at 2,376 m, which makes the mountain range the third highest in the country, after <a href="/wiki/Rila" title="Rila">Rila</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pirin" title="Pirin">Pirin</a>. The mountains are the source of the name of the Balkan Peninsula.</p>
<p>The mountain range forms the watershed between the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">Aegean Sea</a> catchment areas, with the exception of an area in west, where it is crossed by the spectacular <a href="/wiki/Iskar_Gorge" title="Iskar Gorge">Iskar Gorge</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Karst" title="Karst">karst</a> relief determines the large number of caves, including <a href="/wiki/Magura_Cave" title="Magura Cave">Magura</a>, featuring the most important and extended European post-Palaeolithic cave painting, <a href="/wiki/Ledenika" title="Ledenika">Ledenika</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saeva_dupka" title="Saeva dupka">Saeva dupka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bacho_Kiro_cave" title="Bacho Kiro cave">Bacho Kiro</a>, etc. The most notable rock formation are the <a href="/wiki/Belogradchik_Rocks" title="Belogradchik Rocks">Belogradchik Rocks</a> in the west.</p>
<p>There are several important protected areas: <a href="/wiki/Central_Balkan_National_Park" title="Central Balkan National Park">Central Balkan National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vrachanski_Balkan_Nature_Park" title="Vrachanski Balkan Nature Park">Vrachanski Balkan Nature Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgarka_Nature_Park" title="Bulgarka Nature Park">Bulgarka Nature Park</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sinite_Kamani_Nature_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sinite Kamani Nature Park (page does not exist)">Sinite Kamani Nature Park</a>, as well as a number of <a href="/wiki/Nature_reserve" title="Nature reserve">nature reserves</a>. The Balkan Mountains are remarkable for their flora and fauna. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edelweiss" title="Edelweiss">Edelweiss</a> grows there in the region of <i>Kozyata stena</i>. Some of the most striking landscapes are included in the Central Balkan National Park with steep cliffs, the highest waterfalls in the Balkan Peninsula and lush vegetation. There are a number of important nature reserves such as Chuprene, Kozyata stena and others. Most of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>'s large mammals inhabit the area including the <a href="/wiki/Brown_bear" title="Brown bear">brown bear</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf">wolf</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boar" title="Boar">boar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chamois" title="Chamois">chamois</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a>.</p>
<p>The Balkan Mountains played an enormous role in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Bulgaria" title="History of Bulgaria">history of Bulgaria</a> since its foundation in 681 AD, and in the development of the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarians" title="Bulgarians">Bulgarian</a> nation and people.</p>

<p>It is believed the name was brought to the region in the 7th century by <a href="/wiki/Bulgars" title="Bulgars">Bulgars</a> who applied it to the area, as a part of the <a href="/wiki/First_Bulgarian_Empire" title="First Bulgarian Empire">First Bulgarian Empire</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a>, the word <i>balkan</i> (балкан) means "mountain".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It may have derived from the <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> <i>bālkāneh</i> or <i>bālākhāna</i>, meaning "high, above, or proud house."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The name is still preserved in Central Asia with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balkan_Daglary" title="Balkan Daglary">Balkan Daglary</a> (Balkan Mountains)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balkan_Province" title="Balkan Province">Balkan Province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Turkmenistan" title="Turkmenistan">Turkmenistan</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turkish_language" title="Ottoman Turkish language">Turkish</a> <i><span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr">balkan</span></i> means "a chain of wooded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mountains" title="Mountains">mountains</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balkan._5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Balkan.-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Antiquity and the Middle Ages the mountains were known by their <a href="/wiki/Thracian_language" title="Thracian language">Thracian</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heamus_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-heamus-1">[1]</a></sup> name: the <a href="/wiki/Haemus_Mons" title="Haemus Mons">Haemus Mons</a>. Scholars consider that Haemus (Αἷμος) is believed that the name is derived from a <a href="/wiki/Thracian_language" title="Thracian language">Thracian</a> word <i>*saimon</i>, 'mountain ridge'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The name of the place where the range meets the Black Sea, <a href="/wiki/Cape_Emine" title="Cape Emine">Cape Emine</a>, is derived from <i>Aemon</i>. A <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">folk etymology</a> holds that 'Haemus' derives from the Greek word "haema" (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">αἵμα</span>) meaning 'blood', based on a fictional story in Greek mythology during the fight between <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> and the monster/titan <a href="/wiki/Typhon" title="Typhon">Typhon</a>, Zeus injured Typhon with a thunder and Typhon's blood fell on the mountains from which they got their name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other names used to refer to the mountains in different time periods include <i>Aemon</i>, <i>Haemimons</i>, <i>Hem</i>, <i>Emus</i>, the Slavonic <i>Matorni gori</i> and the Turkish <i>Kodzhabalkan</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>Geologically, the Balkan Mountains are a "young" part of the <a href="/wiki/Alpide_belt" title="Alpide belt">Alp-Himalayan</a> chain that stretches across most of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. It can be divided into two parts: the main Balkan Chain and the Pre-Balkans (Fore-Balkan) to the north, which intrude slightly into the <a href="/wiki/Danubian_Plain_(Bulgaria)" title="Danubian Plain (Bulgaria)">Danubian Plain</a>. To the south, the mountain borders with the <a href="/wiki/Sub-Balkan_valleys" title="Sub-Balkan valleys">Sub-Balkan valleys</a> - a row of 11 valleys running from the border with Serbia to the west to the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> to the east which separate the Balkan mountains from a chain of other mountains known as <i>Srednogorie</i> which include <a href="/wiki/Vitosha" title="Vitosha">Vitosha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sredna_Gora" title="Sredna Gora">Sredna Gora</a>.</p>
<p>The range consists of around 30 portions called mountains. The Balkan Mountains can be divided into three sections:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <i>Western Balkan Mountains</i> from <a href="/wiki/Vrashka_Chuka" title="Vrashka Chuka">Vrashka Chuka</a> at the border with Serbia to the Pass of <a href="/wiki/Arabakonak" title="Arabakonak">Arabakonak</a> with a total length of 190 kilometres (120 mi) and highest peak <a href="/wiki/Mid%C5%BEor" title="Midžor">Midžor</a> at 2,169 metres (7,116 ft).</li>
<li>The <i>Central Balkan Mountains</i> from Arabakonak to the <a href="/wiki/Vratnik_Pass_(Bulgaria)" title="Vratnik Pass (Bulgaria)">Vratnik Pass</a> with a length of 207 kilometres (129 mi) and highest peak <a href="/wiki/Botev_Peak" title="Botev Peak">Botev Peak</a> at 2,376 metres (7,795 ft), which is the highest in the range is located in that section.</li>
<li>The <i>Eastern Balkan Mountains</i> from the Vratnik Pass to <a href="/wiki/Cape_Emine" title="Cape Emine">Cape Emine</a> with a length of 160 kilometres (99 mi) and highest peak Balgarka at 1,181 metres (3,875 ft). The eastern Balkan Mountains forms the lowest part of the range.</li>
</ul>
<p>The Balkan Mountains form a water divide between the rivers flowing to the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> in the north and those flowing to the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">Aegean Sea</a> in the south. However, they are crossed by Bulgaria's widest river, the <a href="/wiki/Iskar_(river)" title="Iskar (river)">Iskar</a>, which forms the spectacular <a href="/wiki/Iskar_Gorge" title="Iskar Gorge">Iskar Gorge</a>. Rivers that take their source from the Balkan Mountains and flow northwards to the Danube include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timok_River" title="Timok River">Timok</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archar_River" title="Archar River">Archar</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lom_River" title="Lom River">Lom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tsibritsa" title="Tsibritsa">Tsibritsa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ogosta" title="Ogosta">Ogosta</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skat_River" title="Skat River">Skat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vit" title="Vit">Vit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osam" title="Osam">Osam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yantra" title="Yantra">Yantra</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rusenski_Lom" title="Rusenski Lom">Rusenski Lom</a>. The mountains are also the source of the <a href="/wiki/Kamchiya" title="Kamchiya">Kamchiya</a>, which flows directly into the Black Sea. Although not so abundant in mineral waters as other parts of Bulgaria, there are several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spa_towns" title="Spa towns">spas</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Varshets" title="Varshets">Varshets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shipkovo" title="Shipkovo">Shipkovo</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Voneshta_Voda&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Voneshta Voda (page does not exist)">Voneshta Voda</a>. There are a number of waterfalls, especially in the western and central parts of the range, such as <a href="/wiki/Raysko_Praskalo" title="Raysko Praskalo">Raysko Praskalo</a> which is the highest waterfall in the Balkan Peninsula, <a href="/wiki/Borov_Kamak" title="Borov Kamak">Borov Kamak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Babsko_Praskalo" title="Babsko Praskalo">Babsko Praskalo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Etropole_Waterfall" title="Etropole Waterfall">Etropole Waterfall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karlovsko_Praskalo" title="Karlovsko Praskalo">Karlovsko Praskalo</a>, Skaklya and others.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Passes">Passes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balkan_Mountains&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Passes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The mountains are crossed by 20 passes and two gorges. There are paved roads crossing the Balkan Mountains at the following <a href="/wiki/Mountain_pass" title="Mountain pass">passes</a> (listed from west to east):</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Petrohan_Pass" title="Petrohan Pass">Petrohan Pass</a>: <a href="/wiki/Sofia" title="Sofia">Sofia</a> - <a href="/wiki/Montana,_Bulgaria" title="Montana, Bulgaria">Montana</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Iskar_Gorge" title="Iskar Gorge">Iskar Gorge</a> (<i>Iskarski prolom</i>): Sofia - <a href="/wiki/Vratsa" title="Vratsa">Vratsa</a> (also railroad)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vitinya_Pass" title="Vitinya Pass">Vitinya Pass</a>: <a href="/wiki/Hemus_motorway" title="Hemus motorway">Hemus motorway</a> (A2), Sofia - <a href="/wiki/Botevgrad" title="Botevgrad">Botevgrad</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Beklemeto_Pass" title="Beklemeto Pass">Beklemeto Pass</a>: <a href="/wiki/Troyan" title="Troyan">Troyan</a> - <a href="/wiki/Sopot,_Lovech_Province" title="Sopot, Lovech Province">Sopot</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Shipka_Pass" title="Shipka Pass">Shipka Pass</a>: <a href="/wiki/Gabrovo" title="Gabrovo">Gabrovo</a> - <a href="/wiki/Kazanlak" title="Kazanlak">Kazanlak</a> (also railroad)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pass_of_the_Republic" title="Pass of the Republic">Pass of the Republic</a> (<i>Prohod na republikata</i>): <a href="/wiki/Veliko_Tarnovo" title="Veliko Tarnovo">Veliko Tarnovo</a> - <a href="/wiki/Gurkovo" title="Gurkovo">Gurkovo</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vratnik_Pass_(Bulgaria)" title="Vratnik Pass (Bulgaria)">Vratnik Pass</a>: <a href="/wiki/Elena,_Bulgaria" title="Elena, Bulgaria">Elena</a> - <a href="/wiki/Sliven" title="Sliven">Sliven</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kotel_Pass" title="Kotel Pass">Kotel Pass</a> (<i>Kotlenski prohod</i>): <a href="/wiki/Kotel,_Bulgaria" title="Kotel, Bulgaria">Kotel</a> - Petolachka (<i>Pentagram</i>) crossroads</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Varbitsa_Pass" title="Varbitsa Pass">Varbitsa Pass</a> (<i>Varbishki prohod</i>): <a href="/wiki/Shumen" title="Shumen">Shumen</a> - Petolachka crossroads</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rish_Pass" title="Rish Pass">Rish Pass</a> (<i>Rishki prohod</i>): Shumen - <a href="/wiki/Karnobat" title="Karnobat">Karnobat</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Luda_Kamchiya_Gorge" title="Luda Kamchiya Gorge">Luda Kamchiya Gorge</a> (<i>Ludokamchiyski prolom</i>): <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Provadiya" title="Provadiya">Provadiya</a> - Karnobat (also railroad)</li>
<li>Aytos Pass (Aytoski prohod) - <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Provadiya" title="Provadiya">Provadiya</a> - <a href="/wiki/Aytos" title="Aytos">Aytos</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dyulino_Pass" title="Dyulino Pass">Dyulino Pass</a> (<i>Dyulinski prohod</i>): <a href="/wiki/Varna" title="Varna">Varna</a> - Aytos</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Obzor_Pass" title="Obzor Pass">Obzor Pass</a> (<i>Obzorski prohod</i>): Varna - <a href="/wiki/Burgas" title="Burgas">Burgas</a>, future <a href="/wiki/Cherno_More_motorway" title="Cherno More motorway">Cherno More motorway</a> (A3)</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balkan_Mountains&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Balkan Mountains have had a significant and special place in the history of Bulgaria since its foundation in 681. It was a natural fortress of the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Empire" title="Bulgarian Empire">Bulgarian Empire</a> for centuries and formed an effective barrier to <a href="/wiki/Moesia" title="Moesia">Moesia</a> where most of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> capitals were located. The Balkan mountains were the site of numerous battles between the Bulgarian and the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empires</a> including the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Rishki_Pass" title="Battle of the Rishki Pass">Battle of the Rishki Pass</a> (759), <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Pliska" title="Battle of Pliska">Battle of the Varbitsa Pass</a> (811), <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tryavna" title="Battle of Tryavna">Battle of Tryavna</a> (1190) and many others. In the battle of the Varbitsa Pass, Khan <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Krum_of_Bulgaria" title="Krum of Bulgaria">Krum</a> decisively defeated an enormous Byzantine army, killing Emperor <a href="/wiki/Nikephoros_I" title="Nikephoros I">Nikephoros I</a>. For many centuries the Byzantines feared these mountains, and on several occasions Byzantine armies pulled back only on the news of approaching the Balkan Mountains.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Bulgaria" title="Ottoman Bulgaria">Ottoman rule</a>, many <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haiduk" title="Haiduk">haiduks</a></i> found refuge in the Balkan Mountains. Close to the highest summit, <a href="/wiki/Botev_Peak" title="Botev Peak">Botev Peak</a>, is <a href="/wiki/Kalofer" title="Kalofer">Kalofer</a>, the birthplace of <a href="/wiki/Hristo_Botev" title="Hristo Botev">Hristo Botev</a>, a Bulgarian poet and national hero who died in the western Balkan Mountains near <a href="/wiki/Vratsa" title="Vratsa">Vratsa</a> in 1876 in the struggle against the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>. Also close to Botev Peak is the <a href="/wiki/Shipka_Pass" title="Shipka Pass">Shipka Pass</a>, the scene of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Shipka_Pass" title="Battle of Shipka Pass">four battles</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russo-Turkish_War,_1877-78" title="Russo-Turkish War, 1877-78">Russo-Turkish War, 1877-78</a>, which ended Turkish rule in the Balkans. Close to the pass in the village of Shipka, there is a <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church" title="Russian Orthodox Church">Russian Orthodox</a> church built to commemorate Russian and Bulgarian bravery during pass defense.</p>
