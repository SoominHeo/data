<p><b>The Baltic Fleet</b> (Балтийский флот<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federation" title="Russian Federation">Russian Federation</a> <a href="/wiki/Russian_Navy" title="Russian Navy">Navy</a>'s presence in the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>. In previous historical periods, it has been part of the navy of <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Russian_Navy" title="Imperial Russian Navy">Imperial Russia</a> and later the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Navy" title="Soviet Navy">Union of Soviet Socialist Republics (Soviet Union)</a>. The Fleet gained the '"Twice Red Banner"' appellation during the Soviet period, indicating two awards of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Red_Banner" title="Order of the Red Banner">Order of the Red Banner</a>. It is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Kaliningrad" title="Kaliningrad">Kaliningrad</a> (formerly <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg" title="Königsberg">Königsberg</a> in <a href="/wiki/East_Prussia" title="East Prussia">East Prussia</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_Germany" title="Imperial Germany">Imperial Germany</a>), with its main base in <a href="/wiki/Baltiysk" title="Baltiysk">Baltiysk</a> and another base at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kronshtadt" title="Kronshtadt">Kronshtadt</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Finland" title="Gulf of Finland">Gulf of Finland</a>. Established 18 May 1703, under Czar <a href="/wiki/Peter_the_Great" title="Peter the Great">Peter the Great</a>, the Fleet is the oldest Russian Navy formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Imperial_Russia">Imperial Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Fleet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Imperial Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Imperial Russian Baltic Fleet was created during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Northern_War" title="Great Northern War">Great Northern War</a> at the initiative of Czar <a href="/wiki/Peter_the_Great" title="Peter the Great">Peter the Great</a>, who ordered the first ships for the Baltic Fleet to be constructed at <a href="/wiki/Lodeynoye_Pole" title="Lodeynoye Pole">Lodeynoye Pole</a> in 1702 and 1703. The first commander was a recruited <a href="/wiki/Holland" title="Holland">Dutch</a> admiral, <a href="/wiki/Cornelius_Cruys" title="Cornelius Cruys">Cornelius Cruys</a>, who in 1723 was succeeded by Count <a href="/wiki/Fyodor_Apraksin" title="Fyodor Apraksin">Fyodor Apraksin</a>. In 1703, the main base of the fleet was established in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kronshtadt" title="Kronshtadt">Kronshtadt</a>. One of the fleet's first actions was the taking of <a href="/wiki/Shlisselburg" title="Shlisselburg">Shlisselburg</a>. In 1701 Peter the Great established a special school, the School of Mathematics and Navigation (Russian: Школа математических и навигацких наук), situated in the <a href="/wiki/Sukharev_Tower" title="Sukharev Tower">Sukharev Tower</a> in Moscow. As the territory to the west around the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Finland" title="Gulf of Finland">Gulf of Finland</a> was acquired by Russia for a "warm-water" port giving access for its merchantmen and the buildup of a naval force, the city of St. Petersburg was built and developed an extensive port. The Fleet's base was moved to St. Petersburg and in 1752 it was renamed the Naval Cadet Corps. Today it is the St. Petersburg Naval Institute – Peter the Great Naval Corps.</p>

<p>The Baltic Fleet began to receive new vessels in 1703. The first vessel, the 24-gun three-<a href="/wiki/Mast_(sailing)" title="Mast (sailing)">masted</a> frigate <a href="/wiki/Shtandart_(frigate)" title="Shtandart (frigate)"><i>Shtandart</i></a>, is considered to <a href="/wiki/Flagship" title="Flagship">flagship</a> of the fleet and an example of a newly popular design of warship, the <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigate</a>.</p>
<p>By 1724, the fleet boasted 141 sail warships and hundreds of oar-propelled ships.</p>

<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Great_Northern_War" title="Great Northern War">Great Northern War</a>, the Baltic Fleet assisted in taking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viborg_Province" title="Viborg Province">Viborg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tallinn" title="Tallinn">Tallinn</a>, (<a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>), <a href="/wiki/Riga" title="Riga">Riga</a>, (<a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a>), the <a href="/wiki/West_Estonian_archipelago" title="West Estonian archipelago">West Estonian archipelago</a> (Moonsund archipelago), <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a>, (<a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Turku" title="Turku">Turku</a>. The first claimed victories of the new Imperial Russian Navy were the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gangut" title="Battle of Gangut">Gangut</a> (Swedish: Hangöudd) in 1714 and, arguably, the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Grengam" title="Battle of Grengam">Grengam</a> (Swedish: Ledsund) in 1720. From 1715, the English <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> intervened in the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> on behalf of the German principality of <a href="/wiki/Hanover" title="Hanover">Hanover</a>, (dynastic home of the current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_monarchy" title="British monarchy">British monarchy</a>) and more or less in a tacit alliance with Russia. During the concluding stages of the war, the Russian fleet would land troops along the Swedish coast to devastate coastal settlements. However, after the death of King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_XII" title="Charles XII">Charles XII</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> would rather protect Swedish interests after a rapprochement between the Kingdom of Sweden and <a href="/wiki/George_I_of_Great_Britain" title="George I of Great Britain">King George I</a>. A Russian attempt to reach the Swedish capital of <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> was checked at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_St%C3%A4ket" title="Battle of Stäket">Battle of Stäket</a> in 1719. The losses suffered by the Russian Navy at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Grengam" title="Battle of Grengam">Grengam</a> in 1720, as well as the arrival of a Royal Navy squadron under Admiral <a href="/wiki/John_Norris_(Royal_Navy_officer)" title="John Norris (Royal Navy officer)">John Norris</a>, also prevented further operations of any greater scale before the war ended in 1721.</p>
<p>During the "<a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a>", (1755-1763), the Russian Baltic Sea fleet was active on the <a href="/wiki/Pomerania" title="Pomerania">Pomeranian coast</a> of northern <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a>, helping the infantry to take <a href="/wiki/Klaip%C4%97da" title="Klaipėda">Memel</a> in 1757 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kolberg" title="Kolberg">Kolberg</a> in 1761. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oresund" title="Oresund">Oresund</a> was blockaded in order to prevent the British Navy from entering the Baltic sea. During the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Swedish_War_(1788%E2%80%931790)" title="Russo-Swedish War (1788–1790)">Russo-Swedish War (1788–1790)</a> the fleet, commanded by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Greig" title="Samuel Greig">Samuel Greig</a>, checked the Swedes <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hogland" title="Battle of Hogland">at Hogland</a> (1788) and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Vyborg_Bay_(1790)" title="Battle of Vyborg Bay (1790)">Viborg</a> (1790). An impetuous Russian attack on the Swedish galley flotilla on 9 July 1790 at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_Svensksund" title="Second Battle of Svensksund">Second Battle of Svensksund</a> resulted in a disaster for the Russian Navy who lost some 9,500 out of 14,000 men and about one third of their flotilla. The Russian defeat in this battle effectively ended the war.</p>
<p>During the series of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russo-Turkish_Wars" title="Russo-Turkish Wars">Russo-Turkish Wars</a>, (1710-1711, 1735-1739, 1768-1774, 1787-1792, 1806-1812, 1828-1829), the fleet sailed into the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Archipelago_Expedition" title="First Archipelago Expedition">First</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Archipelago_Expedition" title="Second Archipelago Expedition">Second</a> Archipelago Expeditions and destroyed the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Imperial Navy</a> at the sea <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Chesma" title="Battle of Chesma">Battles of Chesma</a> (1770), the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Dardanelles_(1807)" title="Battle of the Dardanelles (1807)">Dardanelles</a> (1807), <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Athos" title="Battle of Athos">Athos</a> (1807), and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Navarino" title="Battle of Navarino">Navarino</a> (1827). At about the same time, Russian Admiral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ivan_Krusenstern" title="Ivan Krusenstern">Ivan Krusenstern</a> circumnavigated the globe, while another Baltic Fleet officer — <a href="/wiki/Fabian_Gottlieb_von_Bellingshausen" title="Fabian Gottlieb von Bellingshausen">Fabian Gottlieb von Bellingshausen</a> — discovered the southern ice-covered continent, <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_War" title="Crimean War">Crimean War</a>, (1853-1856), the fleet – although stymied in its operations by the absence of steamships – prevented the British and French Allies from occupying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hang%C3%B6" title="Hangö">Hangö</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sveaborg" title="Sveaborg">Sveaborg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a>. Despite being greatly outnumbered by the technologically superior Allies, it was the Russian Fleet that introduced into naval warfare such novelties as torpedo mines, invented by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boris_Yakobi" title="Boris Yakobi">Boris Yakobi</a>. Other outstanding inventors who served in the Baltic Fleet were <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Stepanovich_Popov" title="Alexander Stepanovich Popov">Alexander Stepanovich Popov</a> (who was the first to demonstrate the practical application of electromagnetic (radio) waves<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>), <a href="/wiki/Stepan_Makarov" title="Stepan Makarov">Stepan Makarov</a> (the first to launch <a href="/wiki/Torpedo" title="Torpedo">torpedoes</a> from a boat), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexei_Krylov" title="Alexei Krylov">Alexei Krylov</a> (author of the modern <a href="/wiki/Ship_floodability" title="Ship floodability">ship floodability</a> theory), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Mozhaiski" title="Alexander Mozhaiski">Alexander Mozhaiski</a> (co-inventor of aircraft).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Age_of_iron">Age of iron</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Fleet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Age of iron">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As early as 1861, the first armor-clad ships were built for the Baltic Fleet. In 1863, during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, most of the Fleet's ocean-going ships, including the flagship <a href="/wiki/Russian_frigate_Alexander_Nevsky" title="Russian frigate Alexander Nevsky"><i>"Alexander Nevsky"</i></a> were sent to <a href="/wiki/History_of_New_York_City" title="History of New York City">New York City</a>. At the same time ten <a href="/wiki/Uragan-class_monitor" title="Uragan-class monitor"><i>"Uragan"</i>-class monitors</a> based on an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passaic_class_monitor" title="Passaic class monitor">American-designed "Passaic" monitor</a> were launched. Here it was the policy of the Czar and his government to show support for the Northern <a href="/wiki/Union_Army" title="Union Army">Union Army</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> during their <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>, observing and exchanging naval tactics and cooperation. In 1869, the fleet commissioned the first <a href="/wiki/Turret" title="Turret">turret</a> on a <a href="/wiki/Battleship" title="Battleship">battleship</a> in the world – <a href="/wiki/Russian_ironclad_Petr_Veliky" title="Russian ironclad Petr Veliky"><i>"Petr Veliky"</i></a>. Furthermore, in the second half of the 19th and early 20th Century a strong network of <a href="/wiki/Coastal_artillery" title="Coastal artillery">coastal artillery</a> batteries was created to cover the approaches to St. Petersburg, <a href="/wiki/Riga" title="Riga">Riga</a>, and other important bases.</p>

<p>The Baltic Fleet took a prominent part in the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese War</a>. After the defeat of earlier <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Fleet_(Russia)" title="Pacific Fleet (Russia)">Far East Fleet</a> vessels, in September 1904, a squadron under the command of Admiral <a href="/wiki/Zinovy_Rozhestvensky" title="Zinovy Rozhestvensky">Zinovy Rozhestvensky</a> was sent on a famous high-speed dash around <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> – stopping in French, German and Portuguese colonial ports: <a href="/wiki/Tangier" title="Tangier">Tangier</a> in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dakar" title="Dakar">Dakar</a> in <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a>, Gabon, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ba%C3%ADa_dos_Tigres" title="Baía dos Tigres">Baía dos Tigres</a>, <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCderitz_Bay" title="Lüderitz Bay">Lüderitz Bay</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nossi_Be" title="Nossi Be">Nossi Be</a> (<a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>), then across the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> to <a href="/wiki/Cam_Ranh_Bay" title="Cam Ranh Bay">Cam Ranh Bay</a> in <a href="/wiki/French_Indochina" title="French Indochina">French Indochina</a> and then northward to its doomed encounter with the Japanese fleet at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tsushima" title="Battle of Tsushima">Battle of Tsushima</a> off the east coast of <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> in 1905, ending the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese War</a>. The Imperial German civilian passenger <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hamburg-Amerika_Line" title="Hamburg-Amerika Line">Hamburg-Amerika Line</a> provided 60 colliers to supply the Baltic Fleet on its epic journey. During its passage through the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> the Fleet mistook a fleet of British fishing boats for Japanese torpedo boats and opened fire, killing three sailors in what is known as the "<a href="/wiki/Dogger_Bank_incident" title="Dogger Bank incident">Dogger Bank incident</a>". The decision to send the Fleet to the Pacific was made after Russia had suffered a string of naval defeats in the <a href="/wiki/East_China_Sea" title="East China Sea">East China Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Japan" title="Sea of Japan">Sea of Japan</a> off the coast of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> near its Far East naval base and colony at the hands of the newly emergent Imperial Japanese Navy and Army in <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a>. This historic naval battle broke Russian strength in East Asia and set the stage for the unsuccessful uprising in the abortive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution_of_1905" title="Russian Revolution of 1905">Russian Revolution of 1905</a>, which began the decline that would see the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanov" title="Romanov">Romanov</a> dynasty monarchy eventually brought down with the strains of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Revolutions_of_1917" title="Russian Revolutions of 1917">1917</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_I">World War I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Fleet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: World War I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Buberel_StPetersburg_StNicolas_Cathedral.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="364" data-file-width="399" height="201" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Buberel_StPetersburg_StNicolas_Cathedral.jpg/220px-Buberel_StPetersburg_StNicolas_Cathedral.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Buberel_StPetersburg_StNicolas_Cathedral.jpg/330px-Buberel_StPetersburg_StNicolas_Cathedral.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e4/Buberel_StPetersburg_StNicolas_Cathedral.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

The naval St. Nicholas Cathedral in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Petersburg" title="St. Petersburg">St. Petersburg</a> is the main church of the Russian Navy. Its outside is covered with plaques to Russian sailors/officers lost at sea.</div>

<p>Following the catastrophic losses in battleships during the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese War</a>, Russia embarked on a new naval building program which was to incorporate a number of the most modern <a href="/wiki/HMS_Dreadnought_(1906)" title="HMS Dreadnought (1906)">"dreadnought"</a>-type <a href="/wiki/Battleship" title="Battleship">battleships</a> into the fleet along with other vessels and practices adopted from the Western navies. In late 1914, four "dreadnoughts" of the <a href="/wiki/Gangut-class_battleship" title="Gangut-class battleship"><i>"Gangut"</i> class</a> entered service with the Fleet: <a href="/wiki/Russian_battleship_Gangut_(1911)" title="Russian battleship Gangut (1911)"><i>Gangut</i></a>; <a href="/wiki/Russian_battleship_Poltava_(1911)" title="Russian battleship Poltava (1911)"><i>Poltava</i></a>; <a href="/wiki/Russian_battleship_Petropavlovsk_(1911)" title="Russian battleship Petropavlovsk (1911)"><i>Petropavlovsk</i></a>; and <a href="/wiki/Russian_battleship_Sevastopol_(1911)" title="Russian battleship Sevastopol (1911)"><i>Sevastopol</i></a>. Four more powerful battlecruisers of the <a href="/wiki/Borodino-class_battlecruiser" title="Borodino-class battlecruiser"><i>"Borodino"</i> class</a> were under construction, but were never completed. The Fleet's main operation during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> was the <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cruise_of_the_Baltic_Fleet" title="Ice Cruise of the Baltic Fleet">"Ice Cruise"</a> of the Baltic Fleet (1918), led by Admiral <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Zelenoy" title="Alexander Zelenoy">Alexander Zelenoy</a>. However, on the whole the heavy units of the Fleet remained in port during the war, as the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_German_Navy" title="Imperial German Navy">Imperial German Navy</a>'s superiority in battleships and other vessels was overwhelming and it was difficult to communicate with <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> forces further west in the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> even though they had the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_Germany" title="Imperial Germany">Germans</a> bottled up after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Jutland" title="Battle of Jutland">Battle of Jutland</a> in 1916.</p>
<p>The Imperial Russian Navy's Baltic Fleet included a submarine division that had about 30 submarines (undersea boats) of several classes and various auxiliary vessels, the largest of which were the transport and mother ships <i>"Europa"</i>, <i>"Tosno"</i>, <i>"Khabarovsk"</i>, <i>"Oland"</i> and <i>"Svjatitel Nikolai"</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Some of the Fleet's 355-ton submarines were made by <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_Electric_Boat" title="General Dynamics Electric Boat">Electric Boat Company</a> of <a href="/wiki/Groton,_Connecticut" title="Groton, Connecticut">Groton, Connecticut</a> in the United States, main supplier and builder of subs for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">U.S. Navy</a>. Five of these "AG (Holland)" class submarines were prefabricated by the British Pacific Engineering &amp; Construction Company at <a href="/wiki/Barnet,_British_Columbia" title="Barnet, British Columbia">Barnet</a> (near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vancouver,_British_Columbia" title="Vancouver, British Columbia">Vancouver</a>), in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada's</a> <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, also under contract to the Electric Boat Company. These Canadian-built subs were shipped to Russia, a fellow Ally in the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a> in December 1915.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Four of these submarines, AG 11, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_submarine_AG_12" title="Russian submarine AG 12">AG 12</a>, AG 15 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_submarine_AG_16" title="Russian submarine AG 16">AG 16</a> were scuttled in the harbour of <a href="/wiki/Hanko" title="Hanko">Hanko</a> on 3 April 1918, just before the 10,000-strong Imperial German <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea_Division" title="Baltic Sea Division">Baltic Sea Division</a> landed in support of the <a href="/wiki/White_movement" title="White movement">"Whites"</a> forces in the little known <a href="/wiki/Finnish_Civil_War" title="Finnish Civil War">Finnish Civil War</a>. During the war the Fleet was aided by <a href="/wiki/British_submarine_flotilla_in_the_Baltic" title="British submarine flotilla in the Baltic">a detachment of British Royal Navy submarines</a>. These subs were later scuttled by their crews near the <a href="/wiki/Harmaja" title="Harmaja">Harmaja</a> lighthouse outside <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, on 4 April 1918.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Soviet_era">Soviet era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Fleet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Soviet era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="October_Revolution_and_Russian_Civil_War_.281917.E2.80.9322.29">October Revolution and Russian Civil War (1917–22)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Fleet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: October Revolution and Russian Civil War (1917–22)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Fleet_during_the_October_Revolution_and_Russian_Civil_War" title="Baltic Fleet during the October Revolution and Russian Civil War">Baltic Fleet during the October Revolution and Russian Civil War</a></div>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/October_Revolution" title="October Revolution">October Revolution</a> the sailors of the Baltic Fleet (renamed "Naval Forces of the Baltic Sea" in March 1918)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-militera.lib.ru_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-militera.lib.ru-9">[9]</a></sup> were among the most ardent supporters of <a href="/wiki/Bolsheviks" title="Bolsheviks">Bolsheviks</a>, and formed an elite among Red military forces. Some ships of the fleet took part in the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Civil_War" title="Russian Civil War">Russian Civil War</a>, notably by clashing with the British navy operating in the Baltic as part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allied_Intervention_in_the_Russian_Civil_War" title="Allied Intervention in the Russian Civil War">intervention forces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Over the years, however, the relations of the Baltic Fleet sailors with the Bolshevik regime soured, and they eventually rebelled against the Soviet government in the <a href="/wiki/Kronstadt_rebellion" title="Kronstadt rebellion">Kronstadt rebellion</a> in 1921, but were defeated, and the Fleet de facto ceased to exist as an active military unit.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1922.E2.80.9341">1922–41</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Fleet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1922–41">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Fleet, renamed the Red-Banner Baltic Fleet on 11 January 1935,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-militera.lib.ru_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-militera.lib.ru-9">[9]</a></sup> was developed further during the Soviet years, initially relying on tsarist warships, but adding modern units built in Soviet yards from the 1930s onwards. Among the Fleet's Soviet commanders were <a href="/wiki/Gordey_Levchenko" title="Gordey Levchenko">Gordey Levchenko</a> in 1938–39 and <a href="/wiki/Arseniy_Golovko" title="Arseniy Golovko">Arseniy Golovko</a> in 1952–56. Ships and submarines <a href="/wiki/Ship_commissioning" title="Ship commissioning">commissioned</a> to the fleet included <a href="/wiki/Soviet_submarine_M-256" title="Soviet submarine M-256">Soviet submarine <i>M-256</i></a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_615" title="Project 615">Project 615</a> short-range attack <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel</a> <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarine</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Navy" title="Soviet Navy">Soviet Navy</a>. The fleet also acquired a large number of ground-based aircraft to form a strong <a href="/wiki/Naval_aviation" title="Naval aviation">naval aviation</a> force.</p>
<p>In September 1939, the fleet threatened the Baltic states as part of a series of military actions staged to encourage the Baltics to accept Soviet offers of "mutual assistance."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TM091939_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-TM091939-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Subsequently, in June 1940, the fleet blockaded the Baltics in support of the Soviet invasion.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Winter_War">Winter War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Fleet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Winter War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Finland, which had refused to sign a "pact of mutual assistance", was attacked by the USSR. The fleet played a limited role in the <a href="/wiki/Winter_War" title="Winter War">Winter War</a> with <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> in 1939–1940, mostly through conducting artillery bombardments of Finnish coastal fortifications. Many fleet aircraft were involved in operations against Finland, however. Its operations came to a close with the freezing of the Gulf of Finland during the exceptionally cold winter of that year.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Fleet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea_Campaigns_(1939-1945)" title="Baltic Sea Campaigns (1939-1945)">Baltic Sea Campaigns (1939-1945)</a></div>
<p>In the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Front_(World_War_II)" title="Eastern Front (World War II)">German invasion</a> the Baltic Fleet had 2 <a href="/wiki/Battleship" title="Battleship">battleships</a>, 2 <a href="/wiki/Cruiser" title="Cruiser">cruisers</a>, 2 <a href="/wiki/Flotilla_leader" title="Flotilla leader">flotilla leaders</a>, 19 <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyers</a>, 48 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motor_torpedo_boat" title="Motor torpedo boat">MTBs</a>, 65 <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarines</a> and other ships, and 656 aircraft. During the war the Fleet, commanded by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vice-Admiral" title="Vice-Admiral">Vice-Admiral</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vladimir_Tributz" title="Vladimir Tributz">Vladimir Tributz</a>, defended the <a href="/wiki/Hanko_Peninsula" title="Hanko Peninsula">Hanko Peninsula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tallinn" title="Tallinn">Tallinn</a>, several islands in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estonian_SSR" title="Estonian SSR">Estonian SSR</a>, participated in the break through breach of the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Leningrad" title="Siege of Leningrad">Siege of Leningrad</a>, etc. 137 sailors of the Baltic Fleet were awarded a title of the <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Hero of the Soviet Union">Hero of the Soviet Union</a>. However, for most of the war the fleet was trapped by German and Finnish minefields in Leningrad and nearby Kronstadt, the only bases left in Soviet hands on the Baltic coast. Another key factor was that the Finns had recaptured outer islands of the Gulf of Finland, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suursaari" title="Suursaari">Suursaari</a> being the most important of them. Many of the fleet sailors fought on land as infantry during the siege. Only submarines could risk the passage into the open sea to strike at German shipping. They were particularly successful towards the end of the war, sinking ships like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Gustloff_(ship)" title="Wilhelm Gustloff (ship)">Wilhelm Gustloff</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dampfschiff_General_von_Steuben" title="Dampfschiff General von Steuben">Steuben</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goya_(ship)" title="Goya (ship)">Goya</a>, causing great loss of life.</p>
<p>The Fleet carried out the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_evacuation_of_Tallinn" title="Soviet evacuation of Tallinn">Soviet evacuation of Tallinn</a> in late August 1941.</p>

<p>During the Immediate post-war period the importance of the Red-Banner Baltic Fleet increased despite the Baltic being a shallow sea with the exits easily becoming choke points by other countries. The Baltic Fleet was increased to two Fleets, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=4th_Red-Banner_Baltic_Fleet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="4th Red-Banner Baltic Fleet (page does not exist)">4th Red-Banner Baltic Fleet</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=8th_Red-Banner_Baltic_Fleet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="8th Red-Banner Baltic Fleet (page does not exist)">8th Red-Banner Baltic Fleet</a> on 15 February 1946. However, during the post-Stalinist period and general reforms and downsizing in the Soviet Armed Forces the two fleets of the Baltic were again reduced, with many vessels, some built before the Revolution, were scrapped, and the Fleet was again renamed Red-Banner Baltic Fleet on 24 December 1955.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-militera.lib.ru_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-militera.lib.ru-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Liepāja the Baltic Fleet's 14th submarine squadron, call sign "Kompleks" ("Комплекс") was stationed with 16 submarines (<a href="/wiki/Whiskey-class_submarine" title="Whiskey-class submarine">613</a>, <a href="/wiki/Golf-class_submarine" title="Golf-class submarine">629a</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juliett-class_submarine" title="Juliett-class submarine">651</a>); as was the 6th group of rear supply of Baltic Fleet, and the 81st design bureau and reserve command center of the same force.</p>
<p>Far from being reduced in importance, operations of the Red-Banner Baltic Fleet during the early-<a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> period earned it a great amount of prestige and profile, with the second awarding of the Order of Red Banner being presented on 7 May 1965 when the Fleet was again renamed to Twice Red-Banner Baltic Fleet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-militera.lib.ru_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-militera.lib.ru-9">[9]</a></sup> Although the Soviet Union poured resources into building up the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_Red_Banner_Northern_Fleet" title="Soviet Red Banner Northern Fleet">Northern Fleet</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Pacific_Fleet" title="Russian Pacific Fleet">Pacific Fleet</a>, both of which had easy access to the open ocean, the Twice Red-Banner Baltic Fleet assumed the very important position of supporting the northern flank of the European Theatre in case of a confrontation with NATO. This role was under-rated from the <a href="/wiki/Blue-water_navy" title="Blue-water navy">blue water navies</a> perspective, but was seen as a highly valuable one from the strategic perspective of the Soviet General Staff planning. The Twice Red-Banner Baltic Fleet remained a powerful force, which in the event of war was tasked with conducting <a href="/wiki/Naval_Infantry_(Russia)" title="Naval Infantry (Russia)">amphibious assaults</a> against the coast of Denmark and Germany, in cooperation with allied <a href="/wiki/Polish_Navy" title="Polish Navy">Polish</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East German</a> naval forces.</p>
<p>A notable incident involving the fleet occurred in 1975 when a mutiny broke out on the frigate <a href="/wiki/Soviet_frigate_Storozhevoy" title="Soviet frigate Storozhevoy"><i>Storozhevoy</i></a>. There were also numerous allegations by Sweden of Baltic Fleet submarines illegally penetrating its territorial waters. In October, 1981 the Soviet Whiskey-class submarine <a href="/wiki/Soviet_submarine_S-363" title="Soviet submarine S-363"><i>U 137</i></a> ran aground in Swedish territorial waters, near the important naval base of <a href="/wiki/Karlskrona" title="Karlskrona">Karlskrona</a>, causing a serious diplomatic incident. Swedish naval vessels pulled the submarine into deeper water and permitted it to return to the Soviet fleet in early November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Under_the_Russian_Federation">Under the Russian Federation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Fleet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Under the Russian Federation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Baltic_fleet_headquarters.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="806" data-file-width="1200" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Baltic_fleet_headquarters.jpg/220px-Baltic_fleet_headquarters.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Baltic_fleet_headquarters.jpg/330px-Baltic_fleet_headquarters.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Baltic_fleet_headquarters.jpg/440px-Baltic_fleet_headquarters.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Baltic Fleet headquarters building, Kaliningrad</div>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_Soviet_Union_(1985-1991)" title="History of the Soviet Union (1985-1991)">breakup</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> deprived the Fleet of key bases in <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a>, leaving <a href="/wiki/Kaliningrad_Oblast" title="Kaliningrad Oblast">Kaliningrad Oblast</a> as the Fleet's only <a href="/wiki/Harbor" title="Harbor">ice-free</a> naval outlet to the Baltic Sea. However, the Kaliningrad Oblast between <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a> is not contiguous with the rest of the national territory of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federation" title="Russian Federation">Russian Federation</a>.</p>
<p>In 1989 <a href="/wiki/3rd_Guards_Motor_Rifle_Division" title="3rd Guards Motor Rifle Division">3rd Guards Motor Rifle Division</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Klaipeda" title="Klaipeda">Klaipeda</a> was transferred to the fleet as a coastal defence division. It was disbanded on 1 September 1993.</p>
<p>In the late 1990s the 336th Independent Guards <a href="/wiki/Naval_Infantry_(Russia)" title="Naval Infantry (Russia)">Naval Infantry</a> Brigade and the remnant of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/11th_Guards_Army" title="11th Guards Army">11th Guards Army</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Military_District" title="Baltic Military District">Baltic Military District</a> were subordinated to a single command named the Ground and Coastal Forces of the Baltic Fleet under a deputy fleet commander. The 11th Guards Army remnant included the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/7th_Guards_Motor_Rifle_Regiment" title="7th Guards Motor Rifle Regiment">7th Guards Motor Rifle Regiment</a> and the brigade that was the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/18th_Guards_Motor_Rifle_Division_(Russia)" title="18th Guards Motor Rifle Division (Russia)">18th Guards Motor Rifle Division</a>, plus several Bases for Storage of Weapons and Equipment, holding enough vehicles and weaponry for a division but only having a few hundred men assigned to maintain the equipment and guard the bases. warfare.be listings in 2013 report that the staff of the Ground and Coastal Defence Forces of the Fleet may have been disbanded in November 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFBNavy_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-WFBNavy-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The fleet's aviation units were equipped with a total of 23 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Su-27" title="Su-27">Su-27</a>, 26 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Su-24" title="Su-24">Su-24</a>, 14 <a href="/wiki/Antonov_An-12" title="Antonov An-12">An-12</a>/<a href="/wiki/Antonov_An-24" title="Antonov An-24">24</a>/<a href="/wiki/Antonov_An-26" title="Antonov An-26">26</a>, 2 <a href="/wiki/Antonov_An-12" title="Antonov An-12">An-12 Cub (MR/EW)</a>, 11 <a href="/wiki/Mil_Mi-24" title="Mil Mi-24">Mi-24 Hind</a>, 19 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ka-28" title="Ka-28">Ka-28 Helix</a>, 8 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ka-29" title="Ka-29">Ka-29 Helix</a> assault helicopters, and 17 <a href="/wiki/Mil_Mi-8" title="Mil Mi-8">Mi-8 Hip</a> transport helicopters in 2007, according to the IISS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2008 the Baltic Fleet included about 75 combat ships of various types.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The main bases is in <a href="/wiki/Baltiysk" title="Baltiysk">Baltiysk</a> and a second operational base is in Kronstadt. The <a href="/wiki/Leningrad_Naval_Base" title="Leningrad Naval Base">Leningrad Naval Base</a> is an administrative entity that is not a discrete geographic location but comprises all of the naval institutions and facilities in the St. Petersburg area. It should be noted that the assignment of the 106th Small Missile Ship Battalion is disputed; warfare.be places it under the 64th Naval Region Protection Brigade, while Holm, probably working from older sources, places it under the 36th Missile Ship Brigade.</p>
<p>In June 2016, fleet commander Vice Admiral <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Viktor_Kravchuk&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Viktor Kravchuk (page does not exist)">Viktor Kravchuk</a> and his chief of staff, Vice Admiral <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sergei_Popov_(admiral)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sergei Popov (admiral) (page does not exist)">Sergei Popov</a>, were dismissed for "serious training shortcomings and distortion of the real situation". <a href="/wiki/N._G._Kuznetsov_Naval_Academy" title="N. G. Kuznetsov Naval Academy">N. G. Kuznetsov Naval Academy</a> commander Vice Admiral <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Nosatov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alexander Nosatov (page does not exist)">Alexander Nosatov</a> was made acting commander of the fleet, a position in which he was confirmed on 17 September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
