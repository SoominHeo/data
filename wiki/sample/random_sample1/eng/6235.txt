<p>The <b>Baltic Way</b> or <b>Baltic Chain</b> (also <i>Chain of Freedom</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">Estonian</a>: <span lang="et"><i>Balti kett</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Latvian_language" title="Latvian language">Latvian</a>: <span lang="lv"><i>Baltijas ceļš</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Lithuanian_language" title="Lithuanian language">Lithuanian</a>: <span lang="lt"><i>Baltijos kelias</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Балтийский путь</span>) was a peaceful political demonstration that occurred on 23 August 1989. Approximately two million people joined their hands to form a <a href="/wiki/Human_chain" title="Human chain">human chain</a> spanning 675.5 kilometres (419.7 mi) across the three <a href="/wiki/Baltic_states" title="Baltic states">Baltic states</a> – <a href="/wiki/Estonian_Soviet_Socialist_Republic" title="Estonian Soviet Socialist Republic">Estonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latvian_Soviet_Socialist_Republic" title="Latvian Soviet Socialist Republic">Latvia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lithuanian_Soviet_Socialist_Republic" title="Lithuanian Soviet Socialist Republic">Lithuania</a>, considered at the time to be <a href="/wiki/Republics_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Republics of the Soviet Union">constituent republics</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>.</p>
<p>The demonstration originated in "Black Ribbon Day" protests held in the western cities in the 1980s. It marked the 50th anniversary of the <a href="/wiki/Molotov%E2%80%93Ribbentrop_Pact" title="Molotov–Ribbentrop Pact">Molotov–Ribbentrop Pact</a> between the Soviet Union and <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>. The pact and its secret protocols divided <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a> into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spheres_of_influence" title="Spheres of influence">spheres of influence</a> and led to the <a href="/wiki/Occupation_of_the_Baltic_states" title="Occupation of the Baltic states">occupation of the Baltic states</a> in 1940. The event was organised by Baltic pro-independence movements: <a href="/wiki/Popular_Front_of_Estonia" title="Popular Front of Estonia">Rahvarinne</a> of Estonia, the <a href="/wiki/Popular_Front_of_Latvia" title="Popular Front of Latvia">Tautas fronte</a> of Latvia, and <a href="/wiki/S%C4%85j%C5%ABdis" title="Sąjūdis">Sąjūdis</a> of Lithuania. The protest was designed to draw global attention by demonstrating a popular desire for independence for each of the entities. It also illustrated solidarity among the three nations. It has been described as an effective publicity campaign, and an emotionally captivating and visually stunning scene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drei_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-drei-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The event presented an opportunity for the Baltic activists to publicise the illegal Soviet occupation and position the question of Baltic independence not as a political matter, but as a moral issue. The Soviet authorities in Moscow responded to the event with intense rhetoric,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drei_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-drei-2">[2]</a></sup> but failed to take any constructive actions that could bridge the widening gap between the Baltic states and the Soviet Union. Within seven months of the protest, Lithuania became the first of the Soviet republics to declare independence.</p>
<p>After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fall_of_Communism" title="Fall of Communism">Fall of Communism</a>, 23 August has become an official <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Remembrance_day" title="Remembrance day">remembrance day</a> both in the Baltic countries, in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> and in other countries, known as the Black Ribbon Day or as the <a href="/wiki/European_Day_of_Remembrance_for_Victims_of_Stalinism_and_Nazism" title="European Day of Remembrance for Victims of Stalinism and Nazism">European Day of Remembrance for Victims of Stalinism and Nazism</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Way&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Baltic_stance">Baltic stance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Way&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Baltic stance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Soviet Union denied the existence of the secret protocols to the <a href="/wiki/Molotov%E2%80%93Ribbentrop_Pact" title="Molotov–Ribbentrop Pact">Molotov–Ribbentrop Pact</a>, even though they were widely published by western scholars after surfacing during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Trials" title="Nuremberg Trials">Nuremberg Trials</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-upi_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-upi-4">[4]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_propaganda" title="Soviet propaganda">Soviet propaganda</a> also maintained that there was no occupation and that all three Baltic states voluntarily joined the Union – the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Parliament" title="People's Parliament">People's Parliaments</a> expressed people's will when they petitioned the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Soviet_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Supreme Soviet of the Soviet Union">Supreme Soviet of the Soviet Union</a> to be admitted into the Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conr_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-conr-5">[5]</a></sup> The Baltic states claimed that they were forcefully and illegally incorporated into the Soviet Union. Popular opinion was that the secret protocols proved that the occupation was illegal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Such an interpretation of the Pact had major implications in the Baltic public policy. If Baltic diplomats could link the Pact and the occupation, they could claim that the Soviet rule in the republics had no legal basis and therefore all Soviet laws were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Null_and_void" title="Null and void">null and void</a> since 1940.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dej_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-dej-7">[7]</a></sup> Such a position would automatically terminate the debate over reforming Baltic sovereignty or establishing autonomy within the Soviet Union – the states never <i><a href="/wiki/De_jure" title="De jure">de jure</a></i> belonged to the union in the first place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This would open the possibility of restoring legal continuity of the independent states that existed in the interwar period. Claiming all Soviet laws had no legal power in the Baltics would also cancel the need to follow the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Constitution of the Soviet Union">Constitution of the Soviet Union</a> and other formal secession procedures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In anticipation of the 50th anniversary of the Molotov–Ribbentrop Pact, tensions were rising between the Baltics and Moscow. Lithuanian <a href="/wiki/Romualdas_Ozolas" title="Romualdas Ozolas">Romualdas Ozolas</a> initiated a collection of 2 million signatures demanding withdrawal of the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a> from Lithuania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Lithuania" title="Communist Party of Lithuania">Communist Party of Lithuania</a> was deliberating the possibility of splitting off from the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Communist Party of the Soviet Union</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> On 8 August, 1989, Estonians attempted to amend election laws to limit voting rights of new immigrants (mostly Russian workers).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hyst_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-hyst-12">[12]</a></sup> This provoked mass strikes and protests of Russian workers. Moscow gained an opportunity to present the events as an "inter-ethnic conflict"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> – it could then position itself as "peacemaker" restoring order in a troubled republic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The rising tensions in anticipation of the protest spurred hopes that Moscow would react by announcing constructive reforms to address the demands of the Baltic people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> At the same time fears grew of violent clampdown. <a href="/wiki/Erich_Honecker" title="Erich Honecker">Erich Honecker</a> from <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicolae_Ceau%C5%9Fescu" title="Nicolae Ceauşescu">Nicolae Ceauşescu</a> from <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> offered the Soviet Union military assistance in case it decided to use force and break up the demonstration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soviet_response">Soviet response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Way&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Soviet response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 15 August, official daily <i><a href="/wiki/Pravda" title="Pravda">Pravda</a></i>, in response to worker strikes in Estonia, published sharp criticism of "hysteria" driven by "extremist elements" pursuing selfish "narrow nationalist positions" against the greater benefit of the entire Soviet Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hyst_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-hyst-12">[12]</a></sup> On 17 August, the <a href="/wiki/Central_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Central Committee of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Central Committee of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union</a> published a project of new policy regarding the union republics in <i>Pravda</i>. However, this project offered few new ideas: it preserved Moscow's leadership not only in foreign policy and defense, but also in economy, science, and culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-i334_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-i334-17">[17]</a></sup> The project made few cautious concessions: it proposed the republics the right to challenge national laws in a court (at the time all three Baltic states had amended their constitutions giving their <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Soviet" title="Supreme Soviet">Supreme Soviets</a> the right to veto national laws)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hc_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-hc-18">[18]</a></sup> and the right to promote their national languages to the level of the official state language (at the same time the project emphasised the leading role of the Russian language).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-i334_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-i334-17">[17]</a></sup> The project also included law banning "nationalist and chauvinist organisations," which could be used to persecute pro-independence groups in the Baltics,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hc_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-hc-18">[18]</a></sup> and a proposal to replace the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_on_the_Creation_of_the_USSR" title="Treaty on the Creation of the USSR">Treaty on the Creation of the USSR</a> of 1922 with a new unifying agreement, which would be part of the Soviet constitution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-i334_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-i334-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 18 August, <i>Pravda</i> published an extensive interview with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Nikolaevich_Yakovlev" title="Alexander Nikolaevich Yakovlev">Alexander Nikolaevich Yakovlev</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-var_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-var-19">[19]</a></sup> chairman of a 26-member commission set up by the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_People%27s_Deputies_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Congress of People's Deputies of the Soviet Union">Congress of People's Deputies</a> to investigate the <a href="/wiki/Molotov%E2%80%93Ribbentrop_Pact" title="Molotov–Ribbentrop Pact">Molotov–Ribbentrop Pact</a> and its secret protocols.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-upi_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-upi-4">[4]</a></sup> During the interview, Yakovlev admitted that the secret protocols were genuine. He condemned the protocols, but maintained that they had no impact on the incorporation of the Baltic states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Thus Moscow reversed its long-standing position that the secret protocols did not exist or were forgeries, but did not concede that events of 1940 constituted an occupation. It was clearly not enough to satisfy the Baltics and on 22 August, a commission of the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Soviet_of_the_Lithuanian_SSR" title="Supreme Soviet of the Lithuanian SSR">Supreme Soviet of the Lithuanian SSR</a> announced that the occupation in 1940 was a direct result of the Molotov–Ribbentrop Pact and therefore illegal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> It was the first time that an official Soviet body challenged the legitimacy of the Soviet rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sanf_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-sanf-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protest">Protest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Way&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Protest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Way&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1989_08_23%C5%A0iauliai1Baltijos_kelias.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1464" data-file-width="2176" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/1989_08_23%C5%A0iauliai1Baltijos_kelias.jpg/220px-1989_08_23%C5%A0iauliai1Baltijos_kelias.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/1989_08_23%C5%A0iauliai1Baltijos_kelias.jpg/330px-1989_08_23%C5%A0iauliai1Baltijos_kelias.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/1989_08_23%C5%A0iauliai1Baltijos_kelias.jpg/440px-1989_08_23%C5%A0iauliai1Baltijos_kelias.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In the light of <i><a href="/wiki/Glasnost" title="Glasnost">glasnost</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Perestroika" title="Perestroika">perestroika</a></i>, street demonstrations had been increasingly growing in popularity and support. On 23 August, 1986, Black Ribbon Day demonstrations were held in 21 western cities including <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perth,_Australia" title="Perth, Australia">Perth</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington,_DC" title="Washington, DC">Washington, DC</a> to bring worldwide attention to human rights violations by the Soviet Union. In 1987, Black Ribbon Day protests were held in 36 cities including Vilnius, Lithuania. Protests against the Molotov Ribbentrop Pact were also held in Tallinn and Riga in 1987. In 1988, for the first time, such protests were sanctioned by the Soviet authorities and did not end in arrests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dej_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-dej-7">[7]</a></sup> The activists planned an especially large protest for the 50th anniversary of the Molotov–Ribbentrop Pact in 1989. It is unclear when and by whom the idea of a human chain was advanced. It appears that the idea was proposed during a trilateral meeting in <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A4rnu" title="Pärnu">Pärnu</a> on 15 July.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> An official agreement between the Baltic activists was signed in <a href="/wiki/C%C4%93sis" title="Cēsis">Cēsis</a> on 12 August.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Local Communist Party authorities approved the protest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dobbs_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-dobbs-26">[26]</a></sup> At the same time several different petitions, denouncing Soviet occupation, were gathering hundreds of thousands of signatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imse27_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-imse27-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The organisers mapped out the chain, designating specific locations to specific cities, towns, and villages to make sure that the chain would be uninterrupted. Free bus rides were provided for those who did not have other transportation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a100_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-a100-28">[28]</a></sup> Preparations spread across the country, energising the previously uninvolved rural population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a78_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-a78-29">[29]</a></sup> Some employers did not allow workers to take the day off from work (23 August fell on a Wednesday), while others sponsored the bus rides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a100_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-a100-28">[28]</a></sup> On the day of the event, special radio broadcasts helped to coordinate the effort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dobbs_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-dobbs-26">[26]</a></sup> Estonia declared a public holiday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lod2m_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-lod2m-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Baltic pro-independence movements issued a joint declaration to the world and European community in the name of the protest. The declaration condemned the Molotov–Ribbentrop Pact, calling it a criminal act, and urged declaration that the pact was "null and void from the moment of signing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fax_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-fax-31">[31]</a></sup> The declaration said that the question of the Baltics was a "problem of inalienable <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a>" and accused the European community of "double standards" and turning a blind eye to the "last colonies of Hitler–Stalin era."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fax_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-fax-31">[31]</a></sup> On the day of the protest, <i><a href="/wiki/Pravda" title="Pravda">Pravda</a></i> published an editorial titled "Only the Facts." It was a collection of quotes from pro-independence activists intended to show the unacceptable anti-Soviet nature of their work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s67_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-s67-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_chain">Human chain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Way&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Human chain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Balti_kett_K%C3%A4ru.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="770" data-file-width="1030" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Balti_kett_K%C3%A4ru.jpg/220px-Balti_kett_K%C3%A4ru.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Balti_kett_K%C3%A4ru.jpg/330px-Balti_kett_K%C3%A4ru.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Balti_kett_K%C3%A4ru.jpg/440px-Balti_kett_K%C3%A4ru.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Balti_kett_K%C3%A4ru.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Monument to the Baltic Way in Estonia</div>

<p>The chain connected the three Baltic capitals – <a href="/wiki/Vilnius" title="Vilnius">Vilnius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Riga" title="Riga">Riga</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tallinn" title="Tallinn">Tallinn</a>. It ran from Vilnius along the <a href="/wiki/A2_highway_(Lithuania)" title="A2 highway (Lithuania)">A2 highway</a> through <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0irvintos" title="Širvintos">Širvintos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukmerg%C4%97" title="Ukmergė">Ukmergė</a> to <a href="/wiki/Panev%C4%97%C5%BEys" title="Panevėžys">Panevėžys</a>, then along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Via_Baltica" title="Via Baltica">Via Baltica</a> through <a href="/wiki/Pasvalys" title="Pasvalys">Pasvalys</a> to <a href="/wiki/Bauska" title="Bauska">Bauska</a> in Latvia and through <a href="/wiki/Iecava" title="Iecava">Iecava</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C4%B6ekava" title="Ķekava">Ķekava</a> to <a href="/wiki/Riga" title="Riga">Riga</a> (<a href="/wiki/A7_road_(Latvia)" title="A7 road (Latvia)">Bauska highway</a>, Ziepniekkalna street, Mūkusalas street, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stone_Bridge_(Riga)" title="Stone Bridge (Riga)">Stone bridge</a>, Kaļķu street, Brīvības's street) and then along road <a href="/wiki/A2_road_(Latvia)" title="A2 road (Latvia)">A2</a>, through <a href="/wiki/Vanga%C5%BEi" title="Vangaži">Vangaži</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sigulda" title="Sigulda">Sigulda</a>, <a href="/wiki/L%C4%ABgatne" title="Līgatne">Līgatne</a>, Mūrnieki and <a href="/wiki/Drabe%C5%A1i_parish" title="Drabeši parish">Drabeši</a>, to <a href="/wiki/C%C4%93sis" title="Cēsis">Cēsis</a>, from there, through Lode, to <a href="/wiki/Valmiera" title="Valmiera">Valmiera</a> and then through <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J%C4%93%C4%8Di&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jēči (page does not exist)">Jēči</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lizd%C4%93ni&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lizdēni (page does not exist)">Lizdēni</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Renc%C4%93ni&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rencēni (page does not exist)">Rencēni</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://et.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renc%C4%93ni" title="et:Rencēni">et</a>)</span>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Oleri&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Oleri (page does not exist)">Oleri</a>, <a href="/wiki/R%C5%ABjiena" title="Rūjiena">Rūjiena</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=%C4%B6o%C5%86i&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ķoņi (page does not exist)">Ķoņi</a> to Estonian town <a href="/wiki/Karksi-Nuia" title="Karksi-Nuia">Karksi-Nuia</a> and from there through <a href="/wiki/Viljandi" title="Viljandi">Viljandi</a>, <a href="/wiki/T%C3%BCri" title="Türi">Türi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rapla" title="Rapla">Rapla</a> to Tallinn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The demonstrators peacefully linked hands for 15 minutes at 19:00 local time (16:00 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GMT" title="GMT">GMT</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conr_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-conr-5">[5]</a></sup> Later, a number of local gatherings and protests took place. In Vilnius, about 5,000 people gathered in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathedral_Square_in_Vilnius" title="Cathedral Square in Vilnius">Cathedral Square</a>, holding candles and singing national songs, including <i><a href="/wiki/Tauti%C5%A1ka_giesm%C4%97" title="Tautiška giesmė">Tautiška giesmė</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imse_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-imse-35">[35]</a></sup> Elsewhere, priests held masses or rang church bells. Leaders of the Estonian and Latvian Popular Fronts gathered on the border between their two republics for a symbolic funeral ceremony, in which a giant black cross was set alight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lod2m_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-lod2m-30">[30]</a></sup> The protesters held candles and pre-war national flags decorated with black ribbons in memory of the victims of the Soviet terror: <a href="/wiki/Forest_Brothers" title="Forest Brothers">Forest Brothers</a>, deportees to <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, political prisoners, and other "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enemies_of_the_people" title="Enemies of the people">enemies of the people</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sanf_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-sanf-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imse_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-imse-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Moscow's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pushkin_Square" title="Pushkin Square">Pushkin Square</a>, ranks of special riot police were employed when a few hundred people tried to stage a sympathy demonstration. <a href="/wiki/Telegraph_Agency_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Telegraph Agency of the Soviet Union">TASS</a> said 75 were detained for breaches of the peace, petty vandalism, and other offences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imse_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-imse-35">[35]</a></sup> About 13,000 demonstrated in the <a href="/wiki/Moldavian_Soviet_Socialist_Republic" title="Moldavian Soviet Socialist Republic">Moldavian Soviet Socialist Republic</a> which was also affected by the secret protocol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lod_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-lod-36">[36]</a></sup> A demonstration was held by the Baltic <a href="/wiki/%C3%89migr%C3%A9" title="Émigré">émigré</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a> sympathizers in front of the Soviet embassy in <a href="/wiki/Bonn" title="Bonn">Bonn</a>, then <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a>.</p>

<p>Most estimates of the number of participants vary between one and two million. <a href="/wiki/Reuters" title="Reuters">Reuters</a> News reported the following day that about 700,000 Estonians and 1,000,000 Lithuanians joined the protests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lod_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-lod-36">[36]</a></sup> The Latvian Popular Front estimated an attendance of 400,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Prior to the event, the organisers expected an attendance of 1,500,000 out of the about 8,000,000 inhabitants of the three states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imse_35-3"><a href="#cite_note-imse-35">[35]</a></sup> Such expectations predicted 25–30% turnout among the native population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a78_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-a78-29">[29]</a></sup> According to the official Soviet numbers, provided by TASS, there were 300,000 participants in Estonia and nearly 500,000 in Lithuania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imse_35-4"><a href="#cite_note-imse-35">[35]</a></sup> To make the chain physically possible, an attendance of approximately 200,000 people was required in each state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conr_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-conr-5">[5]</a></sup> Video footage taken from airplanes and helicopters showed an almost continuous line of people across the countryside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Way&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:40%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"Matters have gone far. There is a serious threat to the fate of the Baltic peoples. People should know the abyss into which they are being pushed by their nationalistic leaders. Should they achieve their goals, the possible consequences could be catastrophic to these nations. A question could arise as to their very existence."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">Declaration of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Central Committee of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Central Committee</a> on the situation in the Soviet Baltic republics, 26 August<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>On 26 August, 1989, a pronouncement from the Central Committee of the Communist Party was read during the opening 19 minutes of <i><a href="/wiki/Vremya" title="Vremya">Vremya</a></i>, the main evening news program on Soviet television.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> It was a sternly worded warning about growing "nationalist, extremist groups" which advanced "anti-socialist and anti-Soviet" agendas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rem_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-rem-42">[42]</a></sup> The announcement claimed that these groups discriminated against ethnic minorities and terrorised those still loyal to Soviet ideals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rem_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-rem-42">[42]</a></sup> Local authorities were openly criticised for their failure to stop these activists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s67_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-s67-32">[32]</a></sup> The Baltic Way was referred to as "nationalist hysteria." According to the pronouncement, such developments would lead to an "abyss" and "catastrophic" consequences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imse27_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-imse27-27">[27]</a></sup> The workers and peasants were called on to save the situation and defend Soviet ideals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s67_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-s67-32">[32]</a></sup> Overall, there were mixed messages: while indirectly threatening the use of force it also placed hopes that the conflict could be solved via diplomatic means. It was interpreted that the Central Committee had not yet decided which way to go and had left both possibilities open.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-i347_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-i347-43">[43]</a></sup> The call to pro-Soviet masses illustrated that Moscow believed it still had a significant audience in the Baltics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s67_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-s67-32">[32]</a></sup> Sharp criticism of Baltic Communist Parties was interpreted as signalling that Moscow would attempt to replace their leadership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-i347_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-i347-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>President of the United States <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">George H. W. Bush</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and chancellor of <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> <a href="/wiki/Helmut_Kohl" title="Helmut Kohl">Helmut Kohl</a> urged peaceful reforms and criticised the Molotov–Ribbentrop Pact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> On August 31, the Baltic activists issued a joint declaration to <a href="/wiki/Javier_P%C3%A9rez_de_Cu%C3%A9llar" title="Javier Pérez de Cuéllar">Javier Pérez de Cuéllar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Secretary-General_of_the_United_Nations" title="Secretary-General of the United Nations">Secretary-General</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> They claimed to be under threat of aggression and asked for an international commission to be sent to monitor the situation. Almost immediately after the broadcast the tone in Moscow began to soften<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> and the Soviet authorities failed to follow up on any of their threats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s69_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-s69-48">[48]</a></sup> Eventually, according to historian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfred_Erich_Senn" title="Alfred Erich Senn">Alfred Erich Senn</a>, the pronouncement became a source of embarrassment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s69_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-s69-48">[48]</a></sup> On 19-20 September, the Central Committee of the Communist Party convened to discuss the nationality question – something <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a> had been postponing since early 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The plenum did not specifically address the situation in the Baltic states and reaffirmed old principles regarding the centralised Soviet Union and the dominant role of the Russian language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> It did promise some increase in autonomy, but was contradictory and failed to address the underlying reasons for the conflict.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>

<p>The human chain helped to publicise the Baltic cause around the world and symbolised solidarity among the Baltic peoples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The positive image of the non-violent <a href="/wiki/Singing_Revolution" title="Singing Revolution">Singing Revolution</a> spread among the western media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The activists, including <a href="/wiki/Vytautas_Landsbergis" title="Vytautas Landsbergis">Vytautas Landsbergis</a>, used the increased exposure to position the debate over Baltic independence as a moral, and not just political question: reclaiming independence would be restoration of historical justice and liquidation of Stalinism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> It was an emotional event, strengthening the determination to seek independence. The protest highlighted that the pro-independence movements, established just a year before, became more assertive and radical: they shifted from demanding greater freedom from Moscow to full independence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 1989, the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_People%27s_Deputies_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Congress of People's Deputies of the Soviet Union">Congress of People's Deputies</a> accepted and <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a> signed the report by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Nikolaevich_Yakovlev" title="Alexander Nikolaevich Yakovlev">Yakovlev's</a> commission condemning the secret protocols of the Molotov–Ribbentrop Pact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> In February 1990, the first free democratic elections to the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Soviet" title="Supreme Soviet">Supreme Soviets</a> took place in all three Baltic states and pro-independence candidates won majorities. On 11 March, 1990, within seven months of the Baltic Way, Lithuania became the first Soviet state to declare independence. The independence of all three Baltic states was recognised by most western countries by the end of 1991.</p>
<p>This protest was one of the earliest and longest unbroken <a href="/wiki/Human_chain" title="Human chain">human chains</a> in history. Similar human chains were later organised in many East European countries and regions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR" title="USSR">USSR</a> and, more recently, in <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/228_Hand-in-Hand_Rally" title="228 Hand-in-Hand Rally">228 Hand-in-Hand Rally</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">Catalonia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Catalan_Way" title="Catalan Way">Catalan Way</a>). Documents recording the Baltic Way were added to <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Memory_of_the_World_Register" title="Memory of the World Register">Memory of the World Register</a> in 2009 in recognition of their value in documenting history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mow_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-mow-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
