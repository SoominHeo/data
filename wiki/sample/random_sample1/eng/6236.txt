<p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Whyte_notation" title="Whyte notation">Whyte notation</a> for the classification of <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive" title="Steam locomotive">steam locomotives</a>, <b><span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span></b> represents the <a href="/wiki/Wheel_arrangement" title="Wheel arrangement">wheel arrangement</a> of four <a href="/wiki/Leading_wheel" title="Leading wheel">leading wheels</a>, six powered and coupled <a href="/wiki/Driving_wheel" title="Driving wheel">driving wheels</a> and four <a href="/wiki/Trailing_wheel" title="Trailing wheel">trailing wheels</a>. In France, where the type was first used, it is known as the <b>Baltic</b>, while it became known as the <b>Hudson</b> in most of North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steam_glossary_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-steam_glossary-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-6-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tender_locomotives">Tender locomotives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-6-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tender locomotives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> <a href="/wiki/Tender_(rail)" title="Tender (rail)">tender locomotive</a> was first introduced in 1911 and throughout the 1920s to 1940s, the wheel arrangement was widely used in North America and to a lesser extent in the rest of the world. The type combined the basic design principles of the <a href="/wiki/4-6-2" title="4-6-2"><span class="nowrap">4-6-2</span> Pacific</a> type with an improved <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">boiler</a> and larger <a href="/wiki/Firebox_(steam_engine)" title="Firebox (steam engine)">firebox</a> that necessitated additional support at the rear of the locomotive. In general, the available <a href="/wiki/Tractive_effort" title="Tractive effort">tractive effort</a> differed little from that of the Pacific, but the steam-raising ability was increased, giving more power at speed. The <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> was best suited to high-speed running across flat terrain. Since the type had fewer <a href="/wiki/Driving_wheel" title="Driving wheel">driving wheels</a> than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrying_wheels" title="Carrying wheels">carrying wheels</a>, a smaller percentage of the locomotive's weight contributed to traction, compared to other types. Like the Pacific, it was well suited for high speed passenger trains, but not for starting heavy freight trains and slogging on long sustained grades, where more pairs of driving wheels are better.</p>
<p>The first <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> tender locomotive in the world was a four-cylinder <a href="/wiki/Compound_locomotive" title="Compound locomotive">compound locomotive</a>, designed by <a href="/wiki/Gaston_du_Bousquet" title="Gaston du Bousquet">Gaston du Bousquet</a> for the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemin_de_Fer_du_Nord" title="Chemin de Fer du Nord">Chemin de Fer du Nord</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> in 1911. Since it was designed for the <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>-<a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a> express, it was named the Baltic after the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>, which was a logical extension of the naming convention that started with the <a href="/wiki/4-4-2_(locomotive)" title="4-4-2 (locomotive)"><span class="nowrap">4-4-2</span> Atlantic</a> and <span class="nowrap">4-6-2</span> Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reed_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reed-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> in the United States of America, the J-1 of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Central_Railroad" title="New York Central Railroad">New York Central Railroad</a>, was built in 1927 to the railroad’s design by the <a href="/wiki/American_Locomotive_Company" title="American Locomotive Company">American Locomotive Company</a> (ALCO). There, the type was named the <a href="/wiki/NYC_Hudson" title="NYC Hudson">Hudson</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hudson_Type_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hudson_Type-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The world speed record for steam locomotives was held by a <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> at least twice. In 1934, the <a href="/wiki/Chicago,_Milwaukee,_St._Paul_and_Pacific_Railroad" title="Chicago, Milwaukee, St. Paul and Pacific Railroad">Milwaukee Road’s</a> <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Road_class_F6" title="Milwaukee Road class F6">class F6</a> no. 6402 reached 103.5 miles per hour (166.6 kilometres per hour) and, in 1936, the German class 05.002 reached 124.5 miles per hour (200.4 kilometres per hour). That record was broken by the British <span class="nowrap">4-6-2</span> Pacific <a href="/wiki/LNER_Class_A4_4468_Mallard" title="LNER Class A4 4468 Mallard">no. 4468 <i>Mallard</i></a> on 3 July 1938, when it reached 126 miles per hour (203 kilometres per hour), still the world speed record for steam traction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DB_Museum_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-DB_Museum-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tank_locomotives">Tank locomotives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-6-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tank locomotives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <span class="nowrap">4-6-4T</span> was also a fairly common <a href="/wiki/Wheel_arrangement" title="Wheel arrangement">wheel arrangement</a> for passenger <a href="/wiki/Tank_locomotive" title="Tank locomotive">tank locomotives</a>. As such, it was essentially the tank locomotive equivalent of a <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/4-6-0" title="4-6-0">4-6-0</a></span> tender locomotive, with water tanks and a coal bunker supported by four trailing wheels instead of in a tender. In New Zealand, all 4-6-4T locomotives were tank versions of <a href="/wiki/4-6-2" title="4-6-2">4-6-2</a> locomotives.</p>
<p>The first known <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> tank locomotive was rebuilt from a <a href="/wiki/Natal_Government_Railways" title="Natal Government Railways">Natal Government Railways</a> (NGR) <a href="/wiki/South_African_Class_C_4-6-0T" title="South African Class C 4-6-0T">K&amp;S Class</a> <a href="/wiki/4-6-0" title="4-6-0"><span class="nowrap">4-6-0T</span></a> which was modified in 1896 to enable it to run equally well in either direction on the <a href="/wiki/Colony_of_Natal" title="Colony of Natal">Natal</a> South Coast line, where no turning facilities were available at the time. This sole locomotive later became the <a href="/wiki/South_African_Class_C2_4-6-4T" title="South African Class C2 4-6-4T">Class C2</a> on the South African Railways (SAR). The first known locomotive class to be designed with a <span class="nowrap">4-6-4T</span> wheel arrangement, the NGR’s <a href="/wiki/South_African_Class_E_4-6-4T" title="South African Class E 4-6-4T">Class F</a> tank locomotive, was based on this modified locomotive and built by <a href="/wiki/Neilson_and_Company#Turn_of_the_20th_century" title="Neilson and Company">Neilson, Reid and Company</a> in 1902. These became the Class E on the SAR in 1912.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holland_1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holland_1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paxton-Bourne_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paxton-Bourne-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-6-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-6-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt>Tender locomotives</dt>
</dl>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Victorian_Railways_R_701.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="320" data-file-width="742" height="95" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Victorian_Railways_R_701.jpg/220px-Victorian_Railways_R_701.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Victorian_Railways_R_701.jpg/330px-Victorian_Railways_R_701.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Victorian_Railways_R_701.jpg/440px-Victorian_Railways_R_701.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Victorian_Railways_R_701.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Victorian Railways R class</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Seventy <a href="/wiki/Victorian_Railways_R_class" title="Victorian Railways R class">R class</a> <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> Hudson tender locomotives, the only class of this configuration in Australia and built by <a href="/wiki/North_British_Locomotive_Company" title="North British Locomotive Company">North British Locomotive Company</a>, were introduced by the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_Railways" title="Victorian Railways">Victorian Railways</a> in 1951 for mainline express passenger operations. However, the introduction in 1952 of the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_Railways_B_class_(diesel)" title="Victorian Railways B class (diesel)">B class diesel-electric</a> locomotives saw the R class almost immediately being relegated to secondary passenger and freight use, with many being staged at depots around the state. A number were preserved and some of these continued to operate on special excursion trains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:R711WCR.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="592" data-file-width="896" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/R711WCR.jpg/220px-R711WCR.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/R711WCR.jpg/330px-R711WCR.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/R711WCR.jpg/440px-R711WCR.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:R711WCR.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/West_Coast_Railway_(Victoria)" title="West Coast Railway (Victoria)">West Coast Railway</a> R class 711</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>With the privatisation of regional passenger operations in Victoria in the mid-1990s, two R class locomotives were brought back into normal revenue service by the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_Railway_(Victoria)" title="West Coast Railway (Victoria)">West Coast Railway</a>, for regularly scheduled mainline passenger trains between <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warrnambool" title="Warrnambool">Warrnambool</a>. The locomotives underwent a number of modifications to allow for reliable high speed operation, including dual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lempor" title="Lempor">Lempor</a> exhausts, oil firing and the addition of a diesel control stand for multiple unit operation. The use of these R class locomotives on the <a href="/wiki/Warrnambool_V/Line_rail_service" title="Warrnambool V/Line rail service">Warrnambool line</a> did not continue after the demise of the private operator in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Tank locomotives</dt>
</dl>
<p>The <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> tank locomotive configuration was a popular type with the <a href="/wiki/Western_Australian_Government_Railways" title="Western Australian Government Railways">Western Australian Government Railways</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WAGR_D_Class" title="WAGR D Class">D class</a> was introduced for suburban passenger service in 1912. Its successors, both also of the <span class="nowrap">4-6-4T</span> wheel arrangement, were the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WAGR_Dm_Class" title="WAGR Dm Class">Dm class</a> of 1945 that was rebuilt from older <a href="/wiki/WAGR_E_class" title="WAGR E class">E class</a> <span class="nowrap">4-6-2</span> Pacific tender locomotives, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WAGR_Dd_Class" title="WAGR Dd Class">Dd class</a> of 1946.</p>
<p>The N.S.W. Government Railways 30 Class 4-6-4T locomotives were used on Sydney and Newcastle suburban passenger train workings. No. 3046 is preserved at the Dorrigo Steam Railway and Museum on the N.S.W. north coast.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-6-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt>Tender locomotives</dt>
</dl>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jss1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="693" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Jss1.jpg/220px-Jss1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Jss1.jpg/330px-Jss1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Jss1.jpg/440px-Jss1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jss1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Canadian Pacific Railway no. 2816 at Steamtown in Vermont</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The second-largest user of the <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> type in North America was the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Pacific_Railway" title="Canadian Pacific Railway">Canadian Pacific Railway</a> with 65 H1a to H1e class locomotives, numbered 2800 to 2864 and built by <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Locomotive_Works" title="Montreal Locomotive Works">Montreal Locomotive Works</a> (MLW) between 1929 and 1940. They were highly successful and improved service and journey times on the CPR's transcontinental routes. The third and later batches of CPR Hudsons, H1c to H1e numbers 2820 to 2864, were dubbed <i><a href="/wiki/Royal_Hudson" title="Royal Hudson">Royal Hudsons</a></i> and were semi-streamlined. Royal permission was given for these locomotives to bear the royal crown and arms after locomotive no. 2850 hauled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_VI_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George VI of the United Kingdom">King George VI</a> across Canada in 1939.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hudson_Type_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hudson_Type-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Five CPR Hudsons survived. <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Pacific_2816" title="Canadian Pacific 2816">H1b class no. 2816 <i>Empress</i></a> is the sole remaining unstreamlined CPR Hudson. It was repatriated from static display at Steamtown in Scranton, Pennsylvania, to the CPR in 1998 and was restored and converted to oil-burning to haul excursions for CPR. The other remaining H1 class locomotives are all <i>Royal Hudsons</i>. As of 2008, three were on display in museums, no. 2839 in California, no. 2850 in Quebec and no. 2858 in Ontario, while no. 2860, the first oil-burning <i>Royal Hudson</i> of the class, was operational and based in British Columbia. By 2008, the CPR Hudsons were the only operational Hudsons in North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> (Also see <a href="/wiki/4-6-4#North_American_production_list" title="4-6-4">North American production list</a>)</p>
<dl>
<dt>Tank locomotives</dt>
</dl>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Grand_Trunk_Railway" title="Grand Trunk Railway">Grand Trunk Railway</a> (GTR) had six K2 class <span class="nowrap">4-6-4T</span> locomotives, built in September 1914 by MLW and acquired for suburban service. Numbered 1540 to 1545 on the GTR, they were reclassified as X-10-a and renumbered 45 to 50 after being absorbed by the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_National_Railway" title="Canadian National Railway">Canadian National Railway</a> (CN) in 1923. Three of them are preserved, numbers GT 1541 (CN 46) and <a href="/wiki/Canadian_National_47" title="Canadian National 47"><span class="nowrap">GT 1542</span> (CN 47)</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Steamtown_National_Historic_Site" title="Steamtown National Historic Site">Steamtown National Historic Site</a> in <a href="/wiki/Scranton,_Pennsylvania" title="Scranton, Pennsylvania">Scranton, Pennsylvania</a>, and GT 1544 (CN 49) at the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Railway_Museum" title="Canadian Railway Museum">Canadian Railway Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Delson,_Quebec" title="Delson, Quebec">Delson, Quebec</a>. (Also see <a href="/wiki/4-6-4#North_American_production_list" title="4-6-4">North American production list</a>)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finland">Finland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-6-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Finland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pr2_1800_Haapam%C3%A4ki_02.08.11_(3).JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2170" data-file-width="3350" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Pr2_1800_Haapam%C3%A4ki_02.08.11_%283%29.JPG/220px-Pr2_1800_Haapam%C3%A4ki_02.08.11_%283%29.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Pr2_1800_Haapam%C3%A4ki_02.08.11_%283%29.JPG/330px-Pr2_1800_Haapam%C3%A4ki_02.08.11_%283%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Pr2_1800_Haapam%C3%A4ki_02.08.11_%283%29.JPG/440px-Pr2_1800_Haapam%C3%A4ki_02.08.11_%283%29.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pr2_1800_Haapam%C3%A4ki_02.08.11_(3).JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Finnish VR Class Pr2 no. 1800 at <a href="/wiki/Haapam%C3%A4ki" title="Haapamäki">Haapamäki</a> in <a href="/wiki/Keuruu" title="Keuruu">Keuruu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Finnish State Railways <a href="/wiki/VR_Class_Pr2" title="VR Class Pr2">Class Pr2</a>, nicknamed <i>Henschel</i>, was a <a href="/wiki/5_ft_and_1520_mm_gauge_railways" title="5 ft and 1520 mm gauge railways"><span class="nowrap">5 ft</span></a> (<span class="nowrap">1,524 mm</span>) gauge passenger tank locomotive class, ordered from <a href="/wiki/Henschel_%26_Son" title="Henschel &amp; Son">Henschel &amp; Son</a> by the Estonian State Railways in the spring of 1939 and completed in 1941. The outbreak of the Second World War prevented their delivery to <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>, but a few of these engines did operate in <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a> in 1942. They became superfluous when the Germans began converting the Baltic tracks to <span class="nowrap">4 ft <span class="frac">8<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> in</span> (<span class="nowrap">1,435 mm</span>) <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Standard_gauge" title="Standard gauge">standard gauge</a></span>, and the four locomotives were sold to <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>. They were classified Pr2 and numbered 1800 to 1803 upon their arrival in Finland in December 1942.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Class Pr2 tanks were quite advanced locomotives and were based on the Henschel-built <a href="/wiki/DRG_Class_62" title="DRG Class 62">DRG Class 62</a> tank engine design of 1928 for the <i><a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Reichsbahn#Deutsche_Reichsbahn-Gesellschaft_.28DRG.29" title="Deutsche Reichsbahn">Deutsche Reichsbahn</a></i>. After their initial teething problems were solved, they proved to be fast runners and an ideal addition to the motive power stable. They were originally built as oil-burners and reverted to this type of fuel between 1947 and 1954, when oil prices were low. With its 1,830 millimetres (72 inches) coupled wheels, it was very fast and one of them achieved 144 kilometres per hour (89 miles per hour) during a test run. No. 1803, the last Class Pr2 in service, was withdrawn in May 1960. Only no. 1800 has been preserved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-6-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The four-cylinder <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> <a href="/wiki/Compound_locomotive" title="Compound locomotive">compound locomotive</a> designed by <a href="/wiki/Gaston_du_Bousquet" title="Gaston du Bousquet">Gaston du Bousquet</a> for the French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemin_de_Fer_du_Nord" title="Chemin de Fer du Nord">Chemin de Fer du Nord</a>, of which two were built at the company's workshops in 1911, was the first tender locomotive in the world with this wheel arrangement. Named the <i>Baltic</i> since it was intended for service on the <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>-<a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a> express, its most remarkable feature was the <i>en echelon</i> arrangement of the two low-pressure inside cylinders in order to accommodate the very large bore. One of them was built with a water-tube firebox. Although they were not multiplied, they were the forerunners of the highly successful <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/4-6-2" title="4-6-2">4-6-2</a></span> Nord Pacifics and Super-Pacifics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reed_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reed-2">[2]</a></sup> One survives in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_fran%C3%A7ais_du_chemin_de_fer" title="Musée français du chemin de fer">Musée français du chemin de fer</a></i> (French national railway museum) at Mulhouse in eastern France, cut up in sectioned form to display its interior during the World Exhibition in Paris in 1937. Its tender was not preserved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reed_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Reed-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:232-U1-SNCF.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1191" data-file-width="1722" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/232-U1-SNCF.JPG/220px-232-U1-SNCF.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/232-U1-SNCF.JPG/330px-232-U1-SNCF.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/232-U1-SNCF.JPG/440px-232-U1-SNCF.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:232-U1-SNCF.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
SNCF 232.U.1 class in the French national railway museum</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>France also produced some of the last Baltic locomotives. In 1938, Marc de Caso, the last Chief Mechanical Engineer of the Nord, originated the construction of eight Baltic locomotives, all delivered to the newly established <i>Société Nationale des Chemins de fer Français</i> (French National Railway Corporation or <a href="/wiki/SNCF" title="SNCF">SNCF</a>). Of these eight, three were 232.R class three-cylinder simple expansion (simplex) locomotives with rotary cam poppet valve gear, while four were 232.S class four-cylinder compound locomotives, initially also with poppet valve gear that was later replaced by Walschaerts valve gear driving oscillating cams. Built for comparative purposes, it was found that the compounds outperformed the simples.</p>
<p>The eighth of the class, the final French Baltic type, was completed in 1949 as the 232.U.1 class. This was another four-cylinder compound with Walschaerts valve gear, but with very large and light piston valves, that proved capable of more than 4,000 indicated horsepower (3,000 kilowatts). This locomotive is also preserved at <a href="/wiki/Mulhouse" title="Mulhouse">Mulhouse</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> (Also see <a href="/wiki/4-6-4#Netherlands" title="4-6-4">Netherlands</a>)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-6-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt>Tender locomotives</dt>
</dl>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:05001_Nuernberg_2006-08.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/05001_Nuernberg_2006-08.jpg/220px-05001_Nuernberg_2006-08.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/05001_Nuernberg_2006-08.jpg/330px-05001_Nuernberg_2006-08.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/05001_Nuernberg_2006-08.jpg/440px-05001_Nuernberg_2006-08.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:05001_Nuernberg_2006-08.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
05.001 in the Nuremberg Transport Museum</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Three <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> tender locomotives were built for the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Reichsbahn#Deutsche_Reichsbahn-Gesellschaft" title="Deutsche Reichsbahn">Deutsche Reichsbahn</a> (DRG) by <a href="/wiki/August_Borsig" title="August Borsig">Borsig</a> in 1935. Designated <a href="/wiki/DRG_Class_05" title="DRG Class 05">Class 05</a>, they were designed for high speed running. They were three-cylinder locomotives with giant <span class="frac nowrap">90<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> inches (2,299 millimetres) driving wheels and powerful clasp brakes on all wheels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DB_Museum_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-DB_Museum-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first two locomotives were conventional locomotives, but the third was built as a <a href="/wiki/Cab_forward" title="Cab forward">cab forward</a> and burned pulverised coal. All three were built streamlined, in shrouds that covered the locomotives almost to the railhead. On 11 May 1936, the 05.002 set a world speed record of 124.5 miles per hour (200.4 kilometres per hour) that was bettered by the British <span class="nowrap">4-6-2</span> Pacific <i>Mallard</i> two years later, on 3 July 1938.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DB_Museum_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-DB_Museum-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>All three survived the Second World War and were rebuilt as conventional non-streamlined locomotives in 1950, with new boilers. They worked in this form until 1957, when <a href="/wiki/Electric_locomotive" title="Electric locomotive">electric locomotives</a> took over on the high-speed routes. The first locomotive, 05.001, was restored to its original streamlined configuration in 1961, for display in the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Transport_Museum" title="Nuremberg Transport Museum">Nuremberg Transport Museum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DB_Museum_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-DB_Museum-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Tank locomotives</dt>
</dl>
<p>A number of German <span class="nowrap">4-6-4T</span> locomotive classes were built, the best known being the <a href="/wiki/Prussian_T_18" title="Prussian T 18">Prussian T 18</a> class of 1912. Altogether 534 of them were built by the <a href="/wiki/AG_Vulcan_Stettin" title="AG Vulcan Stettin">Stettiner Maschinenbau AG Vulcan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henschel_%26_Son" title="Henschel &amp; Son">Henschel &amp; Son</a> between 1912 and 1927. Of these, 458 went to the <a href="/wiki/Prussian_state_railways" title="Prussian state railways">Prussian state railways</a> and subsequently the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Reichsbahn" title="Deutsche Reichsbahn">Deutsche Reichsbahn</a>, where they became the DRG Class 78.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-6-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There were two classes of <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> tender locomotives in India, both early in the history of the wheel arrangement and also of unusually narrow gauge. The nine G class locomotives of the <a href="/wiki/2_ft_6_in_gauge_railways" title="2 ft 6 in gauge railways"><span class="nowrap">2 ft 6 in</span></a> (<span class="nowrap">762 mm</span>) gauge <a href="/wiki/Barsi_Light_Railway" title="Barsi Light Railway">Barsi Light Railway</a> in western India were built by <a href="/wiki/Nasmyth,_Gaskell_and_Company" title="Nasmyth, Gaskell and Company">Nasmyth, Wilson and Company</a> in 1928 and 1930 and by <a href="/wiki/W._G._Bagnall" title="W. G. Bagnall">W. G. Bagnall</a> in 1939. The four ND class locomotives of the <a href="/wiki/2_ft_and_600_mm_gauge_railways" title="2 ft and 600 mm gauge railways"><span class="nowrap">2 ft</span></a> (<span class="nowrap">610 mm</span>) gauge Scindia State Railway in <a href="/wiki/Gwalior" title="Gwalior">Gwalior</a> were built in 1928 by <a href="/wiki/Kerr,_Stuart_and_Company" title="Kerr, Stuart and Company">Kerr, Stuart and Company</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indonesia">Indonesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-6-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Indonesia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesian</a> C27 class locomotive of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PT_Kereta_Api" title="PT Kereta Api">Java Staatsspoorwegen</a></i> was introduced to <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> colonial administration. The class was designed to meet the requirement to haul trains of 400 tons at a speed of 50 kilometres per hour (31 miles per hour) on a <span class="nowrap">0.5%</span> incline with 180 metres (590 feet) radius curves. The locomotives also had to be able to negotiate curves with a sharp radius of 120 metres (390 feet) at a speed of 80 kilometres per hour (50 miles per hour). Between 1916 and 1922, altogether 39 locomotives were ordered from three manufacturers, <a href="/wiki/Stork_B.V." title="Stork B.V.">Werkspoor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armstrong_Whitworth" title="Armstrong Whitworth">Armstrong Whitworth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Locomotive_and_Machine_Works" title="Swiss Locomotive and Machine Works">Swiss Locomotive and Machine Works</a>. The C27 class was used on railway lines around <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bandung" title="Bandung">Bandung</a> and <a href="/wiki/Surabaya" title="Surabaya">Surabaya</a> until electrification was carried out around Jakarta between 1925 and 1930. They were then relocated to secondary lines like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merak,_Banten" title="Merak, Banten">Merak</a>-<a href="/wiki/Tanah_Abang" title="Tanah Abang">Tanah Abang</a> in <a href="/wiki/Banten" title="Banten">Banten</a> province, the lines around Kertosono and <a href="/wiki/Blitar" title="Blitar">Blitar</a> and also between <a href="/wiki/Purwokerto" title="Purwokerto">Purwokerto</a>, Kutoarjo and <a href="/wiki/Purworejo_Regency" title="Purworejo Regency">Purworejo</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>From 1921, 58 C28 class express passenger <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> tank locomotives were built for the <i>Java Staatsspoorwegen</i> by three German manufacturers, <a href="/wiki/Henschel_%26_Son" title="Henschel &amp; Son">Henschel &amp; Son</a>, <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A4chsische_Maschinenfabrik" title="Sächsische Maschinenfabrik">Sächsische Maschinenfabrik</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maschinenfabrik_Esslingen" title="Maschinenfabrik Esslingen">Maschinenfabrik Esslingen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henschel_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Henschel-18">[18]</a></sup> The C28 class was one of the most popular in <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, achieving speeds of up to 95 kilometres per hour (59 miles per hour). It was declared the world's fastest steam locomotive on <a href="/wiki/3_ft_6_in_gauge_railways" title="3 ft 6 in gauge railways"><span class="nowrap">3 ft 6 in</span></a> (<span class="nowrap">1,067 mm</span>) gauge when it achieved 110 kilometres per hour (68 miles per hour). In addition, being a tank locomotive, it was able to travel at full speed in both directions.</p>
<p>It was used on the Jakarta-<a href="/wiki/Bandung" title="Bandung">Bandung</a>, Jakarta-<a href="/wiki/Surabaya" title="Surabaya">Surabaya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malang" title="Malang">Malang</a>-Surabaya routes. During the Dutch colonial era, the term <i>Vlugge Vier</i> (Fast Four) was used on the Jakarta-<a href="/wiki/Bandung" title="Bandung">Bandung</a> route, where C28 class locomotives covered the distance of 175 kilometres (109 miles) four times a day at a speed of 65 kilometres per hour (40 miles per hour) with a travel time of about 2 hours 45 minutes. These express trains only stopped for one minute at <a href="/wiki/Karawang" title="Karawang">Karawang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cikampek" title="Cikampek">Cikampek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Purwakarta" title="Purwakarta">Purwakarta</a>. In addition, the C28 class locomotive was used on express trains like the <i>Java Nacht Express</i> (Java Night Express) and the <i>Eendaagsche Express</i> (One Day Express). A C28 class locomotive also hauled the train carrying <a href="/wiki/Sukarno" title="Sukarno">President Sukarno</a> and his entourage to <a href="/wiki/Yogyakarta" title="Yogyakarta">Yogyakarta</a> on 3 January 1948.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-6-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first and longest-lived Baltics in Ireland were two locomotives, built by <a href="/wiki/Nasmyth,_Gaskell_and_Company" title="Nasmyth, Gaskell and Company">Nasmyth, Wilson</a> in 1904 for the narrow-gauge <a href="/wiki/County_Donegal_Railways_Joint_Committee" title="County Donegal Railways Joint Committee">County Donegal Railways</a>. Both were later superheated and one lasted until 1967, albeit derelict.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-6-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Between 1947 and 1961, the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_National_Railways" title="Japanese National Railways">Japanese National Railways</a> built three classes of rather advanced American style <a href="/wiki/3_ft_6_in_gauge_railways" title="3 ft 6 in gauge railways"><span class="nowrap">3 ft 6 in</span></a> (<span class="nowrap">1,067 mm</span>) gauge Hudson tender locomotives.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:JNR-C61-2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="1200" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/JNR-C61-2.jpg/220px-JNR-C61-2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/JNR-C61-2.jpg/330px-JNR-C61-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/JNR-C61-2.jpg/440px-JNR-C61-2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:JNR-C61-2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Japanese Class C61.2 at the Umekoji Museum</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>Between 1947 and 1949, 33 <a href="/wiki/JNR_Class_C61" title="JNR Class C61">Class C61</a> locomotives were rebuilt from former <a href="/wiki/JNR_Class_D51" title="JNR Class D51">Class D51</a> <a href="/wiki/2-8-2" title="2-8-2"><span class="nowrap">2-8-2</span> Mikado</a> freight locomotives. The Class C61 was the first Japanese locomotive with the <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> Hudson wheel arrangement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inoue1999_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-inoue1999-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 1948 and 1949, 49 <a href="/wiki/JNR_Class_C62" title="JNR Class C62">Class C62</a> locomotives were built with new 4-6-4 frames and using the boilers of <a href="/wiki/JNR_Class_D52" title="JNR Class D52">Class D52</a> <span class="nowrap">2-8-2</span> Mikado locomotives. These were the largest and fastest steam passenger locomotives to run in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inoue1999_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-inoue1999-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>Between 1953 and 1961, 47 <a href="/wiki/JNR_Class_C60" title="JNR Class C60">Class C60</a> locomotives were rebuilt from surplus Class C59 <a href="/wiki/4-6-2" title="4-6-2"><span class="nowrap">4-6-2</span> Pacific</a> locomotives at the <a href="/wiki/Hamamatsu" title="Hamamatsu">Hamamatsu</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C5%8Driyama,_Fukushima" title="Kōriyama, Fukushima">Kōriyama</a> factories.</li>
</ul>
<p>The Class C60 and Class C61 were smaller locomotives than the Class C62, which filled the tight Japanese <a href="/wiki/Loading_gauge" title="Loading gauge">loading gauge</a>. They were equipped with <a href="/wiki/Boxpok" title="Boxpok">Boxpok</a> driving wheels and used several American-style appliances, even though they had British-style <a href="/wiki/Smokebox" title="Smokebox">smokebox</a> doors.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Netherlands">Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-6-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Dutch Railways ordered six <span class="nowrap">4-6-4T</span> passenger locomotives from <a href="/wiki/Beyer,_Peacock_and_Company" title="Beyer, Peacock and Company">Beyer, Peacock and Company</a> in 1913. A follow-up order for 34 locomotives was only partly delivered when, due to the downturn in traffic caused by the First World War, the Dutch authorities cancelled the remainder of the order. The forty locomotives as ordered were originally to be numbered 1201 to 1240, but the 36 that were delivered were later renumbered 6001 to 6026.</p>
<p>The fourteen undelivered locomotives were sold to the British <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_Department_(UK)" title="War Department (UK)">War Department</a> for use on the Western Front, where air-braked passenger locomotives were in short supply. They were assigned <a href="/wiki/Railway_Operating_Division" title="Railway Operating Division">Railway Operating Division</a> (ROD) numbers 1 to 12, 14 and 15 and were used on ambulance and troop trains as well as civilian passenger trains in the British sector.</p>
<p>After the war, they were sold to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemin_de_Fer_du_Nord" title="Chemin de Fer du Nord">Chemin de Fer du Nord</a> in France, who numbered them 3.871 to 3.884. In 1938, all fourteen passed on to the <a href="/wiki/SNCF" title="SNCF">SNCF</a>, who renumbered them 232.TB.1 to 232.TB.14. Two were withdrawn in 1946, but the rest remained in service until 1950-1951. They were outlived by their Dutch sister locomotives, of which twenty were still in service in 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> (Also see <a href="/wiki/4-6-4#France" title="4-6-4">France</a>)</p>
<p>There were also ten four-cylinder 6100 class locomotives, built in 1929 by Hohenzollern and Werkspoor and based on the 3700 4-6-0 class. The last two were withdrawn in 1958.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-6-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>No <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> tender locomotives saw service in South Africa, but six <span class="nowrap">4-6-4T</span> tank locomotive classes were used, all of them on <span class="nowrap">3 ft 6 in</span> (<span class="nowrap">1,067 mm</span>) <span class="nowrap"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_gauge" title="Cape gauge">Cape gauge</a></span>.</p>
<p>In 1896, the <a href="/wiki/Natal_Government_Railways" title="Natal Government Railways">Natal Government Railways</a> (NGR) rebuilt one of its <a href="/wiki/South_African_Class_C_4-6-0T" title="South African Class C 4-6-0T">Class K&amp;S 4-6-0</a> tank locomotives to a <span class="nowrap">4-6-4T</span> configuration, as directed by NGR Locomotive Superintendent George William Reid. This was the first known use of this wheel arrangement and was done to enable the locomotive to run equally well in either direction in shuttle service on the <a href="/wiki/Colony_of_Natal" title="Colony of Natal">Natal</a> South Coast line, where no turning facilities were available. In 1912, when it was assimilated into the South African Railways (SAR), this locomotive was designated <a href="/wiki/South_African_Class_C2_4-6-4T" title="South African Class C2 4-6-4T">Class C2</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holland_1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holland_1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paxton-Bourne_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Paxton-Bourne-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SAR_Class_E_87_(4-6-4T)_NGR_Neilson_Reid_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1472" data-file-width="3592" height="90" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/SAR_Class_E_87_%284-6-4T%29_NGR_Neilson_Reid_1.jpg/220px-SAR_Class_E_87_%284-6-4T%29_NGR_Neilson_Reid_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/SAR_Class_E_87_%284-6-4T%29_NGR_Neilson_Reid_1.jpg/330px-SAR_Class_E_87_%284-6-4T%29_NGR_Neilson_Reid_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/SAR_Class_E_87_%284-6-4T%29_NGR_Neilson_Reid_1.jpg/440px-SAR_Class_E_87_%284-6-4T%29_NGR_Neilson_Reid_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>Ten tank locomotives, designed by G.W. Reid, were built for the NGR by <a href="/wiki/Neilson_and_Company#Turn_of_the_20th_century" title="Neilson and Company">Neilson, Reid and Company</a> in 1902. It was the first known locomotive in the world to be designed and built as a <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> Baltic type. Known as the Neilson, Reid locomotives until they were designated the NGR's Class F, they were larger versions of the rebuilt Class H locomotive of 1896 and many of the main dimensions were identical. It had a plate frame, Stephenson valve gear and used saturated steam. In 1912 they became the <a href="/wiki/South_African_Class_E_4-6-4T" title="South African Class E 4-6-4T">Class E</a> on the SAR.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holland_1_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Holland_1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paxton-Bourne_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Paxton-Bourne-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SAR_Klasse_F.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="487" data-file-width="1029" height="104" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/SAR_Klasse_F.jpg/220px-SAR_Klasse_F.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/SAR_Klasse_F.jpg/330px-SAR_Klasse_F.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/SAR_Klasse_F.jpg/440px-SAR_Klasse_F.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SAR_Klasse_F.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
CSAR Class F no. 260, SAR no. 78</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Eight Class F tank locomotives were placed in service on the <a href="/wiki/Central_South_African_Railways" title="Central South African Railways">Central South African Railways</a> (CSAR) in 1904, designed by CSAR Chief Locomotive Superintendent P.A. Hyde and built by <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_Foundry" title="Vulcan Foundry">Vulcan Foundry</a>. It had a bar frame, <a href="/wiki/Stephenson_valve_gear" title="Stephenson valve gear">Stephenson valve gear</a> and used saturated steam, and was acquired for the suburban services between Springs and Randfontein. The double red lining on their black livery and polished copper-capped chimneys, brass domes and boiler bands earned them the nickname <i>Chocolate Boxes</i>. These locomotives retained their <a href="/wiki/South_African_Class_F_4-6-4T" title="South African Class F 4-6-4T">Class F</a> classification on the SAR.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holland_1_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Holland_1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paxton-Bourne_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Paxton-Bourne-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CSAR_Rack_4-6-4T_no._996.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="527" data-file-width="1631" height="71" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/CSAR_Rack_4-6-4T_no._996.jpg/220px-CSAR_Rack_4-6-4T_no._996.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/CSAR_Rack_4-6-4T_no._996.jpg/330px-CSAR_Rack_4-6-4T_no._996.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/CSAR_Rack_4-6-4T_no._996.jpg/440px-CSAR_Rack_4-6-4T_no._996.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CSAR_Rack_4-6-4T_no._996.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/CSAR_Rack_4-6-4RT" title="CSAR Rack 4-6-4RT">CSAR rack tank</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1905, two <a href="/wiki/CSAR_Rack_4-6-4RT" title="CSAR Rack 4-6-4RT">rack tank</a> locomotives were built for the CSAR by <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_Foundry" title="Vulcan Foundry">Vulcan Foundry</a>, for use on the steep rack section between <a href="/wiki/Waterval_Onder" title="Waterval Onder">Waterval Onder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Waterval_Boven" title="Waterval Boven">Waterval Boven</a> on the line to <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a>. Designed as two-cylinder locomotives by Hyde, the design was modified by the builders to four cylinders with the inside cylinders driving the rack equipment, but without a compensating increase in boiler capacity. The locomotives were failures on the rack section, their rack equipment was removed within a year of entering service and they were reassigned to shunting duty. In 1912, they were considered obsolete by the SAR and not classified, but they remained in service until 1915.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holland_1_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Holland_1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paxton-Bourne_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Paxton-Bourne-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SAR_Class_K_(4-6-4T).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="998" data-file-width="1420" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/SAR_Class_K_%284-6-4T%29.jpg/220px-SAR_Class_K_%284-6-4T%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/SAR_Class_K_%284-6-4T%29.jpg/330px-SAR_Class_K_%284-6-4T%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/SAR_Class_K_%284-6-4T%29.jpg/440px-SAR_Class_K_%284-6-4T%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SAR_Class_K_(4-6-4T).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/South_African_Class_K_4-6-4T" title="South African Class K 4-6-4T">SAR Class K</a>, c. 1930</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Seven <a href="/wiki/South_African_Class_K_4-6-4T" title="South African Class K 4-6-4T">Class K</a> tank locomotives which had been built for the <a href="/wiki/Philippine_National_Railways" title="Philippine National Railways">Manila Railway Company</a> in the Philippine Islands by the <a href="/wiki/North_British_Locomotive_Company" title="North British Locomotive Company">North British Locomotive Company</a> (NBL) in 1914, were sold to the SAR in 1917 since delivering them to the Philippines during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a> became impossible. They were superheated, had <a href="/wiki/Walschaerts_valve_gear" title="Walschaerts valve gear">Walschaerts valve gear</a> and were the first locomotives in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> to be equipped with exhaust steam injectors, which were of the <a href="/wiki/Davies_and_Metcalfe" title="Davies and Metcalfe">Davies and Metcalfe</a> pattern. Nicknamed <i>Manila</i>, they remained in service until 1938.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paxton-Bourne_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Paxton-Bourne-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holland_2_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holland_2-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SAR_Class_J_345_(4-6-4T).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1209" data-file-width="2211" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/SAR_Class_J_345_%284-6-4T%29.jpg/220px-SAR_Class_J_345_%284-6-4T%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/SAR_Class_J_345_%284-6-4T%29.jpg/330px-SAR_Class_J_345_%284-6-4T%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/SAR_Class_J_345_%284-6-4T%29.jpg/440px-SAR_Class_J_345_%284-6-4T%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SAR_Class_J_345_(4-6-4T).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/South_African_Class_J_4-6-4T" title="South African Class J 4-6-4T">SAR Class J</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Six tank locomotives, designed by SAR Chief Mechanical Engineer D.A. Hendrie and built by <a href="/wiki/Nasmyth,_Gaskell_and_Company" title="Nasmyth, Gaskell and Company">Nasmyth, Wilson and Company</a>, were introduced on the SAR in 1915. Designated <a href="/wiki/South_African_Class_J_4-6-4T" title="South African Class J 4-6-4T">Class J</a>, they had <a href="/wiki/Walschaerts_valve_gear" title="Walschaerts valve gear">Walschaerts valve gear</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belpaire_firebox" title="Belpaire firebox">Belpaire fireboxes</a> and used saturated steam. Acquired to cope with increasing traffic on the Natal South Coast, but unable to handle the rapidly increasing loads due to their small proportions, they soon ended up being employed as shunting engines in the <a href="/wiki/Durban" title="Durban">Durban</a> harbour, at <a href="/wiki/Mossel_Bay" title="Mossel Bay">Mossel Bay</a> and in the Cape Midlands, until they were withdrawn from service by 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paxton-Bourne_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Paxton-Bourne-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holland_2_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holland_2-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soviet_Union">Soviet Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-6-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Soviet Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Only three prototype Hudson locomotives were built in the former Soviet Union, in 1937 and 1938. They were all streamlined and were the only streamlined series of Soviet steam locomotives, although a later post-war <a href="/wiki/4-8-4#Soviet_Union" title="4-8-4">P36 series</a> <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/4-8-4" title="4-8-4">4-8-4</a></span> Northern locomotive was semi-streamlined. All three were scrapped in the 1950s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul>
<li>In 1937, two were built by the <a href="/wiki/Kolomna" title="Kolomna">Kolomna</a> Locomotive Works. These were known as the 2-3-2K locomotives, designed by Lev Lebedyanskii and rated at 3,070 horsepower (2,290 kilowatts). Designated the P12 series, they were used to haul the <i>Red Arrow</i> passenger train between <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Leningrad</a>. The intention was to build up to ten 2-3-2K locomotives to haul all express passenger trains between Moscow and Leningrad, but these plans were interrupted by the Second World War and not resumed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Another one was built in <a href="/wiki/Luhansk" title="Luhansk">Voroshilovgrad</a> in 1938, known as the 2-3-2V experimental locomotive number 6998. This locomotive was never used on mainline service.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-6-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt>Tender locomotives</dt>
</dl>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Engine_10000_(Wonder_Book_of_Engineering_Wonders,_1931).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="591" data-file-width="1532" height="85" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Engine_10000_%28Wonder_Book_of_Engineering_Wonders%2C_1931%29.jpg/220px-Engine_10000_%28Wonder_Book_of_Engineering_Wonders%2C_1931%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Engine_10000_%28Wonder_Book_of_Engineering_Wonders%2C_1931%29.jpg/330px-Engine_10000_%28Wonder_Book_of_Engineering_Wonders%2C_1931%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Engine_10000_%28Wonder_Book_of_Engineering_Wonders%2C_1931%29.jpg/440px-Engine_10000_%28Wonder_Book_of_Engineering_Wonders%2C_1931%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Engine_10000_(Wonder_Book_of_Engineering_Wonders,_1931).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
LNER Class W1 no. 10000</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The only <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> tender locomotive in the United Kingdom was the <a href="/wiki/London_and_North_Eastern_Railway" title="London and North Eastern Railway">London and North Eastern Railway</a> (LNER) no. 10000, built in 1930 as an experimental high-pressure <a href="/wiki/Compound_locomotive" title="Compound locomotive">compound locomotive</a> with an experimental <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_steam_locomotive" title="High-pressure steam locomotive">high-pressure</a> <a href="/wiki/Water-tube_boiler" title="Water-tube boiler">water-tube boiler</a>. It was the only locomotive of the <a href="/wiki/LNER_Class_W1" title="LNER Class W1">Class W1</a> and became known as the <i>Hush-hush locomotive</i> on account of the great secrecy under which it was built. Its trailing wheels were arranged uniquely. Instead of being in one four-wheel trailing truck, the first pair was a <a href="/wiki/Cartazzi_axle" title="Cartazzi axle">Cartazzi axle</a>, mounted in a rigid frame but still allowed sideways deflection against a centering force, as typical of the LNER’s practice on its Pacific locomotives. The second pair was in a two-wheel trailing truck.</p>
<p>The experiment proved much less successful than had been hoped and in 1936 it was rebuilt along the lines of a streamlined <a href="/wiki/LNER_Class_A4" title="LNER Class A4">LNER Class A4</a> Pacific, though it retained its Baltic wheel arrangement. After being rebuilt, the Class W1 was still easily distinguishable from an A4 at a glance, without looking for the extra trailing wheels, by the fact that it was never officially named even though the name <i>Pegasus</i> had been proposed. It therefore became known among <a href="/wiki/Railfan#Trainspotting" title="Railfan">trainspotters</a> as the <i>Un-named</i> or <i>No-name Streak</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Tank locomotives</dt>
</dl>
<p>A number of <span class="nowrap">4-6-4T</span> locomotives were built for various British railway companies.</p>
<p>The first standard-gauge examples were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Whitelegg" title="Robert Whitelegg">Robert Whitelegg’s</a> design in 1912 for the <a href="/wiki/London,_Tilbury_and_Southend_Railway" title="London, Tilbury and Southend Railway">London, Tilbury and Southend Railway</a> (LT&amp;SR). They were only delivered after the LT&amp;SR had been taken over by the <a href="/wiki/Midland_Railway" title="Midland Railway">Midland Railway</a>, where they were designated the <a href="/wiki/LT%26SR_2100_Class" title="LT&amp;SR 2100 Class">2100 class</a>.</p>
<p>Between 1914 and 1922, the <a href="/wiki/London,_Brighton_and_South_Coast_Railway" title="London, Brighton and South Coast Railway">London, Brighton and South Coast Railway</a> (LB&amp;SCR) built seven <a href="/wiki/LB%26SCR_L_class" title="LB&amp;SCR L class">L class</a> tank locomotives, known as the <i>Brighton Baltics</i>. The first examples suffered from instability problems until they were rebuilt with well-tanks. These high-speed tank locomotives hauled the famous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Belle_(LBSC)" title="Southern Belle (LBSC)"><i>Brighton Belle</i></a> train until the <a href="/wiki/Railway_electrification_system" title="Railway electrification system">electrification</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Brighton_Main_Line" title="Brighton Main Line">Brighton mainline</a> in 1933, after which they were converted into N15X class <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/4-6-0" title="4-6-0">4-6-0</a></span> tender locomotives. They remained in service until 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_and_South_Western_Railway" title="Glasgow and South Western Railway">Glasgow and South Western Railway</a> and a number of other railways also had tank locomotive classes of this wheel arrangement.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hughes_Dreadnought_4-6-4T,_11114_(Wonder_Book_of_Engineering_Wonders,_1931).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="687" data-file-width="1521" height="99" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Hughes_Dreadnought_4-6-4T%2C_11114_%28Wonder_Book_of_Engineering_Wonders%2C_1931%29.jpg/220px-Hughes_Dreadnought_4-6-4T%2C_11114_%28Wonder_Book_of_Engineering_Wonders%2C_1931%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Hughes_Dreadnought_4-6-4T%2C_11114_%28Wonder_Book_of_Engineering_Wonders%2C_1931%29.jpg/330px-Hughes_Dreadnought_4-6-4T%2C_11114_%28Wonder_Book_of_Engineering_Wonders%2C_1931%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Hughes_Dreadnought_4-6-4T%2C_11114_%28Wonder_Book_of_Engineering_Wonders%2C_1931%29.jpg/440px-Hughes_Dreadnought_4-6-4T%2C_11114_%28Wonder_Book_of_Engineering_Wonders%2C_1931%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hughes_Dreadnought_4-6-4T,_11114_(Wonder_Book_of_Engineering_Wonders,_1931).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
L&amp;YR Hughes <i>Dreadnought Tank</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Lancashire_and_Yorkshire_Railway" title="Lancashire and Yorkshire Railway">Lancashire and Yorkshire Railway</a> examples were very rare in having four cylinders. Known as the <a href="/wiki/L%26YR_Hughes_4-6-4T" title="L&amp;YR Hughes 4-6-4T"><i>Dreadnought Tanks</i></a>, they proved to be too large and too complex for the duties they performed.</li>
<li>The saturated steam tank locomotives of the <a href="/wiki/Belfast_and_County_Down_Railway" title="Belfast and County Down Railway">Belfast and County Down Railway</a> were spectacularly unsuccessful because of poor valve settings.</li>
<li>On the other hand, the <a href="/wiki/Furness_Railway" title="Furness Railway">Furness Railway</a> tank locomotives, also using saturated steam and with inside cylinders, were very popular with their crews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_of_America">United States of America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-6-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: United States of America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:New_York_Central_4-6-4_Hudson_locomotive,_5249_(CJ_Allen,_Steel_Highway,_1928).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="303" data-file-width="1288" height="52" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/New_York_Central_4-6-4_Hudson_locomotive%2C_5249_%28CJ_Allen%2C_Steel_Highway%2C_1928%29.jpg/220px-New_York_Central_4-6-4_Hudson_locomotive%2C_5249_%28CJ_Allen%2C_Steel_Highway%2C_1928%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/New_York_Central_4-6-4_Hudson_locomotive%2C_5249_%28CJ_Allen%2C_Steel_Highway%2C_1928%29.jpg/330px-New_York_Central_4-6-4_Hudson_locomotive%2C_5249_%28CJ_Allen%2C_Steel_Highway%2C_1928%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/New_York_Central_4-6-4_Hudson_locomotive%2C_5249_%28CJ_Allen%2C_Steel_Highway%2C_1928%29.jpg/440px-New_York_Central_4-6-4_Hudson_locomotive%2C_5249_%28CJ_Allen%2C_Steel_Highway%2C_1928%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:New_York_Central_4-6-4_Hudson_locomotive,_5249_(CJ_Allen,_Steel_Highway,_1928).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Unstreamlined NYC Hudson</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NYC_Hudson2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="281" data-file-width="522" height="118" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/NYC_Hudson2.jpg/220px-NYC_Hudson2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/NYC_Hudson2.jpg/330px-NYC_Hudson2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/NYC_Hudson2.jpg/440px-NYC_Hudson2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NYC_Hudson2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/NYC_Hudson" title="NYC Hudson">NYC Hudson</a>, streamlined for the <i><a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Limited" title="20th Century Limited">20th Century Limited</a></i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first Hudson locomotive in North America was built in 1927 for the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Central_Railroad" title="New York Central Railroad">New York Central Railroad</a> (NYC) by <a href="/wiki/American_Locomotive_Company" title="American Locomotive Company">American Locomotive Company</a> (ALCO), to the railroad's design. The locomotive proved to be very successful and was named the <a href="/wiki/NYC_Hudson" title="NYC Hudson">Hudson</a> type, after the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a>. Thirteen of these locomotives, one J-1 type and twelve J-3a types, were streamlined for use with named passenger trains like the <i><a href="/wiki/Empire_State_Express" title="Empire State Express">Empire State Express</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Limited" title="20th Century Limited">20th Century Limited</a></i>. Between the NYC and its subsidiaries, the <a href="/wiki/Boston_and_Albany_Railroad" title="Boston and Albany Railroad">Boston and Albany Railroad</a> (B&amp;A), the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland,_Cincinnati,_Chicago_and_St._Louis_Railway" title="Cleveland, Cincinnati, Chicago and St. Louis Railway">Cleveland, Cincinnati, Chicago and St. Louis Railway</a> (CCC&amp;StL or <i>Big Four</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Michigan_Central_Railroad" title="Michigan Central Railroad">Michigan Central Railroad</a> (MC), they acquired altogether 275 <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> locomotives of several different types, the largest Hudson fleet in North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hudson_Type_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hudson_Type-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chicago,_Milwaukee,_St._Paul_and_Pacific_Railroad" title="Chicago, Milwaukee, St. Paul and Pacific Railroad">Chicago, Milwaukee, St. Paul and Pacific Railroad</a> (Milwaukee Road) could have produced the first American <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> since its design work was done earlier than that of the NYC, but financial constraints delayed the project and the Milwaukee's locomotives only emerged in 1930. The Milwaukee called them Baltic, following the European practice started in France. The initial order of fourteen <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Road_class_F6" title="Milwaukee Road class F6">Class F6</a> locomotives was followed by eight more Class F6a locomotives in 1931 and, in 1938, the Milwaukee acquired six streamlined <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Road_class_F7" title="Milwaukee Road class F7">Class F7</a> Baltics with the shrouds. These took over the Milwaukee's crack <a href="/wiki/Hiawatha_Service" title="Hiawatha Service"><i>Hiawatha</i></a> express trains from the <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Road_class_A" title="Milwaukee Road class A">Class A</a> <a href="/wiki/4-4-2_(locomotive)" title="4-4-2 (locomotive)"><span class="nowrap">4-4-2</span> Atlantics</a> and were among the fastest steam locomotives of all time. The schedules of many of these trains required extended running at 100 miles per hour (160 kilometres per hour).</p>
<p>In addition to the Milwaukee’s streamlined Class F7, two other railroads ordered larger and faster <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> locomotives with 84 inches (2,134 millimetres) drivers in the late 1930s. These were the <a href="/wiki/Atchison,_Topeka_and_Santa_Fe_Railway" title="Atchison, Topeka and Santa Fe Railway">Atchison, Topeka and Santa Fe Railway</a> (Santa Fe) with its <a href="/wiki/ATSF_3460_class" title="ATSF 3460 class">3460 Class</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_and_North_Western_Transportation_Company" title="Chicago and North Western Transportation Company">Chicago and North Western</a> (CNW) with its <a href="/wiki/CNW_Class_E-4" title="CNW Class E-4">Class E-4</a>. The Milwaukee and CNW locomotives were all streamlined, and only one of the Santa Fe locomotives, the 3460 <i>Blue Goose</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hudson_Type_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hudson_Type-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aeolus_Burlington_Steam_Streamline_locomotive_1937.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="385" data-file-width="737" height="115" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Aeolus_Burlington_Steam_Streamline_locomotive_1937.jpg/220px-Aeolus_Burlington_Steam_Streamline_locomotive_1937.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Aeolus_Burlington_Steam_Streamline_locomotive_1937.jpg/330px-Aeolus_Burlington_Steam_Streamline_locomotive_1937.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Aeolus_Burlington_Steam_Streamline_locomotive_1937.jpg/440px-Aeolus_Burlington_Steam_Streamline_locomotive_1937.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aeolus_Burlington_Steam_Streamline_locomotive_1937.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Streamlined <i>Aeolus</i> at the CB&amp;Q's Iowa shop in 1937</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1937, the <a href="/wiki/Chicago,_Burlington_and_Quincy_Railroad" title="Chicago, Burlington and Quincy Railroad">Chicago, Burlington and Quincy Railroad</a> (Burlington Route) needed backup locomotives for their streamlined diesel-hauled <i>Zephyr</i> passenger trains. Their solution was to streamline their Baldwin-built no. 3002 in their main <a href="/wiki/Iowa" title="Iowa">Iowa</a> shops. The locomotive was renumbered as no. 4000 and given the name <i>Aeolus</i>, after the mythical keeper of the winds. A second streamlined <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> was built for this purpose and numbered 4001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hudson_Type_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hudson_Type-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>There were also some once-off and experimental <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> locomotives. A number were rebuilt from <a href="/wiki/4-6-2" title="4-6-2"><span class="nowrap">4-6-2</span> Pacific</a> locomotives, or in some cases from other designs.</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_and_Ohio_Railroad" title="Baltimore and Ohio Railroad">Baltimore and Ohio Railroad</a> (B&amp;O) built four as experimental locomotives between 1933 and 1936, using <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_and_Ohio_Railroad_locomotives#.22Uncle_Dan.22_and_Colonel_Emerson" title="Baltimore and Ohio Railroad locomotives">Colonel Emerson’s</a> water-tube fireboxes, but eventually turned to diesel-electric traction instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 1937, the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Central_Railroad" title="Illinois Central Railroad">Illinois Central Railroad</a> (IC) rebuilt a <a href="/wiki/2-8-4" title="2-8-4">2-8-4 Berkshire</a> into its only Hudson, the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Central_No._1" title="Illinois Central No. 1">Illinois Central No. 1</a>, which was not a success and was not repeated.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Wabash_Railroad" title="Wabash Railroad">Wabash Railroad</a> rebuilt its seven <a href="/wiki/Wabash_class_P1" title="Wabash class P1">Class P1</a> Hudsons from their unsuccessful K-4 and K5 Class <a href="/wiki/2-8-2" title="2-8-2">2-8-2 Mikado</a> locomotives.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="North_American_production_list">North American production list</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-6-4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: North American production list">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Altogether 21 railroads in North America owned <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span>s. Many were similar in concept to the NYC Hudsons, with 79 to 80 inches (2,007 to 2,032 millimetres) driving wheels, but most were a little larger than the NYC locomotives, such as the F6 and F6a classes of the Milwaukee Road, the <span class="nowrap">K-5-a</span> class of the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_National_Railway" title="Canadian National Railway">Canadian National</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Pacific_Railway" title="Canadian Pacific Railway">Canadian Pacific</a> locomotives, the <span class="nowrap">S-4</span> class of the Burlington Route, the <span class="nowrap">I-5</span> class of the <a href="/wiki/New_York,_New_Haven_and_Hartford_Railroad" title="New York, New Haven and Hartford Railroad">New Haven</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Delaware,_Lackawanna_and_Western_1151_class" title="Delaware, Lackawanna and Western 1151 class">1151 class</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Delaware,_Lackawanna_and_Western_Railroad" title="Delaware, Lackawanna and Western Railroad">Lackawanna</a>. There were also the lightweights, which include the <span class="nowrap">L-1</span> class of the <a href="/wiki/New_York,_Chicago_and_St._Louis_Railroad" title="New York, Chicago and St. Louis Railroad">Nickel Plate Road</a>, the class D of the <a href="/wiki/Maine_Central_Railroad_Company" title="Maine Central Railroad Company">Maine Central</a> and the class <span class="nowrap">NR-1</span> of the <a href="/wiki/Ferrocarriles_Nacionales_de_M%C3%A9xico" title="Ferrocarriles Nacionales de México">Ferrocarriles Nacionales de México</a> (N de M). On these, the extra axle was used to reduce the axle load in comparison to a <span class="nowrap">4-6-2</span> Pacific locomotive.</p>
<p>Because the <span class="nowrap">4-6-4</span> design was really only optimally suited to express passenger trains, which were dieselised early, the Hudsons were early candidates for withdrawal and scrapping. None of the NYC locomotives survived and neither did any of the Milwaukee locomotives. Five Canadian Pacific Hudsons survive, including four Royal Hudsons and the un-streamlined <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Pacific_2816" title="Canadian Pacific 2816">Canadian Pacific 2816</a>. Five of the Burlington Route locomotives survive, including the <i>Aeolus</i>. Other surviving Hudson locomotives are two each of the Santa Fe and Canadian National, and single examples from the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_and_Ohio_Railway" title="Chesapeake and Ohio Railway">Chesapeake and Ohio Railway</a>, N de M and Nickel Plate Road.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hudson_Type_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hudson_Type-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
