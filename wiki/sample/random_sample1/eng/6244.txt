<p><i><b>Sonchus</b></i> is a genus of flowering plants in the <a href="/wiki/Cichorieae" title="Cichorieae">dandelion tribe</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Asteraceae" title="Asteraceae">sunflower family</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-u_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-u-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of the species are annual herbs, a few are perennial, and a few are even woody (subgenus <i>Dendrosonchus,</i> restricted to the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Annual herbs in the genus are known as <b>sow thistles</b> (less commonly <b>hare thistles</b> or <b>hare lettuces</b>). The genus is named after the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greek</a> for such plants. All are characterized by soft, somewhat irregularly lobed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaves" title="Leaves">leaves</a> that clasp the stem and, at least initially, form a basal rosette. The <a href="/wiki/Plant_stem" title="Plant stem">stem</a> contains a milky <a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Head_(botany)" title="Head (botany)">Flower heads</a> are yellow and range in size from half to one inch in diameter; the <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">florets</a> are all of ray type. Sow thistles are common roadside plants, and while native to <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> and tropical <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, they are found almost worldwide in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">temperate</a> regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mature sow thistle stems can range from 30 cm to 2 m (1 to 6 feet) tall, depending upon species and growing conditions. Colouration ranges from green to purple in older plants. Sow thistles exude a milky <a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a> when any part of the plant is cut or damaged, and it is from this fact that the plants obtained the common name, "sow thistle", as they were fed to lactating <a href="/wiki/Pig" title="Pig">sows</a> in the belief that <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a> production would increase. Sow thistles are known as "milk thistles" in some regions, although true <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milk_thistle" title="Milk thistle">milk thistles</a> belong to the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Silybum" title="Silybum">Silybum</a></i>.</p>
<p>Sow thistles have been used as fodder, particularly for <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a>, hence the other common names of "hare thistle" or "hare lettuce". They are also edible to <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Leaf_vegetable" title="Leaf vegetable">leaf vegetable</a>; old leaves and stalks can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bitter_(taste)" title="Bitter (taste)">bitter</a> but young leaves have a flavour similar to <a href="/wiki/Lettuce" title="Lettuce">lettuce</a>. Going by the name <i>puha</i> or <i>rareke</i> (<i>raraki</i>) it is frequently eaten in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> as a vegetable, particularly by the native <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Māori</a>. When cooked the flavour is reminiscent of <a href="/wiki/Chard" title="Chard">chard</a>.</p>
<p>In many areas sow thistles are considered <a href="/wiki/Noxious_weed" title="Noxious weed">noxious weeds</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> as they grow quickly in a wide range of conditions and their wind-borne <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a> allow them to spread rapidly. <i>Sonchus arvensis</i>, the perennial sow thistle, is considered the most economically detrimental, as it can crowd commercial <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">crops</a>, is a heavy consumer of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> in <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a>, may deplete soil water of land left to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fallow" title="Fallow">fallow</a>, and can regrow and sprout additional plants from its creeping <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">roots</a>. However, sow thistles are easily uprooted by hand, and their soft stems present little resistance to slashing or mowing. Most <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> will readily devour sow thistle in preference to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grass" title="Grass">grass</a>, and this lettuce-relative is edible and nutritious to humans — in fact this is the meaning of the second part of the Latin name of the common sow thistle, <i>oleraceus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Attempts at weed control by herbicidal use, to the neglect of other methods, may have led to a proliferation of this species in some environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In traditional medicine, the plant has medicinal qualities, having "nearly the same properties as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dandelion" title="Dandelion">dandelion</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Succory" title="Succory">succory</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sow thistles are common host plants for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphids" title="Aphids">aphids</a>. Gardeners may consider this a benefit or a curse; aphids may spread from sow thistle to other plants, but alternatively the sow thistle can encourage the growth of beneficial predators such as <a href="/wiki/Hoverfly" title="Hoverfly">hoverflies</a>. In this regard sow thistles make excellent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacrificial_plant" title="Sacrificial plant">sacrificial plants</a>. <i>Sonchus</i> species are used as food plants by the <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> of some <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">Lepidoptera</a> including <i><a href="/wiki/Celypha_rufana" title="Celypha rufana">Celypha rufana</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/Broad-barred_white" title="Broad-barred white">broad-barred white</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grey_chi" title="Grey chi">grey chi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nutmeg_(moth)" title="Nutmeg (moth)">nutmeg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shark_(moth)" title="Shark (moth)">shark</a> moths .
