<div class="hatnote" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Condom" title="Condom">condom</a> which is <i>preservativo</i> in Spanish, <i>préservatif</i> in French, etc.</div>
<p>A <b>preservative</b> is a substance or a chemical that is added to products such as food, beverages, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_drugs" title="Pharmaceutical drugs">pharmaceutical drugs</a>, paints, biological samples, cosmetics, wood, and many other products to prevent <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">decomposition</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbial" title="Microbial">microbial</a> growth or by undesirable <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical changes</a>. In general, preservation is implemented in two modes, chemical and physical. Chemical preservation entails adding <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a> to the product. Physical preservation entails processes such as refrigeration or drying.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-1">[1]</a></sup> Preservative <a href="/wiki/Food_additive" title="Food additive">food additives</a> reduce the risk of <a href="/wiki/Foodborne_illness" title="Foodborne illness">foodborne infections</a>, decrease microbial spoilage, and preserve fresh attributes and nutritional quality. Some physical techniques for food preservation include dehydration, UV-C radiation, freeze-drying, and refrigeration. Chemical preservation and physical preservation techniques are sometimes combined.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Antimicrobial_Preservatives">Antimicrobial Preservatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Preservative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Antimicrobial Preservatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Antimicrobial preservatives prevent degradation by bacteria. This method is the most traditional and ancient type of preserving—ancient methods such as pickling and adding honey prevent microorganism growth by modifying the pH level. The most commonly used antimicrobial preservative is <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a>. Common antimicrobial preservatives are presented in the table.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Msagati_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Msagati-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEN-1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-CEN-1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrates" title="Nitrates">Nitrates</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrites" title="Nitrites">nitrites</a> are also antimicrobial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Field_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Field-6">[6]</a></sup> The detailed mechanism of these chemical compounds range from inhibiting growth of the bacteria to the inhibition of specific enzymes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Antioxidants">Antioxidants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Preservative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Antioxidants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lipid_peroxidation.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1425" data-file-width="1690" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Lipid_peroxidation.svg/220px-Lipid_peroxidation.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Lipid_peroxidation.svg/330px-Lipid_peroxidation.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Lipid_peroxidation.svg/440px-Lipid_peroxidation.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lipid_peroxidation.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)" title="Radical (chemistry)">free radical</a> pathway for the first phase of the oxidative rancidification of fats. This process is slowed by antioxidants.</div>

<p>The oxidation process spoils most food, especially those with a high fat content. Fats quickly <a href="/wiki/Rancidification" title="Rancidification">turn rancid</a> when exposed to oxygen. Antioxidants prevent or inhibit the oxidation process. The most common antioxidant additives are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascorbic_acid" title="Ascorbic acid">ascorbic acid</a> (<a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">vitamin C</a> ) and ascorbates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bhat_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bhat-7">[7]</a></sup> Thus, antioxidants are commonly added to oils, cheese, and chips.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Msagati_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Msagati-2">[2]</a></sup> Other antioxidants include the <a href="/wiki/Phenol" title="Phenol">phenol</a> derivatives <a href="/wiki/Butylated_hydroxyanisole" title="Butylated hydroxyanisole">BHA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Butylated_hydroxytoluene" title="Butylated hydroxytoluene">BHT</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tert-Butylhydroquinone" title="Tert-Butylhydroquinone">TBHQ</a> and <a href="/wiki/Propyl_gallate" title="Propyl gallate">propyl gallate</a>. These agents suppress the formation of hydroperoxides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEN-1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-CEN-1-3">[3]</a></sup> Other preservatives include <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methylchloroisothiazolinone" title="Methylchloroisothiazolinone">methylchloroisothiazolinone</a>.</p>

<p>A variety of agents are added to sequester (deactivate) metal ions that otherwise catalyze the oxidation of fats. Common sequestering agents are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EDTA" title="EDTA">disodium EDTA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid" title="Citric acid">citric acid</a> (and citrates), <a href="/wiki/Tartaric_acid" title="Tartaric acid">tartaric acid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lecithin" title="Lecithin">lecithin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nonsynthetic_compounds_for_food_preservation">Nonsynthetic compounds for food preservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Preservative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nonsynthetic compounds for food preservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Citric_acid" title="Citric acid">Citric</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascorbic_acid" title="Ascorbic acid">ascorbic acids</a> target <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enzymes" title="Enzymes">enzymes</a> that degrade fruits and vegetables, e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phenolase" title="Phenolase">phenolase</a> which turns surfaces of cut apples and potatoes brown. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascorbic_acid" title="Ascorbic acid">Ascorbic acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tocopherol" title="Tocopherol">tocopherol</a>, which are vitamins, are common preservatives. <a href="/wiki/Smoking_(cooking)" title="Smoking (cooking)">Smoking</a> entails exposing food to a variety of phenols, which are antioxidants. Natural preservatives include <a href="/wiki/Rosemary" title="Rosemary">rosemary</a> extract, <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">hops</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vinegar" title="Vinegar">vinegar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">alcohol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diatomaceous_earth" title="Diatomaceous earth">diatomaceous earth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Castor_oil" title="Castor oil">castor oil</a>.</p>
<p>Traditional preservatives, such as sodium benzoate have raised health concerns in the past. Benzoate was shown in a study to cause hypersensitivity in some asthma sufferers. This has caused reexamination of natural preservatives which occur in vegetables.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Preservative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Preservatives have been used since prehistoric times. <a href="/wiki/Smoked_meat" title="Smoked meat">Smoked meat</a> for example has <a href="/wiki/Phenol" title="Phenol">phenols</a> and other chemicals that retard spoilage. The preservation of foods has evolved greatly over the centuries and has been instrumental in increasing food security. The use of preservatives other than traditional oils, salts, paints, etc. in food began in the late 19th century, but was not widespread until the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The use of food preservatives varies greatly depending on the country. Many developing countries that do not have strong governments to regulate food additives face either harmful levels of preservatives in foods or a complete avoidance of foods that are considered unnatural or foreign. These countries have also proven useful in case studies surrounding chemical preservatives, as they have been only recently introduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In urban slums of highly populated countries, the knowledge about contents of food tends to be extremely low, despite consumption of these imported foods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_awareness_of_food_preservation">Public awareness of food preservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Preservative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Public awareness of food preservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Public awareness of food preservatives is uneven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Americans have a perception that food-borne illnesses happen more often in other countries. This may be true, but the occurrence of illnesses, hospitalizations, and deaths are still high. It is estimated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Center_for_Disease_Control" title="Center for Disease Control">Center for Disease Control</a> (CDC) that each year there are 76 million illnesses, 325,000 hospitalizations, and 5,000 deaths linked to food-borne illness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Theron.2C_M._M._2007_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Theron.2C_M._M._2007-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The increasing demand for ready-to-eat fresh food products has led to challenges for food distributors regarding the safety and quality of their foods. Artificial preservatives meet some of these challenges by preserving freshness for longer periods of time, but these preservatives can cause negative side-effects as well. <a href="/wiki/Sodium_nitrite" title="Sodium nitrite">Sodium nitrite</a> is a preservative used in lunch meats, <a href="/wiki/Ham" title="Ham">hams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sausage" title="Sausage">sausages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hot_dog" title="Hot dog">hot dogs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bacon" title="Bacon">bacon</a> to prevent <a href="/wiki/Botulism" title="Botulism">botulism</a>. It serves the important function of controlling the <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> that cause <a href="/wiki/Botulism" title="Botulism">botulism</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Sodium_nitrite" title="Sodium nitrite">sodium nitrite</a> can react with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteins" title="Proteins">proteins</a>, or during cooking at high heats, to form <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogenic N-nitrosamines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Field_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Field-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources_(medicine)" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources (medicine)"><span title="Material near this tag may rely on an unreliable or less reliable medical source. (March 2015)">unreliable medical source?</span></a></i>]</sup> It has also been linked to <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> in lab animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Antinoro_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Antinoro-14">[14]</a></sup> The commonly used <a href="/wiki/Sodium_benzoate" title="Sodium benzoate">sodium benzoate</a> has been found to extend the shelf life of bottled <a href="/wiki/Tomato_paste" title="Tomato paste">tomato paste</a> to 40 weeks without loss of quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bhat_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bhat-7">[7]</a></sup> However, it can form the <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogen</a> <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a> when combined with <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">vitamin C</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Many food manufacturers have reformed their products to eliminate this combination, but a risk still exists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Antinoro_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Antinoro-14">[14]</a></sup> Consumption of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_benzoate" title="Sodium benzoate">sodium benzoate</a> may also cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperactivity" title="Hyperactivity">hyperactivity</a>. For over 30 years, there has been a debate about whether or not preservatives and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_additives" title="Food additives">food additives</a> can cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperactivity" title="Hyperactivity">hyperactivity</a>. Studies have found that there may be increases in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperactivity" title="Hyperactivity">hyperactivity</a> amongst children who consume artificial colorings and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benzoate" title="Benzoate">benzoate</a> preservatives and who are already genetically predisposed to hyperactivity, but these studies were not entirely conclusive. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperactivity" title="Hyperactivity">Hyperactivity</a> only increased moderately, and it was not determined if the preservatives, colorings, or a combination of the two were responsible for the increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
