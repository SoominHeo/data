<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_effects">Health effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiobiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Health effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ionizing radiation is generally harmful and potentially lethal to living things but can have health benefits in <a href="/wiki/Radiation_therapy" title="Radiation therapy">radiation therapy</a> for the treatment of cancer and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thyrotoxicosis" title="Thyrotoxicosis">thyrotoxicosis</a>. Its most common impact is the <a href="/wiki/Radiation-induced_cancer" title="Radiation-induced cancer">induction of cancer</a> with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latent_period" title="Latent period">latent period</a> of years or decades after exposure. High doses can cause visually dramatic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_burns" title="Radiation burns">radiation burns</a>, and/or rapid fatality through <a href="/wiki/Acute_radiation_syndrome" title="Acute radiation syndrome">acute radiation syndrome</a>. Controlled doses are used for <a href="/wiki/Medical_imaging" title="Medical imaging">medical imaging</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiotherapy" title="Radiotherapy">radiotherapy</a>. Some scientists suspect that low doses may have a mild <a href="/wiki/Radiation_hormesis" title="Radiation hormesis">hormetic effect</a> that can improve health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some effects of ionizing radiation on human health are <a href="/wiki/Stochastic" title="Stochastic">stochastic</a>, meaning that their probability of occurrence increases with dose, while the severity is independent of dose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christensen2014_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christensen2014-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Radiation-induced_cancer" title="Radiation-induced cancer">Radiation-induced cancer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teratogenesis" title="Teratogenesis">teratogenesis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_induced_cognitive_decline" title="Radiation induced cognitive decline">cognitive decline</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_disease" title="Heart disease">heart disease</a> are all examples of stochastic effects. Other conditions such as <a href="/wiki/Radiation_burn" title="Radiation burn">radiation burns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acute_radiation_syndrome" title="Acute radiation syndrome">acute radiation syndrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chronic_radiation_syndrome" title="Chronic radiation syndrome">chronic radiation syndrome</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Radiation-induced_thyroiditis" title="Radiation-induced thyroiditis">radiation-induced thyroiditis</a> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deterministic" title="Deterministic">deterministic</a>, meaning they reliably occur above a threshold dose, and their severity increases with dose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christensen2014_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Christensen2014-2">[2]</a></sup> Deterministic effects are not necessarily more or less serious than stochastic effects; either can ultimately lead to a temporary nuisance or a fatality.</p>
<p>Other effects include <a href="/wiki/Radiation-induced_lung_injury" title="Radiation-induced lung injury">radiation-induced lung injury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cataract" title="Cataract">cataracts</a>, and infertility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christensen2014_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Christensen2014-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Quantitative data on the effects of ionizing radiation on human health is relatively limited compared to other medical conditions because of the low number of cases to date, and because of the stochastic nature of some of the effects. Stochastic effects can only be measured through large epidemiological studies where enough data has been collected to remove confounding factors such as smoking habits and other lifestyle factors. The richest source of high-quality data comes from the study of Japanese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_bomb_survivors" title="Atomic bomb survivors">atomic bomb survivors</a>. In vitro and animal experiments are informative, but <a href="/wiki/Radioresistance" title="Radioresistance">radioresistance</a> varies greatly across species.</p>
<p>The consensus of the nuclear industry, regulators and governments regarding radiation health effects is expressed by the <a href="/wiki/International_Commission_on_Radiological_Protection" title="International Commission on Radiological Protection">International Commission on Radiological Protection</a>. (ICRP) Other important organizations studying the topic include</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/International_Commission_on_Radiation_Units_and_Measurements" title="International Commission on Radiation Units and Measurements">International Commission on Radiation Units and Measurements</a> (ICRU)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Scientific_Committee_on_the_Effects_of_Atomic_Radiation" title="United Nations Scientific Committee on the Effects of Atomic Radiation">United Nations Scientific Committee on the Effects of Atomic Radiation</a> (UNSCEAR)</li>
<li>US <a href="/wiki/National_Council_on_Radiation_Protection_and_Measurements" title="National Council on Radiation Protection and Measurements">National Council on Radiation Protection and Measurements</a> (NCRP)</li>
<li>UK <a href="/wiki/Public_Health_England" title="Public Health England">Public Health England</a></li>
<li>US <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> (NAS through the BEIR studies)</li>
<li>French <a href="/wiki/Institut_de_radioprotection_et_de_s%C3%BBret%C3%A9_nucl%C3%A9aire" title="Institut de radioprotection et de sûreté nucléaire">Institut de radioprotection et de sûreté nucléaire</a> (IRSN)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/European_Committee_on_Radiation_Risk" title="European Committee on Radiation Risk">European Committee on Radiation Risk</a> (ECRR) the stage of radiation depends on the stage the body parts are affected</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exposure_pathways">Exposure pathways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiobiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Exposure pathways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="External">External</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiobiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: External">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>External exposure is exposure which occurs when the radioactive source (or other radiation source) is outside (and remains outside) the organism which is exposed. Examples of external exposure include:</p>
<ul>
<li>A person who places a sealed <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_source" title="Radioactive source">radioactive source</a> in his pocket</li>
<li>A space traveller who is irradiated by <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a></li>
<li>A person who is treated for <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> by either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teletherapy" title="Teletherapy">teletherapy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Brachytherapy" title="Brachytherapy">brachytherapy</a>. While in brachytherapy the source is inside the person it is still considered external exposure because it does not result in a <a href="/wiki/Committed_dose" title="Committed dose">committed dose</a>.</li>
<li>A nuclear worker whose hands have been dirtied with radioactive dust. Assuming that his hands are cleaned before any radioactive material can be absorbed, inhaled or ingested, skin contamination is considered external exposure.</li>
</ul>
<p>External exposure is relatively <i>easy</i> to estimate, and the irradiated organism does not become radioactive, except for a case where the radiation is an intense <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutron</a> beam which causes <a href="/wiki/Neutron_activation" title="Neutron activation">activation</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal">Internal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiobiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Internal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Internal exposure occurs when the radioactive material enters the organism, and the radioactive atoms become incorporated into the organism. This can occur through inhalation, ingestion, or injection. Below are a series of examples of internal exposure.</p>
<ul>
<li>The exposure caused by <a href="/wiki/Potassium-40" title="Potassium-40">potassium-40</a> present within a <i>normal</i> person.</li>
<li>The exposure to the ingestion of a soluble radioactive substance, such as <a href="/wiki/Strontium-89" title="Strontium-89"><sup>89</sup>Sr</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cow" title="Cow">cows</a>' <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a>.</li>
<li>A person who is being treated for cancer by means of a <a href="/wiki/Radiopharmaceutical" title="Radiopharmaceutical">radiopharmaceutical</a> where a radioisotope is used as a drug (usually a liquid or pill). A review of this topic was published in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Because the radioactive material becomes intimately mixed with the affected object it is often difficult to decontaminate the object or person in a case where internal exposure is occurring. While some very insoluble materials such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fission_product" title="Fission product">fission products</a> within a <a href="/wiki/Uranium_dioxide" title="Uranium dioxide">uranium dioxide</a> matrix might never be able to truly become part of an organism, it is normal to consider such particles in the lungs and digestive tract as a form of internal contamination which results in internal exposure.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boron_neutron_capture_therapy" title="Boron neutron capture therapy">Boron neutron capture therapy</a> (BNCT) involves injecting a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boron-10" title="Boron-10">boron-10</a> tagged chemical that preferentially binds to tumor cells. Neutrons from a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactor</a> are shaped by a <a href="/wiki/Neutron_moderator" title="Neutron moderator">neutron moderator</a> to the neutron energy spectrum suitable for BNCT treatment. The tumor is selectively bombarded with these neutrons. The neutrons quickly slow down in the body to become low energy <i>thermal neutrons</i>. These <i>thermal neutrons</i> are captured by the injected boron-10, forming excited (boron-11) which breaks down into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithium-7" title="Lithium-7">lithium-7</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Helium-4" title="Helium-4">helium-4</a> <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">alpha particle</a> both of these produce closely spaced ionizing radiation.This concept is described as a binary system using two separate components for the therapy of cancer. Each component in itself is relatively harmless to the cells, but when combined together for treatment they produce a highly cytocidal (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cytotoxic" title="Cytotoxic">cytotoxic</a>) effect which is lethal (within a limited range of 5-9 micrometers or approximately one cell diameter). Clinical trials, with promising results, are currently carried out in Finland and Japan.</li>
</ul>
<p>When radioactive compounds enter the human body, the effects are different from those resulting from exposure to an external radiation source. Especially in the case of alpha radiation, which normally does not penetrate the skin, the exposure can be much more damaging after ingestion or inhalation. The radiation exposure is normally expressed as a <a href="/wiki/Committed_dose" title="Committed dose">committed dose</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiobiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although radiation was discovered in late 19th century, the dangers of radioactivity and of radiation were not immediately recognized. Acute effects of radiation were first observed in the use of X-rays when <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_R%C3%B6ntgen" title="Wilhelm Röntgen">Wilhelm Röntgen</a> intentionally subjected his fingers to X-rays in 1895. He published his observations concerning the burns that developed, though he misattributed them to ozone, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_radical" title="Free radical">free radical</a> produced in air by X-rays. Other free radicals produced within the body are now understood to be more important. His injuries healed later.</p>
<p>As a field of medical sciences, radiobiology originated from <a href="/wiki/Leopold_Freund" title="Leopold Freund">Leopold Freund</a>'s 1896 demonstration of the therapeutic treatment of a hairy <a href="/wiki/Melanocytic_nevus" title="Melanocytic nevus">mole</a> using a new type of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-rays" title="X-rays">x-rays</a>, which was discovered 1 year previously by the German physicist, <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_R%C3%B6ntgen" title="Wilhelm Röntgen">Wilhelm Röntgen</a>. After irradiating frogs and insects with X-rays in early 1896, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ivan_Romanovich_Tarkhanov" title="Ivan Romanovich Tarkhanov">Ivan Romanovich Tarkhanov</a> concluded that these newly discovered rays not only photograph, but also "affect the living function".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> At the same time, Pierre and <a href="/wiki/Marie_Curie" title="Marie Curie">Marie Curie</a> discovered the radioactive polonium and radium later used to treat <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>.</p>
<p>The genetic effects of radiation, including the effects on cancer risk, were recognized much later. In 1927 <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Joseph_Muller" title="Hermann Joseph Muller">Hermann Joseph Muller</a> published research showing genetic effects, and in 1946 was awarded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_prize" title="Nobel prize">Nobel prize</a> for his findings.</p>
<p>Before the biological effects of radiation were known, many physicians and corporations had begun marketing radioactive substances as <a href="/wiki/Patent_medicine" title="Patent medicine">patent medicine</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive_quackery" title="Radioactive quackery">radioactive quackery</a>. Examples were radium <a href="/wiki/Enema" title="Enema">enema</a> treatments, and radium-containing waters to be drunk as tonics. <a href="/wiki/Marie_Curie" title="Marie Curie">Marie Curie</a> spoke out against this sort of treatment, warning that the effects of radiation on the human body were not well understood. Curie later died of <a href="/wiki/Aplastic_anemia" title="Aplastic anemia">aplastic anemia</a> caused by radiation poisoning. <a href="/wiki/Eben_Byers" title="Eben Byers">Eben Byers</a>, a famous American socialite, died of multiple cancers (but not acute radiation syndrome) in 1932 after consuming large quantities of <a href="/wiki/Radium" title="Radium">radium</a> over several years; his death drew public attention to dangers of radiation. By the 1930s, after a number of cases of bone necrosis and death in enthusiasts, radium-containing medical products had nearly vanished from the market.</p>
<p>In the United States, the experience of the so-called <a href="/wiki/Radium_Girls" title="Radium Girls">Radium Girls</a>, where thousands of radium-dial painters contracted oral cancers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_1998-10-06_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_1998-10-06-5">[5]</a></sup> (but no cases of acute radiation syndrome<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>), popularized the warnings of occupational health associated with radiation hazards. <a href="/wiki/Robley_D._Evans_(physicist)" title="Robley D. Evans (physicist)">Robley D. Evans</a>, at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIT" title="MIT">MIT</a>, developed the first standard for permissible body burden of <a href="/wiki/Radium" title="Radium">radium</a>, a key step in the establishment of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_medicine" title="Nuclear medicine">nuclear medicine</a> as a field of study. With the development of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a> in the 1940s, heightened scientific attention was given to the study of all manner of radiation effects.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Atomic_bombings_of_Hiroshima_and_Nagasaki" title="Atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki">atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki</a> resulted in a large number of incidents of radiation poisoning, allowing for greater insight into its symptoms and dangers. Red Cross Hospital Surgeon, Dr. Terufumi Sasaki led intensive research into the Syndrome in the weeks and months following the Hiroshima bombings. Dr Sasaki and his team were able to monitor the effects of radiation in patients of varying proximities to the blast itself, leading to the establishment of three recorded stages of the syndrome. Within 25–30 days of the explosion, the Red Cross surgeon noticed a sharp drop in white blood cell count and established this drop, along with symptoms of fever, as prognostic standards for Acute Radiation Syndrome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Actress <a href="/wiki/Midori_Naka" title="Midori Naka">Midori Naka</a>, who was present during the atomic bombing of Hiroshima, was the first incident of radiation poisoning to be extensively studied. Her death on August 24, 1945 was the first death ever to be officially certified as a result of radiation poisoning (or "Atomic bomb disease").</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Areas_of_interest">Areas of interest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiobiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Areas of interest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The interactions between organisms and electromagnetic fields (EMF) and ionizing radiation can be studied in a number of ways:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">Radiation physics</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Radiation_chemistry" title="Radiation chemistry">Radiation chemistry</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">molecular</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cell_biology" title="Cell biology">cell biology</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Molecular_genetics" title="Molecular genetics">Molecular genetics</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cell_death" title="Cell death">Cell death</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apoptosis" title="Apoptosis">apoptosis</a></li>
<li>Dose modifying agents</li>
<li>Protection and repair mechanisms</li>
<li>Tissue responses to radiation</li>
<li>Radio-adaptation of living organisms</li>
<li>High and low-level <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation_and_health" title="Electromagnetic radiation and health">electromagnetic radiation and health</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Specific_absorption_rate" title="Specific absorption rate">Specific absorption rates</a> of organisms</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_poisoning" title="Radiation poisoning">Radiation poisoning</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_oncology" title="Radiation oncology">Radiation oncology</a> (<a href="/wiki/Radiation_therapy" title="Radiation therapy">radiation therapy</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bioelectromagnetics" title="Bioelectromagnetics">Bioelectromagnetics</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">Electric field</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">Magnetic field</a> - their general nature.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Electrophysiology" title="Electrophysiology">Electrophysiology</a> - the scientific study of the electrical properties of biological cells and tissues.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Biomagnetism" title="Biomagnetism">Biomagnetism</a> - the magnetic properties of living systems (see, for example, the research of <a href="/wiki/David_Cohen_(physicist)" title="David Cohen (physicist)">David Cohen</a> using <a href="/wiki/SQUID" title="SQUID">SQUID</a> imaging) and <a href="/wiki/Magnetobiology" title="Magnetobiology">Magnetobiology</a> - the study of effect of magnets upon living systems. See also <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation_and_health" title="Electromagnetic radiation and health">Electromagnetic radiation and health</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioelectromagnetism" title="Bioelectromagnetism">Bioelectromagnetism</a> - the electromagnetic properties of living systems and <a href="/wiki/Bioelectromagnetics" title="Bioelectromagnetics">Bioelectromagnetics</a> - the study of the effect of electromagnetic fields on living systems.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Electrotherapy" title="Electrotherapy">Electrotherapy</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Radiation_therapy" title="Radiation therapy">Radiation therapy</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Radiogenomics" title="Radiogenomics">Radiogenomics</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Electroconvulsive_therapy" title="Electroconvulsive therapy">Electroconvulsive therapy</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Transcranial_magnetic_stimulation" title="Transcranial magnetic stimulation">Transcranial magnetic stimulation</a> - a powerful electric current produces a transient, spatially focussed magnetic field that can penetrate the scalp and skull of a subject and induce electrical activity in the neurons on the surface of the brain.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">Magnetic resonance imaging</a> - a very powerful magnetic field is used to obtain a 3D image of the density of water molecules of the brain, revealing different anatomical structures. A related technique, <a href="/wiki/Functional_magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Functional magnetic resonance imaging">functional magnetic resonance imaging</a>, reveals the pattern of blood flow in the brain and can show which parts of the brain are involved in a particular task.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Embryogenesis" title="Embryogenesis">Embryogenesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontogeny" title="Ontogeny">Ontogeny</a> and <a href="/wiki/Developmental_biology" title="Developmental biology">Developmental biology</a> - a discipline that has given rise to many scientific field theories.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bioenergetics" title="Bioenergetics">Bioenergetics</a> - the study of energy exchange on the molecular level of living systems.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Biological_psychiatry" title="Biological psychiatry">Biological psychiatry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neurology" title="Neurology">Neurology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychoneuroimmunology" title="Psychoneuroimmunology">Psychoneuroimmunology</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bioluminescence" title="Bioluminescence">Bioluminescence</a> - a marked phosphoresecence found in fungi, deep-sea creatures etc., as against <a href="/wiki/Biophoton" title="Biophoton">Biophoton</a> - a much weaker electromagnetic radiation, thought by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Gurwitsch" title="Alexander Gurwitsch">Alexander Gurwitsch</a>, its discoverer, to be a form of signalling.</li>
</ul>
<p>The activity of biological and astronomical systems inevitably generates magnetic and electrical fields, which can be measured with sensitive instruments and which have at times been suggested as a basis for "<a href="/wiki/Energy_(esotericism)" title="Energy (esotericism)">esoteric</a>" ideas of energy.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiation_sources_for_radiobiology">Radiation sources for radiobiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiobiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Radiation sources for radiobiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Radiobiology experiments typically make use of a radiation source which could be:</p>
<ul>
<li>An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive_isotope" title="Radioactive isotope">isotopic source</a>, typically <a href="/wiki/Caesium-137" title="Caesium-137"><sup>137</sup>Cs</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cobalt-60" title="Cobalt-60"><sup>60</sup>Co</a>.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerator</a> generating high energy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protons" title="Protons">protons</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrons" title="Electrons">electrons</a> or charged ions. Biological samples can be irradiated using either a broad, uniform beam<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> or using a <a href="/wiki/Microbeam" title="Microbeam">microbeam</a>, focused down to cellular or subcellular sizes.</li>
<li>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UV_lamp" title="UV lamp">UV lamp</a>.</li>
</ul>
