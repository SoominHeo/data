<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Direct-broadcast satellite</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>A <b>direct-broadcast satellite</b> (DBS) is a type of artificial <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a> which usually broadcasts <a href="/wiki/Satellite_television" title="Satellite television">satellite television</a> signals for home reception.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The type of satellite television which uses direct-broadcast satellites is known as <a href="/wiki/Direct-broadcast_satellite_television" title="Direct-broadcast satellite television">direct-broadcast satellite television</a> (DBSTV) or direct-to-home television (DTHTV).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The term "direct broadcast" is used to distinguish satellites which transmit radio or television signals directly to receivers in consumers' homes from other <a href="/wiki/Communications_satellite" title="Communications satellite">communications satellites</a> which transmit signals to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellite_ground_station" title="Satellite ground station">satellite ground stations</a>, for example those which distribute <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">cable television</a> signals to cable <a href="/wiki/Cable_television_headend" title="Cable television headend">head end</a> facilities. Since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DBS_receiver" title="DBS receiver">home satellite television receivers</a> use small <a href="/wiki/Satellite_dish" title="Satellite dish">dish antennas</a> less than a meter in diameter, DBS satellites must have much higher <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downlink" title="Downlink">downlink</a> transmitter power than other communication satellites, which are received by larger dishes from 2 up to 50 meters diameter.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, prior to the launch of <a href="/wiki/Astra_1A" title="Astra 1A">Astra 1A</a> in 1988, the term DBS was commonly used to describe the nationally commissioned satellites planned and launched to provide television broadcasts to the home within several European countries (such as <a href="/wiki/British_Satellite_Broadcasting" title="British Satellite Broadcasting">BSB</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and TV-Sat in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>). These services were to use the <a href="/wiki/D-MAC" title="D-MAC">D-Mac and D2-Mac</a> format and BSS frequencies with circular <a href="/wiki/Polarization_(waves)" title="Polarization (waves)">polarization</a> from orbital positions allocated to each country. Before these DBS satellites, home satellite television in Europe was limited to a few channels, really intended for cable distribution, and requiring dishes typically of 1.7 m.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/SES_S.A." title="SES S.A.">SES</a> launched the Astra 1A satellite to provide services to homes across Europe receivable on dishes of just 90 cm and, although these mostly used PAL video format and FSS frequencies with linear polarization, the DBS name slowly came to be applied to all Astra satellites and services too.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology_confusion">Terminology confusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Direct-broadcast_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology confusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a technical matter, DBS (also known by the International Telecommunication Union as Broadcasting Satellite Service, or BSS) refers only to services transmitted by satellite in specific frequency bands: 11.7-12.2 GHz in ITU Region 3 (<a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>), 10.7 - 12.75 GHz in ITU Region 1 (<a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>), and 12.2-12.7 GHz ITU Region 2 (<a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1977, the ITU adopted an international BSS Plan under which each country was allocated specific frequencies at specific orbital locations for domestic service. Over the years, this plan has been modified to, for example, accommodate new countries, increase coverage areas, and reflect digital (rather than analog) technology. At present, numerous countries have brought into use their BSS Plan allocations.</p>
<p>By contrast, the term DTH can apply to similar services transmitted over a wider range of frequencies (including standard <a href="/wiki/Ku_band" title="Ku band">K<sub>u</sub> band</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ka_band" title="Ka band">K<sub>a</sub> band</a>) transmitted from satellites that are not part of any internationally planned band. The term DBS is often used interchangeably with DTH to cover both analog and digital video and audio services (including <a href="/wiki/Video_on_demand" title="Video on demand">video on demand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interactive_television" title="Interactive television">interactive</a> features) received by relatively small dishes (less than 1 meter). A "DBS service" usually refers to either a commercial service, or a group of free channels available from one orbital position targeting one country. In certain regions of the world, especially in North America, DBS is used to refer to providers of subscription satellite packages, and has become applied to the entire equipment chain involved.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_services">Commercial services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Direct-broadcast_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Commercial services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Astro_satellite_dishes.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="360" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Astro_satellite_dishes.jpg/220px-Astro_satellite_dishes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Astro_satellite_dishes.jpg/330px-Astro_satellite_dishes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/53/Astro_satellite_dishes.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The second commercial DBS service, <a href="/wiki/Sky_Television_plc" title="Sky Television plc">Sky Television plc</a> (now <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Sky_Broadcasting" title="British Sky Broadcasting">BSkyB</a> after its merger with <a href="/wiki/British_Satellite_Broadcasting" title="British Satellite Broadcasting">British Satellite Broadcasting</a>'s five-channel network), was launched in 1989. Sky TV started as a four-<a href="/wiki/Channel_(communications)" title="Channel (communications)">channel</a> <a href="/wiki/Free-to-air" title="Free-to-air">free-to-air</a> analogue service on the <a href="/wiki/Astra_1A" title="Astra 1A">Astra 1A</a> <a href="/wiki/Communications_satellite" title="Communications satellite">satellite</a>, serving both <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> By 1991, Sky had changed to a <a href="/wiki/Conditional_access" title="Conditional access">conditional access</a> pay model, and it launched a digital service, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sky_Digital_(UK_%26_Ireland)" title="Sky Digital (UK &amp; Ireland)">Sky Digital</a>, in 1998, with analogue transmission ceasing in 2001. Since the DBS nomenclature is rarely used in the UK or Ireland, the popularity of Sky's service has caused the terms "<a href="/wiki/Minidish" title="Minidish">minidish</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Digibox" title="Digibox">digibox</a>" to be applied to products other than Sky's hardware. <a href="/wiki/News_Corporation" title="News Corporation">News Corporation</a> has a 32% stake in BSkyB.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/PrimeStar" title="PrimeStar">PrimeStar</a> began transmitting an analog service to North America in 1991, and was joined by <a href="/wiki/DirecTV" title="DirecTV">DirecTV</a> (then owned by a division of <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hughes_Aircraft_Company#GM_Hughes_Electronics" title="Hughes Aircraft Company">GM Hughes Electronics</a>), in 1994. At the time, DirecTV's introduction was the most successful <a href="/wiki/Consumer_electronics" title="Consumer electronics">consumer electronics</a> debut in <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> history. Although PrimeStar transitioned to a digital system in 1994, it was ultimately unable to compete with <a href="/wiki/DirecTV" title="DirecTV">DirecTV</a>, which required a smaller satellite dish and could deliver more programming. <a href="/wiki/DirecTV" title="DirecTV">DirecTV</a> purchased PrimeStar in 1999 and moved all of that provider's subscribers to DirecTV equipment. In a series of transactions consummated in 2003, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hughes_Electronics" title="Hughes Electronics">Hughes Electronics</a> was spun out of GM and the News Corporation purchased a controlling interest in the new company, which was renamed The DIRECTV Group. In 2008, <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Media" title="Liberty Media">Liberty Media Corporation</a> purchased News Corporation's controlling interest in DirecTV.</p>
<p>In 1996, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EchoStar_Communications_Corporation" title="EchoStar Communications Corporation">EchoStar</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Dish_Network" title="Dish Network">Dish Network</a> went online in the United States and, as DirecTV's primary competitor, achieved similar success. <a href="/wiki/AlphaStar" title="AlphaStar">AlphaStar</a> also started but soon went under. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astro_(satellite_television)" title="Astro (satellite television)">Astro</a> was also started, using a direct broadcast satellite system.</p>
<p>Dominion Video Satellite Inc.'s <a href="/wiki/Sky_Angel" title="Sky Angel">Sky Angel</a> launched on a satellite platform in the United States in 1996, with its DBS service geared toward the faith and family market. It grew from six to 36 television and radio channels of family entertainment, Christian-inspirational programming, and 24-hour news services. Dominion, under its former corporate name Video Satellite Systems Inc., was actually the second from among the first nine companies to apply to the FCC for a high-power DBS license in 1981, and it was the sole surviving DBS company from the first round of applicants until the sale of their license to EchoStar Communications Corporation in 2007 and departure from satellite distribution in 2008. Sky Angel, although a separate and independent DBS service, used the same satellites, transmission facilities and receiving equipment used for Dish Network through an agreement with Echostar. Because of this, Sky Angel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subscriber" title="Subscriber">subscribers</a> also had the option of subscribing to Dish Network's channels as well.</p>
<p>In 2003, EchoStar attempted to purchase DirecTV, but the FCC and U.S. Department of Justice denied the purchase based on anti-competitive concerns.</p>
<p>As of 2013, India has the most competitive direct-broadcast satellite market with seven operators (six private DTH and one government-owned FTA DTH) vying for more than 110 million television homes. Subscribers to India's six private direct-to-home (DTH) satellite television providers have now reached 56.5 million, according to the latest figures issued by the Telecom Regulations Authority of India (TRAI)on 31 March 2013.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.trai.gov.in/WriteReadData/PIRReport/Documents/Indicator%20Reports%20-01082013.pdf" rel="nofollow">[1]</a></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Free_services">Free services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Direct-broadcast_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Free services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> is likely the leader in <a href="/wiki/Free-to-air" title="Free-to-air">free-to-air</a> (FTA) DBS, with approximately 250 digital FTA channels (including 83 <a href="/wiki/High-definition_television" title="High-definition television">HDTV</a> channels and various regional channels) broadcast from the <a href="/wiki/Astra_19.2%C2%B0E" title="Astra 19.2°E">Astra 19.2°E</a> satellite constellation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> These are not marketed as a DBS service, but are received in approximately 18 million homes, as well as in any home using the <i><a href="/wiki/Sky_Deutschland" title="Sky Deutschland">Sky Deutschland</a></i> commercial DBS system. All German analogue satellite broadcasts ceased on April 30, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> has approximately 160 digital channels (including the regional variations of <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> channels, <a href="/wiki/ITV_(TV_network)" title="ITV (TV network)">ITV</a> channels, <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a> and <a href="/wiki/Channel_5_(UK)" title="Channel 5 (UK)">Channel 5</a>) are broadcast without encryption from the <a href="/wiki/Astra_28.2%C2%B0E" title="Astra 28.2°E">Astra 28.2°E</a> satellite constellation, and receivable on any <a href="/wiki/DVB-S" title="DVB-S">DVB-S</a>-compliant receiver (a <a href="/wiki/DVB-S2" title="DVB-S2">DVB-S2</a> receiver is required for certain high definition television services). Most of these channels are included within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sky_(United_Kingdom)" title="Sky (United Kingdom)">Sky</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EPG" title="EPG">EPG</a>, and an increasing number within the <a href="/wiki/Freesat" title="Freesat">Freesat</a> EPG.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>'s national broadcaster, <a href="/wiki/Doordarshan" title="Doordarshan">Doordarshan</a>, promotes a free-to-air DBS package as "<a href="/wiki/DD_Free_Dish" title="DD Free Dish">DD Free Dish</a>", which is provided as in-fill for the country's terrestrial transmission network. It is broadcast from <a href="/wiki/GSAT-15" title="GSAT-15">GSAT-15</a> at 93.5°E and contains about 80 FTA channels.</p>
<p>While originally launched as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Backhaul_(TV_industry)" title="Backhaul (TV industry)">backhaul</a> for their <a href="/wiki/Digital_terrestrial_television" title="Digital terrestrial television">digital terrestrial television</a> service, a large number of French channels are free-to-air on satellites at 5°W, and have recently been announced as being official in-fill for the DTT network.</p>
<p>In North America (United States, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>) there are over 80 FTA digital channels available on <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_19" title="Galaxy 19">Galaxy 19</a> (with the majority being ethnic or religious in nature). Other FTA satellites include <a href="/wiki/AMC-4" title="AMC-4">AMC-4</a>, <a href="/wiki/AMC-6" title="AMC-6">AMC-6</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_18" title="Galaxy 18">Galaxy 18</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Satmex" title="Satmex">Satmex</a> 5. A company called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GloryStar" title="GloryStar">GloryStar</a> promotes FTA religious broadcasters on <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_19" title="Galaxy 19">Galaxy 19</a>.</p>
