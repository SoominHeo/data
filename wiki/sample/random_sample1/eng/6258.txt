<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Dialect</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">This article is about dialects of spoken and written languages. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Dialect_(disambiguation)" title="Dialect (disambiguation)">Dialect (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>The term <b>dialect</b> (from Latin <i lang="la" xml:lang="la"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dialectus#Latin" title="wikt:dialectus">dialectus</a></i>, <i lang="la" xml:lang="la"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dialectos#Latin" title="wikt:dialectos">dialectos</a></i>, from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> word <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B4%CE%B9%CE%AC%CE%BB%CE%B5%CE%BA%CF%84%CE%BF%CF%82#Ancient_Greek" title="wikt:διάλεκτος">διάλεκτος</a></span>, <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration"><i>diálektos</i></span>, "discourse", from <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B4%CE%B9%CE%AC#Ancient_Greek" title="wikt:διά">διά</a></span>, <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration"><i>diá</i></span>, "through" and <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BB%CE%AD%CE%B3%CF%89#Ancient_Greek" title="wikt:λέγω">λέγω</a></span>, <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration"><i>légō</i></span>, "I speak") is used in two distinct ways to refer to two different types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linguistic" title="Linguistic">linguistic</a> phenomena:</p>
<ul>
<li>One usage—the more common among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linguist" title="Linguist">linguists</a>—refers to a <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">variety</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a> that is a characteristic of a particular group of the language's speakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Despite their differences, these varieties known as dialects are closely related and most often <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">mutually intelligible</a>, especially if close to one another on the <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">dialect continuum</a>. The term is applied most often to regional speech patterns, but a dialect may also be defined by other factors, such as <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">social class</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnicity" title="Ethnicity">ethnicity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A dialect that is associated with a particular <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">social class</a> can be termed a <a href="/wiki/Sociolect" title="Sociolect">sociolect</a>, a dialect that is associated with a particular <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnic group</a> can be termed as <a href="/wiki/Ethnolect" title="Ethnolect">ethnolect</a>, and a regional dialect may be termed a regiolect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> According to this definition, any variety of a given language constitutes "a dialect", including any <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">standard varieties</a>. In this case, the distinction between the "standard language", or the "standard" dialect of a particular language, and the "nonstandard" dialects of the same language is often <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arbitrary" title="Arbitrary">arbitrary</a> and based on social, political, cultural, or historical considerations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chao_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-chao-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lyons_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-lyons-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnson_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-johnson-6">[6]</a></sup> In a similar way, the definition of the terms "language" and "dialect" may also overlap and are often subject to debate, with the differentiation between the two classifications often grounded in arbitrary and/or sociopolitical motives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcworther_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcworther-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>The other usage of the term "dialect", often deployed in colloquial or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sociolinguistic" title="Sociolinguistic">sociolinguistic</a> settings, refers to a language that is <a href="/wiki/Social_stratification" title="Social stratification">socially subordinated</a> to a regional or national <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">standard language</a>, often historically <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Genetic_relationship_(linguistics)" title="Genetic relationship (linguistics)">genetically related</a> to the standard language, but not actually derived <i>from</i> the standard language. In other words, it is not an actual <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">variety</a> of the "standard language" or dominant language, but rather a separate, independently evolved but often distantly related language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In this sense, unlike in the first usage, the standard language would not itself be considered a "dialect", as it is the dominant language in a particular state or region, whether in terms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prestige_(linguistics)" title="Prestige (linguistics)">linguistic prestige</a>, social or political status, <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">official status</a>, predominance or prevalence, or all of the above. Meanwhile, under this usage, the "dialects" subordinate to the standard language are generally not variations on the standard language but rather separate (but often distantly related) languages in and of themselves. Thus, these "dialects" are not dialects or varieties of a particular language in the same sense as in the first usage; though they may share roots in the same <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">family</a> or subfamily as the standard language and may even, to varying degrees, share some <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">mutual intelligibility</a> with the standard language, they often did not evolve closely with the standard language or within the same linguistic subgroup or <a href="/wiki/Speech_community" title="Speech community">speech community</a> as the standard language and instead may better fit the criteria of a separate language.</li>
</ul>
<p>For example, most of the various regional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">Romance</a> <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Italy" title="Languages of Italy">languages of Italy</a>, often colloquially referred to as Italian "dialects," are, in fact, not <i>actually</i> derived from modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Italian" title="Standard Italian">standard Italian</a>, but rather evolved from <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">Vulgar Latin</a> separately and individually from one another and independently of standard Italian, long prior to the diffusion of a national standardized language throughout what is now <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. These various Latin-derived regional languages are therefore, in a linguistic sense, not truly "dialects" of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Italian" title="Standard Italian">standard Italian language</a>, but are instead better defined as their own separate languages. Conversely, with the spread of standard Italian throughout Italy in the 20th century and the increase in <a href="/wiki/Dialect_levelling" title="Dialect levelling">dialect levelling</a>, various regional versions or varieties of standard Italian developed, generally as a mix of the national standard Italian with local regional languages and local accents. These variations on standard Italian, known as <a href="/wiki/Regional_Italian" title="Regional Italian">regional Italian</a>, would more appropriately be called "dialects" in accordance with the first linguistic definition of "dialect," as they are in fact derived partially or mostly from standard Italian. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maiden_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-maiden-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-repetti_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-repetti-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>A dialect is distinguished by its vocabulary, grammar, and pronunciation (<a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonology</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Prosody_(linguistics)" title="Prosody (linguistics)">prosody</a>). Where a distinction can be made only in terms of pronunciation (including prosody, or just prosody itself), the term <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accent_(dialect)" title="Accent (dialect)">accent</a></i> may be preferred over <i>dialect</i>. Other types of speech varieties include <a href="/wiki/Jargon" title="Jargon">jargons</a>, which are characterized by differences in <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">lexicon</a> (<a href="/wiki/Vocabulary" title="Vocabulary">vocabulary</a>); <a href="/wiki/Slang" title="Slang">slang</a>; <a href="/wiki/Patois" title="Patois">patois</a>; <a href="/wiki/Pidgin" title="Pidgin">pidgins</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Argot" title="Argot">argots</a>.</p>
<p>The particular speech patterns used by an individual are termed an <a href="/wiki/Idiolect" title="Idiolect">idiolect</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_and_non-standard_dialect">Standard and non-standard dialect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Standard and non-standard dialect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <i>standard dialect</i> (also known as a <b>standardized dialect</b> or "<a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">standard language</a>") is a dialect that is supported by institutions. Such institutional support may include government recognition or designation; presentation as being the "correct" form of a language in schools; published grammars, dictionaries, and textbooks that set forth a correct spoken and written form; and an extensive formal literature that employs that dialect (prose, poetry, non-fiction, etc.). There may be multiple standard dialects associated with a single language. For example, Standard <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>, Standard <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>, Standard <a href="/wiki/Canadian_English" title="Canadian English">Canadian English</a>, Standard <a href="/wiki/Indian_English" title="Indian English">Indian English</a>, Standard <a href="/wiki/Australian_English" title="Australian English">Australian English</a>, and Standard <a href="/wiki/Philippine_English" title="Philippine English">Philippine English</a> may all be said to be standard dialects of the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a>.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Nonstandard_dialect" title="Nonstandard dialect">nonstandard dialect</a>, like a standard dialect, has a complete vocabulary, grammar, and syntax, but is usually not the beneficiary of institutional support. Examples of a nonstandard English dialect are <a href="/wiki/Southern_American_English" title="Southern American English">Southern American English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Australian_English" title="Western Australian English">Western Australian English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scouse" title="Scouse">Scouse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_dialect" title="Yorkshire dialect">Tyke</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Dialect_Test" title="Dialect Test">Dialect Test</a> was designed by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Wright_(linguist)" title="Joseph Wright (linguist)">Joseph Wright</a> to compare different English dialects with each other.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialect_or_language">Dialect or language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dialect or language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/A_language_is_a_dialect_with_an_army_and_navy" title="A language is a dialect with an army and navy">A language is a dialect with an army and navy</a></div>
<p>There is no universally accepted criterion for distinguishing two different languages from two dialects (i.e. varieties) of the same language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> A number of rough measures exist, sometimes leading to contradictory results. The distinction is therefore subjective and depends upon the user's frame of reference. For example, there has been discussion about whether or not the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lim%C3%B3n_Creole_English" title="Limón Creole English">Limón Creole English</a> should be considered "a kind" of English or a different language. This creole is spoken in the Caribbean coast of Costa Rica (Central America) by descendants of Jamaican people. The position that Costa Rican linguists support depends upon which University they represent.</p>
<p>The most common, and most purely linguistic, criterion is that of <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">mutual intelligibility</a>: two varieties are said to be dialects of the same language if being a speaker of one variety confers sufficient knowledge to understand and be understood by a speaker of the other; otherwise, they are said to be different languages. However, this definition becomes problematic in the case of <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">dialect continua</a>, in which it may be the case that dialect B is mutually intelligible with both dialect A and dialect C but dialects A and C are not mutually intelligible with each other. In this case the criterion of mutual intelligibility makes it impossible to decide whether A and C are dialects of the same language or not. The mutual intelligibility criterion also flounders in cases in which a speaker of dialect X can understand a speaker of dialect Y, but not vice versa.</p>
<p>Another occasionally used criterion for discriminating dialects from languages is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sociolinguistic" title="Sociolinguistic">sociolinguistic</a> notion of <a href="/wiki/Autonomy_and_heteronomy_(sociolinguistics)" title="Autonomy and heteronomy (sociolinguistics)">linguistic authority</a>. According to this definition, two varieties are considered dialects of the same language if (under at least some circumstances) they would defer to the same authority regarding some questions about their language. For instance, to learn the name of a new invention, or an obscure foreign species of plant, speakers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bavarian_German" title="Bavarian German">Bavarian German</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Franconian_German" title="East Franconian German">East Franconian German</a> might each consult a German dictionary or ask a German-speaking expert in the subject. By way of contrast, although Yiddish is classified by linguists as a language in the "Middle High German" group of languages, a Yiddish speaker would not consult a German dictionary to determine the word to use in such a case.</p>
<p>The framework of <a href="/wiki/Abstand_and_ausbau_languages" title="Abstand and ausbau languages">abstand and ausbau languages</a> was developed in 1967 by <a href="/wiki/Heinz_Kloss" title="Heinz Kloss">Heinz Kloss</a> to describe situations in which <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">standard varieties</a> are considered to define distinct languages even though they are mutually intelligible. The Continental <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scandinavian_languages" title="Scandinavian languages">Scandinavian languages</a> are usually cited as an example.</p>
<p>By the definition most commonly used by linguists, any linguistic variety can be considered a "dialect" of <i>some</i> language—"everybody speaks a dialect". According to that interpretation, the criteria above merely serve to distinguish whether two varieties are dialects of the <i>same</i> language or dialects of <i>different</i> languages.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology"><span id="languoid"></span>Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The terms "language" and "dialect" are not necessarily mutually exclusive, although it is often perceived to be.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Thus there is nothing contradictory in the statement "the <i>language</i> of the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Dutch" title="Pennsylvania Dutch">Pennsylvania Dutch</a> is a dialect of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_(language)" title="German (language)">German</a>".</p>
<p>There are various terms that linguists may use to avoid taking a position on whether the speech of a community is an independent language in its own right or a dialect of another language. Perhaps the most common is "<a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">variety</a>";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-finegan_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-finegan-13">[13]</a></sup> "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lect" title="Lect">lect</a>" is another. A more general term is "languoid", which does not distinguish between dialects, languages, and groups of languages, whether genealogically related or not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_factors">Political factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Political factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In many societies, however, a particular dialect, often the sociolect of the <a href="/wiki/Elite" title="Elite">elite</a> class, comes to be identified as the "standard" or "proper" version of a language by those seeking to make a social distinction, and is contrasted with other varieties. As a result of this, in some contexts the term "dialect" refers specifically to varieties with low <a href="/wiki/Social_status" title="Social status">social status</a>. In this secondary sense of "dialect", language varieties are often called <i>dialects</i> rather than <i>languages</i>:</p>
<ul>
<li>if they have no standard or <a href="/wiki/Codification_(linguistics)" title="Codification (linguistics)">codified</a> form,</li>
<li>if they are rarely or never used in writing (outside reported speech),</li>
<li>if the speakers of the given language do not have a <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a> of their own,</li>
<li>if they lack <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prestige_dialect" title="Prestige dialect">prestige</a> with respect to some other, often standardised, variety.</li>
</ul>
<p>The status of "language" is not solely determined by linguistic criteria, but it is also the result of a historical and political development. <a href="/wiki/Romansh_language" title="Romansh language">Romansh</a> came to be a written language, and therefore it is recognized as a language, even though it is very close to the Lombardic alpine dialects. An opposite example is the case of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>, whose variations such as <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yue_Chinese" title="Yue Chinese">Cantonese</a> are often called dialects and not languages, despite their mutual unintelligibility.</p>
<p>Modern <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">Nationalism</a>, as developed especially since the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>, has made the distinction between "language" and "dialect" an issue of great political importance. A group speaking a separate "language" is often seen as having a greater claim to being a separate "people", and thus to be more deserving of its own independent state, while a group speaking a "dialect" tends to be seen not as "a people" in its own right, but as a sub-group, part of a bigger people, which must content itself with regional autonomy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The distinction between language and dialect is thus inevitably made at least as much on a political basis as on a linguistic one, and can lead to great political controversy, or even armed conflict.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yiddish_language" title="Yiddish language">Yiddish</a> linguist <a href="/wiki/Max_Weinreich" title="Max Weinreich">Max Weinreich</a> published the expression, <i>A shprakh iz a dialekt mit an armey un flot</i> (<span dir="rtl" lang="yi" xml:lang="yi">"אַ שפּראַך איז אַ דיאַלעקט מיט אַן אַרמײ און פֿלאָט"</span>‎: "<a href="/wiki/A_language_is_a_dialect_with_an_army_and_navy" title="A language is a dialect with an army and navy">A language is a dialect with an army and navy</a>") in <i>YIVO Bleter</i> 25.1, 1945, p. 13. The significance of the political factors in any attempt at answering the question "what is a language?" is great enough to cast doubt on whether any strictly linguistic definition, without a socio-cultural approach, is possible. This is illustrated by the frequency with which the army-navy aphorism is cited.</p>
<p>See also: <i><a href="/wiki/Mesoamerican_languages#Language_vs._Dialect" title="Mesoamerican languages">Mesoamerican languages § Language vs. Dialect</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="German">German</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: German">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When talking about the German language, the term <a href="/wiki/German_dialects" title="German dialects">German dialects</a> is only used for the traditional regional varieties. That allows them to be distinguished from the regional varieties of modern standard German.</p>
<p>The German dialects show a wide spectrum of variation. Some of them are not mutually intelligible. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_dialectology" title="German dialectology">German dialectology</a> traditionally names the major dialect groups after <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic tribes</a> from which they were assumed to have descended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The extent to which the dialects are spoken varies according to a number of factors: In Northern Germany, dialects are less common than in the South. In cities, dialects are less common than on the countryside. In a public environment, dialects are less common than in a familiar environment.</p>
<p>The situation in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liechtenstein" title="Liechtenstein">Liechtenstein</a> is different from the rest of the German-speaking countries. The <a href="/wiki/Swiss_German" title="Swiss German">Swiss German</a> dialects are the default everyday language in virtually every situation, whereas standard German is seldom spoken. Some Swiss German speakers perceive standard German to be a foreign language.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Low_German" title="Low German">Low German</a> varieties spoken in Germany are often counted among the German dialects. This reflects the modern situation where they are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dachsprache" title="Dachsprache">roofed</a> by standard German. This is different from the situation in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> when Low German had strong tendencies towards an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ausbau_language" title="Ausbau language">ausbau language</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Frisian_languages" title="Frisian languages">Frisian</a> varieties spoken in Germany are often excluded from the German dialects.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Italian">Italian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Italian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Italy" title="Languages of Italy">Languages of Italy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Regional_Italian" title="Regional Italian">Regional Italian</a></div>
<p>Italy is home to a vast array of <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Italy" title="Languages of Italy">native regional minority languages</a>, most of which are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">Romance</a>-based and have their own local variants. These <a href="/wiki/Regional_language" title="Regional language">regional languages</a> are often referred to colloquially or in non-linguistic circles as Italian "dialects," or <i>dialetti</i> (standard Italian for "dialects"). However, the majority of the regional languages in Italy are in fact not actually "dialects" of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Italian" title="Standard Italian">standard Italian</a> in the strict linguistic sense, as they are not derived from modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Italian" title="Standard Italian">standard Italian</a> but instead evolved locally from <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">Vulgar Latin</a> independent of standard Italian, with little to no influence from what is now known as "standard Italian." They are therefore better classified as individual languages rather than "dialects."</p>
<p>In addition to having evolved, for the most part, separately from one another and with distinct individual histories, the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>-based regional Romance languages of Italy are also better classified as separate languages rather than true "dialects" due to the often high degree in which they lack <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">mutual intelligibility</a>. Though mostly mutually unintelligible, the exact degree to which the regional Italian languages are mutual unintelligible varies, often correlating with geographical distance or geographical barriers between the languages, with some regional Italian languages that are closer in geographical proximity to each other or closer to each other on the <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">dialect continuum</a> being more or less mutually intelligible. For instance, a speaker of purely <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Lombard_dialect" title="Eastern Lombard dialect">Eastern Lombard</a>, a language in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Italy" title="Northern Italy">Northern Italy</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">Lombardy region</a> that includes the <a href="/wiki/Bergamasque_dialect" title="Bergamasque dialect">Bergamasque dialect</a>, would have severely limited mutual intelligibility with a purely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Italian" title="Standard Italian">standard Italian</a> speaker and would be nearly completely unintelligible to a speaker of a pure <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">Sicilian language</a> variant. Due to Eastern Lombard's status as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic" title="Gallo-Italic">Gallo-Italic</a> language, an Eastern Lombard speaker may, in fact, have more mutual intelligibility with a <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">Occitan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">Catalan</a>, or <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> speaker than a standard Italian or Sicilian language speaker. Meanwhile, a Sicilian language speaker would have a greater degree of mutual intelligibility with a speaker of the more closely related <a href="/wiki/Neapolitan_language" title="Neapolitan language">Neapolitan language</a>, but far less mutual intelligibility with a person speaking <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic_of_Sicily" title="Gallo-Italic of Sicily">Sicilian Gallo-Italic</a>, a language that developed in isolated Lombard emigrant communities on the <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">same island</a> as the Sicilian language.</p>
<p>Modern standard Italian itself is heavily based on the Latin-derived <a href="/wiki/Florentine_dialect" title="Florentine dialect">Florentine</a> <a href="/wiki/Tuscan_dialect" title="Tuscan dialect">Tuscan language</a>. The Tuscan-based language that would eventually become modern standard Italian had been used in poetry and literature since at least the <a href="/wiki/12th_century" title="12th century">12th century</a>, and it first became widely known in Italy through the works of authors such as <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante Alighieri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Boccaccio" title="Giovanni Boccaccio">Giovanni Boccaccio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli" title="Niccolò Machiavelli">Niccolò Machiavelli</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">Petrarch</a>. Dante's Florentine-Tuscan literary Italian thus became the language of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literate" title="Literate">literate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Upper_class" title="Upper class">upper class</a> in Italy, and it spread throughout the peninsula as the <i><a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a></i> among the Italian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Educated" title="Educated">educated</a> class as well as Italian traveling merchants. The economic prowess and cultural and artistic importance of <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">Late Middle Ages</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> further encouraged the diffusion of the Florentine-Tuscan Italian throughout Italy and among the educated and powerful, though local and regional languages remained the main languages of the common people.</p>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Risorgimento" title="Risorgimento">Risorgimento</a>, proponents of Italian republicanism and Italian nationalism, such as <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Manzoni" title="Alessandro Manzoni">Alessandro Manzoni</a>, stressed the importance of establishing a uniform <a href="/wiki/National_language" title="National language">national language</a> in order to better create an Italian <a href="/wiki/National_identity" title="National identity">national identity</a>. With the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unification_of_Italy" title="Unification of Italy">unification of Italy</a> in the 1860s, standard Italian became the official national language of the new Italian state, while the various unofficial regional languages of Italy gradually became regarded as subordinate "dialects" to Italian, increasingly associated negatively with lack of education or provincialism. However, at the time of the Italian Unification, standard Italian still existed mainly as a literary language, and only 2.5% of Italy's population could speak standard Italian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early 20th century, the vast <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">conscription</a> of Italian men from all throughout Italy during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> is credited with facilitating the diffusion of standard Italian among less educated Italian men, as these men from various regions with various regional languages were forced to communicate with each other in a common tongue while serving in the Italian military. With the eventual spread of the radio and television throughout Italy and the establishment of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_education" title="Public education">public education</a>, Italians from all regions were increasingly exposed to standard Italian, while literacy rates among all social classes improved. Today, the majority of Italians are able to speak standard Italian, though many Italians still speak their regional language regularly or as their primary day-to-day language, especially at home with family or when communicating with Italians from the same town or region. However, to some Italians, speaking a regional language, especially in a formal setting or outside of one's region, may carry a stigma or negative connotations associated with being lower class, uneducated, boorish, or overly informal.</p>
<p>Italians in different regions today may also speak regional varieties of standard Italian, or <a href="/wiki/Regional_Italian" title="Regional Italian">regional Italian</a> dialects, which, unlike the majority of <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Italy" title="Languages of Italy">languages of Italy</a>, are actually dialects of standard Italian rather than separate languages. A regional Italian dialect is generally standard Italian that has been heavily influenced or mixed with local or regional native languages and accents.</p>
<p>The languages of Italy are primarily <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance languages</a>, with the most widely spoken languages falling within the <a href="/wiki/Italo-Dalmatian_languages" title="Italo-Dalmatian languages">Italo-Dalmatian language</a> family. This wide category includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Standard Italian and <a href="/wiki/Tuscan_dialect" title="Tuscan dialect">Tuscan</a>;</li>
<li>the various related <a href="/wiki/Central_Italian" title="Central Italian">Central Italian</a> languages, such as <a href="/wiki/Romanesco_dialect" title="Romanesco dialect">Romanesco</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>;</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Neapolitan_language" title="Neapolitan language">Neapolitan language</a> group (or "Southern Italian"), which encompasses not only the <i>napulitano</i> language of <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a> and <a href="/wiki/Campania" title="Campania">Campania</a> but also a variety of related neighboring variants such as the <a href="/wiki/Irpinian_dialect" title="Irpinian dialect">Irpinian dialect</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abruzzo" title="Abruzzo">Abruzzese</a> and Southern <a href="/wiki/Marche" title="Marche">Marchegiano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molisan" title="Molisan">Molisan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calabrian_language#Northern_Calabrian_.28Cosentino.29" title="Calabrian language">Northern Calabrian or Cosentino</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Bari_dialect" title="Bari dialect">Bari dialect</a>; and</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">Sicilian language</a> group, which includes the <a href="/wiki/Salentino_dialect" title="Salentino dialect">Salentino dialect</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calabrian_languages" title="Calabrian languages">Calabrian languages</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cilentan_dialect" title="Cilentan dialect">Cilentan dialect</a> of <a href="/wiki/Salerno" title="Salerno">Salerno</a> is considered significantly influenced by both the Neapolitan and Sicilian language groups.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_language" title="Sardinian language">Sardinian language</a> is considered to be its own Romance language family, separate not only from standard Italian but also the wider Italo-Dalmatian family, and it includes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Campidanese_Sardinian" title="Campidanese Sardinian">Campidanese Sardinian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Logudorese_dialect" title="Logudorese dialect">Logudorese Sardinian</a> variants. However, <a href="/wiki/Gallurese_dialect" title="Gallurese dialect">Gallurese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sassarese_language" title="Sassarese language">Sassarese</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Corsican_language" title="Corsican language">Corsican</a> are also spoken in <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a>, and these languages are considered closely related or derived from the Italian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuscan_language" title="Tuscan language">Tuscan language</a> and thus are Italo-Dalmatian languages. Furthermore, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallo-Romance" title="Gallo-Romance">Gallo-Romance</a> language of <a href="/wiki/Ligurian_(Romance_language)" title="Ligurian (Romance language)">Ligurian</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">Catalan</a> <a href="/wiki/Algherese_dialect" title="Algherese dialect">Algherese dialect</a> are also spoken in Sardinia.</p>
<p>Aside from the more common <a href="/wiki/Italo-Dalmatian_languages" title="Italo-Dalmatian languages">Italo-Dalmatian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">Romance languages</a> in Italy, other native languages in Italy include:</p>
<ul>
<li>a variety of <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic_languages" title="Gallo-Italic languages">Gallo-Italic languages</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallo-Rhaetian_languages" title="Gallo-Rhaetian languages">Gallo-Rhaetian languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhaeto-Romance_languages" title="Rhaeto-Romance languages">Rhaeto-Romance languages</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibero-Romance_languages" title="Ibero-Romance languages">Ibero-Romance languages</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Emilian-Romagnol_language" title="Emilian-Romagnol language">Emilian-Romagnol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ligurian_(Romance_language)" title="Ligurian (Romance language)">Ligurian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friulian" title="Friulian">Friulian</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lombard_language" title="Lombard language">Lombard languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic_of_Sicily" title="Gallo-Italic of Sicily">Sicilian Gallo-Italic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algherese_dialect" title="Algherese dialect">Algherese</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Vivaro-Alpine_dialect" title="Vivaro-Alpine dialect">Vivaro-Alpine dialect</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ladin_language" title="Ladin language">Ladin</a>, etc.);</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_language" title="Germanic language">Germanic</a> languages, such as the <a href="/wiki/Cimbrian_language" title="Cimbrian language">Cimbrian languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southern_Bavarian" title="Southern Bavarian">Southern Bavarian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walser_German" title="Walser German">Walser German</a> and the <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B2cheno_language" title="Mòcheno language">Mòcheno language</a>;</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">Albanian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arb%C3%ABresh_language" title="Arbëresh language">Arbëresh language</a>;</li>
<li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenic_language" title="Hellenic language">Hellenic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Griko_language" title="Griko language">Griko language</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calabrian_Greek" title="Calabrian Greek">Calabrian Greek</a>;</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian" title="Serbo-Croatian">Serbo-Croatian</a> <a href="/wiki/Slavomolisano_dialect" title="Slavomolisano dialect">Slavomolisano dialect</a>; and</li>
<li>various <a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">Slovene languages</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Gail_Valley_dialect" title="Gail Valley dialect">Gail Valley dialect</a> and <a href="/wiki/Istrian_dialect" title="Istrian dialect">Istrian dialect</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Balkans">The Balkans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Balkans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The classification of speech varieties as dialects or languages and their relationship to other varieties of speech can be controversial and the verdicts inconsistent. English and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian_language" title="Serbo-Croatian language">Serbo-Croatian</a> illustrate the point. English and Serbo-Croatian each have two major variants (<a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">Serbian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">Croatian</a>, respectively), along with numerous other varieties. For political reasons, analyzing these varieties as "languages" or "dialects" yields inconsistent results: British and American English, spoken by close political and military allies, are almost universally regarded as dialects of a single language, whereas the standard languages of <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>, which differ from each other to a similar extent as the dialects of English, are being treated by some linguists from the region as distinct languages, largely because the two countries oscillate from being brotherly to being bitter enemies. (The <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian" title="Serbo-Croatian">Serbo-Croatian language article</a> deals with this topic much more fully.)</p>
<p>Similar examples abound. <a href="/wiki/Macedonian_language" title="Macedonian language">Macedonian</a>, although mutually intelligible with <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a>, certain dialects of Serbian and to a lesser extent the rest of the <a href="/wiki/South_Slavic_languages" title="South Slavic languages">South Slavic</a> <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">dialect continuum</a>, is considered by Bulgarian linguists to be a Bulgarian dialect, in contrast with the contemporary international view and the view in the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Macedonia" title="Republic of Macedonia">Republic of Macedonia</a>, which regards it as a language in its own right. Nevertheless, before the establishment of a literary standard of Macedonian in 1944, in most sources in and out of Bulgaria before the Second World War, the southern Slavonic dialect continuum covering the area of today's Republic of Macedonia were referred to as Bulgarian dialects.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lebanon">Lebanon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lebanon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, a part of the Christian population considers "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lebanese_language" title="Lebanese language">Lebanese</a>" to be in some sense a distinct language from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> and not merely a dialect. During the civil war Christians often used Lebanese Arabic officially, and sporadically used the <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin script</a> to write Lebanese, thus further distinguishing it from Arabic. All Lebanese laws are written in the standard literary form of Arabic, though parliamentary debate may be conducted in Lebanese Arabic.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_Africa">North Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: North Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Tunisia, Algeria, and Morocco, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darija" title="Darija">Darijas</a> (spoken North African languages) are sometimes considered more different from other Arabic dialects. Officially, North African countries prefer to give preference to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literary_Arabic" title="Literary Arabic">Literary Arabic</a> and conduct much of their political and religious life in it (adherence to <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>), and refrain from declaring each country's specific variety to be a separate language, because Literary Arabic is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liturgical_language" title="Liturgical language">liturgical language</a> of Islam and the language of the Islamic sacred book, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">Qur'an</a>. Although, especially since the 1960s, the Darijas are occupying an increasing use and influence in the cultural life of these countries. Examples of cultural elements where Darijas' use became dominant include: theatre, film, music, television, advertisement, social media, folk-tale books and companies' names.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ukraine">Ukraine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ukraine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 19th century, the <a href="/wiki/Tsar" title="Tsar">Tsarist</a> Government of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a> claimed that <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">Ukrainian</a> was merely a dialect of <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a> and not a language on its own. The differences were few and caused by the conquest of western Ukraine by the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth. However, the dialects in Ukraine eventually differed substantially from the dialects in Russia.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a> <a href="/wiki/Ukraine_during_World_War_I" title="Ukraine during World War I">conquered Ukraine</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and was planning on either annexing it or installing a puppet king, but was defeated by the <a href="/wiki/Triple_Entente" title="Triple Entente">Entente</a>, with major involvement by the Ukrainian Bolsheviks. After conquering the rest of Ukraine from the <a href="/wiki/White_movement" title="White movement">Whites</a>, Ukraine joined the USSR and was enlarged (gaining Crimea and then Eastern Galicia), whence a process of Ukrainization was begun, with encouragement from Moscow.</p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, due to <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_collaborationism_with_the_Axis_powers" title="Ukrainian collaborationism with the Axis powers">Ukrainian collaborationism with the Axis powers</a> in an attempt to gain independence, Moscow changed its policy towards repression of the Ukrainian language.</p>
<p>Today the boundaries of the Ukrainian language to the Russian language are still not drawn clearly, with an intermediate dialect between them, called <a href="/wiki/Surzhyk" title="Surzhyk">Surzhyk</a>, developing in Ukraine.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Moldova">Moldova</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Moldova">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There have been cases of a variety of speech being deliberately reclassified to serve political purposes. One example is <a href="/wiki/Moldovan_language" title="Moldovan language">Moldovan</a>. In 1996, the Moldovan parliament, citing fears of "Romanian expansionism", rejected a proposal from <a href="/wiki/President_of_Moldova" title="President of Moldova">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Mircea_Snegur" title="Mircea Snegur">Mircea Snegur</a> to change the name of the language to Romanian, and in 2003 a <a href="/wiki/Moldovan%E2%80%93Romanian_dictionary" title="Moldovan–Romanian dictionary">Moldovan–Romanian dictionary</a> was published, purporting to show that the two countries speak different languages. Linguists of the <a href="/wiki/Romanian_Academy" title="Romanian Academy">Romanian Academy</a> reacted by declaring that all the Moldovan words were also Romanian words; while in Moldova, the head of the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Sciences_of_Moldova" title="Academy of Sciences of Moldova">Academy of Sciences of Moldova</a>, Ion Bărbuţă, described the dictionary as a politically motivated "absurdity".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greater_China">Greater China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Greater China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Chinese#Classification" title="Varieties of Chinese">Varieties of Chinese § Classification</a></div>
<p>Unlike languages that use alphabets to indicate the pronunciation, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">Chinese characters</a> have developed from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logograms" title="Logograms">logograms</a> that do not always give hints to their pronunciation. Although the written characters remained relatively consistent for the last two thousand years, the pronunciation and grammar in different regions has developed to an extent that the <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Chinese" title="Varieties of Chinese">varieties of the spoken language</a> are often mutually unintelligible. As a series of migration to the south throughout the history, the regional languages of the south, including <a href="/wiki/Xiang_Chinese" title="Xiang Chinese">Xiang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wu_Chinese" title="Wu Chinese">Wu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gan_Chinese" title="Gan Chinese">Gan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Min_Chinese" title="Min Chinese">Min</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yue_Chinese" title="Yue Chinese">Yue (Cantonese)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hakka_Chinese" title="Hakka Chinese">Hakka</a> often show traces of <a href="/wiki/Old_Chinese" title="Old Chinese">Old Chinese</a> or <a href="/wiki/Middle_Chinese" title="Middle Chinese">Middle Chinese</a>. From the <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a> onward, Beijing has been the capital of China and the dialect spoken in Beijing has had the most prestige among other varieties. With the founding of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_China_(1912%E2%80%9349)" title="Republic of China (1912–49)">Republic of China</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Mandarin" title="Standard Mandarin">Standard Mandarin</a> was designated as the official language, based on the spoken language of Beijing. Since then, other spoken varieties are regarded as <i>fangyan</i> (dialects). <a href="/wiki/Yue_Chinese" title="Yue Chinese">Cantonese</a> is still the most commonly used language in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a> and among some overseas Chinese communities, whereas <a href="/wiki/Southern_Min" title="Southern Min">Southern Min</a> has been accepted in <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> as an important local language along with <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_linguistics">Historical linguistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Historical linguistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguistics" title="Historical linguistics">historical linguists</a> view any speech form as a dialect of the older medium of communication from which it developed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This point of view sees the modern <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance languages</a> as dialects of <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, modern <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> as a dialect of Ancient Greek, <a href="/wiki/Tok_Pisin" title="Tok Pisin">Tok Pisin</a> as a dialect of English, <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages" title="North Germanic languages">North Germanic</a> as dialects of <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Belarusian_language" title="Belarusian language">Belarusian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rusyn_language" title="Rusyn language">Rusyn</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">Ukrainian</a> as dialects of <a href="/wiki/Ruthenian_language" title="Ruthenian language">Ruthenian</a>. This paradigm is not entirely problem-free. It sees genetic relationships as paramount: the "dialects" of a "language" (which itself may be a "dialect" of a yet older language) may or may not be mutually intelligible. Moreover, a parent language may spawn several "dialects" which themselves subdivide any number of times, with some "branches" of the tree changing more rapidly than others.</p>
<p>This can give rise to the situation in which two dialects (defined according to this paradigm) with a somewhat distant genetic relationship are mutually more readily comprehensible than more closely related dialects. In one opinion, this pattern is clearly present among the modern Romance languages, with <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> having a high degree of mutual comprehensibility, which neither language shares with <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, despite some claiming that both languages are <i>genetically</i> closer to French than to each other:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In fact, French-Italian and French-Spanish relative mutual incomprehensibility is due to French having undergone more rapid and more pervasive phonological change than have Spanish and Italian, not to real or imagined distance in genetic relationship. In fact, Italian and French share many more root words in common that do not even appear in Spanish.</p>
<p>For example, the Italian and French words for various foods, some family relationships, and body parts are very similar to each other, yet most of those words are completely different in Spanish. Italian "avere" and "essere" as auxiliaries for forming compound tenses are used similarly to French "avoir" and "être". Spanish only retains "haber" and has done away with "ser" in forming compound tenses. However, when it comes to phonological structures, Italian and Spanish have undergone less change than French, with the result that some native speakers of Italian and Spanish may attain a degree of mutual comprehension that permits extensive communication.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interlingua">Interlingua</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Interlingua">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Interlingua" title="Interlingua">Interlingua</a></div>
<p>One language, <a href="/wiki/Interlingua" title="Interlingua">Interlingua</a>, was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interlingua_and_eligibility_of_international_words" title="Interlingua and eligibility of international words">developed</a> so that the languages of Western civilization would act as its dialects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coljld_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-coljld-17">[17]</a></sup> Drawing from such concepts as the <a href="/wiki/International_scientific_vocabulary" title="International scientific vocabulary">international scientific vocabulary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Standard_Average_European" title="Standard Average European">Standard Average European</a>, linguists<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (July 2013)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> developed a theory that the modern Western languages were actually dialects of a hidden or latent language.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Researchers at the <a href="/wiki/International_Auxiliary_Language_Association" title="International Auxiliary Language Association">International Auxiliary Language Association</a> extracted words and affixes that they considered to be part of Interlingua's vocabulary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jawmqc_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-jawmqc-18">[18]</a></sup> In theory, speakers of the Western languages would understand written or spoken Interlingua immediately, without prior study, since their own languages were its dialects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coljld_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-coljld-17">[17]</a></sup> This has often turned out to be true, especially, but not solely, for speakers of the Romance languages and educated speakers of English. Interlingua has also been found to assist in the learning of other languages. In one study, Swedish high school students learning Interlingua were able to translate passages from Spanish, Portuguese, and Italian that students of those languages found too difficult to understand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> It should be noted, however, that the vocabulary of Interlingua extends beyond the Western language families.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jawmqc_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-jawmqc-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
