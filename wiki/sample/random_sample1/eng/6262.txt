<p>The <b>emission spectrum</b> of a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> is the spectrum of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequencies" title="Frequencies">frequencies</a> of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> emitted due to an <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a> or molecule making a <a href="/wiki/Atomic_electron_transition" title="Atomic electron transition">transition</a> from a high energy state to a lower energy state. The <a href="/wiki/Photon_energy" title="Photon energy">photon energy</a> of the emitted <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> is equal to the energy difference between the two states. There are many possible electron transitions for each atom, and each transition has a specific energy difference. This collection of different transitions, leading to different radiated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavelengths" title="Wavelengths">wavelengths</a>, make up an emission spectrum. Each element's emission spectrum is unique. Therefore, <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopy</a> can be used to identify the elements in matter of unknown composition. Similarly, the emission spectra of molecules can be used in chemical analysis of substances.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emission">Emission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emission_spectrum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Emission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, emission is the process by which a higher energy quantum mechanical state of a particle becomes converted to a lower one through the emission of a <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a>, resulting in the production of <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a>. The frequency of light emitted is a function of the energy of the transition. Since energy must be conserved, the energy difference between the two states equals the energy carried off by the photon. The energy states of the transitions can lead to emissions over a very large range of frequencies. For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">visible light</a> is emitted by the coupling of electronic states in atoms and molecules (then the phenomenon is called <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence" title="Fluorescence">fluorescence</a> or <a href="/wiki/Phosphorescence" title="Phosphorescence">phosphorescence</a>). On the other hand, nuclear shell transitions can emit high energy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_rays" title="Gamma rays">gamma rays</a>, while nuclear spin transitions emit low energy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_waves" title="Radio waves">radio waves</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Beam_emittance" title="Beam emittance">emittance</a> of an object quantifies how much light is emitted by it. This may be related to other properties of the object through the <a href="/wiki/Stefan%E2%80%93Boltzmann_law" title="Stefan–Boltzmann law">Stefan–Boltzmann law</a>. For most substances, the amount of emission varies with the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopic composition</a> of the object, leading to the appearance of <a href="/wiki/Color_temperature" title="Color temperature">color temperature</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_lines" title="Emission lines">emission lines</a>. Precise measurements at many wavelengths allow the identification of a substance via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_spectroscopy" title="Emission spectroscopy">emission spectroscopy</a>.</p>
<p>Emission of radiation is typically described using semi-classical quantum mechanics: the particle's energy levels and spacings are determined from <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>, and light is treated as an oscillating electric field that can drive a transition if it is in resonance with the system's natural frequency. The quantum mechanics problem is treated using time-dependent <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_theory" title="Perturbation theory">perturbation theory</a> and leads to the general result known as <a href="/wiki/Fermi%27s_golden_rule" title="Fermi's golden rule">Fermi's golden rule</a>. The description has been superseded by <a href="/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics" title="Quantum electrodynamics">quantum electrodynamics</a>, although the semi-classical version continues to be more useful in most practical computations.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emission_spectrum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrons" title="Electrons">electrons</a> in the atom are excited, for example by being heated, the additional <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> pushes the electrons to higher energy orbitals. When the electrons fall back down and leave the excited state, energy is re-emitted in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a>. The wavelength (or equivalently, frequency) of the photon is determined by the difference in energy between the two states. These emitted photons form the element's spectrum.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiation_from_molecules">Radiation from molecules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emission_spectrum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Radiation from molecules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As well as the electronic transitions discussed above, the energy of a molecule can also change via <a href="/wiki/Rotational_transition" title="Rotational transition">rotational</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vibrational_transition" title="Vibrational transition">vibrational</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vibronic_transition" title="Vibronic transition">vibronic</a> (combined vibrational and electronic) transitions. These energy transitions often lead to closely spaced groups of many different spectral lines, known as <a href="/wiki/Spectral_bands" title="Spectral bands">spectral bands</a>. Unresolved band spectra may appear as a spectral continuum.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emission_spectroscopy">Emission spectroscopy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emission_spectrum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Emission spectroscopy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Light consists of electromagnetic radiation of different wavelengths. Therefore, when the elements or their compounds are heated either on a flame or by an electric arc they emit energy in the form of light. Analysis of this light, with the help of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscope" title="Spectroscope">spectroscope</a> gives us a discontinuous spectrum. A spectroscope or a spectrometer is an instrument which is used for separating the components of light, which have different wavelengths. The spectrum appears in a series of lines called the line spectrum. This line spectrum is called an atomic spectrum when it originates from an atom in elemental form. Each element has a different atomic spectrum. The production of line spectra by the atoms of an element indicate that an atom can radiate only a certain amount of energy. This leads to the conclusion that bound electrons cannot have just any amount of energy but only a certain amount of energy.</p>
<p>The emission spectrum can be used to determine the composition of a material, since it is different for each <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">element</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a>. One example is <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy" title="Astronomical spectroscopy">astronomical spectroscopy</a>: identifying the composition of <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> by analysing the received light. The emission spectrum characteristics of some elements are plainly visible to the naked eye when these elements are heated. For example, when platinum wire is dipped into a <a href="/wiki/Strontium" title="Strontium">strontium</a> nitrate solution and then inserted into a flame, the strontium atoms emit a red color. Similarly, when <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> is inserted into a flame, the flame becomes green. These definite characteristics allow elements to be identified by their atomic emission spectrum. Not all emitted lights are perceptible to the naked eye, as the spectrum also includes ultraviolet rays and infrared lighting. An emission is formed when an excited gas is viewed directly through a spectroscope.</p>

<p><b>Emission spectroscopy</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopic</a> technique which examines the wavelengths of <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> emitted by atoms or molecules during their transition from an <a href="/wiki/Excited_state" title="Excited state">excited state</a> to a lower energy state. Each element emits a characteristic set of discrete wavelengths according to its <a href="/wiki/Electronic_structure" title="Electronic structure">electronic structure</a>, and by observing these wavelengths the elemental composition of the sample can be determined. Emission spectroscopy developed in the late 19th century and efforts in theoretical explanation of atomic emission spectra eventually led to <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>.</p>
<p>There are many ways in which atoms can be brought to an excited state. Interaction with electromagnetic radiation is used in <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence_spectroscopy" title="Fluorescence spectroscopy">fluorescence spectroscopy</a>, protons or other heavier particles in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particle-Induced_X-ray_Emission" title="Particle-Induced X-ray Emission">Particle-Induced X-ray Emission</a> and electrons or X-ray photons in <a href="/wiki/Energy-dispersive_X-ray_spectroscopy" title="Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy">Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy</a> or <a href="/wiki/X-ray_fluorescence" title="X-ray fluorescence">X-ray fluorescence</a>. The simplest method is to heat the sample to a high temperature, after which the excitations are produced by collisions between the sample atoms. This method is used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flame_emission_spectroscopy" title="Flame emission spectroscopy">flame emission spectroscopy</a>, and it was also the method used by <a href="/wiki/Anders_Jonas_%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Anders Jonas Ångström">Anders Jonas Ångström</a> when he discovered the phenomenon of discrete emission lines in the 1850s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Although the emission lines are caused by a transition between quantized energy states and may at first look very sharp, they do have a finite width, i.e. they are composed of more than one wavelength of light. This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectral_line_broadening" title="Spectral line broadening">spectral line broadening</a> has many different causes.</p>
<p>Emission spectroscopy is often referred to as <b>optical emission spectroscopy</b> because of the light nature of what is being emitted.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emission_spectrum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Emission lines from hot gases were first discovered<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> by <a href="/wiki/Anders_Jonas_%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Anders Jonas Ångström">Ångström</a>, and the technique was further developed by <a href="/wiki/David_Alter" title="David Alter">David Alter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Kirchhoff" title="Gustav Kirchhoff">Gustav Kirchhoff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bunsen" title="Robert Bunsen">Robert Bunsen</a>.</p>
<p>See the <a href="/wiki/History_of_spectroscopy" title="History of spectroscopy">history of spectroscopy</a> for details.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_technique_in_flame_emission_spectroscopy">Experimental technique in flame emission spectroscopy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emission_spectrum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Experimental technique in flame emission spectroscopy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The solution containing the relevant substance to be analysed is drawn into the burner and dispersed into the flame as a fine spray. The solvent evaporates first, leaving finely divided <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a> particles which move to the hottest region of the flame where gaseous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atoms" title="Atoms">atoms</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ions" title="Ions">ions</a> are produced. Here <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrons" title="Electrons">electrons</a> are excited as described above. It is common for a <a href="/wiki/Monochromator" title="Monochromator">monochromator</a> to be used to allow for easy detection.</p>
<p>On a simple level, flame emission spectroscopy can be observed using just a <a href="/wiki/Flame" title="Flame">flame</a> and samples of metal salts. This method of qualitative analysis is called a <a href="/wiki/Flame_test" title="Flame test">flame test</a>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> salts placed in the flame will glow yellow from sodium ions, while <a href="/wiki/Strontium" title="Strontium">strontium</a> (used in road flares) ions color it red. <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">Copper</a> wire will create a blue colored flame, however in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a> gives green (molecular contribution by CuCl).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emission_coefficient">Emission coefficient</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emission_spectrum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Emission coefficient">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Emission coefficient</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Coefficient" title="Coefficient">coefficient</a> in the power output per unit time of an <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic</a> source, a calculated value in <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>. The emission coefficient of a gas varies with the <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> of the light. It has units of ms<sup>−3</sup>sr<sup>−1</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is also used as a measure of <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environmental</a> emissions (by mass) per MWh of <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">electricity generated</a>, see: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_factor" title="Emission factor">Emission factor</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scattering_of_light">Scattering of light</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emission_spectrum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Scattering of light">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Thomson_scattering" title="Thomson scattering">Thomson scattering</a> a charged particle emits radiation under incident light. The particle may be an ordinary atomic electron, so emission coefficients have practical applications.</p>
<p>If <i><b>X</b></i> d<i>V</i> dΩ dλ is the energy scattered by a volume element d<i>V</i> into solid angle dΩ between wavelengths λ and λ+dλ per unit time then the Emission <a href="/wiki/Coefficient" title="Coefficient">coefficient</a> is <i><b>X</b></i>.</p>
<p>The values of <i><b>X</b></i> in Thomson scattering can be <a href="/wiki/Thomson_scattering" title="Thomson scattering">predicted</a> from incident flux, the density of the charged particles and their Thomson differential cross section (area/solid angle).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spontaneous_emission">Spontaneous emission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emission_spectrum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Spontaneous emission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A warm body emitting <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monochromatic" title="Monochromatic">monochromatic</a> emission coefficient relating to its temperature and total power radiation. This is sometimes called the second <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Einstein_coefficient" title="Einstein coefficient">Einstein coefficient</a>, and can be deduced from <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanical theory</a>.</p>
