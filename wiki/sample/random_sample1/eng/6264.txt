<p>In <a href="/wiki/Computing" title="Computing">computing</a>, a <b>firewall</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Network_security" title="Network security">network security</a> system that monitors and controls the incoming and outgoing network traffic based on predetermined security rules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> A firewall typically establishes a barrier between a trusted, secure internal network and another outside network, such as the Internet, that is assumed not to be secure or trusted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oppliger_1997_94_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oppliger_1997_94-2">[2]</a></sup> Firewalls are often categorized as either <i>network firewalls</i> or <i>host-based firewalls</i>. Network firewalls filter traffic between two or more networks; they are either <a href="/wiki/Software_appliance" title="Software appliance">software appliances</a> running on general purpose hardware, or hardware-based <a href="/wiki/Computer_appliance#Types_of_appliances" title="Computer appliance">firewall computer appliances</a>. Host-based firewalls provide a layer of software on one host that controls network traffic in and out of that single machine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Firewall appliances may also offer other functionality to the internal network they protect, such as acting as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DHCP" title="DHCP">DHCP</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VPN" title="VPN">VPN</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> server for that network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firewall_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i><a href="/wiki/Firewall_(construction)" title="Firewall (construction)">firewall</a></i> originally referred to a wall intended to confine a fire or potential fire within a building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Later uses refer to similar structures, such as the metal sheet separating the engine compartment of a vehicle or aircraft from the passenger compartment.</p>
<p>Firewall technology emerged in the late 1980s when the Internet was a fairly new technology in terms of its global use and connectivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The predecessors to firewalls for network security were the routers used in the late 1980s:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report_unm_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-report_unm-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Clifford_Stoll" title="Clifford Stoll">Clifford Stoll</a>'s discovery of German spies tampering with his system<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report_unm_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-report_unm-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/William_Cheswick" title="William Cheswick">Bill Cheswick</a>'s "Evening with Berferd" 1992, in which he set up a simple electronic "jail" to observe an attacker<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report_unm_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-report_unm-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 1988, an employee at the <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> <a href="/wiki/Ames_Research_Center" title="Ames Research Center">Ames Research Center</a> in California sent a memo by <a href="/wiki/Email" title="Email">email</a> to his colleagues<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> that read, "We are currently under attack from an <a href="/wiki/Computer_virus" title="Computer virus">Internet VIRUS</a>! It has hit <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">Berkeley</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UC_San_Diego" title="UC San Diego">UC San Diego</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Livermore_National_Laboratory" title="Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory">Lawrence Livermore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ames_Research_Center" title="Ames Research Center">NASA Ames</a>."</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morris_Worm" title="Morris Worm">Morris Worm</a> spread itself through multiple vulnerabilities in the machines of the time. Although it was not malicious in intent, the Morris Worm was the first large scale attack on Internet security; the online community was neither expecting an attack nor prepared to deal with one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_generation:_packet_filters">First generation: packet filters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firewall_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: First generation: packet filters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gufw_10.04.4.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="382" data-file-width="355" height="237" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Gufw_10.04.4.png/220px-Gufw_10.04.4.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Gufw_10.04.4.png/330px-Gufw_10.04.4.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/ba/Gufw_10.04.4.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gufw_10.04.4.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Screenshot of <a href="/wiki/Uncomplicated_Firewall#GUIs_for_Uncomplicated_Firewall" title="Uncomplicated Firewall">Gufw</a>: The firewall shows its settings for incoming and outgoing traffic.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first type of firewall was the packet filter which looks at <a href="/wiki/Network_address" title="Network address">network addresses</a> and ports of the packet and determines if that packet should be allowed or blocked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The first paper published on firewall technology was in 1988, when engineers from <a href="/wiki/Digital_Equipment_Corporation" title="Digital Equipment Corporation">Digital Equipment Corporation</a> (DEC) developed filter systems known as packet filter firewalls. This fairly basic system was the first generation of what is now a highly involved and technical internet security feature. At <a href="/wiki/Bell_Labs" title="Bell Labs">AT&amp;T Bell Labs</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Cheswick" title="William Cheswick">Bill Cheswick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steven_M._Bellovin" title="Steven M. Bellovin">Steve Bellovin</a> were continuing their research in packet filtering and developed a working model for their own company based on their original first generation architecture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Packet filters act by inspecting the "packets" which are transferred between computers on the Internet. If a packet does not match the packet filter's set of filtering rules, the packet filter will drop (silently discard) the packet or reject it (discard it, and send "error responses" to the source). Conversely, if the packet matches one or more of the programmed filters, the packet is allowed to pass. This type of packet filtering pays no attention to whether a packet is part of an existing stream of traffic (i.e. it stores no information on connection "state"). Instead, it filters each packet based only on information contained <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPv4_Header" title="IPv4 Header">in the packet itself</a> (most commonly using a combination of the packet's source and destination address, its protocol, and, for <a href="/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol" title="Transmission Control Protocol">TCP</a> and <a href="/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol" title="User Datagram Protocol">UDP</a> traffic, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TCP_and_UDP_port" title="TCP and UDP port">port number</a>). TCP and UDP protocols constitute most communication over the Internet, and because TCP and UDP traffic by convention uses <a href="/wiki/List_of_TCP_and_UDP_port_numbers" title="List of TCP and UDP port numbers">well known ports</a> for particular types of traffic, a "stateless" packet filter can distinguish between, and thus control, those types of traffic (such as web browsing, remote printing, email transmission, file transfer), unless the machines on each side of the packet filter are both using the same non-standard ports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Packet filtering firewalls work mainly on the first three layers of the <a href="/wiki/OSI_model" title="OSI model">OSI reference model</a>, which means most of the work is done between the network and physical layers, with a little bit of peeking into the transport layer to figure out source and destination port numbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> When a packet originates from the sender and filters through a firewall, the device checks for matches to any of the packet filtering rules that are configured in the firewall and drops or rejects the packet accordingly. When the packet passes through the firewall, it filters the packet on a protocol/port number basis (GSS). For example, if a rule in the firewall exists to block telnet access, then the firewall will block the TCP protocol for port number 23.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_generation:_.22stateful.22_filters">Second generation: "stateful" filters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firewall_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title='Edit section: Second generation: "stateful" filters'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Stateful_firewall" title="Stateful firewall">Stateful firewall</a></div>
<p>From 1989–1990 three colleagues from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AT%26T_Bell_Laboratories" title="AT&amp;T Bell Laboratories">AT&amp;T Bell Laboratories</a>, Dave Presotto, Janardan Sharma, and Kshitij Nigam, developed the second generation of firewalls, calling them <a href="/wiki/Circuit-level_gateway" title="Circuit-level gateway">circuit-level gateways</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Second-generation firewalls perform the work of their first-generation predecessors but operate up to layer 4 (<a href="/wiki/OSI_model#Layer_4:_transport_layer" title="OSI model">transport layer</a>) of the OSI model. This is achieved by retaining packets until enough information is available to make a judgement about its state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stateful_packet_inspection" title="Stateful packet inspection">stateful packet inspection</a>, it records all connections passing through it and determines whether a packet is the start of a new connection, a part of an existing connection, or not part of any connection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Though static rules are still used, these rules can now contain <i>connection state</i> as one of their test criteria.</p>
<p>Certain <a href="/wiki/Denial-of-service_attack" title="Denial-of-service attack">denial-of-service attacks</a> bombard the firewall with thousands of fake connection packets in an attempt to overwhelm it by filling its connection state memory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Third_generation:_application_layer">Third generation: application layer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firewall_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Third generation: application layer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Application_level_firewall" title="Application level firewall">Application level firewall</a></div>
<p>Marcus Ranum, Wei Xu, and Peter Churchyard developed an application firewall known as Firewall Toolkit (FWTK). In June 1994, Wei Xu extended the FWTK with the kernel enhancement of IP filter and socket transparent. This was known as the first transparent application firewall, released as a commercial product of Gauntlet firewall at <a href="/wiki/Trusted_Information_Systems" title="Trusted Information Systems">Trusted Information Systems</a>. Gauntlet firewall was rated one of the number one firewalls during 1995–1998.</p>
<p>The key benefit of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Application_layer_firewall" title="Application layer firewall">application layer filtering</a> is that it can "understand" certain applications and protocols (such as <a href="/wiki/File_Transfer_Protocol" title="File Transfer Protocol">File Transfer Protocol</a> (FTP), <a href="/wiki/Domain_Name_System" title="Domain Name System">Domain Name System</a> (DNS), or <a href="/wiki/Hypertext_Transfer_Protocol" title="Hypertext Transfer Protocol">Hypertext Transfer Protocol</a> (HTTP)). This is useful as it is able to detect if an unwanted application or service is attempting to bypass the firewall using a protocol on an allowed <a href="/wiki/List_of_TCP_and_UDP_port_numbers" title="List of TCP and UDP port numbers">port</a>, or detect if a protocol is being abused in any harmful way.</p>
<p>As of 2012, the so-called <a href="/wiki/Next-Generation_Firewall" title="Next-Generation Firewall">next-generation firewall</a> (NGFW) is nothing more than the "wider" or "deeper" inspection at application stack. For example, the existing <a href="/wiki/Deep_packet_inspection" title="Deep packet inspection">deep packet inspection</a> functionality of modern firewalls can be extended to include</p>

<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intrusion_prevention_system" title="Intrusion prevention system">Intrusion prevention systems</a> (IPS)</li>
<li>User <a href="/wiki/Identity_management" title="Identity management">identity management</a> integration (by binding user IDs to IP or MAC addresses for "reputation")</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Web_application_firewall" title="Web application firewall">Web application firewall</a> (WAF). WAF attacks may be implemented in the tool "WAF Fingerprinting utilizing timing side channels" (WAFFle)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firewall_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Firewall.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="420" data-file-width="764" height="121" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Firewall.png/220px-Firewall.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Firewall.png/330px-Firewall.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Firewall.png/440px-Firewall.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Firewall.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An illustration of where a firewall would be located in a network</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Firewalls are generally categorized as network-based or host-based. Network-based firewalls are positioned on the gateway computers of <a href="/wiki/Local_area_network" title="Local area network">LANs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wide_Area_Network" title="Wide Area Network">WANs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Intranet" title="Intranet">intranets</a>. Host-based firewalls are positioned on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Network_node" title="Network node">network node</a> itself. The host-based firewall may be a <a href="/wiki/Daemon_(computing)" title="Daemon (computing)">daemon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Windows_service" title="Windows service">service</a> as a part of the <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a> or an agent application such as <a href="/wiki/Endpoint_security" title="Endpoint security">endpoint security</a> or protection. Each has advantages and disadvantages. However, each has a role in <a href="/wiki/Layered_security" title="Layered security">layered security</a>.</p>
<p>Firewalls also vary in type depending on where communication originates, where it is intercepted, and the state of communication being traced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Network_layer_or_packet_filters">Network layer or packet filters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firewall_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Network layer or packet filters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Network layer firewalls, also called packet filters, operate at a relatively low level of the <a href="/wiki/Internet_protocol_suite" title="Internet protocol suite">TCP/IP</a> <a href="/wiki/Protocol_stack" title="Protocol stack">protocol stack</a>, not allowing packets to pass through the firewall unless they match the established rule set. The firewall administrator may define the rules; or default rules may apply. The term "packet filter" originated in the context of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BSD" title="BSD">BSD</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operating_systems" title="Operating systems">operating systems</a>.</p>
<p>Network layer firewalls generally fall into two sub-categories, <a href="/wiki/Stateful_firewall" title="Stateful firewall">stateful</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stateless_firewall" title="Stateless firewall">stateless</a>. Stateful firewalls maintain context about active sessions, and use that "state information" to speed packet processing. Any existing network connection can be described by several properties, including source and destination IP address, UDP or TCP ports, and the current stage of the connection's lifetime (including session initiation, <a href="/wiki/Handshaking" title="Handshaking">handshaking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Data" title="Data">data</a> transfer, or completion connection). If a packet does not match an existing connection, it will be evaluated according to the ruleset for new connections. If a packet matches an existing connection based on comparison with the firewall's state table, it will be allowed to pass without further processing.</p>
<p>Stateless firewalls require less memory, and can be faster for simple filters that require less time to filter than to look up a session. They may also be necessary for filtering stateless network protocols that have no concept of a session. However, they cannot make more complex decisions based on what stage communications between hosts have reached.</p>
<p>Newer firewalls can filter traffic based on many packet attributes like source <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP address</a>, source <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TCP_and_UDP_port" title="TCP and UDP port">port</a>, destination IP address or port, destination service like HTTP or FTP. They can filter based on protocols, <a href="/wiki/Time_to_live" title="Time to live">TTL</a> values, network block of the originator, of the source, and many other attributes.</p>
<p>Commonly used packet filters on various versions of <a href="/wiki/Unix" title="Unix">Unix</a> are <i><a href="/wiki/Ipfirewall" title="Ipfirewall">ipfw</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/FreeBSD" title="FreeBSD">FreeBSD</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mac_OS_X" title="Mac OS X">Mac OS X</a> (&lt; 10.7)), <i><a href="/wiki/NPF_(firewall)" title="NPF (firewall)">NPF</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/NetBSD" title="NetBSD">NetBSD</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/PF_(firewall)" title="PF (firewall)">PF</a></i> (Mac OS X (&gt; 10.4), <a href="/wiki/OpenBSD" title="OpenBSD">OpenBSD</a>, and some other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BSD" title="BSD">BSDs</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Netfilter" title="Netfilter">iptables</a></i>/<i><a href="/wiki/Ipchains" title="Ipchains">ipchains</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>) and <i><a href="/wiki/IPFilter" title="IPFilter">IPFilter</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Application-layer">Application-layer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firewall_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Application-layer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Application_layer_firewall" title="Application layer firewall">Application layer firewall</a></div>
<p>Application-layer firewalls work on the application level of the TCP/IP stack (i.e., all browser traffic, or all <a href="/wiki/Telnet" title="Telnet">telnet</a> or FTP traffic), and may intercept all packets traveling to or from an application. They block other packets (usually dropping them without acknowledgment to the sender).</p>
<p>On inspecting all packets for improper content, firewalls can restrict or prevent outright the spread of networked <a href="/wiki/Computer_worm" title="Computer worm">computer worms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trojan_horse_(computing)" title="Trojan horse (computing)">Trojans</a>. The additional inspection criteria can add extra latency to the forwarding of packets to their destination.</p>
<p>Application firewalls function by determining whether a process should accept any given connection. Application firewalls accomplish their function by hooking into socket calls to filter the connections between the application layer and the lower layers of the OSI model. Application firewalls that hook into socket calls are also referred to as socket filters. Application firewalls work much like a packet filter but application filters apply filtering rules (allow/block) on a per process basis instead of filtering connections on a per port basis. Generally, prompts are used to define rules for processes that have not yet received a connection. It is rare to find application firewalls not combined or used in conjunction with a packet filter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also, application firewalls further filter connections by examining the process ID of data packets against a rule set for the local process involved in the data transmission. The extent of the filtering that occurs is defined by the provided rule set. Given the variety of software that exists, application firewalls only have more complex rule sets for the standard services, such as sharing services. These per-process rule sets have limited efficacy in filtering every possible association that may occur with other processes. Also, these per-process rule sets cannot defend against modification of the process via exploitation, such as memory corruption exploits. Because of these limitations, application firewalls are beginning to be supplanted by a new generation of application firewalls that rely on <a href="/wiki/Mandatory_access_control" title="Mandatory access control">mandatory access control</a> (MAC), also referred to as <a href="/wiki/Sandbox_(computer_security)" title="Sandbox (computer security)">sandboxing</a>, to protect vulnerable services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proxies">Proxies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firewall_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Proxies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Proxy_server" title="Proxy server">Proxy server</a></div>
<p>A proxy server (running either on dedicated hardware or as software on a general-purpose machine) may act as a firewall by responding to input packets (connection requests, for example) in the manner of an application, while blocking other packets. A proxy server is a gateway from one network to another for a specific network application, in the sense that it functions as a proxy on behalf of the network user.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oppliger_1997_94_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oppliger_1997_94-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Proxies make tampering with an internal system from the external network more difficult, so that misuse of one internal system would not necessarily cause a security breach exploitable from outside the <a href="/wiki/Red/black_concept" title="Red/black concept">firewall</a> (as long as the application proxy remains intact and properly configured). Conversely, intruders may hijack a publicly reachable system and use it as a proxy for their own purposes; the proxy then <a href="/wiki/Spoofing_attack" title="Spoofing attack">masquerades</a> as that system to other internal machines. While use of internal address spaces enhances security, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Security_cracking" title="Security cracking">crackers</a> may still employ methods such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IP_spoofing" title="IP spoofing">IP spoofing</a> to attempt to pass packets to a target network.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Network_address_translation">Network address translation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firewall_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Network address translation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Network_address_translation" title="Network address translation">Network address translation</a></div>
<p>Firewalls often have <a href="/wiki/Network_address_translation" title="Network address translation">network address translation</a> (NAT) functionality, and the hosts protected behind a firewall commonly have addresses in the "private address range", as defined in <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc1918" rel="nofollow">RFC 1918</a>. Firewalls often have such functionality to hide the true address of protected hosts. Originally, the NAT function was developed to address the limited number of IPv4 routable addresses that could be used or assigned to companies or individuals as well as reduce both the amount and therefore cost of obtaining enough public addresses for every computer in an organization. Although NAT on its own is not considered a security feature, hiding the addresses of protected devices has become an often used defense against <a href="/wiki/Vulnerability_scanner" title="Vulnerability scanner">network reconnaissance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
