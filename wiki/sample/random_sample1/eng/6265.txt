<p>An <b>embryo</b> is an early stage of <a href="/wiki/Embryogenesis" title="Embryogenesis">development</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Multicellular_organism" title="Multicellular organism">multicellular</a> <a href="/wiki/Ploidy" title="Ploidy">diploid</a> <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotic</a> organism. In general, in <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> that <a href="/wiki/Sexual_reproduction" title="Sexual reproduction">reproduce sexually</a>, an embryo develops from a <a href="/wiki/Zygote" title="Zygote">zygote</a>, the single cell resulting from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertilization" title="Fertilization">fertilization</a> of the female <a href="/wiki/Egg_cell" title="Egg cell">egg cell</a> by the male <a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a> cell. The zygote possesses half the <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> of each of its two parents. In <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>, and some <a href="/wiki/Protist" title="Protist">protists</a>, the zygote will begin to divide by <a href="/wiki/Mitosis" title="Mitosis">mitosis</a> to produce a multicellular organism. The result of this process is an embryo.</p>
<p>In humans, a pregnancy is generally considered to be in the embryonic stage of development between the fifth and the eleventh weeks after fertilization,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and is expressed as a <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetus</a> from the twelfth week.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Embryo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>First attested in English in the mid-14c., the word <i>embryon</i> derives from <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> <i>embryo</i>, itself from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἔμβρυον</span> (<i>embruon</i>), lit. "young one",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> which is the neuter of <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἔμβρυος</span> (<i>embruos</i>), lit. "growing in",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> from ἐν (<i>en</i>), "in"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and βρύω (<i>bruō</i>), "swell, be full";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> the proper <a href="/wiki/Latinisation_of_names" title="Latinisation of names">Latinized</a> form of the Greek term would be <i>embryum</i>.</p>

<p>In animals, the development of the zygote into an embryo proceeds through specific recognizable stages of <a href="/wiki/Blastula" title="Blastula">blastula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gastrulation" title="Gastrulation">gastrula</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Organogenesis" title="Organogenesis">organogenesis</a>. The blastula stage typically features a fluid-filled cavity, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blastocoele" title="Blastocoele">blastocoel</a>, surrounded by a sphere or sheet of cells, also called <a href="/wiki/Blastomere" title="Blastomere">blastomeres</a>. In a <a href="/wiki/Eutheria" title="Eutheria">placental mammal</a>, an ovum is fertilized in a <a href="/wiki/Fallopian_tube" title="Fallopian tube">fallopian tube</a> through which it travels into the uterus. An embryo is called a fetus at a more advanced stage of development and up until <a href="/wiki/Childbirth" title="Childbirth">birth</a> or hatching. In humans, this is from the eleventh week of <a href="/wiki/Gestation" title="Gestation">gestation</a>. However, animals which develop in eggs outside the mother's body, are usually referred to as embryos throughout development; e.g. one would refer to a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">chick</a> embryo, not a "chick fetus," even at later stages.</p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/Gastrulation" title="Gastrulation">gastrulation</a> the cells of the blastula undergo coordinated processes of cell division, invasion, and/or migration to form two (<a href="/wiki/Diploblasty" title="Diploblasty">diploblastic</a>) or three (<a href="/wiki/Triploblasty" title="Triploblasty">triploblastic</a>) tissue layers. In triploblastic organisms, the three <a href="/wiki/Germ_layer" title="Germ layer">germ layers</a> are called <a href="/wiki/Endoderm" title="Endoderm">endoderm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ectoderm" title="Ectoderm">ectoderm</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mesoderm" title="Mesoderm">mesoderm</a>. The position and arrangement of the germ layers are highly species-specific, however, depending on the type of embryo produced. In <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a>, a special population of embryonic cells called the <a href="/wiki/Neural_crest" title="Neural crest">neural crest</a> has been proposed as a "fourth germ layer", and is thought to have been an important novelty in the evolution of head structures.</p>
<p>During organogenesis, molecular and cellular interactions between germ layers, combined with the cells' developmental potential, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Competence_(biology)" title="Competence (biology)">competence</a> to respond, prompt the further differentiation of organ-specific cell types.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> For example, in <a href="/wiki/Neurogenesis" title="Neurogenesis">neurogenesis</a>, a subpopulation of ectoderm cells is set aside to become the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">spinal cord</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Peripheral_nervous_system" title="Peripheral nervous system">peripheral nerves</a>. Modern developmental biology is extensively probing the molecular basis for every type of organogenesis, including <a href="/wiki/Angiogenesis" title="Angiogenesis">angiogenesis</a> (formation of new blood vessels from pre-existing ones), <a href="/wiki/Chondrogenesis" title="Chondrogenesis">chondrogenesis</a> (cartilage), <a href="/wiki/Myogenesis" title="Myogenesis">myogenesis</a> (muscle), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osteogenesis" title="Osteogenesis">osteogenesis</a> (bone), and many others.</p>
<p>Generally, if a structure pre-dates another structure in evolutionary terms, then it often appears earlier than the second in an embryo; this general observation is sometimes summarized by the phrase "<a href="/wiki/Recapitulation_theory" title="Recapitulation theory">ontogeny recapitulates phylogeny</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> For example, the backbone is a common structure among all vertebrates such as <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, and the backbone also appears as one of the earliest structures laid out in all vertebrate embryos. The <a href="/wiki/Cerebrum" title="Cerebrum">cerebrum</a> in humans, which is the most sophisticated part of the <a href="/wiki/Human_brain" title="Human brain">brain</a>, develops last. This sequencing rule is not absolute, but it is recognized as being partly applicable to development of the human embryo.</p>

</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botany</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seed_plant" title="Seed plant">seed plant</a> <i>embryo</i> is part of a <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seed</a>, consisting of precursor tissues for the leaves, stem (see <b><a href="/wiki/Hypocotyl" title="Hypocotyl">hypocotyl</a></b>), and root (see <b><a href="/wiki/Radicle" title="Radicle">radicle</a></b>), as well as one or more <b><a href="/wiki/Cotyledon" title="Cotyledon">cotyledons</a></b>. Once the embryo begins to <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">germinate</a> — grow out from the seed — it is called a <b>seedling</b> (plantlet).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bryophyte" title="Bryophyte">Bryophytes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">ferns</a> also produce an embryo, but do not produce seeds. In these plants, the embryo begins its existence attached to the inside of the <a href="/wiki/Archegonium" title="Archegonium">archegonium</a> on a parental <a href="/wiki/Gametophyte" title="Gametophyte">gametophyte</a> from which the egg cell was generated. The inner wall of the archegonium lies in close contact with the "foot" of the developing embryo; this "foot" consists of a bulbous mass of cells at the base of the embryo which may receive nutrition from its parent gametophyte. The structure and development of the rest of the embryo varies by group of plants. Once the embryo has expanded beyond the enclosing archegonium, it is no longer termed an embryo.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_and_technology">Research and technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Embryo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Research and technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Embryos are used in various fields of research and in techniques of <a href="/wiki/Assisted_reproductive_technology" title="Assisted reproductive technology">assisted reproductive technology</a>. An egg may be fertilized in vitro and the resulting embryo may be <a href="/wiki/Cryopreservation#Embryos" title="Cryopreservation">frozen</a> for later use. The potential of embryonic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stem_cell_research" title="Stem cell research">stem cell research</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reproductive_cloning" title="Reproductive cloning">reproductive cloning</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germline_engineering" title="Germline engineering">germline engineering</a> are currently being explored. <a href="/wiki/Prenatal_diagnosis" title="Prenatal diagnosis">Prenatal diagnosis</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preimplantation_diagnosis" title="Preimplantation diagnosis">preimplantation diagnosis</a> enables testing embryos for diseases or conditions.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cryoconservation_of_animal_genetic_resources" title="Cryoconservation of animal genetic resources">Cryoconservation of animal genetic resources</a> is a practice in which animal germplasms, such as embryos are collected and stored at low temperatures with the intent of conserving the genetic material.</p>
<p>The embryos of <i><a href="/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana" title="Arabidopsis thaliana">Arabidopsis thaliana</a></i> have been used as a model to understand gene activation, patterning, and organogenesis of seed plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In regards to research using human embryos, the ethics and legalities of this application continue to be debated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossilized_embryos">Fossilized embryos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Embryo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fossilized embryos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_embryos" title="Fossil embryos">Fossil embryos</a></div>
<p>Fossilized animal embryos are known from the Precambrian, and are found in great numbers during the Cambrian period. Even fossilized dinosaur embryos have been discovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Miscarriage_.26_abortion">Miscarriage &amp; abortion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Embryo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Miscarriage &amp; abortion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Miscarriage" title="Miscarriage">Miscarriage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">Abortion</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Human_Embryo_-_Approximately_8_weeks_estimated_gestational_age.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3872" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Human_Embryo_-_Approximately_8_weeks_estimated_gestational_age.jpg/220px-Human_Embryo_-_Approximately_8_weeks_estimated_gestational_age.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Human_Embryo_-_Approximately_8_weeks_estimated_gestational_age.jpg/330px-Human_Embryo_-_Approximately_8_weeks_estimated_gestational_age.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Human_Embryo_-_Approximately_8_weeks_estimated_gestational_age.jpg/440px-Human_Embryo_-_Approximately_8_weeks_estimated_gestational_age.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Human_Embryo_-_Approximately_8_weeks_estimated_gestational_age.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A complete spontaneous abortion at about 6 weeks from <a href="/wiki/Fertilisation" title="Fertilisation">conception</a>, i.e. 8 weeks from <a href="/wiki/Gestational_age" title="Gestational age">LMP</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Some embryos do not survive to the next stage of development. When this happens naturally, it is called spontaneous abortion or miscarriage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> There are many reasons why this may occur. The most common natural cause of miscarriage is chromosomal abnormality in animals<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mednet_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-mednet-13">[13]</a></sup> or genetic load in plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-K.C3.A4rkk.C3.A4inen_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-K.C3.A4rkk.C3.A4inen-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In species which produce multiple embryos at the same time, miscarriage or abortion of some embryos can provide the remaining embryos with a greater share of maternal resources. This can also disturb the pregnancy, causing harm to the second embryo. Genetic strains which miscarry their embryos are the source of commercial seedless fruits.</p>
<p>Abortion is the process of artificially (non-naturally) removing the embryo through deliberate pharmaceutical or surgical methods.</p>
