<p><b>Barry Jenkins</b> (born November 19, 1979) is an American filmmaker based in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>. He is widely known for his films <i><a href="/wiki/Medicine_for_Melancholy" title="Medicine for Melancholy">Medicine for Melancholy</a></i> (2008) and <a href="/wiki/Moonlight_(2016_film)" title="Moonlight (2016 film)"><i>Moonlight</i></a> (2016), the latter of which received massive critical acclaim as well <a href="/wiki/List_of_accolades_received_by_Moonlight_(2016_film)" title="List of accolades received by Moonlight (2016 film)">dozens of accolades</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Motion_Picture_%E2%80%93_Drama" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Motion Picture – Drama">Golden Globe Award for Best Picture – Drama</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-a-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Jenkins_(director)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jenkins was born in 1979 in <a href="/wiki/Liberty_City" title="Liberty City">Liberty City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>, and has three older siblings. His father died when he was 12, and had earlier separated from his mother, believing that Jenkins was not his biological son. During his childhood, Jenkins was raised by another woman in an overcrowded apartment. He attended <a href="/wiki/Miami_Northwestern_Senior_High_School" title="Miami Northwestern Senior High School">Miami Northwestern Senior High School</a>, where he played on the school football team. Jenkins later studied film at <a href="/wiki/Florida_State_University" title="Florida State University">Florida State University</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tallahassee" title="Tallahassee">Tallahassee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FaderInterview_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FaderInterview-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Jenkins_(director)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s:_Early_work">2000s: Early work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Jenkins_(director)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2000s: Early work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jenkins's breakout film was <i><a href="/wiki/Medicine_for_Melancholy" title="Medicine for Melancholy">Medicine for Melancholy</a></i>, a low-budget independent feature released in 2008, starring <a href="/wiki/Wyatt_Cenac" title="Wyatt Cenac">Wyatt Cenac</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tracey_Heggins&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tracey Heggins (page does not exist)">Tracey Heggins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The film was well received by critics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FaderInterview_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FaderInterview-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the success of his previous film, Jenkins wrote an epic for <a href="/wiki/Focus_Features" title="Focus Features">Focus Features</a> about “Stevie Wonder and time travel” and an adaptation to the <a href="/wiki/James_Baldwin" title="James Baldwin">James Baldwin</a> novel <i><a href="/wiki/If_Beale_Street_Could_Talk" title="If Beale Street Could Talk">If Beale Street Could Talk</a></i>, both of which never entered production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FaderInterview_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-FaderInterview-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LATimesMoonlight_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-LATimesMoonlight-4">[4]</a></sup> He later worked as a carpenter and co-founded an advertising company called Strike Anywhere. In 2011, he wrote and directed <i>Remigration</i>, a sci-fi short film about gentrification. Jenkins became a writer for HBO's <a href="/wiki/The_Leftovers_(TV_series)" title="The Leftovers (TV series)"><i>The Leftovers</i></a>, about which he commented, "I didn't get to do much."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FaderInterview_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-FaderInterview-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2016:_Breakthrough_with_Moonlight">2016: Breakthrough with <i>Moonlight</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Jenkins_(director)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2016: Breakthrough with Moonlight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jenkins directed and wrote the drama <a href="/wiki/Moonlight_(2016_film)" title="Moonlight (2016 film)"><i>Moonlight</i></a>, his first feature film in eight years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FaderInterview_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-FaderInterview-2">[2]</a></sup> The film was shot in Miami and premiered at the <a href="/wiki/Telluride_Film_Festival" title="Telluride Film Festival">Telluride Film Festival</a> in 2016 to vast critical acclaim and awards "buzz."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/A._O._Scott" title="A. O. Scott">A. O. Scott</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> wrote: "<i>Moonlight</i> dwells on the dignity, beauty and terrible vulnerability of black bodies, on the existential and physical matter of black lives."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> wrote: "Barry Jenkins' vital portrait of a South Florida youth revisits the character at three stages in his life, offering rich insights into the contemporary African-American experience."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> David Sims of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Atlantic" title="The Atlantic">The Atlantic</a></i> wrote: "Like all great films, <i>Moonlight</i> is both specific and sweeping. It’s a story about identity—an intelligent, challenging work..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film has since won <a href="/wiki/List_of_accolades_received_by_Moonlight_(2016_film)" title="List of accolades received by Moonlight (2016 film)">dozens of accolades</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Motion_Picture_%E2%80%93_Drama" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Motion Picture – Drama">Golden Globe Award for Best Picture – Drama</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-a-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2017.E2.80.93present:_Future_projects">2017–present: Future projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Jenkins_(director)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2017–present: Future projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>His upcoming projects include a series based on <a href="/wiki/Colson_Whitehead" title="Colson Whitehead">Colson Whitehead</a>'s novel <a href="/wiki/The_Underground_Railroad_(novel)" title="The Underground Railroad (novel)"><i>The Underground Railroad</i></a> and a screenplay based on the life of <a href="/wiki/Claressa_Shields" title="Claressa Shields">Claressa Shields</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
