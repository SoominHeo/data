<p><i><b>Batman</b></i> is a 1989 American <a href="/wiki/Superhero_film" title="Superhero film">superhero film</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/Tim_Burton" title="Tim Burton">Tim Burton</a> and produced by <a href="/wiki/Jon_Peters" title="Jon Peters">Jon Peters</a>, based on the <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a> <a href="/wiki/Batman" title="Batman">character of the same name</a>. It is the first installment of <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a>' <a href="/wiki/Batman_(1989_film_series)" title="Batman (1989 film series)">initial <i>Batman</i> film series</a>. The film stars <a href="/wiki/Michael_Keaton" title="Michael Keaton">Michael Keaton</a> as Bruce Wayne/Batman, alongside <a href="/wiki/Jack_Nicholson" title="Jack Nicholson">Jack Nicholson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kim_Basinger" title="Kim Basinger">Kim Basinger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wuhl" title="Robert Wuhl">Robert Wuhl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pat_Hingle" title="Pat Hingle">Pat Hingle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Billy_Dee_Williams" title="Billy Dee Williams">Billy Dee Williams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Gough" title="Michael Gough">Michael Gough</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Palance" title="Jack Palance">Jack Palance</a>. In the film, Batman is widely believed to be an urban legend until he actively goes to war with a rising criminal mastermind known as <a href="/wiki/Joker_(comics)" title="Joker (comics)">the Joker</a>.</p>
<p>After Burton was hired as director in 1986, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Englehart" title="Steve Englehart">Steve Englehart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Julie_Hickson" title="Julie Hickson">Julie Hickson</a> wrote <a href="/wiki/Film_treatment" title="Film treatment">film treatments</a> before <a href="/wiki/Sam_Hamm" title="Sam Hamm">Sam Hamm</a> wrote the first screenplay. <i>Batman</i> was not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenlight" title="Greenlight">greenlit</a> until after the success of Burton's <i><a href="/wiki/Beetlejuice" title="Beetlejuice">Beetlejuice</a></i> (1988). Numerous <a href="/wiki/A-list" title="A-list">A-list</a> actors were considered for the role of Batman before Keaton was cast. Keaton's casting caused a controversy since, by 1988, he had become <a href="/wiki/Typecasting_(acting)" title="Typecasting (acting)">typecast</a> as a comedic actor and many observers doubted he could portray a serious role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elfman_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Elfman-2">[2]</a></sup> Nicholson accepted the role of the Joker under strict conditions that dictated a high salary, a portion of the <a href="/wiki/Box_office" title="Box office">box office</a> profits and his shooting schedule. The tone and themes of the film were influenced in part by <a href="/wiki/Alan_Moore" title="Alan Moore">Alan Moore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brian_Bolland" title="Brian Bolland">Brian Bolland</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Batman:_The_Killing_Joke" title="Batman: The Killing Joke">The Killing Joke</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Frank_Miller_(comics)" title="Frank Miller (comics)">Frank Miller</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dark_Knight_Returns" title="The Dark Knight Returns">The Dark Knight Returns</a></i>. The film notably invents a unique <a href="/wiki/Origin_story" title="Origin story">origin story</a> for the Joker, presenting him as an organized criminal named Jack Napier. Filming took place at <a href="/wiki/Pinewood_Studios" title="Pinewood Studios">Pinewood Studios</a> from October 1988 to January 1989. The budget escalated from <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">$</a>30 million to $48 million, while the <a href="/wiki/1988_Writers_Guild_of_America_strike" title="1988 Writers Guild of America strike">1988 Writers Guild of America strike</a> forced Hamm to drop out. Uncredited rewrites were performed by <a href="/wiki/Warren_Skaaren" title="Warren Skaaren">Warren Skaaren</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_McKeown" title="Charles McKeown">Charles McKeown</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Gems" title="Jonathan Gems">Jonathan Gems</a>.</p>
<p><i>Batman</i> was a critical and financial success, earning over $400 million in box office totals. It was the fifth-highest-grossing film in history at the time of its release. The film received several <a href="/wiki/Saturn_Award" title="Saturn Award">Saturn Award</a> nominations and a <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award" title="Golden Globe Award">Golden Globe</a> nomination, and won an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a>. It also inspired the equally successful <i><a href="/wiki/Batman:_The_Animated_Series" title="Batman: The Animated Series">Batman: The Animated Series</a></i>, paving the way for the <a href="/wiki/DC_animated_universe" title="DC animated universe">DC animated universe</a>, and has influenced <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_States" title="Cinema of the United States">Hollywood</a>'s modern marketing and development techniques of the <a href="/wiki/Superhero_film" title="Superhero film">superhero film</a> genre.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span></h2>
<p>As <a href="/wiki/Gotham_City" title="Gotham City">Gotham City</a> approaches its <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bicentennial" title="wikt:bicentennial">bicentennial</a>, Mayor Borg orders district attorney <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvey_Dent" title="Harvey Dent">Harvey Dent</a> and police commissioner <a href="/wiki/James_Gordon_(comics)" title="James Gordon (comics)">James Gordon</a> to make the city safer. Meanwhile, reporter Alexander Knox and photojournalist <a href="/wiki/Vicki_Vale" title="Vicki Vale">Vicki Vale</a> begin to investigate rumors of a vigilante nicknamed "<a href="/wiki/Batman" title="Batman">Batman</a>", who is targeting the city's criminals.</p>
<p>Mob boss Carl Grissom, who has already been targeted by Dent, discovers his mistress Alicia is involved with his second-in-command Jack Napier. With the help of corrupt police lieutenant Max Eckhardt, Grissom sets Napier up to be killed in a raid at <a href="/wiki/Ace_Chemicals" title="Ace Chemicals">Axis Chemicals</a>. However, Grissom's plan is foiled with the arrival of Commissioner Gordon, who wants Napier captured alive. In the ensuing shootout, Napier kills Eckhardt, but Batman suddenly appears and, in a struggle, Napier is knocked into a vat of chemicals. Batman escapes, and Napier is presumed dead.</p>
<p>Batman's alter-ego is Bruce Wayne, a billionaire industrialist who, as a child, witnessed his parents' murder at the hands of a psychotic mugger. At a fundraiser at his mansion, Bruce meets and falls for Vale, and the two begin a romantic relationship. Meanwhile, Napier is revealed to have survived the accident, but is horribly disfigured with chalk white skin, emerald green hair, and a ruby red grin. Driven insane, Napier calls himself <a href="/wiki/Joker_(comics)" title="Joker (comics)">"the Joker"</a>, killing Grissom and taking over his criminal empire.</p>
<p>The Joker begins to terrorize Gotham City by lacing hygiene products with "Smilex", a deadly chemical which causes victims to <a href="/wiki/Death_from_laughter" title="Death from laughter">die laughing</a> with the same maniacal grin as the Joker. During his search for information on Batman, the Joker also falls for Vale. He lures her to the Gotham Museum of Art, but Batman arrives and rescues her. They escape in the <a href="/wiki/Batmobile" title="Batmobile">Batmobile</a>, but are pursued by the Joker's men, whom Batman manages to defeat. Batman takes Vicki to the <a href="/wiki/Batcave" title="Batcave">Batcave</a>, where he gives her information from his research on Smilex that will allow the city's residents to protect themselves from the toxin. Furious, the Joker vows to kill Batman.</p>
<p>Bruce visits Vicki at her apartment, prepared to tell her about his alter-ego, but the Joker interrupts their meeting, asking Bruce, "Have you ever danced with the devil by the pale moonlight?" before shooting him. Bruce, however, survives and escapes, as he had been wearing body armor. He remembers that the mugger who killed his parents asked the same question, and deduces that Napier was the mugger. Vicki suddenly appears in the Batcave, having been let in by Bruce's butler, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Pennyworth" title="Alfred Pennyworth">Alfred Pennyworth</a>. After telling her that with the Joker terrorizing Gotham, he cannot focus on their relationship, Bruce departs as Batman to destroy the Axis plant. Meanwhile, the Joker lures the citizens of Gotham to a parade with the promise of free money, but while throwing cash at the crowd as promised, also attacks them with Smilex gas released from his giant parade balloons. Batman arrives and saves Gotham City using the <a href="/wiki/Batplane" title="Batplane">Batwing</a>. Furious, the Joker kills Bob, his number one thug, then shoots the Batwing using a long barreled gun, causing it to crash.</p>
<p>The Joker kidnaps Vicki and takes her to the top of a cathedral. Batman, who survived the crash, fends off the Joker's remaining men despite his injuries, and confronts the Joker. The two struggle, with Joker eventually gaining the upper hand and leaving Batman and Vicki clinging onto an outcropping. The Joker tries to escape on his helicopter, but Batman ties the Joker's leg to a <a href="/wiki/Gargoyle" title="Gargoyle">gargoyle</a> with his grappling hook, causing him to fall to his death after it breaks off.</p>
<p>Commissioner Gordon announces that the police have arrested the Joker's men and unveils the <a href="/wiki/Bat-Signal" title="Bat-Signal">Bat-Signal</a>. Harvey Dent reads a note from Batman, promising that he will defend Gotham whenever crime strikes again. Vicki is taken to <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Manor" title="Wayne Manor">Wayne Manor</a> by Alfred, who tells her that Bruce will be a little late. She responds by claiming that she is not a bit surprised, as Batman looks at the bat-signal, standing watch over the city.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes">Themes</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:227px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MikeandJack.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="236" data-file-width="250" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/38/MikeandJack.jpg/225px-MikeandJack.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/3/38/MikeandJack.jpg 1.5x" width="225"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MikeandJack.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
"The duel of the freaks"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elfman_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Elfman-2">[2]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>When discussing the central <a href="/wiki/Theme_(arts)" title="Theme (arts)">theme</a> of <i>Batman</i>, director Tim Burton explained, "the whole film and mythology of the character is a complete duel of the freaks. It's a fight between two disturbed people", adding that "The Joker is such a great character because there's a complete freedom to him. Any character who operates on the outside of society and is deemed a freak and an outcast then has the freedom to do what they want... They are the darker sides of freedom. Insanity is in some scary way the most freedom you can have, because you're not bound by the laws of society".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elfman_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Elfman-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Burton saw Bruce Wayne as the bearer of a double identity, exposing one while hiding the reality from the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elfman_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Elfman-2">[2]</a></sup> Burton biographer Ken Hanke wrote that Bruce Wayne, struggling with his alter-ego as Batman, is depicted as an <a href="/wiki/Antihero" title="Antihero">antihero</a>. Hanke felt that Batman has to push the boundaries of civil justice to deal with certain criminals, such as the Joker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forever_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-forever-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kim_Newman" title="Kim Newman">Kim Newman</a> theorized that "Burton and the writers saw Batman and the Joker as a dramatic antithesis, and the film deals with their intertwined origins and fates to an even greater extent".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Motif_(visual_arts)" title="Motif (visual arts)">visual motif</a> is present in the scene of Batman's first major act of vigilantism at Axis Chemicals, wherein he is carefully framed so that the single word AXIS, in gigantic red neon letters, looms over him, comparing his acts to those of the <a href="/wiki/Totalitarianism" title="Totalitarianism">totalitarian</a> governments of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and thus implying that the dangers of these actions include the transformation of Jack Napier into the Joker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forever_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-forever-3">[3]</a></sup> <i>Batman</i> also conveys trademarks found in 1930s <a href="/wiki/Pulp_magazine" title="Pulp magazine">pulp magazines</a>, notably the design of Gotham City stylized with <a href="/wiki/Art_Deco" title="Art Deco">Art Deco</a> design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hal_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hal-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Corliss" title="Richard Corliss">Richard Corliss</a>, writing for <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i>, observed that Gotham's design was a reference to films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Metropolis_(1927_film)" title="Metropolis (1927 film)">Metropolis</a></i> (1927) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cabinet_of_Dr._Caligari" title="The Cabinet of Dr. Caligari">The Cabinet of Dr. Caligari</a></i> (1920). "Gotham City, despite being shot on a studio <a href="/wiki/Backlot" title="Backlot">backlot</a>", he continued, "is literally another character in the script. It has the demeaning presence of <a href="/wiki/German_Expressionism" title="German Expressionism">German Expressionism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fascist_architecture" title="Fascist architecture">fascist architecture</a>, staring down at the citizens."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Hanke further addressed the notions of <i>Batman</i> being a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Period_piece" title="Period piece">period piece</a>, in that "The citizens, cops, people and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black-and-white" title="Black-and-white">black-and-white</a> television looks like it takes place in 1939"; but later said: "Had the filmmakers made <a href="/wiki/Vicki_Vale" title="Vicki Vale">Vicki Vale</a> a <a href="/wiki/Femme_fatale" title="Femme fatale">femme fatale</a> rather than a <a href="/wiki/Damsel_in_distress" title="Damsel in distress">damsel in distress</a>, this could have made <i>Batman</i> as a homage and tribute to classic <a href="/wiki/Film_noir" title="Film noir">film noir</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manor_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Manor-7">[7]</a></sup> Portions of the <a href="/wiki/Climax_(narrative)" title="Climax (narrative)">climax</a> pay homage to <i><a href="/wiki/Vertigo_(film)" title="Vertigo (film)">Vertigo</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cartoon_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-cartoon-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span></h3>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:35%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"I was never a giant comic book fan, but I've always loved the image of Batman and the Joker. The reason I've never been a comic book fan — and I think it started when I was a child — is because I could never tell which box I was supposed to read. I don't know if it was <a href="/wiki/Dyslexia" title="Dyslexia">dyslexia</a> or whatever, but that's why I loved <i>The Killing Joke</i>, because for the first time I could tell which one to read. It's my favorite. It's the first comic I've ever loved. And the success of those graphic novels made our ideas more acceptable."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">—Tim Burton<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>In the late 1970s, Batman's popularity was waning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBS_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBS-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> was interested in producing a <i>Batman in Outer Space</i> film. Producers <a href="/wiki/Michael_Uslan" title="Michael Uslan">Michael Uslan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Melniker" title="Benjamin Melniker">Benjamin Melniker</a> purchased the <a href="/wiki/Film_rights" title="Film rights">film rights</a> of Batman from <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a> on October 3, 1979. It was Uslan's wish "to make the definitive, dark, serious version of Batman, the way <a href="/wiki/Bob_Kane" title="Bob Kane">Bob Kane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bill_Finger" title="Bill Finger">Bill Finger</a> had envisioned him in 1939. A creature of the night; stalking criminals in the shadows."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBS_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-CBS-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Maibaum" title="Richard Maibaum">Richard Maibaum</a> was approached to write a script with <a href="/wiki/Guy_Hamilton" title="Guy Hamilton">Guy Hamilton</a> to direct, but the two turned down the offer. Uslan was unsuccessful with <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(filmmaking)" title="Pitch (filmmaking)">pitching</a> <i>Batman</i> to various movie studios because they wanted the film to be similar to the <a href="/wiki/Camp_(style)" title="Camp (style)">campy</a> <a href="/wiki/Batman_(TV_series)" title="Batman (TV series)">1960s TV series</a>. <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Artists" title="United Artists">United Artists</a> were among those to turn down the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Columbia_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Columbia-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>A disappointed Uslan then wrote a script titled <i>Return of the Batman</i> to give the film industry a better idea of his vision for the film. Uslan later compared its dark tone to that of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dark_Knight_Returns" title="The Dark Knight Returns">The Dark Knight Returns</a></i>, which his script pre-dated by six years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBS_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-CBS-10">[10]</a></sup> In November 1979, producers <a href="/wiki/Jon_Peters" title="Jon Peters">Jon Peters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Guber" title="Peter Guber">Peter Guber</a> joined the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guber_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guber-12">[12]</a></sup> The four producers felt it was best to pattern the film's development after that of <i><a href="/wiki/Superman_(1978_film)" title="Superman (1978 film)">Superman</a></i> (1978).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-production_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-production-13">[13]</a></sup> Uslan, Melniker and Guber pitched <i>Batman</i> to <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">Universal Pictures</a>, but the studio turned it down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-road_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-road-14">[14]</a></sup> Though no movie studios were yet involved, the project was publicly announced with a budget of $15 million in July 1980 at the <a href="/wiki/Comic_Art_Convention" title="Comic Art Convention">Comic Art Convention</a> in New York. <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a> decided to accept <i>Batman</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tom_Mankiewicz" title="Tom Mankiewicz">Tom Mankiewicz</a> completed a script titled <i>The Batman</i> in June 1983, focusing on Batman and <a href="/wiki/Dick_Grayson" title="Dick Grayson">Dick Grayson</a>'s origins, with the Joker and <a href="/wiki/Rupert_Thorne" title="Rupert Thorne">Rupert Thorne</a> as villains, and <a href="/wiki/Silver_St._Cloud" title="Silver St. Cloud">Silver St. Cloud</a> as the romantic interest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Mankiewicz took inspiration from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Limited_series" title="Limited series">limited series</a> <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Batman:_Strange_Apparitions&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Batman: Strange Apparitions (page does not exist)">Batman: Strange Apparitions</a></i> (<a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/1563895005">ISBN 1-56389-500-5</a>), written by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Englehart" title="Steve Englehart">Steve Englehart</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-White-17">[17]</a></sup> Comic book artist <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Rogers" title="Marshall Rogers">Marshall Rogers</a>, who worked with Englehart on <i>Strange Apparitions</i>, was hired for <a href="/wiki/Concept_art" title="Concept art">concept art</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-road_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-road-14">[14]</a></sup> <i>The Batman</i> was then announced in late 1983 for a mid-1985 release date on a budget of $20 million. Originally, Mankiewicz had wanted an unknown actor for Batman, <a href="/wiki/William_Holden" title="William Holden">William Holden</a> for <a href="/wiki/James_Gordon_(comics)" title="James Gordon (comics)">James Gordon</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Niven" title="David Niven">David Niven</a> as <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Pennyworth" title="Alfred Pennyworth">Alfred Pennyworth</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Peter_O%27Toole" title="Peter O'Toole">Peter O'Toole</a> as <a href="/wiki/Penguin_(comics)" title="Penguin (comics)">the Penguin</a>, whom Mankiewicz wanted to portray as a mobster with low body temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-15">[15]</a></sup> Holden died in 1981 and Niven in 1983, so this would never come to pass. A number of filmmakers were attached to Mankiewicz' script, including <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Reitman" title="Ivan Reitman">Ivan Reitman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joe_Dante" title="Joe Dante">Joe Dante</a>. Reitman wanted to cast <a href="/wiki/Bill_Murray" title="Bill Murray">Bill Murray</a> as Batman and <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Murphy" title="Eddie Murphy">Eddie Murphy</a> as Robin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elfman_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Elfman-2">[2]</a></sup> Nine rewrites were performed by nine separate writers. Most of them were based on <i>Strange Apparitions</i>. However, it was Mankiewicz's script that was still being used to guide the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forever_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-forever-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the financial success of <i><a href="/wiki/Pee-wee%27s_Big_Adventure" title="Pee-wee's Big Adventure">Pee-wee's Big Adventure</a></i> (1985), Warner Bros. hired Tim Burton to direct <i>Batman</i>. Burton had then-girlfriend <a href="/wiki/Julie_Hickson" title="Julie Hickson">Julie Hickson</a> write a new 30-page <a href="/wiki/Film_treatment" title="Film treatment">film treatment</a>, feeling the previous script by Mankiewicz was <a href="/wiki/Camp_(style)" title="Camp (style)">campy</a>. The success of <i>The Dark Knight Returns</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Batman:_The_Killing_Joke" title="Batman: The Killing Joke">Batman: The Killing Joke</a></i> rekindled Warner Bros.' interest in a film adaptation. Burton was initially not a comic book fan, but he was impressed by the dark and serious tone found in both <i>The Dark Knight Returns</i> and <i>The Killing Joke</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elfman_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Elfman-2">[2]</a></sup> Warner Bros. enlisted the aid of <a href="/wiki/Steve_Englehart" title="Steve Englehart">Steve Englehart</a> to write a new treatment in March 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steve_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Steve-18">[18]</a></sup> It included the Joker and <a href="/wiki/Rupert_Thorne" title="Rupert Thorne">Rupert Thorne</a> as the main villains, with a <a href="/wiki/Cameo_appearance" title="Cameo appearance">cameo appearance</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Penguin_(comics)" title="Penguin (comics)">Penguin</a>. <a href="/wiki/Silver_St._Cloud" title="Silver St. Cloud">Silver St. Cloud</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dick_Grayson" title="Dick Grayson">Dick Grayson</a> were key supporting roles. It followed the similar storyline from Englehart's own <i>Strange Apparitions</i> (<a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/1563895005">ISBN 1-56389-500-5</a>). Warner Bros. was impressed, but Englehart felt there were too many characters. He removed the Penguin and Dick Grayson in his second treatment, finishing in May 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steve_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Steve-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Burton approached Sam Hamm, a comic book fan, to write the screenplay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-15">[15]</a></sup> Hamm decided not to use an <a href="/wiki/Origin_story" title="Origin story">origin story</a>, feeling that flashbacks would be more suitable and that "unlocking the mystery" would become part of the storyline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-White-17">[17]</a></sup> He reasoned, "You totally destroy your credibility if you show the literal process by which Bruce Wayne becomes Batman."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hamm_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hamm-19">[19]</a></sup> Hamm replaced Silver St. Cloud with Vicki Vale and Rupert Thorne with his own creation, Carl Grissom. He completed his script in October 1986, which demoted Dick Grayson to a cameo rather than a supporting character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-storm_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-storm-20">[20]</a></sup> One scene in Hamm's script had a young James Gordon on duty the night of the murder of Bruce Wayne's parents. When Hamm's script was rewritten, the scene was deleted, reducing it to a photo in the <i>Gotham Globe</i> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Warner Bros. was less willing to move forward on development, despite their enthusiasm for Hamm's script, which Batman co-creator <a href="/wiki/Bob_Kane" title="Bob Kane">Bob Kane</a> greeted with positive feedback.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elfman_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Elfman-2">[2]</a></sup> Hamm's script was then <a href="/wiki/Bootleg_(comics)" title="Bootleg (comics)">bootlegged</a> at various comic book stores in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-White-17">[17]</a></sup> <i>Batman</i> was finally given the <a href="/wiki/Green-light" title="Green-light">greenlight</a> to commence pre-production in April 1988, after the success of Burton's <i><a href="/wiki/Beetlejuice" title="Beetlejuice">Beetlejuice</a></i> (1988).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elfman_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Elfman-2">[2]</a></sup> When comic book fans found out about Burton directing the film with Michael Keaton starring in the lead role, controversy arose over the tone and direction <i>Batman</i> was going in.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variety_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-variety-22">[22]</a></sup> Hamm explained, "They hear Tim Burton's name and they think of <i><a href="/wiki/Pee-wee%27s_Big_Adventure" title="Pee-wee's Big Adventure">Pee-wee's Big Adventure</a></i>. They hear Keaton's name and they think of any number of Michael Keaton comedies. You think of the 1960s version of <i>Batman</i>, and it was the complete opposite of our film. We tried to market it with a typical dark and serious tone, but the fans didn't believe us."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-White-17">[17]</a></sup> To combat negative reports on the film's production, Kane was hired as creative consultant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-production_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-production-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Casting">Casting</span></h3>
<p>Parallel to the <i><a href="/wiki/Superman_(1978_film)" title="Superman (1978 film)">Superman</a></i> casting, a who's who of <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_States" title="Cinema of the United States">Hollywood top stars</a> were considered for the role of Batman, including <a href="/wiki/Mel_Gibson" title="Mel Gibson">Mel Gibson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Costner" title="Kevin Costner">Kevin Costner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Sheen" title="Charlie Sheen">Charlie Sheen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Selleck" title="Tom Selleck">Tom Selleck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Murray" title="Bill Murray">Bill Murray</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guber_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guber-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Harrison_Ford" title="Harrison Ford">Harrison Ford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Quaid" title="Dennis Quaid">Dennis Quaid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Busch_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Busch-23">[23]</a></sup> Burton was pressured by Warner Bros. to cast an obvious action movie star,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elfman_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-Elfman-2">[2]</a></sup> and had approached <a href="/wiki/Pierce_Brosnan" title="Pierce Brosnan">Pierce Brosnan</a>, but he had no interest in playing a comic book character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Burton was originally interested in casting an unknown actor,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Busch_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Busch-23">[23]</a></sup> and offered <a href="/wiki/Ray_Liotta" title="Ray Liotta">Ray Liotta</a> a chance to audition after having completed <i><a href="/wiki/Something_Wild_(1986_film)" title="Something Wild (1986 film)">Something Wild</a></i>, but Liotta declined, a decision he regrets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Willem_Dafoe" title="Willem Dafoe">Willem Dafoe</a>, who was falsely reported to be considered for the Joker, had actually been considered for Batman early in development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Producer Jon Peters suggested Michael Keaton, arguing he had the right "edgy, tormented quality" after having seen his dramatic performance in <i><a href="/wiki/Clean_and_Sober" title="Clean and Sober">Clean and Sober</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Having directed Keaton in <i><a href="/wiki/Beetlejuice" title="Beetlejuice">Beetlejuice</a></i>, Burton agreed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guber_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Guber-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Keaton's casting caused a controversy among comic book fans,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variety_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-variety-22">[22]</a></sup> with 50,000 protest letters sent to Warner Bros. offices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-storm_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-storm-20">[20]</a></sup> Bob Kane, Sam Hamm and Michael Uslan also heavily questioned the casting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-White-17">[17]</a></sup> "Obviously there was a negative response from the comic book people. I think they thought we were going to make it like the 1960s TV series, and make it campy, because they thought of Michael Keaton from <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._Mom" title="Mr. Mom">Mr. Mom</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Night_Shift_(film)" title="Night Shift (film)">Night Shift</a></i> and stuff like that."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-battle_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-battle-28">[28]</a></sup> Keaton studied <i>The Dark Knight Returns</i> for inspiration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Brad_Dourif" title="Brad Dourif">Brad Dourif</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tim_Curry" title="Tim Curry">Tim Curry</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">David Bowie</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Lithgow" title="John Lithgow">John Lithgow</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Woods" title="James Woods">James Woods</a> were considered for the Joker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hamm_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hamm-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reboot_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-reboot-31">[31]</a></sup> Burton wanted to cast <a href="/wiki/Brad_Dourif" title="Brad Dourif">Brad Dourif</a>, but the studio refused.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robin_Williams" title="Robin Williams">Robin Williams</a> lobbied hard for the part.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-storm_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-storm-20">[20]</a></sup> Jack Nicholson had been producer Michael Uslan's and Bob Kane's choice since 1980. Peters approached Nicholson as far back as 1986, during filming of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Witches_of_Eastwick_(film)" title="The Witches of Eastwick (film)">The Witches of Eastwick</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-empire_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-empire-33">[33]</a></sup> Nicholson had what was known as an "off-the-clock" agreement. His contract specified the number of hours he was entitled to have off each day, from the time he left the set to the time he reported back for filming,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-15">[15]</a></sup> as well as being off for <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Lakers" title="Los Angeles Lakers">Los Angeles Lakers</a> home games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-celfans_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-celfans-34">[34]</a></sup> Nicholson demanded to have all of his scenes shot in a three-week block, but the schedule lapsed into 106 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-empire_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-empire-33">[33]</a></sup> He received a $6 million salary, as well as a large percentage of the <a href="/wiki/Box_office" title="Box office">box office</a> gross estimated between $60 million to $90 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sean_Young" title="Sean Young">Sean Young</a> was originally cast as Vicki Vale, but was injured in a horse-riding accident prior to commencement of filming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shadows_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-shadows-36">[36]</a></sup> Young's departure necessitated an urgent search for an actress who, besides being right for the part, could commit to the film at very short notice. Peters suggested <a href="/wiki/Kim_Basinger" title="Kim Basinger">Kim Basinger</a>: she was able to join the production immediately and was cast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guber_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Guber-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shadows_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-shadows-36">[36]</a></sup> As a fan of <a href="/wiki/Michael_Gough" title="Michael Gough">Michael Gough</a>'s work in various <a href="/wiki/Hammer_Film_Productions" title="Hammer Film Productions">Hammer Film Productions</a>, Burton cast Gough as Bruce Wayne's butler, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Pennyworth" title="Alfred Pennyworth">Alfred Pennyworth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-audio_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-audio-37">[37]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wuhl" title="Robert Wuhl">Robert Wuhl</a> was cast as reporter Alexander Knox. His character was originally supposed to die by the Joker's poison gas in the climax, but the filmmakers "liked [my] character so much," Wuhl said, "that they decided to let me live."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heroes_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-heroes-38">[38]</a></sup> Burton chose <a href="/wiki/Billy_Dee_Williams" title="Billy Dee Williams">Billy Dee Williams</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvey_Dent" title="Harvey Dent">Harvey Dent</a> because he wanted to include the villain <a href="/wiki/Two-Face" title="Two-Face">Two-Face</a> in a future film using the concept of an African-American Two-Face for the black and white concept,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> but <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Lee_Jones" title="Tommy Lee Jones">Tommy Lee Jones</a> was later cast in the role for <i><a href="/wiki/Batman_Forever" title="Batman Forever">Batman Forever</a></i>, which disappointed Williams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heroes_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-heroes-38">[38]</a></sup> Nicholson convinced the filmmakers to cast <a href="/wiki/Tracey_Walter" title="Tracey Walter">Tracey Walter</a> as the Joker's henchman, Bob; in real life, Nicholson and Walter are close friends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kiefer_Sutherland" title="Kiefer Sutherland">Kiefer Sutherland</a> was considered as Robin before the character was deleted from the shooting script.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The rest of the cast included <a href="/wiki/Pat_Hingle" title="Pat Hingle">Pat Hingle</a> as <a href="/wiki/James_Gordon_(comics)" title="James Gordon (comics)">Commissioner Gordon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Hall" title="Jerry Hall">Jerry Hall</a> as Alicia Hunt, <a href="/wiki/Lee_Wallace_(actor)" title="Lee Wallace (actor)">Lee Wallace</a> as Mayor Borg, <a href="/wiki/William_Hootkins" title="William Hootkins">William Hootkins</a> as Lt. Max Eckhardt, and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Palance" title="Jack Palance">Jack Palance</a> as crime boss Carl Grissom.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Knebworth_W_front.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1246" data-file-width="2272" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Knebworth_W_front.JPG/300px-Knebworth_W_front.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Knebworth_W_front.JPG/450px-Knebworth_W_front.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Knebworth_W_front.JPG/600px-Knebworth_W_front.JPG 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Knebworth_W_front.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Knebworth_House" title="Knebworth House">Knebworth House</a> served as the <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Manor" title="Wayne Manor">Wayne Manor</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The filmmakers considered filming <i>Batman</i> entirely on the Warner Bros. <a href="/wiki/Backlot" title="Backlot">backlot</a> in <a href="/wiki/Burbank,_California" title="Burbank, California">Burbank, California</a>, but media interest in the film made them change the location. It was shot at <a href="/wiki/Pinewood_Studios" title="Pinewood Studios">Pinewood Studios</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> from October 1988 to January 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joe_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Joe-42">[42]</a></sup> 18 <a href="/wiki/Sound_stage" title="Sound stage">sound stages</a> were used, almost the entirety of Pinewood's 95-acre backlot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-production_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-production-13">[13]</a></sup> Locations included <a href="/wiki/Knebworth_House" title="Knebworth House">Knebworth House</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hatfield_House" title="Hatfield House">Hatfield House</a> doubling for <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Manor" title="Wayne Manor">Wayne Manor</a>, plus <a href="/wiki/Acton_Lane_Power_Station" title="Acton Lane Power Station">Acton Lane Power Station</a> and <a href="/wiki/Little_Barford_Power_Station" title="Little Barford Power Station">Little Barford Power Station</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manor_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Manor-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The original production budget escalated from $30 million to $48 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guber_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Guber-12">[12]</a></sup> Filming was highly secretive. The unit <a href="/wiki/Publicist" title="Publicist">publicist</a> was offered and refused <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">£</a>10,000 for the first pictures of Jack Nicholson as the Joker. The police were later called in when two reels of footage (about 20 minutes' worth) were stolen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-empire_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-empire-33">[33]</a></sup> With various problems during filming, Burton called it "torture. The worst period of my life!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guber_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-Guber-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hamm was not allowed to perform rewrites during the <a href="/wiki/1988_Writers_Guild_of_America_strike" title="1988 Writers Guild of America strike">1988 Writers Guild of America strike</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_15-5"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Gems" title="Jonathan Gems">Jonathan Gems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warren_Skaaren" title="Warren Skaaren">Warren Skaaren</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_McKeown" title="Charles McKeown">Charles McKeown</a> rewrote the script during filming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Hamm criticized the rewrites, but blamed the changes on Warner Bros.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White_17-5"><a href="#cite_note-White-17">[17]</a></sup> Burton explained, "I don't understand why that became such a problem. We started out with a script that everyone liked, although we recognized it needed a little work."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elfman_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-Elfman-2">[2]</a></sup> Dick Grayson appeared in the shooting script but was deleted, as the filmmakers felt he was irrelevant to the plot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_15-6"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-15">[15]</a></sup> Bob Kane supported this decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-audio_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-audio-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Keaton, who called himself a "logic freak", was concerned that Batman's secret identity would in reality be fairly easy to uncover, and discussed ideas with Burton to better disguise the character, including the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contact_lenses" title="Contact lenses">contact lenses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MTVvoice_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-MTVvoice-45">[45]</a></sup> Ultimately, Keaton decided to perform Batman's voice at a lower register than when he was portraying Bruce Wayne, which became a hallmark of the film version of the character, with <a href="/wiki/Christian_Bale" title="Christian Bale">Christian Bale</a> later using the same technique.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MTVvoice_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-MTVvoice-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Originally in the climax, the Joker was to kill Vicki Vale, sending Batman into a vengeful fury. Jon Peters reworked the climax without telling Burton and commissioned production designer Anton Furst to create a 38-foot (12 m) model of the cathedral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stone_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stone-46">[46]</a></sup> This cost $100,000 when the film was already well over budget. Burton disliked the idea, having no clue how the scene would end: "Here were Jack Nicholson and Kim Basinger walking up this cathedral, and halfway up Jack turns around and says, 'Why am I walking up all these stairs? Where am I going?' 'We'll talk about it when you get to the top!' I had to tell him that I didn't know."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stone_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stone-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span></h3>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:40%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"I envisaged Gotham the way I see it now at Pinewood. They've got it, every building, every trash can, every brick."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">—<i>Batman</i> co-creator Bob Kane when looking at the buildings at Pinewood Studios<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-empire_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-empire-33">[33]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Burton was impressed with <a href="/wiki/Anton_Furst" title="Anton Furst">Anton Furst</a>'s designs in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Company_of_Wolves" title="The Company of Wolves">The Company of Wolves</a></i>, and previously failed to hire Furst as production designer for <i>Beetlejuice</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joe_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-Joe-42">[42]</a></sup> Furst had been too committed on <i><a href="/wiki/High_Spirits_(film)" title="High Spirits (film)">High Spirits</a></i>, a choice he later regretted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_15-7"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-15">[15]</a></sup> Furst enjoyed working with Burton. "I don't think I've ever felt so naturally in tune with a director", he said. "Conceptually, spiritually, visually, or artistically. There was never any problem because we never fought over anything. Texture, attitude and feelings are what Burton is a master at."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-production_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-production-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Keaton_as_Batman.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="347" data-file-width="250" height="243" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/df/Keaton_as_Batman.jpg/175px-Keaton_as_Batman.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/d/df/Keaton_as_Batman.jpg 1.5x" width="175"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Keaton_as_Batman.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Batsuit, worn by Michael Keaton.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Furst and the <a href="/wiki/Art_department" title="Art department">art department</a> deliberately mixed clashing architectural styles to "make Gotham City the ugliest and bleakest <a href="/wiki/Metropolis" title="Metropolis">metropolis</a> imaginable".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Furst continued, "[W]e imagined what New York City might have become without a planning commission. A city run by crime, with a riot of architectural styles. An essay in ugliness. As if hell erupted through the pavement and kept on going".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The 1985 film <i><a href="/wiki/Brazil_(1985_film)" title="Brazil (1985 film)">Brazil</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Terry_Gilliam" title="Terry Gilliam">Terry Gilliam</a> was also a notable influence upon the film's production design, as both Burton and Furst studied it as a reference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-production_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-production-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Derek_Meddings" title="Derek Meddings">Derek Meddings</a> served as the <a href="/wiki/Visual_effects_supervisor" title="Visual effects supervisor">visual effects supervisor</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Keith_Short" title="Keith Short">Keith Short</a> helped construct the newly created <a href="/wiki/Batmobile#Batman_and_Batman_Returns" title="Batmobile">1989 Batmobile</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> adding two <a href="/wiki/M1919_Browning_machine_gun" title="M1919 Browning machine gun">Browning machine guns</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> On designing the Batmobile, Furst explained, "We looked at jet aircraft components, we looked at war machines, we looked at all sorts of things. In the end, we went into pure expressionism, taking the <a href="/wiki/Bonneville_Salt_Flats" title="Bonneville Salt Flats">Salt Flat Racers</a> of the 30s and the <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Corvette_(C2)" title="Chevrolet Corvette (C2)">Sting Ray macho machines</a> of the 50s".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-empire_33-4"><a href="#cite_note-empire-33">[33]</a></sup> The car was built upon a <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Impala" title="Chevrolet Impala">Chevrolet Impala</a> when previous development with a <a href="/wiki/Jaguar_Cars" title="Jaguar Cars">Jaguar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ford_Mustang" title="Ford Mustang">Ford Mustang</a> failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-empire_33-5"><a href="#cite_note-empire-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Costume_design" title="Costume design">Costume designer</a> <a href="/wiki/Bob_Ringwood" title="Bob Ringwood">Bob Ringwood</a> turned down the chance to work on <i><a href="/wiki/Licence_to_Kill" title="Licence to Kill">Licence to Kill</a></i> in favor of <i>Batman</i>. Ringwood found it difficult designing the <a href="/wiki/Batsuit" title="Batsuit">Batsuit</a> because "the image of Batman in the comics is this huge, big six-foot-four hunk with a dimpled chin. Michael Keaton is a guy with average build", he stated. "The problem was to make somebody who was average-sized and ordinary-looking into this bigger-than-life creature."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-batto_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-batto-51">[51]</a></sup> Burton commented, "Michael is a bit <a href="/wiki/Claustrophobia" title="Claustrophobia">claustrophobic</a>, which made it worse for him. The costume put him in a dark, Batman-like mood though, so he was able to use it to his advantage".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-batto_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-batto-51">[51]</a></sup> Burton's idea was to use an all-black suit, and was met with positive feedback by Bob Kane. Jon Peters wanted to use a <a href="/wiki/Nike,_Inc." title="Nike, Inc.">Nike</a> <a href="/wiki/Product_placement" title="Product placement">product placement</a> with the Batsuit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Ringwood studied over 200 comic book issues for inspiration. 28 sculpted latex designs were created; 25 different cape looks and 6 different heads were made, accumulating a total cost of $250,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Comic book fans initially expressed negative feedback against the Batsuit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joe_42-2"><a href="#cite_note-Joe-42">[42]</a></sup> Burton opted not to use tights, spandex, or underpants as seen in the comic book, feeling it was not intimidating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elfman_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-Elfman-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Prosthetic_makeup" title="Prosthetic makeup">Prosthetic makeup</a> designer Nick Dudman used acrylic-based makeup paint called PAX for Nicholson's chalk-white face. Part of Nicholson's contract was approval over the makeup designer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Batman_(album)" title="Batman (album)">Batman (album)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Batman_(score)" title="Batman (score)">Batman (score)</a></div>
<p>Burton hired <a href="/wiki/Danny_Elfman" title="Danny Elfman">Danny Elfman</a>, his collaborator on <i>Pee-wee's Big Adventure</i> and <i>Beetlejuice</i>, to compose the music score. For inspiration, Elfman was given <i>The Dark Knight Returns</i>. Elfman was worried, as he had never worked on a production this large in budget and scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-overtures_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-overtures-55">[55]</a></sup> In addition, producer Jon Peters was skeptical of hiring Elfman, but was later convinced when he heard the opening number.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reborn_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-reborn-56">[56]</a></sup> Peters and Peter Guber wanted <a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">Prince</a> to write music for the Joker and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a> to do the romance songs. Elfman would then combine the style of Prince and Jackson's songs together for the entire film score.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elfman_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-Elfman-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Burton protested the ideas, citing "my movies aren't commercial like <i><a href="/wiki/Top_Gun" title="Top Gun">Top Gun</a></i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elfman_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-Elfman-2">[2]</a></sup> Elfman enlisted the help of <a href="/wiki/Oingo_Boingo" title="Oingo Boingo">Oingo Boingo</a> lead guitarist <a href="/wiki/Steve_Bartek" title="Steve Bartek">Steve Bartek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Walker" title="Shirley Walker">Shirley Walker</a> to arrange the compositions for the orchestra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Elfman was later displeased with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audio_mixing_(film_and_television)" title="Audio mixing (film and television)">audio mixing</a> of his film score. "<i>Batman</i> was done in England by technicians who didn't care, and the non-caring showed," he stated. "I'm not putting down England because they've done gorgeous <a href="/wiki/Dubbing_(filmmaking)" title="Dubbing (filmmaking)">dubs</a> there, but this particular crew elected not to."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> <i>Batman</i> was one of the first films to spawn two soundtracks. One of them featured songs written by Prince while the other showcased Elfman's score. Both were successful,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> and compilations of Elfman's <a href="/wiki/Opening_credits" title="Opening credits">opening credits</a> were used in the <a href="/wiki/Title_sequence" title="Title sequence">title sequence</a> theme for <i><a href="/wiki/Batman:_The_Animated_Series" title="Batman: The Animated Series">Batman: The Animated Series</a></i>, also composed by Shirley Walker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reboot_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-reboot-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing">Marketing</span></h2>
<p>Production designer Anton Furst designed the poster, which he called "evocative but ubiquitous. Only featuring the Bat-Symbol. Not too much and not too little". Earlier designs "had the word 'Batman' spelled in <i><a href="/wiki/RoboCop" title="RoboCop">RoboCop</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Conan_the_Barbarian_(1982_film)" title="Conan the Barbarian (1982 film)">Conan the Barbarian</a></i>-type font".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guber_12-6"><a href="#cite_note-Guber-12">[12]</a></sup> Jon Peters unified all the film's <a href="/wiki/Tie-in" title="Tie-in">tie-ins</a>, even turning down $6 million from <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a> to build the <a href="/wiki/Batmobile" title="Batmobile">Batmobile</a> because the car company would not relinquish creative control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guber_12-7"><a href="#cite_note-Guber-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>During production, Peters read in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> that comic book fans were unsatisfied with the casting of Michael Keaton. In response, Peters rushed the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_trailer" title="Film trailer">film trailer</a> that played in thousands of theaters during Christmas. It was simply an assemblage of scenes without music, but happened to create enormous anticipation for the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guber_12-8"><a href="#cite_note-Guber-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a> allowed screenwriter Sam Hamm to write his own <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Limited_series" title="Limited series">comic book miniseries</a>. Hamm's stories were collected in the <a href="/wiki/Graphic_novel" title="Graphic novel">graphic novel</a> <i>Batman: Blind Justice</i> (<a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9781563890475">ISBN 978-1563890475</a>). <a href="/wiki/Denys_Cowan" title="Denys Cowan">Denys Cowan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dick_Giordano" title="Dick Giordano">Dick Giordano</a> illustrated the artwork.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hamm_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hamm-19">[19]</a></sup> <i>Blind Justice</i> tells the story of Bruce Wayne trying to solve a series of murders connected to <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Enterprises" title="Wayne Enterprises">Wayne Enterprises</a>. It also marks the first appearance of <a href="/wiki/Henri_Ducard" title="Henri Ducard">Henri Ducard</a>, who was later used in the <a href="/wiki/Reboot_(fiction)" title="Reboot (fiction)">rebooted</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Batman_Begins" title="Batman Begins">Batman Begins</a></i>, albeit as an alias for the more notable <a href="/wiki/Ra%27s_al_Ghul" title="Ra's al Ghul">Ra's al Ghul</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hamm_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hamm-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the months pre-dating <i>Batman</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s release in June 1989, a <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">popular culture</a> phenomenon rose known as "Batmania".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joe_42-3"><a href="#cite_note-Joe-42">[42]</a></sup> Over $750 million worth of merchandise was sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reboot_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-reboot-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cult_film" title="Cult film">Cult filmmaker</a> and comic book writer <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Smith" title="Kevin Smith">Kevin Smith</a> remembered: "That summer was huge. You couldn't turn around without seeing the Bat-Signal somewhere. People were cutting it into their fucking heads. It was just the summer of Batman and if you were a comic book fan it was pretty hot."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hachette_Book_Group_USA" title="Hachette Book Group USA">Hachette Book Group USA</a> published a novelization, <i>Batman</i> written by <a href="/wiki/Craig_Shaw_Gardner" title="Craig Shaw Gardner">Craig Shaw Gardner</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> It remained on the <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Best_Seller_list" title="The New York Times Best Seller list"><i>New York Times</i> Best Seller list</a> throughout June 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Burton admitted he was annoyed by the publicity. David Handelman of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_New_York_Observer" title="The New York Observer">The New York Observer</a></i> categorized <i>Batman</i> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_concept" title="High concept">high concept</a> film. He believed "it is less movie than a corporate <a href="/wiki/Behemoth" title="Behemoth">behemoth</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cartoon_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-cartoon-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span></h3>
<p><i>Batman</i> opened on June 23, 1989, grossing $43.6 million in 2,194 theaters during its opening weekend. This broke the opening weekend record, set by <i><a href="/wiki/Ghostbusters_II" title="Ghostbusters II">Ghostbusters II</a></i> one week earlier, with $29.4 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The film closed on December 14, 1989, with a final gross $251.2 million in North America and $160.15 million internationally, totaling $411.35 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> <i>Batman</i> was the first film to earn $100 million in its first ten days of release,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elfman_2-13"><a href="#cite_note-Elfman-2">[2]</a></sup> and was the highest grossing <a href="/wiki/List_of_films_based_on_DC_Comics" title="List of films based on DC Comics">film based on a DC comic book</a> until 2008's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dark_Knight_(film)" title="The Dark Knight (film)">The Dark Knight</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> The film's gross is the 66th highest ever in North American ranks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Although <i><a href="/wiki/Indiana_Jones_and_the_Last_Crusade" title="Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade">Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade</a></i> made the most money worldwide in 1989,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> <i>Batman</i> was able to beat <i>The Last Crusade</i> in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> and made a further $150 million in <a href="/wiki/Home_video" title="Home video">home video</a> sales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mad_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mad-69">[69]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Box_Office_Mojo" title="Box Office Mojo">Box Office Mojo</a> estimates that the film sold more than 60 million tickets in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reaction">Critical reaction</span></h3>
<p><i>Batman</i> was criticized in some quarters for being too dark and too violent, but nonetheless received positive reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elfman_2-14"><a href="#cite_note-Elfman-2">[2]</a></sup> Many observed that Burton was more interested in the Joker and the art and set production design than Batman or anything else in terms of characterization and screentime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elfman_2-15"><a href="#cite_note-Elfman-2">[2]</a></sup> Comic book fans reacted negatively over the Joker murdering <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Wayne" title="Thomas Wayne">Thomas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martha_Wayne" title="Martha Wayne">Martha Wayne</a>; in the comic book, <a href="/wiki/Joe_Chill" title="Joe Chill">Joe Chill</a> is responsible. Writer Sam Hamm said it was Burton's idea to have the Joker murder Wayne's parents. "The Writer's Strike was going on," Hamm said, "and Tim had the other writers do that. I also hold innocent to Alfred letting Vicki Vale into the <a href="/wiki/Batcave" title="Batcave">Batcave</a>. Fans were ticked off with that, and I agree. That would have been Alfred's last day of employment at <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Manor" title="Wayne Manor">Wayne Manor</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reborn_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-reborn-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>The songs written by Prince were criticized for being "too out of place".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_15-8"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-15">[15]</a></sup> While Burton has stated he had no problem with the Prince songs, he was less enthusiastic with their use in the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forever_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-forever-3">[3]</a></sup> On the film, Burton remarked, "I liked parts of it, but the whole movie is mainly boring to me. It's OK, but it was more of a cultural phenomenon than a great movie."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mad_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mad-69">[69]</a></sup> Nonetheless, it received acclaim. Based on 67 reviews collected by <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, 72% of reviewers reacted positively to <i>Batman</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> gives an aggregated score of 66, based on 17 reviews, indicating "generally favorable".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite initial negative reactions from comics fans prior to the film's release, Keaton's portrayal of Batman was generally praised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cartoon_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-cartoon-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/James_Berardinelli" title="James Berardinelli">James Berardinelli</a> called the film entertaining, with the highlight being the production design. However, he concluded, "the best thing that can be said about <i>Batman</i> is that it led to <i><a href="/wiki/Batman_Returns" title="Batman Returns">Batman Returns</a></i>, which was a far superior effort."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> felt "Jack Nicholson stole every scene" but still greeted the film with positive feedback.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> was highly impressed with the production design, but claimed "<i>Batman</i> is a triumph of design over story, style over substance, a great-looking movie with a plot you can't care much about." He also called the film "a depressing experience".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> His reviewing partner <a href="/wiki/Gene_Siskel" title="Gene Siskel">Gene Siskel</a> disagreed, describing the film as having a 'refreshingly adult' approach with performances, direction and set design that 'draws you into a psychological world'.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Anton_Furst" title="Anton Furst">Anton Furst</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Young_(set_decorator)" title="Peter Young (set decorator)">Peter Young</a> won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Art_Direction" title="Academy Award for Best Art Direction">Academy Award for Best Art Direction</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> while Nicholson was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Actor_%E2%80%93_Motion_Picture_Musical_or_Comedy" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Actor – Motion Picture Musical or Comedy">Golden Globe Award for Best Actor (Musical or Comedy)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_of_Film_and_Television_Arts" title="British Academy of Film and Television Arts">British Academy of Film and Television Arts</a> nominated <i>Batman</i> in six categories (<a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Production_Design" title="BAFTA Award for Best Production Design">Production Design</a>, <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Special_Visual_Effects" title="BAFTA Award for Best Special Visual Effects">Visual Effects</a>, <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Costume_Design" title="BAFTA Award for Best Costume Design">Costume Design</a>, <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Makeup_and_Hair" title="BAFTA Award for Best Makeup and Hair">Makeup</a>, <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Sound" title="BAFTA Award for Best Sound">Sound</a> and <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Actor_in_a_Supporting_Role" title="BAFTA Award for Best Actor in a Supporting Role">Actor in a Supporting Role</a> for Nicholson), but it won none of the categories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> Nicholson, Basinger, the makeup department, and <a href="/wiki/Costume_designer" title="Costume designer">costume designer</a> <a href="/wiki/Bob_Ringwood" title="Bob Ringwood">Bob Ringwood</a> all received nominations at the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_Award" title="Saturn Award">Saturn Awards</a>. The film was also nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_Award_for_Best_Fantasy_Film" title="Saturn Award for Best Fantasy Film">Saturn Award for Best Fantasy Film</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Award_for_Best_Dramatic_Presentation" title="Hugo Award for Best Dramatic Presentation">Hugo Award for Best Dramatic Presentation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>The success of <i>Batman</i> prompted <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Animation" title="Warner Bros. Animation">Warner Bros. Animation</a> to create the acclaimed <i><a href="/wiki/Batman:_The_Animated_Series" title="Batman: The Animated Series">Batman: The Animated Series</a></i>, as a result beginning the long-running <a href="/wiki/DC_animated_universe" title="DC animated universe">DC animated universe</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> and helped establish the modern day <a href="/wiki/Superhero_film" title="Superhero film">superhero film</a> genre. Series co-creator <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Timm" title="Bruce Timm">Bruce Timm</a> stated the television show's <a href="/wiki/Art_Deco" title="Art Deco">Art Deco</a> design was inspired from the film. Timm commented, "our show would never have gotten made if it hadn't been for that first <i>Batman</i> movie."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> Burton joked, "ever since I did <i>Batman</i>, it was like the first dark comic book movie. Now everyone wants to do a dark and serious superhero movie. I guess I'm the one responsible for that trend."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Batman</i> initiated the <a href="/wiki/Batman_in_film#Tim_Burton.2FJoel_Schumahcer" title="Batman in film">original <i>Batman</i> film series</a> and spawned three sequels: <i><a href="/wiki/Batman_Returns" title="Batman Returns">Batman Returns</a></i> (1992), <i><a href="/wiki/Batman_Forever" title="Batman Forever">Batman Forever</a></i> (1995) and <i><a href="/wiki/Batman_%26_Robin_(film)" title="Batman &amp; Robin (film)">Batman &amp; Robin</a></i> (1997); the latter two of which were directed by <a href="/wiki/Joel_Schumacher" title="Joel Schumacher">Joel Schumacher</a> instead of Burton, and replaced Keaton as Batman with <a href="/wiki/Val_Kilmer" title="Val Kilmer">Val Kilmer</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Clooney" title="George Clooney">George Clooney</a>, respectively.</p>
<p>Producers <a href="/wiki/Michael_Uslan" title="Michael Uslan">Michael Uslan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Melniker" title="Benjamin Melniker">Benjamin Melniker</a> filed a <a href="/wiki/Breach_of_contract" title="Breach of contract">breach of contract</a> lawsuit in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_County_Board_of_Supervisors" title="Los Angeles County Board of Supervisors">Los Angeles County Superior Court</a> on March 26, 1992. Uslan and Melniker claimed to be "the victims of a sinister campaign of fraud and coercion that has cheated them out of continuing involvement in the production of <i>Batman</i> and its sequels. We were denied proper credits, and deprived of any financial rewards for our indispensable creative contribution to the success of <i>Batman</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guber_12-9"><a href="#cite_note-Guber-12">[12]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Superior_court" title="Superior court">superior court</a> judge rejected the lawsuit. Total revenues of <i>Batman</i> have topped $2 billion, with Uslan claiming to have "not seen a penny more than that since our <a href="/wiki/Net_profit" title="Net profit">net profit</a> participation has proved worthless."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guber_12-10"><a href="#cite_note-Guber-12">[12]</a></sup> Warner Bros. offered the pair an out-of-court pay-off, a sum described by Uslan and Melniker's attorney as "two popcorns and two <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">Cokes</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p>Reflecting on the twentieth anniversary of its release in a retrospective article on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salon.com" title="Salon.com">Salon.com</a>, film commentator Scott Mendelson noted the continuing impact that <i>Batman</i> has had on the motion film industry, including the increasing importance of opening weekend box office receipts; the narrowing window between a film's debut and its video release that caused the demise of second-run movie theaters; the accelerated acquisition of pre-existing, pre-sold properties for film adaptations that can be readily leveraged for merchandizing tie-ins; the primacy of the <a href="/wiki/Motion_Picture_Association_of_America" title="Motion Picture Association of America">MPAA</a> <a href="/wiki/Motion_Picture_Association_of_America_film_rating_system" title="Motion Picture Association of America film rating system">PG-13</a> as the target rating for film producers; and more off-beat, non-traditional casting opportunities for genre films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Film_Institute" title="American Film Institute">American Film Institute</a> anointed Batman the 46th greatest movie hero and the Joker the 45th greatest movie villain on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years...100_Heroes_and_Villains" title="AFI's 100 Years...100 Heroes and Villains">AFI's 100 Years...100 Heroes and Villains</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_video">Home video</span></h3>
<p>Many versions of the film have been released. Included are <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">VHS</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laserdisc" title="Laserdisc">Laserdisc</a>, single-disc <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a>, special edition DVD and an anthology set. The <i>Batman: The Motion Picture Anthology</i> released in 2005 included 2-disc special edition DVDs of the Tim Burton/<a href="/wiki/Joel_Schumacher" title="Joel Schumacher">Joel Schumacher</a> <i>Batman</i> films. This anthology set was re-released on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray</a> on March 10, 2009.</p>
<p>On May 19, 2009 a 20th anniversary stand-alone edition was released. This stand-alone version contains exactly the same special features as its anthology set (both DVD and Blu-ray) counterparts. There are two differences: This version includes a 50-page booklet guide to the film, and a slight variation in packaging from normal Blu-ray cases (i.e. a Warner Bros. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_book" title="Digital book">digibook</a>). They both include a digital copy of the film.</p>
<p>Warner Bros. also released the 25th anniversary edition on Blu Ray on December 20, 2014 with all of the same special features shown on DVD and Blu Ray and comes with a new featurette called "Batman: The Birth of the Modern Blockbuster".</p>
<p>The film was also included in <i>The Tim Burton Collection</i> DVD/Blu-ray set in 2012, alongside its sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/Batman_Returns" title="Batman Returns">Batman Returns</a></i>, and several other Burton films.</p>
