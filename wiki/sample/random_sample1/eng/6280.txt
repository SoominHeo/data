<p><i><b>Batman</b></i> is a 1960s <a href="/wiki/Television_in_the_United_States" title="Television in the United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Live_action" title="Live action">live action</a> <a href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">television series</a>, based on the <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC</a> <a href="/wiki/Comic_book" title="Comic book">comic book</a> <a href="/wiki/Batman" title="Batman">character of the same name</a>. It stars <a href="/wiki/Adam_West" title="Adam West">Adam West</a> as <a href="/wiki/Batman" title="Batman">Batman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Burt_Ward" title="Burt Ward">Burt Ward</a> as <a href="/wiki/Dick_Grayson" title="Dick Grayson">Robin</a> — two crime-fighting heroes who defend <a href="/wiki/Gotham_City" title="Gotham City">Gotham City</a> from a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Batman_television_series_characters#Criminals" title="List of Batman television series characters">variety of arch villains</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It is known for its <a href="/wiki/Camp_(style)" title="Camp (style)">camp style</a>, <a href="/wiki/Batman_Theme" title="Batman Theme">upbeat theme music</a>, and its intentionally humorous, simplistic morality (aimed at its largely teenage audience). This included championing the importance of using seat belts, doing homework, eating vegetables, and drinking milk<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> (it was described by executive producer William Dozier as the only situation comedy on the air without a laughtrack). <a href="/wiki/List_of_Batman_(TV_series)_episodes" title="List of Batman (TV series) episodes">One hundred and twenty episodes</a> aired on the <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a> network for three seasons from January 12, 1966, to March 14, 1968, twice weekly for the first two and weekly for the third. In 2016, television critics <a href="/wiki/Alan_Sepinwall" title="Alan Sepinwall">Alan Sepinwall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matt_Zoller_Seitz" title="Matt Zoller Seitz">Matt Zoller Seitz</a> ranked <i>Batman</i> as the 82nd greatest American television show of all time.</p>

<p>Ostensibly a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Crime_series&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Crime series (page does not exist)">crime series</a>, the style of the show was in fact campy and tongue-in-cheek. It was a true situation comedy, in that situations were exaggerated and were generally played for laughs. This increased as the seasons wore on, with the addition of ever greater absurdity. The characters, however, always took the absurd situations extremely seriously - which added to the comedy.</p>
<p>The series focused on the adventures of Batman and Robin. Although the lives of their alter-egos, millionaire Bruce Wayne and his ward Dick Grayson, were frequently shown, it was usually only briefly, in the context of their being called away on superhero business, or in circumstances where they needed to employ their secret identities to assist in their crime-fighting. The "Dynamic Duo" typically come to the aid of the Gotham City Police upon the latter being stumped by a supervillain. Throughout each episode, Batman and Robin have to follow a series of (wildly improbable) clues to discover the supervillain's plan, then figure out how to thwart that plan and capture the criminal.</p>
<p>For the first two seasons, Batman aired twice a week on consecutive nights. Every story is a two-parter, except for two three-parters featuring villainous team ups (The Joker and The Penguin, The Penguin and Marsha, Queen of Diamonds) in the second season. The titles of each multi-part story usually rhymed. For the third season, which aired once a week, most episodes were self-contained stories. The cliffhangers between multiple-parters typically consisted of the supervillain holding someone captive, usually the Dynamic Duo, with the captives being subject to some elaborate, gruesome - if unlikely - death. This would inevitably be resolved early in the follow-up episode.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Typical_episode_format_and_elements">Typical episode format and elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Typical episode format and elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Each story relies on using the same formula so that the audience quickly come to expect a series of familiar set pieces: a phone call from the police asking for Batman's help, a dash to the Batcave, a race in the Batmobile to police headquarters, a conference in the Commissioner's office, investigating the scene of the crime, examining clues in the crime-lab at the Batcave, rushing to the villain's secret hideout, falling into the pre-arranged trap. By relying heavily on a formula, it becomes easy to spoof various elements of that formula.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Teaser_and_exposition">Teaser and exposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Teaser and exposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The typical story begins with a villain's caper (such as stealing a fabulous treasure, kidnapping a prominent person, or attempting to take over <a href="/wiki/Gotham_City" title="Gotham City">Gotham City</a>). In his office, <a href="/wiki/List_of_Batman_television_series_characters#Commissioner_Gordon" title="List of Batman television series characters">Commissioner Gordon</a>, along with Chief O'Hara, learn of the crime and the culprit. Helpless to stop the villain, they contact Batman via the Batphone - a bright red telephone that provides a direct phone link to Batman (be it at Wayne Manor, the Batcave or the Batmobile). At "stately <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Manor" title="Wayne Manor">Wayne Manor</a>", <a href="/wiki/List_of_Batman_television_series_characters#Alfred_Pennyworth" title="List of Batman television series characters">Alfred</a> (Wayne's butler) answers the Batphone and informs <a href="/wiki/List_of_Batman_television_series_characters#Bruce_Wayne" title="List of Batman television series characters">Bruce Wayne</a> of the call. Frequently, Wayne and his ward, <a href="/wiki/List_of_Batman_television_series_characters#Dick_Grayson" title="List of Batman television series characters">Dick Grayson</a>, are found talking with Dick's aunt, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aunt_Harriet_(Batman_1966)" title="Aunt Harriet (Batman 1966)">Harriet Cooper</a>, who is unaware of Bruce and Dick's secret identities. Alfred discreetly interrupts and they excuse themselves to go to the Batphone in Wayne's study. Upon learning the details from Gordon, Wayne turns a switch concealed within a bust of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a> that stands on his desk to reveal two <a href="/wiki/Fireman%27s_pole" title="Fireman's pole">fireman's poles</a> hidden behind a sliding bookcase. "To the Batpoles!" Wayne exclaims, and he and Grayson slide down the poles that lead to the <a href="/wiki/Batcave" title="Batcave">Batcave</a>.</p>
<p>The title sequence features animated versions of Batman and Robin, drawn in the then-current style of the comic books, running towards camera and then fighting an assortment of villains, including several "marquee" villains such as the Joker and the Penguin.</p>

<p>Similar in style and content to the 1940s serials, Batman and Robin would arrive at the bottom of the Batpoles in the Batcave in full costume (reference is made later in the series to some sort of costuming device that functions on the way down the poles). They then jump into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Batmobile_(1966)" title="Batmobile (1966)">Batmobile</a>. Robin checks the gauges and reports, "<a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">Atomic</a> <a href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">batteries</a> to power...<a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbines</a> to speed." Batman responds, "Roger, ready to move out." With that, after fastening their seat belts, the two would drive out of the cave at high speed. As the Batmobile approached the mouth of the cave (actually a tunnel entrance in Los Angeles's <a href="/wiki/Bronson_Canyon" title="Bronson Canyon">Bronson Canyon</a>), a camouflaged door would swing open and a hinged road barrier outside the Batcave would drop down to allow the car to exit onto the road. The Duo then speeds to police headquarters to meet with Gordon and be briefed on the criminal they must thwart. Most of the footage following the opening title sequence from Batman and Robin sliding down the Batpoles through their arrival at police headquarters was <a href="/wiki/Stock_footage" title="Stock footage">reused</a> in each episode.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Investigation">Investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Investigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The initial discussion of the crime usually leads to Batman and Robin conducting their investigation alone, although the police are often used for assistance and to implement plans or traps that Batman devises to catch the villain. Typically Batman and Robin must use deduction from the clues left by the villain to determine elements such as the villain's identity, plan, target, and/or location. This usually results in a meeting with the villain, a fistfight with the villain's henchmen, and the villain's escape, leaving a further series of unlikely clues for the Duo to investigate. Later, they would face the villain's henchmen again, be captured and one or both heroes placed in a <a href="/wiki/Deathtrap_(plot_device)" title="Deathtrap (plot device)">deathtrap</a> leading to a <a href="/wiki/Cliffhanger" title="Cliffhanger">cliffhanger</a> ending, which was usually resolved in the first few minutes of the next episode.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_cliffhanger">After the cliffhanger</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: After the cliffhanger">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The latter part(s) of multi-episode stories begins with a brief recap of the first part(s). After the opening credits and the theme music, the cliffhanger is resolved.</p>
<p>The same general plot pattern of investigation and confrontation repeats in the following episode until the villain is defeated in a major brawl.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_recurring_elements">Other recurring elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other recurring elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The series used a narrator (executive producer <a href="/wiki/William_Dozier" title="William Dozier">William Dozier</a>, uncredited) who parodied both the breathless narration style of the 1940s serials and <a href="/wiki/Walter_Winchell" title="Walter Winchell">Walter Winchell</a>'s bombastic narration of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Untouchables_(1959_TV_series)" title="The Untouchables (1959 TV series)">The Untouchables</a>.</i> He would end many of the cliffhanger episodes by intoning, "Tune in tomorrow — same Bat-time, same Bat-channel!" In the episodes following cliffhangers, recaps of the previous episode consisted of a series of short phrases from Dozier accompanying short, silent clips of the prior episode, which usually ending in a freeze-frame. In the third season, when single-episode stories were introduced, the narrator would end the show by encouraging viewers to tune in the following week.</p>
<p>During the climactic fistfights in each episode, the punches and other impacts were punctuated by <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeia</a> (sound effects) superimposed in bright colours over the action on the screen, as in comic-book fight scenes ("POW!", "BAM!", "ZONK!", etc.). As a money saving device, after the first season, instead of being superimposed over the fight scene, the sound effects were merely printed on cards and inserted into the action.</p>
<p>Despite the regular fighting on the show, Batman and Robin typically use non-lethal force; only three criminal characters die during the series: the Riddler's <a href="/wiki/Moll_(slang)" title="Moll (slang)">moll</a> Molly (played by <a href="/wiki/Jill_St._John" title="Jill St. John">Jill St. John</a> in the pilot episode), who accidentally falls into the Batcave's atomic reactor, and two out-of-town gunmen who shoot at Batman and Robin, but kill each other instead, toward the end of "Zelda The Great/A Death Worse Than Fate". In the film, six criminals die in total: Five henchmen are dehydrated by the Penguin in order to infiltrate the Batcave, but this plan fails when the henchmen unexpectedly disappear into antimatter once struck. A sixth henchman is presumably killed when he is caught in a trap meant for Batman and sprung into an exploding octopus. Twice, the Catwoman (<a href="/wiki/Julie_Newmar" title="Julie Newmar">Julie Newmar</a>) appears to fall to her death (into a bottomless pit and from a high building into a river), but returned in later episodes; as a "cat", she presumably has nine lives. In "Instant Freeze", Mr. Freeze freezes a butler solid and knocks him over, and sound-effects suggest that he is shattered into pieces. A later reference suggests the butler survived. In "Green Ice", Mr. Freeze freezes a policeman solid; it is left unclear whether he survived. In "The Penguin's Nest", a policeman suffers an electric shock at the hands of the Penguin's accomplices, but he is presumed to survive, as he appears in some later episodes.</p>
<p>A catch-phrase popularized by the series was Robin's saying "Holy [subject], Batman!" whenever he encountered something startling. This phrase was parodied in the 1995 film <i><a href="/wiki/Batman_Forever" title="Batman Forever">Batman Forever</a></i>.</p>
<p>In many episodes, Batman and Robin must get to a high point of a building or other structure. They do this via the Batrope which is thrown and anchored above the high point, and which Batman and Robin climb by walking up the side of the structure with the aid of the rope. The climbing sequences were filmed by rotating the camera 90 degrees and building a set for the "side" of the structure along the studio floor. The heroes' capes were pulled back (to replicate the pull of gravity) with invisible lines. In many episodes, celebrities made <a href="/wiki/Cameo_appearance" title="Cameo appearance">cameo appearances</a> by popping their heads out of windows along these climbs to speak to the heroes.</p>
<p>In one episode, the Catwoman's hideout is a hair salon owned by a "Mr. Oceanbring." The real-life inspiration for the character, hair stylist (and future Manson family victim) <a href="/wiki/Jay_Sebring" title="Jay Sebring">Jay Sebring</a>, appears uncredited during the fight scene and has a couple of spoken lines.</p>
<p>The villains commonly have henchmen whose names are somehow associated with the villain's identity; for example, Catwoman's henchmen have cat-related names like Felix and Leo. Characters commonly use alliterations. Examples include Batman referring to the Joker as a "hateful harlequin" and the Penguin calling Catwoman a "feline floozy".</p>
<p>Only two of the show's guest villains ever discovered Batman's true identity: <a href="/wiki/Egghead_(DC_Comics)" title="Egghead (DC Comics)">Egghead</a> by deductive reasoning, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Batman_television_series_characters#King_Tut" title="List of Batman television series characters">King Tut</a> on two occasions — the first time with a bug placed on the Batmobile and the second time by accidentally mining into the Batcave. Egghead was tricked into disbelieving his discovery, though, as was Tut in the episode when he bugged the Batmobile. In the episode when Tut tunneled into the Batcave, he was hit on the head by a rock, which made him forget his discovery and jarred him back into his identity as a mild-mannered professor of Egyptology at <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a>. While under the spell of the Siren (<a href="/wiki/Joan_Collins" title="Joan Collins">Joan Collins</a>), Commissioner Gordon found the Batcave beneath Wayne Manor and deduced Batman's true identity, but Alfred gassed him to prevent his informing her, the memory of the discovery gone after leaving the Siren's spell.</p>
<p>The show's campiness was played up in elements, including the design of the villains, dialogue and in signs appearing on various props. Batman would frequently reveal one of his many crime-fighting gadgets, which were usually given a ridiculous-sounding name that somehow incorporated the word "bat" — often simply by adding the word "bat" to an otherwise normal descriptor, such as Shark Repellant Bat-Spray, Bat-Computer, Extra-Strong Bat-Knockout Gas, etc. Most of Batman's items in the Batcave, bat-vehicles and on the utility belt were given superfluous and simplistic block-letter labels, even though Batman, Robin and Alfred, the only people who used the equipment, clearly knew what all of it was.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regular_cast">Regular cast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Regular cast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Adam_West" title="Adam West">Adam West</a> as <b><a href="/wiki/List_of_Batman_television_series_characters#Batman" title="List of Batman television series characters">Bruce Wayne / Batman</a></b>: A millionaire whose parents were murdered by criminals, he now secretly uses his vast fortune to fight crime as Batman. Producer William Dozier cast Adam West in the role after seeing him perform as the <a href="/wiki/James_Bond" title="James Bond">James Bond</a>-like spy Captain Q in a Nestlé Quik television ad. <a href="/wiki/Lyle_Waggoner" title="Lyle Waggoner">Lyle Waggoner</a> had screen-tested for the role, though West ultimately won out because, it was said, he was the only person who could deliver the lines with a straight face. West later voiced an animated version of the title character on <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Adventures_of_Batman" title="The New Adventures of Batman">The New Adventures of Batman</a></i> and well as <i><a href="/wiki/Super_Friends:_The_Legendary_Super_Powers_Show" title="Super Friends: The Legendary Super Powers Show">Super Friends: The Legendary Super Powers Show</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Super_Powers_Team:_Galactic_Guardians" title="The Super Powers Team: Galactic Guardians">The Super Powers Team: Galactic Guardians</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Burt_Ward" title="Burt Ward">Burt Ward</a> as <b><a href="/wiki/List_of_Batman_television_series_characters#Robin" title="List of Batman television series characters">Dick Grayson / Robin</a></b>: Batman's faithful (if overly eager) partner and "Boy Wonder", a high school student noted for his recurring interjections in the form of "Holy ________, Batman!" (The series avoided referencing Robin's origins as Bruce Wayne's fellow "crime orphan", as whose legal guardian the courts appoint Bruce.) Ward voiced an animated version of this character on <i>The New Adventures of Batman</i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alan_Napier" title="Alan Napier">Alan Napier</a> as <b><a href="/wiki/List_of_Batman_television_series_characters#Alfred" title="List of Batman television series characters">Alfred</a></b>: Batman's loyal butler and Batgirl's discreet confidant. He is the only person who knows the true identities of Bruce Wayne, Dick Grayson and Barbara Gordon.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Neil_Hamilton_(actor)" title="Neil Hamilton (actor)">Neil Hamilton</a> as <b><a href="/wiki/List_of_Batman_television_series_characters#James_Gordon" title="List of Batman television series characters">Commissioner Gordon</a></b>: The Commissioner of the Gotham City Police Department and one of Batman's two major police contacts. He summons the Dynamic Duo via the Batphone or the Bat Signal.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stafford_Repp" title="Stafford Repp">Stafford Repp</a> as <b><a href="/wiki/List_of_Batman_television_series_characters#Chief_O.27Hara" title="List of Batman television series characters">Chief O'Hara</a></b>: Gotham City's Chief of Police, and Batman's other major police contact. The character was created by Semple for the series, as someone for Gordon to talk to, and later briefly added to the comics.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Madge_Blake" title="Madge Blake">Madge Blake</a> as <b><a href="/wiki/List_of_Batman_television_series_characters#Aunt_Harriet" title="List of Batman television series characters">Harriet Cooper</a></b>: Dick Grayson's maternal aunt. She first appeared in the comics, two years before the series premiered, to give Bruce and Dick a reason to be secretive about their dual identities.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yvonne_Craig" title="Yvonne Craig">Yvonne Craig</a> as <b><a href="/wiki/List_of_Batman_television_series_characters#Batgirl" title="List of Batman television series characters">Barbara Gordon / Batgirl</a></b>: Commissioner Gordon's daughter, Gotham City librarian and crime fighting partner for Batman and Robin for the third season. Occasionally this threesome was nicknamed the "Terrific Trio".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/William_Dozier" title="William Dozier">William Dozier</a> - Executive producer, creator, and narrator (uncredited).</li>
</ul>
<p>According to Adam West's memoir, <i>Back to the Batcave</i>, his first exposure to the series concept was through reading a sample script in which Batman enters a nightclub in his complete costume and requests a booth near the wall, as he "shouldn't wish to attract attention". It was the scrupulously formal dialogue, and the way that Batman earnestly believed he could avoid standing out while wearing a skintight blue-and-grey costume, that convinced West of the character's comic potential.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the early 1960s, Ed Graham Productions <a href="/wiki/Option_(filmmaking)" title="Option (filmmaking)">optioned</a> the television rights to the <a href="/wiki/Comic_strip" title="Comic strip">comic strip</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Batman_(comics)" title="Batman (comics)">Batman</a></i> and planned a straightforward juvenile adventure show, much like <i><a href="/wiki/Adventures_of_Superman_(TV_series)" title="Adventures of Superman (TV series)">Adventures of Superman</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lone_Ranger_(TV_series)" title="The Lone Ranger (TV series)">The Lone Ranger</a>,</i> to air on <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> on Saturday mornings.</p>
<p>East coast ABC executive Yale Udoff, a Batman fan in his childhood, contacted ABC executives <a href="/wiki/Harve_Bennett" title="Harve Bennett">Harve Bennett</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edgar_J._Scherick" title="Edgar J. Scherick">Edgar J. Scherick</a>, who were already considering developing a television series based on a comic-strip action hero, to suggest a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime-time" title="Prime-time">prime-time</a> <i>Batman</i> series in the hip and fun style of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Man_from_U.N.C.L.E." title="The Man from U.N.C.L.E.">The Man from U.N.C.L.E.</a></i> When negotiations between CBS and Graham stalled, DC Comics quickly reobtained rights and made the deal with ABC, which farmed the rights out to <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a> to produce the series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tested_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-tested-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In turn, 20th Century Fox handed the project to <a href="/wiki/William_Dozier" title="William Dozier">William Dozier</a> and his production company, Greenway Productions. ABC and Fox were expecting a hip and fun—yet still serious—adventure show. However, Dozier, who had never before read comic books, concluded, after reading several Batman comics for research, that the only way to make the show work was to do it as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pop-art" title="Pop-art">pop-art</a> <a href="/wiki/Camp_(style)" title="Camp (style)">campy</a> comedy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Originally, espionage novelist <a href="/wiki/Eric_Ambler" title="Eric Ambler">Eric Ambler</a> was to have scripted a TV movie that would launch the television series, but he dropped out after learning of Dozier's campy comedy approach. Eventually, two sets of screen tests were filmed, one with <a href="/wiki/Adam_West" title="Adam West">Adam West</a> and <a href="/wiki/Burt_Ward" title="Burt Ward">Burt Ward</a> and the other with <a href="/wiki/Lyle_Waggoner" title="Lyle Waggoner">Lyle Waggoner</a> and Peter Deyell, with West and Ward winning the roles.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_1">Season 1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Season 1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorenzo_Semple,_Jr." title="Lorenzo Semple, Jr.">Lorenzo Semple, Jr.</a> had signed on as head script writer. He wrote the <a href="/wiki/Television_pilot" title="Television pilot">pilot</a> script, and generally wrote in a pop-art adventure style. <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Ralph_Ross" title="Stanley Ralph Ross">Stanley Ralph Ross</a>, Stanford Sherman, and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Hoffman_(screenwriter)" title="Charles Hoffman (screenwriter)">Charles Hoffman</a> were script writers who generally leaned more toward campy comedy, and in Ross's case, sometimes outright slapstick and satire. It was originally intended as a one-hour show, but ABC pushed up the premiere date from fall 1966 to January of that year. With the network having only two early-evening half-hour time slots available, the show was split into two parts, to air twice a week in 30-minute installments. A cliffhanger connected the two episodes, echoing the old <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Movie_serials" title="Movie serials">movie serials</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joker_(comics)" title="Joker (comics)">The Joker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Penguin_(comics)" title="Penguin (comics)">the Penguin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Riddler" title="Riddler">the Riddler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catwoman" title="Catwoman">Catwoman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mr._Freeze" title="Mr. Freeze">Mr. Freeze</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mad_Hatter_(comics)" title="Mad Hatter (comics)">the Mad Hatter</a>, villains who originated in the comic books, all appeared in the series, the plots for which were deliberately villain driven. According to the producers, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Gorshin" title="Frank Gorshin">Frank Gorshin</a> was selected to portray the <a href="/wiki/Riddler" title="Riddler">Riddler</a> due to the fact that he was a <a href="/wiki/Batman" title="Batman">Batman</a> fan since childhood. <a href="/wiki/Catwoman" title="Catwoman">Catwoman</a> was portrayed by three different actresses during the series run: by <a href="/wiki/Julie_Newmar" title="Julie Newmar">Julie Newmar</a> in the first two seasons, by <a href="/wiki/Lee_Meriwether" title="Lee Meriwether">Lee Meriwether</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Batman_(1966_film)" title="Batman (1966 film)">feature film based on the series</a>, and by <a href="/wiki/Eartha_Kitt" title="Eartha Kitt">Eartha Kitt</a> in the third and final season.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_version_and_season_2">Film version and season 2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Film version and season 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Batman_(1966_film)" title="Batman (1966 film)">Batman (1966 film)</a></div>
<p>A film based on the television show, <i><a href="/wiki/Batman_(1966_film)" title="Batman (1966 film)">Batman</a></i>, was released in 1966. The film was originally intended to be produced before the series as a way to introduce the series to the public. However, the series' premiere was moved up and the film was forced to wait until the summer hiatus after the first season. The film was produced quickly to get into theatres prior to the start of season two of the television series.</p>
<p>The film did not initially perform well at the cinema. Originally, the movie had been conceived to help sell the television series abroad, but the success of the series in America was sufficient publicity. The film was shot after season one was filmed. The movie's budget allowed for producers to build the <a href="/wiki/Batboat" title="Batboat">Batboat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Batcopter" title="Batcopter">Batcopter</a>, which were used in the second and third seasons of the television show. Numerous films featuring Batman have since been produced, but with no relationship to the TV show.</p>
<p>Semple's participation in the series decreased in the second season. In his autobiography <i>Back to the Batcave,</i> Adam West explained that when work on the second season commenced following the completion of the feature film, Dozier, his immediate deputy Howie Horwitz, and the rest of the cast and crew rushed their preparation. Thus, they failed to give themselves enough time to determine what they wanted to do with the series during season two.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_3">Season 3</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Season 3">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>By season three, <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_ratings" title="Nielsen ratings">ratings</a> were falling and the future of the series seemed uncertain. To attract new viewers, Dozier opted to introduce a female character. He came up with the idea of using <a href="/wiki/List_of_Batman_television_series_characters#Batgirl" title="List of Batman television series characters">Batgirl</a>, who in her civilian identity would be Commissioner Gordon's daughter, Barbara, and asked the editor of the Batman comics to further develop the character (who had made her debut in a 1966 issue of <i>Detective Comics</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Back38_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Back38-6">[6]</a></sup> To convince ABC executives to introduce Batgirl as a regular on the show, a promotional short featuring <a href="/wiki/Yvonne_Craig" title="Yvonne Craig">Yvonne Craig</a> as Batgirl and Tim Herbert as <a href="/wiki/Killer_Moth" title="Killer Moth">Killer Moth</a> was produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The show was reduced to once a week, with mostly self-contained episodes, although the following week's villain would be introduced in a tag at the end of each episode, similar to a <a href="/wiki/Soap_opera" title="Soap opera">soap opera</a>. Accordingly, the narrator's cliffhanger phrases were mostly eliminated, most episodes ending with him encouraging viewers to watch next week .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[notes 1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Aunt Harriet was reduced to just two cameo appearances during the third season, due to <a href="/wiki/Madge_Blake" title="Madge Blake">Madge Blake</a>'s poor health and the issue of trying to fit so many characters (Batman, Robin, Batgirl, Alfred, Commissioner Gordon, Chief O'Hara and a guest villain) into a half hour episode. Another cast change for the final season was replacing <a href="/wiki/Julie_Newmar" title="Julie Newmar">Julie Newmar</a>, who had been a popular recurring guest villain as the Catwoman for the first two seasons. Singer-actress <a href="/wiki/Eartha_Kitt" title="Eartha Kitt">Eartha Kitt</a> assumed the role for season three, as Newmar was working on the film <i><a href="/wiki/Mackenna%27s_Gold" title="Mackenna's Gold">Mackenna's Gold</a></i> at that time, hence unable to appear. In America, Kitt's performance in the series marked the second mainstream television success of a black female, following Nichelle Nichols as Lt. Uhura in <i>Star Trek</i> and continued breaking the racial boundaries of the time. Kitt's performance as Catwoman would also, later, inspire <a href="/wiki/Halle_Berry" title="Halle Berry">Halle Berry</a>'s portrayal of the character in the 2004 film <i><a href="/wiki/Catwoman_(film)" title="Catwoman (film)">Catwoman</a></i>, in which Berry would mimic Kitt's famous purrs. Frank Gorshin, the original actor to play the Riddler, returned for a single appearance after a one-season hiatus, during which <a href="/wiki/John_Astin" title="John Astin">John Astin</a> made one appearance in the role.</p>
<p>The nature of the scripts and acting started to enter into the realm of <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">surrealism</a>. For example, the set's backgrounds became mere two-dimensional cut-outs against a stark black stage. In addition, the third season was much more topical, with references to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hippies" title="Hippies">hippies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mod_(subculture)" title="Mod (subculture)">mods</a>, and distinctive 1960s <a href="/wiki/Slang" title="Slang">slang</a>, which the previous two seasons had avoided.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cancellation">Cancellation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cancellation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Near the end of the third season, ratings had dropped significantly, and ABC cancelled the show. But <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> had agreed to take over the series and was ready to continue. Before that could happen, it was discovered that someone had destroyed the Batman sets which cost hundreds of thousands of dollars to build, and the deal with NBC was lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> Reruns of the series have been seen on a regular basis in the United States. They are currently shown on the classic TV network <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Me-TV" title="Me-TV">Me-TV</a> on Saturday nights,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> weekdays dubbed in Spanish on <a href="/wiki/TeleXitos" title="TeleXitos">TeleXitos</a>, as well as Saturday mornings on <a href="/wiki/IFC_(U.S._TV_network)" title="IFC (U.S. TV network)">IFC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tie-in_music">Tie-in music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Tie-in music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several cast members recorded music tied in to the series. Adam West released a single titled "Miranda", a country-tinged pop song that he actually performed in costume during live appearances in the 1960s. Frank Gorshin released a song titled "The Riddler", which was composed and arranged by <a href="/wiki/Mel_Torm%C3%A9" title="Mel Tormé">Mel Tormé</a>. Burgess Meredith recorded a spoken-word single called "The Escape" backed with "The Capture", which consisted of the Penguin narrating his recent crime spree to a jazz beat. Burt Ward recorded a song called "Boy Wonder, I Love You", written and arranged by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Zappa" title="Frank Zappa">Frank Zappa</a>.</p>
<p>In 1966, Batman: The Exclusive Original Soundtrack Album was released on LP, featuring music by Nelson Riddle and snippets of dialogue from Adam West, Burt Ward, Burgess Meredith, Frank Gorshin, Anne Baxter (as Zelda the Great) and George Sanders (the first Mr. Freeze). The Batman Theme was included, along with titles like Batusi A Go! Go!, Batman Thaws Mr. Freeze and Batman Blues. It was reissued later on compact disc.</p>

<p>The show featured numerous adaptations of various Batman comics stories - particularly in its first season. These first-season episodes were adaptations:</p>
<ul>
<li>The episodes "<a href="/wiki/Hi_Diddle_Riddle" title="Hi Diddle Riddle">Hi Diddle Riddle</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Smack_in_the_Middle" title="Smack in the Middle">Smack in the Middle</a>" were adaptations of "Remarkable Ruse of the Riddler" from Batman #171 (May 1965), written by <a href="/wiki/Gardner_Fox" title="Gardner Fox">Gardner Fox</a>; in it, the Riddler, jealous of the attention Batman is giving the Mole Hill Mob, arranges a trap so Batman will apprehend the gang and give the Riddler the Caped Crusader's undivided attention.</li>
<li>The episodes "<a href="/wiki/Fine_Feathered_Finks" title="Fine Feathered Finks">Fine Feathered Finks</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/The_Penguin%27s_a_Jinx" title="The Penguin's a Jinx">The Penguin's a Jinx</a>" are based on "Partners in Plunder!" from Batman #169 (February 1965), written by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Frances_Herron&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Frances Herron (page does not exist)">France Eddie Herron</a>; the only difference is the Penguin steals the jeweled meteorite (which was mentioned in the comic), instead of kidnapping Dawn Robbins (who did not appear in the comic).</li>
<li>Many events of the episodes "<a href="/wiki/The_Joker_Is_Wild_(Batman)" title="The Joker Is Wild (Batman)">The Joker Is Wild</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Batman_Is_Riled" title="Batman Is Riled">Batman Is Riled</a>" are based on the silver age comic book story "Joker's Utility Belt" from Batman #73 (October 1952) by <a href="/wiki/David_Vern_Reed" title="David Vern Reed">David Vern Reed</a>.</li>
<li>The episodes "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Instant_Freeze" title="Instant Freeze">Instant Freeze</a>" and "Rats Like Cheese" were inspired by "The Ice Crimes of Mr. Zero" from Batman #121 (February 1959) by Dave Wood and Sheldon Moldoff.</li>
<li>The episodes "<a href="/wiki/Zelda_The_Great" title="Zelda The Great">Zelda The Great</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/A_Death_Worse_Than_Fate" title="A Death Worse Than Fate">A Death Worse Than Fate</a>" are based on "Batman's Inescapable Doom-Trap!" from Detective Comics #346 (December 1965) by John Broome. Although the evil character Eivol Ekdol appeared in the story, Zelda did not; instead, the magician was a man named Carnado.</li>
<li>The episodes "The Thirteenth Hat" and "Batman Stands Pat" borrow several elements from several comic book stories such as "The Mad Hatter of Gotham City" by <a href="/wiki/Bill_Finger" title="Bill Finger">Bill Finger</a> and Sheldon Moldoff, from Detective Comics #230 (April 1956), and "The New Crimes of the Mad Hatter" by Dave Wood and Sheldon Moldoff from Batman #161 (February 1964). Also the Mad Hatter's plan of impersonating the sculptor working on a Batman statue for the city (for which Batman himself is, of course, posing) in an attempt to steal Batman's cowl comes from the Batman #49 story "The Scoop of the Century!" (October 1948) by <a href="/wiki/Bill_Finger" title="Bill Finger">Bill Finger</a>.</li>
<li>The episodes "<a href="/wiki/The_Penguin_Goes_Straight" title="The Penguin Goes Straight">The Penguin Goes Straight</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Not_Yet,_He_Ain%27t" title="Not Yet, He Ain't">Not Yet, He Ain't</a>" have a plot point of the Penguin framing Batman for a theft actually comes from Detective Comics #58 (December 1941) by Bill Finger, which is ironic as it was to be the issue in which the Penguin first appeared.</li>
<li>The episodes "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Joker_Trumps_an_Ace&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Joker Trumps an Ace (page does not exist)">The Joker Trumps an Ace</a>" and "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_Sets_the_Pace&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Batman Sets the Pace (page does not exist)">Batman Sets the Pace</a>" have several plot points similar to the comic story "A Hairpin, a Hoe, a Hacksaw, a Hole In the Ground!" by Bill Finger, from Batman #53 (June 1949).</li>
<li>The episodes "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Death_in_Slow_Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Death in Slow Motion (page does not exist)">Death in Slow Motion</a>" and "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Riddler%27s_False_Notion&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Riddler's False Notion (page does not exist)">The Riddler's False Notion</a>" used a few elements from "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Joker%27s_Comedy_Capers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Joker's Comedy Capers (page does not exist)">The Joker's Comedy Capers</a>" by Gardner Fox, from Detective #341 (July 1965), only replacing the main villain of the Joker with the Riddler.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crossover_with_The_Green_Hornet_TV_series">Crossover with <i>The Green Hornet</i> TV series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Crossover with The Green Hornet TV series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Green_Hornet_(TV_series)" title="The Green Hornet (TV series)">The Green Hornet (TV series)</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Green_Hornet_and_Kato_on_Batman">The Green Hornet and Kato on <i>Batman</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: The Green Hornet and Kato on Batman">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Van Williams and Bruce Lee made a cameo appearance as the Green Hornet and Kato in "window cameos" while the Batman and Robin scaled a building. This was in part one of a two-part second-season episode of the <i>Batman</i> TV series, "The Spell of Tut", which aired on September 28, 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later that same season, the Green Hornet and Kato appeared in the two-part second-season episodes <i>A Piece of the Action</i> and <i>Batman's Satisfaction</i>, which aired on March 1–2, 1967. In these two episodes, the Green Hornet and Kato are in <a href="/wiki/Gotham_City" title="Gotham City">Gotham City</a> to bust a counterfeit stamp ring run by Colonel Gumm (portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Roger_C._Carmel" title="Roger C. Carmel">Roger C. Carmel</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> The <i>Batman's Satisfaction</i> episode leads to a mixed fight, with both Batman &amp; Robin and The Green Hornet &amp; Kato fighting Colonel Gumm and his gang. Once Gumm's crew was defeated, Batman and Robin squared off against The Green Hornet and Kato, resulting in a stand-off that was interrupted by the police. In this episode, Batman, Robin and the police consider the Green Hornet and Kato to be criminals, although Batman and Robin were cordial to the duo in the earlier window appearance. There is also a mention of <i>The Green Hornet</i> TV series on the <i>Batman</i> TV series episode <i>The Impractical Joker</i> (episode 55, Part 1, aired November 16, 1966): while watching TV together, Alfred, Dick Grayson and Bruce Wayne (who says, "It's time to watch <i>The Green Hornet</i>", and you hear the Hornet buzzing sound) are interrupted by the Joker; then, after the interruption, <i>The Green Hornet</i> TV series theme music is heard.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Batman_and_Robin_on_The_Green_Hornet">Batman and Robin on <i>The Green Hornet</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Batman and Robin on The Green Hornet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the December 9, 1966 <i>Green Hornet</i> episode "The Secret Of The Sally Bell", the Batmobile can be seen revolving on its turntable floor in the batcave on a bad guy's TV set. In the February 3, 1967 <i>Green Hornet</i> episode "Ace in the Hole" (which aired between the September 1966 and March 1967 <i>Batman</i> appearances mentioned above), Batman and Robin can be seen climbing a building on a television set. There was one other Green Hornet &amp; Kato appearance that was NOT on the <i>Batman</i> TV series nor on <i>The Green Hornet</i> TV series: a segment of the <i>Milton Berle Show/The Hollywood Palace</i> aired in the Fall of 1966 brought together The Green Hornet and Kato (Van Williams and Bruce Lee), and Batman (Adam West), in a comedy sketch with Milton Berle, in which Bruce Lee demonstrates his martial arts expertise. Burt Ward as "Robin" was not included in this appearance.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Live_action" title="Live action">live action</a> television show was extraordinarily popular, called "the biggest TV phenomenon of the mid-1960s".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> At the height of its popularity, it was the only prime-time television show other than <i><a href="/wiki/Peyton_Place_(TV_series)" title="Peyton Place (TV series)">Peyton Place</a></i> to be broadcast twice in one week as part of its regular schedule, airing at 7:30 pm Wednesdays and Thursdays. Episodes of the show were filmed as two-part cliffhangers, with each storyline beginning on Wednesday and ending on the Thursday episode. (In the second season, a pair of three-parters was also seen; at the very end of the Thursday night segment, a brief tag featuring the next week's villain would be shown, such as, "Next week: Batman jousts with The Joker again!" This started on the third week of the series' run and continued until the end of season two. The first episode of a storyline would typically end with Batman and Robin being in a <a href="/wiki/Deathtrap_(plot_device)" title="Deathtrap (plot device)">deathtrap</a>, while the narrator (Dozier) would tell viewers to watch the next night with the repeated phrase: "Tune in tomorrow—same Bat-time, same Bat-channel!" Even many years after the show ceased production, this catch-phrase still remains a long-running punchline in popular culture.</p>
<p>The "Captain Crocodile" episode of the TV series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Monkees" title="The Monkees">The Monkees</a></i> featured a parody segment devoted to "Frogman" and "Reuben the Tadpole" (played, respectively, by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Tork" title="Peter Tork">Peter Tork</a> and <a href="/wiki/Davy_Jones_(musician)" title="Davy Jones (musician)">Davy Jones</a>) combating the criminal forces of <a href="/wiki/Michael_Nesmith" title="Michael Nesmith">Michael Nesmith</a> and <a href="/wiki/Micky_Dolenz" title="Micky Dolenz">Micky Dolenz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Television critics <a href="/wiki/Alan_Sepinwall" title="Alan Sepinwall">Alan Sepinwall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matt_Zoller_Seitz" title="Matt Zoller Seitz">Matt Zoller Seitz</a> ranked <i>Batman</i> as the 82nd greatest American television show of all time in their 2016 book titled <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=TV_(The_Book)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="TV (The Book) (page does not exist)">TV (The Book)</a></i>, stating that "West's performance, the brilliance of which has required decades to be properly recognized, played as if series creator William Dozier and chief developer Lorenzo Semple, Jr., had taken the establishment's fantasy of itself and dolled it up in tights and a cape .... The anarchic gangs of supervillains and henchmen that kept trying to capture or destroy Gotham City stood in for the forces of chaos that kept threatening to engulf so-called civilized America throughout the sixties, only made colorfully grotesque and knowingly silly."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> In 1997, <a href="/wiki/TV_Guide" title="TV Guide">TV Guide</a> ranked the episodes "The Purr-fect Crime" and "Better Luck Next Time" #86 on its list of the 100 Greatest Episodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> In 2009, "Better Luck Next Time" was ranked #72.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_service_announcements">Public service announcements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Public service announcements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Batman character appeared in two <a href="/wiki/Public_service_announcement" title="Public service announcement">public service announcements</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Savings_Bonds">U.S. Savings Bonds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: U.S. Savings Bonds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1966, West, as Batman, encouraged schoolchildren to heed then-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/President_Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="President Lyndon B. Johnson">President Lyndon B. Johnson</a>'s call for them to buy U.S. Savings stamps, a children's version of <a href="/wiki/U.S._Savings_bonds" title="U.S. Savings bonds">U.S. Savings bonds</a>, to support the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Department_of_Labor">U.S. Department of Labor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: U.S. Department of Labor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a 1974 PSA for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Labor" title="U.S. Department of Labor">U.S. Department of Labor</a> Wage &amp; Hour Division narrated by William Dozier, Batman and Robin were tied to a post amid the threat of a ticking <a href="/wiki/Time_bomb" title="Time bomb">time bomb</a>, but Batgirl (Yvonne Craig) refused to release them because she was paid less than Robin (Burt Ward), in violation of the Federal Equal Pay Law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Gautier" title="Richard Gautier">Dick Gautier</a> played Batman this time, because West was, at the time, trying to distance himself from the role.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_series_run">After the series run</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: After the series run">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Batman and Robin, along with other characters, started having animated television appearances on <a href="/wiki/Filmation" title="Filmation">Filmation</a>'s series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Batman/Superman_Hour" title="The Batman/Superman Hour">The Batman/Superman Hour</a></i> six months after the live-action <i>Batman</i> series ended its production. Four years later, the Dynamic Duo appeared in two episodes of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Scooby-Doo_Movies" title="The New Scooby-Doo Movies">The New Scooby-Doo Movies</a></i> which were "The Dynamic Scooby-Doo Affair" and "The Caped Crusader Caper".</p>

<p>The series' stars, Adam West and Burt Ward, were <a href="/wiki/Typecasting_(acting)" title="Typecasting (acting)">typecast</a> for decades afterwards, with West especially finding himself unable to escape the reputation of a hammy, camp actor. However, years after the series' impact faded, an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Batman:_The_Animated_Series" title="Batman: The Animated Series">Batman: The Animated Series</a></i> paid tribute to West with an episode titled "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beware_The_Gray_Ghost" title="Beware The Gray Ghost">Beware The Gray Ghost</a>". In this episode, West himself provided the voice of an aging star of a superhero television series Bruce Wayne had watched as a child and from which he later found inspiration. This gave West new popularity with the next generation of fans. He also played Gotham City's Mayor Grange as a somewhat recurring role in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Batman_(TV_series)" title="The Batman (TV series)">The Batman</a></i>. In addition, West played the voice of Thomas Wayne, Bruce Wayne's father in the episode <i>"Chill of the Night"</i> from the series <i>Batman: The Brave and the Bold</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="References_In_Animation">References In Animation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: References In Animation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The animated television series <i><a href="/wiki/Batman:_The_Brave_and_the_Bold" title="Batman: The Brave and the Bold">Batman: The Brave and the Bold</a></i> is influenced by the 1960s television series. The opening credits feature Batman rope-climbing up a building, something that Adam West and Burt Ward often did in the show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> Several villains from the 1960s show including King Tut, Egghead, Mad Hatter, Archer, Bookworm, False Face, Black Widow, Siren, Marsha Queen of Diamonds, Louie the Lilac, Ma Parker, and Shame make cameo appearances as prisoners at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_Heights" title="Iron Heights">Iron Heights</a> prison in the episode "Day of the Dark Knight!" They are all captured by Batman and <a href="/wiki/Green_Arrow" title="Green Arrow">Green Arrow</a> during a mass escape attempt. The episode "Game Over for Owlman!" shows a room in the Batcave containing "souvenirs" of deathtraps that the Joker employed in the 1960s series, with accompanying flashbacks: the giant key from the "Human <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Key_cutter" title="Key cutter">Key Duplicator</a>" from "The Impractical Joker", the <a href="/wiki/Slot_machine" title="Slot machine">slot machine</a>-controlled <a href="/wiki/Electric_chair" title="Electric chair">electric chair</a> from "The Joker Goes to School", and the giant clam from "<a href="/wiki/The_Joker%27s_Hard_Times" title="The Joker's Hard Times">The Joker's Hard Times</a>". The episode "The Color of Revenge!" begins with a flashback to the time of the 1960s television series, using attributes such as the red Batphone, the Shakespeare bust, the <a href="/wiki/Sliding_bookcase" title="Sliding bookcase">sliding bookcase</a>, the Batpoles, Robin in his old television-series costume, and the shot of Batman and Robin fastening their seat belts in the Batmobile. Additionally, the Adam West Batman briefly appears in "Night of the Batmen!" as part of an army of Batmen gathered across the <a href="/wiki/Multiverse_(DC_Comics)" title="Multiverse (DC Comics)">Multiverse</a>.</p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Young_Justice_(TV_series)" title="Young Justice (TV series)">Young Justice</a></i> episode "Schooled" briefly references the show, as well, by featuring a Shakespeare bust in Bruce's office at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waynetech" title="Waynetech">Waynetech</a> building in <a href="/wiki/Metropolis_(comics)" title="Metropolis (comics)">Metropolis</a>. As a further homage to the series, Bruce is shown accessing an emergency <a href="/wiki/Batsuit" title="Batsuit">Batsuit</a> hidden in his desk by flipping a switch concealed within the bust.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Batman_Forever"><i>Batman Forever</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Batman Forever">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A line spoken by Robin (<a href="/wiki/Chris_O%27Donnell" title="Chris O'Donnell">Chris O'Donnell</a>) in <i><a href="/wiki/Batman_Forever" title="Batman Forever">Batman Forever</a></i> is a homage to the television Robin's catch-phrase exclamations that started "Holy" and sometimes ended "Batman!" - for instance "Holy bargain basements, Batman!" (from the television series' first season) and "Holy flypaper, Batman!" (from the television series' second season). During the movie, Robin says "Holy rusted metal, Batman!" after the duo climbs onto twisted metal girders beside some water. This catchphrase also appeared for a time in <i>Batman</i> comic books.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birds_of_Prey"><i>Birds of Prey</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Birds of Prey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the third episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Birds_of_Prey_(TV_series)" title="Birds of Prey (TV series)">Birds of Prey</a></i> "Prey for the Hunter", the famous William Shakespeare bust from the series has a cameo. However, instead of bending the neck back to expose the button, it is simply turned to the left, giving it the same function - exposing a hidden passageway.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Batman_.2766_comic"><i>Batman '66</i> comic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Batman '66 comic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Batman_%2766" title="Batman '66">Batman '66</a></div>
<p>In 2013, DC began publication of <i>Batman '66,</i> a comic book series telling all-new stories set in the world of the 1966–68 TV series. <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Parker_(comics)" title="Jeff Parker (comics)">Jeff Parker</a> writes the series, which features cover art by <a href="/wiki/Mike_Allred" title="Mike Allred">Mike Allred</a> and interior art by different artists each issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> In the course of this series, the Bookworm, the Minstrel, Sandman, Olga Queen of the Cossacks, Zelda The Great, Shame, and Marsha Queen of Diamonds all have their first appearance in Batman comics. Penguin, Joker, Riddler, Catwoman and Mr. Freeze also appear in the series. Issue #3 of <i>Batman '66</i> introduced the <a href="/wiki/Red_Hood" title="Red Hood">Red Hood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harley_Quinn" title="Harley Quinn">Dr. Quinn</a> into the series continuity. In issue #7, Batman used a new vehicle, the <b>Bat-Jet,</b> to follow False-Face to Mount Rushmore. The series was to have introduced <a href="/wiki/Killer_Croc" title="Killer Croc">Killer Croc</a> into the continuity, as well as a new villainess named Cleopatra. In April 2014, the first five issues were compiled into the <i>Batman '66 Vol. 1</i> trade paperback. <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Smith" title="Kevin Smith">Kevin Smith</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Garman" title="Ralph Garman">Ralph Garman</a> likewise worked on a Batman and Green Hornet crossover, titled <i>Batman '66 meets The Green Hornet</i>. The six-issue mini-series began publication in June 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Parker_(comics)" title="Jeff Parker (comics)">Jeff Parker</a> wrote a Batman and <a href="/wiki/The_Man_from_U.N.C.L.E." title="The Man from U.N.C.L.E.">The Man from U.N.C.L.E.</a> team-up titled <i>Batman '66 meets The Man from U.N.C.L.E.</i> released in 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> Ian Edginton wrote a Batman team up with <a href="/wiki/John_Steed" title="John Steed">John Steed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emma_Peel" title="Emma Peel">Emma Peel</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Avengers_(TV_series)" title="The Avengers (TV series)">The Avengers</a></i> titled <i>Batman '66/Steed and Mrs. Peel</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup> Batman team up with <a href="/wiki/Wonder_Woman" title="Wonder Woman">Wonder Woman</a> in the crossover team up "Batman' 66 Meets Wonder Woman '77 writing by both Parker and Marc Andreyko.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> Whether Batman '66 <i>is represented in one of the current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_52" title="New 52">New 52</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DC_Multiverse" title="DC Multiverse">DC Multiverse</a> alternative Earths is uncertain. Thus far, this has not been the case, although several such alternative Earths inhabitants and representative metahumans remain undisclosed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup></i></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_comics">Other comics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Other comics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bluewater_Comics" title="Bluewater Comics">Bluewater Comics</a> has released a series of comics that take their cue from the TV show. They are <i>The Mis-Adventures of Adam West</i>, <i>The Secret Lives of Julie Newmar</i>, and <i>Burt Ward, Boy Wonder</i> and are similar in tone to the TV series. <i>The Mis-Adventures of Adam West</i> had a four-issue miniseries, and a regular series that ran nine issues. <i>The Secret Lives of Julie Newmar</i> was a four-issue miniseries and <i>Burt Ward, Boy Wonder</i> was going to be a four-issue miniseries, but has not yet been published.</p>

The stone bust concealing a button can be seen and interacted with in the Clock Tower and Wayne Office areas of the game. The Batman skin is also featured as DLC in with the 1960s series Robin and Catwoman skins, as well as the Batmobile.</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lego_Batman_3:_Beyond_Gotham"><i>Lego Batman 3: Beyond Gotham</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Lego Batman 3: Beyond Gotham">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <i>Lego Batman 3: Beyond Gotham</i>, an extra level is based on the 1966 <i>Batman</i> TV series, along with characters including: 1966 Batman, 1966 Robin; 1966 Batgirl, 1966 Joker, 1966 Catwoman, 1966 Riddler, and 1966 Penguin. Also, Adam West is a playable character. The 1966 Batmobile is also included as a drivable vehicle. The end credits sequence remakes the Batclimb window cameos.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Robot_Chicken"><i>Robot Chicken</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Robot Chicken">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Adam West and Burt Ward lend their voices and likeness to the third DC Comics Special on <i><a href="/wiki/Robot_Chicken" title="Robot Chicken">Robot Chicken</a></i>. In the plot, when Batman brings over a Superman from another dimension to make him jealous, Superman retaliates by bringing the Adam West Batman, who trumps the Robot Chicken Batman by addressing the glucose issue of muffins and dancing. He is later seen battling the <i>Arkham</i> versions of Batman's villains and is killed by Penguin's anthrax gas. Burt Ward kidnaps him as well as the Robot Chicken Robin and takes them to the Lazarus Pit, where he resurrects West's Batman and makes himself younger to relive the glory days. He only kidnapped Robot Chicken Robin so he could have his outfit.</p>

<p>In January 2014, <a href="/wiki/Conan_O%27Brien" title="Conan O'Brien">Conan O'Brien</a> posted on his Twitter account, and Warner Bros. later confirmed, that Warner Bros. would release an official DVD and Blu-ray boxed set of the complete series sometime in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TVShowsOnDVD011514_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-TVShowsOnDVD011514-42">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 10, 2014, the website tvshowsondvd.com quoted Burt Ward in saying that Warner Bros. would release the complete series on November 11, 2014, in time for the holiday season from <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox_Television" title="20th Century Fox Television">20th Century Fox Television</a>, and that Adam West and he were doing special features for the release.</p>
<p>Prior to the announcement, multiple conflicting reports were given for the reason the series had not been released officially. These included:</p>
<ul>
<li>Negotiations between DC Comics (and parent company <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a>), owners of the Batman character and <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a>, owners of the program itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[notes 2]</a></sup></li>
<li>Greenway/ABC/Fox rights issues: The <i>Batman</i> series was conceived as an equal partnership between William Dozier's Greenway Productions and Fox in 1964, before Fox entered into a separate agreement with <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a> to produce the series in 1965. With three companies involved almost from the outset, some speculation indicates 'these rights' are tangled even before the DC Comics character ownership rights are to be considered. Moreover:
<ul>
<li>In 2006, Deborah Dozier Potter, "the successor-in-interest to Greenway Productions" sued Fox for allegedly withholding monies under the Fox/ABC agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[43]</a></sup> Dozier Potter further claimed that this came to her attention when, in March 2005, "she considered releasing the series on DVD", implying that (from her perspective at least) Greenway/Dozier Potter has some say in the matter of potential DVD release of the series. The case was resolved/dismissed in November 2007. In February 2005, John Stacks had approached Deborah Dozier Potter to market the series on DVD. There were many offers and lots of interest in the release of the series, as can be read in Joel Eisner's <i>The Official Batbook Revised Bat Edition 2008.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[notes 3]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Other complications/rights issues:
<ul>
<li>Christopher D. Heer, writing at the "1966 Batman Message Board", clarified a quote by moderator Lee Kirkham, noting that there will likely be the need for complicated deals regarding cameos, since "...at least some of the cameos were done as uncredited, unpaid walk-ons – which means that Fox does NOT have home video clearances for them. Either those scenes would have to be cut or an agreement reached with the actors."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li>Kirkham's initial quote also noted that, alongside music clearance issues, there could also be problems over some of the costumes, and the original Batmobile:</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>"It may surprise you, but then there are also rights issues concerning the design of the unique Batmobile design used in the show, and possibly a separate issue regarding some of the costumes as well!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[45]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>The series, under the Fox/ABC deal, is still in syndication, and regularly shown on a number of channels around the world, currently appearing in the United States on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Me-TV" title="Me-TV">Me-TV</a> and <a href="/wiki/IFC_(U.S._TV_network)" title="IFC (U.S. TV network)">IFC</a> as of July 4, 2014. Until 2014, only the 1966 feature film was available on DVD from 20th Century Fox Home Entertainment for nonbroadcast viewing in North America. This affected the 2003 television movie reunion <i><a href="/wiki/Return_to_the_Batcave:_The_Misadventures_of_Adam_and_Burt" title="Return to the Batcave: The Misadventures of Adam and Burt">Return to the Batcave: The Misadventures of Adam and Burt</a>,</i> also released to DVD, which was able to make use of footage only from the 1966 movie.</p>
<p>With <i>Batman</i> being unavailable for home-video release until 2014, an unusual situation occurred in which material that would be considered DVD featurettes was released separately. In 2004, <a href="/wiki/Image_Entertainment" title="Image Entertainment">Image Entertainment</a> released <i>Holy Batmania,</i> a two-DVD set that included documentaries on the making of the series, as well as rare footage such as the original screen tests of the cast and Lyle Waggoner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[46]</a></sup> In 2008, Adam West released a privately issued DVD with the tongue-in-cheek title <i>Adam West Naked</i> for which he recorded anecdotes regarding all 120 episodes of the series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[47]</a></sup> In 2013 PBS aired an episode of <i>Pioneers of Television</i> called "Superheroes" that featured interviews with Adam West and Burt Ward, and talked about the 1960s TV series. It was released on DVD March 11, 2013.</p>
<p>Also in 2013, <a href="/wiki/PBS" title="PBS">PBS</a> produced and transmitted a documentary titled <i>Superheroes: A Never-Ending Battle</i>. This documentary talked a little bit about the series and included an interview with Adam West.</p>
<p>On November 11, 2014, <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a> released the full 120-episode Batman collection on Blu-ray and DVD with a variety of extras including a miniature Batmobile, a 32-page episode guide, and <i>Adam West Scrapbook.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[48]</a></sup> A second box released on Warner Bros.' own batmanondvd website replaces the Batmobile and the trading cards with a script from the episode "The Joker is Wild" and a bonus box containing the movie and the "Adam West Naked" documentary. This series is also available at the <a href="/wiki/ITunes_Store" title="ITunes Store">iTunes Store</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collectibles">Collectibles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Batman_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Collectibles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Starting in 1966, an enormous amount of Batman merchandise was manufactured and marketed to cash-in on the TV show's vast popularity. This includes trading cards, bubblegum cards, scale model kits of the Batmobile and Batboat, coloring books, and board games. Items from this particular era have gained substantial collector appeal with their remarkable variety, scarcity, and style.</p>
<p>One of the most desired collectibles involves the episodes introducing Catwoman ("The Purr-fect Crime" / "Better Luck Next Time"), which were the subject of a <a href="/wiki/View-Master" title="View-Master">View-Master</a> reel &amp; booklet set in 1966 (Sawyers Packet # B492). While the series was first-run on ABC, packet cover indicia reflected the "Bat Craze" cultural phenomenon by referring to the booklet as a <i>Batbooklet, Dynamically illustrated.</i> By the time the television series was cancelled in 1968 and GAF had taken over the View-Master product, <i>Batbooklet</i> was removed in favor of then-standard View-Master packaging for all future releases in the decades to follow, right up the period when the standard packet line was discontinued. The first season's superimposed fight onomatopoeias were not used for the View-Master's scenes of fights. Instead, black-lined "blast" balloons (transparent inside), and series-like onomatopoeias were illustrated and superimposed over fight images.</p>
<p>The popularity of the TV series has carried several decades after its debut; toy company <a href="/wiki/Mattel" title="Mattel">Mattel</a> has made the 1966 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Batmobile_(1966)" title="Batmobile (1966)">Batmobile</a> in various scales for the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Wheels" title="Hot Wheels">Hot Wheels</a> product line. The Batmobile with Batboat were also produced under the Matchbox and Corgi names in the UK, during this period.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a> acquired merchandising rights to the series in 2012,<a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.deadline.com/2012/06/warner-bros-launches-merchandise-campaign-for-1960s-batman-tv-series/" rel="nofollow">[2]</a> and in 2013 Mattel released an action figure line based on the television series. To date only a single series of figures have been produced: Batman, the Riddler, the Joker, the Penguin, Catwoman and exclusive to a boxset Robin. Two Batman variants were also produced a limited SDCC exclusive figure with an action feature that replicates the famous Batusi dance and a Surf's Up Batman figure complete with surfboard and trunks. Each figure has the likeness of their respective actor (with Catwoman resembling Newmar and the Riddler resembling Gorshin) and came packaged with a display base and collector card. A Batmobile was also sold to retail making this the first time the classic model has been produced for action figures in the 6-inch scale.</p>
<p>In 2013, Hong Kong–based entertainment collectible manufacturer Hot Toys produced <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>6</sub></span> scale versions of West's Batman and Ward's Robin.</p>
