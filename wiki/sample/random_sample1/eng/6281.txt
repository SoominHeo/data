<p><i><b>Battlestar Galactica</b></i> is an American <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> <a href="/wiki/Media_franchise" title="Media franchise">media franchise</a> created by <a href="/wiki/Glen_A._Larson" title="Glen A. Larson">Glen A. Larson</a>. The franchise began with the <a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica_(1978_TV_series)" title="Battlestar Galactica (1978 TV series)">original television series</a> in 1978 and was later followed by a short-run sequel series (<i><a href="/wiki/Galactica_1980" title="Galactica 1980">Galactica 1980</a></i>), a line of book adaptations, original novels, comic books, a board game, and video games. A re-imagined version of <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> aired as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica_(TV_miniseries)" title="Battlestar Galactica (TV miniseries)">two-part, three-hour miniseries</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/Ronald_D._Moore" title="Ronald D. Moore">Ronald D. Moore</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Eick" title="David Eick">David Eick</a> in 2003. That miniseries led to a <a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica_(2004_TV_series)" title="Battlestar Galactica (2004 TV series)">weekly television series</a>, which later aired up until 2009. A prequel series, <i><a href="/wiki/Caprica_(TV_series)" title="Caprica (TV series)">Caprica</a></i>, aired in 2010.</p>
<p>All <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> productions share the premise that in a distant part of the universe, a human civilization has extended to a group of planets known as the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Colonies" title="Twelve Colonies">Twelve Colonies</a>, to which they have migrated from their ancestral homeworld of <a href="/wiki/Kobol" title="Kobol">Kobol</a>. The Twelve Colonies have been engaged in a lengthy war with a cybernetic race known as the <a href="/wiki/Cylon_(1978)" title="Cylon (1978)">Cylons</a>, whose goal is the extermination of the human race. The Cylons offer peace to the humans, which proves to be a ruse. With the aid of a human named <a href="/wiki/Baltar_(Battlestar_Galactica)" title="Baltar (Battlestar Galactica)">Baltar</a>, the Cylons carry out a massive attack on the Twelve Colonies and on the Colonial Fleet of starships that protect them. These attacks devastate the Colonial Fleet, lay waste to the Colonies, and virtually destroy their populations. Scattered survivors flee into outer space aboard a ragtag array of available spaceships. Of the entire Colonial battle fleet, only the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica_(ship)" title="Battlestar Galactica (ship)">Battlestar <i>Galactica</i></a>, a gigantic battleship and spacecraft carrier, appears to have survived the Cylon attack. Under the leadership of <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Commander_Adama_(disambiguation)" title="Commander Adama (disambiguation)">Commander Adama</a>, the <i>Galactica</i> and the pilots of "<a href="/wiki/Colonial_Viper" title="Colonial Viper">Viper fighters</a>" lead a fugitive fleet of survivors in search of the fabled thirteenth colony known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_in_fiction#Battlestar_Galactica" title="Earth in fiction">Earth</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Original_series_.281978_and_1980.29">Original series (1978 and 1980)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battlestar_Galactica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Original series (1978 and 1980)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Battlestar_Galactica_.281978.29">Battlestar Galactica (1978)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battlestar_Galactica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Battlestar Galactica (1978)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica_(1978_TV_series)" title="Battlestar Galactica (1978 TV series)">Battlestar Galactica (1978 TV series)</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Glen_A._Larson" title="Glen A. Larson">Glen A. Larson</a>, the creator and <a href="/wiki/Executive_producer" title="Executive producer">executive producer</a> of <i>Battlestar Galactica</i>, claimed he had conceived of the <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> premise, which he originally called <i>Adam's Ark</i>, during the late 1960s. As James E. Ford detailed in “<i>Battlestar Galactica</i> and Mormon Theology,” a paper read at the Joint Conference of the American Culture and Popular Culture Associations on 17 April 1980 (and published as “Theology in Prime Time Science Fiction: <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> and Mormon Doctrine,” <i>Journal of Popular Culture</i> #17 [1983]: 83-87), the series originally incorporated many themes from <a href="/wiki/Mormons" title="Mormons">Mormon</a> theology, such as marriage for "time and eternity", a "council of twelve," and a planet called <a href="/wiki/Kobol" title="Kobol">Kobol</a>, as Larson was a member of <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, he was unable to find financial backing for his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TV_series" title="TV series">TV series</a> for a number of years. <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> was finally produced in the wake of the success of the 1977 film <a href="/wiki/Star_Wars_(film)" title="Star Wars (film)"><i>Star Wars</i></a>.</p>
<p>Initially, Larson envisioned <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> as a series of <a href="/wiki/Television_film" title="Television film">made-for-TV movies</a> (a three-hour <a href="/wiki/Television_pilot" title="Television pilot">pilot program</a> plus two two-hour episodes) for the <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">American Broadcasting Company</a> (ABC). A shortened version of the three-hour pilot, <i><a href="/wiki/Saga_of_a_Star_World" title="Saga of a Star World">Saga of a Star World</a></i>, was screened in Canadian theaters (before the TV series was <a href="/wiki/Broadcasting" title="Broadcasting">telecast</a>) and in American, European and Australian theaters later on. Instead of two additional TV movies, ABC decided to commission a weekly TV series of one-hour episodes.</p>
<p>In 1979 at the sixth annual <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Choice_Awards" title="People's Choice Awards">People's Choice Awards</a>, the TV series won in the category of "Best New TV Drama Series".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The initial episode of the TV series (the long pilot TV movie) was broadcast on September 17, 1978. However, about 30 minutes before the end, that broadcast was interrupted by the announcement of the signing of the Egyptian–Israeli <a href="/wiki/Camp_David_Accords" title="Camp David Accords">Camp David Accords</a>. After the interruption (which was nearly an hour in length), the episode picked back up where it left off.</p>
<p>During the eight months after the pilot's initial broadcast, 17 original episodes of the series were made (five of them two-part shows), equivalent to a standard 24-episode TV season. Citing declining ratings and cost overruns, ABC canceled <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> in April 1979. Its final episode "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Hand_of_God_(1978_Battlestar_Galactica)" title="The Hand of God (1978 Battlestar Galactica)">The Hand of God</a>" was telecast on April 29, 1979.</p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Battlestar_Galactica_(1978_TV_series)_and_Galactica_1980_episodes" title="List of Battlestar Galactica (1978 TV series) and Galactica 1980 episodes">List of <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> (1978 TV series) and <i>Galactica 1980</i> episodes</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Galactica_1980">Galactica 1980</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battlestar_Galactica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Galactica 1980">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Galactica_1980" title="Galactica 1980">Galactica 1980</a></div>
<p>During the autumn of 1979, ABC executives met with <i>Battlestar Galactica</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s creator <a href="/wiki/Glen_A._Larson" title="Glen A. Larson">Glen A. Larson</a> to consider restarting the series. A suitable concept was needed to draw viewers, and it was decided that the arrival of the Colonial Fleet at present-day Earth would be the storyline. A new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TV_movie" title="TV movie">TV movie</a> called <i><a href="/wiki/Galactica_1980" title="Galactica 1980">Galactica 1980</a></i> was produced. Again, it was decided this new version of <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> would be made into a weekly TV series. Despite the early success of the premiere, this program failed to achieve the popularity of the original series, and it was canceled after just ten episodes.</p>
<p>In this 1980 sequel series, the Colonial fleet finds the Earth, and then it covertly protects it from the Cylons. This series was a quick failure due to its low budget (e.g., recycling footage from the 1974 <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">Universal Studios</a> movie <a href="/wiki/Earthquake_(film)" title="Earthquake (film)"><i>Earthquake</i></a> during a <a href="/wiki/Cylon_(Battlestar_Galactica)" title="Cylon (Battlestar Galactica)">Cylon</a> attack sequence), widely panned writing, and ill-chosen time slot (Sunday evenings, a time generally reserved for family-oriented programming and, more specifically, also for the <i><a href="/wiki/60_Minutes" title="60 Minutes">60 Minutes</a></i> newsmagazine program). The TV series also had to adhere to strict content restrictions such as limiting the number of acts of violence and being required to shoehorn educational content into the script and dialogue.</p>
<p>To cut costs, the show was set mostly on the contemporary Earth, to the great dismay of fans. Another factor for fan apathy was the nearly complete recasting of the original series: <a href="/wiki/Lorne_Greene" title="Lorne Greene">Lorne Greene</a> reprised his role as Adama (working unpaid),<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herb_Jefferson,_Jr." title="Herb Jefferson, Jr.">Herb Jefferson, Jr.</a> played "Colonel" Boomer in about half of the episodes (with little screentime), and <a href="/wiki/Dirk_Benedict" title="Dirk Benedict">Dirk Benedict</a> as Starbuck for one episode (the abrupt final episode, though his character was to have also appeared in the unfilmed episode 'Wheel of Fire', which was a semi-sequel to 'The Return of Starbuck'). <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hatch_(actor)" title="Richard Hatch (actor)">Richard Hatch</a> (Apollo in the original series) was sent a script for <i>Galactica 1980</i>, but he turned it down since he was not sure what his part in the series would be now that all the characters had changed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_syndication" title="Television syndication">TV syndication</a> packages for <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> incorporate the episodes of this series.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cinema_releases">Cinema releases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battlestar_Galactica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cinema releases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Besides a re-edited version of the <a href="/wiki/Saga_of_a_Star_World" title="Saga of a Star World">pilot</a>, released originally in Canada, Europe, parts of Latin America, and, following the broadcast of the series, in the U.S., two other <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> feature films were released in cinemas. Both <i>Mission Galactica: The Cylon Attack</i> and <i>Conquest of the Earth</i> were made up of various episodes of the original series and <i>Galactica 1980</i> respectively. (See: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Battlestar_Galactica_(1978,_1980)_episodes#Feature_length_films" title="List of Battlestar Galactica (1978, 1980) episodes">List of <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> feature films</a>)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attempted_revivals">Attempted revivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battlestar_Galactica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Attempted revivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The original series maintained a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cult_television" title="Cult television">cult</a> fandom, which has supported efforts by Glen A. Larson, Richard Hatch, and <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Singer" title="Bryan Singer">Bryan Singer</a> (independently of one another) to revive the premise.</p>
<p>Richard Hatch produced a demonstration video in 1998–99 which featured several actors from the original series combined with state-of-the-art special effects. This video, titled <i><a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica:_The_Second_Coming" title="Battlestar Galactica: The Second Coming">Battlestar Galactica: The Second Coming</a></i>, was screened at some <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction_convention" title="Science fiction convention">science fiction conventions</a>, but it did not lead to a new series.</p>
<p>In 1999, the <a href="/wiki/Film_producer" title="Film producer">producer</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wing_Commander_(film)" title="Wing Commander (film)"><i>Wing Commander</i></a>, Todd Moyer, and the producer of the original TV series, Glen Larson, announced plans to produce a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motion_picture" title="Motion picture">motion picture</a> based on the TV series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It would have featured <a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Pegasus" title="Battlestar Pegasus">Battlestar <i>Pegasus</i></a>.</p>
<p>In 2000, the director and an executive producer of the <a href="/wiki/X-Men_(film)" title="X-Men (film)"><i>X-Men</i></a> movie, Bryan Singer and <a href="/wiki/Tom_DeSanto" title="Tom DeSanto">Tom DeSanto</a>, began developing a <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TV_miniseries" title="TV miniseries">TV miniseries</a> under the auspices of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studios_USA" title="Studios USA">Studios USA</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Fox_Broadcasting_Company" title="Fox Broadcasting Company">Fox TV network</a>. A continuation of the original series but set 25 years later, Singer and DeSanto's version included several members of the original cast reprising their original roles as well as newer characters being introduced. It was intended to be telecast as a <a href="/wiki/Television_pilot#Backdoor_pilots" title="Television pilot">backdoor pilot</a> in May 2002, and pre-production commenced and sets had even been partially constructed with a view to filming starting in November 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> However, production delays caused by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/September_11,_2001_terrorist_attacks" title="September 11, 2001 terrorist attacks">September 11, 2001 terrorist attacks</a> meant that Bryan Singer had to drop out, due to his commitment to direct the <a href="/wiki/X2_(film)" title="X2 (film)"><i>X-Men 2</i></a> movie. This caused the executives of Fox TV to lose interest in this project.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2003_reimagining">2003 reimagining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battlestar_Galactica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2003 reimagining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="TV_miniseries">TV miniseries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battlestar_Galactica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: TV miniseries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica_(TV_miniseries)" title="Battlestar Galactica (TV miniseries)">Battlestar Galactica (TV miniseries)</a></div>
<p>Despite attempts to revive the series over the years, none came to fruition until it was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reboot_(continuity)" title="Reboot (continuity)">reimagined</a> in 2003 by <a href="/wiki/Universal_Television" title="Universal Television">Universal Television</a> as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica_(TV_miniseries)" title="Battlestar Galactica (TV miniseries)">Battlestar Galactica</a></i>, a three-hour <a href="/wiki/Miniseries" title="Miniseries">miniseries</a>. Commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/Syfy" title="Syfy">Sci-Fi Channel</a>, screenwriter <a href="/wiki/Ronald_D._Moore" title="Ronald D. Moore">Ronald D. Moore</a> and producer <a href="/wiki/David_Eick" title="David Eick">David Eick</a> were the creative forces behind it. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a>-nominated actor <a href="/wiki/Edward_James_Olmos" title="Edward James Olmos">Edward James Olmos</a> was cast in the role of <a href="/wiki/William_Adama" title="William Adama">Commander Adama</a>, while two-time Academy Award nominee <a href="/wiki/Mary_McDonnell" title="Mary McDonnell">Mary McDonnell</a> was cast as <a href="/wiki/Laura_Roslin" title="Laura Roslin">President Laura Roslin</a>. Starbuck and Boomer were now female characters, portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Katee_Sackhoff" title="Katee Sackhoff">Katee Sackhoff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grace_Park_(actress)" title="Grace Park (actress)">Grace Park</a> respectively. Other cast members included <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Bamber" title="Jamie Bamber">Jamie Bamber</a> (Captain Lee 'Apollo' Adama), <a href="/wiki/James_Callis" title="James Callis">James Callis</a> (Dr. Gaius Baltar), and <a href="/wiki/Tricia_Helfer" title="Tricia Helfer">Tricia Helfer</a> as a Cylon known as "Number Six".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The mini-series was a ratings success for the Sci-Fi Channel and they commissioned a <a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica_(2004_TV_series)" title="Battlestar Galactica (2004 TV series)">new weekly <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> series</a> to follow.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television_series">Television series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battlestar_Galactica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Television series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica_(2004_TV_series)" title="Battlestar Galactica (2004 TV series)">Battlestar Galactica (2004 TV series)</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Battlestar_Galactica_(2004_TV_series)_episodes" title="List of Battlestar Galactica (2004 TV series) episodes">List of Battlestar Galactica (2004 TV series) episodes</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The new TV series was co-funded by the UK's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BSKYB" title="BSKYB">Sky Television</a>, and premiered first in the United Kingdom on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sky1" title="Sky1">Sky1</a> satellite channel in October 2004. The series was then broadcast in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Syfy" title="Syfy">Sci-Fi Channel</a> in January 2005. Continuing where the 2003 mini-series left off, the main cast all returned to reprise their roles. Several new characters were introduced, and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hatch_(actor)" title="Richard Hatch (actor)">Richard Hatch</a>, who played Captain Apollo in the 1970s <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> TV series, also appeared in several episodes as <a href="/wiki/Tom_Zarek" title="Tom Zarek">Tom Zarek</a>, a former political terrorist who later becomes part of the new Colonial government.</p>
<p>An edited version of the pilot <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica_(TV_miniseries)" title="Battlestar Galactica (TV miniseries)">miniseries</a> was aired on <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> on January 9, 2005, five days before the Sci-Fi series premiere. NBC also aired three selected first season episodes to promote the show in advance of the second season premiere in July 2005. The series ultimately ran for four seasons between 2004 and 2009. The second season was split into two halves screened several months apart. Due to production delays caused by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2007%E2%80%932008_Writers_Guild_of_America_strike" title="2007–2008 Writers Guild of America strike">2007–2008 Writers Guild strike</a>, the fourth season was also split into two parts, with a seven-month hiatus in between.</p>
<p>The series has won widespread critical acclaim among many mainstream non-SF-genre publications. <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Newsday" title="New York Newsday">New York Newsday</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> named it the best show on television in 2005. Other publications such as <i>The New York Times</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/National_Review" title="National Review">National Review</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone_(magazine)" title="Rolling Stone (magazine)"><i>Rolling Stone</i> magazine</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> also gave the show positive reviews.</p>
<p>The show has received a <a href="/wiki/Peabody_Award" title="Peabody Award">Peabody Award</a> for overall excellence, several <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Awards</a> for Visual Effects, and Emmy nominations for Writing and Directing. <i>Time Magazine</i> named it one of the 100 Best TV Shows of All Time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Razor_.28TV_movie.29"><i>Razor</i> (TV movie)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battlestar_Galactica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Razor (TV movie)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica:_Razor" title="Battlestar Galactica: Razor">Battlestar Galactica: Razor</a></div>
<p><i>Battlestar Galactica: Razor</i> is a 2007 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_movie" title="Television movie">television movie</a> produced and broadcast in the gap between Seasons 3 and 4 of the re-imagined series. <i>Razor</i> is also technically the first two episodes of Season 4 though it chronicles events on <a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Pegasus" title="Battlestar Pegasus">Battlestar <i>Pegasus</i></a> in two time periods, both of which are "in the past" with respect to the Season 4 continuity. The "present day" framing scenes are set during Lee Adama's command of the <i>Pegasus</i> in the latter half of Season 2, while "flashback" scenes depict <a href="/wiki/Helena_Cain" title="Helena Cain">Helena Cain</a>'s command in the period between the Cylon attack (shown in the 2003 mini-series) and the reunion with the <i>Galactica</i> in the second season. It aired in the United States and Canada on November 24, 2007 and in the UK and Ireland on December 18, 2007. An expanded version of the movie was released on DVD on December 4, 2007.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Webisodes">Webisodes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battlestar_Galactica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Webisodes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica:_The_Resistance" title="Battlestar Galactica: The Resistance">Battlestar Galactica: The Resistance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica:_Razor_Flashbacks" title="Battlestar Galactica: Razor Flashbacks">Battlestar Galactica: Razor Flashbacks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica:_The_Face_of_the_Enemy" title="Battlestar Galactica: The Face of the Enemy">Battlestar Galactica: The Face of the Enemy</a></div>
<p>The first set of <a href="/wiki/Webisode" title="Webisode">webisodes</a> were a series of shorts produced in 2006 to promote the third season of the re-imagined show. Made as an "optional extra" to Season 3, the webisodes filled in some of the events between the second and third seasons and featured some of the main cast, though did not reveal what would happen in the beginning of Season 3, nor was viewing them essential to follow the story of the third season. Each of the ten webisodes was approximately three minutes long, and they were released twice a week leading up to the U.S. Season 3 premiere in 2006.</p>
<p>The <i>Razor Flashbacks</i> were a series of seven webisodes produced in 2007. Set some 40 years earlier during William Adama's fighter pilot days during the later stages of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Cylon_War" title="First Cylon War">First Cylon War</a>. They were released on the Internet as "webisodes" leading up to <i>Razor'</i>s release. They are now available on the DVD and Blu-Ray releases of <i>Battlestar Galactica: Razor</i>, and some are inserted into both the broadcast and extended cuts of the movie on DVD and Blu-Ray. The installments that did not make the final cut include 1, 2, and the latter half of 7.</p>
<p>In May 2008, a set of 10 webisodes were announced to be in the works which were released during the seven-month hiatus between episodes 10 and 11 of Season 4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Titled <i>The Face of the Enemy</i>, the web series premiered on December 12, 2008 on SciFi.com. Again, viewing of the webisodes was optional prior to the second half of Season 4.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Plan_.28TV_movie.29"><i>The Plan</i> (TV movie)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battlestar_Galactica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The Plan (TV movie)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica:_The_Plan" title="Battlestar Galactica: The Plan">Battlestar Galactica: The Plan</a></div>
<p>In August 2008, the Sci Fi Channel officially announced the production of a two-hour TV movie which was planned to air after the final episode of the series in 2009. The movie began production on September 8, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morecast_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-morecast-18">[18]</a></sup> The movie premiered exclusively on DVD, Blu-ray and digital download on October 27, 2009 and aired on January 10, 2010, on Sci Fi. Written by <a href="/wiki/Jane_Espenson" title="Jane Espenson">Jane Espenson</a> and directed by <a href="/wiki/Edward_James_Olmos" title="Edward James Olmos">Edward James Olmos</a>, <i>The Plan</i> storyline begins before the attack on the 12 colonies and shows events primarily from the perspective of the Cylons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Edward_James_Olmos" title="Edward James Olmos">Edward James Olmos</a> reprised his role as Adama, and ten of the eleven actors who played Cylons appeared, including <a href="/wiki/Michael_Trucco" title="Michael Trucco">Michael Trucco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Douglas_(actor)" title="Aaron Douglas (actor)">Aaron Douglas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dean_Stockwell" title="Dean Stockwell">Dean Stockwell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tricia_Helfer" title="Tricia Helfer">Tricia Helfer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grace_Park_(actress)" title="Grace Park (actress)">Grace Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rick_Worthy" title="Rick Worthy">Rick Worthy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Bennett" title="Matthew Bennett">Matthew Bennett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Callum_Keith_Rennie" title="Callum Keith Rennie">Callum Keith Rennie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Hogan_(Canadian_actor)" title="Michael Hogan (Canadian actor)">Michael Hogan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rekha_Sharma" title="Rekha Sharma">Rekha Sharma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morecast_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-morecast-18">[18]</a></sup> The only "Cylon" actor not present was <a href="/wiki/Lucy_Lawless" title="Lucy Lawless">Lucy Lawless</a> (although previously filmed footage of her was included).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tvmovie_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-tvmovie-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caprica"><i>Caprica</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battlestar_Galactica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Caprica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Caprica_(TV_series)" title="Caprica (TV series)">Caprica (TV series)</a></div>
<p><i>Caprica</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Prequel" title="Prequel">prequel</a> television series to the re-imagined <i>Battlestar Galactica</i>. It premiered on Syfy (formerly Sci-Fi) on January 22, 2010, and was described as "television's first science fiction <a href="/wiki/Family_saga" title="Family saga">family saga</a>". It was originally a two-hour <a href="/wiki/Television_pilot#Backdoor_pilots" title="Television pilot">back door pilot</a> for a possible weekly television series, but on December 2, 2008, Syfy gave the go-ahead to expand the project into a full, 20-episode series. <i>Caprica</i> is set on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caprica_(planet)" title="Caprica (planet)">titular planet</a>, 58 years before the events of <i>Battlestar Galactica</i>. The show revolves around two families, the Adamas and the Graystones, and the creation of the Cylons.</p>
<p>The pilot was directed by <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Reiner" title="Jeffrey Reiner">Jeffrey Reiner</a> and starred <a href="/wiki/Eric_Stoltz" title="Eric Stoltz">Eric Stoltz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Esai_Morales" title="Esai Morales">Esai Morales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paula_Malcomson" title="Paula Malcomson">Paula Malcomson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alessandra_Torresani" title="Alessandra Torresani">Alessandra Torresani</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Polly_Walker" title="Polly Walker">Polly Walker</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The pilot was released on DVD on April 21, 2009<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and the series was broadcast in January 2010.</p>
<p>On October 27, 2010, Syfy canceled <i>Caprica</i> due to low ratings. The final five episodes were aired in the US on January 4, 2011<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> though they had aired a couple of months earlier on the Canadian network <a href="/wiki/Space_(TV_channel)" title="Space (TV channel)">Space</a>. The entire series was released on DVD in 2011.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blood_.26_Chrome"><i>Blood &amp; Chrome</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battlestar_Galactica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Blood &amp; Chrome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica:_Blood_%26_Chrome" title="Battlestar Galactica: Blood &amp; Chrome">Battlestar Galactica: Blood &amp; Chrome</a></div>
<p><i>Battlestar Galactica: Blood &amp; Chrome</i> was to be an upcoming spin-off series from the re-imagined <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenlit_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-greenlit-24">[24]</a></sup> Syfy approached show runner Ronald D. Moore to produce another spin-off set in the reimagined <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> universe, which was to begin as a two-hour pilot focused on William "Husker" Adama (portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Luke_Pasqualino" title="Luke Pasqualino">Luke Pasqualino</a>) during the First Cylon War (as was glimpsed in <i>Razor</i> and the corresponding webisodes).</p>
<p>Syfy decided against moving forward with the <i>Blood and Chrome</i> TV series, but on November 5, 2012 it was announced that a 10-part webseries would begin on November 9, 2012 and be released over four weeks via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Machinima.com" title="Machinima.com">Machinima.com</a>. The webseries was also aired as a 2-hour movie on Syfy on February 10, 2013,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and was released on DVD shortly afterwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feature_film">Feature film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battlestar_Galactica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Feature film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Creator Glen A. Larson entered negotiations with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Pictures" title="Universal Pictures">Universal Pictures</a> for a film adaptation of the 1978 series in February 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Singer" title="Bryan Singer">Bryan Singer</a> signed on to direct the <a href="/wiki/Reboot_(fiction)" title="Reboot (fiction)">reboot</a> the following August, but was obligated to direct <i><a href="/wiki/Jack_the_Giant_Slayer" title="Jack the Giant Slayer">Jack the Giant Slayer</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In October 2011 <a href="/wiki/John_Orloff" title="John Orloff">John Orloff</a> was hired to write the script. "I have wanted to write this movie since I was 12 years old, and built a Galactica model from scratch out of balsa wood, cardboard, old model parts and LEDs," Orloff told <a href="/wiki/Deadline.com" title="Deadline.com">Deadline.com</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> By August 2012 the script was being rewritten, with Singer explaining that "It will exist, I think, quite well between the Glen Larson and Ron Moore universes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> On April 7, 2014, the studio hired Jack Paglen to write the script for the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> On February 12, 2016, Universal signed <a href="/wiki/Michael_De_Luca" title="Michael De Luca">Michael De Luca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scott_Stuber" title="Scott Stuber">Scott Stuber</a> and Dylan Clark to produce the <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> On June 9, 2016, <a href="/wiki/Lisa_Joy" title="Lisa Joy">Lisa Joy</a> was reportedly writing the film, and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Lawrence" title="Francis Lawrence">Francis Lawrence</a> was in talks to direct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comic_books">Comic books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battlestar_Galactica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Comic books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica_(comics)" title="Battlestar Galactica (comics)">Battlestar Galactica (comics)</a></div>
<p>A series of comic book publishers have adapted <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> since its inception.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a> published a 23-issue comic book series based upon the show between 1978 and 1981. Walt Simonson, who later wrote and drew <i>Thor</i> and had a long stint on Marvel's <i>Star Wars</i> comic, was the artist for the series at its conclusion. Other comics have since been published by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximum_Press" title="Maximum Press">Maximum Press</a>, Grandreams, <i><a href="/wiki/Look-in" title="Look-in">Look-in</a></i> magazine, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Realm_Press&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Realm Press (page does not exist)">Realm Press</a> and, currently, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dynamite_Comics" title="Dynamite Comics">Dynamite Comics</a>. Of all these series, only those by Marvel, Grandreams, and <i>Look-In</i> actually completed their storylines and brought the story to a conclusion. All the other series were cancelled at various points during their run, with no resolutions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Both the Grandreams and <i>Look-In</i> comic strips take place early in the series. The other comic series based on the 1978 series have been set after the final episode of the series and ignored <i><a href="/wiki/Galactica_1980" title="Galactica 1980">Galactica 1980</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Maximum Press series began with the discovery of a completely unpopulated Earth some fifteen years after the TV show. The look and the feel of the comics was changed considerably from the series, to give the stories a "more nineties" feel.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Realm Press series picked up immediately after the original series' final episode, in an attempt to present what they called "Season Two" of the original show.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dynamite_Entertainment" title="Dynamite Entertainment">Dynamite Entertainment</a> was the last company to publish comic books featuring both the classic and reimagined <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> series. They also released a 4-issue <i>Galactica 1980</i> comic miniseries written by <a href="/wiki/Marc_Guggenheim" title="Marc Guggenheim">Marc Guggenheim</a>. The limited miniseries was a re-imagining of the original series but at the end featured a second, smaller <i>Battlestar</i> (replacing the original which was destroyed) also named <i>Galactica</i> but strongly resembling the ship seen in the reimagined Sci-Fi Channel series.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Books">Books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battlestar_Galactica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Both the original and the reimagined series have had books published about the series, academically oriented analysis, novelizations, and new works based on the characters.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Original_series_books">Original series books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battlestar_Galactica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Original series books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> softcover novelisations were written by <a href="/wiki/Glen_A._Larson" title="Glen A. Larson">Glen A. Larson</a> with the authors listed below.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The first ten novels adapt the episode of the same title except as indicated. All novels except <i>Battlestar Galactica 14: Surrender the Galactica!</i> (ACE publishing) were originally published by Berkley, and have been republished, recently, by I Books, which called them <i>Battlestar Galactica Classic</i> to differentiate it from the reimagined series. The episodic novels featured expanded scenes, excerpts from "The Adama Journals," more background on the characters, and the expansion of the ragtag fleet to almost 22,000 ships as opposed to the 220 in the TV series. A new book series written by series star <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hatch_(actor)" title="Richard Hatch (actor)">Richard Hatch</a> starting in the 1990s continued the original story based on his attempt to revive the series, and ignored the events of <i><a href="/wiki/Galactica_1980" title="Galactica 1980">Galactica 1980</a>.</i> His series picked up several years after the TV series ended, and featured Apollo in command of the <i>Galactica</i> after the death of Adama, a grown-up Boxey, who was now a Viper pilot, and the rediscovery of Commander Cain and the battlestar <i>Pegasus,</i> who had started a new colony and was preparing to restart the war with the Cylons.</p>

<p>The original series inspired a <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> board game. The game is set during a training mission, where two to four players maneuver pieces representing Colonial Vipers to capture a damaged <a href="/wiki/Cylon_Raider" title="Cylon Raider">Cylon Raider</a>. Play includes using terrain elements and a number of special-ability cards to the players' advantage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1978, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mattel_Electronics" title="Mattel Electronics">Mattel Electronics</a> released a handheld electronic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LED" title="LED">LED</a> game based on the series. The player tries to defend <i>Galactica</i> from <a href="/wiki/Kamikaze" title="Kamikaze">kamikaze</a> Cylon Raiders by manipulating a switch on the game unit to direct their fire, triggered by a red button to the left of the unit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mattel-game-manual_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-mattel-game-manual-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1979, FASA released a tabletop counter piece game for <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> based on the fighter combat, which included the <i>Galactica</i> and a Cylon Basestar to be launched from, attack with and be attacked/defended. The counters for the Vipers and the Raiders included three model versions MKI/MKII/MKIII, not just the MKII Viper and Raider MKI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2003, shortly before the premiere of the re-imagined TV series, Sierra released a 3D space combat <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> computer game for the original <a href="/wiki/Xbox_(console)" title="Xbox (console)">Xbox</a>, <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">PlayStation 2</a>, and PC. The game took place 40 years before the original series and featured an ensign Adama flying a Viper during the Cylon war. The game was developed by Warthog.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/WizKids" title="WizKids">Wiz Kids, Inc.</a> (a collectible game manufacturer) produced the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica_Collectable_Card_Game" title="Battlestar Galactica Collectable Card Game">Battlestar Galactica Collectable Card Game</a></i> based on the 2003 mini-series and 2004 TV show. The premier set of this game was released in May 2006. After the release of one expansion set, Wizkids announced the game's cancellation on March 13, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <i><a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica_Role_Playing_Game" title="Battlestar Galactica Role Playing Game">Battlestar Galactica</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_game" title="Role-playing game">role-playing game</a> was released in August 2007 by <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Weis_Productions" title="Margaret Weis Productions">Margaret Weis Productions</a> at <a href="/wiki/Gen_Con" title="Gen Con">Gen Con</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Fantasy_Flight_Games" title="Fantasy Flight Games">Fantasy Flight Games</a> produced <i><a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica:_The_Board_Game" title="Battlestar Galactica: The Board Game">Battlestar Galactica: The Board Game</a></i>, based on the 2003 re-imagined series. It is a semi-cooperative game of strategy for 3-6 players with some players being Cylon agents, either aware at start of the game or become aware later, as <a href="/wiki/Sleeper_agent" title="Sleeper agent">Sleeper agents</a>. Each of the 10 playable character has its own abilities and weaknesses, and they must all work together in order for humanity to survive, as well as attempt to expose the traitor while fuel shortages, food contaminations, and political unrest threaten to tear the fleet apart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> In June 2009, a "<i>Pegasus</i> expansion" to the board game was announced, adding seven new characters and extra gameboards representing <i>Battlestar Pegasus</i> and <i>New Caprica</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> A further expansion, <i>Exodus</i> was released in December 2010 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and <i>Daybreak</i>, the final expansion, was released in Summer of 2013.</p>
<p>There is also a 2D <a href="/wiki/Xbox_Live_Arcade" title="Xbox Live Arcade">Xbox 360 Live Arcade</a> title called <i>Battlestar Galactica</i> wherein players can co-op or dogfight with up to 8 people over <a href="/wiki/Xbox_Live" title="Xbox Live">Xbox Live</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica_Online" title="Battlestar Galactica Online">Battlestar Galactica Online</a></i> is a 3D <a href="/wiki/Browser_game" title="Browser game">browser-based</a> <a href="/wiki/Massively_multiplayer_online_game" title="Massively multiplayer online game">MMOG</a> released as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_beta" title="Open beta">open beta</a> on February 8, 2011 by <a href="/wiki/Bigpoint_Games" title="Bigpoint Games">Bigpoint Games</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
