<p><i><b>Hyakki Yagyō,</b></i> variation: <i><b>Hyakki Yakō</b></i>, (<span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">百鬼夜行</span>, "Night Parade of One Hundred Demons"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Transfiguration_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Transfiguration-2">[2]</a></sup>) is a concept in <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Japan" title="Culture of Japan">Japanese</a> <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a>. It is a parade which is composed of a hundred kinds of <i><a href="/wiki/Y%C5%8Dkai" title="Yōkai">yōkai</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyakki_Yagy%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Legend has it that "every year the <i>yokai</i> <a href="/wiki/Nurarihyon" title="Nurarihyon">Nurarihyon</a>, will lead all of the <i><a href="/wiki/Y%C5%8Dkai" title="Yōkai">yōkai</a></i> through the streets of Japan during summer nights." Anyone who comes across the procession would perish or be spirited away by the <i>yōkai</i>, unless protected by handwritten scrolls by anti-yokai <i>onmyoji</i> spellcasters. It is said that only an onmyoji clan head is strong enough to pass Nurarihyon's Hyakki Yagyo unharmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-youkaijiten_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-youkaijiten-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the account in the Shūgaishō (拾芥抄), a medieval Japanese encyclopedia, the only way to be kept safe from the night parade if it were to come by your house is to stay inside on the specific nights associated with the Chinese zodiac or to chant the magic spell: "<i>KA-TA-SHI-HA-YA, E-KA-SE-NI-KU-RI-NI, TA-ME-RU-SA-KE, TE-E-HI, A-SHI-E-HI, WA-RE-SHI-KO-NI-KE-RI</i>" (カタシハヤ, エカセニクリニ, タメルサケ, テエヒ, アシエヒ, ワレシコニケリ).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_literature">In literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyakki_Yagy%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>hyakki yagyō</i> has appeared in several tales collected by Japanese folklorists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-youkaijiten_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-youkaijiten-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Uji shui Monogatari (宇治拾遺物語), in which a monk encounters a group of a hundred youkai which pass by the <a href="/wiki/Ry%C5%ABsenji" title="Ryūsenji">Ryūsenji temple</a>.</li>
<li>Konjaku Monogatari Shuu (今昔物語集), which tells that during the <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Seiwa" title="Emperor Seiwa">Jougan Era</a> (859–877), the eldest son of minister <a href="/wiki/Fujiwara_no_Mototsune" title="Fujiwara no Mototsune">Fujiwara</a> was on his way to his lover's place when he saw 100 demons walking from the direction of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tokyo" title="University of Tokyo">University of Tokyo</a> Miya main street. Since his attire had the <i>sonjoushi</i> written on it, the demons who noticed this ran away.</li>
<li>The Great Mirror(大鏡, Ookagami)</li>
<li>Goudanshou (江談抄)</li>
<li>Kohonsetsuwashuu (古本説話集)</li>
<li>Houbutsushuu (宝物集)</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_art">In art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyakki_Yagy%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The night parade was a popular theme in Japanese visual art.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Transfiguration_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Transfiguration-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the oldest and most famous examples is the 16th-century <a href="/wiki/Emakimono" title="Emakimono">handscroll</a> <i>Hyakki Yagyō Zu</i> (百鬼夜行図), erroneously attributed to <a href="/wiki/Tosa_Mitsunobu" title="Tosa Mitsunobu">Tosa Mitsunobu</a>, located in the Shinju-an of <a href="/wiki/Daitoku-ji" title="Daitoku-ji">Daitoku-ji</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Transfiguration_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Transfiguration-2">[2]</a></sup> For other picture scrolls, the <i>Hyakki Yagyō Emaki</i> (百鬼夜行絵巻), contains the details of each member in the parade from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muromachi" title="Muromachi">Muromachi</a> period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-youkaijiten_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-youkaijiten-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other notable works in this motif include those by <a href="/wiki/Toriyama_Sekien" title="Toriyama Sekien">Toriyama Sekien</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Gazu_Hyakki_Yagy%C5%8D" title="Gazu Hyakki Yagyō">Gazu Hyakki Yagyō</a></i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pandemonium_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pandemonium-5">[5]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Utagawa_Yoshiiku" title="Utagawa Yoshiiku">Utagawa Yoshiiku</a>. However, Toriyama's work presents <i>yokai</i> in separate, encyclopedic entries rather than assembled in a parade,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pandemonium_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pandemonium-5">[5]</a></sup> while Utagawa's <i>Kokkei Wanisshi-ki</i> ("Comical Record of Japanese History") employs the theme of 100 demons to comment on contemporary Japanese military actions in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boone_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boone-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
