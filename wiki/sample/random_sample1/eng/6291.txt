<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">For 14th century <i>White Lotus Revolution</i>, see <a href="/wiki/Red_Turban_Rebellion" title="Red Turban Rebellion">Red Turban Rebellion</a>.</div>
<p>The <b>White Lotus Rebellion</b> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%B7%9D" title="wikt:川">川</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%A5%9A" title="wikt:楚">楚</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%99%BD" title="wikt:白">白</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%93%AE" title="wikt:蓮">蓮</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%95%99" title="wikt:教">教</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%B5%B7" title="wikt:起">起</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%BE%A9" title="wikt:義">義</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Chu%C4%81n" title="wikt:Chuān">Chuān</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ch%C5%AD" title="wikt:chŭ">chŭ</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/b%C3%A1i" title="wikt:bái">bái</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/li%C3%A1n" title="wikt:lián">lián</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ji%C3%A0o" title="wikt:jiào">jiào</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/q%C7%90" title="wikt:qǐ">qǐ</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/y%C3%AC" title="wikt:yì">yì</a></span></i>, 1796–1806) was a <a href="/wiki/Rebellion" title="Rebellion">rebellion</a> initiated by followers of the <a href="/wiki/White_Lotus" title="White Lotus">White Lotus</a> movement during the <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing dynasty</a> of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. The rebellion began in 1794, when large groups of rebels claiming White Lotus affiliations rose up within the mountainous region that separated <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">Sichuan</a> province from <a href="/wiki/Hubei" title="Hubei">Hubei</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shaanxi" title="Shaanxi">Shaanxi</a> provinces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> A smaller precursor to the main rebellion broke out in 1774, under the leadership of the martial-arts and herbal-healing expert <a href="/wiki/Wang_Lun" title="Wang Lun">Wang Lun</a> in <a href="/wiki/Shandong" title="Shandong">Shandong</a> province of northern China.</p>
<p>Although the rebellion was finally crushed by the Qing government in 1804, it marked a turning point in the history of the Qing dynasty. Qing control weakened and prosperity diminished by the 19th century. The rebellion is estimated to have caused the deaths of some 100,000 rebels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_White_Lotus_Society">The White Lotus Society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White_Lotus_Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The White Lotus Society">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/White_Lotus" title="White Lotus">White Lotus</a></div>
<p>The White Lotus Rebellion was initiated as a <a href="/wiki/Tax_resistance" title="Tax resistance">tax protest</a> led by the <a href="/wiki/White_Lotus" title="White Lotus">White Lotus Society</a>, a secret religious society. The White Lotus Society is traditionally considered to have first appeared during the 14th century under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mongol_people" title="Mongol people">Mongol</a>-led <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">Yuan dynasty</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Red_Turban_Rebellion" title="Red Turban Rebellion">Red Turban Rebellion</a> which took place in 1352, was led by the White Lotus group. By 1387, after more than 30 years of war, their leader, <a href="/wiki/Hongwu_Emperor" title="Hongwu Emperor">Zhu Yuanzhang</a> conquered the North China Plain and occupied the Yuan capital <a href="/wiki/Khanbaliq" title="Khanbaliq">Khanbaliq</a> (present-day <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>). Having attained the <a href="/wiki/Mandate_of_Heaven" title="Mandate of Heaven">Mandate of Heaven</a> and the status of Emperor, Zhu Yuanzhang named his <a href="/wiki/Chinese_era_name" title="Chinese era name">period of reign</a> "Hongwu" (thus he was known as the Hongwu Emperor) and founded a new dynasty – the <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a>. The group later reemerged in the late 18th century in the form of an inspired Chinese movement.</p>
<p>Though many movements and rebellions were considered by imperial bureaucrats to have been led by White Lotus Society leaders, there is reason to doubt that the White Lotus Society had any organizational unity. BJ Ter Haar has argued that the term "White Lotus" was used primarily by Ming and Qing imperial bureaucrats to disparagingly explain a wide range of unconnected millenarian traditions, rebel movements, and popular religious practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> According to Ter Haar, it is clear that the "White Lotus" rebels of the uprisings that occurred between 1796 and 1804 did not voluntarily use the term "White Lotus" to refer to themselves or their movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The term was only used by the millenarian rebels under intense pressure during government interrogations. It is only as historical sources look back upon these events do they began to summarize the various aspects of these uprisings as the "White Lotus rebellion."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Members of the society were not ethnically different from Han Chinese, but subscribed to a belief based on a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">Taoism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Manichaeism" title="Manichaeism">Manichaeism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elleman2001_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Elleman2001-7">[7]</a></sup> The group forecast the advent of <a href="/wiki/Maitreya" title="Maitreya">Maitreya</a>, advocated restoration of the <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Han Chinese</a>-led <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a>, and promised personal <a href="/wiki/Salvation" title="Salvation">salvation</a> to its followers while promising the return of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddha" title="Buddha">Buddha</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> "Return of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddha" title="Buddha">Buddha</a>" is popularly used to describe the coming descent of Maitreya as Buddha on earth: "Buddha come-again".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White_Lotus_Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wang_Lun_Uprising">Wang Lun Uprising</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White_Lotus_Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wang Lun Uprising">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1774, one instance of a derivative sect of the White Lotus, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ba_gua" title="Ba gua">Eight Trigrams</a> arose in the form of underground meditation teachings and practice in <a href="/wiki/Shandong" title="Shandong">Shandong</a> province, not far from <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a> near the city of <a href="/wiki/Linqing" title="Linqing">Linqing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spence_1991_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spence_1991-9">[9]</a></sup> The leader, herbalist and martial artist <a href="/wiki/Wang_Lun" title="Wang Lun">Wang Lun</a>, led an uprising that captured three small cities and laid siege to the larger city of Linqing, a strategic location on the north-south Grand Canal transportation route.</p>
<p>Wang Lun likely failed because he did not make any attempts to raise wide public support. He did not distribute captured wealth or food supplies, nor did he promise to lessen the tax burden. Unable to build up a support base, he was forced to quickly flee all three cities that he attacked in order to evade government troops. Though he passed through an area inhabited by almost a million peasants, his army never measured more than 4,000 soldiers, many of whom had been forced into service.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="White_Lotus_Rebellion">White Lotus Rebellion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White_Lotus_Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: White Lotus Rebellion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1794, a similar movement arose in the mountainous region that separated <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">Sichuan</a> province from <a href="/wiki/Hubei" title="Hubei">Hubei</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shaanxi" title="Shaanxi">Shaanxi</a> provinces in central China, initially as a tax protest. The White Lotus led impoverished settlers into rebellion, promising personal salvation in return for their loyalty. Beginning as tax protests, the eventual rebellion gained growing support and sympathy from many ordinary people. The rebellion grew in number and power and eventually, into a serious concern for the government.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Suppression">Suppression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White_Lotus_Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Suppression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Qianlong_Emperor" title="Qianlong Emperor">Qianlong Emperor</a> (r. 1735–96) sent <a href="/wiki/Helin" title="Helin">Helin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fuk%27anggan" title="Fuk'anggan">Fuk'anggan</a> to quell the uprising. Surprisingly, the ill-organized rebels managed to defeat the inadequate and inefficient Qing imperial forces. After both died in battle in 1796, the Qing government sent new officials, but none were successful. Only after 1800 did the Qing government adopt new tactics that established local militias (<i>tuan</i>) to help surround and destroy the White Lotus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elleman2001_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Elleman2001-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Qing commanders sent to repress the rebellion had a difficult time putting down the White Lotus. The White Lotus bands mainly used guerrilla tactics, and once they disbanded were virtually indistinguishable from the local population. As one Qing official complained:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elleman2001_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Elleman2001-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>"The rebels are all our own subjects. They are not like some external tribe ... that can be demarcated by a territorial boundary and identified by its distinctive clothing and language ... When they congregate and oppose the government, they are rebels; when they disperse and depart, they are civilians once more."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Without any clear enemy to fight, brutality against civilians became more common. Because of the brutality of the Qing troops, however, the troops were soon nicknamed the "Red Lotus" Society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elleman2001_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Elleman2001-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>A systematic program of pacification followed: resettling the populace in hundreds of stockaded villages and organizing them into militias. In its last stage, the Qing suppression policy combined pursuit and extermination of rebel guerrilla bands with a program of amnesty for deserters. The imperial authorities suppressed the White Lotus Rebellion in 1805 using a combination of military and social policies. Approximately 7,000 Banner troops were sent in from Manchuria in combination with <a href="/wiki/Green_Standard_Army" title="Green Standard Army">Green Standard Army</a> soldiers from Guizhou and Yunnan as well as tens of thousands of local mercenaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elleman2001_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Elleman2001-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>A decree from the <a href="/wiki/Daoguang_Emperor" title="Daoguang Emperor">Daoguang Emperor</a> admitted, "it was extortion by local officials that goaded the people into rebellion..." Using the arrest of sectarian members as a threat, local officials and police extorted money from people. Actual participation in sect activities had no impact on an arrest; whether or not monetary demands were met, however, did.</p>
<p>Administrators also seized and destroyed sectarian scriptures used by the religious groups. One such official was <a href="/wiki/Huang_Yupian" title="Huang Yupian">Huang Yupian</a> (<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">黃育楩</span>), who refuted the ideas found in the scriptures with orthodox Confucian and Buddhist views in <i>A Detailed Refutation of Heresy</i> (<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">破邪詳辯</span> <i>Pōxié Xiángbiàn</i>), which was written in 1838. This book has since become an invaluable source in understanding the beliefs of these groups.</p>
<p>The end of the White Lotus Rebellion in 1804 also brought an end to the myth of the military invincibility of the Manchus, perhaps contributing to the greater frequency of rebellions in the 19th century. The White Lotus continued to be active, and might have influenced the next major domestic rebellion, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eight_Trigrams_Uprising_of_1813" title="Eight Trigrams Uprising of 1813">Eight Trigrams Uprising of 1813</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elleman2001_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-Elleman2001-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White_Lotus_Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After 48 years, <a href="/wiki/Zeng_Guofan" title="Zeng Guofan">Zeng Guofan</a> studied and was inspired by the Qing government's methods during the White Lotus Rebellion while considering ways to suppress the <a href="/wiki/Taiping_Rebellion" title="Taiping Rebellion">Taiping Rebellion</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rebel_leaders">Rebel leaders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White_Lotus_Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rebel leaders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
