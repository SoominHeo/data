<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">White Rod</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>The <b>White Rod</b>, <b>White Wand</b>, <b>Rod of Inauguration</b>, or <b>Wand of Sovereignty</b>, in the <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish language</a> variously called the <b>slat na ríghe</b> (rod of kingship) and <b>slat tighearnais</b> (rod of lordship), was the primary symbol of a <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Gaelic</a> king or lord's legitimate authority and the principal prop used in his inauguration ceremony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> First documented in the 12th century <i>Life of Máedóc of Ferns</i>, but assumed to have been used long before then,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> it is last documented in Ireland in the early 17th century. In Scotland the rod was used into the 13th century for the inauguration of its last Gaelic kings,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and for the Norse-Gaelic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lords_of_the_Isles" title="Lords of the Isles">Lords of the Isles</a> into the 15th.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the reception of the rod was in origin a Gaelic cultural feature, following the <a href="/wiki/Norman_invasion_of_Ireland" title="Norman invasion of Ireland">Norman invasion of Ireland</a> some foreign families became significantly <a href="/wiki/Gaelicisation" title="Gaelicisation">Gaelicised</a>. A notable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman" title="Anglo-Norman">Anglo-Norman</a> example were the great <a href="/wiki/House_of_Burke" title="House of Burke">De Burgh</a> magnates styled <a href="/wiki/Mac_William_%C3%8Dochtar" title="Mac William Íochtar">Mac William Íochtar</a>, who had become completely Gaelicised and received the White Rod.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Qualities_and_symbolism">Qualities and symbolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White_Rod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Qualities and symbolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The rod was required to be both white and straight, with the colour representing purity and the straightness of justice, according to the account given by <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Keating" title="Geoffrey Keating">Geoffrey Keating</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ceremony">Ceremony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White_Rod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ceremony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Although the meaning and purpose were always the same, the particulars of the ceremony appear to have varied across the Gaelic world. Most notably who presented the new lord or king with the rod depended on the history and traditions of each kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>A note to the pedigree of the <a href="/wiki/O%27Mahony" title="O'Mahony">O'Mahonys</a> at Lambeth, written by <a href="/wiki/George_Carew_(diplomat)" title="George Carew (diplomat)">Sir George Carew</a>, circa 1600/3:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><a href="/wiki/McMahon" title="McMahon">O'Mahon's</a> country doeth follow the ancient <a href="/wiki/Tanistry" title="Tanistry">Tanist law</a> of Ireland; and unto whom <a href="/wiki/MacCarthy_Reagh" title="MacCarthy Reagh">Mac Carthy Reagh</a> shall give a white rod, he is O'Mahon, or Lord of the Country; but the giving of the rod avails nothing except he be chosen by the followers, nor yet the election without the rod. The MacCarthy Reagh was inaugurated with the same ceremonial with which he inaugurated the O'Mahon and other dependent chiefs. There was a grievance attached to this, and it did not escape the keen eyes of the Cork juries, who presented: 'That when any Lord or Gentleman of the Irishry within this county, is made Lord or Captain of his name or kindredtie, he taketh of every inhabitant, <a href="/wiki/Freehold_(law)" title="Freehold (law)">freeholder</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Leasehold_estate" title="Leasehold estate">tenant</a> under him, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cow" title="Cow">cow</a> to be paid for erecting a rod in that name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scottish_Parliament">Scottish Parliament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White_Rod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Scottish Parliament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_with_England_in_1707" title="Union with England in 1707">Union with England in 1707</a>, there was a <b>Gentleman Usher of the White Rod</b> in the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Scotland" title="Parliament of Scotland">Parliament of Scotland</a>, who had a similar role to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gentleman_Usher_of_the_Black_Rod" title="Gentleman Usher of the Black Rod">Gentleman Usher of the Black Rod</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Great_Britain" title="Parliament of Great Britain">British</a> and then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK_Parliament" title="UK Parliament">UK Parliaments</a>.</p>
<p>The Heritable Usher of the White Rod' is the only example of an office of <a href="/wiki/The_Crown" title="The Crown">the Crown</a> becoming incorporated as a company. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Walker_Trust_Act,_1877&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Walker Trust Act, 1877 (page does not exist)">Walker Trust Act, 1877</a>, incorporated the office into the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Walker_Trustees&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Walker Trustees (page does not exist)">Walker Trustees</a>, entitling the trustees to charge dues from anyone receiving an honour from the Crown. In 1908 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Society_of_Knights_Bachelor" title="Society of Knights Bachelor">Society of Knights Bachelor</a> was formed to contest this right, but a <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Session" title="Court of Session">Court of Session</a> case the following year confirmed the right of the Walker Trustees to charge recipients of honours. However, the Society of Knights Bachelor won an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Appeal_to_the_House_of_Lords" title="Appeal to the House of Lords">appeal to the House of Lords</a> in 1911.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Bishop of Edinburgh as Ex Officio chair of the Walker Trustees is the <b>Heritable Usher of the White Rod</b>. The current holder is the <a href="/wiki/John_Armes" title="John Armes">Rt. Revd Dr John A Armes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Edinburgh" title="Bishop of Edinburgh">Bishop of Edinburgh</a>, but the role carries no duties.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
