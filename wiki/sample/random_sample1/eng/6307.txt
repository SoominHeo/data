<p><b>Vancouver International Airport</b> (<a href="/wiki/International_Air_Transport_Association_airport_code" title="International Air Transport Association airport code">IATA</a>: <b>YVR</b>, <a href="/wiki/International_Civil_Aviation_Organization_airport_code" title="International Civil Aviation Organization airport code">ICAO</a>: <b>CYVR</b>) is located on <a href="/wiki/Sea_Island_(British_Columbia)" title="Sea Island (British Columbia)">Sea Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_British_Columbia" title="Richmond, British Columbia">Richmond, British Columbia</a>, Canada, about 12 km (7.5 mi) from <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Vancouver" title="Downtown Vancouver">Downtown</a> <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a>. It is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_the_busiest_airports_in_Canada" title="List of the busiest airports in Canada">second busiest airport in Canada</a> by aircraft movements (316,182<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2015pax_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-2015pax-1">[1]</a></sup>) and passengers (20.3 million<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2015pax_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-2015pax-1">[1]</a></sup>), behind <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Pearson_International_Airport" title="Toronto Pearson International Airport">Toronto Pearson International Airport</a>. It has non-stop flights daily to Asia, Europe, Oceania, the United States, Mexico and other airports within Canada. The airport has won several notable international best airport awards; it won the <a href="/wiki/Skytrax" title="Skytrax">Skytrax</a> Best North American Airport award in 2007 and 2010 through 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The airport also made the list of top 10 airports in the world for the first time in 2012, rated at 9th (2012), 8th (2013), and 9th (2014) overall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-5">[5]</a></sup> It is the only North American airport included in the top 10 for 2013 and 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> YVR also retains the distinction of Best Canadian Airport in the regional results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It is a <a href="/wiki/Airline_hub" title="Airline hub">hub</a> for <a href="/wiki/Air_Canada" title="Air Canada">Air Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/WestJet" title="WestJet">WestJet</a>. It is also an operating base for <a href="/wiki/Air_Transat" title="Air Transat">Air Transat</a>. Vancouver International Airport is one of eight Canadian airports that have <a href="/wiki/United_States_border_preclearance" title="United States border preclearance">US Border Preclearance facilities</a>. It is also one of the few major international airports to have a terminal for scheduled floatplanes.</p>
<p>Vancouver International Airport is owned by <a href="/wiki/Transport_Canada" title="Transport Canada">Transport Canada</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and is managed by Vancouver Airport Authority,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> which also manages other airports around the world through its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vancouver_Airport_Services" title="Vancouver Airport Services">Vancouver Airport Services</a> subsidiary.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1929 the city of Vancouver purchased land on Sea Island for aviation purposes, replacing the original grass airstrip at <a href="/wiki/Minoru_Park" title="Minoru Park">Minoru Park</a>. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the airports and its original terminal, now the South Terminal, were leased to the Federal government and operated by the Department of National Defence and the Department of Transport. The airport was a base for <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Air_Force" title="Royal Canadian Air Force">Royal Canadian Air Force</a> training. The crews and their families were housed in a new townsite on the island, named Burkeville after Boeing president Stanley Burke. Funds from the lease were used to purchase additional land for new hangars and a production plant for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_Aircraft_of_Canada" title="Boeing Aircraft of Canada">Boeing Aircraft of Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The present main terminal was completed in 1968 and has since been expanded to include separate domestic and international terminals. A north runway was completed in 1996.</p>
<p>In 2011 the airport announced that it will enact a program aiming to encourage airlines to start more flights between Vancouver and Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term <b>Pacific Gateway</b> describes YVR for its proximity to Asia in relation to the rest of Canada, as well as the large Asian population and Canadian-Asian business connections in the region, make Vancouver International Airport the major gateway between Canada and Asia. It has more trans-Pacific flights than any other airport in Canada.</p>

<p>Vancouver International Airport has two <a href="/wiki/Airport_terminal" title="Airport terminal">terminals</a>: the Main Terminal and the South Terminal. Free Wi-Fi is available in all sections of the airport.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_Terminal">Main Terminal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Main Terminal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Main Terminal is divided in two sections: the Domestic Terminal and the International Terminal. The majority of flights operate at the Main Terminal. The two sections are connected both pre-security and post-security; however, the US border preclearance area is separated off using movable glass partitions which can be adjusted based on the arrangement of swing gates between concourses D and E.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Domestic_Terminal">Domestic Terminal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Domestic Terminal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Domestic Terminal was constructed in 1968 by the <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a>-based firm Thompson, Berwick and Pratt<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thecanadianencyclopedia.com_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-thecanadianencyclopedia.com-14">[14]</a></sup> and recently given a top-to-bottom renovation by Vancouver architect Kasian Kennedy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The Domestic terminal consists of three concourses (A-C).</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Concourse_A">Concourse A</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Concourse A">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Concourse A consists of six temporary gates: A6 through A10 and A12. These gates are used by <a href="/wiki/WestJet_Encore" title="WestJet Encore">WestJet Encore</a>. A new permanent ground-loading concourse is planned to start construction in 2016.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Concourse_B">Concourse B</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Concourse B">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Concourse B comprises gates B12 through B23 and B26 through B28, for a total of 15 gates. Gates B14 to B17 have provisions allowing international arrivals in the future. Gate B23 is a ground loading ramp accessing stands 23A, 23B, and 23C. Concourse B is for domestic flights by <a href="/wiki/Air_North" title="Air North">Air North</a>, <a href="/wiki/Air_Transat" title="Air Transat">Air Transat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Mountain_Air" title="Central Mountain Air">Central Mountain Air</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sunwing_Airlines" title="Sunwing Airlines">Sunwing Airlines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/WestJet" title="WestJet">WestJet</a>.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Concourse_C">Concourse C</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Concourse C">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>There are 24 gates in Concourse C: C29 through C52. This is the hub for <a href="/wiki/Air_Canada" title="Air Canada">Air Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_Canada_Express" title="Air Canada Express">Air Canada Express</a> domestic flights. C50, C51 and C52 are also frequently used by <a href="/wiki/WestJet" title="WestJet">WestJet</a> as they are swing gates and can be used for international arrivals and departures (designated as D50 to D52). In addition, C48 and C49 can receive international/US arrivals.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Terminal">International Terminal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: International Terminal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The International Terminal was designed by <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Architectura" title="Architectura">Architectura</a> (now <a class="external text" href="http://www.stantec.com/our-work/sectors/airports.html" rel="nofollow">Stantec Architecture Ltd.</a>) with <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City,_Missouri" title="Kansas City, Missouri">Kansas City</a>-based <a href="/wiki/HNTB" title="HNTB">HNTB Corporation</a> (1994–96).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thecanadianencyclopedia.com_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-thecanadianencyclopedia.com-14">[14]</a></sup> YVR is one of eight Canadian airports with <a href="/wiki/United_States_border_preclearance" title="United States border preclearance">United States border preclearance</a> facilities, which are situated in the International Terminal. All gates in the International Terminal can accept flights arriving from all international and US origins: passengers are directed onto overhead walkways which lead to the customs and immigration area.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Concourse_D">Concourse D</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Concourse D">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Concourse D is used by all international-bound and select US-bound flights from Vancouver. There are 14 gates: D50 to D55, D58, D64 to D67, D70, D71, and D73. All of these gates can handle <a href="/wiki/Wide-body_aircraft" title="Wide-body aircraft">wide-body aircraft</a>; six gates are fitted with 2 <a href="/wiki/Jet_bridge" title="Jet bridge">jet bridges</a>, two of which can handle the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A380" title="Airbus A380">Airbus A380</a>. <a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a> operates the A380 seasonally to Vancouver.</p>
<p>D50 to D52 are swing gates that can be used by domestic flights (designated C50 to C52). D67 through D73 are swing gates which may be partitioned off to allow for a higher number of US preclearance flights (designated E67 through E73).</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Concourse_E">Concourse E</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Concourse E">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Concourse E is the US border preclearance area and consists of 20 gates: E75 to E87 and E90 to E96. Gates E75 to E78 can accommodate smaller wide-body aircraft and are swing gates which can be used for international departures (as gates D75 to D78). Gate E95 accesses stands 95A and 95B.</p>
<p>The majority of US-bound flights operate from Concourse E; the only exceptions are flights from an international origin (<a href="/wiki/Cathay_Pacific" title="Cathay Pacific">Cathay Pacific</a>'s Hong Kong—Vancouver—New York and <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Airlines" title="Philippine Airlines">Philippine Airlines</a>' Manila—Vancouver—New York flights), or flights that depart after 8:30pm (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Delta_Air_Lines" title="Delta Air Lines">Delta Air Lines</a>' Vancouver—New York flight).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Terminal">South Terminal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: South Terminal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The South Terminal is a portion of the original pre-1968 terminal which is still in use. This includes the floatplane terminal. It also houses the corporate headquarters and main base of <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Coastal_Airlines" title="Pacific Coastal Airlines">Pacific Coastal Airlines</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harbour_Air" title="Harbour Air">Harbour Air</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The South Terminal serves <a href="/wiki/Regional_airline" title="Regional airline">regional airlines</a> which fly mostly within British Columbia, in addition to helicopters and chartered flights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>YVR has been awarded the Skytrax World Airport Awards Best Airport in North America a record seven years in a row. It is also currently ranked 14th worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>YVR's interior has a uniquely British Columbian theme, featuring one of the most extensive collections of <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Pacific_Northwest_Coast" title="Indigenous peoples of the Pacific Northwest Coast">Pacific Northwest Coast Native</a> art in the world, and blues and greens to reflect the colours of the land, sea and sky. This theme was put in place by Vancouver-based Architectura (now <a class="external text" href="http://www.stantec.com/our-work/sectors/airports.html" rel="nofollow">Stantec Architecture Ltd</a>). The airport uses a great deal of carpet and vast expanses of glass to let in large amounts of natural light. One of the most noticeable places for an arriving passenger is the International arrivals hall, a large area where customs and immigration procedures are completed. Arriving passengers come down escalators leading to a platform across a large waterfall. The YVR aboriginal art collection includes wooden sculptures and <a href="/wiki/Totem_pole" title="Totem pole">totem poles</a>. <a href="/wiki/Bill_Reid" title="Bill Reid">Bill Reid</a>'s sculpture in bronze, "The <a href="/wiki/Spirit_of_Haida_Gwaii" title="Spirit of Haida Gwaii">Spirit of Haida Gwaii</a>, The Jade Canoe", is displayed in the international departures area. This is the second of two castings of this sculpture; the first casting, "The Spirit of Haida Gwaii, The Black Canoe", is now displayed outside the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Embassy_in_Washington" title="Canadian Embassy in Washington">Canadian Embassy</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_D.C." title="Washington D.C.">Washington D.C.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The Institute for Stained Glass in Canada has documented the stained glass at Vancouver International Airport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accessibility">Accessibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Accessibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vancouver International Airport is well regarded as one of the most accessible airports in the world. In 2004, the Vancouver International Airport was awarded of the Rick Hansen Accessibility Award, which recognizes "facilities and communities that improve the quality of life for people with mobility limitations".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Picard.2C_A._2004_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Picard.2C_A._2004-22">[22]</a></sup> The Vancouver International Airport has exceeded national building code requirements with respect to access for people with disabilities, which Hansen has said "benefits not only people in wheelchairs, but the elderly, the blind, parents pushing strollers and everyone else with mobility problems".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Picard.2C_A._2004_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Picard.2C_A._2004-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 1992, the Vancouver Airport Authority has been working with an independent accessibility consultant to eliminate the physical barriers in the built environment and is "committed to providing fully accessible terminal facilities for people of all backgrounds and capabilities".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yvr.ca_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-yvr.ca-23">[23]</a></sup> With 80% of the vacationing traveling public over the age of 55, and with more than 550 million people world-wide that have a disability, the Vancouver International Airport's commitment to meaningful access is a fundamental part of good customer service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Designated short-term parking spaces and curb-side ramps are available on each level of the terminal building for vehicles displaying a valid SPARC permit, and are located next to main doors near check-in counters and baggage claim areas for easier access. Lowered counters with toe clearance for wheelchair users are also available at check-in, customer care and all retail outlets in the Vancouver Airport. Bathrooms have also been designed to be wheelchair accessible with doorless and no-touch entry features, lowered sinks and handsfree bathroom dispensers. Grab bars and emergency call buttons are also present in all wheelchair accessible toilet stalls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada._2012_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canada._2012-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Low resistance carpeting and other materials such as laminate flooring have been utilized throughout the airport to make it easier for people using wheelchairs and walkers to move throughout the airport. Elevators are large and allow for easy turning in a wheelchair and special wheelchairs designed to fit down aircraft aisles are utilized to assist with boarding and de-planing. Wheelchair lifts have been installed at aircraft gates to provide disabled passengers with their own wheelchairs as quickly as possible after an aircraft lands in Vancouver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yvr.ca_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-yvr.ca-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Features that have been implemented throughout the airport to aid those with hearing loss include a public address system to reduce noise pollution for those with hearing aids. The Vancouver International Airport has installed more individual speakers in a given space than is standard, which allows the volume of the speakers to be turned down and provides a better quality of sound. At check-in counters, amplified handsets are available to aid those with hearing aids, and all telephones throughout the airport have adjustable volume controls. "Visual pagers" are dedicated video monitors that are located throughout the airport and convey important information to travellers that have hearing impairments. In the event of an emergency, a video override system displays large bold messages on all entertainment systems, and provides information about the type of emergency and the required course of action from the public. Strobe fire alarms have also been installed throughout the airport and have been carefully programmed to prevent seizures to those with epilepsy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada._2012_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Canada._2012-25">[25]</a></sup> The Vancouver Airport has its own TTY telephone number for incoming inquiries about airport operations and within the terminal there are also 23 public telephone equipped with TTY at both stand up and seated positions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada._2012_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Canada._2012-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Vancouver International Airport also has numerous features that have been implemented to assist visually impaired travellers. Three types of flooring are utilized throughout the terminal and function as a texturized guide to assist travellers in identifying their location within the airport. In areas with tile or terrazzo, patterns in the tile help to identify exits. Areas that have carpet help to identify that a gate is close by and areas with laminate flooring indicate retail spaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yvr.ca_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-yvr.ca-23">[23]</a></sup> Tactile maps are also available at customer service counters throughout the airport, and braille and tactile lettering are used throughout the airport to indicate building features such as washrooms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada._2012_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-Canada._2012-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_Coat_Ambassadors">Green Coat Ambassadors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Green Coat Ambassadors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vancouver Airport Authority was one of the first airports in North America to institute a volunteer program in 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Volunteers in green vest/jacket are deployed around the airport to provide information, customer service and be the 'eyes and ears' for the various partners in the airport community between the hours of 6 am to 10 pm everyday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Volunteers are given basic training in airport operations and undertake many of the similar trainings mandated to airport employees. Each volunteer is required to obtain Transportation Security Clearance and Restricted Area Identification Card for the purposes of accessing the restricted and sterile areas of the terminal.</p>

<p>In May 2005, the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Canada" title="Government of Canada">federal government</a>, which owns the airport land, announced it was cutting rent costs by 54%. The rent reductions will cut the cost of the lease by approximately $840 million CAD between 2006–2020, or $5.0 billion over the term of the lease, which ends in 2052. Currently, the airport authority pays about $80 million each year in rent.</p>
<p>Passengers traveling through YVR are no longer required to pay a separate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airport_Improvement_Fee" title="Airport Improvement Fee">airport improvement fee</a>; it is now included in the ticket price.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rapid_transit_.28SkyTrain.29">Rapid transit (SkyTrain)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Rapid transit (SkyTrain)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vancouver_Airport_Skytrain_Station_2008-04-22.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Vancouver_Airport_Skytrain_Station_2008-04-22.JPG/220px-Vancouver_Airport_Skytrain_Station_2008-04-22.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Vancouver_Airport_Skytrain_Station_2008-04-22.JPG/330px-Vancouver_Airport_Skytrain_Station_2008-04-22.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Vancouver_Airport_Skytrain_Station_2008-04-22.JPG/440px-Vancouver_Airport_Skytrain_Station_2008-04-22.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

Construction of the Skytrain Canada Line .</div>

<p>The international and domestic terminals are served by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/YVR%E2%80%93Airport_Station" title="YVR–Airport Station">YVR–Airport Station</a>, a terminus station of the <a href="/wiki/Canada_Line" title="Canada Line">Canada Line</a>. A connector ($117 million, completed in 2007) links the international terminal with the domestic terminal and serves as the arrival and departure area for users of the Canada Line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The Canada Line, one of three existing lines of <a href="/wiki/Metro_Vancouver" title="Metro Vancouver">Metro Vancouver</a>'s <a href="/wiki/SkyTrain_(Vancouver)" title="SkyTrain (Vancouver)">SkyTrain</a> <a href="/wiki/Rapid_transit" title="Rapid transit">rapid transit</a> network, opened in August 2009, in advance of the <a href="/wiki/2010_Winter_Olympics" title="2010 Winter Olympics">2010 Winter Olympics</a> in the following February.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Vancouver's airport was the only one in Canada with a rail rapid transit connection, until <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toronto_Pearson" title="Toronto Pearson">Toronto Pearson</a> opened the <a href="/wiki/Union_Pearson_Express" title="Union Pearson Express">Union Pearson Express</a> in 2015. Vancouver International Airport contributed $300 million to the Canada Line construction.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bus">Bus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Bus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Late at night and during Canada Line service interruptions, the N10 <a href="/wiki/Night_bus_service" title="Night bus service">night bus</a> connects the airport's international and domestic terminals to Richmond and downtown Vancouver. The airport's south terminal is served by the C92 bus, which connects to the Canada Line at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bridgeport_Station_(TransLink)" title="Bridgeport Station (TransLink)">Bridgeport Station</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion">Expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>YVR completed a $1.4-billion multi-year capital development plan, which included a four-gate expansion to the International Terminal Wing, completed in June 2007. Two of the four new gates are conventional wide-bodied gates and two are able to accommodate the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A380" title="Airbus A380">Airbus A380</a>. The international terminal addition includes interior design elements intended to represent British Columbia, including a stream in a pathway and fish and jellyfish tanks.</p>
<p>A five-gate and food and retail expansion was completed in 2009 for Domestic Terminal's C-Pier. The train that links downtown Vancouver, YVR and Richmond opened in August 2009.</p>
<p>Vancouver International Airport Authority has developed a 2007-2027 Master Plan and Land Use Plan, a look forward 20 years to ensure YVR will be able to accommodate the passengers it expects. It is asking the community for input and toured local malls with an informational display to elicit feedback. The tour is complete, but the public can still provide feedback through the Master Plan section of the YVR website, where a copy of the draft Master Plan recommendations is also available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation_Yellow_Ribbon">Operation Yellow Ribbon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Operation Yellow Ribbon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Operation_Yellow_Ribbon" title="Operation Yellow Ribbon">Operation Yellow Ribbon</a></div>
<p>As a result of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/September_11_Attacks" title="September 11 Attacks">September 11 Attacks</a>, the airspace over the United States was shut down. Aircraft over the North Atlantic and Pacific bound for the United States were therefore diverted to Canadian airports. Vancouver International Airport accommodated 34 of these flights (3rd highest after <a href="/wiki/Halifax_Stanfield_International_Airport" title="Halifax Stanfield International Airport">Halifax</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gander_International_Airport" title="Gander International Airport">Gander</a>) amounting to a total of 8,500 passengers.</p>
<p>The airport won the 2001 Airport Management Award from the B.C. Aviation Council<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> and was cited for overcoming many challenges in a professional and compassionate way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vancouver_International_Airport_Authority">Vancouver International Airport Authority</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Vancouver International Airport Authority">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>YVR is managed by <b>Vancouver International Airport Authority</b>, created in 1992 to take over day-to-day management of the airport from <a href="/wiki/Transport_Canada" title="Transport Canada">Transport Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-45">[45]</a></sup> VIAA has a staff of 400.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>The VIAA is headed by the CEO and President <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Craig_Richmond&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Craig Richmond (page does not exist)">Craig Richmond</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_and_policing">Fire and policing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Fire and policing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Vancouver International Airport Emergency Services Team is the primary fire services at the airport. The airport fire station and tenders (4) are owned by the Vancouver Airport Authority. Richmond Fire has additional resources when required from Fire Hall 1 at 6960 Gilbert Road, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Coast_Guard" title="Canadian Coast Guard">Canadian Coast Guard</a>.</p>
<p>Policing at the airport is provided by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Mounted_Police" title="Royal Canadian Mounted Police">Royal Canadian Mounted Police</a> Airport Detachment.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_incidents">Accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vancouver_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Accidents and incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vancouver_Airport_Tower.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="614" data-file-width="461" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Vancouver_Airport_Tower.jpg/170px-Vancouver_Airport_Tower.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Vancouver_Airport_Tower.jpg/255px-Vancouver_Airport_Tower.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Vancouver_Airport_Tower.jpg/340px-Vancouver_Airport_Tower.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vancouver_Airport_Tower.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
YVR ATC Tower</div>

<ul>
<li>On February 7, 1968, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Pacific_Airlines" title="Canadian Pacific Airlines">Canadian Pacific Airlines</a> <a href="/wiki/Boeing_707" title="Boeing 707">Boeing 707</a> overran a runway and hit a building, while landing in heavy fog, killing one crew member.</li>
<li>On March 1, 1970, <a href="/wiki/Vickers_Viscount" title="Vickers Viscount">Vickers Viscount</a> CF-THY of <a href="/wiki/Air_Canada" title="Air Canada">Air Canada</a> collided in mid-air with <a href="/wiki/ERCO_Ercoupe" title="ERCO Ercoupe">Ercoupe 415</a> CF-SHN on approach to Vancouver International Airport. The Ercoupe pilot was killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASN010370_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-ASN010370-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li>On August 19, 1995, <a href="/wiki/Douglas_C-47_Skytrain" title="Douglas C-47 Skytrain">Douglas C-47B</a> (DC-3) C-GZOF of <a href="/wiki/Air_North" title="Air North">Air North</a> crashed during an emergency return to the airport killing one of the three crew. The aircraft was on a ferry flight to <a href="/wiki/Prince_Rupert_Airport" title="Prince Rupert Airport">Prince Rupert Airport</a> when the starboard propellor went into <a href="/wiki/Overspeed_(engine)" title="Overspeed (engine)">overspeed</a> and the decision was made to return to Vancouver International.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASN190895_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-ASN190895-48">[48]</a></sup></li>
<li>On October 19, 1995, a Canadian Airlines <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_DC-10" title="McDonnell Douglas DC-10">McDonnell Douglas DC-10</a> aborted takeoff on runway 26 (now 26L) two seconds after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V_Speeds" title="V Speeds">V<sub>1</sub></a> call. The aircraft ended up in the soft ground west of the end of runway, causing the failure of the nose gear. All 243 passengers and 14 crew escaped with no more than minor injuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></li>
<li>On September 11, 2001, an <a href="/wiki/Air_China" title="Air China">Air China</a> <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">747</a> from <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a> to <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, was escorted by two U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-15_Eagle" title="F-15 Eagle">F-15s</a> onto the airport's north runway during <a href="/wiki/Operation_Yellow_Ribbon" title="Operation Yellow Ribbon">Operation Yellow Ribbon</a>, apparently due to a communication problem.</li>
<li>On October 14, 2007, a Polish immigrant, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Dzieka%C5%84ski_Taser_incident" title="Robert Dziekański Taser incident">Robert Dziekanski</a>, died after being shot with a <a href="/wiki/Taser" title="Taser">taser</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Mounted_Police" title="Royal Canadian Mounted Police">Royal Canadian Mounted Police</a> at the airport. Dziekański, who did not speak English, became agitated after waiting approximately 10 hours at the arrivals hall because he could not find his mother. While police were attempting to take Dziekanski into custody he was tased by officers and subsequently died. The subsequent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Braidwood_Inquiry" title="Braidwood Inquiry">Braidwood Inquiry</a> began in May 2008. In June 2010, the judge found that the use of the Taser was not justified. The RCMP issued an apology to Dziekanski's mother. The commission also found that Tasers have the capability to injure or kill by causing heart irregularities, especially where the individual is medically or emotionally compromised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></li>
<li>On October 19, 2007, at approximately 4:10 pm, a <a href="/wiki/Piper_PA-34_Seneca" title="Piper PA-34 Seneca">Piper Seneca</a> bound for <a href="/wiki/Pitt_Meadows_Airport" title="Pitt Meadows Airport">Pitt Meadows Airport</a> took off from YVR and crashed into a nearby apartment building in Richmond. The pilot was the sole occupant of the plane. He was killed in the crash. Two others were injured, both of whom were in the apartment building at the time.</li>
<li>On December 10, 2007 Sikh protesters paralyzed the airport caused by the deportation of illegal paralyzed refuge Laibar Singh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li>On September 18, 2008, in the afternoon, an Air Canada <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A340" title="Airbus A340">Airbus A340</a> collided with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Canada_Jazz" title="Air Canada Jazz">Air Canada Jazz</a> <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Dash_8" title="Bombardier Dash 8">Dash 8</a> aircraft. The Jazz flight was <a href="/wiki/Taxiing" title="Taxiing">taxiing</a> on the runway when it collided. The Air Canada flight was bound for <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>. Both aircraft received damage but there were no injuries or fatalities.</li>
<li>On July 9, 2009, at approximately 10:08pm, a <a href="/wiki/Piper_PA-31_Navajo" title="Piper PA-31 Navajo">Piper Navajo</a> airplane originating from <a href="/wiki/Victoria,_British_Columbia" title="Victoria, British Columbia">Victoria</a> crashed into an industrial area in <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_British_Columbia" title="Richmond, British Columbia">Richmond, British Columbia</a>. The two pilots were killed. It was owned and operated by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_Air_Charters&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Canadian Air Charters (page does not exist)">Canadian Air Charters</a> and was carrying units of blood for <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Blood_Services" title="Canadian Blood Services">Canadian Blood Services</a> at the time. Officials say that wake turbulence was the main cause of the crash. Fatigue, along with diminished depth perception in darkness, was also a factor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wake_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-wake-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
<li>On October 27, 2011, at around 4:15pm, a <a href="/wiki/Northern_Thunderbird_Air" title="Northern Thunderbird Air">Northern Thunderbird Air</a> <a href="/wiki/Beechcraft_King_Air" title="Beechcraft King Air">Beechcraft King Air 100</a> attempted to land on the south runway but missed by about 900 metres (3,000 ft), hitting a lamppost and car, then crashing on nearby Russ Baker Way and Gilbert Road at the west end of <a href="/wiki/Dinsmore_Bridge" title="Dinsmore Bridge">Dinsmore Bridge</a>. There were seven passengers and two crew members on board; only the pilot was confirmed dead at 9:00 pm that evening, while the others survived with various injuries. Two on the ground were also injured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> On November 16, 2011, the co-pilot of the flight died as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
