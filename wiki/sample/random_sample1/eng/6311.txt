<p><b>Banff</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a town in the <a href="/wiki/Banff_and_Buchan" title="Banff and Buchan">Banff and Buchan</a> area of <a href="/wiki/Aberdeenshire" title="Aberdeenshire">Aberdeenshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. Banff is situated on <a href="/wiki/Banff_Bay" title="Banff Bay">Banff Bay</a> and faces the town of <a href="/wiki/Macduff,_Aberdeenshire" title="Macduff, Aberdeenshire">Macduff</a> across the estuary of the <a href="/wiki/River_Deveron" title="River Deveron">River Deveron</a>. Banff is a former <a href="/wiki/Royal_burgh" title="Royal burgh">royal burgh</a>, and is the traditional <a href="/wiki/County_town" title="County town">county town</a> of <a href="/wiki/Banffshire" title="Banffshire">Banffshire</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Banff,_Aberdeenshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origin of the name is uncertain. It may be derived from the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a> <i>banbh</i> meaning 'piglet'; <i>buinne</i>, a stream; or a contraction of <i>Bean-naomh</i> meaning 'holy woman' (as reflected in the burgh's coat of arms which features the Virgin Mary).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crammond_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-crammond-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/William_J._Watson" title="William J. Watson">William J. Watson</a> writes: "It is true that Banff is Banb in the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Deer" title="Book of Deer">Book of Deer</a> and Banbh in modern Gaelic — one syllable. On the other hand, banbh, a sucking pig, is not appropriate — one might say it is impossible — as the name of a place or district."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Banff,_Aberdeenshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Banff's first <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castle</a> was built to repel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking" title="Viking">Viking</a> invaders and a charter of 1163 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AD" title="AD">AD</a> shows that <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_IV_of_Scotland" title="Malcolm IV of Scotland">Malcolm IV</a> was living there at that time. During this period the town was a busy trading centre in the "free hanse" of Northern Scottish burghs, despite not having its own harbour until 1775. The first recorded <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_of_Banff" title="Sheriff of Banff">Sheriff of Banff</a> was Richard de Strathewan in 1264, and in 1372 Royal Burgh status was conferred by King <a href="/wiki/Robert_II_of_Scotland" title="Robert II of Scotland">Robert II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crammond_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-crammond-3">[3]</a></sup> By the 15th century Banff was one of three principal towns exporting salmon to the continent of Europe, along with <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Montrose,_Angus" title="Montrose, Angus">Montrose</a>.</p>
<p>Seventeenth century Scotland was lawless and some of the worst offenders were members of the nobility. According to records kept by historian <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=William_Cramond&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="William Cramond (page does not exist)">William Cramond</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tolbooth" title="Tolbooth">tolbooth</a> (courthouse and prison) of Banff was, in 1628, the site of an altercation between Lord Banff and James Ogilvie, his relative. Reportedly, he struck James Ogilvie upon the head with a baton during a court hearing. Twenty of his friends and followers then attacked Ogilvie with swords before chasing him into the street and finishing him off with a pistol shot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cooper_1999_3_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper_1999_3-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Banff and Macduff are separated by the valley of the <a href="/wiki/River_Deveron" title="River Deveron">River Deveron</a>. This unpredictable river was finally tamed by the seven arched bridge completed in 1779<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/John_Smeaton" title="John Smeaton">John Smeaton</a>. An earlier bridge had been built in 1765, but was swept away in 1768. The old ferry was brought back into use, until it was lost in a flood in 1773.</p>
<p>A public meeting was held in 1800 and passed a resolution for the building of a <a href="/wiki/Turnpike_trusts" title="Turnpike trusts">turnpike</a> road between Turiff and Banff as the existing road was in a sad state of repair. Later 19th century transport improvements included the building of two railway lines, from Macduff to Turiff in 1860 and the Banff, Portsoy and Strathisla railway in 1859 which connected to the main Aberdeen to Inverness line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cooper_1999_3_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper_1999_3-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 19th Century the Banff Fishery District (comprising the ports from Crovie to Sandend) was important to the herring trade, with production peaking in 1853 at more than sixty-thousand barrels, of which nearly thirty-four thousand were exported.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> however by 1912 production had declined to just over eight thousand barrels.</p>
<p>Currently, the languages spoken in the town and in its vicinity tend to be the <a href="/wiki/Doric_dialect_(Scotland)" title="Doric dialect (Scotland)">Doric dialect</a> of <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a>, and English.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attractions">Attractions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Banff,_Aberdeenshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Attractions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The modern-day town has a golf course (Duff House Royal), beaches, and was home to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Colleonard_Sculpture_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Colleonard Sculpture Park (page does not exist)">Colleonard Sculpture Park</a> which is now relocated in <a href="/wiki/Aviemore" title="Aviemore">Aviemore</a>.</p>
<p>COAST Festival of the Visual Arts is an annual festival of weekend-long events and attractions in both Banff and Macduff. It runs over the <a href="/wiki/Bank_holiday" title="Bank holiday">bank holiday</a> weekend at the end of May each year.</p>
<p>The townscape, which is one of the best-preserved in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, has many historic buildings including fragments of the former royal <a href="/wiki/Banff_Castle" title="Banff Castle">Banff Castle</a>, a pre-Reformation market cross, a fine tolbooth, many vernacular townhouses, and a museum donated by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Carnegie" title="Andrew Carnegie">Andrew Carnegie</a>. Close by is <a href="/wiki/Duff_House" title="Duff House">Duff House</a>, designed by <a href="/wiki/William_Adam_(architect)" title="William Adam (architect)">William Adam</a> in 1730, and one of Scotland's finest classical houses. It is open to the public as an out-station of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Gallery_of_Scotland" title="National Gallery of Scotland">National Gallery of Scotland</a>. Also open to the public are the Wrack Woods, due south of Duff House. The woods contain an old ice house, a mausoleum, and a walk to the secluded Bridge of Alvah, a single-arch bridge spanning the river Deveron. The Deveron is known for its salmon and trout fishing.</p>
<p>Many of the nearby villages also contribute to tourism in the area; in particular <a href="/wiki/Gardenstown" title="Gardenstown">Gardenstown</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pennan" title="Pennan">Pennan</a>. Banff's Tourist Information Centre opens during the summer and can be found by St Mary's car park adjacent ot St Mary's Parish Church on Banff's High Street. Their audio tours provide an insight into the town, its history and architecture.</p>
<p>Though no longer a commercial port, the harbour has been subject to redevelopment during the latter half of 2006 and now has a marina which serves leisure traffic and small fishing boats. The newly constructed marina was only accessible +3hrs mlw due to heavy and rapid siltation. By 2012 the silting problem had been resolved and the entrance is kept dredged to Chart Datum which makes it accessible over longer periods of the tide, especially to boats of a metre or less draft.</p>
<p>The Canadian town of <a href="/wiki/Banff,_Alberta" title="Banff, Alberta">Banff, Alberta</a> with its <a href="/wiki/Banff_National_Park" title="Banff National Park">National Park</a> are named after Banff.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Railways">Railways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Banff,_Aberdeenshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Railways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Banff was served by the <a href="/wiki/Banff,_Portsoy_and_Strathisla_Railway" title="Banff, Portsoy and Strathisla Railway">Banff, Portsoy and Strathisla Railway</a> (BPSR) from 1857 (to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Banff_Harbour_railway_station&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Banff Harbour railway station (page does not exist)">Banff Harbour station</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Banff,_Macduff_and_Turriff_Junction_Railway" title="Banff, Macduff and Turriff Junction Railway">Banff, Macduff and Turriff Junction Railway</a> belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Great_North_of_Scotland_Railway" title="Great North of Scotland Railway">Great North of Scotland Railway</a> (GNSR) from 1860. The latter went to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Banff_%26_Macduff_railway_station&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Banff &amp; Macduff railway station (page does not exist)">Banff &amp; Macduff station</a>, almost a mile from Banff. The GNSR later took over operation and then ownership of the older BPSR line.</p>
<p>In 1872 the line to Banff &amp; Macduff station benefited from replacement stations closer to the town centre of Macduff; <a href="/wiki/Banff_Bridge_railway_station" title="Banff Bridge railway station">Banff Bridge</a> opened near the bridge between Banff and Macduff, on the Macduff side of the river, with its line then continuing into <a href="/wiki/Macduff_railway_station" title="Macduff railway station">Macduff railway station</a>. The original Banff &amp; Macduff station closed on 1 July 1872.</p>

<p>All the lines suffered from mid-20th century railway cuts, with Banff Bridge station closing by the end of 1951, and Banff Harbour (known simply as Banff from 1928) closing on 6 July 1964. The nearest open stations are Huntly and Keith, both around 20 miles away.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Banff,_Aberdeenshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Banff and surrounding areas have a local football team, <a href="/wiki/Deveronvale_F.C." title="Deveronvale F.C.">Deveronvale F.C.</a>, and a rugby team, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Banff_RFC&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Banff RFC (page does not exist)">Banff RFC</a>. <a href="/wiki/Duff_House_Royal_Golf_Club" title="Duff House Royal Golf Club">Duff House Royal Golf Club</a> course is bordered by the River Deveron and Duff House.Some interesting holes which can be difficult depending on the wind.</p>
