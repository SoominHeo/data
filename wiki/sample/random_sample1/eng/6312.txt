<p>The <b>Colubridae</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>coluber</i>, snake) are a <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> of <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakes</a>. With 304 <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genera</a> and 1,938 <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> they are the largest snake family, and include about two-thirds of all known living snake species. The earliest species of the family date back to the <a href="/wiki/Oligocene" title="Oligocene">Oligocene</a> epoch. Colubrid species are found on every continent except <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoR_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colubridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While most colubrids are not venomous (or have venom that is not known to be harmful to humans) and are mostly harmless, a few groups, such as genus <i><a href="/wiki/Boiga" title="Boiga">Boiga</a></i>, can produce medically significant bites, while the <a href="/wiki/Boomslang" title="Boomslang">boomslang</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Twig_snake" title="Twig snake">twig snakes</a>, and the Asian genus <i><a href="/wiki/Rhabdophis" title="Rhabdophis">Rhabdophis</a></i> have caused human fatalities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoR_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-EoR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruna_Azara_1995_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bruna_Azara_1995-2">[2]</a></sup> Colubridae also include the few species of actually <i>poisonous</i> snakes, notably <i><a href="/wiki/Rhabdophis_tigrinus" title="Rhabdophis tigrinus">Rhabdophis tigrinus</a></i>, the Japanese grass snake or <i>yamakagashi</i>, the difference between poisonous and venomous being that venom requires direct administration (i.e. intravenous injection) and poison can be administered indirectly (i.e. skin contact or ingestion).</p>
<p>Some colubrids are described as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snake_dentition" title="Snake dentition">opisthoglyphous</a>, meaning they have elongated, grooved teeth located in the back of the upper jaw. The opisthoglyphous dentition appears at least twice in the history of snakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruna_Azara_1995_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bruna_Azara_1995-2">[2]</a></sup> These are unlike those of <a href="/wiki/Viperidae" title="Viperidae">vipers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elapids" title="Elapids">elapids</a>, which are located in the front.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoR_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-EoR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruna_Azara_1995_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bruna_Azara_1995-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colubridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Colubridae as traditionally defined are not a <a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">natural group</a>, as many are more closely related to other groups, such as <a href="/wiki/Elapidae" title="Elapidae">elapids</a>, than to each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This family has classically been a "<a href="/wiki/Wastebasket_taxon" title="Wastebasket taxon">garbage bin taxon</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WeinsteinWarrell2011_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-WeinsteinWarrell2011-4">[4]</a></sup> for snakes that do not fit elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Additional research will be necessary to sort out the relations within this group.</p>
