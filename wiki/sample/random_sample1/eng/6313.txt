<p>The <b>sunbittern</b> (<i>Eurypyga helias</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Bittern" title="Bittern">bittern</a>-like bird of tropical regions of the Americas, and the <a href="/wiki/Monotypic_taxon" title="Monotypic taxon">sole member</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <b>Eurypygidae</b> (sometimes spelled <b>Eurypigidae</b>) and <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><b>Eurypyga</b></i>. It ranges from <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a> to southern <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, showing three extant <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>. The sunbittern shows both morphological and molecular similarities with the <a href="/wiki/Kagu" title="Kagu">kagu</a> (<i>Rhynochetos jubatus</i>) of <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a>, indicating an <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">gondwanic</a> origin, being both placed in the <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> <a href="/wiki/Eurypygiformes" title="Eurypygiformes">Eurypygiformes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW3_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-HBW3-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunbittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The sunbittern is usually placed in the Gruiformes, but this was always considered preliminary. Altogether, the bird is most similar to another bird that was provisionally placed in the Gruiformes, the <a href="/wiki/Kagu" title="Kagu">kagu</a> (<i>Rhynochetos jubatus</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Molecular studies seem to confirm that the kagu and sunbittern are each other's closest living relatives and have a similar wing display.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> They are probably not Gruiformes (though the proposed Metaves are just as weakly supported).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Altogether, the two species seem to form a minor <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwanan</a> lineage which could also include the extinct <a href="/wiki/Adzebill" title="Adzebill">adzebills</a> and/or the <a href="/wiki/Mesite" title="Mesite">mesites</a>, and is of unclear relation to the Gruiformes proper. Notably, the kagu and mesites also have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Powder_down" title="Powder down">powder down</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunbittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There were formerly two species (<i>E. helias</i> and <i>E. major</i>), but now <i>E. helias</i> is divided into three subspecies differentiated by plumage characters and size. The three subspecies are apparently <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allopatry" title="Allopatry">allopatric</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW3_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-HBW3-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>E. h. helias</i>. East of the Andes in lowland tropical South America, from the <a href="/wiki/Orinoco" title="Orinoco">Orinoco</a> basin, through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazonia" title="Amazonia">Amazonia</a>.</li>
<li><i>E. h. major</i>. Found at various altitudes ranging from southern <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>.</li>
<li><i>E. h. meridionalis</i>. Found in southern central <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, in the lower subtropical zone (800–1830m).</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunbittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The bird has a generally subdued coloration, with fine linear patterns of black, grey and brown. Its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Remiges" title="Remiges">remiges</a> however have vividly colored middle webs, which with wings fully spread show bright eyespots in red, yellow, and black. These are shown to other sunbitterns in courtship and threat displays, or used to startle potential predators. Male and female adult sunbitterns can be differentiated by small differences in the feather patterns of the throat and head. Like some other birds, the sunbittern has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Powder_down" title="Powder down">powder down</a>.</p>
<p>The sunbittern has a long and pointed bill, a short <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hallux" title="Hallux">hallux</a> as in shorebirds and rails, and orange-yellow feet in the lowland individuals in the east of the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a>, while those of the <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> species (<i>E. h. major</i>) are much redder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW3_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-HBW3-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>The sunbittern's <a href="/wiki/Range_(biology)" title="Range (biology)">range</a> extends from <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a> to <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, in the humid Neotropical forests, generally with and open understorey and near rivers and streams. The species may also be present in southern <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. It has been traditionally reported from the Atlantic slope of <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a>, but no specimens are known and there have been no recent records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunbittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The sunbittern is a non-migrant bird that is normally found foraging on the ground and scratching for insects. They are cryptic birds that display their large wings, that exhibits a pattern that resemble eyes, when they feel threatened. Sunbitterns start nesting in the early wet season and before it starts they make flight displays 10–15 m high in the forest canopy. They build open nests in trees, and lay two eggs with blotched markings. The young are <a href="/wiki/Precocial" title="Precocial">precocial</a>, but remain in the nest for several weeks after hatching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW3_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-HBW3-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoB_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoB-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
