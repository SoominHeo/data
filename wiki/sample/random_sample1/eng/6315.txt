<p><b>Martin Christopher Kennedy</b> (17 June 1962 – 1 November 2016), known as <b>Bap Kennedy</b>, was a <a href="/wiki/Singer-songwriter" title="Singer-songwriter">singer-songwriter</a> from <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a>, Northern Ireland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artistdirect_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-artistdirect-1">[1]</a></sup> He was noted for his collaborations with <a href="/wiki/Steve_Earle" title="Steve Earle">Steve Earle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Van_Morrison" title="Van Morrison">Van Morrison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shane_MacGowan" title="Shane MacGowan">Shane MacGowan</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artistdirect_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-artistdirect-1">[1]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Mark_Knopfler" title="Mark Knopfler">Mark Knopfler</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mark_Knopfler_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mark_Knopfler-2">[2]</a></sup> as well as for writing the song "Moonlight Kiss" which was on the soundtrack for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Serendipity_(film)" title="Serendipity (film)">Serendipity</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsletter_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-newsletter-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kennedy was in the rock band <a href="/wiki/Energy_Orchard" title="Energy Orchard">Energy Orchard</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> for many years and also recorded a number of well-received solo albums including <i><a href="/wiki/Domestic_Blues" title="Domestic Blues">Domestic Blues</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Picture_(Bap_Kennedy_album)" title="The Big Picture (Bap Kennedy album)">The Big Picture</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sailor%27s_Revenge" title="The Sailor's Revenge">The Sailor's Revenge</a></i> and <i>Let's Start Again</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> During his solo career, Kennedy performed, written and recorded songs with artists such as <a href="/wiki/Steve_Earle" title="Steve Earle">Steve Earle</a> (on <i>Domestic Blues</i>), <a href="/wiki/Van_Morrison" title="Van Morrison">Van Morrison</a> (on <i>The Big Picture</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Mark_Knopfler" title="Mark Knopfler">Mark Knopfler</a> (on <i>The Sailor's Revenge</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artistdirect_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-artistdirect-1">[1]</a></sup> Following the releases of <i>The Big Picture</i> and <i>The Sailor's Revenge</i>, he toured the USA and Europe with <a href="/wiki/Mark_Knopfler" title="Mark Knopfler">Mark Knopfler</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dire_Straits" title="Dire Straits">Dire Straits</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> who also produced Kennedy's 2012 album <i>The Sailor's Revenge</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artistdirect_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-artistdirect-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_career">Musical career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bap_Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Musical career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_musical_career_and_10_Past_7_.281978.E2.80.9387.29">Early musical career and 10 Past 7 (1978–87)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bap_Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early musical career and 10 Past 7 (1978–87)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kennedy's first band was the punk band Sellout formed in 1978 at age 16. The band was formed to support rock group Uncle Waldo, playing in a local <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a> club.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdbaby_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-cdbaby-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1980 after having played in several other local bands, Kennedy formed the band 10 past 7,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-triste_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-triste-6">[6]</a></sup> with whom he received his first national recognition when they appeared on <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a> music show <a href="/wiki/The_Tube_(TV_series)" title="The Tube (TV series)">The Tube</a> in 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irish_rock_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Irish_rock-7">[7]</a></sup> The band was managed by Mark Kelly, <small><a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">MBE</a></small>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> 10 Past 7 were signed to the <a href="/wiki/Good_Vibrations_(record_label)" title="Good Vibrations (record label)">Good Vibrations</a> record label by <a href="/wiki/Terri_Hooley" title="Terri Hooley">Terri Hooley</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belfast_Telegraph_Feb_2014_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Belfast_Telegraph_Feb_2014-8">[8]</a></sup> - who had discovered <a href="/wiki/The_Undertones" title="The Undertones">The Undertones</a> several years earlier<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belfast_Telegraph_July_2011_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Belfast_Telegraph_July_2011-9">[9]</a></sup> - and they released two singles <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irish_rock_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Irish_rock-7">[7]</a></sup> before disbanding when Bap left Belfast for <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> to pursue a recording contract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_Orchard_.281987.E2.80.9396.29">Energy Orchard (1987–96)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bap_Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Energy Orchard (1987–96)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Energy_Orchard" title="Energy Orchard">Energy Orchard</a></div>
<p>In 1987, after moving to London, Kennedy formed the band <a href="/wiki/Energy_Orchard" title="Energy Orchard">Energy Orchard</a>, for whom he was the rhythm guitarist, lead singer and primary songwriter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> Although the band was based in London, all of its members had originally come from Belfast like Kennedy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/MCA_Records" title="MCA Records">MCA Records</a> gave the band a major record deal in 1988 and they would go on to release two albums with the label, before signing with <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_Records" title="Transatlantic Records">Transatlantic</a> for a further two studio albums as well as the live album <i>Orchardville</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdbaby_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-cdbaby-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Energy Orchard went on to establish themselves as regulars on the live scene in London, including a performance at the first London <a href="/wiki/Fleadh_Cheoil" title="Fleadh Cheoil">Fleadh</a> in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The band also toured extensively throughout the UK, Europe and America, including several support slots with <a href="/wiki/Van_Morrison" title="Van Morrison">Van Morrison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Earle" title="Steve Earle">Steve Earle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Barnes" title="Jimmy Barnes">Jimmy Barnes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> Energy Orchard disbanded in 1996 with a farewell London gig on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Patrick%E2%80%99s_Day" title="St Patrick’s Day">St Patrick’s Day</a> which would be recorded for the live album <i>Orchardville</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solo_career">Solo career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bap_Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Solo career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Domestic_Blues_and_Steve_Earle_.281998.E2.80.932000.29"><i>Domestic Blues</i> and Steve Earle (1998–2000)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bap_Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Domestic Blues and Steve Earle (1998–2000)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Domestic_Blues" title="Domestic Blues">Domestic Blues</a></div>
<p>After Energy Orchard split up, Kennedy received a phone call from <a href="/wiki/Steve_Earle" title="Steve Earle">Steve Earle</a>, who offered to produce Kennedy's first solo record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loose_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Loose-13">[13]</a></sup> Shortly afterwards, Kennedy joined him in <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville, Tennessee</a>, and recorded his first solo album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artistdirect_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-artistdirect-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Domestic_Blues" title="Domestic Blues">Domestic Blues</a></i>, which was released in 1998, was produced by the “Twang Trust” of Earle and <a href="/wiki/Ray_Kennedy_(country_singer)" title="Ray Kennedy (country singer)">Ray Kennedy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_Domestic_Blues_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic_Domestic_Blues-14">[14]</a></sup> and Earle played guitar and sang backing vocals on the record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> The album also features <a href="/wiki/Slide_guitar" title="Slide guitar">slide guitar</a> player <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Douglas" title="Jerry Douglas">Jerry Douglas</a>, guitarist <a href="/wiki/Peter_Rowan" title="Peter Rowan">Peter Rowan</a> and singer <a href="/wiki/Nanci_Griffith" title="Nanci Griffith">Nanci Griffith</a> amongst its session musicians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loose_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Loose-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>All the songs on <i>Domestic Blues</i> were written by Kennedy except <i>Angel is the Devil</i> which was written by Earle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> Earle was later to describe Kennedy as “the best songwriter I ever saw”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> The album was released on Earle’s record label <a href="/wiki/E-Squared_Records" title="E-Squared Records">E-Squared</a> and received generally favorable reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artistdirect_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-artistdirect-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_Domestic_Blues_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic_Domestic_Blues-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It was also a commercial success, charting at number eight on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several songs from the album were used for movie soundtracks, including <i>Vampire</i> appearing in the soundtrack for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Oscar</a> nominated<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMDB_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMDB-16">[16]</a></sup> film <i><a href="/wiki/You_Can_Count_On_Me" title="You Can Count On Me">You Can Count On Me</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artistdirect_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-artistdirect-1">[1]</a></sup> which also won the Grand Jury Prize at the <a href="/wiki/Sundance_Film_Festival" title="Sundance Film Festival">Sundance Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMDB_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-IMDB-16">[16]</a></sup> Three other songs from <i>Domestic Blues</i> were also used in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Southie_(film)" title="Southie (film)">Southie</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, the album’s title track was used as the theme song for <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">BBC Radio 4</a> comedy <i>Happy Tuesdays</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> After the release of <i>Domestic Blues</i>, Kennedy toured with Steve Earle<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-12"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> alongside other artists on the E-Squared label, including <a href="/wiki/Cheri_Knight" title="Cheri Knight">Cheri Knight</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_V-Roys" title="The V-Roys">The V-Roys</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>While he was in Nashville, Kennedy developed a keen interest in the music of <a href="/wiki/Hank_Williams" title="Hank Williams">Hank Williams</a>, whom Kennedy described as his “favourite song writer”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsletter_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-newsletter-3">[3]</a></sup> and a major influence. After he had finished recording <i>Domestic Blues</i>, Kennedy recorded an album of Hank Williams covers entitled <i>Hillbilly Shakespeare</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artistdirect_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-artistdirect-1">[1]</a></sup> which featured <a href="/wiki/Pedal_steel_guitar" title="Pedal steel guitar">pedal steel guitar</a> playing from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B.J._Cole" title="B.J. Cole">B.J. Cole</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The album had a very limited release as the first record on Kennedy’s own label, Lonely Street Discs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artistdirect_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-artistdirect-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-13"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lonely_Street.2C_Serendipity_and_Moonlight_Kiss_.282000.E2.80.9305.29"><i>Lonely Street</i>, <i>Serendipity</i> and <i>Moonlight Kiss</i> (2000–05)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bap_Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lonely Street, Serendipity and Moonlight Kiss (2000–05)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After returning to the UK, Kennedy briefly managed an <a href="/wiki/Elvis_impersonator" title="Elvis impersonator">Elvis impersonator</a> named The King, and was the producer for The King’s album Gravelands – a collection of songs by other deceased artists performed in the style of <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdbaby_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-cdbaby-5">[5]</a></sup> The record became a firm favourite of <a href="/wiki/John_Peel" title="John Peel">John Peel</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Red_Hot_Chili_Peppers" title="Red Hot Chili Peppers">Red Hot Chili Peppers</a>. Kennedy's continued interest in the music of <a href="/wiki/Hank_Williams" title="Hank Williams">Hank Williams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a> led to him writing Lonely Street, a <a href="/wiki/Concept_album" title="Concept album">concept album</a> based on the lives of his two musical heroes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artistdirect_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-artistdirect-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The album, which was released in 2000 on Kennedy’s Lonely Street Discs label,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdbaby_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-cdbaby-5">[5]</a></sup> features bass guitar playing by <a href="/wiki/Herbie_Flowers" title="Herbie Flowers">Herbie Flowers</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-14"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> who is noted for playing the famous bass line on <a href="/wiki/Lou_Reed" title="Lou Reed">Lou Reed</a>’s hit song <a href="/wiki/Walk_on_the_Wild_Side_(Lou_Reed_song)" title="Walk on the Wild Side (Lou Reed song)">Walk on the Wild Side</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> It received generally positive reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Kennedy's ballad "Moonlight Kiss" from the album was later used in the soundtrack for hit Hollywood movie <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serendipity_(movie)" title="Serendipity (movie)">Serendipity</a> (starring <a href="/wiki/John_Cusack" title="John Cusack">John Cusack</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kate_Beckinsale" title="Kate Beckinsale">Kate Beckinsale</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-15"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the release of Lonely Street, Kennedy spent several years touring,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boogaloo_songkick_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boogaloo_songkick-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Putney_songkick_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Putney_songkick-24">[24]</a></sup> including performances with <a href="/wiki/Shane_MacGowan" title="Shane MacGowan">Shane MacGowan</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Pogues" title="The Pogues">The Pogues</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pete_Doherty" title="Pete Doherty">Pete Doherty</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Libertines" title="The Libertines">The Libertines</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boogaloo_songkick_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Boogaloo_songkick-23">[23]</a></sup> and at the respected <a href="/wiki/The_Half_Moon,_Putney" title="The Half Moon, Putney">Half Moon, Putney</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Putney_songkick_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Putney_songkick-24">[24]</a></sup> which also regularly held the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rolling_Stones" title="Rolling Stones">Rolling Stones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Big_Picture.2C_Van_Morrison_and_Shane_MacGowan_.282005.E2.80.9309.29"><i>The Big Picture</i>, Van Morrison and Shane MacGowan (2005–09)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bap_Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Big Picture, Van Morrison and Shane MacGowan (2005–09)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Big_Picture_(Bap_Kennedy_album)" title="The Big Picture (Bap Kennedy album)">The Big Picture (Bap Kennedy album)</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_Pogues" title="The Pogues">The Pogues</a>’ lead singer <a href="/wiki/Shane_MacGowan" title="Shane MacGowan">Shane MacGowan</a> provided guest vocals on Kennedy’s next album, <a href="/wiki/The_Big_Picture_(Bap_Kennedy_album)" title="The Big Picture (Bap Kennedy album)">The Big Picture</a>, with MacGowan providing lead vocals on the last verse of Kennedy’s song On the Mighty Ocean Alcohol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fame_magazine_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-fame_magazine-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The late <a href="/wiki/Carolyn_Cassady" title="Carolyn Cassady">Carolyn Cassady</a>, another guest vocalist on the album,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loose_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Loose-13">[13]</a></sup> was the basis of the character Camille in <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kerouac" title="Jack Kerouac">Jack Kerouac</a>'s book <a href="/wiki/On_the_Road" title="On the Road">On the Road</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> At the end of Kennedy's song Moriarty's Blues, Cassady gave a reading from her own book <a href="/wiki/Off_the_Road" title="Off the Road">Off the Road</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-16"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> "Moriarty's Blues" is a ballad about the <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Road" title="On the Road">On the Road</a></i> character Dean Moriarty,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-17"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> which was based on Cassady's husband <a href="/wiki/Neal_Cassady" title="Neal Cassady">Neal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Big Picture was also a return to working with <a href="/wiki/Van_Morrison" title="Van Morrison">Van Morrison</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artistdirect_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-artistdirect-1">[1]</a></sup> who had supported Kennedy since his <a href="/wiki/Energy_Orchard" title="Energy Orchard">Energy Orchard</a> days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-18"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> The album was recorded at Morrison’s studio and featured the song Milky Way which was co-written by Morrison and Kennedy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fame_magazine_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-fame_magazine-26">[26]</a></sup> The musicians appearing on the album include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedal_steel" title="Pedal steel">pedal steel</a> guitarist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B.J._Cole" title="B.J. Cole">B.J. Cole</a> and guitarist James Walbourne,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> who is a member of rock group <a href="/wiki/The_Pretenders" title="The Pretenders">The Pretenders</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-19"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> The album was released in 2005 to generally positive reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loose_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Loose-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the release of the album, Kennedy was approached by former <a href="/wiki/Dire_Straits" title="Dire Straits">Dire Straits</a> frontman <a href="/wiki/Mark_Knopfler" title="Mark Knopfler">Mark Knopfler</a>, who asked Kennedy to play as Knopfler’s support artist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artistdirect_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-artistdirect-1">[1]</a></sup> This led to Kennedy working as the support artist and special guest for Knopfler’s tours of the USA and Europe, including a five night stint at London’s <a href="/wiki/Royal_Albert_Hall" title="Royal Albert Hall">Royal Albert Hall</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fame_magazine_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-fame_magazine-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Howl_On_and_Glastonbury.282009.E2.80.932012.29"><i>Howl On</i> and Glastonbury(2009–2012)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bap_Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Howl On and Glastonbury(2009–2012)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Howl_On" title="Howl On">Howl On</a></div>
<p>Kennedy’s 2009 album <i><a href="/wiki/Howl_On" title="Howl On">Howl On</a></i> was, like <i>Lonely Street</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Concept_album" title="Concept album">concept album</a> based upon a real life subject which Kennedy was interested in. <i>Howl On</i> was a look back at the <a href="/wiki/Space_Race" title="Space Race">Space Race</a> of the late 1960s, with Kennedy examining the lives and backgrounds of the astronauts and scientists who worked on the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fame_magazine_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-fame_magazine-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The only cover on the album,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howl_On_notes_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Howl_On_notes-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hey_Joe" title="Hey Joe">Hey Joe</a> was also chosen as a nod to this time period, as it had been the last song performed by <a href="/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix" title="Jimi Hendrix">Jimi Hendrix</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woodstock_Festival" title="Woodstock Festival">Woodstock Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The song also features guest guitarist <a href="/wiki/Henry_McCullough" title="Henry McCullough">Henry McCullough</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fame_magazine_26-4"><a href="#cite_note-fame_magazine-26">[26]</a></sup> who was, as a member of <a href="/wiki/Joe_Cocker" title="Joe Cocker">Joe Cocker</a>’s band, the only Irishman to play at Woodstock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCullough_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-McCullough-33">[33]</a></sup> McCullough also worked with <a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wings_(band)" title="Wings (band)">Wings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCullough_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-McCullough-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Howl on received generally positive reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_Howl_On_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-about_Howl_On-35">[35]</a></sup> The album was followed by a successful tour of the UK, Europe and America, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_by_South_West" title="South by South West">South by South West</a> festival<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-20"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> and a performance at the 2009 <a href="/wiki/Glastonbury_Festival" title="Glastonbury Festival">Glastonbury Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fame_magazine_26-5"><a href="#cite_note-fame_magazine-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Sailor.27s_Revenge_and_Mark_Knopfler_.282012.E2.80.9314.29"><i>The Sailor's Revenge</i> and Mark Knopfler (2012–14)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bap_Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Sailor's Revenge and Mark Knopfler (2012–14)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Sailor%27s_Revenge" title="The Sailor's Revenge">The Sailor's Revenge</a></div>
<p>Kennedy recorded a new album in early 2011, entitled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sailor%27s_Revenge" title="The Sailor's Revenge">The Sailor's Revenge</a></i>, produced by former <a href="/wiki/Dire_Straits" title="Dire Straits">Dire Straits</a> frontman <a href="/wiki/Mark_Knopfler" title="Mark Knopfler">Mark Knopfler</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artistdirect_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-artistdirect-1">[1]</a></sup> at Knopfler's <a href="/wiki/British_Grove_Studios" title="British Grove Studios">British Grove Studios</a> in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guy_Fletcher_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guy_Fletcher-36">[36]</a></sup> The album was a close collaboration between Kennedy and Knopfler, with the two artists having worked together on the project for 2 years. As well as producing the record, Knopfler had been heavily involved in choosing which songs to record for the album,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-21"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> and also played guitar on multiple songs and sang backing vocals for the song "Celtic Sea".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TSR_notes_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-TSR_notes-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>The album features several members of Knopfler's touring band, such as <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Worf" title="Glenn Worf">Glenn Worf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guy_Fletcher" title="Guy Fletcher">Guy Fletcher</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_McCusker" title="John McCusker">John McCusker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_McGoldrick" title="Michael McGoldrick">Michael McGoldrick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Bennett_(guitarist)" title="Richard Bennett (guitarist)">Richard Bennett</a>. Also playing on the album were session musician <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Douglas" title="Jerry Douglas">Jerry Douglas</a>, who had previously worked with Kennedy on <i><a href="/wiki/Domestic_Blues" title="Domestic Blues">Domestic Blues</a></i>, and James Walbourne of <a href="/wiki/The_Pretenders" title="The Pretenders">The Pretenders</a>, who had played on Kennedy's 2005 album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Picture_(Bap_Kennedy_album)" title="The Big Picture (Bap Kennedy album)">The Big Picture</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-22"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Sailor’s Revenge</i> was released in 2012 on <a href="/wiki/Proper_Records" title="Proper Records">Proper Records</a> and received widespread critical acclaim internationally<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> including No 1 - Best of 2012 in influential American magazine <i><a href="/wiki/No_Depression_(magazine)" title="No Depression (magazine)">No Depression</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> where it was described as "truly a masterpiece from a man who is finally comfortable in his place in the world – The Celtic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_On_The_Tracks" title="Blood On The Tracks">Blood On The Tracks</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> It was also listed by <i><a href="/wiki/Mojo_(magazine)" title="Mojo (magazine)">Mojo</a></i> in the Top 10 <a href="/wiki/Americana_(music)" title="Americana (music)">Americana</a> albums of 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>The album was supported by tours of the USA, UK and Europe with Mark Knopfler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Let.27s_Start_Again_.282014.E2.80.932016.29"><i>Let's Start Again</i> (2014–2016)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bap_Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Let's Start Again (2014–2016)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the Celtic melancholy of <i>The Sailor’s Revenge</i>, Kennedy moved to an upbeat Americana style for the follow up <i>Let’s Start Again</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belfast_Telegraph_Feb_2014_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Belfast_Telegraph_Feb_2014-8">[8]</a></sup> The album, which Kennedy recorded in his native <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artistdirect_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-artistdirect-1">[1]</a></sup> was produced by Mudd Wallace,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irish_World_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Irish_World-45">[45]</a></sup> who produced several albums for Northern Irish <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Therapy%3F" title="Therapy?">Therapy?</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mudd_Wallace_discogs_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mudd_Wallace_discogs-46">[46]</a></sup> Wallace was a long-time friend of Kennedy and had worked as producer for Kennedy’s early band 10 Past 7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belfast_Telegraph_Feb_2014_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Belfast_Telegraph_Feb_2014-8">[8]</a></sup> Let's Start Again uses local Northern Irish session musicians, and features several musicians from Kennedy’s live band, including his wife Brenda Kennedy, who plays bass guitar and sings backing vocals on the album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irish_World_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Irish_World-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>The album had a full international release on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CD" title="CD">CD</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vinyl_record" title="Vinyl record">vinyl</a> formats by <a href="/wiki/Proper_Records" title="Proper Records">Proper Records</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> and has received widespread critical acclaim including a 5 star review from <a href="/wiki/No_Depression_(magazine)" title="No Depression (magazine)">No Depression</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bap_Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Martin Christopher Kennedy was born on 17 June 1962<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artistdirect_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-artistdirect-1">[1]</a></sup> to Lily and Jim Kennedy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>He is the brother of <a href="/wiki/Brian_Kennedy_(singer)" title="Brian Kennedy (singer)">Brian Kennedy</a>. The family grew up on the <a href="/wiki/Falls_Road,_Belfast" title="Falls Road, Belfast">Falls Road, Belfast</a>, in the 1970s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-23"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> where he got his nickname of Bap, as a childhood reference to the rolls (or "baps") of bread sold by local firm Kennedy's Bakery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irish_World_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-Irish_World-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kennedy grew up in West Belfast at the height of <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">the Troubles</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irish_World_45-3"><a href="#cite_note-Irish_World-45">[45]</a></sup> and his close friend and 10 Past 7 manager Mark Kelly,<small><a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">MBE</a></small> lost both legs in a no-warning bomb in Belfast in 1976.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kennedy left <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> in 1985<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> and worked as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labourer" title="Labourer">labourer</a> on building sites in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> prior to becoming a professional musician.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Culture_Northern_Ireland_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Culture_Northern_Ireland-53">[53]</a></sup> While living in England, Kennedy became a qualified diamond <a href="/wiki/Gemology" title="Gemology">gemmologist</a>, accredited by the <a href="/wiki/Gemmological_Association_of_Great_Britain" title="Gemmological Association of Great Britain">Gemmological Association of Great Britain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Culture_Northern_Ireland_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-Culture_Northern_Ireland-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, Kennedy moved back to his native Northern Ireland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-24"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> Shortly after returning, Kennedy hosted a songwriting workshop, where he met author and former lawyer Brenda Boyd, who was participating in the workshop. The two soon became a couple, and they were married in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fame_magazine_26-6"><a href="#cite_note-fame_magazine-26">[26]</a></sup> Brenda, who has written several advice books for parents of children with <a href="/wiki/Autism" title="Autism">autism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asperger_syndrome" title="Asperger syndrome">Asperger syndrome</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> has since sung backing vocals on three of Kennedy’s albums – <i>Howl On</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howl_On_notes_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Howl_On_notes-31">[31]</a></sup> <i>The Sailor’s Revenge</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TSR_notes_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-TSR_notes-37">[37]</a></sup> and <i>Let’s Start Again</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-25"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> – and is also a regular member of Kennedy’s live band, as his bass guitarist and backing singer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artistdirect_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-artistdirect-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Through his marriage to Brenda, Kennedy is also stepfather to Christine White and Kenneth Hall, who is also an author, having written about his experience as a child with <a href="/wiki/Asperger_syndrome" title="Asperger syndrome">Asperger syndrome</a> in Asperger Syndrome, the Universe and Everything.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-26"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup> As a result of his family’s links with <a href="/wiki/Autism" title="Autism">autism</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asperger%27s_syndrome" title="Asperger's syndrome">Asperger's syndrome</a>, Kennedy became co-patron (with his wife) of the charity Autism NI in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belfast_Telegraph_Feb_2014_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Belfast_Telegraph_Feb_2014-8">[8]</a></sup> Outside of music, Kennedy had a keen interest in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_space_program" title="Apollo space program">Apollo space program</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_Howl_On_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-about_Howl_On-35">[35]</a></sup> which would be the inspiration for Kennedy’s 2009 album <i>Howl On</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_4-27"><a href="#cite_note-biography-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cancer_battle">Cancer battle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bap_Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cancer battle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In May 2016, Kennedy started a blog on his personal website, detailing his progress after a sudden cancer diagnosis. He died on 1 November 2016 in Belfast in hospice care from <a href="/wiki/Pancreatic_cancer" title="Pancreatic cancer">pancreatic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bowel_cancer" title="Bowel cancer">bowel cancer</a>. He had been receiving palliative care at Belfast's Marie Curie clinic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
