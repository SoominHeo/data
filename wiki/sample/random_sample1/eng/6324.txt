<p>The <b>Burma Railway</b>, also known as the <b>Death Railway</b>, the <b>Burma–Siam Railway</b>, the <b>Thailand–Burma Railway</b> and similar names, was a 415-kilometre (258 mi) railway between <a href="/wiki/Ban_Pong_District" title="Ban Pong District">Ban Pong</a>, Thailand, and <a href="/wiki/Thanbyuzayat" title="Thanbyuzayat">Thanbyuzayat</a>, Burma, built by the <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">Empire of Japan</a> in 1943 to support its forces in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burma_campaign" title="Burma campaign">Burma campaign</a> of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. This railway completed the rail link between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bangkok,_Thailand" title="Bangkok, Thailand">Bangkok, Thailand</a> and Rangoon, Burma (now <a href="/wiki/Yangon" title="Yangon">Yangon</a>). The line was closed in 1947, but the section between <a href="/wiki/Nong_Pladuk_Junction_Railway_Station" title="Nong Pladuk Junction Railway Station">Nong Pla Duk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nam_Tok_Railway_Station" title="Nam Tok Railway Station">Nam Tok</a> was reopened ten years later in 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beattie_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beattie-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forced_labour" title="Forced labour">Forced labour</a> was used in its construction. More than 180,000—possibly many more—<a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asian</a> civilian labourers (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romusha" title="Romusha">Romusha</a>) and 60,000 <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prisoners_of_war" title="Prisoners of war">prisoners of war</a> (POWs) worked on the railway. Javanese, <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Malaysians" title="Tamil Malaysians">Malayan Tamils</a> of Indian origin, Burmese, Chinese, Thai and other Southeast Asians, forcibly drafted by the Imperial Japanese Army to work on the railway, died in its construction — including 100,000 Tamils alone.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (October 2016)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> 12,621 Allied POWs died during the construction. The dead POWs included 6,904 <a href="/wiki/British_people" title="British people">British</a> personnel, 2,802 <a href="/wiki/Australians" title="Australians">Australians</a>, 2,782 <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch</a>, and 133 <a href="/wiki/Americans" title="Americans">Americans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mansell.com_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-mansell.com-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the end of World War II, 111 Japanese military officials were tried for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_crimes" title="War crimes">war crimes</a> because of their brutalization of POWs during the construction of the railway, with 32 of these sentenced to death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> No compensation or reparations have been provided to Southeast Asian victims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burma_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Death_Railway.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="936" data-file-width="723" height="324" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Death_Railway.png/250px-Death_Railway.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Death_Railway.png/375px-Death_Railway.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Death_Railway.png/500px-Death_Railway.png 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Death_Railway.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of the Burma Railway</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A railway route between Burma and Thailand, crossing <a href="/wiki/Three_Pagodas_Pass" title="Three Pagodas Pass">Three Pagodas Pass</a> and following the valley of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kwhae_Noi" title="Kwhae Noi">Kwhae Noi</a> river in Thailand had been surveyed by the British government of Burma as early as 1885, but the proposed course of the line – through hilly jungle terrain divided by many rivers – was considered too difficult to undertake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In early 1942, Japanese forces invaded Burma and seized control of the colony from the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. To supply their forces in Burma, the Japanese depended upon the sea, bringing supplies and troops to Burma around the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malay_peninsula" title="Malay peninsula">Malay peninsula</a> and through the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Malacca" title="Strait of Malacca">Strait of Malacca</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Andaman_Sea" title="Andaman Sea">Andaman Sea</a>. This route was vulnerable to attack by Allied submarines, especially after the Japanese defeat at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Midway" title="Battle of Midway">Battle of Midway</a> in June 1942. To avoid a hazardous 2,000 miles (3,200 km) sea journey around the Malay peninsula, a railway from Bangkok, Thailand to Rangoon, Burma seemed a feasible alternative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The Japanese began the project in June 1942.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Death_Railway_in_Thanbyuzayat.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="4608" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Death_Railway_in_Thanbyuzayat.JPG/250px-Death_Railway_in_Thanbyuzayat.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Death_Railway_in_Thanbyuzayat.JPG/375px-Death_Railway_in_Thanbyuzayat.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Death_Railway_in_Thanbyuzayat.JPG/500px-Death_Railway_in_Thanbyuzayat.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Death_Railway_in_Thanbyuzayat.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Abandoned section of Burma Railway in Thanbyuzayat, Burma</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The project aimed to connect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoe_Ban_Pong" title="Amphoe Ban Pong">Ban Pong</a> in Thailand with <a href="/wiki/Thanbyuzayat" title="Thanbyuzayat">Thanbyuzayat</a> in Burma, linking up with existing railways at both places. Its route was through Three Pagodas Pass on the border of Thailand and Burma. 69 miles (111 km) of the railway were in Burma and the remaining 189 miles (304 km) were in Thailand. The movement of POWs northward from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Changi_prison" title="Changi prison">Changi prison</a> in <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> and other prison camps in Southeast Asia began in May 1942. After preliminary work of airfields and infrastructure, construction of the railway began in Burma on 15 September 1942 and in Thailand in November. The projected completion date was December 1943.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Most of the construction materials, including tracks and sleepers, were brought from dismantled branches of <a href="/wiki/British_Malaya" title="British Malaya">Malaya's</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federated_Malay_States_Railway" title="Federated Malay States Railway">Federated Malay States Railway</a> network and the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies" title="Dutch East Indies">East Indies'</a> <a href="/wiki/History_of_rail_transport_in_Indonesia" title="History of rail transport in Indonesia">various rail networks</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thailand_Burma_Railway_Bridge.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Thailand_Burma_Railway_Bridge.jpg/250px-Thailand_Burma_Railway_Bridge.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Thailand_Burma_Railway_Bridge.jpg/375px-Thailand_Burma_Railway_Bridge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Thailand_Burma_Railway_Bridge.jpg/500px-Thailand_Burma_Railway_Bridge.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Thailand_Burma_Railway_Bridge.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
One of many bridges built by romusha and POWs on the Thai Burma railway.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The railway was completed ahead of schedule. On 17 October 1943, construction gangs originating in Burma and working south met up with construction gangs originating in Thailand and working north. The two sections of the line met at kilometre 263, about 18 km (11 mi) south of the Three Pagodas Pass at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Konkuita&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Konkuita (page does not exist)">Konkuita</a> (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kaeng_Khoi_Tha&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kaeng Khoi Tha (page does not exist)">Kaeng Khoi Tha</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoe_Sangkhla_Buri" title="Amphoe Sangkhla Buri">Sangkhla Buri district</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kanchanaburi_Province" title="Kanchanaburi Province">Kanchanaburi Province</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Burma railway was an impressive accomplishment. As an American engineer said after viewing the project, “What makes this an engineering feat is the totality of it, the accumulation of factors. The total length of miles, the total number of bridges — over 600, including six to eight long-span bridges — the total number of people who were involved (one-quarter of a million), the very short time in which they managed to accomplish it, and the extreme conditions they accomplished it under. They had very little transportation to get stuff to and from the workers, they had almost no medication, they couldn’t get food let alone materials, they had no tools to work with except for basic things like spades and hammers, and they worked in extremely difficult conditions — in the jungle with its heat and humidity. All of that makes this railway an extraordinary accomplishment.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The total freight carried during the war was 500,000 tonnes and two Japanese Army divisions. Even without accounting for the deaths and deprivation of romusha, prisoners, and Japanese this is a poor return for the effort of the railway's construction and indicates that the railway was a strategic failure.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Workers">Workers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burma_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Workers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese">Japanese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burma_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Japanese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>12,000 Japanese soldiers, including 800 Koreans, were employed on the railway as engineers, guards, and supervisors of the POW and romusha labourers. Although working conditions were far better for the Japanese than the POW and romusha workers, about 1,000 (8 percent) of them died during the construction. Japanese soldiers are widely remembered as being cruel and indifferent to the fate of Allied prisoners of war and the Asian rōmusha. Many men in the railway workforce bore the brunt of pitiless or uncaring guards. Cruelty could take different forms, from extreme violence and torture to minor acts of physical punishment, humiliation and neglect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Romusha">Romusha</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burma_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Romusha">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The number of Southeast Asian workers recruited or impressed to work on the Burma railway has been estimated to have been upwards of 180,000 of whom as many as one-half may have died. In the initial stages of the construction of the railway, Burmese and Thai were employed in their respective countries, but Thai workers, in particular, were likely to abscond from the project and the number of Burmese workers recruited was insufficient. The Burmese had welcomed the invasion by Japan to end British rule and cooperated with Japan in recruiting workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In early 1943, the Japanese advertised for workers in Malaya, Singapore, and the Dutch East Indies, promising good wages, short contracts, and housing for families. When that failed to attract sufficient workers, they resorted to more coercive methods, rounding up workers and impressing them, especially in Malaya. Most of the romusha working on the railway were probably coerced, rather than being volunteers. Approximately 90,000 Burmese and 75,000 Malayans worked on the railroad. Other nationalities and ethnic groups working on the railway were Tamils, Chinese, Karen, Javanese, and Singaporean Chinese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Malaysian_Tamils.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1714" data-file-width="2322" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Malaysian_Tamils.jpg/220px-Malaysian_Tamils.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Malaysian_Tamils.jpg/330px-Malaysian_Tamils.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Malaysian_Tamils.jpg/440px-Malaysian_Tamils.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Malaysian_Tamils.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Malayan Tamils during the construction of Death railway between June 1942 to October 1943</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>But other documents shows that more than 100,000 Malayan Tamils were brought into this project and around 60,000 perished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The working conditions for the romusha were deadly. A British doctor said:</p>
<p>"The conditions in the <a href="/wiki/Coolie" title="Coolie">coolie</a> [romusha] camps down river are terrible...They are kept isolated from Japanese and British camps. They have no latrines. Special British prisoner parties...bury about 20 coolies a day.</p>
<p>"These Tamil coolies have been brought from Malaya under false pretences – ' easy work, good pay, good houses!' Some have even brought wives and children. Now they find themselves dumped in these <a href="/wiki/Charnel_house" title="Charnel house">charnel houses</a>, driven and brutally knocked about by the Jap and Korean guards, unable to buy extra food, bewildered, sick, frightened. Yet many of them have shown extraordinary kindness to sick British prisoners passing down the river, giving them sugar and helping them into the railway trucks."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prisoners_of_War">Prisoners of War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burma_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Prisoners of War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first prisoners of war, 3,000 Australians, to go to Burma departed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Changi_prison" title="Changi prison">Changi prison</a> at <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> on 14 May 1942 and journeyed by sea to near <a href="/wiki/Thanbyuzayat" title="Thanbyuzayat">Thanbyuzayat</a>, the northern terminus of the railway. They worked on airfields and other infrastructure initially before beginning construction of the railway in October 1942. The first prisoners of war to work in Thailand, 3,000 British soldiers, left Changi by train in June 1942 to <a href="/wiki/Ban_Pong,_Ratchaburi" title="Ban Pong, Ratchaburi">Ban Pong</a>, the southern terminus of the railway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> More prisoners of war were imported from Singapore and the Dutch East Indies as construction advanced. Construction camps housing at least 1,000 workers each were established every five to 10 miles (8 to 17 km) of the route. Workers were moved up and down the railway line as needed.</p>
<p>The construction camps consisted of open-sided barracks built of bamboo poles with thatched roofs. The barracks were about sixty metres (66 yd) long with sleeping platforms raised above the ground on each side of an earthen floor. Two hundred men were housed in each barracks, giving each man a two-foot wide space in which to live and sleep. Camps were usually named after the kilometer where they were located.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conditions_during_construction">Conditions during construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burma_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Conditions during construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Portrait_of_%22Dusty%22_Rhodes_by_Ashley_George_Old.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="670" data-file-width="464" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Portrait_of_%22Dusty%22_Rhodes_by_Ashley_George_Old.jpg/150px-Portrait_of_%22Dusty%22_Rhodes_by_Ashley_George_Old.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Portrait_of_%22Dusty%22_Rhodes_by_Ashley_George_Old.jpg/225px-Portrait_of_%22Dusty%22_Rhodes_by_Ashley_George_Old.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Portrait_of_%22Dusty%22_Rhodes_by_Ashley_George_Old.jpg/300px-Portrait_of_%22Dusty%22_Rhodes_by_Ashley_George_Old.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Portrait_of_%22Dusty%22_Rhodes_by_Ashley_George_Old.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Portrait of POW "Dusty" Rhodes. A three-minute sketch by <a href="/wiki/Ashley_George_Old" title="Ashley George Old">Ashley George Old</a> painted in Thailand in 1944.</div>

</div>
<p>The prisoners of war "found themselves at the bottom of a social system that was harsh, punitive, fanatical, and often deadly."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The living and working conditions on the Burma Railway were often described as "horrific", with maltreatment, sickness, and starvation. The estimated total number of civilian labourers and POWs who died during construction varies considerably, but the Australian Government figures suggest that of the 330,000 people that worked on the line (including 250,000 Asian labourers and 61,000 Allied POWs) about 90,000 of the labourers and about 16,000 Allied prisoners died.</p>
<p>Life in the POW camps was recorded at great risk to themselves by artists such as <a href="/wiki/Jack_Bridger_Chalker" title="Jack Bridger Chalker">Jack Bridger Chalker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philip_Meninsky" title="Philip Meninsky">Philip Meninsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Mennie" title="John Mennie">John Mennie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ashley_George_Old" title="Ashley George Old">Ashley George Old</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Searle" title="Ronald Searle">Ronald Searle</a>. Human hair was often used for brushes, plant juices and blood for paint, and toilet paper as the "canvas". Some of their works were used as evidence in the trials of Japanese war criminals. Many are now held by the <a href="/wiki/Australian_War_Memorial" title="Australian War Memorial">Australian War Memorial</a>, <a href="/wiki/State_Library_of_Victoria" title="State Library of Victoria">State Library of Victoria</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_War_Museum" title="Imperial War Museum">Imperial War Museum</a> in London.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:POWs_Burma_Thai_RR.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="439" data-file-width="639" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/POWs_Burma_Thai_RR.jpg/250px-POWs_Burma_Thai_RR.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/POWs_Burma_Thai_RR.jpg/375px-POWs_Burma_Thai_RR.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/POWs_Burma_Thai_RR.jpg/500px-POWs_Burma_Thai_RR.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:POWs_Burma_Thai_RR.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Starving Australian and Dutch POWs on the Burma railway.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>One of the earliest and most respected accounts is ex-POW John Coast's <i>Railroad of Death</i> first published in 1946 and republished in a new edition in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coast_2014_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coast_2014-21">[21]</a></sup> Coast's work is noted for its detail on the brutality of some Japanese and Korean guards as well as the humanity of others. It also describes the living and working conditions experienced by the POWs, together with the culture of the Thai towns and countryside that became many POWs home after leaving Singapore with the working parties sent to the railway. Coast also details the camaraderie, pastimes and humour of the POWs in the face of adversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his book, <i>Last Man Out</i>, H. Robert Charles, an American Marine survivor of the sinking of the <a href="/wiki/USS_Houston_(CA-30)" title="USS Houston (CA-30)">USS <i>Houston</i></a>, writes in depth about a Dutch doctor, Henri Hekking, a fellow POW who probably saved the lives of many who worked on the "Death Railway". In the foreword to Charles's book, James D. Hornfischer summarizes: "Dr. Henri Hekking was a tower of psychological and emotional strength, almost shamanic in his power to find and improvise medicines from the wild prison of the jungle". Hekking died in 1994. Charles died in December 2009.</p>
<p>Except for the worst months of the construction period, known as the "Speedo" (mid-spring to mid-October 1943), one of the ways the Allied POWs kept their spirits up was to ask one of the musicians in their midst to play his guitar or accordion, or lead them in a group sing-along, or request their camp comedians to tell some jokes or put on a skit.</p>
<p>After the railway was completed, the POWs still had almost two years to survive before their liberation. During this time, most of the POWs were moved to hospital and relocation camps where they could be available for maintenance crews or sent to Japan to alleviate the manpower shortage there. In these camps entertainment flourished as an essential part of their rehabilitation. Theatres of bamboo and <a href="/wiki/Nypa_fruticans" title="Nypa fruticans">attap</a> (palm fronds) were built, set, lighting, costumes and makeup devised, and an array of entertainment produced that included music halls, variety shows, cabarets, plays, and musical comedies – even pantomimes. These activities engaged numerous POWs as actors, singers, musicians, designers, technicians, and female impersonators.</p>
<p>POWs and Asian workers were also used to build the Kra Isthmus Railway from <a href="/wiki/Chumphon" title="Chumphon">Chumphon</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kra_Buri_District" title="Kra Buri District">Kra Buri</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Sumatra_Railway" title="Sumatra Railway">Sumatra or Palembang Railway</a> from <a href="/wiki/Pekanbaru" title="Pekanbaru">Pekanbaru</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muaro" title="Muaro">Muaro</a>.</p>
<p>The construction of the Burma Railway is counted as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_war_crime" title="Japanese war crime">war crime committed by Japan</a> in Asia. <a href="/wiki/Hiroshi_Abe_(war_criminal)" title="Hiroshi Abe (war criminal)">Hiroshi Abe</a>, the first lieutenant who supervised construction of the railway at <a href="/wiki/Sonkrai" title="Sonkrai">Sonkrai</a> where over 3,000 POWs died, was sentenced to death, later commuted to 15 years in prison, as a B/C class war criminal.</p>
<p>After the completion of the railroad, most of the POWs were then transported to Japan. Those left to maintain the line still suffered from appalling living conditions as well as increasing Allied air raids.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_bridge_on_the_River_Khwai">The bridge on the River Khwai</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burma_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The bridge on the River Khwai">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bridge_over_the_River_Kwai_Art.IWMARTLD6035.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="655" data-file-width="800" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Bridge_over_the_River_Kwai_Art.IWMARTLD6035.jpg/220px-Bridge_over_the_River_Kwai_Art.IWMARTLD6035.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Bridge_over_the_River_Kwai_Art.IWMARTLD6035.jpg/330px-Bridge_over_the_River_Kwai_Art.IWMARTLD6035.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Bridge_over_the_River_Kwai_Art.IWMARTLD6035.jpg/440px-Bridge_over_the_River_Kwai_Art.IWMARTLD6035.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bridge_over_the_River_Kwai_Art.IWMARTLD6035.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bridge over the River Kwai by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Leo_Rawlings&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Leo Rawlings (page does not exist)">Leo Rawlings</a> (1943); four prisoners of war carry a large log across the river, waist deep in water</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The most famous portion of the railway is Bridge 277, "the bridge on the River Kwai", which was built over a stretch of river which was then known as part of the <a href="/wiki/Mae_Klong" title="Mae Klong">Mae Klong</a>. The greater part of the Thai part of the route followed the valley of the <a href="/wiki/Khwae_Noi_River" title="Khwae Noi River">Khwae Noi River</a> (<i>Khwae</i> branch or tributary, <i>Noi</i> small; <i>Khwae</i> is frequently mispronounced by non-Thai speakers as <i>Kwai</i>, the Thai word for water buffalo). This gave rise to the name "River Kwai" in English. In 1960, because of the discrepancy between fact and fiction, the part of the Mae Klong which passes under the famous bridge was renamed as the <a href="/wiki/Khwae_Yai_River" title="Khwae Yai River">Khwae Yai</a> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a> แควใหญ่, English "big tributary").</p>
<p>This bridge was immortalised by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Boulle" title="Pierre Boulle">Pierre Boulle</a> in his book and the film based on it, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bridge_on_the_River_Kwai" title="The Bridge on the River Kwai">The Bridge on the River Kwai</a></i>. However, there are many who point out that both Boulle's story and the film based on it were utterly unrealistic and do not show how bad the conditions and treatment of prisoners were.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> On the part of the Japanese, many resented the movie's depiction that their engineers' capabilities were inferior to those of British engineers. In fact, Japanese engineers had been surveying the route of the railway since 1937 and they were highly organized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The Japanese also accused the film of "glorification of the superiority of Western civilization" because the British in the film were able to build a bridge that the Japanese could not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an interview made by former POW John Coast, which forms part of the 1969 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BBC2" title="BBC2">BBC2</a> documentary <i>Return to the River Kwai</i>, Boulle outlined the reasoning which led him to conceive the character of Lt-Col Nicholson, who works to build the fictional bridge and ultimately tries to prevent its destruction. A transcription of the interview and the documentary as a whole can be found in the new edition of John Coast's book <i>Railroad of Death</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coast_2014_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Coast_2014-21">[21]</a></sup> The first wooden bridge over the Khwae Yai was finished in February 1943, followed by a concrete and steel bridge in June 1943. It was this bridge 277 that was meant to be attacked with the use of the first-ever example of a <a href="/wiki/Precision-guided_munition" title="Precision-guided munition">precision-guided munition</a> in American service, the <a href="/wiki/Azon" title="Azon">VB-1 Azon</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MCLOS" title="MCLOS">MCLOS</a>-guided 1,000 lb ordnance on 23 January 1945<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> but bad weather scrubbed the mission.</p>
<p>According to Hellfire Tours in Thailand, "The two bridges were successfully bombed on 13 February 1945 by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a>. Repairs were carried out by POW labour and by April the wooden <a href="/wiki/Trestle_bridge" title="Trestle bridge">trestle bridge</a> was back in operation. On 3 April a second raid by <a href="/wiki/Consolidated_B-24_Liberator" title="Consolidated B-24 Liberator">Liberator bombers</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Air_Forces" title="U.S. Army Air Forces">U.S. Army Air Forces</a> damaged the wooden bridge once again. Repair work continued and both bridges were operational again by the end of May. A second raid by the R.A.F. on 24 June put the railway out of commission for the rest of the war. After the Japanese surrender, the British Army removed 3.9 kilometers of track on the Thai-Burma border. A survey of the track had shown that its poor construction would not support commercial traffic. The track was sold to Thai Railways and the 130 km Ban Pong–Namtok section relaid and is in use today."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The new railway did not fully connect with the Burmese system, as no bridge crossed the river between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moulmein" title="Moulmein">Moulmein</a> on the south bank with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martaban" title="Martaban">Martaban</a> on the north bank. Thus ferries were needed. A bridge was not built until <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thanlwin_Bridge" title="Thanlwin Bridge">Thanlwin Bridge</a> in 2000–05.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hellfire_Pass">Hellfire Pass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burma_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hellfire Pass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hellfire_Pass" title="Hellfire Pass">Hellfire Pass</a></div>

<p>Hellfire Pass in the <a href="/wiki/Tenasserim_Hills" title="Tenasserim Hills">Tenasserim Hills</a> was a particularly difficult section of the line to build due to it being the largest rock cutting on the railway, coupled with its general remoteness and the lack of proper construction tools during building. The Australian, British, Dutch, other allied prisoners of war, along with Chinese, Malay, and Tamil labourers, were required by the Japanese to complete the cutting. Sixty-nine men were beaten to death by Japanese guards in the twelve weeks it took to build the cutting, and many more died from <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dysentery" title="Dysentery">dysentery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Starvation" title="Starvation">starvation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(medical)" title="Fatigue (medical)">exhaustion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anzac-day_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-anzac-day-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war">Post-war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burma_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Post-war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the war the railway was in very poor condition and needed heavy reconstruction for use by the <a href="/wiki/State_Railway_of_Thailand" title="State Railway of Thailand">Royal Thai Railway</a> system. On 24 June 1949, the portion from <a href="/wiki/Kanchanaburi" title="Kanchanaburi">Kanchanaburi</a> to <a href="/wiki/Nong_Pladuk_Junction_Railway_Station" title="Nong Pladuk Junction Railway Station">Nong Pla Duk</a> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a> หนองปลาดุก) was finished; on the first of April 1952, the next section up to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wang_Pho&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wang Pho (page does not exist)">Wang Pho</a> (Wangpo) was done. Finally, on 1 July 1958 the rail line was completed to <a href="/wiki/Nam_Tok_Sai_Yok_Noi" title="Nam Tok Sai Yok Noi">Nam Tok</a> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a> น้ำตก, English Sai Yok "waterfalls".) The portion in use today is some 130 km (81 mi) long. The line was abandoned beyond <a href="/wiki/Nam_Tok_Sai_Yok_Noi" title="Nam Tok Sai Yok Noi">Nam Tok Sai Yok Noi</a>; the steel rails were salvaged for reuse in expanding the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bangsue" title="Bangsue">Bangsue</a> railway yard, reinforcing the BKK-Banphachi double track, rehabilitating the track from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thung_Song" title="Thung Song">Thung Song</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trang,_Trang" title="Trang, Trang">Trang</a>, and constructing both the Nong Pla Duk–<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suphanburi" title="Suphanburi">Suphanburi</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ban_Thung_Pho&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ban Thung Pho (page does not exist)">Ban Thung Pho</a>–<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Khirirat_Nikhom&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Khirirat Nikhom (page does not exist)">Khirirat Nikhom</a> branch lines. Parts of the abandoned route have been converted into a <a href="/wiki/List_of_rail_trails" title="List of rail trails">walking trail</a>.</p>
<p>Since the 1990s various proposals have been made to rebuild the complete railway, but as of 2014<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Burma_Railway&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> these plans had not been realised. Since a large part of the original railway line is now submerged by the <a href="/wiki/Vajiralongkorn_Dam" title="Vajiralongkorn Dam">Vajiralongkorn Dam</a>, and the surrounding terrain is mountainous, it would take extensive tunneling to reconnect Thailand with Burma by rail.</p>

<p>Estimates of the deaths directly related to the construction of the Burma railway differ depending upon the source. For the impressed workers from Southeast Asian (romusha), estimates vary widely, although authorities agree that the percentage of deaths among the romuska was much higher than among the foreign prisoners of war. The total number of romusha working on the railroad may have reached 300,000 and the number of deaths among them has been estimated as high as fifty percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The lower death rate of the Dutch POWs compared to other POWs is attributed to the fact that many of them had been born in the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies" title="Dutch East Indies">Dutch East Indies</a>, some with local heritage, and were thus more accustomed to tropical conditions than the British, Australians, and Americans. Officers fared much better than enlisted men; their weight loss during the construction was 20 to 30 pounds less than that of enlisted men. The survival of POWs was also due to luck. Some cohorts of workers, especially those in more isolated areas, suffered a much higher death rate than others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The quality of medical care the POWs received from their medical personnel for <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dysentery" title="Dysentery">dysentery</a> and especially <a href="/wiki/Tropical_ulcer" title="Tropical ulcer">tropical ulcers</a> was important. The difference among POW cohorts depending upon the quality and number of medical doctors was substantial. Many European and American doctors had little experience with tropical diseases. As an example, one cohort of Americans suffered 100 deaths out of 450 personnel. Another American group to which Dr. Hekking, the Dutch doctor with long experience in tropical medicine, was assigned suffered only 9 deaths out of 190 personnel. A Dutch group of 400 workers which included three doctors with extensive tropical medicine experience suffered no deaths at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cemeteries_and_memorials">Cemeteries and memorials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burma_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cemeteries and memorials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the war, the remains of most of the war dead were moved from former POW camps, burial grounds and lone graves along the rail line to official war cemeteries.</p>
<p>Three cemeteries maintained by the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_War_Graves_Commission" title="Commonwealth War Graves Commission">Commonwealth War Graves Commission</a> (CWGC) contain the vast majority of Allied military personnel who died on the Burma Railway.</p>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Kanchanaburi_War_Cemetery" title="Kanchanaburi War Cemetery">Kanchanaburi War Cemetery</a></b>, in the city of Kanchanaburi, contains the graves of 6,982 personnel comprising:</p>
<ul>
<li>3,585 British;</li>
<li>1,896 Dutch;</li>
<li>1,362 Australians;</li>
<li>12 members of the <a href="/wiki/British_Indian_Army" title="British Indian Army">Indian Army</a> (including British officers)</li>
<li>2 New Zealanders;</li>
<li>2 Danish, and;</li>
<li>1 Canadian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cwgcsearch_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-cwgcsearch-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dutch_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dutch-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>A memorial at the Kanchanaburi cemetery lists 11 other members of the Indian Army, who are buried in nearby Muslim cemeteries.</p>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Thanbyuzayat_War_Cemetery" title="Thanbyuzayat War Cemetery">Thanbyuzayat War Cemetery</a></b>, at <a href="/wiki/Thanbyuzayat" title="Thanbyuzayat">Thanbyuzayat</a>, has the graves of 3,617 POWs who died on the Burmese portion of the line.</p>
<ul>
<li>1,651 British;</li>
<li>1,335 Australians;</li>
<li>621 Dutch;</li>
<li>15 Indian Army;</li>
<li>3 New Zealanders, and;</li>
<li>1 Canadian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cwgcsearch_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-cwgcsearch-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dutch_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dutch-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p><b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chungkai_War_Cemetery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chungkai War Cemetery (page does not exist)">Chungkai War Cemetery</a></b>, near Kanchanaburi, has a further 1,693 war graves.</p>
<ul>
<li>1,373 British;</li>
<li>314 Dutch;</li>
<li>6 Indian Army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cwgcsearch_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-cwgcsearch-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dutch_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dutch-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The remains of United States personnel were <a href="/wiki/Repatriation" title="Repatriation">repatriated</a>. Of the 668 US personnel forced to work on the railway, 133 died. This included personnel from USS <i>Houston</i> and the 131st Field Artillery Regiment of the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Army_National_Guard" title="Texas Army National Guard">Texas Army National Guard</a>. The Americans were called the <a href="/wiki/Lost_Battalion_(Pacific,_World_War_II)" title="Lost Battalion (Pacific, World War II)">Lost Battalion</a> as their fate was unknown to the United States for more than two years after their capture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several museums are dedicated to those who perished building the railway. The largest of these is at <a href="/wiki/Hellfire_Pass" title="Hellfire Pass">Hellfire Pass</a> (north of the current terminus at <a href="/wiki/Nam_Tok_Railway_Station" title="Nam Tok Railway Station">Nam Tok</a>), a cutting where the greatest number of lives were lost. An Australian memorial is at Hellfire Pass. Two other museums are in <a href="/wiki/Kanchanaburi" title="Kanchanaburi">Kanchanaburi</a>: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thailand-Burma_Railway_Museum" title="Thailand-Burma Railway Museum">Thailand-Burma Railway Museum</a>, opened in March 2003, and the <a href="/wiki/JEATH_War_Museum" title="JEATH War Museum">JEATH War Museum</a>. There is a memorial plaque at the Kwai bridge itself and an historic wartime steam locomotive is on display.</p>
<p>A preserved section of line has been rebuilt at the <a href="/wiki/National_Memorial_Arboretum" title="National Memorial Arboretum">National Memorial Arboretum</a> in England.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_references">Cultural references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burma_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cultural references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The construction of the railway has been the subject of a novel and an award-winning film, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bridge_on_the_River_Kwai" title="The Bridge on the River Kwai">The Bridge on the River Kwai</a></i> (itself an adaptation of the French language novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bridge_over_the_River_Kwai" title="The Bridge over the River Kwai">The Bridge over the River Kwai</a></i>); a novel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Narrow_Road_to_the_Deep_North_(novel)" title="The Narrow Road to the Deep North (novel)">The Narrow Road to the Deep North</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Flanagan" title="Richard Flanagan">Richard Flanagan</a>, as well as a large number of personal accounts of POW experiences. More recently, the motion picture <i><a href="/wiki/The_Railway_Man_(film)" title="The Railway Man (film)">The Railway Man</a></i> (based on the <a href="/wiki/The_Railway_Man_(book)" title="The Railway Man (book)">book of the same name</a>) also gives insight into the barbaric conditions and suffering that were inflicted upon the workers who built the railway. <a href="/wiki/Richard_Flanagan" title="Richard Flanagan">Richard Flanagan</a>'s 2013 book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Narrow_Road_to_the_Deep_North_(novel)" title="The Narrow Road to the Deep North (novel)">The Narrow Road to the Deep North</a></i> centres on a group of Australian POWs and their experiences building the railway as slave labour. It was awarded the <a href="/wiki/2014_Man_Booker_Prize" title="2014 Man Booker Prize">2014 Man Booker Prize</a>.</p>
