<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Bourbon whiskey</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Bourbon whiskey</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ɜːr/ 'ur' in 'fur'">ɜːr</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ən/ 'on' in 'button'">ən</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a type of <a href="/wiki/American_whiskey" title="American whiskey">American whiskey</a>: a barrel-aged <a href="/wiki/Distilled_beverage" title="Distilled beverage">distilled spirit</a> made primarily from <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a>. The name is ultimately derived from the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France" title="Kingdom of France">French</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bourbon_dynasty" title="Bourbon dynasty">Bourbon dynasty</a>, although it is disputed whether <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_County,_Kentucky" title="Bourbon County, Kentucky">Bourbon County</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_Street" title="Bourbon Street">Bourbon Street</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> inspired the whiskey's name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bstreet_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-bstreet-1">[1]</a></sup> Bourbon has been distilled since the 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zeldes_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-zeldes-2">[2]</a></sup> The use of the term "bourbon" for the whiskey has been traced to the 1820s, and the term began to be used consistently in Kentucky in the 1870s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bstreet_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-bstreet-1">[1]</a></sup> While bourbon may be made anywhere in the United States, it is strongly associated with the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">American South</a>, and with <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> in particular. As of 2014, the distillers' wholesale market revenue for bourbon sold within the U.S. is about $2.7 billion, and bourbon makes up about two-thirds of the $1.6 billion of U.S. exports of distilled spirits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Distilled_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Distilled-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourbon_whiskey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beam_Rack_House.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2324" data-file-width="3583" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Beam_Rack_House.jpg/300px-Beam_Rack_House.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Beam_Rack_House.jpg/450px-Beam_Rack_House.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Beam_Rack_House.jpg/600px-Beam_Rack_House.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Beam_Rack_House.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Quercus_alba" title="Quercus alba">White oak</a> <a href="/wiki/Barrel" title="Barrel">barrels</a> filled with new bourbon whiskey and resting in a rack house for a period of four to nine years, giving bourbon its well-known copper color.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The origin of bourbon is not well documented. There are many conflicting legends and claims, some more credible than others. For example, the invention of bourbon is often attributed to <a href="/wiki/Elijah_Craig" title="Elijah Craig">Elijah Craig</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baptist" title="Baptist">Baptist</a> minister and distiller credited with many Kentucky firsts (e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fulling_mill" title="Fulling mill">fulling mill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paper_mill" title="Paper mill">paper mill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ropewalk" title="Ropewalk">ropewalk</a>) who is also said to have been the first to age the product in charred oak casks, a process which gives bourbon its reddish color and distinctive taste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Across the county line in Bourbon County, an early distiller named Jacob Spears is credited with being the first to label his product as Bourbon whiskey. Spears' home, Stone Castle, warehouse and spring house survive; one can drive by the Spears' home on Clay-Kiser Road.</p>
<p>Although still popular and often repeated, the Craig legend is apocryphal. Similarly, the Spears story is a local favorite, rarely repeated outside the county. There likely was no single "inventor" of bourbon, which developed into its present form only in the late 19th century. Essentially any type of grain can be used to make whiskey, and the practice of aging whiskey and charring the barrels for better flavor had also been known in Europe for centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The late date of the Bourbon County etymology has led <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville</a> historian Michael Veach to dispute its authenticity. He proposes that the whiskey was named after <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_Street" title="Bourbon Street">Bourbon Street</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>, a major port where shipments of Kentucky whiskey sold well as a cheaper alternative to French <a href="/wiki/Cognac" title="Cognac">cognac</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bstreet_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-bstreet-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Distilling probably was brought to present-day Kentucky in the late 18th century by Scots, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scotch-Irish_American" title="Scotch-Irish American">Scots-Irish</a>, and other settlers (including English, Irish, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welsh_American" title="Welsh American">Welsh</a>, German and French) who began to farm the area in earnest. The spirit they made evolved, and became known as <i>bourbon</i> in the early 19th century due to its historical association with the geographic area known as <i>Old Bourbon</i> (this consisted of the original Bourbon County of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> as organized in 1785, a region that included much of today's Eastern Kentucky – including 34 of today's counties in Kentucky).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This area included the current <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_County,_Kentucky" title="Bourbon County, Kentucky">Bourbon County</a> of Kentucky, which became a county of Kentucky when Kentucky was separated from Virginia as a new state in 1792.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CowderyNaming_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-CowderyNaming-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CowderyStraight_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-CowderyStraight-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>When American pioneers pushed west of the <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Mountains" title="Allegheny Mountains">Allegheny Mountains</a> following the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>, the first counties they founded covered vast regions. One of these original, huge counties was Bourbon, established in 1785 and named after the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon" title="House of Bourbon">French royal family</a>. While this vast county was being carved into many smaller ones, early in the 19th century, many people continued to call the region <i>Old Bourbon</i>. Located within <i>Old Bourbon</i> was the principal port on the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maysville,_Kentucky" title="Maysville, Kentucky">Maysville, Kentucky</a>, from which whiskey and other products were shipped. "Old Bourbon" was stencilled on the barrels to indicate their port of origin. <i>Old Bourbon</i> whiskey was different because it was the first corn whiskey most people had ever tasted. In time, <i>bourbon</i> became the name for any corn-based whiskey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CowderyStraight_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-CowderyStraight-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Although many distilleries operated in Bourbon County historically, there were no distilleries operating there between 1919, when <a href="/wiki/Prohibition_in_the_United_States" title="Prohibition in the United States">Prohibition</a> began in Kentucky, and late 2014, when a small distillery opened – a period of 95 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>A refinement often dubiously<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Veach_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Veach-13">[13]</a></sup> credited to <a href="/wiki/James_C._Crow" title="James C. Crow">James C. Crow</a> was the <a href="/wiki/Sour_mash" title="Sour mash">sour mash</a> process, by which each new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(food)" title="Fermentation (food)">fermentation</a> is conditioned with some amount of spent mash. Spent mash is also known as spent beer, distillers' spent grain, stillage, and slop or feed mash, so named because it is used as animal feed. The <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a> introduced by using the sour mash controls the growth of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterium" title="Bacterium">bacteria</a> that could taint the whiskey and creates a proper <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> balance for the yeast to work.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Concurrent_resolution" title="Concurrent resolution">concurrent resolution</a> adopted by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> in 1964 declared bourbon to be a "distinctive product of the United States" and asked "the appropriate agencies of the United States Government... [to] take appropriate action to prohibit importation into the United States of whiskey designated as 'Bourbon Whiskey.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78stat_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-78stat-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Federal_Regulations" title="Code of Federal Regulations">Federal regulation</a> now defines "bourbon whiskey" to only include "bourbon" produced in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrb1_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-cfrb1-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In recent years, bourbon and <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_whiskey" title="Tennessee whiskey">Tennessee whiskey</a> (which is sometimes regarded as a different type of spirit but which generally meets the legal requirements for being called bourbon) have enjoyed significant growth and popularity. The <a href="/wiki/Distilled_Spirits_Council_of_the_United_States" title="Distilled Spirits Council of the United States">Distilled Spirits Council of the United States</a>, the industry trade group, tracks sales of bourbon and Tennessee whiskey together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-AP-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the Distilled Spirits Council, during 2009–14, the volume of 9-liter cases of whiskey increased by 28.5% overall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Distilled_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Distilled-4">[4]</a></sup> Higher-end bourbon and whiskeys experienced the greatest growth: during 2009–14 the volume of the value segment increased by 12.1%, premium by 25.8%, high-end premium by 27.8% and super-premium by 123.8%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Distilled_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Distilled-4">[4]</a></sup> Gross supplier revenues (including <a href="/wiki/Excise_tax_in_the_United_States" title="Excise tax in the United States">federal excise tax</a>) for U.S. bourbon and Tennessee whiskey increased by 46.7% over the 2009–14 period, with the greatest growth coming from high-end products (18.7% growth for value, 33.6% for premium, 44.5% for high-end premium, and 137.2% for super-premium).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Distilled_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Distilled-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2014, more than 19 million nine-liter cases of bourbon and Tennessee whiskey were sold in the U.S., generating almost $2.7 billion in wholesale distillery revenue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Distilled_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Distilled-4">[4]</a></sup> U.S. exports of bourbon whiskey surpassed $1 billion for the first time in 2013; distillers hailed the rise of a "golden age of Kentucky bourbon" and predicted further growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-AP-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2014, it was estimated that U.S. bourbon whiskey exports surpassed $1 billion (making up the majority of the U.S. total of $1.6 billion in spirits exports).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-AP-3">[3]</a></sup> Major export markets for U.S. spirits are, in descending order: Canada, the United Kingdom, Germany, Australia and France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-AP-3">[3]</a></sup> The largest percentage increases in U.S. exports were, in descending order: Brazil, the Dominican Republic, Bahamas, Israel and United Arab Emirates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-AP-3">[3]</a></sup> Key elements of growth in the markets showing the largest increases have been changes of law, trade agreements, and reductions of tariffs, as well as increased consumer demand for premium-category spirits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_requirements">Legal requirements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourbon_whiskey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legal requirements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bourbon's legal definition varies somewhat from country to country, but many trade agreements require the name <i>bourbon</i> to be reserved for products made in the United States. The U.S. regulations for labeling and advertising bourbon apply only to products made for consumption within the United States; they do not apply to distilled spirits made for export.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfr51_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-cfr51-18">[18]</a></sup> Canadian law requires products labeled <i>bourbon</i> to be made in the United States and also to conform to the requirements that apply within the United States. But in countries other than the United States and Canada, products labeled <i>bourbon</i> may not adhere to the same standards. For example, in the European Union, products labeled as <i>bourbon</i> are not required to conform to all of the regulations that apply within the United States, though they still must be made in the U.S.</p>
<p>The Federal Standards of Identity for <a href="/wiki/Distilled_beverage" title="Distilled beverage">Distilled Spirits</a> (<span><a href="/wiki/Title_27_of_the_Code_of_Federal_Regulations" title="Title 27 of the Code of Federal Regulations">27 C.F.R.</a> <a class="external text" href="https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/27/5" rel="nofollow">5</a></span>) state that bourbon made for U.S. consumption<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfr51_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-cfr51-18">[18]</a></sup> must be:</p>
<ul>
<li>Produced in the United States<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrl1_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-cfrl1-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>Made from a grain mixture that is at least 51% <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrb1i_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-cfrb1i-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>Aged in new, charred oak containers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrb1i_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-cfrb1i-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distilled" title="Distilled">Distilled</a> to no more than 160 (U.S.) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_proof" title="Alcoholic proof">proof</a> (80% <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_by_volume" title="Alcohol by volume">alcohol by volume</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrb1i_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-cfrb1i-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>Entered into the barrel for aging at no more than 125 proof (62.5% <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_by_volume" title="Alcohol by volume">alcohol by volume</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrb1i_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-cfrb1i-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>Bottled (like other whiskeys) at 80 proof or more (40% <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_by_volume" title="Alcohol by volume">alcohol by volume</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrb_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-cfrb-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Bourbon has no minimum specified duration for its aging period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CowderyMyths_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-CowderyMyths-22">[22]</a></sup> Products aged for as little as three months are sold as bourbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The exception is <a href="/wiki/Straight_whiskey" title="Straight whiskey">straight</a> bourbon, which has a minimum aging requirement of two years. In addition, any bourbon aged less than four years must include an age statement on its label.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfr540_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-cfr540-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bourbon that meets the above requirements, has been aged for a minimum of two years, and does not have added coloring, flavoring, or other spirits <i>may</i> (but is not required to) be called <i><a href="/wiki/Straight_whiskey" title="Straight whiskey">straight</a></i> bourbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrb1iii_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-cfrb1iii-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Bourbon that is labeled as <i>straight</i> that has been aged under four years must be labeled with the duration of its aging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfr540a_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-cfr540a-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li>Bourbon that has an age stated on its label must be labeled with the age of the <i>youngest</i> whiskey in the bottle (not counting the age of any added <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutral_grain_spirits" title="Neutral grain spirits">neutral grain spirits</a> in a bourbon that is labeled as <i><a href="/wiki/Blended_whiskey" title="Blended whiskey">blended</a></i>, as neutral-grain spirits are not considered whiskey under the regulations and are not required to be aged at all).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfr540_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-cfr540-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Bourbon that is labeled <i>blended</i> (or as <i>a blend</i>) may contain added coloring, flavoring, and other spirits (such as un-aged neutral grain spirits); but at least 51% of the product must be <i>straight</i> bourbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrb4_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-cfrb4-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfr523_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-cfr523-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_origin">Geographic origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourbon_whiskey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geographic origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On May 4, 1964, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> recognized bourbon whiskey as a "distinctive product of the United States" by concurrent resolution. Bourbon may be produced anywhere in the United States where it is legal to distill spirits, but most brands are produced in Kentucky, where bourbon production has a strong historical association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Iron-free water that has been filtered through the high concentrations of limestone, unique to the area, is often touted by bourbon distillers in Kentucky as a signature step in the bourbon-making process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Barrels_with_a_flavour_to_impart_-_geograph.org.uk_-_286747.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="480" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Barrels_with_a_flavour_to_impart_-_geograph.org.uk_-_286747.jpg/220px-Barrels_with_a_flavour_to_impart_-_geograph.org.uk_-_286747.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Barrels_with_a_flavour_to_impart_-_geograph.org.uk_-_286747.jpg/330px-Barrels_with_a_flavour_to_impart_-_geograph.org.uk_-_286747.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Barrels_with_a_flavour_to_impart_-_geograph.org.uk_-_286747.jpg/440px-Barrels_with_a_flavour_to_impart_-_geograph.org.uk_-_286747.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>On August 2, 2007, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Senate" title="U.S. Senate">U.S. Senate</a> passed a resolution sponsored by Senator <a href="/wiki/Jim_Bunning" title="Jim Bunning">Jim Bunning</a> (R-KY) officially declaring September 2007 to be <a href="/wiki/National_Bourbon_Heritage_Month" title="National Bourbon Heritage Month">National Bourbon Heritage Month</a>, marking the history of bourbon whiskey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sres_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-sres-32">[32]</a></sup> Notably, the resolution claimed that Congress had declared bourbon to be "America's Native Spirit" in its 1964 resolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sres_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-sres-32">[32]</a></sup> However, the 1964 resolution had not contained such a statement; it had declared bourbon to be a distinctive product identifiable with the United States (in a similar way that <a href="/wiki/Scotch_whisky" title="Scotch whisky">Scotch</a> is considered identifiable with Scotland).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78stat_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-78stat-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CowderyNative_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-CowderyNative-33">[33]</a></sup> The resolution was passed again in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CowderyNative_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-CowderyNative-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2014, approximately 95% of all bourbon is produced in Kentucky. The state has more than 5.3 million barrels of bourbon that are aging – a number that exceeds the state population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-AP-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bardstown,_Kentucky" title="Bardstown, Kentucky">Bardstown, Kentucky</a> is home to the annual <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Bourbon_Festival" title="Kentucky Bourbon Festival">Bourbon Festival</a> held each September. It has been called the "<i>Bourbon Capital of the World</i>" by the Bardstown Tourism Commission<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and the Kentucky Bourbon Festival organizers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> who have registered the phrase as a <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademark</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Bourbon_Trail" title="Kentucky Bourbon Trail">Kentucky Bourbon Trail</a> is the name of a tourism promotion program organized by the Kentucky Distillers' Association and aimed at attracting visitors to the distilleries in Kentucky, primarily including <a href="/wiki/Four_Roses" title="Four Roses">Four Roses</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lawrenceburg,_Kentucky" title="Lawrenceburg, Kentucky">Lawrenceburg</a>), <a href="/wiki/Heaven_Hill" title="Heaven Hill">Heaven Hill</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bardstown,_Kentucky" title="Bardstown, Kentucky">Bardstown</a>), <a href="/wiki/Jim_Beam" title="Jim Beam">Jim Beam</a> (<a href="/wiki/Clermont,_Kentucky" title="Clermont, Kentucky">Clermont</a>), <a href="/wiki/Maker%27s_Mark" title="Maker's Mark">Maker's Mark</a> (<a href="/wiki/Loretto,_Kentucky" title="Loretto, Kentucky">Loretto</a>), <a href="/wiki/Town_Branch" title="Town Branch">Town Branch</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lexington,_Kentucky" title="Lexington, Kentucky">Lexington</a>), <a href="/wiki/Wild_Turkey_(bourbon)" title="Wild Turkey (bourbon)">Wild Turkey</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lawrenceburg,_Kentucky" title="Lawrenceburg, Kentucky">Lawrenceburg</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Woodford_Reserve" title="Woodford Reserve">Woodford Reserve</a> (<a href="/wiki/Versailles,_Kentucky" title="Versailles, Kentucky">Versailles</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a> is home to other major bourbon producers, though most of its producers do not call their finished product bourbon; <a href="/wiki/Jack_Daniel%27s" title="Jack Daniel's">Jack Daniel's</a> is the most well-known example. The methods for producing <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_whiskey" title="Tennessee whiskey">Tennessee whiskey</a> fit the characteristics of bourbon production, and "Tennessee whiskey" is legally defined under the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Free_Trade_Agreement" title="North American Free Trade Agreement">North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA)</a> and at least one other international trade agreement as the recognized name for a <a href="/wiki/Straight_whiskey" title="Straight whiskey">straight</a> bourbon whiskey produced in Tennessee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nafta313_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-nafta313-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SICE_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-SICE-40">[40]</a></sup> It is also required to meet the legal definition of bourbon under Canadian law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CanadaRequirementTennWhisky_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-CanadaRequirementTennWhisky-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although some producers have pointed to the use of a production process known as the <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_County_Process" title="Lincoln County Process">Lincoln County Process</a> as a characteristic that makes Tennessee whiskey distinct from bourbon, the U.S. regulations defining bourbon do not prohibit the use of that process when making bourbon, so Tennessee whiskey can generally be regarded as a particular type of bourbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CowderyMyths_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-CowderyMyths-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nafta313_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-nafta313-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CowderyTennessee_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-CowderyTennessee-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Prior to 2013, the use of the Lincoln County Process was not actually required for making products identified as Tennessee whiskey. However, on May 13, 2013, the <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Tennessee" title="Governor of Tennessee">Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Bill_Haslam" title="Bill Haslam">Bill Haslam</a> of Tennessee signed House Bill 1084, requiring the Lincoln County process and the existing requirements for bourbon to be used for products identified as "Tennessee whiskey". As a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grandfathering" title="Grandfathering">grandfathering</a> measure, the law exempted one small producer, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benjamin_Prichard%27s" title="Benjamin Prichard's">Benjamin Prichard's</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> As U.S. federal law requires statements of origin on labels to be accurate, and various international trade agreements also codify this requirement, the Tennessee law effectively gives a firm definition to Tennessee whiskey.</p>
<p>Bourbon also has been made in other U.S. states as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_process">Production process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourbon_whiskey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The typical grain mixture for bourbon, known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mash_ingredient" title="Mash ingredient">mash bill</a>, is a minimum of 51% corn, with the remainder being <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malt" title="Malt">malted</a> <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>, or some combination of these.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zeldes_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-zeldes-2">[2]</a></sup> A mash bill that contains wheat instead of rye produces what is known as a <i>wheated</i> bourbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The grain is ground and mixed with water. Usually, though not always, mash from a previous distillation is added to ensure a consistent <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> across batches, and a mash produced in that manner is referred to as a <i><a href="/wiki/Sour_mash" title="Sour mash">sour mash</a></i>. Finally, yeast is added and the mash is <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fermentation" title="Ethanol fermentation">fermented</a>. The fermented mash then is <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distilled</a> to (typically) between 65% and 80% alcohol. Distillation was historically performed using an <a href="/wiki/Alembic" title="Alembic">alembic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pot_still" title="Pot still">pot still</a> until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continuous_still" title="Continuous still">continuous still</a> was invented which made the distillation process much more economically efficient. However, column stills do not seem to produce the best flavored spirits when used by themselves, so in current bourbon production, most typically the spirit is initially distilled using a column still and then again in a "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Doubler_(distillation)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Doubler (distillation) (page does not exist)">doubler</a>" (alternatively known as a "thumper" or "retort") that is basically a pot still.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>The resulting clear spirit, called "white dog",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> is placed in newly charred <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_oak" title="American oak">American oak</a> barrels for aging, during which it gains color and flavor from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caramelized" title="Caramelized">caramelized</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugars" title="Sugars">sugars</a> in the charred wood. Changes to the spirit also occur due to <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> and chemical processes such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a>. Bourbons gain more color and flavor the longer they mature. Maturity, not a particular age, is the goal. Bourbon can age too long and become woody and unbalanced.</p>
<p>After maturing, bourbon is withdrawn from the barrel, usually filtered and diluted with water, and bottled to at least 80 US proof (40% abv).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrb_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-cfrb-21">[21]</a></sup> Most bourbon whiskey is sold at 80 US proof. Other common proofs are 86, 90, and 100, and all "<a href="/wiki/Bottled_in_bond" title="Bottled in bond">bottled in bond</a>" bourbon is bottled at 100 proof. Some higher-proof bottlings are marketed as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barrel_proof" title="Barrel proof">barrel proof</a>", meaning that they have not been diluted or have been only lightly diluted after removal from the barrels. Bourbon whiskey may be sold at less than 80 proof but must be labeled as "diluted bourbon".</p>
<p>After processing, barrels still contain some bourbon soaked into the wooden staves. This may be up to ten gallons of liquid, but is usually 2–3 gallons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-modernmarvels_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-modernmarvels-53">[53]</a></sup> After the first use, oak barrels cannot be used again for bourbon, and most of them are then sold to distilleries in Canada, Scotland, Ireland, Mexico, or the Caribbean for aging other spirits. They are also used for making various other barrel-aged products, including amateur and professional production of bourbon-barrel-aged beer, barbecue sauce, wine, hot sauce, and others.</p>
<p>The bottling operation for bourbon is the process of filtering, mixing together straight whiskey from different barrels (sometimes from different distilleries), dilution with water, blending with other ingredients (if producing blended bourbon), and filling of containers to produce the final product that is marketed to consumers. The phrase "bottled by", by itself, means only that. Only if the bottler operates the distillery that produced the whiskey may "distilled by" be added to the label.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CFR5.36_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-CFR5.36-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>Labeling requirements for Bourbon and other alcoholic beverages (including the requirements for what is allowed to be called Bourbon under U.S. law) are defined in the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Federal_Regulations" title="Code of Federal Regulations">Code of Federal Regulations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> No whiskey made outside the United States may be labeled "bourbon" or sold as "bourbon" inside the United States (and in various other countries that have trade agreements with the United States to recognize bourbon as a distinctive product of the United States).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourbon_whiskey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bourbon is served <a href="/wiki/Bartending_terminology" title="Bartending terminology">neat</a>, diluted with water, over ice cubes ("on the rocks"), or mixed with soda and into cocktails, including the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_(cocktail)" title="Manhattan (cocktail)">Manhattan</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Old_Fashioned" title="Old Fashioned">Old Fashioned</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Whiskey_sour" title="Whiskey sour">whiskey sour</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Mint_julep" title="Mint julep">mint julep</a>. Bourbon is also used in <a href="/wiki/Cooking" title="Cooking">cooking</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zeldes_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-zeldes-2">[2]</a></sup> and was historically used for medicinal purposes.</p>
