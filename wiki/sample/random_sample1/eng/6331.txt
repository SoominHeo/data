<p>The <b>Virginia Squires</b> were a basketball franchise in the former <a href="/wiki/American_Basketball_Association" title="American Basketball Association">American Basketball Association</a> from 1970 until just before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ABA%E2%80%93NBA_merger" title="ABA–NBA merger">ABA–NBA merger</a> in 1976.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Oakland">In Oakland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virginia_Squires&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In Oakland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Oaks_(ABA)" title="Oakland Oaks (ABA)">Oakland Oaks (ABA)</a></div>
<p>The Squires were founded in 1967 as the <b>Oakland Oaks</b>, a charter member of the ABA. The team colors were green and gold. An earlier <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Oaks_(ABL)" title="Oakland Oaks (ABL)">Oakland Oaks</a> basketball team played in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=American_Basketball_League_1961%E2%80%9363&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="American Basketball League 1961–63 (page does not exist)">American Basketball League</a> in 1962.</p>
<p>The Oaks were owned in part by pop singer <a href="/wiki/Pat_Boone" title="Pat Boone">Pat Boone</a>. A major contract dispute with the cross-bay <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Warriors" title="San Francisco Warriors">San Francisco Warriors</a> of the established <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_Association" title="National Basketball Association">National Basketball Association</a> over the rights to star player <a href="/wiki/Rick_Barry" title="Rick Barry">Rick Barry</a>. He was a former NBA Rookie of the Year who had led the Warriors to the NBA finals in the same year the Oaks had formed, but due to being angered by management's failure to pay him certain incentive awards he felt he was due, he sat out the 1967–68 season, and the following season he joined the Oaks, leading the franchise to its one and only ABA championship in 1969.</p>
<p>However, even with Barry the team proved to be a very poor investment for Boone and his co-owners. Despite winning the ABA championship, the Oaks were an abysmal failure at the box office, due in large part to the proximity of the NBA Warriors who at the time were also playing some home games in Oakland (and would eventually move to Oakland in 1971). At one point they only drew 2,500 fans per game.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Washington">In Washington</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virginia_Squires&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In Washington">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Washington_Caps" title="Washington Caps">Washington Caps</a></div>
<p>Facing foreclosure on a loan from <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_America" title="Bank of America">Bank of America</a>, Boone sold the team to Washington, D.C. lawyer <a href="/wiki/Earl_Foreman" title="Earl Foreman">Earl Foreman</a>, who moved the team to Washington for the 1969–70 season as the <b>Washington Caps.</b> The team colors of green and gold were retained, but the logo was a red, white and blue rendition of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">United States Capitol</a>. They played at the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Coliseum" title="Washington Coliseum">Washington Coliseum</a>. However, for reasons that remain unknown, they remained in the Western Division—forcing them on the longest road trips in the league. Attendance was no better in Washington than it was in Oakland because the Coliseum was located in the North East Washington area, which was considered to be a bad neighborhood. Miraculously, they managed to finish four games above .500, but lost in the first round to the powerful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denver_Rockets" title="Denver Rockets">Denver Rockets</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Virginia">In Virginia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virginia_Squires&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In Virginia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Merger talks with the NBA were already underway, but a major stumbling block was the presence of the Caps in Washington. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltimore_Bullets_(1963%E2%80%9373)" title="Baltimore Bullets (1963–73)">Baltimore Bullets</a> owner <a href="/wiki/Abe_Pollin" title="Abe Pollin">Abe Pollin</a> wanted to move his team to Washington, but did not want the Caps there. The other ABA owners persuaded Foreman to move the Caps for the second time in as many seasons. Foreman decided to make the Caps a regional franchise, the Virginia Squires. The team would be based in <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk</a>, and also played home games in <a href="/wiki/Hampton,_Virginia" title="Hampton, Virginia">Hampton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">Richmond</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roanoke,_Virginia" title="Roanoke, Virginia">Roanoke</a>. However, Roanoke was dropped from the list of "home" cities after only one season. The Squires' colors were red, white, and blue.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rick_Barry" title="Rick Barry">Rick Barry</a>, who originally played with the inaugural Oaks, appeared on the August 24, 1970 front cover of Sports Illustrated in a Squires uniform; in the accompanying article inside the magazine, Barry made several negative remarks about the Commonwealth of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>. (He angered Southerners by remarking that he did not want his children to grow up saying, "Hi, y'all, Dad.") On September 1, 1970, the Squires traded Barry to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Nets" title="New York Nets">New York Nets</a> for a draft pick and $200,000. While the negative comments had been a contributing factor to the trade, it primarily came because Foreman was still bogged down by financial troubles and had to sell Barry to help meet his expenses.</p>
<p>The Squires played most of their games at <a href="/wiki/Old_Dominion_University" title="Old Dominion University">Old Dominion University</a>'s fieldhouse in their first season as a "regional" franchise, with other matches at the <a href="/wiki/Richmond_Arena" title="Richmond Arena">Richmond Arena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Coliseum" title="Hampton Coliseum">Hampton Coliseum</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roanoke_Civic_Center" title="Roanoke Civic Center">Roanoke Civic Center</a>. In spite of the initial controversy surrounding former player Barry, the Squires finished their inaugural season in Virginia by winning the Eastern Division by 11 games. They defeated the New York Nets in the first round of the ABA playoffs but went on to be upset by the <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Colonels" title="Kentucky Colonels">Kentucky Colonels</a>. In 1971, the Squires make their biggest draft pick ever by drafting <a href="/wiki/Julius_Erving" title="Julius Erving">Julius Erving</a> from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst" title="University of Massachusetts Amherst">University of Massachusetts Amherst</a>. During the 1971–72 season, Erving became an instant sensation with his scoring prowess and dazzling on-court acrobatics; the Squires defeated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Floridians" title="The Floridians">The Floridians</a> in the first round of the playoffs but lost to the New York Nets in the second round.</p>
<p>The 1972–73 season marked the beginning of the Virginia Squires downturn. Although blessed with a combination of Julius Erving ("Dr. J") and a young <a href="/wiki/George_Gervin" title="George Gervin">George Gervin</a>, the duo only played together late in the season. The Squires lost to their division rival Kentucky in the first round of the playoffs. During the summer of 1973, Dr. J was sold to the New York Nets for cash.</p>
<p>During the 1974 ABA All-Star Weekend, rumors abounded that Gervin was about to be sold to the <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio_Spurs" title="San Antonio Spurs">San Antonio Spurs</a>. These rumors became fact on January 30, when the Squires sold Gervin to the Spurs for $225,000. ABA commissioner <a href="/wiki/Mike_Storen" title="Mike Storen">Mike Storen</a> tried to block the sale on the grounds that selling the team's last true star was not in the best interest of the league. However, the sale was eventually upheld.</p>
<p>While the trades may have provided enough short-term financing to keep the Squires in business, the loss of so much talent angered the fans. The Squires' attendance fell through the floor and never recovered. The Squires' final two seasons in the ABA were forgettable as the losses mounted and popular coach <a href="/wiki/Al_Bianchi" title="Al Bianchi">Al Bianchi</a> was fired. The 1974–75 and 1975–76 teams went 15-69 (17.8%). It was the worst winning percentages in ABA history. The team was coming unraveled off the court as well. In 1974, <a href="/wiki/Barry_Parkhill" title="Barry Parkhill">Barry Parkhill</a> sued the team after his paychecks bounced. The Squires nearly shut down for good in February 1976, but only managed to stay afloat by a sale of advertising banners and a $250,000 loan from a local bank. Even if the Squires had been on stronger financial ground, they stood no chance of being included in any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ABA%E2%80%93NBA_merger" title="ABA–NBA merger">ABA–NBA merger</a>. "Regional" franchises were not considered viable, and none of the Squires' home cities were nearly big enough to support an NBA team.</p>
<p>On May 11, 1976—only a month after the end of the season—the ABA canceled the franchise after it missed a $75,000 assessment. This cost the Squires a chance to be compensated as part of the merger, which closed only a month later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
