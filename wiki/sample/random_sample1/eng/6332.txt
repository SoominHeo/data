<p><b>Sarah Virginia Wade</b>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OBE" title="OBE">OBE</a> (born 10 July 1945) is a former professional <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">tennis</a> player from Great Britain. She won three <a href="/wiki/Grand_Slam_(tennis)" title="Grand Slam (tennis)">Grand Slam</a> singles championships and four Grand Slam doubles championships, and is the only British woman in history to have won titles at all four Grand Slam tournaments. She was ranked as high as No. 2 in the world in singles, and No. 1 in the world in doubles. She won the women's singles championship at <a href="/wiki/The_Championships,_Wimbledon" title="The Championships, Wimbledon">Wimbledon</a> on 1 July 1977, in that tournament's centenary year, and was the last British tennis player to have won a Grand Slam singles tournament until <a href="/wiki/Andy_Murray" title="Andy Murray">Andy Murray</a> won the US Open in 2012. She remains the last British female to have won a Grand Slam singles title. After retiring from competitive tennis, she coached for four years<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and has also worked as a tennis commentator and game analyst for the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurosport" title="Eurosport">Eurosport</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virginia_Wade&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 10 July 1945, Wade was born in <a href="/wiki/Bournemouth" title="Bournemouth">Bournemouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. Her father was the <a href="/wiki/Archdeacon" title="Archdeacon">Archdeacon</a> of <a href="/wiki/Durban" title="Durban">Durban</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>At age one, Wade was moved to <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> with her parents. In <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, Wade learned to play tennis. When Wade was 15, the family moved back to England and she went to <a href="/wiki/Tunbridge_Wells_Girls%27_Grammar_School" title="Tunbridge Wells Girls' Grammar School">Tunbridge Wells Girls' Grammar School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Talbot_Heath_School" title="Talbot Heath School">Talbot Heath School</a>, Bournemouth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1961 Wade was in the tennis team of Wimbledon County Girls' Grammar School. She went on to study mathematics and physics at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Sussex" title="University of Sussex">University of Sussex</a>, graduating in 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>Wade's tennis career spanned the end of the amateur era and the start of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_tennis#Open_Era" title="History of tennis">open era</a>. In 1968, she scored two notable firsts. As an amateur, she won the inaugural open tennis competition — the British Hard Court Open at Bournemouth. She turned down the US$720 first prize. Five months later, she had turned professional and won the women's singles championship at the first <a href="/wiki/US_Open_(tennis)" title="US Open (tennis)">US Open</a> (and prize-money of $6,000)($41,322 today), defeating <a href="/wiki/Billie_Jean_King" title="Billie Jean King">Billie Jean King</a> in the final.</p>
<p>Wade's second <a href="/wiki/Grand_Slam_(tennis)" title="Grand Slam (tennis)">Grand Slam</a> singles championship came in 1972 at the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Open" title="Australian Open">Australian Open</a>. There, she defeated the Australian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evonne_Goolagong" title="Evonne Goolagong">Evonne Goolagong</a> in the final 6–4, 6–4.</p>
<p>Wade won at <a href="/wiki/The_Championships,_Wimbledon" title="The Championships, Wimbledon">Wimbledon</a>, in 1977. It was the 16th year in which Wade had played at Wimbledon, and she made her first appearance in the final by beating the defending champion <a href="/wiki/Chris_Evert" title="Chris Evert">Chris Evert</a> in a semifinal 6–2, 4–6, 6–1. In the final, she faced <a href="/wiki/Betty_St%C3%B6ve" title="Betty Stöve">Betty Stöve</a>. Not only was 1977 the 100th anniversary of the founding of the Wimbledon Championships, but it was also the 25th year of the reign (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silver_Jubilee" title="Silver Jubilee">Silver Jubilee</a>) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Elizabeth II of the United Kingdom">Queen Elizabeth II</a>. The queen attended the Wimbledon championships for the first time in 25 years to watch the final. Wade beat Stöve in three sets to claim the championship, nine days before her 32nd birthday.</p>
<p>She was the subject of <i><a href="/wiki/This_Is_Your_Life_(UK_TV_series)" title="This Is Your Life (UK TV series)">This Is Your Life</a></i> in December 1977 when she was surprised by <a href="/wiki/Eamonn_Andrews" title="Eamonn Andrews">Eamonn Andrews</a> at the National Sports Stadium at London's Crystal Palace.</p>
<p>Wade also won four Grand Slam women's doubles championships with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Margaret_Smith_Court" title="Margaret Smith Court">Margaret Smith Court</a> – two of them at the U.S. Open Tennis Tournament, one at the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Open" title="Australian Open">Australian Open</a>, and one at the <a href="/wiki/French_Open" title="French Open">French Open</a>.</p>
<p>Over her career, Wade won 55 professional singles championships and amassed $1,542,278 dollars in career prize money. She was ranked in the world's top 10 continuously from 1967 to 1979. Her career spanned a total of 26 years. She retired from singles competition at the end of the 1985 tennis season, and then from doubles at the end of 1986.</p>
<p>In 1983, at the age of 37, she won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rome_Masters" title="Rome Masters">Italian Open</a> women's doubles championship, along with her teammate <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Ruzici" title="Virginia Ruzici">Virginia Ruzici</a> of <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>.</p>
<p>The 26 times that Wade played at Wimbledon is an all-time record,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> 24 of those times being in the women's singles.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="After_tennis">After tennis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virginia_Wade&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: After tennis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 1981, while she was still playing, Wade has been a reporter on tennis events for the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_bio_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_bio-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1982, Wade became the first woman to be elected to the Wimbledon Committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1986, Wade was honoured with the distinction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Officer_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Officer of the Order of the British Empire">Officer of the Order of the British Empire</a> (OBE).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_bio_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_bio-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1989, Wade was also inducted into the <a href="/wiki/International_Tennis_Hall_of_Fame" title="International Tennis Hall of Fame">International Tennis Hall of Fame</a> in <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Rhode_Island" title="Newport, Rhode Island">Newport, Rhode Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
