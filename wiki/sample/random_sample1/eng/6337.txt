<p>The <b>Virtual Boy</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[a]</a></sup> is a 32-bit table-top video game console developed and manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a>. Released in 1995, it was marketed as the first console capable of displaying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereoscopic_3D" title="Stereoscopic 3D">stereoscopic 3D</a> graphics, a form of <a href="/wiki/Virtual_reality" title="Virtual reality">virtual reality</a>. The player uses the console in a manner similar to a <a href="/wiki/Head-mounted_display" title="Head-mounted display">head-mounted display</a>, placing their head against the eyepiece to see a red monochrome display. The games use a <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">parallax</a> effect to create the illusion of depth. Sales failed to meet targets, and by early 1996, Nintendo ceased distribution and game development after shipping 1.26 million units and releasing 22 games.</p>
<p>Development of the Virtual Boy lasted four years, and began originally under the project name of <b>VR32</b>. Nintendo entered a licensing agreement to utilize a 3D LED eyepiece technology originally developed by US-based company Reflection Technology. They also built a factory in China to be used exclusively for Virtual Boy manufacturing. Over the course of development, the console technology was down-scaled due to high costs and potential health concerns. Furthermore, an increasing amount of company resources were being re-allocated to <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_64" title="Nintendo 64">Nintendo 64</a> development. Lead Nintendo game designer, <a href="/wiki/Shigeru_Miyamoto" title="Shigeru Miyamoto">Shigeru Miyamoto</a>, had little involvement with the Virtual Boy software. The console was pushed to market in an unfinished state in 1995 to focus on Nintendo 64 development.</p>
<p>The Virtual Boy was panned by critics and a <a href="/wiki/List_of_commercial_failures_in_video_gaming" title="List of commercial failures in video gaming">commercial failure</a>. Its failure has been cited as due to its high price, monochrome display, unimpressive 3D effect, lack of true portability, health concerns, and low quality games. Its negative reception was unaffected by continued price drops. 3D technology in video game consoles re-emerged in later years to more success, including in Nintendo's own <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_3DS" title="Nintendo 3DS">3DS</a> handheld console. The Virtual Boy is Nintendo's second lowest-selling platform after the <a href="/wiki/64DD" title="64DD">64DD</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_Boy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_Boy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 1985, a red LED eyepiece display technology called Scanned Linear Array was developed by <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>-based Reflection Technology, Inc. (RTI).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GPro67_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-GPro67-3">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unraveling_the_Enigma_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Unraveling_the_Enigma-4">[3]</a></sup> The company produced a 3D stereoscopic head-tracking prototype called the Private Eye, featuring a tank game. Seeking funding and partnerships by which to develop it into a commercial technology, RTI demonstrated Private Eye to the consumer electronics market, including <a href="/wiki/Mattel" title="Mattel">Mattel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hasbro" title="Hasbro">Hasbro</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unraveling_the_Enigma_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Unraveling_the_Enigma-4">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">Sega</a> declined the technology, due to its single-color display and concerns about motion sickness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unraveling_the_Enigma_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Unraveling_the_Enigma-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nintendo enthusiastically received the Private Eye, as led by <a href="/wiki/Gunpei_Yokoi" title="Gunpei Yokoi">Gunpei Yokoi</a>, the general manager of Nintendo's <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Research_%26_Development_1" title="Nintendo Research &amp; Development 1">R&amp;D1</a> and the inventor of the <a href="/wiki/Game_%26_Watch" title="Game &amp; Watch">Game &amp; Watch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy" title="Game Boy">Game Boy</a> handheld consoles. He saw this as a unique technology that competitors would find difficult to emulate. Additionally, the resulting game console was intended to enhance Nintendo's reputation as an innovator<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unraveling_the_Enigma_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Unraveling_the_Enigma-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup> and to "encourage more creativity" in games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ultimate_History_of_Video_Games_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ultimate_History_of_Video_Games-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:514</sup> Codenaming the project "VR32",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unraveling_the_Enigma_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Unraveling_the_Enigma-4">[3]</a></sup> Nintendo entered into an exclusive agreement with Reflection Technology, Inc. to license the technology for its displays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GPro67_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-GPro67-3">[2]</a></sup> While Nintendo's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nintendo_Research_%26_Development_3" title="Nintendo Research &amp; Development 3">Research &amp; Development 3 division</a> (R&amp;D3) was focused on developing the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_64" title="Nintendo 64">Nintendo 64</a>, the other two engineering units were free to experiment with new product ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spending four years in development and eventually building a dedicated manufacturing plant in China,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unraveling_the_Enigma_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Unraveling_the_Enigma-4">[3]</a></sup> Nintendo worked to turn its VR32 vision into an affordable and health-conscious console design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup> Yokoi retained RTI's choice of red LED because it was the cheapest,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup> and because unlike a totally backlit LCD, its perfect blackness could achieve a more immersive sense of infinite depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unraveling_the_Enigma_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Unraveling_the_Enigma-4">[3]</a></sup> RTI and Nintendo said a color LCD system would have been prohibitively expensive,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unraveling_the_Enigma_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-Unraveling_the_Enigma-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rafferty_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rafferty-8">[7]</a></sup> retailing for more than <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$500</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ultimate_History_of_Video_Games_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ultimate_History_of_Video_Games-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:514</sup> A color LCD system was also said to have caused "jumpy images in tests".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rafferty_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rafferty-8">[7]</a></sup> With ongoing concerns about motion sickness, the risk of developing lazy eye conditions in young children, and Japan's new Product Liability Act of 1995, Nintendo eliminated the head tracking functionality and converted its headmounted goggle design into a stationary, heavy, precision steel-shielded, tabletop form factor conformant to the recommendation of the Schepens Eye Research Institute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unraveling_the_Enigma_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-Unraveling_the_Enigma-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ultimate_History_of_Video_Games_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ultimate_History_of_Video_Games-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:514</sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>[W]e experimented with a color LCD screen, but the users did not see depth, they just saw double. Color graphics give people the impression that a game is high tech. But just because a game has a beautiful display does not mean that the game is fun to play. ... Red uses less battery and red is easier to recognize. That is why red is used for traffic lights.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Gunpei Yokoi<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ultimate_History_of_Video_Games_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ultimate_History_of_Video_Games-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:514</sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>A number of technology demonstrations were used to show the Virtual Boy's capabilities. <i>Driving Demo</i> is one of the more advanced demos; its 30-second clip shows a first-person view of driving by road signs and palm trees. This demo was shown at <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Entertainment_Expo" title="Electronic Entertainment Expo">E3</a> and <a href="/wiki/Consumer_Electronics_Show" title="Consumer Electronics Show">CES</a> in 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Driving_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Driving-9">[8]</a></sup> The start-up screen of the Virtual Boy Prototype was shown at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shoshinkai" title="Shoshinkai">Shoshinkai</a> in 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mario_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mario-10">[9]</a></sup> The demo of what would have been a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_Fox_(SNES)" title="Star Fox (SNES)">Star Fox</a></i> game showed a <i>Star Fox</i>-like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arwing" title="Arwing">Arwing</a> doing various spins and motions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Star_Fox_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Star_Fox-11">[10]</a></sup> Cinematic camera angles were a key element, as they were in <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Fox_2" title="Star Fox 2">Star Fox 2</a></i>. It was shown at E3 and CES in 1995.</p>
<p>As a result of increasing competition for internal resources alongside the flagship Nintendo 64, Virtual Boy software development proceeded without the company's full attention, and with very little involvement by lead game designer <a href="/wiki/Shigeru_Miyamoto" title="Shigeru Miyamoto">Shigeru Miyamoto</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unraveling_the_Enigma_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-Unraveling_the_Enigma-4">[3]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/David_Sheff" title="David Sheff">David Sheff</a>'s book <i><a href="/wiki/Game_Over_(book)" title="Game Over (book)">Game Over</a></i>, the increasingly reticent Yokoi never actually intended for the increasingly downscaled console to be released in its final form. However, Nintendo pushed the Virtual Boy to market so that it could focus development resources on the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_64" title="Nintendo 64">Nintendo 64</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Game_Over_by_David_Sheff_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Game_Over_by_David_Sheff-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_Boy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> previewed the Virtual Boy on November 13, 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_Nintendo_counts_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Nintendo_counts-13">[12]</a></sup> The console was officially announced via press release the next day, November 14. Nintendo promised that Virtual Boy would "totally immerse players into their own private universe."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-press1_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-press1-14">[13]</a></sup> Initial press releases and interviews about the system focused on its technological capabilities, avoiding discussion of the actual games that would be released.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup> The system was formally unveiled the next day at the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Space_World" title="Nintendo Space World">Shoshinkai (初心会) Show</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nintendo_of_America" title="Nintendo of America">Nintendo of America</a> showed the Virtual Boy at the <a href="/wiki/Consumer_Electronics_Show" title="Consumer Electronics Show">Consumer Electronics Show</a> on January 6, 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-press1_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-press1-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Even with cost-saving measures in place, Nintendo priced the Virtual Boy at a relatively high <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$179.95</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unraveling_the_Enigma_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-Unraveling_the_Enigma-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ultimate_History_of_Video_Games_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ultimate_History_of_Video_Games-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:513</sup> Though slightly less expensive and significantly less powerful than a home console, this was considerably more costly than the Game Boy handheld. With seemingly more advanced graphics than Game Boy, the Virtual Boy was not intended to replace the handheld in Nintendo's product line, as use of the Virtual Boy requires a steady surface and completely blocks the player's peripheral vision. <i><a href="/wiki/Design_News" title="Design News">Design News</a></i> described the Virtual Boy as the logical evolution of the <a href="/wiki/View-Master" title="View-Master">View-Master</a> 3D image viewer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Virtual Boy was released on July 21, 1995<span style="display:none"> (<span class="bday dtstart published updated">1995-07-21</span>)</span> in Japan and on August 16, 1995<span style="display:none"> (<span class="bday dtstart published updated">1995-08-16</span>)</span> in North America<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> with the launch titles <i><a href="/wiki/Mario%27s_Tennis" title="Mario's Tennis">Mario's Tennis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Alarm" title="Red Alarm">Red Alarm</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Teleroboxer" title="Teleroboxer">Teleroboxer</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Galactic_Pinball" title="Galactic Pinball">Galactic Pinball</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAT_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-LAT-17">[16]</a></sup> It was not released in PAL markets. In North America, Nintendo shipped <i><a href="/wiki/Mario%27s_Tennis" title="Mario's Tennis">Mario's Tennis</a></i> with every Virtual Boy sold, as a <a href="/wiki/Pack-in_game" title="Pack-in game">pack-in game</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> Nintendo had initially projected sales of 3 million consoles and 14 million games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-press1_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-press1-14">[13]</a></sup> The system arrived later than other 32-bit systems from Sony, Panasonic, and Sega, but at a lower price.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_Super_Mario_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_Super_Mario-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the system's release, Nintendo of America projected hardware sales of 1.5 million units and software sales numbering 2.5 million by the end of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ-21">[20]</a></sup> Nintendo had shipped 350,000 units of the Virtual Boy by December 1995, around three and a half months after its North American release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ty_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ty-22">[21]</a></sup> The system made number 5 on <i><a href="/wiki/GamePro" title="GamePro">GamePro</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s the "Top 10 Worst Selling Consoles of All Time" list in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamepro_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamepro-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Virtual Boy had a short market timespan following its disappointing sales. The last official title to be released for the Virtual Boy was <i><a href="/wiki/3D_Tetris" title="3D Tetris">3D Tetris</a></i>, released on March 22, 1996<span style="display:none"> (<span class="bday dtstart published updated">1996-03-22</span>)</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> Nintendo announced additional titles for the system at the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Entertainment_Expo" title="Electronic Entertainment Expo">Electronic Entertainment Expo</a> in 1996, but these games were never released.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup> The Virtual Boy was discontinued in late 1995 in Japan and in early 1996 in North America. Nintendo discontinued the system without fanfare, avoiding an official press release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Promotion">Promotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_Boy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Promotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nintendo extensively advertised the Virtual Boy, and claimed to have spent <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>25 million on early promotional activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup> Advertising promoted the system as a paradigm shift from past consoles; some pieces used <a href="/wiki/Caveman" title="Caveman">cavemen</a> to indicate a historical evolution, while others utilized psychedelic imagery. Nintendo targeted an older audience with advertisements for the Virtual Boy, shifting away from the traditional child-focused approach it had employed in the past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-10"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was to be more than just another gaming console; Nintendo portrayed the system as a type of virtual reality, as its name indicates. Nintendo also focused on the technological aspects of the new console in its press releases, neglecting to detail specific games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-11"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Confronted with the challenge of showing 3-dimensional gameplay on 2-dimensional advertisements, the company partnered with <a href="/wiki/Blockbuster_LLC" title="Blockbuster LLC">Blockbuster</a> and <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> in a coordinated effort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-12"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GPro83_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-GPro83-24">[23]</a></sup> A $5 million campaign promoted NBC's fall lineup alongside the Virtual Boy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_addenda_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_addenda-25">[24]</a></sup> American viewers were encouraged via television advertisements on NBC to rent the console for US$10 at a local Blockbuster. This made it affordable for a large number of gamers to try the system,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-13"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup> and produced 750,000 rentals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mediaweek_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mediaweek-26">[25]</a></sup> Upon returning the unit, renters received a coupon for $10 off the purchase of a Virtual Boy from any store.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GPro83_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-GPro83-24">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BB_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-BB-27">[26]</a></sup> 3,000 Blockbuster locations were included in the promotion, which included a sweepstakes with prizes including trips to see the taping of NBC shows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_addenda_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_addenda-25">[24]</a></sup> Despite its popularity, the rental system proved harmful to the Virtual Boy's long-term success, allowing gamers to see just how un-immersive the console was.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-14"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup> Taken as a whole, the marketing campaign was commonly thought of as a failure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tomb_Space_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tomb_Space-28">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hardware">Hardware</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_Boy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hardware">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Virtual_Boy_hardware" title="Virtual Boy hardware">Virtual Boy hardware</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">central processing unit</a> is a 32-bit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RISC" title="RISC">RISC</a> chip,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GPro67_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-GPro67-3">[2]</a></sup> making the Virtual Boy Nintendo's first 32-bit system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_Super_Mario_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_Super_Mario-19">[18]</a></sup> The Virtual Boy system uses a pair of 1×224 linear arrays (one per eye) and rapidly scans the array across the eye's field of view using flat oscillating mirrors. These mirrors vibrate back and forth at a very high speed, thus the mechanical humming noise from inside the unit. Each Virtual Boy game cartridge has a yes/no option to automatically pause every 15–30 minutes so that the player may take a break before any injuries come to the eyes. One speaker per ear provides the player with audio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Powell_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Powell-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Display">Display</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_Boy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Display">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Virtual-Boy-Eyes.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2514" data-file-width="4040" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Virtual-Boy-Eyes.jpg/220px-Virtual-Boy-Eyes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Virtual-Boy-Eyes.jpg/330px-Virtual-Boy-Eyes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Virtual-Boy-Eyes.jpg/440px-Virtual-Boy-Eyes.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Virtual-Boy-Eyes.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The screens of the Virtual Boy</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Virtual Boy is the first video game console that was supposed to be capable of displaying stereoscopic 3D graphics, marketed as a form of virtual reality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup> Whereas most video games use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monocular_depth_cues" title="Monocular depth cues">monocular cues</a> to achieve the illusion of three dimensions on a two-dimensional screen, the Virtual Boy creates an illusion of depth through the effect known as <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">parallax</a>. In a manner similar to using a <a href="/wiki/Head-mounted_display" title="Head-mounted display">head-mounted display</a>, the user looks into an eyepiece made of <a href="/wiki/Neoprene" title="Neoprene">neoprene</a> on the front of the machine, and then an eyeglass-style projector allows viewing of the <a href="/wiki/Monochrome" title="Monochrome">monochromatic</a> (in this case, red) image. Nintendo claimed that a color display would have made the system too expensive and resulted in "jumpy" images, so the company opted for a monochrome display.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_Super_Mario_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_Super_Mario-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Control">Control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_Boy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Virtual-Boy-Controller.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2640" data-file-width="3120" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Virtual-Boy-Controller.jpg/220px-Virtual-Boy-Controller.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Virtual-Boy-Controller.jpg/330px-Virtual-Boy-Controller.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Virtual-Boy-Controller.jpg/440px-Virtual-Boy-Controller.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Virtual-Boy-Controller.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Virtual Boy controller</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Virtual Boy was meant to be used sitting down at a table,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_Nintendo_counts_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Nintendo_counts-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup> although Nintendo said it would release a harness for players to use while standing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_Super_Mario_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_Super_Mario-19">[18]</a></sup> One of the unique features of the controller is the extendable power supply that slides onto the back. It houses the six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AA_batteries" title="AA batteries">AA batteries</a> required to power the system. This can be substituted with a wall adapter, though a "slide-on" attachment is required for the switchout. Once the slide-on adapter is installed, a power adapter can be attached to provide constant power.</p>
<p>The Virtual Boy, being a system with heavy emphasis on three-dimensional movement, needed a controller that could operate along a Z axis. The Virtual Boy's controller was an attempt to implement dual digital "<a href="/wiki/D-pad" title="D-pad">D-pads</a>" to control elements in the aforementioned 3D environment. The controller itself is shaped like an "M" (similar to a <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_64_controller" title="Nintendo 64 controller">Nintendo 64 controller</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> One holds onto either side of the controller and the part that dips down in the middle contains the battery pack.</p>
<p>In more traditional 2-dimensional games, the two directional pads are interchangeable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup> For others with a more 3D environment, like <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Alarm" title="Red Alarm">Red Alarm</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/3D_Tetris" title="3D Tetris">3D Tetris</a></i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Teleroboxer" title="Teleroboxer">Teleroboxer</a></i>, each pad controls a different feature. The symmetry of the controller also allows <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left-handed" title="Left-handed">left-handed</a> gamers to reverse the controls (similar to the <a href="/wiki/Atari_Lynx" title="Atari Lynx">Atari Lynx</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Connectivity">Connectivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_Boy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Connectivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During development, Nintendo promised the ability to link systems for competitive play.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rafferty_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rafferty-8">[7]</a></sup> The system's EXT (extension) port, located on the underside of the system below the controller port, was never officially supported since no "official" multiplayer games were ever published, nor was an official link cable released. Although <i>Waterworld</i> and <i>Faceball</i> were intended to use the EXT port for multiplayer play, the multiplayer features in the former were removed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup> and the latter game was canceled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup></p>

<p>Nintendo initially showcased three games for the Virtual Boy. They planned to release three titles at launch, and two or three per month thereafter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rafferty_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Rafferty-8">[7]</a></sup> Given the system's short lifespan, only 22 games were released. Of them, 19 games were released in the Japanese market, while 14 were released in North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> Third party support was extremely limited compared to previous Nintendo platforms. According to <a href="/wiki/Gunpei_Yokoi" title="Gunpei Yokoi">Gunpei Yokoi</a>, Nintendo president <a href="/wiki/Hiroshi_Yamauchi" title="Hiroshi Yamauchi">Hiroshi Yamauchi</a> had dictated that only a select few third party developers be shown the Virtual Boy hardware prior to its formal unveiling, in order to limit the risk of poor-quality software appearing on the system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>When asked if Virtual Boy games were going to be available for download on the <a href="/wiki/Virtual_Console" title="Virtual Console">Virtual Console</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_3DS" title="Nintendo 3DS">Nintendo 3DS</a>, Nintendo of America President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reggie_Fils-Aime" title="Reggie Fils-Aime">Reggie Fils-Aime</a> said he could not answer, as he was unfamiliar with the platform. He noted that, given his lack of familiarity, he would be hard-pressed to make the case for inclusion of the games on the Virtual Console.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hobbyist community at <i>Planet Virtual Boy</i> has developed Virtual Boy software.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-15"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup> Two previously unreleased games, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bound_High" title="Bound High">Bound High</a></i> and the Japanese version of <i><a href="/wiki/MIDI_Maze" title="MIDI Maze">Faceball</a></i> (known as <i>NikoChan Battle</i>) were released.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_Boy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

</div>
<p>The Virtual Boy was overwhelmingly panned by critics and was a <a href="/wiki/List_of_commercial_failures_in_video_gaming" title="List of commercial failures in video gaming">commercial failure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup> The Virtual Boy failed for a number of reasons, among them "its high price, the discomfort caused by play [...] and what was widely judged to have been a poorly handled marketing campaign."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tomb_Space_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tomb_Space-28">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gamers who previewed the system at the Shoshinkai 1994 show complained that the <i>Mario</i> demo was not realistic enough, was not in full color, and didn't allow for "tracking" (the movement of the image when the player turns his or her head).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_Super_Mario_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_Super_Mario-19">[18]</a></sup> In the lead editorial of <i><a href="/wiki/Electronic_Gaming_Monthly" title="Electronic Gaming Monthly">Electronic Gaming Monthly</a></i> following the show, Ed Semrad predicted that the Virtual Boy would have poor launch sales due to the monochrome screen, lack of true portability, unimpressive lineup of games seen at the Shoshinkai show, and the price, which he argued was as low as it could get given the hardware but still too expensive for the experience the system offered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Next_Generation_(magazine)" title="Next Generation (magazine)">Next Generation</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s editors were also dubious of the Virtual Boy's prospects when they left the show, and concluded their article on the system by commenting, "But who will buy it? It's not portable, it's awkward to use, it's 100% antisocial (unlike multiplayer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SNES" title="SNES">SNES</a>/<a href="/wiki/Sega_Genesis" title="Sega Genesis">Genesis</a> games), it's too expensive and - most importantly - the 'VR' (i.e. 3D effect) doesn't add to the game at all: it's just a novelty."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> Following its release, reviews of the Virtual Boy tended to praise its novelty, but questioned its ultimate purpose and longtime viability. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Los_Angeles_Times" title="The Los Angeles Times">The Los Angeles Times</a></i> described gameplay as being "at once familiar and strange."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAT_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-LAT-17">[16]</a></sup> The column praised the quality of motion and immersive graphics, but considered the hardware itself tedious to use and non-portable. A later column by the same reviewer found the system to be somewhat <a href="/wiki/Asociality" title="Asociality">asocial</a>, although it held out hope for the console's future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAT2_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-LAT2-43">[42]</a></sup> Reviewing the system shortly after its North American launch, <i>Next Generation</i> said, "Unusual and innovative, the Virtual Boy can be seen as a gamble in the same way that the <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy" title="Game Boy">Game Boy</a> was, but it's a lot harder to see the VB succeeding to the same world-conquering extent that the Game Boy did." They elaborated that while the sharp display and unique 3D effect are impressive, aspects such as the monochrome display and potential vision damage to young gamers severely limit the system's appeal. They added that the software library was decent, but failed to capitalize on Nintendo's best-selling franchises (Zelda and Metroid games were absent, and the Mario games were not in the same style as the series's most successful installments) and lacked a system seller to compare with the Game Boy's <i><a href="/wiki/Tetris_(Game_Boy)" title="Tetris (Game Boy)">Tetris</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NGen12_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-NGen12-44">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Nintendo had promised a virtual reality experience, the monochrome display limited the Virtual Boy's potential for immersion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-16"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NGen12_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-NGen12-44">[43]</a></sup> Reviewers often considered the 3-dimensional features a gimmick, added to games that were essentially 2-<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-17"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup> or even 1-dimensional.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WaPo_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-WaPo-45">[44]</a></sup> <i>The Washington Post</i> felt that, even when a game gives the impression of 3-dimensionality, it suffers from "hollow vector graphics."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WaPo_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-WaPo-45">[44]</a></sup> Yokoi, the system's inventor, said the system did best with action and puzzle games,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NGen12_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-NGen12-44">[43]</a></sup> although those types of games provided only minimal immersion. Multiple critics<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-18"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tomb_Space_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tomb_Space-28">[27]</a></sup> lamented the absence of head-tracking in the Virtual Boy hardware. Critics found that, as a result, players were unable to immerse themselves in the game worlds of Virtual Boy games. Instead, they interacted with the fictional worlds in the manner of any traditional 2-dimensional game (that is, via a controller). Boyer said the console "struggles to merge the two distinct media forms of home consoles and virtual reality devices." While the device employed virtual reality techniques, it did so via the traditional home console. No feedback from the body was incorporated into gameplay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-19"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many reviewers complained of painful and frustrating physiological symptoms when playing the Virtual Boy. Bill Frischling, writing for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i>, experienced "dizziness, nausea and headaches."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WaPo_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-WaPo-45">[44]</a></sup> Reviewers attributed the problems to both the monochromatic display and uncomfortable ergonomics. Several prominent scientists concluded that the long-term side effects could be more serious, and articles published in magazines such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_Engineering_Times" title="Electronic Engineering Times">Electronic Engineering Times</a></i> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CMP_Media" title="CMP Media">CMP Media</a>'s <i>TechWeb</i> speculated that using the Virtual Boy (or any VR headset) could cause sickness, <a href="/wiki/Flashback_(psychology)" title="Flashback (psychology)">flashbacks</a>, and even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permanent_brain_damage" title="Permanent brain damage">permanent brain damage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> Nintendo, in the years after Virtual Boy's demise, has been frank about its failure. <a href="/wiki/Howard_Lincoln" title="Howard Lincoln">Howard Lincoln</a>, chairman of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nintendo_of_America" title="Nintendo of America">Nintendo of America</a>, said flatly that the Virtual Boy "just failed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-20"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_Boy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to <i>Game Over</i>, Nintendo laid blame for the machine's faults directly on its creator, Gunpei Yokoi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Game_Over_by_David_Sheff_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Game_Over_by_David_Sheff-12">[11]</a></sup> The commercial demise of the Virtual Boy was said by members of the video game press to be a contributing factor to Yokoi's withdrawal from Nintendo, despite the fact that he had planned to retire years before and finished another more successful project for the company, the Game Boy Pocket, which was released shortly before his leave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-N-Sider_Profiles_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-N-Sider_Profiles-47">[46]</a></sup> Nevertheless, <i>The New York Times</i> maintained that Yokoi kept a close relationship with Nintendo<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTimes_-_Gunpei_Yokoi.2C_Chief_Designer_Of_Game_Boy.2C_Is_Dead_at_56_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes_-_Gunpei_Yokoi.2C_Chief_Designer_Of_Game_Boy.2C_Is_Dead_at_56-48">[47]</a></sup> After leaving Nintendo, Yokoi founded his own company, Koto, and collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Bandai" title="Bandai">Bandai</a> to create the <a href="/wiki/WonderSwan" title="WonderSwan">WonderSwan</a>, a handheld system competing with the Game Boy.</p>
<p>The commercial failure of the Virtual Boy reportedly did little to alter Nintendo's development approach and focus on innovation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-21"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup> While the console itself is said to have failed in many regards, its focus on peripherals and haptic technology reemerged in later years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Velvet_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Velvet-49">[48]</a></sup> Development of virtual reality gaming platforms has continued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_6-22"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-6">[5]</a></sup> Because Nintendo only shipped 1.26 million Virtual Boy units worldwide, it is considered a valuable collector's item.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-wired-50">[49]</a></sup> The original inventor, Reflection Technology, Inc., was reportedly financially "devastated" by the Virtual Boy's performance, with dwindling operations by 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unraveling_the_Enigma_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-Unraveling_the_Enigma-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the launch of the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_3DS" title="Nintendo 3DS">Nintendo 3DS</a> console in 2011, Nintendo released a handheld gaming console with autostereoscopic 3D visuals. In other words, this console produces the desired depth effects without any special glasses and is portable. In the period leading up to the release of the Nintendo 3DS, <a href="/wiki/Shigeru_Miyamoto" title="Shigeru Miyamoto">Shigeru Miyamoto</a> discussed his view of the issues with the Virtual Boy. One was the actual use of the three-dimensional effects; while it was designed to render wireframe graphics, the effects are generally used to separate two-dimensional games into different planes separated by depth. Further, Miyamoto stated that the graphics are not as appealing, and while developing the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_64" title="Nintendo 64">Nintendo 64</a>, had ruled out the use of wireframe graphics as too sparse to draw player characters. Finally, he stated that he perceived the Virtual Boy as a novelty that should not have used the Nintendo license so prominently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup> In a 2014 interview with IGN, Miyamoto stated that Nintendo was working on a new virtual reality console based on 3DS technology.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, in February 2016, <a href="/wiki/Tatsumi_Kimishima" title="Tatsumi Kimishima">Tatsumi Kimishima</a> stated that Nintendo was "looking into" virtual reality but also explained that it would take more time and effort for them to asses the technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Reggie_Fils-Aim%C3%A9" title="Reggie Fils-Aimé">Reggie Fils-Aimé</a> also stated in a June 2016 interview with Bloomberg that the virtual reality market needs to become mainstream in order for Nintendo to jump in.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_Boy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several references to the Virtual Boy are related to the Nintendo 3DS game <i><a href="/wiki/Tomodachi_Life" title="Tomodachi Life">Tomodachi Life</a></i> (<i>Tomodachi Collection: New Life</i> in Japan). In the Localization Trailer for the game, there is a clip of Nintendo staff (personified as in-game Miis) dancing around a Virtual Boy, exclaiming: "All Hail The Virtual Boy!".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tomodachi_Life_Direct_4.10.14_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tomodachi_Life_Direct_4.10.14-54">[53]</a></sup> Upon buying or selling a Virtual Boy, the shopkeeper says "dig the red and black styling". A Virtual Boy can be an opponent in the minigame <i>Tomodachi Quest</i>.</p>
<p>The Virtual Boy makes a cameo appearance in the <a href="/wiki/GameCube" title="GameCube">GameCube</a> game, <i><a href="/wiki/Super_Smash_Bros._Melee" title="Super Smash Bros. Melee">Super Smash Bros. Melee</a></i> as one of the many Nintendo systems that can be seen on the shelves in the Trophy Collection if the game's language is set to Japanese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSBM_at_PVB_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-SSBM_at_PVB-55">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Super_Smash_Bros._Brawl" title="Super Smash Bros. Brawl">Super Smash Bros. Brawl</a></i>, all of the games that were manufactured for the Virtual Boy in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> appear in the form of a list within the chronicle section.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[55]</a></sup></p>
