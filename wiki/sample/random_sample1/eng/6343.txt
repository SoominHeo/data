<p><b>Burt Freeman Bacharach</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ər/ 'er' in 'finger'">ər</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">BAK</span></span></b>-ə-rak</i></a></span>; born May 12, 1928) is an American composer, songwriter, record producer, pianist, and singer. A six-time <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> winner<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and three-time <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a> winner,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> he is known for his popular hit songs and compositions from the late 1950s through the 1980s, many with lyrics written by <a href="/wiki/Hal_David" title="Hal David">Hal David</a>.</p>
<p>Most of Bacharach &amp; David hits were written specifically for and performed by <a href="/wiki/Dionne_Warwick" title="Dionne Warwick">Dionne Warwick</a>, but earlier associations (from 1957–63) saw the composing duo work with <a href="/wiki/Marty_Robbins" title="Marty Robbins">Marty Robbins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perry_Como" title="Perry Como">Perry Como</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gene_McDaniels" title="Gene McDaniels">Gene McDaniels</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Butler" title="Jerry Butler">Jerry Butler</a>. Following the initial success of these collaborations, Bacharach went on to write hits for <a href="/wiki/Gene_Pitney" title="Gene Pitney">Gene Pitney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cilla_Black" title="Cilla Black">Cilla Black</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dusty_Springfield" title="Dusty Springfield">Dusty Springfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jackie_DeShannon" title="Jackie DeShannon">Jackie DeShannon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bobbie_Gentry" title="Bobbie Gentry">Bobbie Gentry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Jones_(singer)" title="Tom Jones (singer)">Tom Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herb_Alpert" title="Herb Alpert">Herb Alpert</a>, <a href="/wiki/B._J._Thomas" title="B. J. Thomas">B. J. Thomas</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Carpenters" title="The Carpenters">The Carpenters</a>, among numerous other artists.</p>
<p>As of 2014<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Burt_Bacharach&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Bacharach had written 73 US and 52 UK <a href="/wiki/Top_40" title="Top 40">Top 40</a> hits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-house_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-house-4">[4]</a></sup> Songs that he co-wrote which have topped the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billboard_Top_100" title="Billboard Top 100"><i>Billboard</i> Top 100</a> include "<a href="/wiki/This_Guy%27s_in_Love_with_You" title="This Guy's in Love with You">This Guy's in Love with You</a>" (1968), "<a href="/wiki/Raindrops_Keep_Fallin%27_on_My_Head" title="Raindrops Keep Fallin' on My Head">Raindrops Keep Fallin' on My Head</a>" (1969), "<a href="/wiki/Arthur%27s_Theme_(Best_That_You_Can_Do)" title="Arthur's Theme (Best That You Can Do)">Arthur's Theme (Best That You Can Do)</a>" (1981), "<a href="/wiki/Songbird_(Kenny_G_composition)" title="Songbird (Kenny G composition)">Songbird</a>" (1986), "<a href="/wiki/Silhouette_(Kenny_G_instrumental)" title="Silhouette (Kenny G instrumental)">Silhouette</a>" (1988), "<a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Make_Me_Wait_for_Love" title="Don't Make Me Wait for Love">Don't Make Me Wait for Love</a>" (1986), "<a href="/wiki/Going_Home_(Kenny_G_composition)" title="Going Home (Kenny G composition)">Going Home</a>" (1989), "<a href="/wiki/The_Look_of_Love_(1967_song)" title="The Look of Love (1967 song)">The Look of Love</a>", (1967) and "<a href="/wiki/On_My_Own_(Patti_LaBelle_and_Michael_McDonald_song)" title="On My Own (Patti LaBelle and Michael McDonald song)">On My Own</a>" (1986).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burt_Bacharach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burt_Bacharach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bacharach was born in <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City,_Missouri" title="Kansas City, Missouri">Kansas City</a>, Missouri, and grew up in the <a href="/wiki/Forest_Hills,_Queens" title="Forest Hills, Queens">Forest Hills</a> section of New York City, graduating from <a href="/wiki/Forest_Hills_High_School_(New_York)" title="Forest Hills High School (New York)">Forest Hills High School</a> in 1946. He is the son of Irma M. (née Freeman) and Mark Bertram "Bert" Bacharach, a well-known syndicated newspaper columnist,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-film_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-film-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> His family was <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jewish</a>, he writes in his biography, and adds that "no one in my family went to synagogue or paid much attention to being Jewish. . . . but the kids I knew were Catholic. . . I was Jewish but I didn't want anybody to know about it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bacharach showed a keen interest in <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> as a teenager, disliking his classical piano lessons, and often using <a href="/wiki/Identity_document_forgery" title="Identity document forgery">fake ID</a> to gain admission into <a href="/wiki/52nd_Street_(Manhattan)" title="52nd Street (Manhattan)">52nd Street</a> nightclubs such as <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Spotlite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Spotlite (page does not exist)">Spotlite</a></i>, and listened avidly to bebop musicians such as <a href="/wiki/Dizzy_Gillespie" title="Dizzy Gillespie">Dizzy Gillespie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Parker" title="Charlie Parker">Charlie Parker</a>, who were a major influence on him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jazz_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-jazz-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bacharach studied music (Bachelor of Music, 1948) at <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>'s <a href="/wiki/McGill_University" title="McGill University">McGill University</a>, under Helmut Blume, at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mannes_College_of_Music" title="Mannes College of Music">Mannes School of Music</a>, and at the <a href="/wiki/Music_Academy_of_the_West" title="Music Academy of the West">Music Academy of the West</a> in <a href="/wiki/Montecito,_California" title="Montecito, California">Montecito</a>, California. During this period he studied a range of music, including <a href="/wiki/Jazz_harmony" title="Jazz harmony">jazz harmony</a>, which has since been important to songs which are generally considered pop music. His composition teachers included <a href="/wiki/Darius_Milhaud" title="Darius Milhaud">Darius Milhaud</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cowell" title="Henry Cowell">Henry Cowell</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pc24_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-pc24-9">[9]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Bohuslav_Martin%C5%AF" title="Bohuslav Martinů">Bohuslav Martinů</a>. Bacharach cites Milhaud as his biggest influence and has said, "Before I went into the service during the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a> I studied with Milhaud at the Music Academy of the West which was a summer program. I wrote a 'Sonatina for Violin, Oboe and Piano.' There were five in the class, and we were writing in a modern style. I had been hanging out in New York with <a href="/wiki/John_Cage" title="John Cage">John Cage</a>. The middle section of my piece was very melodic, and I worried that it was too melodic. Milhaud said, 'Never be afraid to be melodic.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jazz_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-jazz-8">[8]</a></sup> Following a tour of duty in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a>, Bacharach worked as a pianist, both as a soloist and as an accompanist for singers such as <a href="/wiki/Vic_Damone" title="Vic Damone">Vic Damone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polly_Bergen" title="Polly Bergen">Polly Bergen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Lawrence" title="Steve Lawrence">Steve Lawrence</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ames_Brothers" title="Ames Brothers">Ames Brothers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paula_Stewart" title="Paula Stewart">Paula Stewart</a> (who became his first wife).</p>
<p>For many years, he was the musical arranger for <a href="/wiki/Marlene_Dietrich" title="Marlene Dietrich">Marlene Dietrich</a>, as well as touring as her musical director.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_songwriting_work">Early songwriting work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burt_Bacharach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early songwriting work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1957, Bacharach and lyricist <a href="/wiki/Hal_David" title="Hal David">Hal David</a> met while at the <a href="/wiki/Brill_Building" title="Brill Building">Brill Building</a> (which Bacharach described as a "music factory") in New York City, and began their writing partnership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burt_Bacharach_remembers_Hal_David_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burt_Bacharach_remembers_Hal_David-10">[10]</a></sup> Almost a year later, they received a significant career breakthrough when their song "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Story_of_My_Life_(Burt_Bacharach_and_Hal_David_song)" title="The Story of My Life (Burt Bacharach and Hal David song)">The Story of My Life</a>" was recorded by <a href="/wiki/Marty_Robbins" title="Marty Robbins">Marty Robbins</a> for <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a>, becoming a number 1 hit on the U.S. country music chart and reaching #15 on the Billboard Hot 100 in late 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pc24_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-pc24-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>Soon afterwards, "<a href="/wiki/Magic_Moments" title="Magic Moments">Magic Moments</a>" was recorded by <a href="/wiki/Perry_Como" title="Perry Como">Perry Como</a> for <a href="/wiki/RCA_Records" title="RCA Records">RCA Records</a>, and became a number 4 U.S. hit in February of that year. These two songs were back-to-back No. 1 singles in the UK ("The Story of My Life" in a version by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Holliday" title="Michael Holliday">Michael Holliday</a>), giving Bacharach and David the honor of being the first songwriters to have written consecutive No. 1 UK singles. Bacharach also worked with other lyricists at first, including <a href="/wiki/Bob_Hilliard" title="Bob Hilliard">Bob Hilliard</a> and Hal David's brother, <a href="/wiki/Mack_David" title="Mack David">Mack David</a> but he and Hal David decided to form an exclusive writing partnership together in 1963. Bacharach has said what really got his career going, was when <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Carter" title="Calvin Carter">Calvin Carter</a>,who at the time was chief of A&amp;R at <a href="/wiki/Vee-Jay_Records" title="Vee-Jay Records">Vee-Jay Records</a>, called saying that <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Butler" title="Jerry Butler">Jerry Butler</a> wanted to do his song <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Make_it_Easy_on_Yourself" title="Make it Easy on Yourself">Make it Easy on Yourself</a> and asked him to come out to New York and take charge of recording the song. It was the first time that Bacharach was in control of the whole recording process of one of his own songs. He says "I just went from there" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early and mid-1960s, Bacharach wrote well over a hundred songs with David. He produced a number of songs on New York soul singer <a href="/wiki/Lou_Johnson_(singer)" title="Lou Johnson (singer)">Lou Johnson</a>, including the original recordings of "Always Something There To Remind Me", "Kentucky Bluebird (Message To Martha)" and "Reach Out For Me", but the two were mainly associated throughout the decade with <a href="/wiki/Dionne_Warwick" title="Dionne Warwick">Dionne Warwick</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/College_or_university_school_of_music" title="College or university school of music">conservatory</a>-trained vocalist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pc24_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-pc24-9">[9]</a></sup> Bacharach and David started writing a large portion of their work with Warwick in mind, leading to one of the most successful teams in popular music history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-collection_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-collection-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Over a 20-year period, beginning in the early 1960s, Warwick charted 38 singles co-written or produced by Bacharach and David, including 22 Top 40, 12 Top 20 and nine Top 10 hits on the American <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> charts. During the early 1960s, Bacharach also collaborated with Bob Hilliard on a number of songs, including "Please Stay" and "Mexican Divorce" for <a href="/wiki/The_Drifters" title="The Drifters">The Drifters</a>, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Any_Day_Now_(1962_song)" title="Any Day Now (1962 song)">Any Day Now</a>" for <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Jackson" title="Chuck Jackson">Chuck Jackson</a>, "<a href="/wiki/Tower_of_Strength_(Gene_McDaniels_song)" title="Tower of Strength (Gene McDaniels song)">Tower of Strength</a>" for <a href="/wiki/Gene_McDaniels" title="Gene McDaniels">Gene McDaniels</a>, and "Dreamin' All the Time" and "Pick Up the Pieces" for <a href="/wiki/Jack_Jones_(singer)" title="Jack Jones (singer)">Jack Jones</a>.</p>
<p>Bacharach released his first solo album in 1965 on the <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Records" title="Liberty Records">Liberty Records</a> label. "Hit Maker! Burt Bacharach Plays His Hits" was largely ignored in the US but rose to #3 on the UK album charts, where his version of "<a href="/wiki/Trains_and_Boats_and_Planes" title="Trains and Boats and Planes">Trains and Boats and Planes</a>" had become a top 5 single. In 1967, Bacharach signed as an artist with A&amp;M Records, recording a mix of new material and re-arrangements of his best-known songs. He recorded for A&amp;M until 1978.</p>
<p>Other singers of Bacharach songs in the '60s and '70s included <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Vinton" title="Bobby Vinton">Bobby Vinton</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Blue_on_Blue_(song)" title="Blue on Blue (song)">Blue on Blue</a>"); Dusty Springfield ("<a href="/wiki/The_Look_of_Love_(1967_song)" title="The Look of Love (1967 song)">The Look of Love</a>" from <a href="/wiki/Casino_Royale_(1967_film)" title="Casino Royale (1967 film)">Casino Royale</a>), (a cover of Dionne Warwick's "Wishin' and Hopin<span style="padding-right:.2em;">'</span>"); <a href="/wiki/Cilla_Black" title="Cilla Black">Cilla Black</a> (a cover of <a href="/wiki/Dionne_Warwick" title="Dionne Warwick">Dionne Warwick</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Anyone_Who_Had_a_Heart_(song)" title="Anyone Who Had a Heart (song)">Anyone Who Had a Heart</a>"), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delfonics" title="Delfonics">Delfonics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cher" title="Cher">Cher</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Alfie_(song)" title="Alfie (song)">Alfie</a>" - originally recorded by Cilla Black); <a href="/wiki/The_Shirelles" title="The Shirelles">The Shirelles</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Baby_It%27s_You" title="Baby It's You">Baby, It's You</a>"); <a href="/wiki/The_Carpenters" title="The Carpenters">The Carpenters</a> ("<a href="/wiki/(They_Long_to_Be)_Close_to_You" title="(They Long to Be) Close to You">(They Long to Be) Close to You</a>"); <a href="/wiki/Aretha_Franklin" title="Aretha Franklin">Aretha Franklin</a> ("I Say a Little Prayer"); <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Hayes" title="Isaac Hayes">Isaac Hayes</a> ("Walk on By", from the <i>Hot Buttered Soul</i> album); <a href="/wiki/B._J._Thomas" title="B. J. Thomas">B. J. Thomas</a> ("Raindrops Keep Falling on My Head", "Everybody's Out of Town"); <a href="/wiki/Tom_Jones_(singer)" title="Tom Jones (singer)">Tom Jones</a> ("<a href="/wiki/What%27s_New_Pussycat%3F_(song)" title="What's New Pussycat? (song)">What's New Pussycat?</a>"); <a href="/wiki/Engelbert_Humperdinck_(singer)" title="Engelbert Humperdinck (singer)">Engelbert Humperdinck</a> ("I'm a Better Man"); Sandie Shaw ("Always Something There to Remind Me"); <a href="/wiki/Jack_Jones_(singer)" title="Jack Jones (singer)">Jack Jones</a> ("Wives and Lovers"); <a href="/wiki/Jackie_DeShannon" title="Jackie DeShannon">Jackie DeShannon</a> ("What the World Needs Now Is Love"); <a href="/wiki/Gene_Pitney" title="Gene Pitney">Gene Pitney</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Only_Love_Can_Break_a_Heart" title="Only Love Can Break a Heart">Only Love Can Break a Heart</a>", "<a href="/wiki/(The_Man_Who_Shot)_Liberty_Valance" title="(The Man Who Shot) Liberty Valance">(The Man Who Shot) Liberty Valance</a>", "24 Hours from Tulsa" and "True Love Never Runs Smooth"); <a href="/wiki/Herb_Alpert" title="Herb Alpert">Herb Alpert</a>, ("This Guy's in Love with You");<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pc24_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-pc24-9">[9]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sergio_Mendes" title="Sergio Mendes">Sergio Mendes</a> &amp; Brasil '66 ("The Look of Love"); <a href="/wiki/The_Stylistics" title="The Stylistics">The Stylistics</a>, ("You'll Never Get To Heaven If you Break My Heart"); <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jerry_Butler_(singer)" title="Jerry Butler (singer)">Jerry Butler</a>, the Walker Brothers ("Make It Easy on Yourself"); and the Fifth Dimension ("One Less Bell to Answer").</p>
<p>Although Bacharach's compositions are typically more complex than the average pop song, he has expressed surprise in the fact that many jazz musicians have sought much inspiration from his works, saying "I've sometimes felt that my songs are restrictive for a jazz artist. I was excited when [Stan] Getz did a whole album of my music (<i>What The World Needs Now: Stan Getz Plays The Burt Bacharach Songbook</i>, Verve, 1968) but you're never 100 percent sure of how good you are even after you've had success. But one night I had dinner with <a href="/wiki/Miles_Davis" title="Miles Davis">Miles Davis</a> and he said, '"Alfie," that's a great song.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jazz_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-jazz-8">[8]</a></sup> Bacharach songs were adapted by jazz artists of the time, such as <a href="/wiki/Stan_Getz" title="Stan Getz">Stan Getz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cal_Tjader" title="Cal Tjader">Cal Tjader</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wes_Montgomery" title="Wes Montgomery">Wes Montgomery</a>. The Bacharach/David composition "My Little Red Book", originally recorded by <a href="/wiki/Manfred_Mann" title="Manfred Mann">Manfred Mann</a> for the film <i><a href="/wiki/What%27s_New_Pussycat%3F" title="What's New Pussycat?">What's New Pussycat?</a></i>, and promptly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cover_song" title="Cover song">covered</a> by <a href="/wiki/Love_(band)" title="Love (band)">Love</a> in 1966, has become a rock standard; however, according to <a href="/wiki/Robin_Platts" title="Robin Platts">Robin Platts</a>' book "Burt Bacharach and Hal David", the composer did not like Love's version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The title of the song is likely a <a href="/wiki/Tongue-in-cheek" title="Tongue-in-cheek">tongue-in-cheek</a> reference to <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao Zedong</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quotations_from_Chairman_Mao" title="Quotations from Chairman Mao">Little Red Book</a>, which was first published by the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Communist Party of China</a> in April 1964.</p>
<p>Bacharach composed and arranged the soundtrack of the 1967 film <i><a href="/wiki/Casino_Royale_(1967_film)" title="Casino Royale (1967 film)">Casino Royale</a>,</i> which included "The Look of Love", performed by Dusty Springfield, and the title song, an instrumental Top 40 single for Herb Alpert and the Tijuana Brass. Bacharach and David also collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a> producer <a href="/wiki/David_Merrick" title="David Merrick">David Merrick</a> on the 1968 musical <i><a href="/wiki/Promises,_Promises_(musical)" title="Promises, Promises (musical)">Promises, Promises</a></i>, which yielded two hits, the title tune and "I'll Never Fall in Love Again", for Dionne Warwick. The year 1969 marked, perhaps, the most successful Bacharach-David collaboration, the Oscar-winning "Raindrops Keep Falling on My Head", written for and prominently featured in the acclaimed film, <i><a href="/wiki/Butch_Cassidy_and_the_Sundance_Kid" title="Butch Cassidy and the Sundance Kid">Butch Cassidy and the Sundance Kid</a></i>.</p>
<p>The two were awarded a Grammy for Best Cast album of the year for "Promises, Promises" and the score was also nominated for a Tony award. There were other Oscar nominations for Best Song for "The Look Of Love", "<a href="/wiki/What%27s_New_Pussycat%3F_(song)" title="What's New Pussycat? (song)">What's New Pussycat</a>" and "Alfie".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burt_Bacharach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bacharach's music is characterized by unusual chord progressions, influenced by his background in jazz harmony, with striking syncopated rhythmic patterns, irregular phrasing, frequent modulation, and odd, changing meters. Bacharach has arranged, conducted, and produced much of his recorded output.</p>
<p>An example of his distinctive use of changing meter is found in "Promises, Promises" (from his score for the musical of the same name). His style is sometimes also associated with particular instrumental combinations he is assumed to favor or to have favored, including the prominent use of the <a href="/wiki/Flugelhorn" title="Flugelhorn">flugelhorn</a> in such works as "Walk on By", "Nikki", and "Toledo".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s_and_1980s">1970s and 1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burt_Bacharach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1970s and 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Throughout the late 1960s and early 1970s, Bacharach continued to write and produce for artists, compose for stage, TV, and film, and release his own albums. He enjoyed a great deal of visibility in the public spotlight, appearing frequently on TV (hosting several variety specials under his own name) and performing live in concert.</p>
<p>In 1973, Bacharach and David were commissioned to score the <a href="/wiki/Ross_Hunter" title="Ross Hunter">Ross Hunter</a>-produced <a href="/wiki/Lost_Horizon_(1973_film)" title="Lost Horizon (1973 film)">revival</a> of the 1937 film, <i><a href="/wiki/Lost_Horizon_(1937_film)" title="Lost Horizon (1937 film)">Lost Horizon</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a>. The film was a critical and commercial disaster and a flurry of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lawsuits" title="Lawsuits">lawsuits</a> resulted between the composer and the lyricist, as well as from Warwick. She reportedly felt abandoned when Bacharach and David refused to work together. Bacharach tried several solo projects (including the 1977 album <i>Futures</i>), but the projects failed to yield hits. He and David reunited briefly in 1975 to write and produce <a href="/wiki/Stephanie_Mills" title="Stephanie Mills">Stephanie Mills</a>'s second album <i><a href="/wiki/For_the_First_Time_(Stephanie_Mills_album)" title="For the First Time (Stephanie Mills album)">For the First Time</a></i> released on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motown_Records" title="Motown Records">Motown Records</a>.</p>
<p>By the early 1980s, Bacharach's marriage to <a href="/wiki/Angie_Dickinson" title="Angie Dickinson">Angie Dickinson</a> had ended, but a new partnership with lyricist <a href="/wiki/Carole_Bayer_Sager" title="Carole Bayer Sager">Carole Bayer Sager</a> proved rewarding, both commercially and personally. The two married and collaborated on several major hits during the decade, including "<a href="/wiki/Arthur%27s_Theme_(Best_That_You_Can_Do)" title="Arthur's Theme (Best That You Can Do)">Arthur's Theme (Best That You Can Do)</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Christopher_Cross" title="Christopher Cross">Christopher Cross</a>), co-written with Cross and Peter Allen; "<a href="/wiki/Heartlight_(song)" title="Heartlight (song)">Heartlight</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Neil_Diamond" title="Neil Diamond">Neil Diamond</a>); "<a href="/wiki/Making_Love_(song)" title="Making Love (song)">Making Love</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Roberta_Flack" title="Roberta Flack">Roberta Flack</a>); "<a href="/wiki/On_My_Own_(Patti_LaBelle_and_Michael_McDonald_song)" title="On My Own (Patti LaBelle and Michael McDonald song)">On My Own</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Patti_LaBelle" title="Patti LaBelle">Patti LaBelle</a> with <a href="/wiki/Michael_McDonald_(singer)" title="Michael McDonald (singer)">Michael McDonald</a>), and perhaps most memorably, "<a href="/wiki/That%27s_What_Friends_Are_For" title="That's What Friends Are For">That's What Friends Are For</a>" in 1985, actually the second single which reunited Bacharach and singer Warwick. The profits for the latter song were given to AIDS research. Bacharach's 1980s tunes showed a new sound.</p>
<p>Other artists continued to revive Bacharach's earlier hits in the 1980s and 1990s. Examples included Luther Vandross' recording of "A House is Not a Home"; <a href="/wiki/Naked_Eyes" title="Naked Eyes">Naked Eyes</a>' 1983 pop hit version of "<a href="/wiki/(There%27s)_Always_Something_There_to_Remind_Me" title="(There's) Always Something There to Remind Me">(There's) Always Something There to Remind Me</a>", and <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Milsap" title="Ronnie Milsap">Ronnie Milsap</a>'s 1982 country version of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Any_Day_Now_(1962_song)" title="Any Day Now (1962 song)">Any Day Now</a>". Bacharach continued a concert career, appearing at auditoriums throughout the world, often with large orchestras. He occasionally joined Warwick for sold-out concerts in New York, Las Vegas, and Los Angeles.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s_and_beyond">1990s and beyond</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burt_Bacharach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1990s and beyond">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 1990, <a href="/wiki/Deacon_Blue" title="Deacon Blue">Deacon Blue</a> charted number 2 in the UK singles chart with an EP entitled "<a href="/wiki/Four_Bacharach_%26_David_Songs" title="Four Bacharach &amp; David Songs">Four Bacharach &amp; David Songs</a>", with the first track, "I'll Never Fall in Love Again" receiving extensive media coverage. In 1996, jazz pianist <a href="/wiki/McCoy_Tyner" title="McCoy Tyner">McCoy Tyner</a> recorded an album of nine Bacharach standards that featured Tyner's trio with an orchestra arranged and conducted by John Clayton. In 1998, Bacharach co-wrote and recorded a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy</a>-winning album with <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Costello" title="Elvis Costello">Elvis Costello</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Painted_from_Memory" title="Painted from Memory">Painted from Memory</a></i>, on which the compositions began to take on the sound of his earlier work. In 2006, he recorded a jazz album with <a href="/wiki/Trijntje_Oosterhuis" title="Trijntje Oosterhuis">Trijntje Oosterhuis</a> and the Metropole Orchestra called <i><a href="/wiki/The_Look_of_Love_(Trijntje_Oosterhuis_album)" title="The Look of Love (Trijntje Oosterhuis album)">The Look of Love (Burt Bacharach Songbook)</a></i> which was released in November that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Bacharach collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Cathy_Dennis" title="Cathy Dennis">Cathy Dennis</a> in 2002 to write an original song for the <i><a href="/wiki/Pop_Idol" title="Pop Idol">Pop Idol</a></i> winner <a href="/wiki/Will_Young" title="Will Young">Will Young</a>. This was "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=What%27s_in_Goodbye&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="What's in Goodbye (page does not exist)">What's in Goodbye</a>", and it appears on Young's debut album <i><a href="/wiki/From_Now_On_(album)" title="From Now On (album)">From Now On</a></i>. During July 2002, Young was a guest vocalist at two of Bacharach's concerts, one at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMV_Hammersmith_Apollo" title="HMV Hammersmith Apollo">Hammersmith Apollo</a> and the other at Liverpool Pops.</p>
<p>In 2003, he teamed with legendary singer and songwriter <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Isley" title="Ronald Isley">Ronald Isley</a> to release the album <i>Here I Am</i>, which revisited a number of his 1960s compositions in Isley's signature R&amp;B style. Bacharach's 2005 solo album <i><a href="/wiki/At_This_Time" title="At This Time">At This Time</a></i> was a departure from past works in that Bacharach penned his own lyrics, some of which dealt with political themes. Guest stars on the album included <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Costello" title="Elvis Costello">Elvis Costello</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rufus_Wainwright" title="Rufus Wainwright">Rufus Wainwright</a>, and hip-hop producer <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>.</p>
<p>On October 24, 2008, Bacharach opened the BBC <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_Proms" title="Electric Proms">Electric Proms</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Roundhouse" title="The Roundhouse">The Roundhouse</a> in London, performing with the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Concert_Orchestra" title="BBC Concert Orchestra">BBC Concert Orchestra</a> accompanied by guest vocalists <a href="/wiki/Adele" title="Adele">Adele</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beth_Rowley" title="Beth Rowley">Beth Rowley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Cullum" title="Jamie Cullum">Jamie Cullum</a>. The concert was a retrospective look back at his six-decade career, including classics such as "<a href="/wiki/Walk_On_By_(song)" title="Walk On By (song)">Walk On By</a>", "The Look of Love", "<a href="/wiki/I_Say_a_Little_Prayer" title="I Say a Little Prayer">I Say a Little Prayer</a>", "<a href="/wiki/What_the_World_Needs_Now_Is_Love" title="What the World Needs Now Is Love">What the World Needs Now Is Love</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Anyone_Who_Had_a_Heart_(song)" title="Anyone Who Had a Heart (song)">Anyone Who Had a Heart</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Twenty_Four_Hours_from_Tulsa" title="Twenty Four Hours from Tulsa">Twenty Four Hours from Tulsa</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Make_It_Easy_on_Yourself" title="Make It Easy on Yourself">Make It Easy on Yourself</a>", featuring Jamie Cullum.</p>
<p>In early 2009, Bacharach worked with Italian soul singer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Karima_Ammar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Karima Ammar (page does not exist)">Karima Ammar</a> and produced her debut single <i>Come In Ogni Ora</i>. A #4 hit, the song has been heard during the 59th <a href="/wiki/Sanremo_Music_Festival" title="Sanremo Music Festival">Sanremo Music Festival</a> and also features him playing piano.</p>
<p>Bacharach and David were awarded the 2011 <a href="/wiki/Gershwin_Prize" title="Gershwin Prize">Gershwin Prize</a> for Popular Song bestowed by the <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a>, the first time that a songwriting team has been given the honor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GrshnPrz_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-GrshnPrz-15">[15]</a></sup> David died the following year on September 1 at age 91.</p>
<p>On June 27, 2015, Bacharach performed at the <a href="/wiki/Glastonbury_Festival" title="Glastonbury Festival">Glastonbury Festival</a> UK. On July 15, Bacharach appeared on stage at the <a href="/wiki/Menier_Chocolate_Factory" title="Menier Chocolate Factory">Menier Chocolate Factory</a> to launch 'What's It All About? Bacharach Reimagined', a 90-minute live arrangement of his greatest hits. In his five-star review, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Coveney" title="Michael Coveney">Michael Coveney</a> of Whatsonstage.com called it 'Supremely theatrical, musically enthralling and the best sort of tribute show to a legend in his own lifetime, the inimitable Burt Bacharach, master magician of Motown, lyrical pop and beautiful ballad.'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_and_television">Film and television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burt_Bacharach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Film and television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Throughout the 1960s and 1970s, Bacharach was featured in a dozen TV musical and variety specials videotaped in the UK for <a href="/wiki/ITC_Entertainment" title="ITC Entertainment">ITC</a>, several were nominated for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emmy" title="Emmy">Emmy</a> awards for direction (by <a href="/wiki/Dwight_Hemion" title="Dwight Hemion">Dwight Hemion</a>). The guests included artists such as <a href="/wiki/Joel_Grey" title="Joel Grey">Joel Grey</a>, Dusty Springfield, <a href="/wiki/Dionne_Warwick" title="Dionne Warwick">Dionne Warwick</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Barbra_Streisand" title="Barbra Streisand">Barbra Streisand</a>. Bacharach and David did the score for an original musical for ABC-TV titled <i>On the Flip Side</i>, broadcast on <i><a href="/wiki/ABC_Stage_67" title="ABC Stage 67">ABC Stage 67</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Nelson" title="Ricky Nelson">Ricky Nelson</a> as a faded pop star trying for a comeback. While the ratings were dismal, the soundtrack showcased Bacharach's abilities to try different kinds of musical styles, ranging from (almost) 1960s rock, to pop, ballads, and Latin-tinged dance numbers.</p>
<p>In 1969, <a href="/wiki/Harry_Betts" title="Harry Betts">Harry Betts</a> arranged Bacharach's instrumental composition "Nikki" (named for Bacharach's daughter) into a new theme for the <i><a href="/wiki/ABC_Movie_of_the_Week" title="ABC Movie of the Week">ABC Movie of the Week</a></i>, a TV series which ran on the U.S. network until 1976.</p>
<p>During the 1970s, Bacharach and then-wife Angie Dickinson appeared in several TV commercials for <a href="/wiki/Martini_%26_Rossi" title="Martini &amp; Rossi">Martini &amp; Rossi</a> beverages, and even penned a short jingle ("Say Yes") for the spots. Bacharach also occasionally appeared on TV/variety shows, such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Merv_Griffin_Show" title="The Merv Griffin Show">The Merv Griffin Show</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tonight_Show_Starring_Johnny_Carson" title="The Tonight Show Starring Johnny Carson">The Tonight Show Starring Johnny Carson</a></i>, and many others.</p>
<p>In the 1990s and 2000s, Bacharach had <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cameo_role" title="Cameo role">cameo roles</a> in Hollywood movies, including all three <a href="/wiki/Austin_Powers_(film_series)" title="Austin Powers (film series)">Austin Powers</a> movies. His music is credited as providing inspiration for these movies, partially stemming from Bacharach's score for the 1967 <a href="/wiki/James_Bond" title="James Bond">James Bond</a> parody film <i><a href="/wiki/Casino_Royale_(1967_film)" title="Casino Royale (1967 film)">Casino Royale</a></i>. During subsequent Bacharach concert tours, each show would open with a very brief video clip from the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Austin_Powers:_International_Man_of_Mystery" title="Austin Powers: International Man of Mystery">Austin Powers: International Man of Mystery</a></i>, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mike_Myers_(actor)" title="Mike Myers (actor)">Mike Myers</a> (as Austin Powers) uttering "Ladies and Gentlemen, Mr. Burt Bacharach."</p>

<p>Bacharach appeared as a celebrity performer and guest vocal coach for contestants on the television show, "<a href="/wiki/American_Idol" title="American Idol">American Idol</a>" during the 2006 season, during which an entire episode was dedicated to his music. In late 2006, Bacharach appeared as the celebrity in a Geico auto insurance commercial, where he sings and plays the piano. He translates the customer's story through song ("I was hit...in the rear!")</p>
<p>In 2008, Bacharach featured in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BBC_Electric_Proms" title="BBC Electric Proms">BBC Electric Proms</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Roundhouse" title="The Roundhouse">The Roundhouse</a> with the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Concert_Orchestra" title="BBC Concert Orchestra">BBC Concert Orchestra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-17">[17]</a></sup> He performed similar shows in the same year at the <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Concert_Hall" title="Walt Disney Concert Hall">Walt Disney Concert Hall</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-npr-18">[18]</a></sup> and with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sydney_Symphony" title="Sydney Symphony">Sydney Symphony</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burt_Bacharach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bacharach has been married four times. His first marriage was to <a href="/wiki/Paula_Stewart" title="Paula Stewart">Paula Stewart</a> and lasted five years (1953–58). His second marriage was to actress <a href="/wiki/Angie_Dickinson" title="Angie Dickinson">Angie Dickinson</a>, lasting for 15 years (1965–80).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-film_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-film-5">[5]</a></sup> Bacharach and Dickinson had a daughter, Nikki Bacharach, who committed suicide in 2007 at the age of 40.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mirror_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-mirror-19">[19]</a></sup> His third marriage was to lyricist <a href="/wiki/Carole_Bayer_Sager" title="Carole Bayer Sager">Carole Bayer Sager</a>; this lasted nine years (1982–91). Bacharach and Bayer Sager collaborated on a number of musical pieces and adopted a son, Cristopher. Bacharach married his current wife, Jane Hansen, in 1993; they have two children, a son, Oliver, and a daughter, Raleigh. His autobiography, <i>Anyone Who Had a Heart</i>, was published in 2013.</p>

<li><i>This Girl's In Love (A Bacharach &amp; David Songbook)</i>, to be released in October 2016 by Anglo-Pakistani singer/songwriter <a href="/wiki/Rumer_(musician)" title="Rumer (musician)">Rumer</a> (real name Sarah Joyce).</li>
