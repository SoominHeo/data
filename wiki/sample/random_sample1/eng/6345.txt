<p><b>Burford</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ɜːr/ 'ur' in 'fur'">ɜːr</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/ər/ 'er' in 'finger'">ər</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> town on the <a href="/wiki/River_Windrush" title="River Windrush">River Windrush</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Cotswolds" title="Cotswolds">Cotswold</a> hills in <a href="/wiki/West_Oxfordshire" title="West Oxfordshire">West Oxfordshire</a>, England. It is often referred to as the 'gateway' to the Cotswolds. Burford is located 18 miles (29 km) west of <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a> and 22 miles (35 km) southeast of <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham" title="Cheltenham">Cheltenham</a>, about 2 miles (3 km) from the <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a> boundary. The <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">toponym</a> derives from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> words <i><a href="/wiki/Burh" title="Burh">burh</a></i> meaning fortified town or hilltown and <i><a href="/wiki/Ford_(crossing)" title="Ford (crossing)">ford</a></i>, the crossing of a river. The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2011" title="United Kingdom Census 2011">2011 Census</a> recorded the population of Burford parish as 1,410<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and Burford Ward as 1,847.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Priory">Priory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Priory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Burford_Priory001.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="652" data-file-width="962" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Burford_Priory001.jpg/220px-Burford_Priory001.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Burford_Priory001.jpg/330px-Burford_Priory001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Burford_Priory001.jpg/440px-Burford_Priory001.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Burford_Priory001.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Burford Priory</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Burford_Priory" title="Burford Priory">Burford Priory</a> is a <a href="/wiki/English_country_house" title="English country house">country house</a> that stands on the site of a 13th-century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustinian" title="Augustinian">Augustinian</a> hospital. In the 1580s an <a href="/wiki/Elizabethan_architecture" title="Elizabethan architecture">Elizabethan house</a> was built incorporating remnants of the priory hospital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESherwoodPevsner1974510_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESherwoodPevsner1974510-3">[3]</a></sup> It was remodelled in <a href="/wiki/Jacobean_architecture" title="Jacobean architecture">Jacobean style</a>, probably after 1637, when the estate had been bought by <a href="/wiki/William_Lenthall" title="William Lenthall">William Lenthall</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_British_House_of_Commons" title="Speaker of the British House of Commons">Speaker of the House of Commons</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Long_Parliament" title="Long Parliament">Long Parliament</a>. The house and later the chapel were restored for the philanthropist <a href="/wiki/Emslie_Horniman" title="Emslie Horniman">Emslie John Horniman</a>, M.P., after 1912 by the architect <a href="/wiki/Walter_Godfrey" title="Walter Godfrey">Walter Godfrey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESherwoodPevsner1974510.E2.80.93511_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESherwoodPevsner1974510.E2.80.93511-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1949 Burford Priory housed the Society of the Salutation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mary_(mother_of_Jesus)" title="Mary (mother of Jesus)">Our Lady</a>, a community of <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> nuns. In the 1980s, its numbers dwindled, so in 1987 it became a mixed community including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Order_of_St._Benedict_(Anglican)" title="Order of St. Benedict (Anglican)">Church of England Benedictine</a> <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">monks</a>. In 2008 the community sold the property and it has now returned to being a private house.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_and_social_history">Economic and social history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Economic and social history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The town began in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_England" title="Anglo-Saxon England">middle Saxon period</a> with the founding of a village near the site of the modern priory building. This settlement continued in use until just after the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman conquest of England</a> when the new town of Burford was built. On the site of the old village a hospital was founded which remained open until the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Monasteries" title="Dissolution of the Monasteries">Dissolution of the Monasteries</a> by <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">King Henry VIII</a>. The modern priory building was constructed some 40 years later around 1580.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1649, the church was used as a prison (during the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">Civil War</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> when the <a href="/wiki/New_Model_Army" title="New Model Army">New Model Army</a> <a href="/wiki/Banbury_mutiny" title="Banbury mutiny">Banbury mutineers</a> were held there. Some of the 340 prisoners left carvings and graffiti, which still survive in the church.</p>
<p>Between the 14th and 17th centuries Burford was important for its wool. The Tolsey, midway along Burford's High Street, was once the centre of the local wool trade, and is now a museum.</p>
<p>The town centre has some 15th century houses and the <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> town house that is now <a href="/wiki/Burford_Methodist_Church" title="Burford Methodist Church">Burford Methodist Church</a>. Its most notable building is the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England_parish_church" title="Church of England parish church">Church of England parish church</a> of Saint <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">John the Baptist</a>, which is known for its merchants' <a href="/wiki/Guild" title="Guild">guild</a> chapel, memorial to Henry VIII's barber-surgeon, <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Harman" title="Edmund Harman">Edmund Harman</a>, featuring South American Indians and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Eamer_Kempe" title="Charles Eamer Kempe">Kempe</a> stained glass.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bell-foundry">Bell-foundry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Bell-foundry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Burford has twice had a <a href="/wiki/Bellfounding" title="Bellfounding">bell-foundry</a>: one run by the Neale family in the 17th century and the other run by the Bond family in the 19th and 20th centuries.</p>
<p>Henry Neale was a bell-founder between 1627 and 1641 and also had a foundry at <a href="/wiki/Somerford_Keynes" title="Somerford Keynes">Somerford Keynes</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DoveFounders_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-DoveFounders-8">[8]</a></sup> Edward Neale had joined him as a bell-founder at Burford by 1635 and continued the business until 1685.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DoveFounders_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-DoveFounders-8">[8]</a></sup> Numerous Neale bells remain in use, including at <a href="/wiki/Brize_Norton#Churches" title="Brize Norton">St Britius, Brize Norton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buscot#Parish_church" title="Buscot">St Mary's, Buscot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fulbrook,_Oxfordshire#Parish_church" title="Fulbrook, Oxfordshire">St James the Great, Fulbrook</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steeple_Aston#Church_and_chapel" title="Steeple Aston">SS Peter and Paul, Steeple Aston</a>. A few Neale bells that are no longer rung are displayed in Burford parish church.</p>
<p>Henry Bond had a bell-foundry at Westcot from 1851 to 1861. He then moved it to Burford where he continued until 1905.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DoveFounders_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-DoveFounders-8">[8]</a></sup> He was then succeeded by Thomas Bond, who continued bell-founding at Burford until 1947.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DoveFounders_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-DoveFounders-8">[8]</a></sup> Bond bells still in use include four of the ring of six at <a href="/wiki/Taynton,_Oxfordshire#Parish_church" title="Taynton, Oxfordshire">St John the Evangelist, Taynton</a>, one and a <a href="/wiki/Church_bell#Sanctus_Bells" title="Church bell">Sanctus bell</a> at <a href="/wiki/Chadlington#Church_of_England" title="Chadlington">St Nicholas, Chadlington</a> and one each at <a href="/wiki/Chalgrove#Parish_church" title="Chalgrove">St Mary the Virgin, Chalgrove</a> and <a href="/wiki/Whatcote#Parish_church" title="Whatcote">St Peter's, Whatcote</a> in <a href="/wiki/Warwickshire" title="Warwickshire">Warwickshire</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Easter_Synod">Easter Synod</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Easter Synod">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Burford-belfry-lores.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="248" data-file-width="324" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b7/Burford-belfry-lores.jpg/220px-Burford-belfry-lores.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/b/b7/Burford-belfry-lores.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Burford-belfry-lores.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Belfry of St John the Baptist parish church</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>For many years before the 7th century there had been strife between the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Christianity#Easter_calculation" title="Celtic Christianity">Celtic Church</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Chalcedonian_Christianity" title="Chalcedonian Christianity">Early Church</a> over the question of when Easter Day should be celebrated. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Britons_(historic)" title="Britons (historic)">Britons</a> adhered to the rule laid at the Council of <a href="/wiki/Arles" title="Arles">Arles</a> in AD 314, that Easter Day should be the 14th day of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paschal_Full_Moon" title="Paschal Full Moon">Paschal moon</a>, even if the moon were on a Sunday. The Roman Church <a href="/wiki/Computus" title="Computus">had decided</a> that when the 14th day of the Paschal moon was a Sunday, Easter Day should be the Sunday after.</p>
<p>Various <a href="/wiki/Synod" title="Synod">Synods</a> were held in different parts of the kingdom with the object of settling this controversy, and one was held for this object at Burford in AD 685.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMonk189129_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMonk189129-9">[9]</a></sup> Monk deduces from the fact of the Synod being held at Burford, that the Britons in some numbers had settled in the town and neighbourhood. This Synod was attended by <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelred_of_Mercia" title="Æthelred of Mercia">Æthelred</a>, King of <a href="/wiki/Mercia" title="Mercia">Mercia</a>, and his nephew Berthwald (who had been granted the southern part of his uncle's kingdom); <a href="/wiki/Theodore_of_Tarsus" title="Theodore of Tarsus">Theodore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a>; <a href="/wiki/Bosel" title="Bosel">Bosel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Worcester" title="Bishop of Worcester">Bishop of Worcester</a>; <a href="/wiki/Seaxwulf" title="Seaxwulf">Seaxwulf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Lichfield" title="Bishop of Lichfield">Bishop of Lichfield</a>; <a href="/wiki/Aldhelm" title="Aldhelm">Aldhelm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malmesbury_Abbey" title="Malmesbury Abbey">Abbot of Malmesbury</a>; and many others.</p>
<p>Aldhelm was ordered at this conference to write a book against the error of the Britons in the observance of Easter. At this Synod Berthwald gave 40 cassates of land (a cassate is enough land to support a family) to Aldhelm who afterwards became Bishop of Shereborne. According to Spelman, the notes of the Synod were published in AD 705.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Battle_of_Burford_and_the_Golden_Dragon">Battle of Burford and the Golden Dragon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Battle of Burford and the Golden Dragon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Malmesbury and other chroniclers record a battle between the <a href="/wiki/Wessex" title="Wessex">West Saxons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mercia" title="Mercia">Mercians</a> at Burford in AD 752.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMonk18917_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMonk18917-10">[10]</a></sup> In the end Æthelhum, the Mercian standard-bearer who carried the flag with a golden <a href="/wiki/Dragon" title="Dragon">dragon</a> on it, was killed by the lance of his Saxon rival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGardner1852not_cited_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGardner1852not_cited-11">[11]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a></i> records <i>"A.D 752. This year <a href="/wiki/Cuthred_of_Wessex" title="Cuthred of Wessex">Cuthred</a>, king of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Saxons" title="West Saxons">West Saxons</a>, in the 12th year of his reign, fought at Burford, against <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelbald_of_Mercia" title="Æthelbald of Mercia">Æthelbald</a> king of the <a href="/wiki/Mercia" title="Mercia">Mercians</a>, and put him to flight."</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGardner1852not_cited_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGardner1852not_cited-11">[11]</a></sup> The historian <a href="/wiki/William_Camden" title="William Camden">William Camden</a> (1551–1623) wrote</p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>"... in Saxon Beorgford</i> [i.e. Burford]<i>, where Cuthred, king of the West Saxons, then tributary to the Mercians, not being able to endure any longer the cruelty and base exactions of King Æthelbald, met him in the open field with an army and beat him, taking his standard, which was a portraiture of a golden dragon."</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGardner1852not_cited_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGardner1852not_cited-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The origin of the golden dragon standard is attributed to that of <a href="/wiki/Uther_Pendragon" title="Uther Pendragon">Uther Pendragon</a>, the father of <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a> of whom <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_of_Monmouth" title="Geoffrey of Monmouth">Geoffrey of Monmouth</a> wrote:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>[Uther Pendragon] <i>"... ordered two dragons to be fashioned in gold, in the likeness of the one which he had seen in the ray which shone from that star. As soon as the Dragons had been completed this with the most marvellous craftsmanship – he made a present of one of them to the congregation of the cathedral church of the see of <a href="/wiki/Winchester" title="Winchester">Winchester</a>. The second one he kept for himself, so that he could carry it around to his wars."</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMonmouth1966202_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMonmouth1966202-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In the late 16th or early 17th century the people of Burford still celebrated the anniversary of the battle. Camden wrote: <i>"There has been a custom in the town of making a great dragon yearly, and carrying it up and down the streets in great jollity on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._John%27s_Eve" title="St. John's Eve">St John's Eve</a>"</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMonk18918_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMonk18918-13">[13]</a></sup> The field traditionally claimed to be that of the battle is still called <a href="/wiki/Battle_Edge" title="Battle Edge">Battle Edge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGardner1852not_cited_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGardner1852not_cited-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Reverend Francis Knollis' description of the discovery, "On 21 November 1814 a large <a href="/wiki/Freestone_(masonry)" title="Freestone (masonry)">freestone</a> <a href="/wiki/Sarcophagus" title="Sarcophagus">sarcophagus</a> was discovered near Battle Edge 3 feet (0.91 m) below ground, weighing 16 long hundredweight (1,800 lb; 810 kg) with the feet pointing almost due south. The interior is 6 feet (1.8 m) long and 2 feet 2 inches (0.66 m) wide. It was found to contain the remains of a human body, with portions of a leather <a href="/wiki/Cuirass" title="Cuirass">cuirass</a> studded with metal nails. The skeleton was found in near perfect state due to the exclusion of air from the sarcophagus."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The coffin is now preserved in Burford churchyard, near the west gate.</p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>Whose fame is in that dark green tomb? Four stones with their heads of moss stand there. They mark the narrow house of death. Some chief of fame is here! Raise the songs of old! Awake their memory in the tomb.</i> – <a href="/wiki/Ossian" title="Ossian">Ossian</a></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Amenities">Amenities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Amenities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Burford County Primary School is the town's primary school. <a href="/wiki/Burford_School" title="Burford School">Burford School</a>, a mixed <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_school" title="Comprehensive school">comprehensive school</a>, is the town's secondary school. The primary school <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fete" title="Fete">fete</a>, held every summer, includes a procession (including a dragon) down High Street to the school, where there are stalls and games.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Blue_Cross_(animal_charity)" title="Blue Cross (animal charity)">Blue Cross</a> National Animal Welfare Charity is based at Burford.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Burford is the main setting for <a href="/wiki/Cynthia_Harnett" title="Cynthia Harnett">Cynthia Harnett</a>'s historical novel for children, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wool-Pack" title="The Wool-Pack">The Wool-Pack</a></i>.</p>
<p>Various writers live in Burford, including children's book writer, Ray Murray, author of the Arabella Parker series of books.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_legend">Local legend</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Local legend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Local legend tells of a fiery coach containing Sir <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Tanfield" title="Lawrence Tanfield">Lawrence Tanfield</a> of <a href="/wiki/Burford_Priory" title="Burford Priory">Burford Priory</a> and/or his wife flying around the town that brings a curse upon all who see it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Andrews<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> speculates that the apparition may have been caused by a local tradition of burning effigies of the unpopular couple that began after their deaths. The visitations were reportedly ended when local clergymen trapped Lady Tanfield's ghost in a corked glass bottle during an exorcism and cast it into the River Windrush.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> During droughts locals would fill the river from buckets to ensure that the bottle did not rise above the surface and free the spirit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
