<p>The <b>Buffalo Bisons</b> were an <a href="/wiki/American_Hockey_League" title="American Hockey League">American Hockey League</a> <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">ice hockey</a> franchise that played from 1940 to 1970 in <a href="/wiki/Buffalo,_New_York" title="Buffalo, New York">Buffalo, New York</a>. They replaced the <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Bisons_(IHL)" title="Buffalo Bisons (IHL)">original Buffalo Bisons hockey team</a>, which left the area in 1936 after <a href="/wiki/Peace_Bridge_Arena" title="Peace Bridge Arena">its arena</a> collapsed. They were the first professional hockey team to play their games in the Buffalo city proper; the previous team had played across the border at an arena in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Erie,_Ontario" title="Fort Erie, Ontario">Fort Erie, Ontario</a>.</p>
<p>The Bisons played at the newly constructed <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Memorial_Auditorium" title="Buffalo Memorial Auditorium">Memorial Auditorium</a>, and at various times had affiliations with the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Canadiens" title="Montreal Canadiens">Montreal Canadiens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Blackhawks" title="Chicago Blackhawks">Chicago Black Hawks</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_York_Rangers" title="New York Rangers">New York Rangers</a>. The team was brought to Buffalo from Syracuse by Louis M. Jacobs, then owner of the buffalo based <a href="/wiki/Delaware_North" title="Delaware North">Jacob's Concessions</a> and the father of <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Jacobs" title="Jeremy Jacobs">Jeremy Jacobs</a> the current owner of the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Bruins" title="Boston Bruins">Boston Bruins</a>.</p>
<p>The team's unusual logo stems from the Bisons being purchased in 1956 by the owner of the local franchise of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pepsi-Cola" title="Pepsi-Cola">Pepsi-Cola</a>, who changed the team's colors and logo to reflect the soft drink company; the Bisons retained the logo for the rest of their existence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>They were <a href="/wiki/Calder_Cup" title="Calder Cup">Calder Cup</a> champions in 1943, 1944, 1946, 1963 and 1970, and runners-up in 1948, 1951, 1955, 1959 and 1962.</p>
<p>The team ceased operations after the 1969–70 season due to the awarding of a <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League" title="National Hockey League">National Hockey League</a> expansion team, the <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Sabres" title="Buffalo Sabres">Buffalo Sabres</a>, to begin play in 1970–71. Like the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Hornets" title="Pittsburgh Hornets">Pittsburgh Hornets</a> three years earlier (also shut down because of NHL expansion), the Bisons closed out their existence with one final championship.</p>
<p>Goaltender <a href="/wiki/Roger_Crozier" title="Roger Crozier">Roger Crozier</a> had the unusual distinction of playing for both the Bisons and the Sabres. He played eight games for the former and eight years for the latter. Broadcaster <a href="/wiki/Rick_Jeanneret" title="Rick Jeanneret">Rick Jeanneret</a> also called several games during the Bisons' final season and moved into a similar role with the Sabres in 1971.</p>
<p>After the Bisons folded, the Sabres were granted an AHL franchise, who used it to establish the <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Swords" title="Cincinnati Swords">Cincinnati Swords</a> in 1971.</p>
<p>On September 18, 2010, the Sabres announced that they would be adopting a third jersey that pays homage to the Bisons during their <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_Buffalo_Sabres_season" title="2010–11 Buffalo Sabres season">2010–11 season</a>. The Bisons-inspired third jersey was used for that and the following season before being discontinued.</p>
