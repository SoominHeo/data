<p><b>Bugs Bunny</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Animated_cartoon" title="Animated cartoon">animated cartoon</a> character; created in 1940 by <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Cartoons" title="Warner Bros. Cartoons">Leon Schlesinger Productions</a> (later <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Cartoons" title="Warner Bros. Cartoons">Warner Bros. Cartoons</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Voice_acting" title="Voice acting">voiced</a> originally by the legendary vocal artist, <a href="/wiki/Mel_Blanc" title="Mel Blanc">Mel Blanc</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BTVA_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BTVA-2">[2]</a></sup> Bugs is best known for his starring roles in the <i><a href="/wiki/Looney_Tunes" title="Looney Tunes">Looney Tunes</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Merrie_Melodies" title="Merrie Melodies">Merrie Melodies</a></i> series of animated <a href="/wiki/Short_film" title="Short film">short films</a>, produced by <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Golden_age_of_American_animation" title="Golden age of American animation">golden age of American animation</a>. His popularity during this era led to his becoming an American <a href="/wiki/Cultural_icon" title="Cultural icon">cultural icon</a>, as well as the official <a href="/wiki/Mascot" title="Mascot">mascot</a> of <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros. Entertainment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bugs is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropomorphic" title="Anthropomorphic">anthropomorphic</a> gray <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_fictional_hares_and_rabbits" title="List of fictional hares and rabbits">hare or rabbit</a> who is famous for his flippant, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/insouciant" title="wikt:insouciant">insouciant</a> personality; a <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_English" title="New York City English">Brooklyn</a> accent; his portrayal as a <a href="/wiki/Trickster" title="Trickster">trickster</a>; and his catch phrase "Eh... What's up, doc?", usually spoken while chewing a <a href="/wiki/Carrot" title="Carrot">carrot</a>. Though a similar rabbit character began appearing in the Warner Bros. cartoon shorts during the late 1930s, the definitive character of Bugs Bunny is widely credited to have made his debut in director <a href="/wiki/Tex_Avery" title="Tex Avery">Tex Avery</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Oscar</a>-nominated film <i><a href="/wiki/A_Wild_Hare" title="A Wild Hare">A Wild Hare</a></i> (1940).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adamson_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Adamson-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since his debut, Bugs has appeared in various short films, feature films, compilations, TV series, music records, comic books, video games, award shows, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amusement_park_ride" title="Amusement park ride">amusement park rides</a> and commercials. He has also appeared in more films than any other cartoon <a href="/wiki/Character_(arts)" title="Character (arts)">character</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guinness_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guinness-4">[4]</a></sup> is the ninth most-portrayed film personality in the world,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guinness_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guinness-4">[4]</a></sup> and has his own star on the <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Walk_of_Fame" title="Hollywood Walk of Fame">Hollywood Walk of Fame</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hollywood_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hollywood-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Animation_history">Animation history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bugs_Bunny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Animation history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bugs_Bunny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Development_of_Bugs_Bunny" title="Development of Bugs Bunny">Development of Bugs Bunny</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bugs_Bunny%27s_Evolution.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="157" data-file-width="462" height="119" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f1/Bugs_Bunny%27s_Evolution.PNG/350px-Bugs_Bunny%27s_Evolution.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/f/f1/Bugs_Bunny%27s_Evolution.PNG 1.5x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bugs_Bunny%27s_Evolution.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A depiction of Bugs' evolution throughout the years.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Chase_Craig" title="Chase Craig">Chase Craig</a>, who was a member of <a href="/wiki/Tex_Avery" title="Tex Avery">Tex Avery</a>'s cartoon unit and later wrote and drew the first Bugs Bunny comic Sunday pages and the first Bugs comic book; "Bugs was not the creation of any one man but rather represented the creative talents of perhaps five or six directors and many cartoon-writers. In those days the stories were often the work of a group who suggested various gags, bounced them around and finalized them in a joint story conference."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A rabbit with some of the personality of Bugs, though looking very different, was originally featured in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Porky%27s_Hare_Hunt" title="Porky's Hare Hunt">Porky's Hare Hunt</a></i>, released on April 30, 1938. It was co-directed by <a href="/wiki/Ben_Hardaway" title="Ben Hardaway">Ben "Bugs" Hardaway</a> and an uncredited <a href="/wiki/Cal_Dalton" title="Cal Dalton">Cal Dalton</a> (who was responsible for the initial design of the rabbit). This cartoon has an almost identical plot to Avery's <i><a href="/wiki/Porky%27s_Duck_Hunt" title="Porky's Duck Hunt">Porky's Duck Hunt</a></i> (1937), which had introduced <a href="/wiki/Daffy_Duck" title="Daffy Duck">Daffy Duck</a>. <a href="/wiki/Porky_Pig" title="Porky Pig">Porky Pig</a> is again cast as a hunter tracking a silly prey who is more interested in driving his pursuer insane and less interested in escaping. <i>Hare Hunt</i> replaces the little black duck with a small white rabbit. The rabbit introduces himself with the odd expression "Jiggers, fellers," and <a href="/wiki/Mel_Blanc" title="Mel Blanc">Mel Blanc</a> gave the character a voice and laugh much like those he would later use for <a href="/wiki/Woody_Woodpecker" title="Woody Woodpecker">Woody Woodpecker</a>. <i>Hare Hunt</i> also gives its rabbit the famous <a href="/wiki/Groucho_Marx" title="Groucho Marx">Groucho Marx</a> line, "Of course you realize, this means war!" The rabbit character was popular enough with audiences that the <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Cartoons" title="Warner Bros. Cartoons">Termite Terrace</a> staff decided to use it again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brittanica_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brittanica-8">[8]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Friz_Freleng" title="Friz Freleng">Friz Freleng</a>, Hardaway and Dalton had decided to dress the duck in a rabbit suit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walz_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walz-9">[9]</a></sup> The white rabbit had an oval head and a shapeless body. In characterization, he was "a rural <a href="/wiki/Jester#Buffoon" title="Jester">buffoon</a>". He was loud, zany with a goofy, <a href="/wiki/Guttural" title="Guttural">guttural</a> laugh. Blanc provided him with a <a href="/wiki/Yokel" title="Yokel">hayseed</a> voice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barrier2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barrier2-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rabbit comes back in <i><a href="/wiki/Prest-O_Change-O" title="Prest-O Change-O">Prest-O Change-O</a></i> (1939), directed by <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Jones" title="Chuck Jones">Chuck Jones</a>, where he is the pet rabbit of <a href="/wiki/Unseen_character" title="Unseen character">unseen character</a> Sham-Fu the Magician. Two dogs, fleeing the local dogcatcher, enter his absent master's house. The rabbit harasses them, but is ultimately bested by the bigger of the two dogs. This version of the rabbit was cool, graceful, and controlled. He retained the guttural laugh but was otherwise silent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barrier2_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barrier2-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rabbit's third appearance comes in <i><a href="/wiki/Hare-um_Scare-um" title="Hare-um Scare-um">Hare-um Scare-um</a></i> (1939), directed again by Dalton and Hardaway. This cartoon—the first in which he is depicted as a gray bunny instead of a white one—is also notable as the rabbit's first singing role. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charlie_Thorson" title="Charlie Thorson">Charlie Thorson</a>, lead animator on the film, gave the character a name. He had written "Bugs' Bunny" on the model sheet that he drew for Hardaway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brittanica_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brittanica-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barrier_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barrier-11">[11]</a></sup> In promotional material for the cartoon, including a surviving 1939 presskit, the name on the model sheet was altered to become the rabbit's own name: "Bugs" Bunny (quotation marks only used, on and off, until 1944).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In his autobiography, Blanc claimed that another proposed name for the character was "Happy Rabbit."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanc_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blanc-13">[13]</a></sup> In the actual cartoons and publicity, however, the name "Happy" only seems to have been used in reference to Bugs Hardaway. In <i>Hare-um Scare-um</i>, a newspaper headline reads, "Happy Hardaway."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thorson had been approached by <a href="/wiki/Tedd_Pierce" title="Tedd Pierce">Tedd Pierce</a>, head of the story department, and asked to design a better rabbit. The decision was influenced by Thorson's experience in designing hares. He had designed Max Hare in <i><a href="/wiki/Toby_Tortoise_Returns" title="Toby Tortoise Returns">Toby Tortoise Returns</a></i> (1936). For Hardaway, Thorson created the model sheet previously mentioned, with six different rabbit poses. Thorson's model sheet is "a comic rendition of the stereotypical fuzzy bunny". He had a pear-shaped body with a protruding rear end. His face was flat and had large expressive eyes. He had an exaggerated long neck, gloved hands with three fingers, oversized feet, and a "smart aleck" grin. The end result was influenced by <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Animation_Studios" title="Walt Disney Animation Studios">Walt Disney Animation Studios</a>' tendency to draw animals in the style of cute infants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walz_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Walz-9">[9]</a></sup> He had an obvious Disney influence, but looked like an awkward merger of the lean and streamlined Max Hare from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tortoise_and_the_Hare_(film)" title="The Tortoise and the Hare (film)">The Tortoise and the Hare</a></i> (1935), and the round, soft bunnies from <i><a href="/wiki/Little_Hiawatha" title="Little Hiawatha">Little Hiawatha</a></i> (1937).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barrier2_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Barrier2-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Jones' <i><a href="/wiki/Elmer%27s_Candid_Camera" title="Elmer's Candid Camera">Elmer's Candid Camera</a></i> (1940) the rabbit first meets <a href="/wiki/Elmer_Fudd" title="Elmer Fudd">Elmer Fudd</a>. This time the rabbit looks more like the present-day Bugs, taller and with a similar face—but retaining the more primitive voice. <i>Candid Camera'</i>s Elmer character design is also different: taller and chubbier in the face than the modern model, though <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Q._Bryan" title="Arthur Q. Bryan">Arthur Q. Bryan</a>'s character voice is already established.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Official_debut">Official debut</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bugs_Bunny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Official debut">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FirstBugs.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="364" data-file-width="439" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a5/FirstBugs.jpg/220px-FirstBugs.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a5/FirstBugs.jpg/330px-FirstBugs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/a/a5/FirstBugs.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FirstBugs.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bugs' first appearance in <i><a href="/wiki/A_Wild_Hare" title="A Wild Hare">A Wild Hare</a></i> (1940).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>While <i><a href="/wiki/Porky%27s_Hare_Hunt" title="Porky's Hare Hunt">Porky's Hare Hunt</a></i> was the first Warner Bros. cartoon to feature a Bugs Bunny-like rabbit, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Wild_Hare" title="A Wild Hare">A Wild Hare</a></i>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Tex_Avery" title="Tex Avery">Tex Avery</a> and released on July 27, 1940, is widely considered to be the first official Bugs Bunny cartoon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adamson_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Adamson-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It is the first film where both Elmer Fudd and Bugs, both redesigned by <a href="/wiki/Bob_Givens" title="Bob Givens">Bob Givens</a>, are shown in their fully developed forms as hunter and tormentor, respectively; the first in which <a href="/wiki/Mel_Blanc" title="Mel Blanc">Mel Blanc</a> uses what would become Bugs' standard voice; and the first in which Bugs uses his catchphrase, "What's up, Doc?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Avery_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Avery-16">[16]</a></sup> <i>A Wild Hare</i> was a huge success in theaters and received an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a> nomination for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Animated_Short_Film" title="Academy Award for Animated Short Film">Best Cartoon Short Subject</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>For the film Avery asked Givens to remodel the rabbit. The result had a closer resemblance to Max Hare. He had a more elongated body, stood more erect, and looked more poised. If Thorson's rabbit looked like an infant, Givens' version looked like an adolescent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walz_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Walz-9">[9]</a></sup> Blanc gave Bugs the voice of a city slicker. The rabbit was as audacious as he had been in <i>Hare-um Scare-um</i> and as cool and collected as in <i>Prest-O Change-O</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barrier2_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Barrier2-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Immediately following on <i>A Wild Hare</i>, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Clampett" title="Bob Clampett">Bob Clampett</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Patient_Porky" title="Patient Porky">Patient Porky</a></i> (1940) features a <a href="/wiki/Cameo_appearance" title="Cameo appearance">cameo appearance</a> by Bugs, announcing to the audience that 750 rabbits have been born. The gag uses Bugs' <i>Wild Hare</i> visual design, but his goofier pre-<i>Wild Hare</i> voice characterization.</p>
<p>The second full-fledged role for the mature Bugs, <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Jones" title="Chuck Jones">Chuck Jones</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Elmer%27s_Pet_Rabbit" title="Elmer's Pet Rabbit">Elmer's Pet Rabbit</a></i> (1941), is the first to use Bugs' name on-screen: it appears in a title card, "featuring Bugs Bunny," at the start of the film (which was edited in following the success of <i>A Wild Hare</i>). However, Bugs' voice and personality in this cartoon is noticeably different, and his design was slightly altered as well; Bugs' visual design is based on the prototype rabbit in <i>Candid Camera</i>, but with yellow gloves and no buck teeth, has a lower-pitched voice and a more aggressive, arrogant and thuggish personality instead of a fun loving personality. After <i>Pet Rabbit</i>, however, subsequent Bugs appearances returned to normal: the <i>Wild Hare</i> visual design and personality returned, and Blanc re-used the <i>Wild Hare</i> voice characterization.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Hiawatha%27s_Rabbit_Hunt" title="Hiawatha's Rabbit Hunt">Hiawatha's Rabbit Hunt</a></i> (1941), directed by <a href="/wiki/Friz_Freleng" title="Friz Freleng">Friz Freleng</a>, became the second Bugs Bunny cartoon to receive an Academy Award nomination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The fact that it didn't win the award was later spoofed somewhat in <i><a href="/wiki/What%27s_Cookin%27_Doc%3F" title="What's Cookin' Doc?">What's Cookin' Doc?</a></i> (1944), in which Bugs demands a recount (claiming to be a victim of "<a href="/wiki/Sabotage" title="Sabotage">sa-bo-TAH-gee</a>") after losing the Oscar to <a href="/wiki/James_Cagney" title="James Cagney">Jimmy Cagney</a> and presents a clip from <i>Hiawatha's Rabbit Hunt</i> to prove his point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bugs_Bunny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By 1942, Bugs had become the number one star of <i>Merrie Melodies</i>. The series was originally intended only for one-shot characters in films after several early attempts to introduce characters (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foxy_(cartoon_character)" title="Foxy (cartoon character)">Foxy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goopy_Geer" title="Goopy Geer">Goopy Geer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Piggy_(Merrie_Melodies)" title="Piggy (Merrie Melodies)">Piggy</a>) failed under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hugh_Harman" title="Hugh Harman">Harman</a>–<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rudolph_Ising" title="Rudolph Ising">Ising</a>. By the mid-1930s, under <a href="/wiki/Leon_Schlesinger" title="Leon Schlesinger">Leon Schlesinger</a>, <i>Merrie Melodies</i> started introducing newer characters. <i><a href="/wiki/Bugs_Bunny_Gets_the_Boid" title="Bugs Bunny Gets the Boid">Bugs Bunny Gets the Boid</a></i> (1942) shows a slight redesign of Bugs, with less-prominent front teeth and a rounder head. The character was reworked by <a href="/wiki/Robert_McKimson" title="Robert McKimson">Robert McKimson</a>, then an animator in Clampett's unit. The redesign at first was only used in the films created by Clampett's unit, but in time it would be taken up by the other directors, with Freleng and <a href="/wiki/Frank_Tashlin" title="Frank Tashlin">Frank Tashlin</a> the first. When McKimson was himself promoted to director, he created yet another version, with more slanted eyes, longer teeth and a much larger mouth. He used this version until 1949 (as did <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Davis_(animator)" title="Arthur Davis (animator)">Art Davis</a> for the one Bugs Bunny film he directed) when he started using the version he had designed for Clampett. Jones would come up with his own slight modification, and the voice had slight variations between the units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barrier_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barrier-11">[11]</a></sup> Bugs also made cameos in Avery's final Warner Bros. cartoon, <i><a href="/wiki/Crazy_Cruise" title="Crazy Cruise">Crazy Cruise</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since Bugs' debut in <i>A Wild Hare</i>, he appeared only in color <i>Merrie Melodies</i> films (making him one of the few recurring characters created for that series in the Schlesinger era prior to the full conversion to color), alongside Elmer predecessor Egghead, <a href="/wiki/Inki" title="Inki">Inki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sniffles" title="Sniffles">Sniffles</a>, and Elmer himself. While Bugs made a cameo in <i><a href="/wiki/Porky_Pig%27s_Feat" title="Porky Pig's Feat">Porky Pig's Feat</a></i> (1943), this was his only appearance in a black-and-white <i>Looney Tunes</i> film. He did not star in a <i>Looney Tunes</i> film until that series made its complete conversion to only color cartoons beginning in 1944. <i><a href="/wiki/Buckaroo_Bugs" title="Buckaroo Bugs">Buckaroo Bugs</a></i> was Bugs' first film in the <i>Looney Tunes</i> series, and was also the last Warner Bros. cartoon to credit Schlesinger (as he had retired and sold his studio to Warner Bros. that year).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bugs' popularity soared during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> because of his free and easy attitude, and he began receiving special star billing in his cartoons by 1943. By that time Warner Bros. had become the most profitable cartoon studio in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In company with cartoon studios such as Disney and <a href="/wiki/Famous_Studios" title="Famous Studios">Famous Studios</a>, Warners pitted its characters against <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">Francisco Franco</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Empire" title="Japanese Empire">Japanese</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Bugs_Bunny_Nips_the_Nips" title="Bugs Bunny Nips the Nips">Bugs Bunny Nips the Nips</a></i> (1944) features Bugs at odds with a group of Japanese soldiers. This cartoon has since been pulled from distribution due to its depiction of Japanese people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bcdb_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-bcdb-23">[23]</a></sup> He also faces off against <a href="/wiki/Hermann_G%C3%B6ring" title="Hermann Göring">Hermann Göring</a> and Hitler in <i><a href="/wiki/Herr_Meets_Hare" title="Herr Meets Hare">Herr Meets Hare</a></i> (1945), which introduced his well-known reference to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albuquerque" title="Albuquerque">Albuquerque</a> as he mistakenly winds up in the <a href="/wiki/Black_Forest" title="Black Forest">Black Forest</a> of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">'Joimany'</a> instead of <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas" title="Las Vegas">Las Vegas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Bugs also appeared in the 1942 two-minute U.S. <a href="/wiki/War_bond" title="War bond">war bonds</a> commercial film <i><a href="/wiki/Any_Bonds_Today%3F" title="Any Bonds Today?">Any Bonds Today?</a></i>, along with Porky and Elmer.</p>
<p>At the end of <i><a href="/wiki/Super-Rabbit" title="Super-Rabbit">Super-Rabbit</a></i> (1943), Bugs appears wearing a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">United States Marine Corps</a> dress blue uniform. As a result, the Marine Corps made Bugs an honorary Marine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Master_Sergeant" title="Master Sergeant">Master Sergeant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> From 1943 to 1946, Bugs was the official mascot of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingman_Army_Airfield" title="Kingman Army Airfield">Kingman Army Airfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingman,_Arizona" title="Kingman, Arizona">Kingman, Arizona</a>, where thousands of aerial gunners were trained during World War II. Some notable trainees included <a href="/wiki/Clark_Gable" title="Clark Gable">Clark Gable</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bronson" title="Charles Bronson">Charles Bronson</a>. Bugs also served as the mascot for 530 Squadron of the 380th Bombardment Group, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/5th_Air_Force" title="5th Air Force">5th Air Force</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a>, which was attached to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Australian_Air_Force" title="Royal Australian Air Force">Royal Australian Air Force</a> and operated out of Australia's <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory" title="Northern Territory">Northern Territory</a> from 1943 to 1945, flying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-24_Liberator" title="B-24 Liberator">B-24 Liberator</a> bombers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Bugs riding an air delivered torpedo served as the squadron logo for Marine Torpedo/Bomber Squadron 242 in the Second World War. Additionally, Bugs appeared on the nose of B-24J #42-110157, in both the 855th Bomb Squadron of the 491st Bombardment Group (Heavy) and later in the 786th BS of the 466th BG(H), both being part of the 8th Air Force operating out of England.</p>

<p>In 1944, Bugs Bunny made a cameo appearance in <i><a href="/wiki/Jasper_Goes_Hunting" title="Jasper Goes Hunting">Jasper Goes Hunting</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Puppetoons" title="Puppetoons">Puppetoons</a> film produced by rival studio <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a>. In this cameo (animated by McKimson, with Blanc providing the usual voice), Bugs (after being threatened at gunpoint) pops out of a rabbit hole, saying his usual catchphrase; after hearing the orchestra play the wrong theme song, he realizes "Hey, I'm in the wrong picture!" and then goes back in the hole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Bugs also made a cameo in the <a href="/wiki/Private_Snafu" title="Private Snafu">Private Snafu</a> short <i><a href="/wiki/Gas_(1944_film)" title="Gas (1944 film)">Gas</a></i>, in which he is found stowed away in the titular private's belongings; his only spoken line is his usual catchphrase.</p>
<p>Although it was usually Porky Pig who brought the <i>Looney Tunes</i> films to a close with his stuttering, "That's all, folks!", Bugs replaced him at the end of <i><a href="/wiki/Hare_Tonic" title="Hare Tonic">Hare Tonic</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Baseball_Bugs" title="Baseball Bugs">Baseball Bugs</a></i>, bursting through a drum just as Porky did, but munching on a carrot and saying in his Bronx-Brooklyn accent, "And that's the end!"</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war_era">Post-war era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bugs_Bunny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post-war era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After World War II, Bugs continued to appear in numerous Warner Bros. cartoons, making his last "Golden Age" appearance in <i><a href="/wiki/False_Hare" title="False Hare">False Hare</a></i> (1964). He starred in over 167 theatrical short films, most of which were directed by Friz Freleng, Robert McKimson and Chuck Jones. Freleng's <i><a href="/wiki/Knighty_Knight_Bugs" title="Knighty Knight Bugs">Knighty Knight Bugs</a></i> (1958), in which a medieval Bugs trades blows with <a href="/wiki/Yosemite_Sam" title="Yosemite Sam">Yosemite Sam</a> and his fire-breathing dragon (which has a cold), won an <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Animated_Short_Film" title="Academy Award for Best Animated Short Film">Academy Award for Best Cartoon Short Subject</a> (becoming the first Bugs Bunny cartoon to win said award).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Three of Jones' films — <i><a href="/wiki/Rabbit_Fire" title="Rabbit Fire">Rabbit Fire</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rabbit_Seasoning" title="Rabbit Seasoning">Rabbit Seasoning</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Duck!_Rabbit,_Duck!" title="Duck! Rabbit, Duck!">Duck! Rabbit, Duck!</a></i> — compose what is often referred to as the "Rabbit Season/Duck Season" trilogy and are famous for originating the "historic" rivalry between Bugs and Daffy Duck.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Jones' classic <i><a href="/wiki/What%27s_Opera,_Doc%3F" title="What's Opera, Doc?">What's Opera, Doc?</a></i> (1957), casts Bugs and Elmer Fudd in a parody of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Ring_des_Nibelungen" title="Der Ring des Nibelungen">Der Ring des Nibelungen</a></i>. It was deemed "culturally significant" by the United States <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a> and selected for preservation in the <a href="/wiki/National_Film_Registry" title="National Film Registry">National Film Registry</a> in 1992, becoming the first cartoon short to receive this honor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the fall of 1960, <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a> debuted the prime-time television program <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bugs_Bunny_Show" title="The Bugs Bunny Show">The Bugs Bunny Show</a></i>. This show packaged many of the post-1948 Warners cartoons with newly animated wraparounds. After two seasons, it was moved from its evening slot to reruns on Saturday mornings. <i>The Bugs Bunny Show</i> changed format and exact title frequently, but remained on network television for 40 years. The packaging was later completely different, with each cartoon simply presented on its own, title and all, though some clips from the new bridging material were sometimes used as filler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bugs_Bunny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Bugs did not appear in any of the post-1964 <i>Looney Tunes</i> and <i>Merrie Melodies</i> films produced by <a href="/wiki/DePatie-Freleng_Enterprises" title="DePatie-Freleng Enterprises">DePatie-Freleng Enterprises</a> or <a href="/wiki/Seven_Arts_Productions" title="Seven Arts Productions">Seven Arts Productions</a>, nor did he appear in the lone <i>Looney Tunes</i> <a href="/wiki/Daffy_Duck_and_Porky_Pig_Meet_the_Groovie_Goolies" title="Daffy Duck and Porky Pig Meet the Groovie Goolies">TV special</a> produced by <a href="/wiki/Filmation" title="Filmation">Filmation</a>. He would not appear in new material on-screen again until <i><a href="/wiki/Bugs_and_Daffy%27s_Carnival_of_the_Animals" title="Bugs and Daffy's Carnival of the Animals">Bugs and Daffy's Carnival of the Animals</a></i> aired in 1976.</p>
<p>From the late 1970s through the early 1990s, Bugs was featured in various animated specials for network television, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Bugs_Bunny%27s_Thanksgiving_Diet" title="Bugs Bunny's Thanksgiving Diet">Bugs Bunny's Thanksgiving Diet</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bugs_Bunny%27s_Looney_Christmas_Tales" title="Bugs Bunny's Looney Christmas Tales">Bugs Bunny's Looney Christmas Tales</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Bugs_Bunny%27s_Bustin%27_Out_All_Over" title="Bugs Bunny's Bustin' Out All Over">Bugs Bunny's Bustin' Out All Over</a></i>. Bugs also starred in several theatrical compilation features during this time, including the <a href="/wiki/United_Artists" title="United Artists">United Artists</a>-distributed documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Bugs_Bunny:_Superstar" title="Bugs Bunny: Superstar">Bugs Bunny: Superstar</a></i> (1975)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and Warner Bros.' own releases: <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bugs_Bunny/Road_Runner_Movie" title="The Bugs Bunny/Road Runner Movie">The Bugs Bunny/Road Runner Movie</a></i> (1979), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Looney_Looney_Looney_Bugs_Bunny_Movie" title="The Looney Looney Looney Bugs Bunny Movie">The Looney Looney Looney Bugs Bunny Movie</a></i> (1981), <i><a href="/wiki/Bugs_Bunny%27s_3rd_Movie:_1001_Rabbit_Tales" title="Bugs Bunny's 3rd Movie: 1001 Rabbit Tales">Bugs Bunny's 3rd Movie: 1001 Rabbit Tales</a></i> (1982) and <i><a href="/wiki/Daffy_Duck%27s_Quackbusters" title="Daffy Duck's Quackbusters">Daffy Duck's Quackbusters</a></i> (1988).</p>
<p>In the 1988 <a href="/wiki/Live-action_animated_film" title="Live-action animated film">live-action/animated</a> comedy, <i><a href="/wiki/Who_Framed_Roger_Rabbit" title="Who Framed Roger Rabbit">Who Framed Roger Rabbit</a></i>, Bugs appeared as one of the inhabitants of <a href="/wiki/Toontown" title="Toontown">Toontown</a>. However, since the film was being produced by <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Pictures" title="Walt Disney Pictures">Disney</a>, Warner Bros. would only allow the use of their biggest star if he got an equal amount of screen time as Disney's biggest star, <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Mouse" title="Mickey Mouse">Mickey Mouse</a>. Because of this, both characters are always together in frame when onscreen. <i>Roger Rabbit</i> was also one of the final productions in which Mel Blanc voiced Bugs (as well as the other <i>Looney Tunes</i> characters) before his death in 1989.</p>
<p>Bugs later appeared in another animated production featuring numerous characters from rival studios: the 1990 drug prevention TV special <i><a href="/wiki/Cartoon_All-Stars_to_the_Rescue" title="Cartoon All-Stars to the Rescue">Cartoon All-Stars to the Rescue</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> This special is notable for being the first time that someone other than Blanc voiced Bugs and Daffy (both characters were voiced by <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Bergman" title="Jeff Bergman">Jeff Bergman</a> for this). Bugs also made guest appearances in the early 1990s television series <i><a href="/wiki/Tiny_Toon_Adventures" title="Tiny Toon Adventures">Tiny Toon Adventures</a></i>, as the principal of Acme Looniversity and the mentor of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babs_and_Buster_Bunny" title="Babs and Buster Bunny">Buster Bunny</a>. He made further cameos in Warner Bros.' subsequent animated TV shows <i><a href="/wiki/Taz-Mania" title="Taz-Mania">Taz-Mania</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Animaniacs" title="Animaniacs">Animaniacs</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Histeria!" title="Histeria!">Histeria!</a></i></p>
<p>Bugs returned to the silver screen in <i><a href="/wiki/Box-Office_Bunny" title="Box-Office Bunny">Box-Office Bunny</a></i> (1990). This was the first Bugs Bunny cartoon since 1964 to be released in theaters and it was created for Bugs' 50th anniversary celebration. It was followed by <i><a href="/wiki/(Blooper)_Bunny" title="(Blooper) Bunny">(Blooper) Bunny</a>,</i> a cartoon that was shelved from theaters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> but later premiered on <a href="/wiki/Cartoon_Network" title="Cartoon Network">Cartoon Network</a> in 1997 and has since gained a cult following among animation fans for its edgy humor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1996, Bugs and the other <i>Looney Tunes</i> characters appeared in the live-action/animated film, <i><a href="/wiki/Space_Jam" title="Space Jam">Space Jam</a></i>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Joe_Pytka" title="Joe Pytka">Joe Pytka</a> and starring <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_Association" title="National Basketball Association">NBA</a> superstar <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jordan" title="Michael Jordan">Michael Jordan</a>. The film also introduced the character <a href="/wiki/Lola_Bunny" title="Lola Bunny">Lola Bunny</a>, who becomes Bugs' new love interest. <i>Space Jam</i> received mixed reviews from critics,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> but was a box office success (grossing over $230 million worldwide).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The success of <i>Space Jam</i> led to the development of another live-action/animated film, <i><a href="/wiki/Looney_Tunes:_Back_in_Action" title="Looney Tunes: Back in Action">Looney Tunes: Back in Action</a></i>, released in 2003 and directed by <a href="/wiki/Joe_Dante" title="Joe Dante">Joe Dante</a>. Unlike <i>Space Jam</i>, <i>Back in Action</i> was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Box-office_bomb" title="Box-office bomb">box-office bomb</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnimatedMovieGuide_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-AnimatedMovieGuide-44">[44]</a></sup> though it did receive more positive reviews from critics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1997, Bugs appeared on a <a href="/wiki/Stamp_collecting" title="Stamp collecting">U.S. postage stamp</a>, the first cartoon to be so honored, beating the iconic Mickey Mouse. The stamp is number seven on the list of the ten most popular U.S. stamps, as calculated by the number of stamps purchased but not used. The introduction of Bugs onto a stamp was controversial at the time, as it was seen as a step toward the 'commercialization' of stamp art. The postal service rejected many designs, and went with a postal-themed drawing. Avery Dennison printed the Bugs Bunny stamp sheet, which featured "a special ten-stamp design and was the first self-adhesive <a href="/wiki/Miniature_sheet" title="Miniature sheet">souvenir sheet</a> issued by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Postal_Service" title="U.S. Postal Service">U.S. Postal Service</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>A younger version of Bugs is the main character of <i><a href="/wiki/Baby_Looney_Tunes" title="Baby Looney Tunes">Baby Looney Tunes</a>,</i> which debuted on <a href="/wiki/Kids%27_WB" title="Kids' WB">Kids' WB</a> in 2001. In the action comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Loonatics_Unleashed" title="Loonatics Unleashed">Loonatics Unleashed</a></i>, his definite descendant Ace Bunny is the leader of the Loonatics team and seems to have inherited his ancestor's Brooklyn accent and comic wit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="More_recent_years">More recent years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bugs_Bunny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: More recent years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bugsbunny2011.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="217" data-file-width="150" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/7/79/Bugsbunny2011.png" width="150"/></a>

<p>In 2011, Bugs Bunny and the rest of the <i>Looney Tunes</i> gang returned to television in the Cartoon Network sitcom, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Looney_Tunes_Show" title="The Looney Tunes Show">The Looney Tunes Show</a></i>. The characters feature new designs by artist Jessica Borutski. Among the changes to Bugs' appearance were the simplification and enlargement of his feet, as well as a change to his fur from gray to a shade of <a href="/wiki/Mauve" title="Mauve">mauve</a> (though in the second season, his fur was changed back to gray).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> In the series, Bugs and Daffy Duck are portrayed as best friends as opposed to their usual pairing as rivals. At the same time, Bugs is more openly annoyed at Daffy's antics in the series (sometimes to the point of aggression), compared to his usual carefree personality from the original cartoons. Bugs and Daffy are close friends with Porky Pig in the series, although Bugs tends to be a more reliable friend to Porky than Daffy is. Bugs also dates Lola Bunny in the show, although at first he finds her to be "crazy" and a bit too talkative (he later learns to accept her personality quirks, similar to his tolerance for Daffy). Unlike the original cartoons, Bugs lives in a regular home, which he shares with Daffy, <a href="/wiki/Tasmanian_Devil_(Looney_Tunes)" title="Tasmanian Devil (Looney Tunes)">Taz</a> (whom he treats as a pet dog) and <a href="/wiki/Speedy_Gonzales" title="Speedy Gonzales">Speedy Gonzales</a>, in the middle of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cul-de-sac" title="Cul-de-sac">cul-de-sac</a> with their neighbors Yosemite Sam, <a href="/wiki/Granny_(Looney_Tunes)" title="Granny (Looney Tunes)">Granny</a> and <a href="/wiki/Witch_Hazel_(Looney_Tunes)" title="Witch Hazel (Looney Tunes)">Witch Lazeh</a>.</p>
<p>In 2015, Bugs starred in the direct-to-video film <i><a href="/wiki/Looney_Tunes:_Rabbits_Run" title="Looney Tunes: Rabbits Run">Looney Tunes: Rabbits Run</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> and later returned to television yet again as the star of Cartoon Network and <a href="/wiki/Boomerang_(TV_channel)" title="Boomerang (TV channel)">Boomerang</a>'s new comedy series <i><a href="/wiki/Wabbit_(TV_series)" title="Wabbit (TV series)">Wabbit. A Looney Tunes Production</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WabbitVariety_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-WabbitVariety-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bugs has also appeared in numerous <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video games</a>, including the <i>Bugs Bunny's Crazy Castle</i> series, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bugs_Bunny_Birthday_Blowout" title="The Bugs Bunny Birthday Blowout">Bugs Bunny Birthday Blowout</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bugs_Bunny_Rabbit_Rampage" title="Bugs Bunny Rabbit Rampage">Bugs Bunny: Rabbit Rampage</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bugs_Bunny_in_Double_Trouble" title="Bugs Bunny in Double Trouble">Bugs Bunny in Double Trouble</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Looney_Tunes_B-Ball" title="Looney Tunes B-Ball">Looney Tunes B-Ball</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Looney_Tunes_Racing" title="Looney Tunes Racing">Looney Tunes Racing</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Looney_Tunes:_Space_Race" title="Looney Tunes: Space Race">Looney Tunes: Space Race</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bugs_Bunny_Lost_in_Time" title="Bugs Bunny Lost in Time">Bugs Bunny Lost in Time</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bugs_Bunny_and_Taz_Time_Busters" title="Bugs Bunny and Taz Time Busters">Bugs Bunny and Taz Time Busters</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Loons:_The_Fight_for_Fame" title="Loons: The Fight for Fame">Loons: The Fight for Fame</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Looney_Tunes:_Acme_Arsenal" title="Looney Tunes: Acme Arsenal">Looney Tunes: Acme Arsenal</a></i>, <i>Scooby Doo and Looney Tunes: Cartoon Universe</i> and <i>Looney Tunes Dash</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personality_and_catchphrases">Personality and catchphrases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bugs_Bunny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Personality and catchphrases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:30em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"Some people call me cocky and brash, but actually I am just self-assured. I'm nonchalant, im­perturbable, contemplative. I play it cool, but I can get hot under the collar. And above all I'm a very 'aware' character. I'm well aware that I am appearing in an animated car­toon....And sometimes I chomp on my carrot for the same reason that a stand-up comic chomps on his cigar. It saves me from rushing from the last joke to the next one too fast. And I sometimes don't act, I react. And I always treat the contest with my pursuers as 'fun and games.' When momentarily I appear to be cornered or in dire danger and I scream, don't be consoined – it's actually a big put-on. Let's face it, Doc. I've read the script and I al­ready know how it turns out."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">—<a href="/wiki/Bob_Clampett" title="Bob Clampett">Bob Clampett</a> on Bugs Bunny, written in <a href="/wiki/First-person_narrative" title="First-person narrative">first person</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WBCharDesign_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-WBCharDesign-54">[54]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Bugs Bunny is characterized as being clever and capable of outsmarting anyone who antagonizes him, including <a href="/wiki/Elmer_Fudd" title="Elmer Fudd">Elmer Fudd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yosemite_Sam" title="Yosemite Sam">Yosemite Sam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tasmanian_Devil_(Looney_Tunes)" title="Tasmanian Devil (Looney Tunes)">Tasmanian Devil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marvin_the_Martian" title="Marvin the Martian">Marvin the Martian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wile_E._Coyote_and_The_Road_Runner" title="Wile E. Coyote and The Road Runner">Wile E. Coyote</a>, <a href="/wiki/Witch_Hazel_(Looney_Tunes)" title="Witch Hazel (Looney Tunes)">Witch Hazel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rocky_and_Mugsy" title="Rocky and Mugsy">Rocky and Mugsy</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Looney_Tunes_characters#The_Crusher" title="List of Looney Tunes characters">The Crusher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beaky_Buzzard" title="Beaky Buzzard">Beaky Buzzard</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Looney_Tunes_characters#Willoughby" title="List of Looney Tunes characters">Willoughby the dog</a>, <a href="/wiki/Count_Blood_Count" title="Count Blood Count">Count Blood Count</a> and a host of others.</p>
<p>(For the record, one of the characters who holds the rare distinction of defeating Bugs is <a href="/wiki/Cecil_Turtle" title="Cecil Turtle">Cecil Turtle</a>, following the pattern <a href="/wiki/Aesop" title="Aesop">Aesop</a>'s famous <a href="/wiki/Fable" title="Fable">fable</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tortoise_and_the_Hare" title="The Tortoise and the Hare">The Tortoise and the Hare</a></i>, where the rabbit was the antagonist, while the turtle was the protagonist (Bugs and Cecil, respectively). Their encounters were depicted in <i><a href="/wiki/Tortoise_Beats_Hare" title="Tortoise Beats Hare">Tortoise Beats Hare</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Tortoise_Wins_by_a_Hare" title="Tortoise Wins by a Hare">Tortoise Wins by a Hare</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Rabbit_Transit_(film)" title="Rabbit Transit (film)">Rabbit Transit</a></i>)</p>
<p>Bugs almost always wins these conflicts, a plot pattern which recurs in <i>Looney Tunes</i> films directed by <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Jones" title="Chuck Jones">Chuck Jones</a>. Concerned that viewers would lose sympathy for an aggressive protagonist who always won, Jones arranged for Bugs to be bullied, cheated, or threatened by the <a href="/wiki/Antagonist" title="Antagonist">antagonists</a> while minding his own business, justifying his subsequent antics as retaliation or self-defense. He's also been known to break the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_wall" title="Fourth wall">fourth wall</a> by "communicating" with the audience, either by explaining the situation (e.g. "Be with you in a minute, folks!"), describing someone to the audience (e.g. "Feisty, ain't they?"), clueing in on the story (e.g. "That happens to him all during the picture, folks."), explaining that one of his antagonists' actions have pushed him to the breaking point ("Of course you realize, this means war."), admitting his own deviousness toward his antagonists ("Gee, ain't I a stinker?"), etc.</p>
<p>Bugs will usually try to placate the antagonist and avoid conflict, but when an antagonist pushes him too far, Bugs may address the audience and invoke his catchphrase "Of course you realize this means <i>war!</i>" before he retaliates, and the retaliation will be devastating. This line was taken from <a href="/wiki/Groucho_Marx" title="Groucho Marx">Groucho Marx</a> and others in the 1933 film <i><a href="/wiki/Duck_Soup_(1933_film)" title="Duck Soup (1933 film)">Duck Soup</a></i> and was also used in the 1935 Marx film <i><a href="/wiki/A_Night_at_the_Opera_(film)" title="A Night at the Opera (film)">A Night at the Opera</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duck_Soup_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Duck_Soup-55">[55]</a></sup> Bugs would pay homage to Groucho in other ways, such as occasionally adopting his stooped walk or leering eyebrow-raising (in <i><a href="/wiki/Hair-Raising_Hare" title="Hair-Raising Hare">Hair-Raising Hare</a></i>, for example) or sometimes with a direct impersonation (as in <i><a href="/wiki/Slick_Hare" title="Slick Hare">Slick Hare</a></i>). Other directors, such as <a href="/wiki/Friz_Freleng" title="Friz Freleng">Friz Freleng</a>, characterized Bugs as altruistic. When Bugs meets other successful characters (such as Cecil Turtle in <i><a href="/wiki/Tortoise_Beats_Hare" title="Tortoise Beats Hare">Tortoise Beats Hare</a></i>, or the Gremlin in <i><a href="/wiki/Falling_Hare" title="Falling Hare">Falling Hare</a></i>), his overconfidence becomes a disadvantage.</p>
<p>Bugs' nonchalant carrot-chewing standing position, as explained by Freleng, Jones and <a href="/wiki/Bob_Clampett" title="Bob Clampett">Bob Clampett</a>, originated in a scene from the 1934 film <i><a href="/wiki/It_Happened_One_Night" title="It Happened One Night">It Happened One Night</a></i>, in which <a href="/wiki/Clark_Gable" title="Clark Gable">Clark Gable</a>'s character Peter Warne leans against a fence, eating carrots rapidly and talking with his mouth full to <a href="/wiki/Claudette_Colbert" title="Claudette Colbert">Claudette Colbert</a>'s character. This scene was well known while the film was popular, and viewers at the time likely recognized Bugs Bunny's behavior as <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satire</a>. Coincidentally, the film also features a minor character, Oscar Shapely, who addresses Peter Warne as "Doc", and Warne mentions an imaginary person named "Bugs Dooley" to frighten Shapely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-filmsite_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-filmsite-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"'What's up Doc?' is a very simple thing. It's only funny because it's in a situation. It was an all Bugs Bunny line. It wasn't funny. If you put it in human terms; you come home late one night from work, you walk up to the gate in the yard, you walk through the gate and up into the front room, the door is partly open and there's some guy shooting under your living room. So what do you do? You run if you have any sense, the least you can do is call the cops. But what if you come up and tap him on the shoulder and look over and say 'What's up Doc?' You're interested in what he's doing. That's ridiculous. That's not what you say at a time like that. So that's why it's funny, I think. In other words it's asking a perfectly legitimate question in a perfectly illogical situation."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">—<a href="/wiki/Chuck_Jones" title="Chuck Jones">Chuck Jones</a> on Bugs Bunny's catchphrase "What's up Doc?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>The carrot-chewing scenes are generally followed by Bugs' most well-known catchphrase, "What's up, Doc?", which was written by director <a href="/wiki/Tex_Avery" title="Tex Avery">Tex Avery</a> for his first Bugs Bunny film, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Wild_Hare" title="A Wild Hare">A Wild Hare</a></i> (1940). Avery explained later that it was a common expression in his native Texas and that he did not think much of the phrase. When the cartoon was first screened in theaters, the "What's up, Doc?" scene generated a tremendously positive audience reaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Avery_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Avery-16">[16]</a></sup> As a result, the scene became a recurring element in subsequent cartoons. The phrase was sometimes modified for a situation. For example, Bugs says "What's up, dogs?" to the antagonists in <i><a href="/wiki/A_Hare_Grows_in_Manhattan" title="A Hare Grows in Manhattan">A Hare Grows in Manhattan</a></i>, "What's up, Duke?" to the knight in <i><a href="/wiki/Knight-mare_Hare" title="Knight-mare Hare">Knight-mare Hare</a></i> and "What's up, prune-face?" to the aged Elmer in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Old_Grey_Hare" title="The Old Grey Hare">The Old Grey Hare</a></i>. He might also greet Daffy with "What's up, Duck?" He used one variation, "What's all the hub-bub, bub?" only once, in <i><a href="/wiki/Falling_Hare" title="Falling Hare">Falling Hare</a>.</i> Another variation is used in <i><a href="/wiki/Looney_Tunes:_Back_in_Action" title="Looney Tunes: Back in Action">Looney Tunes: Back in Action</a></i>, when he greets a <a href="/wiki/Raygun" title="Raygun">blaster</a>-wielding Marvin the Martian saying "What's up, <a href="/wiki/Darth_Vader" title="Darth Vader">Darth</a>?"</p>
<p>In many of Bugs' appearances in the 1940s <i>Merrie Melodies</i> and <i>Looney Tunes</i> shorts, his carrot-chewing made its way into the opening sequence of the cartoon. In these cases, Bugs would be lying atop the Warner Brothers shield logo as it came onto the screen and eating his carrot. After a few seconds, Bugs would stop eating and shoot the audience a dirty look for staring at him. From there, one of two things would happen. Frequently, the open would simply dissolve into the cartoon series logo, but on occasion Bugs would reach up to the top of the screen and pull the logo down like a curtain to give himself some privacy. This formed the basis for the later intro to Bugs' cartoons, where he would pull the bottom of the screen up and be shown sitting atop his own intro screen while eating a carrot.</p>
<p>Several Chuck Jones films in the late 1940s and 1950s depict Bugs travelling via cross-country (and, in some cases, intercontinental) tunnel-digging, ending up in places as varied as Barcelona, Spain (<i><a href="/wiki/Bully_for_Bugs" title="Bully for Bugs">Bully for Bugs</a></i>), the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Abominable_Snow_Rabbit" title="The Abominable Snow Rabbit">The Abominable Snow Rabbit</a></i>) and <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Frigid_Hare" title="Frigid Hare">Frigid Hare</a></i>) all because he "shoulda taken that left toin at <a href="/wiki/Albuquerque,_New_Mexico" title="Albuquerque, New Mexico">Albukoikee</a>." He first utters that phrase in <i><a href="/wiki/Herr_Meets_Hare" title="Herr Meets Hare">Herr Meets Hare</a></i> (1945), when he emerges in the <a href="/wiki/Black_Forest" title="Black Forest">Black Forest</a>, a cartoon seldom seen today due to its blatantly topical subject matter. When <a href="/wiki/Hermann_G%C3%B6ring" title="Hermann Göring">Hermann Göring</a> says to Bugs, "There is no Las Vegas in 'Chermany'" and takes a potshot at Bugs, Bugs dives into his hole and says, "Joimany! Yipe!", as Bugs realizes he's behind enemy lines. The confused response to his "left toin" comment also followed a pattern. For example, when he tunnels into Scotland in <i><a href="/wiki/My_Bunny_Lies_over_the_Sea" title="My Bunny Lies over the Sea">My Bunny Lies over the Sea</a></i> (1948), while thinking he's heading for the <a href="/wiki/La_Brea_Tar_Pits" title="La Brea Tar Pits">La Brea Tar Pits</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles, California</a>, it provides another chance for an ethnic joke: "Therrre arrre no La Brrrea Tarrr Pits in Scotland!" (to which Bugs responds, "Scotland!? Eh...what's up, Mac-doc?"). A couple of late-1950s/early-1960s cartoons of this ilk also featured Daffy Duck travelling with Bugs ("Since when is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pismo_Beach" title="Pismo Beach">Pismo Beach</a> inside a cave?!").</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice_actors">Voice actors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bugs_Bunny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Voice actors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following are the various vocal artists who have voiced Bugs Bunny over the last 75-plus years for Warner Bros.' animated productions:</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mel_Blanc_-_1959.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="749" data-file-width="582" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Mel_Blanc_-_1959.jpg/170px-Mel_Blanc_-_1959.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Mel_Blanc_-_1959.jpg/255px-Mel_Blanc_-_1959.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Mel_Blanc_-_1959.jpg/340px-Mel_Blanc_-_1959.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mel_Blanc_-_1959.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Mel Blanc was the original voice of Bugs and would voice the character for nearly five decades.</div>

<dt><a href="/wiki/Mel_Blanc" title="Mel Blanc">Mel Blanc</a></dt>
</dl>
<p>Mel Blanc voiced the character for almost 50 years, from Bugs' debut in the 1940 short <i><a href="/wiki/A_Wild_Hare" title="A Wild Hare">A Wild Hare</a></i> until Blanc's death in 1989. Blanc described the voice as a combination of <a href="/wiki/The_Bronx" title="The Bronx">Bronx</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a> accents; however, <a href="/wiki/Tex_Avery" title="Tex Avery">Tex Avery</a> claimed that he asked Blanc to give the character not a New York accent <i>per se</i>, but a voice like that of actor <a href="/wiki/Frank_McHugh" title="Frank McHugh">Frank McHugh</a>, who frequently appeared in supporting roles in the 1930s and whose voice might be described as <a href="/wiki/Irish_Americans" title="Irish Americans">New York Irish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barrier_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Barrier-11">[11]</a></sup> In Bugs' second cartoon <i><a href="/wiki/Elmer%27s_Pet_Rabbit" title="Elmer's Pet Rabbit">Elmer's Pet Rabbit</a></i>, Blanc created a completely new voice for Bugs, which sounded like a <a href="/wiki/James_Stewart" title="James Stewart">Jimmy Stewart</a> impression, but the directors decided the previous voice was better. Though Blanc's best-known character was the carrot-chomping rabbit, munching on the carrots interrupted the dialogue. Various substitutes, such as <a href="/wiki/Celery" title="Celery">celery</a>, were tried, but none of them <i>sounded</i> like a carrot. So for the sake of expedience, he would munch and then spit the carrot bits into a <a href="/wiki/Spittoon" title="Spittoon">spittoon</a> rather than swallowing them, and continue with the dialogue. One often-repeated story, possibly originating from <i><a href="/wiki/Bugs_Bunny:_Superstar" title="Bugs Bunny: Superstar">Bugs Bunny: Superstar</a></i>, is that Blanc was allergic to carrots and <i>had</i> to spit them out to minimize any allergic reaction — but his autobiography makes no such claim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanc_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blanc-13">[13]</a></sup> In fact, in a 1984 interview with <a href="/wiki/Tim_Lawson_(writer)" title="Tim Lawson (writer)">Tim Lawson</a>, co-author of <i>The Magic Behind The Voices: A Who's Who of Voice Actors</i>, Blanc emphatically denied being allergic to carrots.</p>

<p>Like <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Mouse" title="Mickey Mouse">Mickey Mouse</a> for <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">Disney</a>, Bugs Bunny has served as the mascot for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Entertainment" title="Warner Bros. Entertainment">Warner Bros.</a> and its various divisions. According to <i><a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness World Records</a></i>, Bugs has appeared in more films (both short and feature-length) than any other cartoon <a href="/wiki/Character_(arts)" title="Character (arts)">character</a> and is the ninth most-portrayed film personality in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guinness_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Guinness-4">[4]</a></sup> On December 10, 1985, Bugs became the second cartoon character (after Mickey) to receive a star on the <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Walk_of_Fame" title="Hollywood Walk of Fame">Hollywood Walk of Fame</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hollywood_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hollywood-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>He also has been a pitchman for companies including <a href="/wiki/Kool-Aid" title="Kool-Aid">Kool-Aid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nike,_Inc." title="Nike, Inc.">Nike</a>. His Nike commercials with <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jordan" title="Michael Jordan">Michael Jordan</a> as "Hare Jordan" for the Air Jordan VII and VIII became precursors to <i><a href="/wiki/Space_Jam" title="Space Jam">Space Jam</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> As a result, he has spent time as an honorary member of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jordan_Brand" title="Jordan Brand">Jordan Brand</a>, including having Jordan's Jumpman logo done in his image.</p>
<p>In 2002, <i><a href="/wiki/TV_Guide" title="TV Guide">TV Guide</a></i> compiled a list of the 50 greatest cartoon characters of all time as part of the magazine's 50th anniversary. Bugs Bunny was given the honor of number 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNNBugs_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNNBugs-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> In a <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a> broadcast on July 31, 2002, a <i>TV Guide</i> editor talked about the group that created the list. The editor also explained why Bugs pulled top billing: "His stock...has never gone down...Bugs is the best example...of the smart-aleck American comic. He not only is a great cartoon character, he's a great comedian. He was written well. He was drawn beautifully. He has thrilled and made many generations laugh. He is tops."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
