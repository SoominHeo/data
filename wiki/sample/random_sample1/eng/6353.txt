<p><b>Translation studies</b> is an academic interdiscipline dealing with the systematic study of the theory, description and application of <a href="/wiki/Translation" title="Translation">translation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interpreting" title="Interpreting">interpreting</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Language_localisation" title="Language localisation">localization</a>. As an interdiscipline, Translation Studies borrows much from the various fields of study that support translation. These include <a href="/wiki/Comparative_literature" title="Comparative literature">comparative literature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philology" title="Philology">philology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Semiotics" title="Semiotics">semiotics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Terminology" title="Terminology">terminology</a>.</p>
<p>The term <i>translation studies</i> was coined by the Amsterdam-based American scholar James S. Holmes in his paper "The name and nature of translation studies",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> which is considered a foundational statement for the discipline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In English, writers occasionally use the term "<b>translatology</b>" (and less commonly "<b>traductology</b>") to refer to translation studies, and the corresponding French term for the discipline is usually <i>traductologie</i> (as in the <a class="external text" href="http://www.societe-francaise-traductologie.com/" rel="nofollow">Société Française de Traductologie</a>). In the United States there is a preference for the term <i>Translation and Interpreting Studies</i> (as in the <a class="external text" href="http://www.atisa.org/" rel="nofollow">American Translation and Interpreting Studies Association</a>), although European tradition includes interpreting within translation studies (as in the <a href="/wiki/European_Society_for_Translation_Studies" title="European Society for Translation Studies">European Society for Translation Studies</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_studies">Early studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Historically, translation studies has long been <i>prescriptive</i> (telling translators how to translate), to the point that discussions of translation that were not prescriptive were generally not considered to be about translation at all. When historians of translation studies trace early Western thought about translation, for example, they most often set the beginning at <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a>'s remarks on how he used translation from Greek to Latin to improve his oratorical abilities—an early description of what <a href="/wiki/Jerome" title="Jerome">Jerome</a> ended up calling <a href="/wiki/Sense-for-sense_translation" title="Sense-for-sense translation">sense-for-sense translation</a>. The descriptive history of interpreters in Egypt provided by <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> several centuries earlier is typically not thought of as translation studies—presumably because it does not tell translators how to translate. In <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, the discussion on <a href="/wiki/Chinese_translation_theory" title="Chinese translation theory">how to translate</a> originated with the translation of <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road_transmission_of_Buddhism" title="Silk Road transmission of Buddhism">Buddhist sutras</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">Han Dynasty</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Calls_for_an_academic_discipline">Calls for an academic discipline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Calls for an academic discipline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1958, at the Second Congress of Slavists in Moscow, the debate between linguistic and literary approaches to translation reached a point where it was proposed that the best thing might be to have a separate science that was able to study all forms of translation, without being wholly within Linguistics or wholly within Literary Studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Within Comparative Literature, translation workshops were promoted in the 1960s in some American universities like the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Iowa" title="University of Iowa">University of Iowa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> During the 1950s and 1960s, systematic linguistic-oriented studies of translation began to appear. In 1958, the French linguists Jean-Paul Vinay and Jean Darbelnet carried out a contrastive comparison of French and English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1964, <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Nida" title="Eugene Nida">Eugene Nida</a> published <i>Toward a Science of Translating</i>, a manual for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bible_translation" title="Bible translation">Bible translation</a> influenced to some extent by <a href="/wiki/Zellig_Harris" title="Zellig Harris">Harris</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Transformational_grammar" title="Transformational grammar">transformational grammar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1965, <a href="/wiki/Ian_Catford" title="Ian Catford">John C. Catford</a> theorized translation from a linguistic perspective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In the 1960s and early 1970s, the Czech scholar <a href="/wiki/Ji%C5%99%C3%AD_Lev%C3%BD" title="Jiří Levý">Jiří Levý</a> and the Slovak scholars <a href="/wiki/Anton_Popovi%C4%8D" title="Anton Popovič">Anton Popovič</a> and František Miko worked on the stylists of literary translation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>These initial steps toward research on literary translation were collected in James S. Holmes' paper at the Third International Congress of Applied Linguistics held in <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a> in 1972. In that paper, "The name and nature of translation studies", Holmes asked for the consolidation of a separate discipline and proposed a classification of the field. A visual "map" of Holmes' proposal would later be presented by <a href="/wiki/Gideon_Toury" title="Gideon Toury">Gideon Toury</a> in his 1995 <i>Descriptive Translation Studies and beyond</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prior to the 1990s, translation scholars tended to form particular schools of thought, particularly within the prescriptive, descriptive, and Skopos paradigms. Since the "cultural turn" in the 1990s, the discipline has tended to divide into separate fields of inquiry, where research projects run parallel to each other, borrowing methodologies from each other and from other academic disciplines.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Schools_of_thought">Schools of thought</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Schools of thought">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main schools of thought on the level of research have tended to cluster around key theoretical concepts, most of which have become objects of debate.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Equivalence">Equivalence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Equivalence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Through to the 1950s and 1960s, discussions in translation studies tended to concern how best to attain "equivalence". The term "equivalence" had two distinct meanings, corresponding to different schools of thought. In the Russian tradition, "equivalence" was usually a one-to-one correspondence between linguistic forms, or a pair of authorized technical terms or phrases, such that "equivalence" was opposed to a range of "substitutions". However, in the French tradition of Vinay and Darbelnet, drawing on <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bally" title="Charles Bally">Bally</a>, "equivalence" was the attainment of equal functional value, generally requiring <i>changes</i> in form. <a href="/wiki/Ian_Catford" title="Ian Catford">Catford</a>'s notion of equivalence in 1965 was as in the French tradition. In the course of the 1970s, Russian theorists adopted the wider sense of "equivalence" as something <i>resulting</i> from linguistic transformations.</p>
<p>At about the same time, the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Interpretive_Theory_of_Translation" title="The Interpretive Theory of Translation">Interpretive Theory of Translation</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> introduced the notion of deverbalized sense into translation studies, drawing a distinction between word correspondences and sense equivalences, and showing the difference between dictionary definitions of words and phrases (word correspondences) and the sense of texts or fragments thereof in a given context (sense equivalences).</p>
<p>The discussions of equivalence accompanied typologies of translation solutions (also called "procedures", "techniques" or "strategies"), as in Fedorov (1953) and Vinay and Darbelnet (1958). In 1958 Loh Dianyang's <i>Translation: Its Principles and Techniques</i> (英汉翻译理论与技巧) drew on Fedorov and English linguistics to present a typology of translation solutions between Chinese and English.</p>
<p>In these traditions, discussions of the ways to attain equivalence have mostly been prescriptive and have been related to translator training.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Descriptive_translation_studies">Descriptive translation studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Descriptive translation studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Descriptive translation studies (a term coined after Toury's 1995 book <i>Descriptive Translation Studies and Beyond</i>) aims at building an empirical descriptive discipline, to fill one section of the Holmes map. The idea that scientific methodology could be applicable to cultural products had been developed by the Russian Formalists in the early years of the 20th century, and had been recovered by various researchers in <a href="/wiki/Comparative_literature" title="Comparative literature">Comparative Literature</a>. It was now applied to literary translation. Part of this application was the theory of polysystems (Even-Zohar 1990<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>) in which translated literature is seen as a sub-system of the receiving or target literary system. Gideon Toury bases his theory on the need to consider translations "facts of the target culture" for the purposes of research. The concepts of "manipulation"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and "patronage"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> have also been developed in relation to literary translations.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Skopos_theory"><i>Skopos</i> theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Skopos theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another paradigm shift in translation theory can be dated from 1984 in Europe. That year saw the publication of two books in German: <i>Foundation for a General Theory of Translation</i> by <a href="/wiki/Katharina_Reiss" title="Katharina Reiss">Katharina Reiss</a> (also written Reiß) and <a href="/wiki/Hans_Vermeer" title="Hans Vermeer">Hans Vermeer</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and <i>Translatorial Action</i> (Translatorisches Handeln) by Justa Holz-Mänttäri.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> From these two came what is known as <a href="/wiki/Skopos_theory" title="Skopos theory">Skopos theory</a>, which gives priority to the purpose to be fulfilled by the translation instead of prioritizing equivalence.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_translation">Cultural translation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cultural translation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The cultural turn meant still another step forward in the development of the discipline. It was sketched by <a href="/wiki/Susan_Bassnett" title="Susan Bassnett">Susan Bassnett</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Lefevere" title="André Lefevere">André Lefevere</a> in <i>Translation - History - Culture</i>, and quickly represented by the exchanges between translation studies and other area studies and concepts: <a href="/wiki/Gender_studies" title="Gender studies">gender studies</a>, cannibalism, post-colonialism<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> or cultural studies, among others.</p>
<p>The concept of "cultural translation" largely ensues from <a href="/wiki/Homi_K._Bhabha" title="Homi K. Bhabha">Homi Bhabha</a>'s reading of <a href="/wiki/Salman_Rushdie" title="Salman Rushdie">Salman Rushdie</a> in <i>The Location of Culture</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cultural_translation" title="Cultural translation">Cultural translation</a> is a concept used in <a href="/wiki/Cultural_studies" title="Cultural studies">cultural studies</a> to denote the process of transformation, linguistic or otherwise, in a given <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>. The concept uses linguistic translation as a tool or metaphor in analyzing the nature of transformation and interchange in cultures. "Nonetheless, despite the fact that translation brings cultures nearer, in each translation, there will be a definite deformation between cultures."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eco-translatology">Eco-translatology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Eco-translatology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Eco-translatology is a research orientation developed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hu_Gengshen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hu Gengshen (page does not exist)">Hu Gengshen</a> of the Macao Polytechnic Institute. It sees translation as being involved in processes of adaptation and selection across cultures, focusing on the integrity of translational ecosystems and the central role of the translator. Its key concepts are "translator-centeredness", "eco-balance", and translation as working on "textual transplants". The International Association for Eco-translatology Research has organized five symposia on eco-translatogy and has published the <i>Journal of Eco-Translatology</i> since 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fields_of_inquiry">Fields of inquiry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Fields of inquiry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Translation_history">Translation history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Translation history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Translation history concerns the history of translators as a professional and social group, as well as the history of translations as indicators of the way cultures develop, interact, and may die. Some principles for translation history have been proposed by Lieven D'hulst<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Pym" title="Anthony Pym">Pym</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Major projects in translation history have included the <a class="external text" href="https://global.oup.com/academic/content/series/o/oxford-history-of-literary-translation-in-english-hlte/?cc=es&amp;lang=en&amp;" rel="nofollow">Oxford History of Literary Translation in English</a> and <a class="external text" href="http://editions-verdier.fr/livre/histoire-des-traductions-en-langue-francaise/" rel="nofollow">Histoire des traductions en langue française</a>.</p>
<p>Historical anthologies of translation theories have been compiled by <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Robinson_(academic)" title="Douglas Robinson (academic)">Robinson</a> (2002)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> for Western theories up to Nietzsche; by D'hulst (1990)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> for French theories, 1748–1847; by Santoyo (1987)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> for the Spanish tradition; by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Balcerzan" title="Edward Balcerzan">Edward Balcerzan</a> (1977)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> for the Polish experience, 1440–1974; and by <a href="/wiki/Martha_Cheung" title="Martha Cheung">Cheung</a> (2006)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> for Chinese.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociologies_of_translation">Sociologies of translation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sociologies of translation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The sociology of translation includes the study of who translators are, what their forms of work are (workplace studies), and what data on translations can say about the movements of ideas between languages.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postcolonial_translation_studies">Postcolonial translation studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Postcolonial translation studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Postcolonial studies look at translations between a metropolis and former colonies, or within complex former colonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> They radically question the assumption that translation occurs between cultures and languages that are radically separated.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gender_studies">Gender studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Gender studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gender studies look at the sexuality of translators,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> at the gendered nature of the texts they translate,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> at the possibly gendered translation processes employed, and at the gendered metaphors used to describe translation. Pioneering studies are by Luise von Flotow, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sherry_Simon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sherry Simon (page does not exist)">Sherry Simon</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sherry_Simon" title="de:Sherry Simon">de</a>)</span>, and Keith Harvey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The effacement or inability to efface threatening forms of same-sex sexuality is a topic taken up, when for instance ancient writers are translated by Renaissance thinkers in a Christian context <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethics">Ethics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Ethics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the field of ethics, much discussed publications have been the essays of <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Berman" title="Antoine Berman">Antoine Berman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Venuti" title="Lawrence Venuti">Lawrence Venuti</a> that differ in some aspects but agree on the idea of emphasizing the differences between source and target language and culture when translating. Both are interested in how the "cultural other [...] can best preserve [...] that otherness".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In more recent studies scholars have applied <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_Levinas" title="Emmanuel Levinas">Emmanuel Levinas</a>’ philosophical work on ethics and subjectivity on this issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> As his publications have been interpreted in different ways, various conclusions on his concept of ethical responsibility have been drawn from this. Some have come to the assumption that the idea of translation itself could be ethically doubtful, while others receive it as a call for considering the relationship between <a href="/wiki/Author" title="Author">author</a> or text and <a href="/wiki/Translation#Translators" title="Translation">translator</a> as more interpersonal, thus making it an equal and reciprocal process.</p>
<p>Parallel to these studies the general recognition of the translator's responsibility has increased. More and more translators and interpreters are being seen as active participants in geopolitical conflicts, which raises the question of how to act ethically independent from their own identity or judgement. This leads to the conclusion that translating and interpreting cannot be considered solely as a process of <a href="/wiki/Language_transfer" title="Language transfer">language transfer</a>, but also as socially and politically directed activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is general agreement on the need for an ethical <a href="/wiki/Ethical_code#Code_of_practice_.28professional_ethics.29" title="Ethical code">code of practice</a> providing some guiding principles to reduce uncertainties and improve professionalism, as having been stated in other disciplines (for example <a href="/wiki/Military_medical_ethics" title="Military medical ethics">military medical ethics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Legal_ethics" title="Legal ethics">legal ethics</a>). However, as there is still no clear understanding of the concept of <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a> in this field, opinions about the particular appearance of such a code vary considerably.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Audiovisual_translation_studies">Audiovisual translation studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Audiovisual translation studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Audiovisual translation studies (AVT) is concerned with <a href="/wiki/Translation" title="Translation">translation</a> that takes place in audio and/or visual settings, such as the cinema, television, video games and also some live events such as opera performances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The common denominator for studies in this field is that translation is carried out on multiple semiotic systems, as the translated texts (so-called polysemiotic texts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>) have messages that are conveyed through more than one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semiotic" title="Semiotic">semiotic</a> channel, i.e. not just through the written or spoken word, but also via sound and/or images.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The main translation modes under study are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtitling" title="Subtitling">subtitling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dubbing_(filmmaking)" title="Dubbing (filmmaking)">dubbing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Voice-over" title="Voice-over">voice-over</a>, but also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surtitling" title="Surtitling">surtitling</a> for the opera and theatre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Media accessibility studies is often considered a part of this field as well,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Audio_description" title="Audio description">audio description for the blind and partially sighted</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtitling#SDH" title="Subtitling">subtitles for the deaf or hard-of-hearing</a> being the main objects of study. In audiovisual translation studies, the various conditions and constraints imposed by the different media forms and translation modes influence how translation is carried out, and this often at the heart of most studies of the product or process of AVT. Many researchers in the field of AVT Studies are organized in the European Association for Studies in Screen Translation (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=ESIST&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="ESIST (page does not exist)">ESIST</a>), as are many practitioners in the field.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-professional_translation">Non-professional translation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Non-professional translation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Non-professional translation refers to the translation activities performed by translators who are not working professionally, usually in ways made possible by the Internet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> These practices have mushroomed with the recent <a href="/wiki/Democratization_of_technology" title="Democratization of technology">democratization of technology</a> and the popularization of the Internet. Volunteer translation initiatives have emerged all around the world, and deal with the translations of various types of written and multimedia products.</p>
<p>Normally, it is not required for volunteers to have been trained in translation, but trained translators could also participate, such as the case of Translators without Borders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Depending on the feature that each scholar considers the most important, different terms have been used to label <i>non-professional translation</i>. O'Hagan has used <i>user-generated translation</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Fan_translation" title="Fan translation">fan translation</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> and <i>community translation</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Fernández-Costales and Jiménez-Crespo prefer <i>collaborative translation</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> while Pérez-González labels it <i>amateur subtitling</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Pym proposes that the fundamental difference between this type of translation and professional translation relies on monetary reward, and he suggests it should be called <i>volunteer translation</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some of the most popular fan-controlled non-professional translation practices are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fansubbing" title="Fansubbing">Fansubbing</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fandubbing&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fandubbing (page does not exist)">Fandubbing</a>, <a href="/wiki/ROM_hacking" title="ROM hacking">ROM hacking</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fan_translation_of_video_games" title="Fan translation of video games">Fan translation of video games</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scanlation" title="Scanlation">Scanlation</a>. These practices are mostly supported by a strong and consolidated fan base, although larger non-professional translation projects normally apply <a href="/wiki/Crowdsourcing" title="Crowdsourcing">Crowdsourcing</a> models and are controlled by companies or organizations. Since 2008 <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> has used <a href="/wiki/Crowdsourcing" title="Crowdsourcing">crowdsourcing</a> to have its website translated by its users, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TED_conference" title="TED conference">TED conference</a> has set up an Open Translation Project<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> in which volunteers use the Amara<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> platform to create subtitles online for the TED talks.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Localization">Localization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Localization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Studies of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Language_localization" title="Language localization">localization</a> concern the way the contemporary language industries translate and adapt ("localize") technical texts across languages, tailoring them for a specific "<a href="/wiki/Locale_(computer_software)" title="Locale (computer software)">locale</a>" (a target location defined by language variety and various cultural parameters). Localization usually concerns software, product documentation, websites and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Game_localization" title="Game localization">video games</a>, where the technological component is key.</p>
<p>A key concept in localization is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Localization_and_internationalization" title="Localization and internationalization">internationalization</a>, in which the start product is stripped of its culture-specific features in such a way that it can be simultaneously localized into several languages.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Translator_education">Translator education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Translator education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpreting_Studies">Interpreting Studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Interpreting Studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The discipline of Interpreting Studies is often referred to as the sister of Translation Studies. This is due to the similarities between the two languages, consisting in the transfer of ideas from one language into another. Indeed, interpreting as an activity was long seen as a specialized form of translation, before scientifically founded Interpreting Studies emancipated gradually from Translation Studies in the second half of the 20th century. While they were strongly oriented towards the theoretic framework of Translation Studies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Interpreting Studies have always been concentrating on the practical and pedagogical aspect of the activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> This led to the steady emancipation of the discipline and the consecutive development of a separate theoretical framework based - as are Translation Studies - on interdisciplinary premises. Interpreting Studies have developed several approaches and undergone various paradigm shifts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> leading to the most recent surge of sociological studies of interpreters and their work(ing conditions).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cognition_and_process_studies">Cognition and process studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Cognition and process studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Translation_technologies">Translation technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Translation technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_prospects">Future prospects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Translation_studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Future prospects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Translation studies has developed alongside the growth in translation schools and courses at university level. In 1995, a study of 60 countries revealed there were 250 bodies at university level offering courses in translation or interpreting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> In 2013, the same database listed 501 translator-training institutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Accordingly, there has been a growth in <a class="external text" href="http://usuaris.tinet.cat/apym/conferences/index.html" rel="nofollow">conferences on translation</a>, <a class="external text" href="http://www.est-translationstudies.org/resources/journals_index.html" rel="nofollow">translation journals</a> and translation-related publications. The visibility acquired by translation has also led to the development of national and international associations of translation studies. Ten of these associations formed the <a class="external text" href="http://www.est-translationstudies.org/news/2016_INTISA.html" rel="nofollow">International Network of Translation and Intrepreting Studies Associations (INTISA)</a> in September 2016.</p>
<p>The growing variety of paradigms is mentioned as one of the possible sources of conflict in the discipline. As early as 1999, the conceptual gap between non-essentialist and empirical approaches came up for debate at the Vic Forum on Training Translators and Interpreters: New Directions for the Millennium. The discussants, Rosemary Arrojo and <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Chesterman" title="Andrew Chesterman">Andrew Chesterman</a>, explicitly sought common shared ground for both approaches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Interdisciplinarity has made the creation of new paradigms possible, as most of the developed theories grew from contact with other disciplines like linguistics, comparative literature, cultural studies, philosophy, sociology or historiography. At the same time, it might have provoked the fragmentation of translation studies as a discipline on its own right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>A second source of conflict rises from the breach between theory and practice. As the prescriptivism of the earlier studies gives room to descriptivism and theorization, professionals see less applicability of the studies. At the same time, university research assessment places little if any importance on translation practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>Translation studies has shown a tendency to broaden its fields of inquiry, and this trend may be expected to continue. This particularly concerns extensions into <a class="external text" href="http://www.adaptation.uk.com/" rel="nofollow">adaptation studies</a>, intralingual translation, translation between semiotic systems (image to text to music, for example), and translation as the form of all interpretation and thus of all understanding, as suggested in <a href="/wiki/On_Linguistic_Aspects_of_Translation" title="On Linguistic Aspects of Translation">the work of Roman Jakobson</a>.</p>
