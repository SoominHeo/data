<p><b>Bungie, Inc.</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Video_game_developer" title="Video game developer">video game developer</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Bellevue,_Washington" title="Bellevue, Washington">Bellevue, Washington</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The company was established in May 1991 as Bungie Software Products Corporation by <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Undergraduate" title="Undergraduate">undergraduate</a> student <a href="/wiki/Alex_Seropian" title="Alex Seropian">Alex Seropian</a>, who later brought in programmer <a href="/wiki/Jason_Jones_(programmer)" title="Jason Jones (programmer)">Jason Jones</a> after publishing Jones' game <i><a href="/wiki/Minotaur:_The_Labyrinths_of_Crete" title="Minotaur: The Labyrinths of Crete">Minotaur: The Labyrinths of Crete</a></i>. Originally based in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>, the company concentrated primarily on Macintosh games during its early years and created two very successful video game franchises called <i><a href="/wiki/Marathon_Trilogy" title="Marathon Trilogy">Marathon</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myth_(computer_game_series)" title="Myth (computer game series)">Myth</a></i>. A West Coast offshoot produced the PC and console title <i><a href="/wiki/Oni_(video_game)" title="Oni (video game)">Oni</a></i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> acquired Bungie in 2000; the project it was working on was repurposed into a launch title for Microsoft's <a href="/wiki/Xbox_(console)" title="Xbox (console)">Xbox</a> console, called <i><a href="/wiki/Halo:_Combat_Evolved" title="Halo: Combat Evolved">Halo: Combat Evolved</a></i>. <i>Halo</i> became the Xbox's "<a href="/wiki/Killer_application" title="Killer application">killer application</a>", selling millions of copies and spawning a <a href="/wiki/Halo_(series)" title="Halo (series)">billion dollar franchise</a>. On October 5, 2007, Bungie announced that it had split from Microsoft and become a privately held independent company, Bungie <a href="/wiki/Limited_liability_company" title="Limited liability company">LLC</a> (Microsoft retained ownership of the <i>Halo</i> franchise <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_property" title="Intellectual property">IP</a>).</p>
<p>Among Bungie's side projects are <a href="#Bungie.net">Bungie.net</a>, the company's official website, which includes company information, forums, and statistics-tracking and integration with many of its games. Bungie.net also serves as the platform from which Bungie sells company-related merchandise out of the Bungie Store and runs other projects, including Bungie Aerospace, its charitable organization, the Bungie Foundation, a <a href="/wiki/Podcast" title="Podcast">podcast</a>, and online publications about game topics. The company is known for its informal and dedicated workplace culture, and recently signed a ten-year publishing deal with <a href="/wiki/Activision" title="Activision">Activision</a>. Their first project together was the 2014 first-person shooter, <i><a href="/wiki/Destiny_(video_game)" title="Destiny (video game)">Destiny</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bungie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding">Founding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bungie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the early 1990s, <a href="/wiki/Alex_Seropian" title="Alex Seropian">Alex Seropian</a> was pursuing a mathematics degree at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>, as the university did not offer undergraduate degrees in computer science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uc-they.27ve_got_game_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-uc-they.27ve_got_game-5">[5]</a></sup> Seropian's first video game was a <i><a href="/wiki/Pong" title="Pong">Pong</a></i> clone called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gnop!" title="Gnop!">Gnop!</a></i> (<i>Pong</i> spelled backwards). Seropian released <i>Gnop!</i> free of charge, though a few players paid Seropian for the source code.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_of_bungie-gnop.2Fds.2Fminotaur_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-history_of_bungie-gnop.2Fds.2Fminotaur-6">[6]</a></sup> Living at home shortly before graduation, his father's wishes for him to get a job convinced Seropian to start his own game company instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uc-they.27ve_got_game_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-uc-they.27ve_got_game-5">[5]</a></sup> Seropian founded Bungie in 1991 to publish <i><a href="/wiki/Operation:_Desert_Storm_(video_game)" title="Operation: Desert Storm (video game)">Operation: Desert Storm</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_of_bungie-gnop.2Fds.2Fminotaur_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-history_of_bungie-gnop.2Fds.2Fminotaur-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamesradar-history_of_halo_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamesradar-history_of_halo-7">[7]</a></sup> Seropian culled funding from friends and family, assembling the game boxes and writing the disks himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-game_icons_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-game_icons-8">[8]</a></sup> <i>Operation: Desert Storm</i> sold 2,500 copies, and Seropian looked for another game to publish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_of_bungie-gnop.2Fds.2Fminotaur_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-history_of_bungie-gnop.2Fds.2Fminotaur-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Seropian met programmer <a href="/wiki/Jason_Jones_(programmer)" title="Jason Jones (programmer)">Jason Jones</a> in an artificial intelligence course at the University of Chicago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_of_bungie-gnop.2Fds.2Fminotaur_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-history_of_bungie-gnop.2Fds.2Fminotaur-6">[6]</a></sup> Jones was a longtime programmer who was porting a game he wrote, called <i>Minotaur</i>, from an <a href="/wiki/Apple_II" title="Apple II">Apple II</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apple_Macintosh" title="Apple Macintosh">Apple Macintosh</a> platform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-img-93_interview_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-img-93_interview-9">[9]</a></sup> Jones recalled, "I didn't really know [Alex] in the class. I think he actually thought I was a dick because I had a fancy computer."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_of_bungie-gnop.2Fds.2Fminotaur_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-history_of_bungie-gnop.2Fds.2Fminotaur-6">[6]</a></sup> Seropian and Jones partnered to release the <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing video game</a> as <i><a href="/wiki/Minotaur:_The_Labyrinths_of_Crete" title="Minotaur: The Labyrinths of Crete">Minotaur: The Labyrinths of Crete</a></i> in 1992; while Jones finished the coding, Seropian handled design and publicity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-img-93_interview_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-img-93_interview-9">[9]</a></sup> The game relied on a then-uncommon internet modems and <a href="/wiki/AppleTalk" title="AppleTalk">AppleTalk</a> connections for play and sold around 2,500 copies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamesradar-history_of_halo_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamesradar-history_of_halo-7">[7]</a></sup> but it developed a devoted following.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_of_bungie-gnop.2Fds.2Fminotaur_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-history_of_bungie-gnop.2Fds.2Fminotaur-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The team focused on the Macintosh platform, not <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Windows</a>-based personal computers, because the Mac market was more open and Jones had been raised on the platform. While Jones was responsible for many of the creative and technical aspects, Seropian was a businessman and marketer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-game_icons_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-game_icons-8">[8]</a></sup> "What I liked about [Seropian] was that he never wasted any money," Jones recalled. With no money to hire other personnel, the two assembled <i>Minotaur</i> boxes by hand in Seropian's apartment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_of_bungie-gnop.2Fds.2Fminotaur_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-history_of_bungie-gnop.2Fds.2Fminotaur-6">[6]</a></sup> While the pair remained low on funds—Seropian's wife was largely supporting him—the modest success of <i>Minotaur</i> gave the duo enough money to develop another project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chicago-marathon_run_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-chicago-marathon_run-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Inspired by the <a href="/wiki/Shooter_game" title="Shooter game">shooter game</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Wolfenstein_3D" title="Wolfenstein 3D">Wolfenstein 3D</a></i>, Jones wrote a 3D game engine for the Mac.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-img-making_of_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-img-making_of-11">[11]</a></sup> Bungie's next game was intended to be a 3D port of <i>Minotaur</i>, but Jones and Seropian found that <i>Minotaur</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s top-down perspective gameplay did not translate well to the 3D perspective, and did not want to rely on modems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-img-93_interview_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-img-93_interview-9">[9]</a></sup> Instead, they developed a new storyline for the <a href="/wiki/First-person_shooter" title="First-person shooter">first-person shooter</a> that became <i><a href="/wiki/Pathways_into_Darkness" title="Pathways into Darkness">Pathways into Darkness</a></i>, released in 1993. Jones did all the coding, with his friend Colin Brent creating the game's art.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_of_bungie-pathways_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-history_of_bungie-pathways-12">[12]</a></sup> The game was a critical and commercial success, winning awards including Inside Mac Games' "Adventure Game of the Year" and <i>Macworld</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s "Best Role-Playing Game."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_of_bungie-pathways_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-history_of_bungie-pathways-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Pathways</i> beat sales expectations and became Bungie's first commercial success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-game_icons_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-game_icons-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bungie-brave_new_world_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-bungie-brave_new_world-13">[13]</a></sup> Bungie moved from a one-bedroom apartment to a studio in Chicago's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Side_(Chicago)" title="South Side (Chicago)">South Side</a> on <a href="/wiki/Halsted_Street" title="Halsted Street">South Halsted Street</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chicago-marathon_run_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-chicago-marathon_run-10">[10]</a></sup> Seropian and Jones's first full-time employee, Doug Zartman, joined in May 1994 to provide support for <i>Pathways</i>, but became Bungie's public relations person, honing Bungie's often sophomoric sense of humor and irreverence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monsters_in_a_box_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-monsters_in_a_box-14">[14]</a></sup> Bungie composer <a href="/wiki/Martin_O%27Donnell" title="Martin O'Donnell">Martin O'Donnell</a> remembered that the studio's location, a former girls' school next to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crack_house" title="Crack house">crack house</a>, "smelled like a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fraternity_and_sorority_houses" title="Fraternity and sorority houses">frat house</a> after a really long weekend" and reminded staff of a locale from the <i><a href="/wiki/Silent_Hill" title="Silent Hill">Silent Hill</a></i> horror video games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bungie_podcast-_odonnell_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-bungie_podcast-_odonnell-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marathon.2C_Myth_and_Oni"><i>Marathon</i>, <i>Myth</i> and <i>Oni</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bungie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marathon, Myth and Oni">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bungie's next project began as a sequel to <i><a href="/wiki/Pathways_into_Darkness" title="Pathways into Darkness">Pathways into Darkness</a></i>, but evolved into a futuristic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_person_shooter" title="First person shooter">first person shooter</a> called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marathon_(computer_game_series)" title="Marathon (computer game series)">Marathon</a></i>. Not only did it introduce the <a href="/wiki/Rocket_jumping" title="Rocket jumping">rocket jumping</a> mechanic to gamers for the first time (then known as "grenade hopping"), it was the first control system where players could use the mouse to look up and down as well as pan side-to-side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <i>Pathways</i> had taught Bungie the importance of story in a game,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_of_bungie-marathon_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-history_of_bungie-marathon-17">[17]</a></sup> and <i>Marathon</i> featured computer terminals where players could choose to learn more about the game's fiction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The studio became what one employee termed "your stereotypical vision of a small computer-game company—eating a lot of pizza, drinking a lot of Coke" while the development team worked 14 hours every day for nearly six months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monsters_in_a_box_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-monsters_in_a_box-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>After showing the game at the Macworld Expo, Bungie was mobbed with interest and orders for the game. The game was not finished until December 14, 1994; Jones and a few other employees spent a day at a warehouse assembling boxes so that some of the orders could be filled before Christmas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monsters_in_a_box_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-monsters_in_a_box-14">[14]</a></sup> The game was a critical and commercial success,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_of_bungie-marathon_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-history_of_bungie-marathon-17">[17]</a></sup> and is regarded as a relatively unknown but important part of gaming history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> It served as the Mac alternative to DOS PC-only games like <i><a href="/wiki/Doom_(1993_video_game)" title="Doom (1993 video game)">Doom</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/System_Shock" title="System Shock">System Shock</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-game_icons_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-game_icons-8">[8]</a></sup> The game's volume of orders was unprecedented for the studio, who found that its old method of mail or phone orders could not scale to the demand and hired another company to handle the tens of thousands of orders. <i>Marathon</i> also brought Bungie attention from press outside the small Mac gaming market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monsters_in_a_box_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-monsters_in_a_box-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first game's success led to a sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/Marathon_2:_Durandal" title="Marathon 2: Durandal">Marathon 2: Durandal</a></i>. The series introduced several elements, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cooperative_mode" title="Cooperative mode">cooperative mode</a>, which made their way to later Bungie games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_of_bungie-marathon_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-history_of_bungie-marathon-17">[17]</a></sup> The game was released November 24, 1995, and outsold its predecessor. When Bungie announced its intention to port the game to the <a href="/wiki/Windows_95" title="Windows 95">Windows 95</a> operating system, however, many Mac players felt betrayed, and Bungie received a flood of negative mail. Seropian saw the value of moving into new markets and partnering with larger supply chains, although he lamented the difficult terms and "sucky" contracts distributors provided.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monsters_in_a_box_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-monsters_in_a_box-14">[14]</a></sup> The game released on <a href="/wiki/Windows_95" title="Windows 95">Windows 95</a> in September 1996.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Marathon_Infinity" title="Marathon Infinity">Marathon Infinity</a></i> was released the following year.</p>
<p>After <i>Marathon</i>, Bungie moved away from first-person shooters to release a strategy game, <i><a href="/wiki/Myth:_The_Fallen_Lords" title="Myth: The Fallen Lords">Myth: The Fallen Lords</a></i>. The games stressed tactical unit management as opposed to the resource gathering model of other combat strategy titles. The <i>Myth</i> games won several awards and spawned a large and active online community. <i>Myth: The Fallen Lords</i> was the first Bungie game to be released simultaneously for both Mac and Windows platforms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-game_icons_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-game_icons-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_of_bungie-myth_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-history_of_bungie-myth-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The success of <i>Myth</i> enabled Bungie to change Chicago offices and establish a <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose, California</a> based branch of the studio, Bungie West, in 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-game_icons_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-game_icons-8">[8]</a></sup> Bungie West's first and only game would be <i><a href="/wiki/Oni_(video_game)" title="Oni (video game)">Oni</a></i>, an action title for the Mac, PC and <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">PlayStation 2</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_of_bungie-oni_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-history_of_bungie-oni-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Halo_and_buyout"><i>Halo</i> and buyout</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bungie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Halo and buyout">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1999, Bungie announced its next product, <i><a href="/wiki/Halo:_Combat_Evolved" title="Halo: Combat Evolved">Halo</a></i>, originally intended to be a <a href="/wiki/Third-person_shooter" title="Third-person shooter">third-person shooter</a> game for <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Windows</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macintosh" title="Macintosh">Macintosh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ignpreview_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-ignpreview-22">[22]</a></sup> <i>Halo</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s public unveiling occurred at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macworld_Conference_%26_Expo" title="Macworld Conference &amp; Expo">Macworld Expo</a> 1999 keynote address by Apple's then-interim-CEO <a href="/wiki/Steve_Jobs" title="Steve Jobs">Steve Jobs</a> (after a closed-door screening at E3 in 1999).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ignpreview_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-ignpreview-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 19, 2000, soon after <i>Halo</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s preview at Electronic Entertainment Expo 2000, <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> announced that it had acquired Bungie Software and that Bungie would become a part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microsoft_Game_Division" title="Microsoft Game Division">Microsoft Game Division</a> under the name Bungie Studios. <i>Halo</i> would be developed as an exclusive, <a href="/wiki/First-person_shooter" title="First-person shooter">first-person shooter</a> title for the Xbox. The reasons for Bungie accepting Microsoft's offer were varied. Jones stated that "I don't remember the details exactly, it was all a blur. We'd been talking to people for years and years—before we even published <i>Marathon</i>, <a href="/wiki/Activision" title="Activision">Activision</a> made a serious offer. But the chance to work on Xbox—the chance to work with a company that took the games seriously. Before that we worried that we'd get bought by someone who just wanted Mac ports or didn't have a clue."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_of_bungie-halo_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-history_of_bungie-halo-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Martin_O%27Donnell" title="Martin O'Donnell">Martin O'Donnell</a>, who had joined Bungie as an employee only ten days before the merger was announced, remembers that the stability of the Xbox as a development platform was not the only benefit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bungie_podcast-_odonnell_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-bungie_podcast-_odonnell-15">[15]</a></sup> Shortly before <i>Myth II</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s release, it was discovered versions of the game could erase a player's hard drive; the glitch led to a massive recall of the games right before they shipped,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-game_icons_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-game_icons-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_of_bungie-marathon_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-history_of_bungie-marathon-17">[17]</a></sup> which cost Bungie nearly one million dollars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-game_icons_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-game_icons-8">[8]</a></sup> O'Donnell stated in a Bungie podcast that this recall created some financial uncertainty, although accepting the offer was not something Bungie "had to do."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bungie_podcast-_odonnell_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-bungie_podcast-_odonnell-15">[15]</a></sup> Seropian and Jones had refused to accept Microsoft's offer until the entire studio agreed to the buyout.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-game_icons_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-game_icons-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a result of the buyout, the rights to <i>Myth</i> and <i>Oni</i> were transferred to <a href="/wiki/Take-Two_Interactive" title="Take-Two Interactive">Take-Two Interactive</a> as part of the three-way deal between Microsoft, Bungie and Take-Two; most of the original <i>Oni</i> developers were able to continue working on <i>Oni</i> until its release in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <i>Halo: Combat Evolved</i>, meanwhile, went on to become a critically acclaimed hit, selling more than 6.5 million copies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sales_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-sales-25">[25]</a></sup> and becoming the Xbox's flagship franchise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnet_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnet-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Halo</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s success led to Bungie creating two sequels. <i><a href="/wiki/Halo_2" title="Halo 2">Halo 2</a></i> was released on November 9, 2004, making more than $125 million on release day and setting a record in the entertainment industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Halo_3" title="Halo 3">Halo 3</a></i>, the final installment in the original <i>Halo</i> trilogy, was released on September 25, 2007 and surpassed <i>Halo 2</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s records, making $170 million in its first twenty-four hours of release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Independent_company">Independent company</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bungie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Independent company">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On October 1, 2007, <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> and Bungie announced that Bungie was splitting off from its parent and becoming a privately held <a href="/wiki/Limited_liability_company" title="Limited liability company">limited liability company</a> named Bungie, LLC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bungie_split_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-bungie_split-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bungie_split_.232_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bungie_split_.232-30">[30]</a></sup> As outlined in a deal between the two, Microsoft would retain a minority stake and continue to partner with Bungie on publishing and marketing both <i><a href="/wiki/Halo_(series)" title="Halo (series)">Halo</a></i> and future projects, with the <i>Halo</i> intellectual property belonging to Microsoft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-split_faq_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-split_faq-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Bungie planned on revealing a new game at <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Entertainment_Expo" title="Electronic Entertainment Expo">E3</a> 2008, Bungie studio head Harold Ryan announced that the unveiling was canceled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Almost three months later, Bungie announced that the new game was a prequel and expansion to <i>Halo 3</i> titled <i>Halo 3: Recon</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The next month, Bungie changed game's title from <i>Halo 3: Recon</i> to <i><a href="/wiki/Halo_3:_ODST" title="Halo 3: ODST">Halo 3: ODST</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> At E3 2009, Bungie and Microsoft revealed the company was developing another <i>Halo</i>-related game, <i><a href="/wiki/Halo:_Reach" title="Halo: Reach">Halo: Reach</a></i>, for release in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> <i>Reach</i> was the last game in the <i>Halo</i> franchise to be developed by Bungie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra-07.2F09_reports_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra-07.2F09_reports-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bungie continued expanding, though it did not commit to details about new projects and ship dates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The company grew from roughly 120 employees in May 2008<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> to 165 in June 2009, outgrowing the studio Microsoft developed. Ryan helped redesign a former multiplex movie theater in <a href="/wiki/Bellevue,_Washington" title="Bellevue, Washington">Bellevue</a> into new Bungie offices, with 80,000 square feet (7,400 m<sup>2</sup>) replacing the 41,000 square feet (3,800 m<sup>2</sup>) the company occupied previously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seattletimes-bungie_spring_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-seattletimes-bungie_spring-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 29, 2010, Bungie announced that it was entering into a 10-year publishing agreement with publisher <a href="/wiki/Activision_Blizzard" title="Activision Blizzard">Activision Blizzard</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-activision_announcement_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-activision_announcement-41">[41]</a></sup> Under Bungie's agreement with Activision, new <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_property" title="Intellectual property">intellectual property</a> developed by Bungie will be owned by Bungie, not Activision, in a deal similar to the EA (Electronic Arts) Partners Program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-activision_announcement_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-activision_announcement-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 30, 2011, Bungie announced the "Bungie Aerospace" project (Slogan "Per audacia ad astra" meaning "Boldly to the stars".) The project is intended to provide independent game developers with publishing, resources, and support, including access to the Bungie.net platform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> On November 18, 2011, Bungie Aerospace published its first game, <i>Crimson: Steam Pirates</i>, for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IOS_(Apple)" title="IOS (Apple)">iOS</a>, developed by startup video game developer <a href="/wiki/Harebrained_Schemes" title="Harebrained Schemes">Harebrained Schemes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> In addition to publishing and distributing <i>Crimson</i>, Bungie Aerospace also provided players with statistical support and a dedicated discussion forum on Bungie.net.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 17, 2013, Bungie announced <i><a href="/wiki/Destiny_(video_game)" title="Destiny (video game)">Destiny</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Destiny_ViDoc_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Destiny_ViDoc-46">[46]</a></sup> which launched for the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a>, <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_4" title="PlayStation 4">PlayStation 4</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xbox_360" title="Xbox 360">Xbox 360</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Xbox_One" title="Xbox One">Xbox One</a> platforms on September 9, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-release_update_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-release_update-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bungie_PS4_Announce_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bungie_PS4_Announce-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Xbox_One_Destiny_Announce_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Xbox_One_Destiny_Announce-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 27, 2016, Ryan stepped down as president and Pete Parsons, who had been the company's chief operating officer and executive producer since 2002, became its chief executive officer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bungie.net">Bungie.net</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bungie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Bungie.net">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bungie.net serves as the main official portal for interaction between company staff and the community surrounding Bungie's games. When Bungie was bought by Microsoft, the site was originally seen as in competition with Microsoft's own Xbox.com site, but community management eventually won out as the bigger concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inside_bungie_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-inside_bungie-51">[51]</a></sup> The site has been redesigned several times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>During Bungie's involvement with the <i>Halo</i> franchise, the site recorded statistics for each game played.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bungie_podcast-_h3_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-bungie_podcast-_h3-53">[53]</a></sup> This information included statistics on each player in the game,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bungie_podcast-_h3_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-bungie_podcast-_h3-53">[53]</a></sup> and a map of the game level showing where kills occurred, called "heatmaps."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> On January 31, 2012, Bungie announced that, as of March 31, 2012, Bungie.net would no longer update <i>Halo</i> game statistics and <i>Halo</i> player service records, host new user-generated <i>Halo</i> content, or operate <i>Halo's</i> "Bungie Pro" service. Bungie's cessation of these services on March 31 completed the transition process of all data for <i>Halo</i> games being managed by <a href="/wiki/343_Industries" title="343 Industries">343 Industries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Currently, Bungie.net records player's statistics for their game franchise Destiny. In addition to the collection of data and the management of Destiny player's accounts, the website serves as a form of communication between Bungie and the community.</p>
<p>While Bungie had long provided places for fans to congregate and talk about games, as well as releasing new information and screenshots over Bungie.net, it historically had made less effort and been less successful at providing access to the inside workings of Bungie and its staff. As part of a move to become more familiar with fans, Bungie recruited recognized and respected voices from the fan community, including writers <a href="/wiki/Luke_Smith_(writer)" title="Luke Smith (writer)">Luke Smith</a>, Eric Osborne, and others. The developer hosts a podcast where staff members are interviewed in a round-table, informal atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra-shaping_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra-shaping-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bungie also has an iOS and Google Play application that allows stat-tracking for their game <i>Destiny</i> on the go.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bungie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Martin O'Donnell described Bungie's workplace culture as "a slightly irreverent attitude, and not corporate, bureaucratic or business-focused";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-single_player_interview_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-single_player_interview-58">[58]</a></sup> artist Shi Kai Wang noted that when he walked into Bungie for an interview, "I realized that I was the one who was over-dressed, [and] I knew this was the place I wanted to work."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-makers_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-makers-59">[59]</a></sup> Frank O'Connor comically noted that at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamestop" title="Gamestop">Gamestop</a> conference, the Bungie team was told to wear <a href="/wiki/Business_casual" title="Business casual">business casual</a>, to which O'Connor replied "We [Bungie] don't do business casual."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bungie_podcast-_h3_53-2"><a href="#cite_note-bungie_podcast-_h3-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>This informal, creative culture was one of the reasons Microsoft was interested in acquiring Bungie,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buyfaq_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-buyfaq-60">[60]</a></sup> although game designer <a href="/wiki/Jordan_Weisman" title="Jordan Weisman">Jordan Weisman</a> said that Microsoft came close to destroying the company's development culture, as it had with the now-defunct <a href="/wiki/FASA_Studio" title="FASA Studio">FASA Studio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Studio head Harold Ryan emphasized that even when Bungie was bought by Microsoft, the team was still independent:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>One of the first things [Microsoft] tried after acquiring Bungie, after first attempting to fully assimilate them, was to move Bungie into a standard Microsoft building with the rest of the game group. But unlike the rest of the teams they'd brought in previously, Bungie didn't move into Microsoft corporate offices – we tore all of the walls out of that section of the building and sat in a big open environment. Luckily Alex and Jason [Seropian and Jones, Bungie's founders] were pretty steadfast at the time about staying somewhat separate and isolated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-single_player_interview_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-single_player_interview-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Microsoft eventually moved the studio to <a href="/wiki/Kirkland,_Washington" title="Kirkland, Washington">Kirkland, Washington</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-single_player_interview_58-2"><a href="#cite_note-single_player_interview-58">[58]</a></sup> Despite the move, financial analyst Roger Ehrenberg declared the Bungie-Microsoft marriage "doomed to fail" due to these fundamental differences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-split_inevitable_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-split_inevitable-62">[62]</a></sup> Bungie also pointed out that it was tired of new <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_property" title="Intellectual property">intellectual property</a> being cast aside to work on the <i>Halo</i> franchise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-single_player_interview_58-3"><a href="#cite_note-single_player_interview-58">[58]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Edge_(magazine)" title="Edge (magazine)">Edge</a></i> described the typical Bungie employee as "simultaneously irreverent and passionately loyal; fiercely self-critical; full of excitement at the company's achievements, no matter how obscure; [and] recruited from its devoted fanbase."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inside_bungie_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-inside_bungie-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Bungie workplace is highly informal, with new and old staff willing to challenge each other on topics, such as fundamental game elements. Staff are able to publicly criticize their own games and each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inside_bungie_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-inside_bungie-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bungie_podcast-_allen_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-bungie_podcast-_allen-63">[63]</a></sup> Fostering studio cooperation and competition, Bungie holds events such as the "Bungie Pentathlon", in which staff square off in teams playing games such as <i>Halo</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pictionary" title="Pictionary">Pictionary</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Dance_Dance_Revolution" title="Dance Dance Revolution">Dance Dance Revolution</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Rock_Band_(video_game)" title="Rock Band (video game)">Rock Band</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bungie_podcast-_allen_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-bungie_podcast-_allen-63">[63]</a></sup> Bungie also faced off against professional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESport" title="ESport">eSports</a> teams and other game studios in <i>Halo</i> during "<a href="/wiki/Wednesday" title="Wednesday">Humpdays</a>", with the results of the multiplayer matches being posted on Bungie.net.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bungie's staff and fans, known as the "Seventh Column," have banded together for charity and other causes. After <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>, Bungie was one of several game companies to announce its intention to help those affected by the hurricane, with Bungie donating the proceeds of special T-shirts to the <a href="/wiki/American_Red_Cross" title="American Red Cross">American Red Cross</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> after the <a href="/wiki/2010_Haiti_earthquake" title="2010 Haiti earthquake">2010 Haiti earthquake</a>, Bungie sold "Be a Hero" T-shirts and donated money to the Red Cross for every <i>Halo 3</i> or <i>ODST</i> player on Xbox Live who wore a special heart-shaped emblem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Other charity work Bungie has done included auctioning off a painting of "Mister Chief" by Frank O'Connor,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> a <i>Halo 2</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soda_machine" title="Soda machine">soda machine</a> from Bungie's offices,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> and collaborating with <a href="/wiki/Child%27s_Play_(charity)" title="Child's Play (charity)">Child's Play</a> auctions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> In 2011, Bungie formed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonprofit" title="Nonprofit">nonprofit</a> organization, named Bungie Foundation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_companies">Related companies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bungie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Related companies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many of Bungie's employees have left the company to form their own studios. <a href="/wiki/Double_Aught" title="Double Aught">Double Aught</a> was a short-lived company composed of several former Bungie team members, founded by Greg Kirkpatrick. Seropian left to form <a href="/wiki/Wideload_Games" title="Wideload Games">Wideload Games</a>, creator of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stubbs_the_Zombie_in_%22Rebel_Without_a_Pulse%22" title='Stubbs the Zombie in "Rebel Without a Pulse"'>Stubbs the Zombie in "Rebel Without a Pulse"</a></i>. Other companies include <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Giant_Bite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Giant Bite (page does not exist)">Giant Bite</a>, founded by Hamilton Chu (former lead producer of Bungie Studios) and Michal Evans (former Bungie programmer),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Certain_Affinity" title="Certain Affinity">Certain Affinity</a>, founded by Max Hoberman (the multiplayer design lead for <i>Halo 2</i> and <i>Halo 3</i>); Certain Affinity's team included former Bungie employees David Bowman and Chad Armstrong (who later returned to Bungie). The company collaborated with Bungie in releasing the last two downloadable maps for <i>Halo 2</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> and the downloadable Defiant Map Pack for <i>Halo: Reach</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/343_Industries" title="343 Industries">343 Industries</a>, a game studio formed by <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> to manage the <i>Halo</i> series following the launch of <i>Halo: Reach,</i> also includes a few former Bungie employees, including former Community Manager Frank O'Connor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> In 2015, long time ex-Bungie employee Marty O'Donnell started a new game studio known as Highwire Games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
