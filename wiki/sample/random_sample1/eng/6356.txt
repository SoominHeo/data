<p><i><b>Pinguicula</b></i>, commonly known as the <b>butterworts</b>, is a genus of <a href="/wiki/Carnivorous_plant" title="Carnivorous plant">carnivorous plants</a> that use sticky, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glandular" title="Glandular">glandular</a> leaves to lure, trap, and digest <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> in order to supplement the poor mineral nutrition they obtain from the environment. Of the roughly 80 currently known <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, 12 are native to <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, 9 to <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, and some in northern <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. The largest number of species is in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name <i>Pinguicula</i> is derived from a term coined by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conrad_Gesner" title="Conrad Gesner">Conrad Gesner</a>, who in his 1561 work entitled <i>Horti Germaniae</i> commented on the glistening leaves: <i>"propter pinguia et tenera folia…"</i> (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>pinguis</i>, "fat"). The common name "butterwort" reflects this characteristic.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The majority of <i>Pinguicula</i> are <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennial plants</a>. The only known <a href="/wiki/Annual_plant" title="Annual plant">annuals</a> are <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_sharpii&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula sharpii (page does not exist)"><i>P. sharpii</i></a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_takakii&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula takakii (page does not exist)"><i>P. takakii</i></a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_crenatiloba&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula crenatiloba (page does not exist)"><i>P. crenatiloba</i></a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_pumila&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula pumila (page does not exist)"><i>P. pumila</i></a>. All species form stemless <a href="/wiki/Rosette_(botany)" title="Rosette (botany)">rosettes</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Habit">Habit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Butterworts can be divided roughly into two main groups based on the <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> in which they grow; each group is the further subdivided based on <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphological</a> characteristics. Although these groups are not <a href="/wiki/Cladistics" title="Cladistics">cladistically</a> supported by genetic studies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cieslak2005_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-cieslak2005-1">[1]</a></sup> these groupings are nonetheless convenient for horticultural purposes. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">Tropical</a> butterworts either form somewhat compact winter rosettes composed of fleshy leaves or retain carnivorous leaves year-round. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">Temperate</a> species often form tight buds (called <a href="/wiki/Hibernaculum_(botany)" title="Hibernaculum (botany)">hibernacula</a>) composed of scale-like leaves during a winter <a href="/wiki/Dormancy" title="Dormancy">dormancy</a> period. During this time the roots (with the exception of <a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_alpina" title="Pinguicula alpina"><i>P. alpina</i></a>) and carnivorous leaves wither.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legendre2000_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-legendre2000-2">[2]</a></sup> Temperate species flower when they form their summer rosettes while tropical species flower at each rosette change.</p>
<p>Many butterworts cycle between rosettes composed of carnivorous and non-carnivorous leaves as the seasons change, so these two ecological groupings can be further divided according to their ability to produce different leaves during their growing season. If the growth in the summer is different in size or shape to that in the early spring (for temperate species) or in the winter (tropical species), then plants are considered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterophyllous" title="Heterophyllous">heterophyllous</a>; whereas uniform growth identifies a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Homophyllous&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Homophyllous (page does not exist)">homophyllous</a> species.</p>
<p>This results in four groupings:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Tropical butterworts</b>: species which do not undergo a winter dormancy but continue to alternately bloom and form rosettes.
<ul>
<li><i><b>Heterophyllous tropical species</b></i>: species that alternate between rosettes of carnivorous leaves during the warm season and compact rosettes of fleshy non-carnivorous leaves during the cool season. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_moranensis" title="Pinguicula moranensis"><i>P. moranensis</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_gypsicola" title="Pinguicula gypsicola"><i>P. gypsicola</i></a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_laxifolia" title="Pinguicula laxifolia"><i>P. laxifolia</i></a>.</li>
<li><i><b>Homophyllous tropical species</b></i>: these species produce rosettes of carnivorous leaves of roughly uniform size throughout the year, such as <a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_gigantea" title="Pinguicula gigantea"><i>P. gigantea</i></a>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>Temperate butterworts</b>: these plants are native to climate zones with cold winters. They produce a winter-resting bud (<a href="/wiki/Hibernaculum_(botany)" title="Hibernaculum (botany)">hibernaculum</a>) during the winter.
<ul>
<li><i><b>Heterophyllous temperate species</b></i>: species where the vegetative and generative rosettes differ in shape and/or size, as seen in <a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_lutea" title="Pinguicula lutea"><i>P. lutea</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_lusitanica" title="Pinguicula lusitanica"><i>P. lusitanica</i></a>.</li>
<li><i><b>Homophyllous temperate species</b></i>: the vegetative and generative rosettes appear identical, as exhibited by <a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_alpina" title="Pinguicula alpina"><i>P. alpina</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_grandiflora" title="Pinguicula grandiflora"><i>P. grandiflora</i></a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_vulgaris" title="Pinguicula vulgaris"><i>P. vulgaris</i></a>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roots">Roots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Roots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">root system</a> of <i>Pinguicula</i> species is relatively undeveloped. The thin, white roots serve mainly as an <a href="/wiki/Anchor" title="Anchor">anchor</a> for the plant and to absorb moisture (nutrients are absorbed through carnivory). In temperate species these roots wither (except in <a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_alpina" title="Pinguicula alpina"><i>P. alpina</i></a>) when the <a href="/wiki/Hibernaculum_(botany)" title="Hibernaculum (botany)">hibernaculum</a> is formed. In the few <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epiphytic" title="Epiphytic">epiphytic</a> species (such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_lignicola&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula lignicola (page does not exist)"><i>P. lignicola</i></a>), the roots form anchoring suction cups.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leaves_and_carnivory">Leaves and carnivory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Leaves and carnivory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pinguicula_with_prey.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Pinguicula_with_prey.JPG/210px-Pinguicula_with_prey.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Pinguicula_with_prey.JPG/315px-Pinguicula_with_prey.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Pinguicula_with_prey.JPG/420px-Pinguicula_with_prey.JPG 2x" width="210"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pinguicula_with_prey.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A fly trapped on a butterwort leaf. Glandular hairs are visible</div>
</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaf</a> <a href="/wiki/Blade" title="Blade">blade</a> of a butterwort is smooth, rigid, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Succulent" title="Succulent">succulent</a>, usually bright green or pinkish in colour. Depending on species, the leaves are between 2 and 30 cm.(1-12") long. The leaf shape depends on the species, but is usually roughly <a href="/wiki/Leaf#Terminology" title="Leaf">obovate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leaf#Terminology" title="Leaf">spatulate</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Leaf#Terminology" title="Leaf">linear</a>.</p>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pinguicula_vector_en.svg" title="Vector graphic of the trapping and digestive features of a Pinguicula leaf"><img alt="Vector graphic of the trapping and digestive features of a Pinguicula leaf" data-file-height="650" data-file-width="720" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Pinguicula_vector_en.svg/250px-Pinguicula_vector_en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Pinguicula_vector_en.svg/375px-Pinguicula_vector_en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Pinguicula_vector_en.svg/500px-Pinguicula_vector_en.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a></div>
<p>Like all members of the family <a href="/wiki/Lentibulariaceae" title="Lentibulariaceae">Lentibulariaceae</a>, butterworts are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carnivorous" title="Carnivorous">carnivorous</a>. In order to catch and digest insects, the leaf of a butterwort uses two specialized glands which are scattered across the leaf surface (usually only on the upper surface, with the exception of <a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_gigantea" title="Pinguicula gigantea"><i>P. gigantea</i></a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_longifolia" title="Pinguicula longifolia">P. longifolia ssp. longifolia</a></i>). One is termed a <i>peduncular gland</i>, and consists of a few <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secrete" title="Secrete">secretory</a> cells on top of a single stalk cell. These cells produce a <a href="/wiki/Mucilage" title="Mucilage">mucilagenous</a> secretion which forms visible <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Droplet" title="Droplet">droplets</a> across the leaf surface. This wet appearance probably helps lure prey in search of water (a similar phenomenon is observed in the <a href="/wiki/Drosera" title="Drosera">sundews</a>). The droplets secrete limited amounts of digestive enzymes, and serve mainly to entrap insects. On contact with an insect, the peduncular glands release additional mucilage from special reservoir cells located at the base of their stalks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legendre2000_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-legendre2000-2">[2]</a></sup> The insect will begin to struggle, triggering more glands and encasing itself in mucilage. Some species can bend their leaf edges slightly by <a href="/wiki/Thigmotropism" title="Thigmotropism">thigmotropism</a>, bringing additional glands into contact with the trapped insect. The second type of gland found on butterwort leaves are <i>sessile glands</i> which lie flat on the leaf surface. Once the prey is entrapped by the peduncular glands and digestion begins, the initial flow of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> triggers enzyme release by the sessile glands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legendre2000_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-legendre2000-2">[2]</a></sup> These enzymes, which include <a href="/wiki/Amylase" title="Amylase">amylase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Esterase" title="Esterase">esterase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphatase" title="Phosphatase">phosphatase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protease" title="Protease">protease</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ribonuclease" title="Ribonuclease">ribonuclease</a> break down the digestible components of the insect body. These fluids are then absorbed back into the leaf surface through <a href="/wiki/Plant_cuticle" title="Plant cuticle">cuticular</a> holes, leaving only the <a href="/wiki/Chitin" title="Chitin">chitin</a> <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeleton</a> of the larger insects on the leaf surface.</p>
<p>The holes in the cuticle which allow for this digestive mechanism also pose a challenge for the plant, since they serve as breaks in the <a href="/wiki/Plant_cuticle" title="Plant cuticle">cuticle</a> (waxy layer) that protects the plant from <a href="/wiki/Desiccation" title="Desiccation">desiccation</a>. As a result, most butterworts live in humid environments.</p>

<p>Butterworts are usually only able to trap small insects and those with large wing surfaces. They can also digest <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a> which lands on their leaf surface. The secretory system can only function a single time, so that a particular area of the leaf surface can only be used to digest insects once.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legendre2000_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-legendre2000-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flowers">Flowers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Flowers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pinguicula_hybrid_ne.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1483" data-file-width="1279" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Pinguicula_hybrid_ne.JPG/180px-Pinguicula_hybrid_ne.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Pinguicula_hybrid_ne.JPG/270px-Pinguicula_hybrid_ne.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Pinguicula_hybrid_ne.JPG/360px-Pinguicula_hybrid_ne.JPG 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pinguicula_hybrid_ne.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The flower of a hybrid butterwort</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As with almost all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carnivorous_plants" title="Carnivorous plants">carnivorous plants</a>, the flowers of butterworts are held far above the rest of the plant by a long stalk, in order to reduce the probability of trapping potential pollinators. The single, long-lasting flowers are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zygomorphic" title="Zygomorphic">zygomorphic</a>, with two lower lip petals characteristic of the <a href="/wiki/Lentibulariaceae" title="Lentibulariaceae">bladderwort family</a>, and a spur extending from the back of the flower. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sepals" title="Sepals">calyx</a> has five sepals, and the <a href="/wiki/Petal" title="Petal">petals</a> are arranged in a two-part lower lip and a three-part upper lip. Most butterwort flowers are blue, violet or white, often suffused with a yellow, greenish or reddish tint. <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_laueana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula laueana (page does not exist)">P. laueana</a></i> and the newly described <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_caryophyllacea&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula caryophyllacea (page does not exist)"><i>P. caryophyllacea</i></a> are unique in having a strikingly red flowers. Butterworts are often cultivated and hybridized primarily for their flowers.</p>
<p>The shape and colors of butterwort flowers are distinguishing characteristics which are used to divide the genus into subgenera and to distinguish individual species from one another.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fruit_and_seed">Fruit and seed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fruit and seed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The round to egg-shaped <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capsule_(fruit)" title="Capsule (fruit)">seed capsules</a> open when dry into two halves, exposing numerous small (0.5–1 mm), brown <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seeds" title="Seeds">seeds</a>. If moisture is present the <a href="/wiki/Silique" title="Silique">silique</a> closes, protecting the seed and opening again upon dryness to allow for <a href="/wiki/Seed_dispersal#Wind" title="Seed dispersal">wind dispersal</a>. Many species have a net-like pattern on their seed surface to allow them to land on water surfaces without sinking, since many non-epiphytic butterworts grow near water sources. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haploid" title="Haploid">haploid</a> <a href="/wiki/Chromosome" title="Chromosome">chromosome</a> number of butterworts is either n = 8 or n = 11 (or a multiple thereof), depending on species. The exception is <i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_lusitanica" title="Pinguicula lusitanica">P. lusitanica</a></i>, whose chromosome count is n = 6.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vegetative_propagation">Vegetative propagation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Vegetative propagation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As well as <a href="/wiki/Sexual_reproduction" title="Sexual reproduction">sexual reproduction</a> by <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seed</a>, many butterworts can reproduce <a href="/wiki/Asexual_reproduction" title="Asexual reproduction">asexually</a> by <a href="/wiki/Vegetative_reproduction" title="Vegetative reproduction">vegetative reproduction</a>. Many members of the genus form offshoots during or shortly after flowering (<i>e.g.</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_vulgaris" title="Pinguicula vulgaris">P. vulgaris</a></i>), which grow into new <a href="/wiki/Cloning" title="Cloning">genetically identical</a> adults. A few other species form new <a href="/wiki/Offshoot_(plant)" title="Offshoot (plant)">offshoots</a> using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stolons" title="Stolons">stolons</a> (<i>e.g.</i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_calyptrata&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula calyptrata (page does not exist)">P. calyptrata</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_vallisneriifolia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula vallisneriifolia (page does not exist)">P. vallisneriifolia</a></i>) while others form plantlets at the leaf margins (<i>e.g.</i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_heterophylla&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula heterophylla (page does not exist)">P. heterophylla</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_primuliflora" title="Pinguicula primuliflora">P. primuliflora</a></i>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pinguicula_distribution.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1538" data-file-width="2753" height="134" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Pinguicula_distribution.svg/240px-Pinguicula_distribution.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Pinguicula_distribution.svg/360px-Pinguicula_distribution.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Pinguicula_distribution.svg/480px-Pinguicula_distribution.svg.png 2x" width="240"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pinguicula_distribution.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Pinguicula</i> distribution</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Butterworts are distributed throughout the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere">northern hemisphere</a> (<a class="external text" href="http://www.pinguicula.org/pages/culture/Overview_fichiers/image002.jpg" rel="nofollow">map</a>). The greatest concentration of species, however, is in humid mountainous regions of <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>) and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, where populations can be found as far south as <a href="/wiki/Tierra_del_Fuego" title="Tierra del Fuego">Tierra del Fuego</a>. <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> is the only <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continent</a> without any native butterworts.</p>
<p>Butterworts probably originated in Central America, as this is the center of <i>Pinguicula</i> diversity – roughly 50% of butterwort species are found here.</p>
<p>The great majority of individual <i>Pinguicula</i> species have a very limited <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Population_distribution" title="Population distribution">distribution</a>. The two butterwort species with the widest distribution - <i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_alpina" title="Pinguicula alpina">P. alpina</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_vulgaris" title="Pinguicula vulgaris">P. vulgaris</a></i> - are found throughout much of Europe and North America. Other species found in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> include <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_caerulea&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula caerulea (page does not exist)">P. caerulea</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_ionantha" title="Pinguicula ionantha">P.ionantha</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_lutea" title="Pinguicula lutea">P. lutea</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_macroceras" title="Pinguicula macroceras">P. macroceras</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_planifolia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula planifolia (page does not exist)">P. planifolia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_primuliflora" title="Pinguicula primuliflora">P. primuliflora</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_pumila&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula pumila (page does not exist)">P. pumila</a></i>, and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_villosa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula villosa (page does not exist)">P. villosa</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In general, butterworts grow in nutrient-poor, alkaline soils. Some species have adapted to other soil types, such as acidic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peat_bog" title="Peat bog">peat bogs</a> (ex. <i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_vulgaris" title="Pinguicula vulgaris">P. vulgaris</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_calyptrata&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula calyptrata (page does not exist)">P. calyptrata</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_lusitanica" title="Pinguicula lusitanica">P. lusitanica</a></i>), soils composed of pure <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_gypsicola" title="Pinguicula gypsicola">P. gypsicola</a></i> and other Mexican species), or even vertical rock walls (<i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_ramosa" title="Pinguicula ramosa">P. ramosa</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_vallisneriifolia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula vallisneriifolia (page does not exist)">P. vallisneriifolia</a></i>, and most of the Mexican species). A few species are epiphytes (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_casabitoana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula casabitoana (page does not exist)">P. casabitoana</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_hemiepiphytica&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula hemiepiphytica (page does not exist)">P. hemiepiphytica</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_lignicola&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula lignicola (page does not exist)">P. lignicola</a></i>). Many of the Mexican species commonly grow on mossy banks, rock, and roadsides in oak-pine forests. <i>Pinguicula macroceras ssp. nortensis</i> has even been observed growing on hanging dead grasses. <i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_lutea" title="Pinguicula lutea">P. lutea</a></i> grows in pine <a href="/wiki/Flatwoods" title="Flatwoods">flatwoods</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Other species, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_vulgaris" title="Pinguicula vulgaris">P. vulgaris</a></i>, grow in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fens" title="Fens">fens</a>. Each of these environments is nutrient-poor, allowing butterworts to escape competition from other canopy-forming species, particularly grasses and sedges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Butterworts need habitats that are almost constantly moist or wet, at least during their carnivorous growth stage. Many Mexican species lose their carnivorous leaves, and sprout succulent leaves, or die back to onion-like "bulbs" to survive the winter drought, at which point they can survive in bone-dry conditions. The moisture they need for growing can be supplied by either a high <a href="/wiki/Water_table" title="Water table">groundwater table</a>, or by high humidity or high precipitation. Unlike many other carnivorous plants that require sunny locations, many butterworts thrive in part-sun or even shady conditions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The environmental threats faced by various <i>Pinguicula</i> species depend on their location and on how widespread their distribution is. Most endangered are the species which are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemic_(ecology)" title="Endemic (ecology)">endemic</a> to small areas, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_ramosa" title="Pinguicula ramosa">P. ramosa</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_casabitoana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula casabitoana (page does not exist)">P. casabitoana</a></i>, and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_fiorii&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula fiorii (page does not exist)">P. fiorii</a></i>. These populations are threatened primarily by <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a>. <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">Wetland</a> destruction has threatened several US species. Most of these are federally listed as either threatened or endangered, and <i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_ionantha" title="Pinguicula ionantha">P. ionantha</a></i> is listed on <a href="/wiki/CITES" title="CITES">CITES</a> appendix I, giving it additional protection.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Botanical_history">Botanical history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Botanical history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The first mention of butterworts in botanical literature is an entry entitled <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Zitroch chrawt oder smalz chrawt</i></span> ("lard herb") by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vitus_Auslasser&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vitus Auslasser (page does not exist)">Vitus Auslasser</a> in his 1479 work on medicinal herbs entitled <i>Macer de Herbarium</i>. The name <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Zittrochkraut</i></span> is still used for butterworts in <a href="/wiki/Tyrol_(state)" title="Tyrol (state)">Tirol</a>, Austria.</p>
<p>In 1583, <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Clusius" title="Carolus Clusius">Clusius</a> already distinguished between two forms in his <i>Historia stirpium rariorum per Pannoniam, Austriam</i>: a blue-flowered form (<i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_vulgaris" title="Pinguicula vulgaris">P. vulgaris</a></i>) and a white-flowered form (<i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_alpina" title="Pinguicula alpina">Pinguicula alpina</a></i>). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a> added <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_villosa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula villosa (page does not exist)">P. villosa</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_lusitanica" title="Pinguicula lusitanica">P. lusitanica</a></i> when he published his <i><a href="/wiki/Species_Plantarum" title="Species Plantarum">Species Plantarum</a></i> in 1753. The number of known species rose sharply with the exploration of the new continents in the 19th century; by 1844, 32 species were known.</p>
<p>It was only in the late 19th century that the carnivory of this genus began to be studied in detail. In a letter to <a href="/wiki/Asa_Gray" title="Asa Gray">Asa Gray</a> dated June 3, 1874, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> mentioned his early observations of the butterwort's digestive process and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insectivorous" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a> nature. (See Gray Herbarium of Harvard University (103).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A more detailed citation is required. (October 2009)">full citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Darwin studied these plants extensively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Siegfried_Jost_Casper" title="Siegfried Jost Casper">S. J. Casper's</a> large 1966 monograph of the genus<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-casper1966_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-casper1966-6">[6]</a></sup> included 46 species, a number which has almost doubled since then. Many exciting discoveries have been made in recent years, especially in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. Another important development in the history of butterworts is the formation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Pinguicula_Study_Group" title="International Pinguicula Study Group">International Pinguicula Study Group</a>, an organization dedicated to furthering the knowledge of this genus and promoting its popularity in cultivation, in the 1990s.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Butterworts are widely cultivated by carnivorous plant enthusiasts. The temperate species and many of the Mexican butterworts are relatively easy to grow and have therefore gained relative popularity. Two of the most widely grown plants are the hybrid <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a> <i>Pinguicula</i> × 'Sethos' and <i>Pinguicula</i> × 'Weser'. Both are crosses of <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula_ehlersiae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinguicula ehlersiae (page does not exist)">Pinguicula ehlersiae</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pinguicula_moranensis" title="Pinguicula moranensis">Pinguicula moranensis</a></i>, and are employed by commercial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orchid" title="Orchid">orchid</a> nurseries to combat pests.</p>
<p>Butterworts also produce a strong bactericide which prevents insects from rotting while they are being digested. According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, this property has long been known by northern Europeans, who applied butterwort leaves to the <a href="/wiki/Ulcer_(dermatology)" title="Ulcer (dermatology)">sores</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a> to promote healing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Additionally, butterwort leaves were used to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curdle" title="Curdle">curdle</a> milk and form a buttermilk-like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermented_milk_product" title="Fermented milk product">fermented milk product</a> called <span lang="sv" xml:lang="sv"><i><a href="/wiki/Filmj%C3%B6lk" title="Filmjölk">filmjölk</a></i></span> (Sweden) and <span lang="no" xml:lang="no"><i>tjukkmjølk</i></span> (Norway).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinguicula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Pinguicula_species" title="List of Pinguicula species">List of Pinguicula species</a></div>
<p><i>Pinguicula</i> belong to the Bladderwort family (<a href="/wiki/Lentibulariaceae" title="Lentibulariaceae">Lentibulariaceae</a>), along with <i><a href="/wiki/Utricularia" title="Utricularia">Utricularia</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Genlisea" title="Genlisea">Genlisea</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Siegfried_Jost_Casper" title="Siegfried Jost Casper">Siegfried Jost Casper</a> <a href="/wiki/Systematics" title="Systematics">systematically</a> divided them into three <a href="/wiki/Subgenus" title="Subgenus">subgenera</a> with 15 <a href="/wiki/Section_(botany)" title="Section (botany)">sections</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-casper1966_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-casper1966-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>A detailed study of the phylogenetics of butterworts by Cieslak <i>et al.</i> (2005)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cieslak2005_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-cieslak2005-1">[1]</a></sup> found that all of the currently accepted <a href="/wiki/Subgenus" title="Subgenus">subgenera</a> and many of the <a href="/wiki/Section_(biology)" title="Section (biology)">sections</a> were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyphyletic" title="Polyphyletic">polyphyletic</a>. The diagram below gives a more accurate representation of the correct <a href="/wiki/Cladogram" title="Cladogram">cladogram</a>. Polyphyletic sections are marked with an <b>*</b>.</p>
<pre>

                  │             Heterophyllum <b>*</b>, Agnata <b>*</b>, Isoloba <b>*</b>, Crassifolia)
                  │
              ┌───┤
              │   │
              │   │
       ┌──────┤   └────Clade II (Section Micranthus <b>*</b> = <i>P. alpina</i>)
       │      │
       │      │
   ┌───┤      └────────Clade III (Sections Micranthus <b>*</b>, Nana)
   │   │
   │   │
───┤   └───────────────Clade IV (Section Pinguicula)
   │
