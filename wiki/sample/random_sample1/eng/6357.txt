<p><b>Burlingame</b> is a city in <a href="/wiki/San_Mateo_County,_California" title="San Mateo County, California">San Mateo County</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. It is located on the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Peninsula" title="San Francisco Peninsula">San Francisco Peninsula</a> and has a significant shoreline on <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay" title="San Francisco Bay">San Francisco Bay</a>. An early suburb of <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, the city is named after diplomat <a href="/wiki/Anson_Burlingame" title="Anson Burlingame">Anson Burlingame</a> and is often referred to as the City of Trees due to the number of trees within the city and its numerous eucalyptus groves. Burlingame is known for its high residential quality of life with an excellent public school system. The current median home value in Burlingame is $1,800,000+ and as of the 2010 U.S. Census, Burlingame had a population of 28,807.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burlingame,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Burlingame is on the <a href="/wiki/Ranchos_of_California" title="Ranchos of California">Mexican land grant</a> <a href="/wiki/Rancho_San_Mateo" title="Rancho San Mateo">Rancho San Mateo</a> given by Governor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pio_Pico" title="Pio Pico">Pio Pico</a> to his secretary, Cayetano Arena in 1845. Cayetano soon sold the land to San Francisco-based merchant <a href="/wiki/William_Davis_Merry_Howard" title="William Davis Merry Howard">William Davis Merry Howard</a>. Howard retired to live on the rancho for the remaining eight years of his life. Howard planted many eucalyptus trees on his property.</p>
<p>Howard's early death in 1856 led to the sale of most of the land to <a href="/wiki/William_Chapman_Ralston" title="William Chapman Ralston">William C. Ralston</a>, a prominent banker. In 1866, Anson Burlingame, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Ambassador_to_China" title="United States Ambassador to China">US Minister to China</a> visited Ralston, and by the time he left he was the owner of 1,043 acres (4 km<sup>2</sup>) of land. In 1868, Ralston named the settlement after his friend, Anson Burlingame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, Burlingame would not come back to the area again because on a visit to Russia in 1870, Burlingame died. With his death the land reverted to Ralston.</p>
<p>Ralston had plans for the area which he called "Ralstonville", but he died in 1875 without many of his plans being realized. The land then passed to Ralston's business partner Senator <a href="/wiki/William_Sharon" title="William Sharon">William Sharon</a>. Sharon died in 1885, and Sharon's son-in-law, <a href="/wiki/Francis_G._Newlands" title="Francis G. Newlands">Francis G. Newlands</a>, became executor of Sharon’s estate. Newlands had grand plans of his own. His vision was to build estates that surrounded a country club, similar to the development he helped create in <a href="/wiki/Chevy_Chase,_Maryland" title="Chevy Chase, Maryland">Chevy Chase, Maryland</a>. The Burlingame Country Club was organized in 1893.</p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/1906_San_Francisco_earthquake" title="1906 San Francisco earthquake">1906 San Francisco earthquake</a>, many people looking to escape the hardships of a city in ruins flocked south. Hundreds of lots in Burlingame were sold in 1906 and 1907. Just two years after the quake, the town of Burlingame was incorporated June 6, 1908. By 1910, the neighboring town of Easton, on the former <a href="/wiki/Rancho_Buri_Buri" title="Rancho Buri Buri">Rancho Buri Buri</a>, was annexed and became part of Burlingame as well.</p>
<dl>
<dt>City of Trees</dt>
</dl>
<p>Burlingame is known as the "City of Trees" due to the number of trees within the city (18,000 public trees). In 1908, the Burlingame board of trustees passed an ordinance "prohibiting cutting, injuring, or destroying trees".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Most residential properties have trees owned and protected by the city on their public right of way. In addition the city has many parks and Eucalyptus groves that add to the overall tree numbers. The Eucalyptus groves are west of the city on <a href="/wiki/Interstate_280_(California)" title="Interstate 280 (California)">Interstate 280</a> and grow along many city streets, such as the heritage Jules Francard Grove along the Caltrain tracks north of Burlingame Avenue,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> following <a href="/wiki/El_Camino_Real_(California)" title="El Camino Real (California)">El Camino Real</a>, and along other smaller local streets, such as Burlingame Avenue. Washington Park, with Burlingame Avenue at its southern edge, Burlingame High School at its northern edge, and the Caltrain line at its western edge is the oldest park in Burlingame. It was originally part of the estate of millionaire cigar retailer Moses A. Gunst, and some of the existing large trees within the park were part of this estate.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burlingame,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Highways">Highways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burlingame,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Highways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are four highways passing through Burlingame. <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_101_in_California" title="U.S. Route 101 in California">Highway 101</a> runs near the bay, coming from <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose</a> and going to <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>. <a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_82" title="California State Route 82">Highway 82</a>, also known as <a href="/wiki/El_Camino_Real_(California)" title="El Camino Real (California)">El Camino Real</a>, follows a parallel course. <a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_35" title="California State Route 35">Highway 35</a> connects with <a href="/wiki/Interstate_280_(California)" title="Interstate 280 (California)">Interstate 280</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caltrain">Caltrain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burlingame,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Caltrain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Caltrain" title="Caltrain">Caltrain</a> has been serving Burlingame since 1985 when it bought out <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Pacific" title="Southern Pacific">Southern Pacific</a>. It used the same depot that was used in the early 20th century. It was labeled Burlingame, for the country club.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="BART">BART</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burlingame,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: BART">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bay_Area_Rapid_Transit" title="Bay Area Rapid Transit">Bay Area Rapid Transit</a> has its final stop in Millbrae, just north of Burlingame. BART's tracks are within Burlingame city limits.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burlingame,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the city has a total area of 6.1 square miles (16 km<sup>2</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> 4.4 square miles (11 km<sup>2</sup>) of it is land and 1.7 square miles (4.4 km<sup>2</sup>) of it (27.25%) is water.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burlingame,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This region experiences warm (but not hot) and dry summers, with no average monthly temperatures above 71.6 °F. According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_Climate_Classification" title="Köppen Climate Classification">Köppen Climate Classification</a> system, Burlingame has a <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate#Warm-summer_Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">warm-summer Mediterranean climate</a>, abbreviated "Csb" on climate maps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010">2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burlingame,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2010">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 United States Census</a><sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Burlingame,_California&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> reported that Burlingame had a population of 28,806. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 6,537.9 people per square mile (2,524.4/km²). The racial makeup of Burlingame was 19,510 (67.7%) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 360 (1.2%) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 74 (0.3%) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 5,841 (20.3%) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 139 (0.5%) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 1,451 (5.0%) from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 1,431 (5.0%) from two or more races. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 3,966 persons (13.8%).</p>
<p>The Census reported that 28,357 people (98.4% of the population) lived in households, 155 (0.5%) lived in non-institutionalized group quarters, and 294 (1.0%) were institutionalized.</p>
<p>There were 12,361 households, out of which 3,640 (29.4%) had children under the age of 18 living in them, 5,735 (46.4%) were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">opposite-sex married couples</a> living together, 1,039 (8.4%) had a female householder with no husband present, 409 (3.3%) had a male householder with no wife present. There were 627 (5.1%) <a href="/wiki/POSSLQ" title="POSSLQ">unmarried opposite-sex partnerships</a>, and 101 (0.8%) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Same-sex_partnerships" title="Same-sex partnerships">same-sex married couples or partnerships</a>. 4,276 households (34.6%) were made up of individuals and 1,201 (9.7%) had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.29. There were 7,183 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Family_(U.S._Census)" title="Family (U.S. Census)">families</a> (58.1% of all households); the average family size was 3.02.</p>
<p>The population was spread out with 6,256 people (21.7%) under the age of 18, 1,496 people (5.2%) aged 18 to 24, 8,872 people (30.8%) aged 25 to 44, 8,136 people (28.2%) aged 45 to 64, and 4,046 people (14.0%) who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 40.5 years. For every 100 females there were 90.4 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 88.2 males.</p>
<p>There were 13,027 housing units at an average density of 2,956.7 per square mile (1,141.6/km²), of which 5,821 (47.1%) were owner-occupied, and 6,540 (52.9%) were occupied by renters. The homeowner vacancy rate was 1.3%; the rental vacancy rate was 4.5%. 15,345 people (53.3% of the population) lived in owner-occupied housing units and 13,012 people (45.2%) lived in rental housing units.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000">2000</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burlingame,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2000">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of the U.S Census of 2000, there were 27,363 people and 25,577 households residing in the city. There were 11,615 housing units with 5,389 owner-occupied homes costing at a median value of $1,000,000+.</p>
<p>There were 12,511 households out of which 24.1% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 45.1% were married couples living together, 7.7% had a female householder with no husband present, and 44.4% were non-families. 35.6% of all households were made up of individuals and 10.0% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.21 and the average family size was 2.93. The age distribution is: 19.2% under the age of 18, 5.5% from 18 to 24, 36.9% from 25 to 44, 23.2% from 45 to 64, and 15.2% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 38 years. For every 100 females there were 91.5 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 87.8 males.</p>
<p>According to a 2009 estimate, the median income for a household was $92,295, and the median income for a family was $113,440.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-factfinder.census.gov_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-factfinder.census.gov-18">[18]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the city was $46,573. About 3% of families and 6% of the population were below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-factfinder.census.gov_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-factfinder.census.gov-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burlingame,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1920s Burlingame became a location for automobile retailers. In 1958 Burlingame annexed the area including Burlingame Plaza and Mills Peninsula Hospital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sevendev_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sevendev-21">[21]</a></sup> Due to the proximity to San Francisco International Airport and a population increase beginning in the 1960s, various airline support service businesses opened in Burlingame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SJMagazine_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-SJMagazine-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mills Peninsula Health Services, the largest employer in Burlingame, employs around 2,500 people. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LSG/Sky_Chefs,_Inc." title="LSG/Sky Chefs, Inc.">LSG/Sky Chefs, Inc.</a>, the eighth-largest employer in terms of Burlingame operations, has around 281 people employed there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Top15_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Top15-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Guittard_Chocolate_Company" title="Guittard Chocolate Company">Guittard Chocolate Company</a> is headquartered in Burlingame;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> as the city's 10th largest employer it has around 210 employees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Top15_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Top15-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Virgin_America" title="Virgin America">Virgin America</a>'s headquarters are located in Suite 450 at Bay Park Plaza II in Burlingame;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simmers_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Simmers-26">[26]</a></sup> as the 13th largest employer, Virgin America has around 200 employees at its headquarters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Top15_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Top15-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The online discount brokerage <a href="/wiki/Zecco.com" title="Zecco.com">Zecco.com</a> operates one of two California offices in Burlingame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The United States division of <a href="/wiki/Natsume_(company)" title="Natsume (company)">Natsume</a>, a video game company, is headquartered in Burlingame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/China_Airlines" title="China Airlines">China Airlines</a> operates the San Francisco Branch Office (Chinese: 舊金山分公司 <i>Jiùjīnshān Fēngōngsī</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>) in Burlingame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In previous eras <a href="/wiki/All_Nippon_Airways" title="All Nippon Airways">All Nippon Airways</a> located its San Francisco Office in Burlingame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preschools">Preschools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burlingame,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Preschools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

They are A Child's Way, Palcare, Stepping Stone, <a href="/wiki/Peninsula_Temple_Sholom" title="Peninsula Temple Sholom">Peninsula Temple Sholom</a>, Bridge Point Academy, Burlingame Montessori, St. Paul's Co-Op Nursery School, Learning Links, Morning Glory Montessori, Sunshine Family Child Care, United Methodist Co-Op Nursery School: Toddler Room, Kiddie Lab, First Presbyterian School, and Tout About Toys.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_and_secondary_schools">Primary and secondary schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burlingame,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Primary and secondary schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt>Public schools</dt>
</dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/San_Mateo_Union_High_School_District" title="San Mateo Union High School District">San Mateo Union High School District</a> operates local high schools while the <a href="/wiki/Burlingame_School_District" title="Burlingame School District">Burlingame School District</a> operates elementary and middle schools.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Burlingame_High_School_(California)" title="Burlingame High School (California)">Burlingame High School</a> is the city's sole public high school. Burlingame Intermediate School is Burlingame's sole public middle school. There are six public elementary schools serving Burlingame. They are Franklin Elementary, Lincoln Elementary, McKinley Elementary, Roosevelt Elementary, Washington Elementary, and the newly reopened Hoover Elementary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> According to the 2009 Base Academic Performance Index (API) Scores from the California Department of Education, the Burlingame School District ranks among the best in the state, with 4 out of their 6 public elementary schools (Roosevelt Elementary, Washington Elementary, Franklin Elementary, and Lincoln Elementary) scoring well between 880 and 925, and with ratings of 9 or 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Burlingame school district enrollment has continually been increasing as young families move to the city. The city has passed two bond measures to add new facilities to fit all the new enrollment with new buildings added to Lincoln, McKinley and BIS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> There are still students in portable classrooms so, the school district also has purchased and has renovated the Hoover School, which was built in 1931 and needed modernization. A lawsuit was filed by neighbors of the school,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> but it has since been resolved and the school opened on schedule for the Fall, 2016 term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Private Schools</dt>
</dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mercy_High_School_(Burlingame,_California)" title="Mercy High School (Burlingame, California)">Mercy High School</a> is the only private Catholic all-girls high school in Burlingame. It was founded in 1931 by the Sisters of Mercy. The school itself is located in the prestigious Kohl Mansion which is a Historic Landmark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Also Our Lady of Angels School and St. Catherine of Siena School are located in Burlingame. In 1921, the silent version of the film Little Lord Fauntleroy, with Mary Pickford, was filmed in the Kohl Mansion.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_libraries">Public libraries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burlingame,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Public libraries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Burlingame Library is located in Burlingame. It was established by city ordinance October 11, 1909. Following the Loma Prieta earthquake in 1989 the City approved a bond issue to reconstruct the library. The architecture has won awards and was featured in Library Journal as well as earning a cover story in American Libraries. There is a secondary location on Easton Drive, which is substantially smaller than the main branch. Both are operated by the <a href="/wiki/Peninsula_Library_System" title="Peninsula Library System">Peninsula Library System</a>, the library authority for the county.</p>
