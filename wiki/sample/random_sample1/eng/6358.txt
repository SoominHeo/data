<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Honeycomb structure</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Honeycomb structures</b> are natural or man-made structures that have the geometry of a <a href="/wiki/Honeycomb" title="Honeycomb">honeycomb</a> to allow the minimization of the amount of used material to reach minimal weight and minimal material cost. The geometry of honeycomb structures can vary widely but the common feature of all such structures is an array of hollow cells formed between thin vertical walls. The cells are often <a href="/wiki/Column" title="Column">columnar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hexagon" title="Hexagon">hexagonal</a> in shape. A honeycomb shaped structure provides a material with minimal density and relative high out-of-plane <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compression_strength" title="Compression strength">compression properties</a> and out-of-plane <a href="/wiki/Shear_modulus" title="Shear modulus">shear properties</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wahl_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-wahl-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Man-made honeycomb structural materials are commonly made by layering a honeycomb material between two thin layers that provide strength in tension. This forms a plate-like assembly. Honeycomb materials are widely used where flat or slightly curved surfaces are needed and their high <a href="/wiki/Specific_strength" title="Specific strength">Specific strength</a> is valuable. They are widely used in the <a href="/wiki/Aerospace" title="Aerospace">aerospace</a> industry for this reason, and honeycomb materials in aluminum, fibreglass and advanced composite materials have been featured in aircraft and rockets since the 1950s. They can also be found in many other fields, from packaging materials in the form of paper-based honeycomb cardboard, to sporting goods like skis and snowboards.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honeycomb_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Natural honeycomb structures include <a href="/wiki/Beehive" title="Beehive">beehives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honeycomb_weathering" title="Honeycomb weathering">honeycomb weathering</a> in rocks, <a href="/wiki/Tripe" title="Tripe">tripe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a>.</p>
<p>Man-made honeycomb structures include <a href="/wiki/Sandwich-structured_composite" title="Sandwich-structured composite">sandwich-structured composites</a> with honeycomb <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sandwich_core&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sandwich core (page does not exist)">cores</a>. Man-made honeycomb structures are manufactured by using a variety of different materials, depending on the intended application and required characteristics, from <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermoplastics" title="Thermoplastics">thermoplastics</a>, used for low strength and stiffness for low load applications, to high strength and stiffness for high performance applications, from aluminum or fiber reinforced plastics. The strength of laminated or sandwich panels depends on the size of the panel, facing material used and the number or density of the honeycomb cells within it. Honeycomb composites are used widely in many industries, from aerospace industries, automotive and furniture to packaging and logistics. The material takes its name from its visual resemblance to a bee's <a href="/wiki/Honeycomb" title="Honeycomb">honeycomb</a> – a hexagonal sheet structure.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honeycomb_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The hexagonal comb of the <a href="/wiki/Honey_bee" title="Honey bee">honey bee</a> has been admired and wondered about from ancient times. The first man-made honeycomb is said to have been manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Daedalus" title="Daedalus">Daedalus</a> from gold by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lost_wax_casting" title="Lost wax casting">lost wax casting</a> more than 3000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marcus_Varro" title="Marcus Varro">Marcus Varro</a> reports that the Greek geometricians <a href="/wiki/Euclid" title="Euclid">Euclid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zenodorus_(mathematician)" title="Zenodorus (mathematician)">Zenodorus</a> found that the <a href="/wiki/Hexagon" title="Hexagon">hexagon</a> shape makes most efficient use of space and building materials. The interior ribbing and hidden chambers in the <a href="/wiki/Dome" title="Dome">dome</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Pantheon,_Rome" title="Pantheon, Rome">Pantheon</a> in Rome is an early example of a honeycomb structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a> discusses in 1638 the resistance of hollow solids: "Art, and nature even more, makes use of these in thousands of operations in which robustness is increased without adding weight, as is seen in the bones of birds and in many stalks that are light and very resistant to bending and breaking”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hooke" title="Robert Hooke">Robert Hook</a> discovers in 1665 that the natural cellular structure of cork is similar to the hexagonal honeybee comb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> states in 1859 that "the comb of the hive-bee, as far as we can see, is absolutely perfect in economizing labour and wax”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first paper honeycomb structures might have been made by the <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> 2000 years ago for ornaments, but no reference for this has been found. Paper honeycombs and the expansion production process has been invented in Halle/Saale in Germany by Hans Heilbrun in 1901<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> for decorative applications. First honeycomb structures from corrugated metal sheets had been proposed for bee keeping in 1890.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> For the same purpose, as foundation sheets to harvest more honey, a honeycomb moulding process using a paper paste glue mixture had been patented in 1878.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The three basic techniques for honeycomb production that are still used today—expansion, corrugation and moulding—were already developed by 1901 for non-sandwich applications.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hugo_Junkers" title="Hugo Junkers">Hugo Junkers</a> first explored the idea of a honeycomb core within a laminate structure. He proposed and patented the first honeycomb cores for aircraft application in 1915.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> He described in detail his concept to replace the fabric covered aircraft structures by metal sheets and reasoned that a metal sheet can also be loaded in compression if it is supported at very small intervals by arranging side by side a series of square or rectangular cells or triangular or hexagonal hollow bodies. The problem of bonding a continuous skin to cellular cores led Junkers later to the open corrugated structure, which could be riveted or welded together.</p>
<p>The first use of honeycomb structures for structural applications had been independently proposed for building application and published already in 1914.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1934 <a href="/wiki/Edward_G._Budd" title="Edward G. Budd">Edward G. Budd</a> patented a welded steel honeycomb sandwich panel from corrugated metal sheets and <a href="/wiki/Claude_Dornier" title="Claude Dornier">Claude Dornier</a> aimed 1937 to solve the core-skin bonding problem by rolling or pressing a skin which is in a plastic state into the core cell walls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The first successful structural adhesive bonding of honeycomb sandwich structures was achieved by <a href="/wiki/Norman_de_Bruyne" title="Norman de Bruyne">Norman de Bruyne</a> of <a href="/wiki/Aero_Research_Limited" title="Aero Research Limited">Aero Research Limited</a>, who patented an adhesive with the right viscosity to form resin fillets on the honeycomb core in 1938.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/North_American_XB-70_Valkyrie" title="North American XB-70 Valkyrie">North American XB-70 Valkyrie</a> made extensive use of <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a> honeycomb panels using a <a href="/wiki/Brazing" title="Brazing">brazing</a> process they developed.</p>

<li>36 BC <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marcus_Varro" title="Marcus Varro">Marcus Varro</a> reports most efficient use of space and building materials by <a href="/wiki/Hexagon" title="Hexagon">hexagonal</a> shape.</li>

<li>1638 <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a> discusses hollow solids and their increase of resistance without adding weight.</li>
<li>1665 <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hooke" title="Robert Hooke">Robert Hook</a> discovers that the natural cellular structure of cork is similar to the hexagonal honeybee comb.</li>
<li>1859 <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> states that the comb of the hive-bee is absolutely perfect in economizing labour and wax.</li>
<li>1877 F. H. Küstermann invents a honeycomb moulding process using a paper paste glue mixture.</li>
<li>1890 Julius Steigel invents the honeycomb production process from corrugated metal sheets.</li>
<li>1901 Hans Heilbrun invents the hexagonal paper honeycombs and the expansion production process.</li>
<li>1914 R. Höfler and S. Renyi patent the first use of honeycomb structures for structural applications.</li>
<li>1915 <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Junkers" title="Hugo Junkers">Hugo Junkers</a> patents the first honeycomb cores for aircraft application.</li>
<li>1931 George Thomson proposes to use decorative expended paper honeycombs for lightweight plasterboard panels.</li>
<li>1934 <a href="/wiki/Edward_G._Budd" title="Edward G. Budd">Edward G. Budd</a> patents welded steel honeycomb sandwich panel from corrugated metal sheets.</li>
<li>1937 <a href="/wiki/Claude_Dornier" title="Claude Dornier">Claude Dornier</a> patents a honeycomb sandwich panel with skins pressed in a plastic state into the core cell walls.</li>
<li>1938 <a href="/wiki/Norman_de_Bruyne" title="Norman de Bruyne">Norman de Bruyne</a> patents the structural adhesive bonding of honeycomb sandwich structures.</li>
<li>1941 John D. Lincoln proposes the use expanded paper honeycombs for aircraft radomes</li>

<li>1980s Thermoplastic honeycombs produced by extrusion processes are introduced.</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacture">Manufacture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honeycomb_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Manufacture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The three traditional honeycomb production techniques, expansion, corrugation, and moulding, were all developed by 1901 for non-sandwich applications. For decorative applications the expanded honeycomb production reached a remarkable degree of automation in the first decade of the 20th century.</p>
<p>Today honeycomb cores are manufactured via the expansion process and the corrugation process from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Composite_materials" title="Composite materials">composite materials</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass-reinforced_plastic" title="Glass-reinforced plastic">glass-reinforced plastic</a> (also known as fiberglass), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber_reinforced_plastic" title="Carbon fiber reinforced plastic">carbon fiber reinforced plastic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nomex" title="Nomex">Nomex</a> aramide paper reinforced plastic, or from a metal (usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">aluminum</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Honeycombs from metals (like aluminum) are today produced by the expansion process. Continuous processes of folding honeycombs from a single aluminum sheet after cutting slits had been developed already around 1920.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Continuous in-line production of metal honeycomb can be done from metal rolls by cutting and bending.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thermoplastic honeycomb cores (usually from <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">polypropylene</a>) are usually made by extrusion processed via a block of extruded profiles<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> or extruded tubes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> from which the honeycomb sheets are sliced.</p>
<p>Recently a new, unique process to produce thermoplastic honeycombs has been implemented, allowing a continuous production of a honeycomb core as well as in-line production of honeycombs with direct lamination of skins into cost efficient sandwich panel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Today, a wide variety of materials can be formed into a honeycomb composite. For example <a href="/wiki/Paperboard" title="Paperboard">paperboard</a> honeycomb is used in <a href="/wiki/Paper_pallet" title="Paper pallet">paper pallets</a> and package <a href="/wiki/Cushioning" title="Cushioning">cushioning</a>, blocking and bracing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bitzer_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bitzer-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>A honeycomb <a href="/wiki/Mesh" title="Mesh">mesh</a> is often used in <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamics</a> to reduce or to create wind <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulence</a>. It is also used to obtain a standard profile in a <a href="/wiki/Wind_tunnel" title="Wind tunnel">wind tunnel</a> (temperature, flow speed). A major factor in choosing the right mesh is the length ratio (length vs honeycomb cell diameter) <i>L/d</i>.</p>
<p>Length ratio &lt; 1: Honeycomb meshes of low length ratio can be used on vehicles front <a href="/wiki/Grille" title="Grille">grille</a>. Beside the aesthetic reasons, these meshes are used as screens to get a uniform profile and to reduce the intensity of turbulence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WTdesi_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-WTdesi-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Length ratio &gt;&gt; 1: Honeycomb meshes of large length ratio reduce lateral turbulence and <a href="/wiki/Eddy_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Eddy (fluid dynamics)">eddies</a> of the flow. Early wind tunnels used them with no screens; unfortunately, this method introduced high turbulence intensity in the test section. Most modern tunnels use both honeycomb and screens.</p>
<p>While aluminium honeycombs are common use in the industry, other materials are offered for specific applications. People using metal structures should take care of removing <a href="/wiki/Burr_(edge)" title="Burr (edge)">burrs</a> as they can introduce additional turbulences. <a href="/wiki/Polycarbonate" title="Polycarbonate">Polycarbonate</a> structures are a low-cost alternative.</p>
<p>The honeycombed, screened center of this open-circuit air intake for Langley's first wind tunnel ensured a steady, non-turbulent flow of air. Two mechanics pose near the entrance end of the actual tunnel, where air was pulled into the test section through a honeycomb arrangement to smooth the flow.</p>
<p>Honeycomb is not the only cross-section available in order to reduce eddies in an airflow. Square, rectangular, circular and hexagonal cross-sections are other choices available, although honeycomb is generally the preferred choice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honeycomb_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In combination with two skins applied on the honeycomb, the structure offers a sandwich panel with excellent rigidity at minimal weight. The behavior of the honeycomb structures is <a href="/wiki/Orthotropic_material" title="Orthotropic material">orthotropic</a>, meaning the panels react differently depending on the orientation of the structure. It is therefore necessary to distinguish between the directions of <a href="/wiki/Symmetry" title="Symmetry">symmetry</a>, the so-called L and W-direction. The L-direction is the strongest and the stiffest direction. The weakest direction is at 60° from the L-direction (in the case of a regular <a href="/wiki/Hexagon" title="Hexagon">hexagon</a>) and the most compliant direction is the W-direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wahl_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-wahl-1">[1]</a></sup> Another important property of honeycomb sandwich core is its compression strength. Due to the efficient hexagonal configuration, where walls support each other, compression strength of honeycomb cores is typically higher (at same weight) compared to other sandwich core structures such as, for instance, foam cores or corrugated cores.</p>
