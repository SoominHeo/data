<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Pan-Germanicism</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Pan-Germanism" title="Pan-Germanism">Pan-Germanism</a></div>
<p><b>Pan-Germanicism</b> is an ideology that promotes the unity of the <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic peoples</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-Germanicism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pan-Germanicism became especially significant beginning in 19th century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Republic_of_Germany" title="Federal Republic of Germany">Germany</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nationalist" title="Nationalist">nationalist</a> concept where it was promoted alongside <a href="/wiki/Pan-Germanism" title="Pan-Germanism">pan-German</a> ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 20th century the German <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a> sought to create a <a href="/wiki/Greater_Germanic_Reich" title="Greater Germanic Reich">Greater Germanic Reich</a> that would include most of the Germanic peoples of Europe within it under the leadership of Germany, including peoples such as the <a href="/wiki/Danes" title="Danes">Danes</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Norwegians" title="Norwegians">Norwegians</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Flemish_people" title="Flemish people">Flemish</a> within it, with the possible exception of the <a href="/wiki/British_people" title="British people">British</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pan-Germanicism met resistance within <a href="/wiki/Scandinavians" title="Scandinavians">Scandinavian</a> nations in the 19th century. They promoted <a href="/wiki/Scandinavism" title="Scandinavism">Scandinavism</a> (or pan-Scandinavianism) while rejecting pan-Germanicism, viewing Scandinavians as the "Old North" - descendents of the original Nordic peoples. Consequently, they were disgusted by the Germans attempting to dominate them, as they viewed Germans as inheritors of Nordic culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Anti-German Scandinavism surged in Denmark in the 1930s and 1940s in response to the pan-Germanic ambitions of Nazi Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
