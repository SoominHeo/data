<p><b>Crime fiction</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Literary_genre" title="Literary genre">literary genre</a> that fictionalises <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crimes</a>, their detection, criminals, and their <a href="/wiki/Motive_(law)" title="Motive (law)">motives</a>. It is usually distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Literary_fiction" title="Literary fiction">mainstream</a> fiction and other genres such as <a href="/wiki/Historical_fiction" title="Historical fiction">historical fiction</a> or <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a>, but the boundaries are indistinct. Crime fiction has multiple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subgenres" title="Subgenres">subgenres</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Detective_fiction" title="Detective fiction">detective fiction</a> (such as the <a href="/wiki/Whodunit" title="Whodunit">whodunit</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Courtroom_drama" title="Courtroom drama">courtroom drama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hardboiled" title="Hardboiled">hard-boiled</a> fiction and <a href="/wiki/Legal_thriller" title="Legal thriller">legal thrillers</a>. <a href="/wiki/Suspense" title="Suspense">Suspense</a> and mystery are key elements that are nearly ubiquitous to the genre.</p>
<p>In Italy people commonly call a story about detectives or crimes <i><a href="/wiki/Giallo" title="Giallo">giallo</a></i> (English: yellow), because books of crime fiction have usually had a yellow cover since the 1930s.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_crime_fiction">History of crime fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crime_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of crime fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_crime_fiction" title="History of crime fiction">History of crime fiction</a></div>
<p>The earliest known crime fiction is Thomas Skinner Sturr's anonymous <i>Richmond, or stories in the life of a Bow Street officer</i> (1827); the earliest full-length novel in the genre is <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rector_of_Veilbye" title="The Rector of Veilbye">The Rector of Veilbye</a></i> by the Danish author <a href="/wiki/Steen_Steensen_Blicher" title="Steen Steensen Blicher">Steen Steensen Blicher</a>, published in 1829. Better known are the earlier dark works of <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">Edgar Allan Poe</a> (e.g., "<a href="/wiki/The_Murders_in_the_Rue_Morgue" title="The Murders in the Rue Morgue">The Murders in the Rue Morgue</a>" (1841), "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Mystery_of_Marie_Roget" title="The Mystery of Marie Roget">The Mystery of Marie Roget</a>" (1842), and "<a href="/wiki/The_Purloined_Letter" title="The Purloined Letter">The Purloined Letter</a>" (1844)). <a href="/wiki/Wilkie_Collins" title="Wilkie Collins">Wilkie Collins</a>' epistolary novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Woman_in_White_(novel)" title="The Woman in White (novel)">The Woman in White</a></i> was published in 1860, while <i><a href="/wiki/The_Moonstone" title="The Moonstone">The Moonstone</a></i> (1868) is often thought to be his masterpiece. French author <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Gaboriau" title="Émile Gaboriau">Émile Gaboriau</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Monsieur_Lecoq_(novel)" title="Monsieur Lecoq (novel)">Monsieur Lecoq</a></i> (1868) laid the groundwork for the methodical, scientifically minded detective. The evolution of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Locked_room_mystery" title="Locked room mystery">locked room mysteries</a> was one of the landmarks in the history of crime fiction. The <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a> mysteries of <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Arthur Conan Doyle">Arthur Conan Doyle</a> are said to have been singularly responsible for the huge popularity in this genre. A precursor was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_F%C3%A9val" title="Paul Féval">Paul Féval</a>, whose series <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Habits_Noirs" title="Les Habits Noirs">Les Habits Noirs</a></i> (1862–67) features <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Yard" title="Scotland Yard">Scotland Yard</a> detectives and criminal conspiracies. The best-selling crime novel of the nineteenth century was <a href="/wiki/Fergus_Hume" title="Fergus Hume">Fergus Hume</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mystery_of_a_Hansom_Cab" title="The Mystery of a Hansom Cab">The Mystery of a Hansom Cab</a></i> (1886), set in Melbourne, Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The evolution of the print <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">mass media</a> in the United Kingdom and the United States in the latter half of the 19th century was crucial in popularising crime fiction and related genres. Literary 'variety' magazines like <i>Strand</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/McClure%27s" title="McClure's">McClure's</a></i>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harper%27s" title="Harper's">Harper's</a></i> quickly became central to the overall structure and function of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Popular_fiction" title="Popular fiction">popular fiction</a> in society, providing a <a href="/wiki/Mass_production" title="Mass production">mass-produced</a> medium that offered cheap, illustrated publications that were essentially disposable.</p>
<p>Like the works of many other important fiction writers of his day—e.g. <a href="/wiki/Wilkie_Collins" title="Wilkie Collins">Wilkie Collins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a>—Arthur Conan Doyle's Sherlock Holmes stories first appeared in serial form in the monthly <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strand_Magazine" title="Strand Magazine">Strand</a></i> magazine in the United Kingdom. The series quickly attracted a wide and passionate following on both sides of the Atlantic, and when Doyle killed off Holmes in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Final_Problem" title="The Final Problem">The Final Problem</a></i>, the public outcry was so great, and the publishing offers for more stories so attractive, that he was reluctantly forced to resurrect him.</p>
<p>Later a set of stereotypic formulae began to appear to cater to various tastes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Categories_of_crime_fiction">Categories of crime fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crime_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Categories of crime fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Crime fiction can be divided into the following four branches:</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Detective_fiction">Detective fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crime_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Detective fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Detective_fiction" title="Detective fiction">Detective fiction</a></div>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Cozy_mystery" title="Cozy mystery">cozy mystery</a>: a subgenre of detective fiction in which profanity, sex, and violence are downplayed or treated humorously.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Locked_room_mystery" title="Locked room mystery">locked room mystery</a>: a specialized kind of a whodunit in which the crime is committed under apparently impossible circumstances, such as a locked room which no intruder could have entered or left.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Whodunit" title="Whodunit">whodunit</a>: the most common form of detective fiction. It features a complex, plot-driven story in which the reader is provided with clues from which the identity of the perpetrator of the crime may be deduced before the solution is revealed at the end of the book.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical_whodunnit" title="Historical whodunnit">historical whodunnit</a>: also a subgenre of <a href="/wiki/Historical_fiction" title="Historical fiction">historical fiction</a>. The setting of the story and the crime has some historical significance</li>
<li>The American <a href="/wiki/Hardboiled" title="Hardboiled">hardboiled</a> school: distinguished by the unsentimental portrayal of sex and violence; the sleuth usually also confronts danger and engages in violence.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Police_procedural" title="Police procedural">police procedural</a>: the detective is a member of the police, and thus the activities of a police force are usually convincingly depicted.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forensic" title="Forensic">forensic</a> crime fiction; similar to the police procedural. The detective the reader follows is usually a medical examiner or pathologist—they must use the forensic evidence left on the body and at the crime scene to catch the killer. This subgenre was first introduced by <a href="/wiki/Patricia_Cornwell" title="Patricia Cornwell">Patricia Cornwell</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Legal_thriller" title="Legal thriller">legal thriller</a>: the major characters are lawyers and their employees, and they become involved in proving their cases.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Spy_fiction" title="Spy fiction">spy novel</a>: the major characters are <a href="/wiki/Espionage" title="Espionage">spies</a>, usually working for an <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_agency" title="Intelligence agency">intelligence agency</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Caper_story" title="Caper story">caper story</a> and the criminal novel: the stories are told from the point of view of the criminals.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Psychological_thriller" title="Psychological thriller">psychological thriller</a> or psychological suspense: this specific subgenre of the <a href="/wiki/Thriller_(genre)" title="Thriller (genre)">thriller genre</a> also incorporates elements from detective fiction, as the protagonist must solve the mystery of the psychological conflict presented in these types of stories.</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crime_fiction_and_mainstream_fiction">Crime fiction and mainstream fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crime_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Crime fiction and mainstream fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>When trying to pigeon-hole fiction, it is extraordinarily difficult to tell where crime fiction starts and where it ends. This is largely attributed to the fact that love, danger and death are central <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motif_(literature)" title="Motif (literature)">motifs</a> in fiction.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A less obvious reason is that the classification of a work may very well be related to the author's reputation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Somerset_Maugham" title="William Somerset Maugham">William Somerset Maugham</a>'s (1874–1965) <a href="/wiki/Novella" title="Novella">novella</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Up_at_the_Villa" title="Up at the Villa">Up at the Villa</a></i> (1941) could very well be classified as crime fiction. This short novel revolves around a woman having a one-night stand with a total stranger who suddenly and unexpectedly commits <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a> in her bedroom and the woman's attempts at disposing of the body so as not to cause a scandal about herself or be suspected of killing the man. As Maugham is not usually rated as a writer of crime novels, <i>Up at the Villa</i> is hardly ever considered to be a crime novel and accordingly can be found in bookshops among his other, "mainstream" novels.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A more recent example is <a href="/wiki/Bret_Easton_Ellis" title="Bret Easton Ellis">Bret Easton Ellis</a>' seminal novel <i><a href="/wiki/American_Psycho" title="American Psycho">American Psycho</a></i> (1991) about the double life of Patrick Bateman, a <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a> <a href="/wiki/Yuppie" title="Yuppie">yuppie</a> and serial killer in New York City in the 1980s. Even though in <i>American Psycho</i> the most heinous crimes are depicted in minute detail, the novel has never been labelled a "crime novel", maybe because it is never explicitly mentioned whether Bateman actually commits the crimes or rather just fantasizes about them.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On the other hand, U.S. author <a href="/wiki/James_M._Cain" title="James M. Cain">James M. Cain</a> is normally seen as a writer belonging to the "hard-boiled" school of crime fiction. However, his novel <i><a href="/wiki/Mildred_Pierce" title="Mildred Pierce">Mildred Pierce</a></i> (1941) is really about the rise to success of an ordinary housewife developing her entrepreneurial skills and—legally—outsmarting her business rivals, and the domestic trouble caused by her success, with, in turn, her husband, her daughter and her lover turning against her. Although no crime is committed anywhere in the book, the novel was reprinted in 1989 by Random House, alongside Cain's thriller <i><a href="/wiki/The_Postman_Always_Rings_Twice_(novel)" title="The Postman Always Rings Twice (novel)">The Postman Always Rings Twice</a></i> (1934), under the heading "Vintage Crime".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>When film director <a href="/wiki/Michael_Curtiz" title="Michael Curtiz">Michael Curtiz</a> adapted <i>Mildred Pierce</i> for the big screen in 1945, he lived up to the cinemagoers' and the producers' expectations by adding a murder that is not in the novel. As potential cinemagoers had been associating Cain with hard-boiled crime fiction only, this trick—exploited in advertisements and trailers—in combination with the casting of then Hollywood star <a href="/wiki/Joan_Crawford" title="Joan Crawford">Joan Crawford</a> in the title role made sure that the film was going to be a box office hit even before it was released.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Seen from a practical point of view, one could argue that a crime novel is simply a novel that can be found in a bookshop on shelves labelled "Crime". (This suggestion has actually been made about <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> but it can be applied here as well.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Penguin_Books" title="Penguin Books">Penguin Books</a> has had a long-standing tradition of publishing crime novels in paperback editions with green covers and spines (as opposed to the orange spines of mainstream literature), thus attracting the eyes of potential buyers when they enter the shop. However, this clever marketing strategy does not tell casual browsers what they are really in for when they buy a particular book.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_discrepancy_between_taste_and_acclaim">The discrepancy between taste and acclaim</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crime_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The discrepancy between taste and acclaim">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Up to the 1960s or so, reading the <a href="/wiki/Paperback" title="Paperback">paperback</a> edition of a crime novel was usually considered a cheap thrill—with the word "cheap" used in both meanings: "inexpensive" and "of minor quality". The educated and civilized world was often interested, at least ostensibly, in the "high art" categorised by <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical music</a>, paintings by renowned artists, famous <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a> and plays like those of <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>. The term "popular art" referred to <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_%27n%27_roll" title="Rock 'n' roll">rock 'n' roll</a>, photography, the <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a> of everyday objects, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comic" title="Comic">comics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a>, detective stories or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erotic_fiction" title="Erotic fiction">erotic fiction</a> (the latter circulating in private prints only to beat the censor), to quote a few examples. The idea of a "<a href="/wiki/Mainstream" title="Mainstream">mainstream</a>" of literary output suggested that any book deviating, in either content or form or both, from the established norm of "high art" was "cheap", and anyone interested in popular culture was uneducated and unsophisticated and most probably originated in a lower socio-economic division of the contextual society. The <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">universities</a> and the other institutions of higher learning also looked down on artists producing "popular art" and categorically refused to critically assess it.</p>
<p>This often did not correlate with the immense popularity of popular art on both sides of the Atlantic, sometimes due to <a href="/wiki/Sensationalism" title="Sensationalism">sensationalism</a>. For example, the British had been fascinated by <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Wallace" title="Edgar Wallace">Edgar Wallace</a>'s (1875–1932) crime novels ever since the author set up a competition offering a reward to any reader who could figure out and describe just how the murder in his first book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Four_Just_Men_(novel)" title="The Four Just Men (novel)">The Four Just Men</a></i> (1906), was committed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="A_reassessment_of_critical_ideals">A reassessment of critical ideals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crime_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: A reassessment of critical ideals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the long run, the vast output of popular fiction could no longer be ignored, and literary critics—gradually, carefully and tentatively—started questioning and assessing the complete notion of the perceived gap between "high art" (or "serious literature") and "popular art" (in America often referred to as "pulp fiction", often verging on "smut and filth"). One of the first scholars to do so was American critic <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Fiedler" title="Leslie Fiedler">Leslie Fiedler</a>. In his book <i>Cross the Border—Close the Gap</i> (1972), he advocates a thorough re-assessment of science fiction, the western, <a href="/wiki/Pornography" title="Pornography">pornographic literature</a> and all the other subgenres that previously had not been considered as "high art", and their inclusion in the literary <a href="/wiki/Canon_(fiction)" title="Canon (fiction)">canon</a>:</p>
<dl>
<dd>The notion of one art for the 'cultural,' i.e., the favored few in any given society and of another subart for the 'uncultured,' i.e., an excluded majority as deficient in Gutenberg skills as they are untutored in 'taste,' in fact represents the last survival in mass industrial societies (capitalist, socialist, communist—it makes no difference in this regard) of an invidious distinction proper only to a class-structured community. Precisely because it carries on, as it has carried on ever since the middle of the eighteenth century, a war against that <a href="/wiki/Anachronism" title="Anachronism">anachronistic</a> survival, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pop_Art" title="Pop Art">Pop Art</a> is, whatever its overt politics, subversive: a threat to all hierarchies insofar as it is hostile to order and ordering in its own realm. What the final intrusion of Pop into the citadels of High Art provides, therefore, for the critic is the exhilarating new possibility of making judgments about the 'goodness' and 'badness' of art quite separated from distinctions between 'high' and 'low' with their concealed class bias.</dd>
</dl>
<p>In other words, it was now up to the literary critics to devise criteria with which they would then be able to assess any new literature along the lines of "good" or "bad" rather than "high" versus "popular".</p>
<p>Accordingly,</p>
<ul>
<li>A conventionally written and dull novel about, say, a "fallen woman" could be ranked lower than a terrifying vision of the future full of action and suspense.</li>
<li>A story about industrial relations in the United Kingdom in the early 20th century—a novel about shocking working conditions, trade unionists, strikers and scabs—need not be more acceptable subject-matter per se than a well-crafted and fast-paced thriller about modern life.</li>
</ul>
<p>But, according to Fiedler, it was also up to the critics to reassess already existing literature. In the case of U.S. crime fiction, writers that so far had been regarded as the authors of nothing but "pulp fiction"—<a href="/wiki/Raymond_Chandler" title="Raymond Chandler">Raymond Chandler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dashiell_Hammett" title="Dashiell Hammett">Dashiell Hammett</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_M._Cain" title="James M. Cain">James M. Cain</a>, and others—were gradually seen in a new light. Today, Chandler's creation, private eye <a href="/wiki/Philip_Marlowe" title="Philip Marlowe">Philip Marlowe</a>—who appears, for example, in his novels <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Sleep" title="The Big Sleep">The Big Sleep</a></i> (1939) and <i><a href="/wiki/Farewell,_My_Lovely" title="Farewell, My Lovely">Farewell, My Lovely</a></i> (1940)—has achieved cult status and has also been made the topic of literary seminars at universities round the world, whereas on first publication Chandler's novels were seen as little more than cheap entertainment for the uneducated masses.</p>
<p>Nonetheless, "murder stories" such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dostoyevsky" title="Dostoyevsky">Dostoyevsky</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Crime_and_Punishment" title="Crime and Punishment">Crime and Punishment</a></i> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Macbeth" title="Macbeth">Macbeth</a></i> are not dependent on their honorary membership in this genre for their acclaim.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pseudonymous_authors">Pseudonymous authors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crime_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pseudonymous authors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As far as the history of crime fiction is concerned, some authors have been reluctant to publish their crime novels under their real names. More currently, some publish pseudonymously because of the belief that since the large booksellers are aware of their historical sales figures, and command a certain degree of influence over publishers, the only way to "break out" of their current advance numbers is to publish as someone with no track record.</p>
<p>In the late 1930s and 40s, British County Court judge Arthur Alexander Gordon Clark (1900–1958) published a number of detective novels under the alias <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyril_Hare" title="Cyril Hare">Cyril Hare</a> in which he made use of his profoundly extensive knowledge of the English legal system. In <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_at_Law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tragedy at Law (page does not exist)">Tragedy at Law</a></i> (1942). Scottish journalist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leopold_Horace_Ognall" title="Leopold Horace Ognall">Leopold Horace Ognall</a> (1908–1979) authored over ninety novels as <a href="/wiki/Hartley_Howard" title="Hartley Howard">Hartley Howard</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Harry_Carmichael&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Harry Carmichael (page does not exist)">Harry Carmichael</a>. When he was still young and unknown, award-winning British novelist <a href="/wiki/Julian_Barnes" title="Julian Barnes">Julian Barnes</a> (born 1946) published some crime novels under the alias Dan Kavanagh. Other authors take delight in cherishing their <a href="/wiki/Alter_ego" title="Alter ego">alter egos</a>: <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Rendell" title="Ruth Rendell">Ruth Rendell</a> (born 1930) writes one sort of crime novels as Ruth Rendell and another type as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barbara_Vine" title="Barbara Vine">Barbara Vine</a>; <a href="/wiki/John_Dickson_Carr" title="John Dickson Carr">John Dickson Carr</a> also used the pseudonym <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carter_Dickson" title="Carter Dickson">Carter Dickson</a>. The author <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evan_Hunter" title="Evan Hunter">Evan Hunter</a> (which itself was a pseudonym) wrote his crime fiction under the name of Ed McBain.</p>

Both cater to the need of the average audience to escape into an idealist world, where the good reaps the rewards, and the bad incur their punishment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Videogames">Videogames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crime_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Videogames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Crime fiction has also expanded to the world of video games, examples being <i><a href="/wiki/L.A._Noire" title="L.A. Noire">L.A. Noire</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Danganronpa" title="Danganronpa">Danganronpa</a></i> series.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Availability_of_crime_novels">Availability of crime novels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crime_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Availability of crime novels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quality_and_availability">Quality and availability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crime_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Quality and availability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As with any other entity, quality of a crime fiction book is not in any meaningful proportion to its availability. Some of the crime novels generally regarded as the finest, including those regularly chosen by experts as belonging to the best 100 crime novels ever written (see bibliography), have been <a href="/wiki/Out_of_print" title="Out of print">out of print</a> ever since their first publication, which often dates back to the 1920s or 30s. The bulk of books that can be found today on the shelves labelled "Crime" consists of recent first publications usually no older than a few years.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classics_and_bestsellers">Classics and bestsellers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crime_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Classics and bestsellers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Furthermore, only a select few authors have achieved the status of "classics" for their published works. A classic is any text that can be received and accepted universally, because they transcend context. A popular, well known example is <a href="/wiki/Agatha_Christie" title="Agatha Christie">Agatha Christie</a>, whose texts, originally published between 1920 and her death in 1976, are available in UK and US editions in all English speaking nations. Christie's works, particularly featuring detectives <a href="/wiki/Hercule_Poirot" title="Hercule Poirot">Hercule Poirot</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miss_Jane_Marple" title="Miss Jane Marple">Miss Jane Marple</a>, have given her the title the 'Queen of Crime' and made her one of the most important and innovative writers in the development of the genre. Her most famous novels include <i><a href="/wiki/Murder_on_the_Orient_Express" title="Murder on the Orient Express">Murder on the Orient Express</a></i> (1934), <i><a href="/wiki/Death_on_the_Nile" title="Death on the Nile">Death on the Nile</a></i> (1937), and the world's best-selling mystery <i><a href="/wiki/And_Then_There_Were_None" title="And Then There Were None">And Then There Were None</a></i> (1939).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other less successful, contemporary authors who are still writing have seen reprints of their earlier works, due to current overwhelming popularity of crime fiction texts among audiences (One only has to look at the amount of crime related television series to observe the astonishing popularity). One example is <a href="/wiki/Val_McDermid" title="Val McDermid">Val McDermid</a>, whose first book appeared as far back as 1987; another is <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>-based author <a href="/wiki/Carl_Hiaasen" title="Carl Hiaasen">Carl Hiaasen</a>, who has been publishing books since 1981, all of which are readily available.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forgotten_classics">Forgotten classics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crime_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Forgotten classics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On the other hand, English crime writer <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Wallace" title="Edgar Wallace">Edgar Wallace</a>, who was immensely popular with the English readership during the early decades of the 20th century (and who achieved fame in German-speaking countries due to the many <a href="/wiki/B_movie" title="B movie">B movies</a> made in the 1950s, 60s, and 70s that were based on his novels), had almost been forgotten in his home country until <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=House_of_Stratus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="House of Stratus (page does not exist)">House of Stratus</a> eventually started republishing many of his 170 books around the turn of the millennium. Similarly, the books by the equally successful American author <a href="/wiki/Erle_Stanley_Gardner" title="Erle Stanley Gardner">Erle Stanley Gardner</a> (1889–1970), creator of the lawyer <a href="/wiki/Perry_Mason" title="Perry Mason">Perry Mason</a>, which have frequently been adapted for film, radio, and TV, were only recently republished in the United Kingdom—books such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Case_of_the_Stuttering_Bishop" title="The Case of the Stuttering Bishop">The Case of the Stuttering Bishop</a></i> (1937), <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Case_of_the_Green-Eyed_Sister&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Case of the Green-Eyed Sister (page does not exist)">The Case of the Green-Eyed Sister</a></i> (1953), etc.</p>
<p>Even television adaptations are not enough to save some authors. <a href="/wiki/Gladys_Mitchell" title="Gladys Mitchell">Gladys Mitchell</a> rivalled Agatha Christie for UK sales in the 1930s and 1940s but only one of her 66 novels remains in print despite a <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> television series of the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mrs_Bradley_Mysteries" title="The Mrs Bradley Mysteries">The Mrs Bradley Mysteries</a></i> in 1999.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Revival_of_past_classics">Revival of past classics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crime_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Revival of past classics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From time to time publishing houses decide, for commercial purposes, to revive long-forgotten authors and reprint one or two of their more commercially successful novels. Apart from <a href="/wiki/Penguin_Books" title="Penguin Books">Penguin Books</a>, who for this purpose have resorted to their old green cover and dug out some of their vintage authors, Pan started a series in 1999 entitled "Pan Classic Crime," which includes a handful of novels by <a href="/wiki/Eric_Ambler" title="Eric Ambler">Eric Ambler</a>, but also American <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Waugh" title="Hillary Waugh">Hillary Waugh</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Last_Seen_Wearing_..._(Hillary_Waugh_novel)" title="Last Seen Wearing ... (Hillary Waugh novel)">Last Seen Wearing ...</a></i>. In 2000, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Canongate_Books" title="Canongate Books">Canongate Books</a> started a series called "Canongate Crime Classics," in which they published <a href="/wiki/John_Franklin_Bardin" title="John Franklin Bardin">John Franklin Bardin</a>'s <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Deadly_Percheron&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Deadly Percheron (page does not exist)">The Deadly Percheron</a></i> (1946)—both a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whodunnit" title="Whodunnit">whodunnit</a> and a <i>roman noir</i> about <a href="/wiki/Amnesia" title="Amnesia">amnesia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insanity" title="Insanity">insanity</a>—and other novels. However, books brought out by smaller publishers like Canongate Books are usually not stocked by the larger bookshops and overseas booksellers. The British Library has also (since 2012) starting republishing "lost" crime classics, with the collection referred to on their website as "British Library Crime Classics series".</p>
<p>Sometimes older crime novels are revived by screenwriters and directors rather than publishing houses. In many such cases, publishers then follow suit and release a so-called "film tie-in" edition showing a still from the movie on the front cover and the film credits on the back cover of the book—yet another marketing strategy aimed at those cinemagoers who may want to do both: first read the book and then watch the film (or vice versa). Recent examples include <a href="/wiki/Patricia_Highsmith" title="Patricia Highsmith">Patricia Highsmith</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Talented_Mr._Ripley" title="The Talented Mr. Ripley">The Talented Mr. Ripley</a></i> (originally published in 1955), <a href="/wiki/Ira_Levin" title="Ira Levin">Ira Levin</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Sliver_(novel)" title="Sliver (novel)">Sliver</a></i> (1991), with the cover photograph depicting a steamy sex scene between <a href="/wiki/Sharon_Stone" title="Sharon Stone">Sharon Stone</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Baldwin" title="William Baldwin">William Baldwin</a> straight from the 1993 movie, and, again, <a href="/wiki/Bret_Easton_Ellis" title="Bret Easton Ellis">Bret Easton Ellis</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/American_Psycho" title="American Psycho">American Psycho</a></i> (1991). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bloomsbury_Publishing_PLC" title="Bloomsbury Publishing PLC">Bloomsbury Publishing PLC</a> on the other hand have launched what they call "Bloomsbury Film Classics"—a series of original novels on which feature films were based. This series includes, for example, <a href="/wiki/Ethel_Lina_White" title="Ethel Lina White">Ethel Lina White</a>'s novel <i>The Wheel Spins</i> (1936), which <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">Alfred Hitchcock</a>—before he went to Hollywood—turned into a much-loved movie entitled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Lady_Vanishes_(1938_film)" title="The Lady Vanishes (1938 film)">The Lady Vanishes</a></i> (1938), and <a href="/wiki/Ira_Levin" title="Ira Levin">Ira Levin</a>'s (born 1929) science fiction thriller <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boys_from_Brazil_(novel)" title="The Boys from Brazil (novel)">The Boys from Brazil</a></i> (1976), which was filmed in <a href="/wiki/The_Boys_from_Brazil_(film)" title="The Boys from Brazil (film)">1978</a>.</p>
<p>Older novels can often be retrieved from the ever-growing <a href="/wiki/Project_Gutenberg" title="Project Gutenberg">Project Gutenberg</a> database.</p>
