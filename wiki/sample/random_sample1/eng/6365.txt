<p>The <i><b>Laws</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a>: Νόμοι; <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>: <i>De Legibus</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) is <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>'s last and longest <a href="/wiki/Dialogue" title="Dialogue">dialogue</a>. The conversation depicted in the work's twelve books begins with the question of who is given the credit for establishing a civilization's <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">laws</a>. Its musings on the ethics of government and law have established it as a classic of <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political philosophy</a> alongside Plato's more widely read <i><a href="/wiki/Republic_(Plato)" title="Republic (Plato)">Republic</a></i>.</p>
<p>Scholars generally agree that Plato wrote this dialogue as an older man, having failed in his effort in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syracuse,_Italy" title="Syracuse, Italy">Syracuse</a> on the island of <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> to guide a tyrant's rule, instead having been thrown in prison. These events are alluded to in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seventh_Letter_(Plato)" title="Seventh Letter (Plato)">Seventh Letter</a></i>. The text is noteworthy as Plato's only undisputed dialogue not to feature <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">Socrates</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary">Summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laws_(dialogue)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Setting">Setting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laws_(dialogue)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Setting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unlike most of Plato's dialogues, Socrates does not appear in the <i>Laws</i>: the dialogue takes place on the island of <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>, and Socrates appears outside of Athens in Plato's writings only twice, in the <i><a href="/wiki/Phaedrus_(dialogue)" title="Phaedrus (dialogue)">Phaedrus</a></i>, where he is just outside the city's walls, and in the <i>Republic</i>, where he goes down to the seaport <a href="/wiki/Piraeus" title="Piraeus">Piraeus</a> five miles outside of Athens. The conversation is instead led by an <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athenian</a> Stranger (in Greek, ξένος <i>xenos</i>) and two other old men, the ordinary <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">Spartan</a> citizen Megillos and the Cretan politician and lawgiver Clinias from <a href="/wiki/Knossos" title="Knossos">Knossos</a>.</p>
<p>The Athenian Stranger, who resembles Socrates but whose name is never mentioned, joins the other two on their religious pilgrimage from Knossos to the cave of <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a>. The entire dialogue takes place during this journey, which mimics the action of <a href="/wiki/Minos" title="Minos">Minos</a>: said by the Cretans to have made their ancient laws, Minos walked this path every nine years in order to receive instruction from Zeus on lawgiving. It is also said to be the longest day of the year, allowing for the densely packed twelve chapters.</p>
<p>By the end of the third book Clinias announces that he has in fact been given the responsibility of creating the laws for a new Cretan colony, and that he would like the Stranger's assistance. The rest of the dialogue proceeds with the three old men, walking towards the cave and making laws for this new city which is called the city of the Magnetes (or Magnesia).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Topics">Topics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laws_(dialogue)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Topics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The question asked at the beginning is not "What is law?" as one would expect. That is the question of the apocryphal Platonic dialogue <i><a href="/wiki/Minos_(dialogue)" title="Minos (dialogue)">Minos</a></i>. The dialogue rather proceeds from the question of who it is that receives credit for creating laws.</p>
<p>The questions of the <i>Laws</i> are quite numerous, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Divine revelation, divine law and law-giving</li>
<li>The role of intelligence in law-giving</li>
<li>The relations of philosophy, religion, and politics</li>
<li>The role of music, exercise and dance in education</li>
<li>Natural law and natural right</li>
</ul>
<p>The dialogue uses primarily the Athenian and Spartan (Lacedaemonian) law systems as background for pinpointing a choice of laws, which the speakers imagine as a more or less coherent set for the new city they are talking about.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparisons">Comparisons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laws_(dialogue)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Comparisons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparisons_to_Plato.27s_other_dialogues">Comparisons to Plato's other dialogues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laws_(dialogue)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Comparisons to Plato's other dialogues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The <i>Laws</i> is similar to and yet in opposition to the <i><a href="/wiki/Republic_(Plato)" title="Republic (Plato)">Republic</a></i>. It is similar in that both dialogues concern the making of a city in speech. The city of the <i>Laws</i> is described as "second best", not because the city of the <i>Republic</i> is the best, but because it is the city of gods and their children. The city of the <i>Laws</i> differs in its allowance of private property and private families, and in the very existence of written laws, from the city of the <i>Republic,</i> with its property-system and community of wives for the guardians, and absence of written law. Also, whereas the <i>Republic</i> is a dialogue between Socrates and several young men, the <i>Laws</i> is a discussion among old men, where children are not allowed and there is always a pretense of piety and ritualism.</p>
<p>Traditionally, the <i>Minos</i> is thought to be the preface, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Epinomis" title="Epinomis">Epinomis</a></i> the epilogue, to the <i>Laws</i>, but these are generally considered by scholars to be spurious.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <i>Laws</i>, Plato takes a harsh view of homosexual relations, and proposes to legislate against them. This differs from the stance taken by <a href="/wiki/Aristophanes" title="Aristophanes">Aristophanes</a> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Symposium_(Plato)" title="Symposium (Plato)">Symposium</a></i> and is in stark contrast to the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phaedrus_(Plato)" title="Phaedrus (Plato)">Phaedrus</a></i>, which presents pederasty in a positive light.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparisons_to_other_works_on_Greek_law">Comparisons to other works on Greek law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laws_(dialogue)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Comparisons to other works on Greek law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Plato was not the only <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greek</a> author writing about the law systems of his day, and making comparisons between the <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athenian</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">Spartan</a> laws. Notably, the <i>Constitution of the Spartans</i> by <a href="/wiki/Xenophon" title="Xenophon">Xenophon</a>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Athenians_(Pseudo-Xenophon)" title="Constitution of the Athenians (Pseudo-Xenophon)">Constitution of the Athenians</a></i>, wrongly attributed to Xenophon, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Athenians_(Aristotle)" title="Constitution of the Athenians (Aristotle)">Constitution of the Athenians</a></i>, possibly by <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> or one of his students, have also survived.</p>
<p>Some centuries later <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a> would also devote attention to the topic of Ancient Greek law systems, e.g. in his <i>Life of Lycurgus</i>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lycurgus_(Sparta)" title="Lycurgus (Sparta)">Lycurgus</a> was the legendary law-giver of the Lacedaemonians. Plutarch compares Lycurgus and his Spartan laws to the law system <a href="/wiki/Numa_Pompilius" title="Numa Pompilius">Numa Pompilius</a> introduced in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> around 700 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both pseudo-Xenophon and Plutarch are stark admirers of the Spartan system, showing less reserve than Plato in expressing that admiration.</p>
