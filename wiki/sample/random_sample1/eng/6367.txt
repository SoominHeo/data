<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Judicial scrivener</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>"<b>Judicial scrivener</b>" is a term used to refer to similar legal professions in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>. Judicial scriveners assist clients in commercial and real estate registration procedures and in the preparation of documents for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Litigation" title="Litigation">litigation</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_scrivener&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, judicial scriveners <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">司法書士</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i><b>shihō shoshi</b></i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> are authorized to represent their clients in <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">real estate</a> registrations, commercial registrations (e.g. the incorporation of companies), preparation of court documents and filings with legal affairs bureaus. Judicial scriveners may also represent clients in summary courts, <a href="/wiki/Arbitration" title="Arbitration">arbitration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mediation" title="Mediation">mediation</a> proceedings, but are not allowed to represent clients in district courts or more advanced stages of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Litigation" title="Litigation">litigation</a>. The more familiar term "solicitor" is also sometimes used to refer to them, although the division of responsibilities is not the same as between <a href="/wiki/Solicitor" title="Solicitor">solicitors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barrister" title="Barrister">barristers</a> in the English legal system. The term "judicial scrivener", while somewhat archaic in tone, is a fairly accurate literal translation of the Japanese term.</p>
<p>Judicial scriveners must pass an examination administered by the Ministry of Justice. The examination tests knowledge of twelve Japanese statutes, the four principal ones being the Civil Code, Real Estate Registration Act, Commercial Code and Commercial Registration Act. (The Corporations Act was added to the examination in 2006.) The examination consists of two written tests followed by one oral test; the overall pass rate is 2.8%. A person may also become qualified as a judicial scrivener by working for ten years as a court secretary, judicial secretary, or prosecutor's secretary.</p>
<p>Judicial scriveners must maintain a membership in the judicial scrivener association <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">司法書士会</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>shihō shoshi kai</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> for the prefecture in which they work. They can be found in solo practice or attached to law firms as employees of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attorney_at_law_(Japan)" title="Attorney at law (Japan)">attorneys at law</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_scrivener&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When Japan adopted a Western-style court system in 1872, it established a profession of <i>daishonin</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">代書人</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> to represent clients in the preparation of documents, alongside the <i>daigennin</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">代言人</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> who represented clients in courtroom arguments (this latter profession became known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attorney_at_law_(Japan)" title="Attorney at law (Japan)"><b>Attorneys at law</b></a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">弁護士</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>bengoshi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> in 1890). A 1919 statute established a separate tier of <i>shihō daishonin</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">司法代書人</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> to handle court documents. The modern <i>shihō shoshi</i> title was adopted under a revised statute in 1935, which was superseded by a new law in 1950.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The other half of the <i>daishonin</i> profession was replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Administrative_scrivener" title="Administrative scrivener">administrative scrivener</a> profession.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Korea">South Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_scrivener&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: South Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> has a similar legal profession known as <i><b>beopmusa</b></i> (Hangul: <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">법무사</span>, Hanja: <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">法務士</span>).</p>
