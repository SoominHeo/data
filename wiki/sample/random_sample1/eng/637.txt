<p>A <b>period 4 element</b> is one of the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical elements</a> in the fourth row (or <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periodic_table_period" title="Periodic table period">period</a></i>) of the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table of the elements</a>. The periodic table is laid out in rows to illustrate recurring (periodic) trends in the chemical behaviour of the elements as their atomic number increases: a new row is begun when chemical behaviour begins to repeat, meaning that elements with similar behaviour fall into the same vertical columns. The fourth period contains 18 elements, beginning with <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> and ending with <a href="/wiki/Krypton" title="Krypton">krypton</a>. As a rule, period 4 elements fill their 4s <a href="/wiki/Electron_shell" title="Electron shell">shells</a> first, then their 3d and 4p shells, in that order; however, there are exceptions, such as <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Period_4_element&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Every single one of these elements is <a href="/wiki/Chemical_stability" title="Chemical stability">stable</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and many are extremely common in the earth's crust and/or core; it is the last period with no unstable elements at all. Many of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transition_metals" title="Transition metals">transition metals</a> in period 4 are incredibly strong, and therefore commonly used in industry, especially <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>. Copper is one of three metals that are not silver or gray in color, along with <a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">caesium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>. Three adjacent elements are known to be toxic, with <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> one of the most well-known <a href="/wiki/Poison" title="Poison">poisons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a> being toxic to humans in large quantities, and bromine, a very toxic liquid. Many elements are essential to humans' survival, such as <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> being what forms bones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gray_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gray-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">Potassium</a> (K) is an <a href="/wiki/Alkali_metal" title="Alkali metal">alkali metal</a>, placed under <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> and over <a href="/wiki/Rubidium" title="Rubidium">rubidium</a>, and is the first element of period 4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is one of the most <a href="/wiki/Reactivity_(chemistry)" title="Reactivity (chemistry)">reactive</a> elements in the periodic table, therefore usually only found in <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compounds</a>. It tends to oxidize in air very rapidly, thus accounting for its rapid reaction with oxygen when freshly exposed to air. When freshly exposed, it is rather silvery, but it quickly begins to tarnish as it reacts with air. It is soft enough to be cut with a knife and it is the second least <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">dense</a> element.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">Potassium</a> has a relatively low <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a>; it will melt just by putting it under a small open flame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It also is less dense than water, and can, in turn, float.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">Calcium</a> (Ca) is the second element in period 4, between potassium and scandium. An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkali_earth_metal" title="Alkali earth metal">alkali earth metal</a>, calcium is almost never found in nature due to its high reactivity with water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It has one of the most widely known and acknowledged biological roles in all animals and some plants, making up bones and teeth, and used in some applications in <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>, such as signals for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cellular_processes" title="Cellular processes">cellular processeses</a>. It is regarded as the most abundant <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> in the body's mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Scandium" title="Scandium">Scandium</a> (Sc) is the third element in period 4, between calcium and titanium, and is the first <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metal</a> in the periodic table. Scandium is quite common in nature, but difficult to find because it is most prevalent in <a href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" title="Rare earth element">rare earth</a> compounds, which are difficult to isolate elements from. Scandium has very few commercial applications because of the aforementioned facts, and currently its only major application is in <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> alloys.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">Titanium</a> (Ti) is an element in period 4, between scandium and vanadium. Titanium is both one of the least dense metals and one of the strongest and most corrosion-resistant, and as such has many applications, especially in alloys with other elements, such as iron. Due to its aforementioned properties, it is commonly used in <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">airplanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Golf_club" title="Golf club">golf clubs</a>, and other objects that must be strong, but lightweight.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">Vanadium</a> (V) is an element in period 4, between titanium and chromium. Vanadium is never found in pure form in nature, but is commonly found in compounds. Vanadium is similar to titanium in many ways, such as being very corrosion-resistant, however, unlike titanium, it oxidizes in air even at room temperature. All vanadium compounds have at least some level of toxicity, with some of them being extremely toxic.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">Chromium</a> (Cr) is an element in period 4, between vanadium and manganese. Chromium is, like titanium and vanadium before it, extremely resistant to corrosion, and is indeed one of the main components of <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a>. Chromium also has many colorful compounds, and as such is very commonly used in pigments, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chrome_green" title="Chrome green">chrome green</a>.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">Manganese</a> (Mn) is an element in period 4, between chromium and iron. Manganese is often found free in nature, but is also found in combination with iron. Manganese, like chromium before it, is an important component in <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a>, preventing the iron from rusting. Manganese is also often used in pigments, again like chromium. Manganese is also poisonous; if enough is inhaled, it can cause irreversible neurological damage.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">Iron</a> (Fe) is an element in period 4, between manganese and cobalt. Iron is probably the most well-known element in period 4, being the most common element in the earth and a major component of steel. <a href="/wiki/Iron-56" title="Iron-56">Iron-56</a> has the lowest energy density of any isotope of any element, meaning that it is the most massive element that can be produced in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supergiant" title="Supergiant">supergiant</a> <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>. Iron also has some applications in the human body; <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a> is partly iron.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">Cobalt</a> (Co) is an element in period 4, between iron and nickel. Cobalt is commonly used in pigments, as many compounds of cobalt are blue in color. Cobalt is also a core component of many magnetic and high-strength alloys. The only stable isotope, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cobalt-59" title="Cobalt-59">cobalt-59</a>, is an important component of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vitamin_B-12" title="Vitamin B-12">vitamin B-12</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a>-60 is a component of nuclear fallout and can be dangerous in large enough quantities due to its radioactivity.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">Nickel</a> (Ni) is an element in period 4, between cobalt and copper. Nickel is rare in the earth's crust, mainly due to the fact that it reacts with oxygen in the air, with most of the nickel on earth coming from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel_iron_meteorite" title="Nickel iron meteorite">nickel iron meteorites</a>. However, nickel is incredibly common in the earths core; along with iron it is one of the two main components. Nickel is an important component of stainless steel, and in many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superalloys" title="Superalloys">superalloys</a>.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">Copper</a> (Cu) is an element in period 4, between nickel and zinc. Copper is one of the few metals that is not white or gray in color, the only others being <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">caesium</a>. Copper has been used by humans for thousands of years to provide a reddish tint to many objects, and is even an essential nutrient to humans, although too much is poisonous. Copper is also commonly used as a wood preservative or <a href="/wiki/Fungicide" title="Fungicide">fungicides</a>.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">Zinc</a> (Zn) is an element in period 4, between copper and gallium. Zinc is one of the main components of <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brass</a>, being used since the 10th century BCE. Zinc is also incredibly important to humans; almost 2 billion people in the world suffer from zinc deficiency. However, too much zinc can cause copper deficiency. Zinc is often used in batteries, aptly named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon-zinc_batteries" title="Carbon-zinc batteries">carbon-zinc batteries</a>, and is important in many platings, as zinc is very corrosion resistant.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gallium">Gallium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Period_4_element&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Gallium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Gallium" title="Gallium">Gallium</a> (Ga) is an element in period 4, between zinc and germanium. Gallium is noteworthy because it has a melting point at about 303 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">Kelvin</a>, right around room temperature. For example, it will be solid on a typical spring day, but will be liquid on a hot summer day. Gallium is an important component in the alloy <a href="/wiki/Galinstan" title="Galinstan">galinstan</a>, along with tin. Gallium can also be found in semiconductors.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germanium">Germanium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Period_4_element&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Germanium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">Germanium</a> (Ge) is an element in period 4, between gallium and arsenic. Germanium, like <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> above it, is an important <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> and is commonly used in diodes and transistors, often in combination with arsenic. Germanium is fairly rare on earth, leading to its comparatively late discovery. Germanium, in compounds, can sometimes irritate the eyes, skin, or lungs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arsenic">Arsenic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Period_4_element&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Arsenic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:77px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arsen_1a.jpg"><img alt="Arsen 1a.jpg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="255" data-file-width="445" height="43" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Arsen_1a.jpg/75px-Arsen_1a.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Arsen_1a.jpg/113px-Arsen_1a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Arsen_1a.jpg/150px-Arsen_1a.jpg 2x" width="75"/></a>

</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">Arsenic</a> (As) is an element in period 4, between germanium and selenium. Arsenic, as mentioned above, is often used in semiconductors in alloys with germanium. Arsenic, in pure form and some alloys, is incredibly poisonous to all multicellular life, and as such is a common component in pesticides. Arsenic was also used in some pigments before its toxicity was discovered.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Selenium">Selenium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Period_4_element&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Selenium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">Selenium</a> (Se) is an element in period 4, between arsenic and bromine. Selenium is the first nonmetal in period 4, with properties similar to <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>. Selenium is quite rare in pure form in nature, mostly being found in minerals such as <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">pyrite</a>, and even then it is quite rare. Selenium is necessary for humans in trace amounts, but is toxic in larger quantities.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bromine">Bromine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Period_4_element&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Bromine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">Bromine</a> (Br) is an element in period 4, between selenium and krypton. Bromine is a <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogen</a>, never existing in pure form in nature. Bromine is barely liquid at room temperature, boiling at about 330 Kelvin. Bromine is also quite toxic and corrosive, but bromide ions, which are relatively inert, can be found in <a href="/wiki/Halite" title="Halite">halite</a>, or table salt. Bromine is often used as a <a href="/wiki/Fire_retardant" title="Fire retardant">fire retardant</a> because many compounds can be made to release free bromine atoms.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Krypton" title="Krypton">Krypton</a> (Kr) is a <a href="/wiki/Noble_gas" title="Noble gas">noble gas</a>, placed under <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a> and over <a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">xenon</a>. Being a noble gas, krypton rarely interacts with itself or other elements; although compounds have been detected, they are all unstable and decay rapidly, and as such, krypton is often used in fluorescent lights. Krypton, like most noble gases, is also used in lighting because of its many spectral lines and the aforementioned reasons.</p>
