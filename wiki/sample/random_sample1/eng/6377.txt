<p>The <b>politics of <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a></b> occurs in a framework explained in <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Venezuela" title="Government of Venezuela">Government of Venezuela</a>.</p>
<p>Venezuela has a <a href="/wiki/Dominant-party_system" title="Dominant-party system">dominant-party system</a>, dominated by the <a href="/wiki/United_Socialist_Party_of_Venezuela" title="United Socialist Party of Venezuela">United Socialist Party of Venezuela</a> and where other numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_parties" title="Political parties">parties</a> exist. The governing <a href="/wiki/United_Socialist_Party_of_Venezuela" title="United Socialist Party of Venezuela">United Socialist Party of Venezuela</a> (<i>Partido Socialista Unido de Venezuela</i>, PSUV) was created in 2007, uniting a number of smaller parties supporting <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="Hugo Chávez">Hugo Chávez</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Bolivarian_Revolution" title="Bolivarian Revolution">Bolivarian Revolution</a> with Chávez's <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Republic_Movement" title="Fifth Republic Movement">Fifth Republic Movement</a>. PSUV and its forerunners have held the Presidency and National Assembly since 1998. The <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Unity_Roundtable" title="Democratic Unity Roundtable">Democratic Unity Roundtable</a> (<i>Mesa de la Unidad Democrática</i>, MUD), created in 2008, unites much of the opposition (<a href="/wiki/A_New_Era" title="A New Era">A New Era</a> (UNT), <a href="/wiki/Project_Venezuela" title="Project Venezuela">Project Venezuela</a>, <a href="/wiki/Justice_First" title="Justice First">Justice First</a>, <a href="/wiki/Movement_for_Socialism_(Venezuela)" title="Movement for Socialism (Venezuela)">Movement for Socialism (Venezuela)</a> and others). <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="Hugo Chávez">Hugo Chávez</a>, the central figure of the Venezuelan political landscape since <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_presidential_election,_1998" title="Venezuelan presidential election, 1998">his election to the Presidency in 1998</a> as a political outsider, died in office in early 2013, and was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Nicol%C3%A1s_Maduro" title="Nicolás Maduro">Nicolás Maduro</a> (initially as interim President, before narrowly winning the <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_presidential_election,_2013" title="Venezuelan presidential election, 2013">Venezuelan presidential election, 2013</a>). Venezuela is a presidential government.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Venezuela&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1958.E2.80.931999">1958–1999</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Venezuela&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1958–1999">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Background to the current political landscape is the development of democracy in Venezuela during the twentieth century, in which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_Action" title="Democratic Action">Democratic Action</a> (ADˣ or Acción Democrática in Spanish,founded in 1941) and its predecessors playing an important role in the early years. Democratic Action led the government during Venezuela's first democratic period (<a href="/wiki/El_Trienio_Adeco" title="El Trienio Adeco">1945–1948</a>). After an intervening decade of dictatorship (<a href="/wiki/History_of_Venezuela_(1948%E2%80%9358)" title="History of Venezuela (1948–58)">1948–1958</a>) saw ADˣ excluded from power, four <a href="/wiki/President_of_Venezuela" title="President of Venezuela">Venezuelan presidents</a> came from Democratic Action from the 1960s to the 1990s. This period, known as the "Fourth Republic", is marked by the development of the 1958 <a href="/wiki/Punto_Fijo_Pact" title="Punto Fijo Pact">Punto Fijo Pact</a> between the major parties (originally including the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republican_Union" title="Democratic Republican Union">Democratic Republican Union</a>, which later dwindled in significance), with the notable exclusion of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Venezuela" title="Communist Party of Venezuela">Communist Party of Venezuela</a>.</p>
<p>By the end of the 1990s, however, the now two-party system's credibility was almost nonexistent, mostly because of the corruption and poverty that Venezuelans experienced as oil wealth poured in during the 1970s and the debt crisis developed during the 1980s. Democratic Action's last president (<a href="/wiki/Carlos_Andr%C3%A9s_P%C3%A9rez" title="Carlos Andrés Pérez">Carlos Andrés Pérez</a>) was impeached for corruption in 1993, and spent several years in prison as a result. The other main traditional party <a href="/wiki/Copei" title="Copei">Copei</a>, provided two Venezuelan presidents (<a href="/wiki/Rafael_Caldera" title="Rafael Caldera">Rafael Caldera</a>, 1969-1974, and <a href="/wiki/Luis_Herrera_Campins" title="Luis Herrera Campins">Luis Herrera Campins</a>, 1979-1983). Confidence in the traditional parties collapsed enough that the <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_general_election,_1993" title="Venezuelan general election, 1993">1993 presidential elections</a> were won by <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Caldera" title="Rafael Caldera">Rafael Caldera</a> on around 30% of the vote, representing a new electoral coalition, <a href="/wiki/National_Convergence_(Venezuela)" title="National Convergence (Venezuela)">National Convergence</a>. By 1998, support for Democratic Action and COPEI had fallen still further, and the <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_presidential_election,_1998" title="Venezuelan presidential election, 1998">1998 election</a> was won by political outsider <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="Hugo Chávez">Hugo Chávez</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1999.E2.80.93present">1999–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Venezuela&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1999–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Venezuela_(1999%E2%80%93present)" title="History of Venezuela (1999–present)">History of Venezuela (1999–present)</a></div>
<p>Chávez launched what he called the "<a href="/wiki/Bolivarian_Revolution" title="Bolivarian Revolution">Bolivarian Revolution</a>", and fulfilled an election promise by calling a <a href="/wiki/1999_Constituent_Assembly_of_Venezuela" title="1999 Constituent Assembly of Venezuela">Constituent Assembly in 1999</a>, which drafted the new <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Venezuela" title="Constitution of Venezuela">Constitution of Venezuela</a>. Opposition attempts to unseat Chávez included the <a href="/wiki/2002_Venezuelan_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat_attempt" title="2002 Venezuelan coup d'état attempt">2002 Venezuelan coup d'état attempt</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venezuelan_general_strike_of_2002%E2%80%932003" title="Venezuelan general strike of 2002–2003">Venezuelan general strike of 2002–2003</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_recall_referendum,_2004" title="Venezuelan recall referendum, 2004">Venezuelan recall referendum, 2004</a> and a last-minute boycott of the <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_parliamentary_election,_2005" title="Venezuelan parliamentary election, 2005">2005 parliamentary elections</a>. Chávez was re-elected <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_presidential_election,_2006" title="Venezuelan presidential election, 2006">in 2006</a>, but narrowly failed to convince the electorate to approve a package of constitutional amendments aimed at deepening the Bolivarian Revolution, losing the <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_constitutional_referendum,_2007" title="Venezuelan constitutional referendum, 2007">2007 constitutional referendum</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Socialist_Party_of_Venezuela" title="United Socialist Party of Venezuela">United Socialist Party of Venezuela</a> (<i>Partido Socialista Unido de Venezuela</i>, PSUV) was created in 2007, uniting a number of smaller parties supporting <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="Hugo Chávez">Hugo Chávez</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Bolivarian_Revolution" title="Bolivarian Revolution">Bolivarian Revolution</a> with Chávez's <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Republic_Movement" title="Fifth Republic Movement">Fifth Republic Movement</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Unity_Roundtable" title="Democratic Unity Roundtable">Democratic Unity Roundtable</a> (<i>Mesa de la Unidad Democrática</i>, MUD), created in 2008, unites much of the opposition (<a href="/wiki/A_New_Era" title="A New Era">A New Era</a> (UNT), <a href="/wiki/Project_Venezuela" title="Project Venezuela">Project Venezuela</a>, <a href="/wiki/Justice_First" title="Justice First">Justice First</a>, <a href="/wiki/Movement_for_Socialism_(Venezuela)" title="Movement for Socialism (Venezuela)">Movement for Socialism (Venezuela)</a> and others). <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="Hugo Chávez">Hugo Chávez</a>, the central figure of the Venezuelan political landscape since <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_presidential_election,_1998" title="Venezuelan presidential election, 1998">his election to the Presidency in 1998</a> as a political outsider, died in office in early 2013, and was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Nicol%C3%A1s_Maduro" title="Nicolás Maduro">Nicolás Maduro</a> (initially as interim President, before narrowly winning the <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_presidential_election,_2013" title="Venezuelan presidential election, 2013">Venezuelan presidential election, 2013</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Miscellaneous">Miscellaneous</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Venezuela&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Miscellaneous">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The National Assembly has twice voted to grant Chávez the ability to <a href="/wiki/Rule_by_decree" title="Rule by decree">rule by decree</a> in several broadly defined areas, once in 2000 and again in 2007. (This power had been granted to previous administrations as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polar_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-polar-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globovision20061128_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-globovision20061128-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cidob_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-cidob-3">[3]</a></sup>)</p>
<p>In 2008, the government expelled the US-based <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Watch" title="Human Rights Watch">Human Rights Watch</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> which was criticizing the government's <a href="/wiki/Human_rights_in_Venezuela" title="Human rights in Venezuela">Human rights</a> record.</p>
<p>There is a history of tension between church and state in the country. The Catholic Church has accused Chavez of concentrating power in his own hands. In its 2009 Easter address to the nation, the bishops said the country's democracy was in "serious danger of collapse."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009 when Caracas, the capital, elected an "opposition" mayor, the government gave control of his budget to an appointed official.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Venezuela abolished the <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">death penalty</a> in 1863, making it the country where this practice has been outlawed the longest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elections">Elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Venezuela&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Elections_in_Venezuela" title="Elections in Venezuela">Elections in Venezuela</a></div>
<p>Venezuela elects at a national level the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Venezuela" title="President of Venezuela">President of Venezuela</a> as <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a> and <a href="/wiki/Head_of_government" title="Head of government">head of government</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Unicameralism" title="Unicameralism">unicameral</a> <a href="/wiki/Federalism" title="Federalism">federal</a> <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislature</a>. The <a href="/wiki/President_of_Venezuela" title="President of Venezuela">President of Venezuela</a> is elected for a six-year term by <a href="/wiki/Direct_election" title="Direct election">direct election</a> <a href="/wiki/Plurality_voting_system" title="Plurality voting system">plurality voting</a>, and is (since the <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_constitutional_referendum,_2009" title="Venezuelan constitutional referendum, 2009">Venezuelan constitutional referendum, 2009</a>) eligible for re-election. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Venezuela" title="National Assembly of Venezuela">National Assembly</a> <i>(Asamblea Nacional)</i> has 165 members <i>(diputados)</i>, elected for five-year terms. Elections also take place at <a href="/wiki/States_of_Venezuela" title="States of Venezuela">state level</a> and local level.</p>
