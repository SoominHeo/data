<p>The <b>Venetian Lagoon</b> (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><i>Laguna di Venezia</i></span>; <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">Venetian</a>: <span lang="vec" xml:lang="vec"><i>Łaguna de Venesia</i></span>) is an enclosed bay of the <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Sea" title="Adriatic Sea">Adriatic Sea</a>, in northern Italy, in which the city of <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> is situated. Its name in the <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">Venetian</a> languages, <i><span lang="vec" xml:lang="vec">Laguna Veneta</span></i>—cognate of Latin <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">lacus</span></i>, "lake"—has provided the international name for an enclosed, shallow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embayment" title="Embayment">embayment</a> of salt water, a <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">lagoon</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venetian_Lagoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The Venetian Lagoon stretches from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_Sile" title="River Sile">River Sile</a> in the north to the <a href="/wiki/Brenta_(river)" title="Brenta (river)">Brenta</a> in the south, with a surface area of around 550 square kilometres (212 square miles). It is around 8% land, including Venice itself and many smaller islands. About 11% is permanently covered by open water, or <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canal</a>, as the network of dredged channels are called, while around 80% consists of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mud_flat" title="Mud flat">mud flats</a>, tidal shallows and <a href="/wiki/Salt_marsh" title="Salt marsh">salt marshes</a>. The lagoon is the largest <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetland</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin" title="Mediterranean Basin">Mediterranean Basin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is connected to the <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Sea" title="Adriatic Sea">Adriatic Sea</a> by three <a href="/wiki/Inlet" title="Inlet">inlets</a>: <a href="/wiki/Lido_di_Venezia" title="Lido di Venezia">Lido</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malamocco" title="Malamocco">Malamocco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chioggia" title="Chioggia">Chioggia</a>. Sited at the end of a largely enclosed sea, the lagoon is subject to high variations in water level,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the most extreme being the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spring_tide" title="Spring tide">spring tides</a> known as the <i><span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acqua_Alta" title="Acqua Alta">acqua alta</a></span></i> (Italian for "high water"), which regularly flood much of Venice.</p>
<p>The nearby <a href="/wiki/Marano_lagoon" title="Marano lagoon">Marano-Grado Lagoon</a>, with a surface area of around 160 square kilometres (62 square miles), is the northernmost lagoon in the Adriatic Sea and is called sometimes the "twin sister of the Venice lagoon".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venetian_Lagoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Lagoon of Venice is the most important survivor of a system of <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuarine</a> lagoons that in Roman times extended from <a href="/wiki/Ravenna" title="Ravenna">Ravenna</a> north to <a href="/wiki/Trieste" title="Trieste">Trieste</a>. In the sixth century, the Lagoon gave security to Romanised people fleeing invaders (mostly the <a href="/wiki/Huns" title="Huns">Huns</a>). Later, it provided naturally protected conditions for the growth of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venetian_Republic" title="Venetian Republic">Venetian Republic</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Thalassocracy" title="Thalassocracy">maritime empire</a>. It still provides a base for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seaport" title="Seaport">seaport</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Venetian_Arsenal" title="Venetian Arsenal">Venetian Arsenal</a>, and for <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a>, as well as a limited amount of <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunting</a> and the newer industry of <a href="/wiki/Fish_farming" title="Fish farming">fish farming</a>.</p>
<p>The Lagoon was formed about six to seven thousand years ago, when the marine transgression following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_Age" title="Ice Age">Ice Age</a> flooded the upper Adriatic coastal plain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[a]</a></sup> Deposition of river sediments compensated for the sinking coastal plain, and coastwise drift from the mouth of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Po_River" title="Po River">Po</a> tended to close tidal inlets with sand bars.</p>
<p>The present aspect of the Lagoon is due to human intervention. In the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, Venetian hydraulic projects to prevent the lagoon from turning into a marsh reversed the natural evolution of the Lagoon. Pumping of <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a> since the nineteenth century has increased <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a>. Originally many of the Lagoon’s islands were marshy, but a gradual programme of drainage rendered them habitable. Many of the smaller islands are entirely artificial, while some areas around the seaport of the <a href="/wiki/Mestre" title="Mestre">Mestre</a> are also reclaimed islands. The remaining islands are essentially <a href="/wiki/Dune" title="Dune">dunes</a>, including those of the coastal strip (<a href="/wiki/Lido_di_Venezia" title="Lido di Venezia">Lido</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pellestrina" title="Pellestrina">Pellestrina</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treporti" title="Treporti">Treporti</a>).</p>
<p>Venice Lagoon was inhabited from the most ancient times, but it was only during and after the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a> that many people, coming from the <a href="/wiki/Veneto" title="Veneto">Venetian mainland</a>, settled in a number large enough to found the city of <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>. Today, the main cities inside the lagoon are Venice (at the centre of it) and <a href="/wiki/Chioggia" title="Chioggia">Chioggia</a> (at the southern inlet); <a href="/wiki/Lido_di_Venezia" title="Lido di Venezia">Lido di Venezia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pellestrina" title="Pellestrina">Pellestrina</a> are inhabited as well, but they are part of Venice. However, the most part of the inhabitants of Venice, as well as its economic core, its airport and its harbor, stand on the western border of the lagoon, around the former towns of <a href="/wiki/Mestre" title="Mestre">Mestre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marghera" title="Marghera">Marghera</a>. At the northern end of the lagoon, there is the town of <a href="/wiki/Jesolo" title="Jesolo">Jesolo</a>, a famous sea resort; and the town of <a href="/wiki/Cavallino-Treporti" title="Cavallino-Treporti">Cavallino-Treporti</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystem">Ecosystem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venetian_Lagoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecosystem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Occasionally, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bottlenose_Dolphin" title="Bottlenose Dolphin">Bottlenose Dolphins</a> enter the lagoon, possibly for feeding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The level of pollution in the lagoon has long been a concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> The large phytoplankton and macroalgae blooms of the late 1980s were particularly devastating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pranovi_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pranovi-8">[7]</a></sup> Recently the lagoon has also been identified as being one of the primary areas where non-indigenous species are introduced into the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Islands">Islands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venetian_Lagoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Islands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Venice Lagoon is mostly included in the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_City_of_Venice" title="Metropolitan City of Venice">Metropolitan City of Venice</a>, but the south-western area is part of the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Padua" title="Province of Padua">Province of Padua</a>.</p>
