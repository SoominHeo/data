<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Venetian glass</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Venetian glass</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> object made in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, primarily on the island of <a href="/wiki/Murano" title="Murano">Murano</a>. It is world-renowned for being colorful, elaborate, and skillfully made.</p>
<p>Many of the important characteristics of these objects had been developed by the thirteenth century. Toward the end of that century, the centre of the Venetian glass industry moved to <a href="/wiki/Murano" title="Murano">Murano</a>.</p>
<p>Byzantine craftsmen played an important role in the development of Venetian glass,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> an art form for which the city is well known. When <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> was sacked by the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Crusade" title="Fourth Crusade">Fourth Crusade</a> in 1204, some fleeing artisans came to Venice<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. This happened again when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottomans" title="Ottomans">Ottomans</a> took Constantinople in 1453, supplying Venice with still more glassworkers<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. By the sixteenth century, Venetian artisans had gained even greater control over the colour and transparency of their glass, and had mastered a variety of decorative techniques.</p>
<p>Despite efforts to keep Venetian glassmaking techniques within Venice, they became known elsewhere, and Venetian-style glassware was produced in other Italian cities and other countries of Europe.</p>
<p>Some of the most important brands of glass in the world today, including Venini,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Barovier_%26_Toso" title="Barovier &amp; Toso">Barovier &amp; Toso</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pauly_%26_C._-_Compagnia_Venezia_Murano" title="Pauly &amp; C. - Compagnia Venezia Murano">Pauly</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seguso" title="Seguso">Seguso</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> are still produced in the historical glass factories on Murano. Barovier &amp; Toso is considered to be one of the 100 <a href="/wiki/List_of_oldest_companies" title="List of oldest companies">oldest companies</a> in continuous operation in the world, having been founded in 1295.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_Murano_Glassmaking">History of Murano Glassmaking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venetian_glass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of Murano Glassmaking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Murano’s reputation as a centre for glassmaking was born when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venetian_Republic" title="Venetian Republic">Venetian Republic</a>, fearing fire and destruction to the city’s mostly wood buildings, ordered the destruction of all the foundries within the city in 1291.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Though the Republic ordered the destruction of the foundries, it authorised and encouraged construction outside the city, and by the late 13th century, the glassmaking industry was centred in Murano. Murano glass is still interwoven with Venetian glass.</p>
<p>Murano's glassmakers were soon the island’s most prominent citizens. By the 14th century, glassmakers were allowed to wear swords, enjoyed immunity from prosecution by the Venetian state and found their daughters married into Venice’s most affluent families. Of course there was a catch: Glassmakers were not allowed to leave the Republic. Many craftsmen took this risk, however, and set up glass furnaces in surrounding cities and as far afield as England and the Netherlands.</p>
<p>Murano’s glassmakers held a monopoly on quality glassmaking for centuries, developing or refining many technologies including crystalline <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, enameled glass (<i>smalto</i>), glass with threads of gold (<i>aventurine</i>), multicoloured glass (<i><a href="/wiki/Millefiori" title="Millefiori">millefiori</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Milk_glass" title="Milk glass">milk glass</a> (<i>lattimo</i>), and imitation gemstones made of glass. Today, the artisans of Murano are still employing these centuries-old techniques, crafting everything from contemporary art glass and glass jewelry to Murano glass chandeliers and wine stoppers.</p>
<p>Today, Murano is home to the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Museo_Vetrario&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Museo Vetrario (page does not exist)">Museo Vetrario</a></i> or Glass Museum in the Palazzo Giustinian, which holds displays on the history of glassmaking, as well as glass samples ranging from Egyptian times through the present day.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Art_of_Glassmaking">The Art of Glassmaking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venetian_glass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Art of Glassmaking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The process of making Murano glass is rather complex. The glass is made from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a>, which becomes liquid at high temperatures. As the glass passes from a <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a> state, there is an interval when the glass is soft before it hardens completely. This is when the glass-master can shape the material.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials">Materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venetian_glass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The other raw materials, called <a href="/wiki/Ceramic_flux" title="Ceramic flux">ceramic fluxes</a> or melting agents, allow the glass to soften at lower temperatures. The more <a href="/wiki/Sodium_oxide" title="Sodium oxide">sodium oxide</a> present in the glass, the slower it solidifies. This is important for hand-working because it allows the glassmaker more time to shape the material. The various raw materials an artisan might add to a glass mixture are <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> (to reduce melting temperature), <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> (to eliminate bubbles) and colouring or opacifying substances.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colours.2C_techniques_and_materials">Colours, techniques and materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venetian_glass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Colours, techniques and materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Venetian_Goblet_QM_r.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2560" data-file-width="1920" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Venetian_Goblet_QM_r.jpg/220px-Venetian_Goblet_QM_r.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Venetian_Goblet_QM_r.jpg/330px-Venetian_Goblet_QM_r.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Venetian_Goblet_QM_r.jpg/440px-Venetian_Goblet_QM_r.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Venetian_Goblet_QM_r.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Venetian artisans use special tools including: borselle (tongs to hand-form the red-hot glass); canna da soffio (a blowing pipe); pontello (an iron rod that helps with adding the finishing touches); scagno (a work bench); and tagianti (glass-cutting clippers).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Colours, techniques and materials vary depending upon the look a glassmaker is trying to achieve. Aquamarine is created through the use of copper and cobalt compounds, whereas ruby red uses a gold solution as a colouring agent. <a href="/wiki/Murrine" title="Murrine">Murrine</a> technique begins with the layering of coloured liquid glass, which is then stretched into long rods called canes (<a href="/wiki/Caneworking" title="Caneworking">caneworking</a>). When cold, these canes are then sliced in cross-section, which has the layered pattern. The better-known term <i>millefiori</i> is a style of murrine that is defined by each layer of molten colour being shaped by a mold into a star, then cooled and layered again. When sliced, this type of murrine has many points, thus mille (thousand) fiori (flowers). <a href="/wiki/Filigree" title="Filigree">Filigree</a> (a type of <a href="/wiki/Caneworking" title="Caneworking">caneworking</a>), <i>incalmo</i>, <a href="/wiki/Vitreous_enamel" title="Vitreous enamel">enamel painted</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glass_engraving" title="Glass engraving">glass engraving</a>, gold engraving, <i>lattimo</i>, ribbed glass and submersion are just a few of the other techniques a glassmaker can employ.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tools">Tools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venetian_glass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Murano artisans use specialised tools in the making of their glass. Some of these tools include <i>borselle</i> (tongs or pliers used to hand-form the red-hot glass), <i>canna da soffio</i> (blowing pipe), <i>pontello</i> (an iron rod to which the craftsman attaches the glass after blowing to add final touches), <i>scagno</i> (the glass-master's work bench) and <i>tagianti</i> (large glass-cutting clippers).</p>
