<p>In <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a>, a <b>4-polytope</b> (sometimes also called a <b>polychoron</b>, <b>polycell</b>, or <b>polyhedroid</b>) is a four-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Polytope" title="Polytope">polytope</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It is a connected and closed figure, composed of lower-dimensional polytopal elements: <a href="/wiki/Vertex_(geometry)" title="Vertex (geometry)">vertices</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edge_(geometry)" title="Edge (geometry)">edges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Face_(geometry)" title="Face (geometry)">faces</a> (<a href="/wiki/Polygon" title="Polygon">polygons</a>), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cell_(mathematics)" title="Cell (mathematics)">cells</a> (<a href="/wiki/Polyhedron" title="Polyhedron">polyhedra</a>). Each face is shared by exactly two cells.</p>
<p>The two-dimensional analogue of a 4-polytope is a <a href="/wiki/Polygon" title="Polygon">polygon</a>, and the three-dimensional analogue is a <a href="/wiki/Polyhedron" title="Polyhedron">polyhedron</a>.</p>
<p>Topologically 4-polytopes are closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Convex_uniform_honeycomb" title="Convex uniform honeycomb">uniform honeycombs</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Cubic_honeycomb" title="Cubic honeycomb">cubic honeycomb</a>, which tessellate 3-space; similarly the 3D <a href="/wiki/Cube" title="Cube">cube</a> is related to the infinite 2D <a href="/wiki/Square_tiling" title="Square tiling">square tiling</a>. Convex 4-polytopes can be <i>cut and unfolded</i> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyhedral_net" title="Polyhedral net">nets</a> in 3-space.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-polytope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A 4-polytope is a closed <a href="/wiki/Four-dimensional_space" title="Four-dimensional space">four-dimensional</a> figure. It comprises <a href="/wiki/Vertex_(geometry)" title="Vertex (geometry)">vertices</a> (corner points), <a href="/wiki/Edge_(geometry)" title="Edge (geometry)">edges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Face_(geometry)" title="Face (geometry)">faces</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cell_(mathematics)" title="Cell (mathematics)">cells</a>. A cell is the three-dimensional analogue of a face, and is therefore a <a href="/wiki/Polyhedron" title="Polyhedron">polyhedron</a>. Each face must join exactly two cells, analogous to the way in which each edge of a polyhedron joins just two faces. Like any polytope, the elements of a 4-polytope cannot be subdivided into two or more sets which are also 4-polytopes, i.e. it is not a compound.</p>
<p>The most familiar 4-polytope is the <a href="/wiki/Tesseract" title="Tesseract">tesseract</a> or hypercube, the 4D analogue of the cube.</p>

<p>4-polytopes cannot be seen in three-dimensional space due to their extra dimension. Several techniques are used to help visualise them.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Orthogonal projection</dt>
</dl>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthogonal_projection" title="Orthogonal projection">Orthogonal projections</a> can be used to show various symmetry orientations of a 4-polytope. They can be drawn in 2D as vertex-edge graphs, and can be shown in 3D with solid faces as visible <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Projective_envelope" title="Projective envelope">projective envelopes</a>.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Perspective projection</dt>
</dl>
<p>Just as a 3D shape can be projected onto a flat sheet, so a 4-D shape can be projected onto 3-space or even onto a flat sheet. One common projection is a <a href="/wiki/Schlegel_diagram" title="Schlegel diagram">Schlegel diagram</a> which uses <a href="/wiki/Stereographic_projection" title="Stereographic projection">stereographic projection</a> of points on the surface of a <a href="/wiki/3-sphere" title="3-sphere">3-sphere</a> into three dimensions, connected by straight edges, faces, and cells drawn in 3-space.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Sectioning</dt>
</dl>
<p>Just as a slice through a polyhedron reveals a cut surface, so a slice through a 4-polytope reveals a cut "hypersurface" in three dimensions. A sequence of such sections can be used to build up an understanding of the overall shape. The extra dimension can be equated with time to produce a smooth animation of these cross sections.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Nets</dt>
</dl>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Net_(polytope)" title="Net (polytope)">net</a> of a 4-polytope is composed of polyhedral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cell_(geometry)" title="Cell (geometry)">cells</a> that are connected by their faces and all occupy the same three-dimensional space, just as the polygon faces of a <a href="/wiki/Net_(polyhedron)" title="Net (polyhedron)">net of a polyhedron</a> are connected by their edges and all occupy the same plane.</p>

<p>The topology of any given 4-polytope is defined by its <a href="/wiki/Betti_number" title="Betti number">Betti numbers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torsion_coefficient_(topology)" title="Torsion coefficient (topology)">torsion coefficients</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-richeson_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-richeson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The value of the <a href="/wiki/Euler_characteristic" title="Euler characteristic">Euler characteristic</a> used to characterise polyhedra does not generalize usefully to higher dimensions, and is zero for all 4-polytopes, whatever their underlying topology. This inadequacy of the Euler characteristic to reliably distinguish between different topologies in higher dimensions led to the discovery of the more sophisticated Betti numbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-richeson_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-richeson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Similarly, the notion of orientability of a polyhedron is insufficient to characterise the surface twistings of toroidal 4-polytopes, and this led to the use of torsion coefficients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-richeson_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-richeson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-polytope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criteria">Criteria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=4-polytope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Criteria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like all polytopes, 4-polytopes may be classified based on properties like "<a href="/wiki/Convex_set" title="Convex set">convexity</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Symmetry" title="Symmetry">symmetry</a>".</p>
<ul>
<li>A 4-polytope is <i><a href="/wiki/Convex_polytope" title="Convex polytope">convex</a></i> if its boundary (including its cells, faces and edges) does not intersect itself and the line segment joining any two points of the 4-polytope is contained in the 4-polytope or its interior; otherwise, it is <i>non-convex</i>. Self-intersecting 4-polytopes are also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_4-polytope" title="Star 4-polytope">star 4-polytopes</a>, from analogy with the star-like shapes of the non-convex <a href="/wiki/Star_polygon" title="Star polygon">star polygons</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kepler%E2%80%93Poinsot_polyhedra" title="Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra">Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra</a>.</li>
<li>A 4-polytope is <i><a href="/wiki/Regular_polytope" title="Regular polytope">regular</a></i> if it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transitive_group_action" title="Transitive group action">transitive</a> on its <a href="/wiki/Flag_(geometry)" title="Flag (geometry)">flags</a>. This means that its cells are all <a href="/wiki/Congruence_(geometry)" title="Congruence (geometry)">congruent</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regular_polyhedra" title="Regular polyhedra">regular polyhedra</a>, and similarly its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertex_figures" title="Vertex figures">vertex figures</a> are congruent and of another kind of regular polyhedron.</li>
<li>A convex 4-polytope is <i><a href="/wiki/Semiregular_polytope" title="Semiregular polytope">semi-regular</a></i> if it has a <a href="/wiki/Symmetry_group" title="Symmetry group">symmetry group</a> under which all vertices are equivalent (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertex-transitive" title="Vertex-transitive">vertex-transitive</a>) and its cells are <a href="/wiki/Regular_polyhedron" title="Regular polyhedron">regular polyhedra</a>. The cells may be of two or more kinds, provided that they have the same kind of face. There are only 3 cases identified by <a href="/wiki/Thorold_Gosset" title="Thorold Gosset">Thorold Gosset</a> in 1900: the <a href="/wiki/Rectified_5-cell" title="Rectified 5-cell">rectified 5-cell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rectified_600-cell" title="Rectified 600-cell">rectified 600-cell</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Snub_24-cell" title="Snub 24-cell">snub 24-cell</a>.</li>
<li>A 4-polytope is <i><a href="/wiki/Uniform_polytope" title="Uniform polytope">uniform</a></i> if it has a <a href="/wiki/Symmetry_group" title="Symmetry group">symmetry group</a> under which all vertices are equivalent, and its cells are <a href="/wiki/Uniform_polyhedron" title="Uniform polyhedron">uniform polyhedra</a>. The faces of a uniform 4-polytope must be <a href="/wiki/Regular_polygon" title="Regular polygon">regular</a>.</li>
<li>A 4-polytope is <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scaliform_polytope" title="Scaliform polytope">scaliform</a></i> if it is vertex-transitive, and has all equal length edges. This allows cells which are not uniform, such as the regular-faced convex <a href="/wiki/Johnson_solid" title="Johnson solid">Johnson solids</a>.</li>
<li>A regular 4-polytope which is also <a href="/wiki/Convex_polytope" title="Convex polytope">convex</a> is said to be a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convex_regular_4-polytope" title="Convex regular 4-polytope">convex regular 4-polytope</a>.</li>
<li>A 4-polytope is <i>prismatic</i> if it is the <a href="/wiki/Cartesian_product" title="Cartesian product">Cartesian product</a> of two or more lower-dimensional polytopes. A prismatic 4-polytope is uniform if its factors are uniform. The <a href="/wiki/Tesseract" title="Tesseract">hypercube</a> is prismatic (product of two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square_(geometry)" title="Square (geometry)">squares</a>, or of a <a href="/wiki/Cube" title="Cube">cube</a> and <a href="/wiki/Line_segment" title="Line segment">line segment</a>), but is considered separately because it has symmetries other than those inherited from its factors.</li>
<li>A <i><a href="/wiki/Tessellation" title="Tessellation">tiling</a> or <a href="/wiki/Honeycomb_(geometry)" title="Honeycomb (geometry)">honeycomb</a> of 3-space</i> is the division of three-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_space" title="Euclidean space">Euclidean space</a> into a repetitive <a href="/wiki/Grid_(spatial_index)" title="Grid (spatial index)">grid</a> of polyhedral cells. Such tilings or tessellations are infinite and do not bound a "4D" volume, and are examples of infinite 4-polytopes. A <i>uniform tiling of 3-space</i> is one whose vertices are congruent and related by a <a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> and whose cells are <a href="/wiki/Uniform_polyhedron" title="Uniform polyhedron">uniform polyhedra</a>.</li>
</ul>

<li><a href="/wiki/Regular_4-polytope" title="Regular 4-polytope">Regular 4-polytope</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/3-sphere" title="3-sphere">3-sphere</a> (or glome) is another commonly discussed figure that resides in 4-dimensional space. This is not a 4-polytope, since it is not bounded by polyhedral cells.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Duocylinder" title="Duocylinder">duocylinder</a> is a figure in 4-dimensional space related to the <a href="/wiki/Duoprism" title="Duoprism">duoprisms</a>. It is also not a 4-polytope because its bounding volumes are not polyhedral.</li>
