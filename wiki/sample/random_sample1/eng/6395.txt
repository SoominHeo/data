<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Bernard VII, Count of Armagnac</h1>

<p><b>Bernard VII, Count of Armagnac</b> (1360 – 12 June 1418) was <a href="/wiki/Count_of_Armagnac" title="Count of Armagnac">Count of Armagnac</a> and <a href="/wiki/Constable_of_France" title="Constable of France">Constable of France</a>. He was the son of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_II_of_Armagnac" title="John II of Armagnac">John II</a> and Jeanne de Périgord. He succeeded in Armagnac at the death of his brother, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_III_of_Armagnac" title="John III of Armagnac">John III</a>, in 1391. After prolonged fighting, he also became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Count_of_Comminges" title="Count of Comminges">Count of Comminges</a> in 1412.</p>
<p>When his brother, who claimed the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Majorca" title="Kingdom of Majorca">Kingdom of Majorca</a>, invaded northern <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">Catalonia</a> late in 1389 in an attempt to seize the kingdom's continental possessions (the <a href="/wiki/County_of_Roussillon" title="County of Roussillon">County of Roussillon</a>), Bernard commanded part of his forces.</p>
<p>Bernard's wife was <a href="/wiki/Bonne_of_Berry" title="Bonne of Berry">Bonne</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> the daughter of <a href="/wiki/John,_Duke_of_Berry" title="John, Duke of Berry">John, Duke of Berry</a>, and widow of Count <a href="/wiki/Amadeus_VII,_Count_of_Savoy" title="Amadeus VII, Count of Savoy">Amadeus VII, Count of Savoy</a>. He first gained influence at the French court when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_of_Valois,_Duke_of_Orl%C3%A9ans" title="Louis of Valois, Duke of Orléans">Louis, Duke of Orléans</a> married <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valentina_Visconti" title="Valentina Visconti">Valentina Visconti</a>, the daughter of <a href="/wiki/Gian_Galeazzo_Visconti" title="Gian Galeazzo Visconti">Gian Galeazzo Visconti</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duke_of_Milan" title="Duke of Milan">Duke of Milan</a>. Bernard's sister Beatrice married Valentina's brother Carlo.</p>
<p>After Louis' <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_Louis_I,_Duke_of_Orl%C3%A9ans" title="Assassination of Louis I, Duke of Orléans">assassination</a> in 1407, Armagnac remained attached to the cause of Orléans. He married his daughter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonne_d%27Armagnac" title="Bonne d'Armagnac">Bonne</a> to the young <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Duke_of_Orl%C3%A9ans" title="Charles, Duke of Orléans">Charles, Duke of Orléans</a> in 1410.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Bernard d'Armagnac became the nominal head of the faction which opposed <a href="/wiki/John_the_Fearless" title="John the Fearless">John the Fearless</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Armagnac%E2%80%93Burgundian_Civil_War" title="Armagnac–Burgundian Civil War">Armagnac–Burgundian Civil War</a>, and the faction came to be called the "Armagnacs" as a consequence.</p>
<p>He became constable of France in 1415 and was the head of the government of the <a href="/wiki/Dauphin_of_France" title="Dauphin of France">Dauphin</a>, the future <a href="/wiki/Charles_VII_of_France" title="Charles VII of France">Charles VII</a>, until the Burgundians invaded Paris in the night of 28-29 May 1418. On 12 June 1418, he was one of the first victims of the massacres in which over 550 of his real or suspected followers were killed in the course of weeks throughout the summer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
