<p><b>Bernard Jean Étienne Arnault</b> (<small>French: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[bɛʁnaːʁ aʁno]</a></span>; born 5 March 1949) is a French business magnate, an investor, and an art collector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Arnault is the <a href="/wiki/Chairman" title="Chairman">Chairman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">Chief Executive Officer (CEO)</a> of <a href="/wiki/LVMH" title="LVMH">LVMH</a>. He is the richest man in France, and the 12th richest person in the world. In November 2016, his wealth was estimated to be US$37.3 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bernard_Arnault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After graduating from the Maxence Van Der Meersch High School in <a href="/wiki/Roubaix" title="Roubaix">Roubaix</a>, Arnault was admitted to <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_Polytechnique" title="École Polytechnique">École Polytechnique</a>, from which he graduated with an Engineering degree in 1971.</p>
<p>His father, Jean Leon Arnault, a graduate of <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_Centrale_Paris" title="École Centrale Paris">École Centrale Paris</a>, was a manufacturer and the owner of the <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a> company, <i>Ferret-Savinel</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bernard_Arnault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="F.C3.A9rinel">Férinel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bernard_Arnault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Férinel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After graduation, Arnault joined his father's company in 1971. In 1976, he convinced his father to liquidate the construction division of the company for 40 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_francs" title="French francs">French francs</a>, and to change the focus of the company to real estate. Using the name Férinel, the new company developed a specialty in <a href="/wiki/Lodging" title="Lodging">holiday accommodation</a>. Named Director of Company Development in 1974, he became the CEO in 1977. In 1979, he succeeded his father as President of the company.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christian_Dior">Christian Dior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bernard_Arnault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Christian Dior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1984, with the help of Antoine Bernheim, a Senior Partner of Lazard Frères et Cie., Arnault acquired the Financière Agache, a <a href="/wiki/Luxury_goods" title="Luxury goods">luxury goods</a> company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He became the CEO of Financière Agache, and subsequently took control of Boussac, a textile company in turmoil. Boussac owned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_Dior_S.A." title="Christian Dior S.A.">Christian Dior</a>, the department store <a href="/wiki/Le_Bon_March%C3%A9" title="Le Bon Marché">Le Bon Marché</a>, the retail shop Conforama and the diapers industrial Peaudouce. He sold nearly all the company's assets, keeping only the prestigious Christian Dior brand, and Le Bon Marché department store.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="LVMH">LVMH</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bernard_Arnault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: LVMH">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1987, shortly after the creation of LVMH, the new luxury group resulting from the merger between two companies, Arnault mediated a conflict between Alain Chevalier, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mo%C3%ABt_Hennessy" title="Moët Hennessy">Moët Hennessy</a>'s CEO, and Henri Racamier, president of Louis Vuitton. The new group held property rights to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dior" title="Dior">Dior</a> perfumes, which Arnault believed should be incorporated into Dior Couture.</p>
<p>In July 1988, Arnault provided $1.5 billion to form a holding company with <a href="/wiki/Guinness" title="Guinness">Guinness</a> that held 24% of LVMH's shares. In response to rumors that the Louis Vuitton group was buying LVMH's stock to form a "blocking minority", Arnault spent $600 million to buy 13.5% more of LVMH, making him LVMH's first shareholder. In January 1989, Bernard Arnault spent another $500 million to gain control a total of 43.5% of LVMH, and 35% of voting rights, thus reaching the "blocking minority" he needed to stop the dismantlement of the LVMH group. On 13 January 1989, he was unanimously elected chairman of the executive management board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since then, Arnault led the company through an ambitious development plan, transforming it into one of the largest luxury groups in the world, alongside Swiss luxury giant <a href="/wiki/Richemont" title="Richemont">Richemont</a> and French-based <a href="/wiki/Kering" title="Kering">Kering</a>. In eleven years, the <a href="/wiki/Market_capitalization" title="Market capitalization">market value</a> of LVMH has multiplied by at least fifteen, while, simultaneously, the sales and profit rose by 500%. He promoted decisions towards decentralizing the group's brands. As a result of these measures, the brands are now viewed as independent firms with their own history.</p>
<p>Arnault professional decisions support the idea that LVMH has "shared advantages": the strongest brands help finance those that are still developing. The portfolio of major luxury brands has a history of stability, and thus its solidity allows for new acquisitions and group development. It is because of this strategy that Christian Lacroix could open his own fashion house.</p>
<p>In July 1988, Arnault acquired <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9line_(brand)" title="Céline (brand)">Céline</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1993, LVMH acquired <a href="/wiki/Berluti" title="Berluti">Berluti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kenzo_(brand)" title="Kenzo (brand)">Kenzo</a>. In the same year, Arnault bought out the French economic newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/La_Tribune" title="La Tribune">La Tribune</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The company never achieved the desired success, despite his 150 million euro investment, and he sold it in November 2007 in order to buy a different French economic newspaper <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Les_%C3%89chos_(France)" title="Les Échos (France)">Les Échos</a></i>, for 240 million Euros.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1994, LVMH acquired the perfume firm <a href="/wiki/Guerlain" title="Guerlain">Guerlain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1996, Arnault bought out <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loewe,_S.A." title="Loewe, S.A.">Loewe</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> followed by <a href="/wiki/Marc_Jacobs" title="Marc Jacobs">Marc Jacobs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sephora" title="Sephora">Sephora</a> in 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> These brands were also integrated into the group: <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Pink" title="Thomas Pink">Thomas Pink</a> in 1999, <a href="/wiki/Emilio_Pucci" title="Emilio Pucci">Emilio Pucci</a> in 2000 and <a href="/wiki/Fendi" title="Fendi">Fendi</a>, <a href="/wiki/DKNY" title="DKNY">DKNY</a> and <a href="/wiki/La_Samaritaine" title="La Samaritaine">La Samaritaine</a> in 2001.</p>
<p>In the 1990s, Arnault decided to develop a centre in New York to manage LVMH's presence in the United States. He chose <a href="/wiki/Christian_de_Portzamparc" title="Christian de Portzamparc">Christian de Portzamparc</a> to supervise this project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The result was the <a href="/wiki/LVMH_Tower" title="LVMH Tower">LVMH Tower</a>, which opened in December 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_investments">Other investments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bernard_Arnault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other investments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1998 to 2001, Arnault invested in a variety of web companies: <a href="/wiki/Boo.com" title="Boo.com">Boo.com</a>, Libertysurf and Zebank through his holding Europatweb. Groupe Arnault also invested in <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a> in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, his company Blue Capital, that Arnault owns jointly with the California property firm Colony Capital, acquired 10.69% of France's largest supermarket retailer and the world's second-largest food distributor, Carrefour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, he entered the yacht business and bought <a href="/wiki/Princess_Yachts" title="Princess Yachts">Princess Yachts</a> for 253 million Euros.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> He subsequently took control of <a href="/wiki/Feadship" title="Feadship">Royal van Lent</a> for an almost identical sum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_collector">Art collector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bernard_Arnault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Art collector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Arnault is a noted art collector, and is known for his contemporary collection that includes pieces by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Picasso" title="Picasso">Picasso</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yves_Klein" title="Yves Klein">Yves Klein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Moore" title="Henry Moore">Henry Moore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andy_Warhol" title="Andy Warhol">Andy Warhol</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> He was also instrumental in establishing <a href="/wiki/LVMH" title="LVMH">LVMH</a> as a major patron of Art in France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The LVMH group created The "Young Fashion Designer" Prize. It is an international competition which is open to students from Fine arts schools. Every year, the winner is awarded with a grant to support the creation of his label and with a year of mentorship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1999 to 2003, he owned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phillips_de_Pury_%26_Company" title="Phillips de Pury &amp; Company">Phillips de Pury &amp; Company</a>, an art <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auction_house" title="Auction house">auction house</a>, and bought out the first French auctioneer, Tajan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, Arnault started the building project of the <a href="/wiki/Louis_Vuitton_Foundation" title="Louis Vuitton Foundation">Louis Vuitton Foundation</a>. Dedicated to creation and contemporary art, the building was designed by the architect <a href="/wiki/Frank_Gehry" title="Frank Gehry">Frank Gehry</a>, who also designed the <a href="/wiki/Guggenheim_Museum_Bilbao" title="Guggenheim Museum Bilbao">Guggenheim Museum Bilbao</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The Foundation's grand opening at the <a href="/wiki/Jardin_d%27Acclimatation" title="Jardin d'Acclimatation">Jardin d'Acclimatation</a> was held on 20 October 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bernard_Arnault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Arnault has been married twice, and has a daughter and four sons. He was married to Anne Dewavrin from 1973 to 1990, and the couple have a daughter <a href="/wiki/Delphine_Arnault" title="Delphine Arnault">Delphine</a> and a son <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Arnault" title="Antoine Arnault">Antoine</a>. Arnault married his second wife, Hélène Mercier, a Canadian pianist from <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, in 1991. The couple have three sons, Alexandre, Frédéric, and Jean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-THP_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-THP-35">[35]</a></sup>}}</p>
<p>Arnault was a witness at President <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Sarkozy" title="Nicolas Sarkozy">Nicolas Sarkozy</a>'s wedding to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%A9cilia_Ciganer-Alb%C3%A9niz" title="Cécilia Ciganer-Albéniz">Cécilia Ciganer-Albéniz</a>.</p>
<p>Arnault owned the 70 m (230 ft) converted research vessel <i>Amadaeus</i>, which was sold in late 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> His current 101.5 m (333 ft) yacht <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Symphony_(yacht)" title="Symphony (yacht)"><i>Symphony</i></a> was built in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> by <a href="/wiki/Feadship" title="Feadship">Feadship</a>, delivered in mid-2015.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Request_for_Belgian_nationality">Request for Belgian nationality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bernard_Arnault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Request for Belgian nationality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It was disclosed that Arnault was planning to apply for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belgian_citizenship" title="Belgian citizenship">Belgian citizenship</a> and was considering moving to <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In April 2013, Arnault said that he had been misquoted and that had never had the intention to leave France : "I repeatedly said that I would stay resident in France and that I would continue to pay my taxes. In vain: the message is not passed. Today, I decided to remove any ambiguity. I withdraw my request of Belgian nationality. Request Belgian nationality was to better protect the foundation that I created, with the sole purpose of ensuring the continuity and integrity of the LVMH group if I were to disappear".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> On 10 April 2013, Arnault announced he had decided to abandon his application for Belgian citizenship, saying he did not want the move to be misinterpreted as a measure of tax evasion at a time when France faced economic and social challenges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Arnault also stated that several employees requested to leave France for tax purposes but he declined their request, and that "the 75-percent tax would not raise a lot of revenue but should prove less divisive now that it was set to be levied on firms rather than people and only due to stay in place for two years."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
