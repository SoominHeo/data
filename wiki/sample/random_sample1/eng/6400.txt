<p><b>Berbérati</b> is the third-largest city in the <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Republic" title="Central African Republic">Central African Republic</a>, with a population of 76,918 (2003 census). Located in the south-west of the country near the border with <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a>, it serves as capital of the <a href="/wiki/Mamb%C3%A9r%C3%A9-Kad%C3%A9%C3%AF" title="Mambéré-Kadéï">Mambéré-Kadéï</a> <a href="/wiki/Prefectures_of_the_Central_African_Republic" title="Prefectures of the Central African Republic">Prefecture</a>.</p>
<p>Its <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">cathedral</a> is the episcopal see of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Berb%C3%A9rati" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Berbérati">Roman Catholic Diocese of Berbérati</a>. The city also has the diocese's former cathedral Sainte Anne, devoted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Anna" title="Saint Anna">Saint Anna</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berb%C3%A9rati&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early 20th century Berbérati was part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oubangui-Chari" title="Oubangui-Chari">Oubangui-Chari</a>, one of the four territories comprising <a href="/wiki/French_Equatorial_Africa" title="French Equatorial Africa">French Equatorial Africa</a> which later became Central African Republic. In 1911 it was ceded to <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a> under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Morocco%E2%80%93Congo_Treaty" title="Morocco–Congo Treaty">Morocco–Congo Treaty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Fez" title="Treaty of Fez">Treaty of Fez</a>, becoming part of the German colony of <a href="/wiki/Neukamerun" title="Neukamerun">Neukamerun</a>, until it was reconquered by the French in 1916 following the defeat of German forces in western Africa during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilties">Facilties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berb%C3%A9rati&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Facilties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The state-owned <a href="/wiki/Teaching_hospital" title="Teaching hospital">university hospital</a> of Berbérati is an unfenced complex of several bungalows near the town center. The hospital was constructed in the 1950s and operated by French military doctors until the 1980s. The French hospital administrators were succeed by an expatriate Italian Catholic nun, although the hospital also receives Protestant support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Berbérati is served by the <a href="/wiki/Berb%C3%A9rati_Airport" title="Berbérati Airport">Berbérati Airport</a> and is often visited by tourists on their way to the <a href="/wiki/Dzanga-Sangha_Special_Reserve" title="Dzanga-Sangha Special Reserve">Dzanga-Sangha Special Reserve</a>. It is also home of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Berb%C3%A9rati" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Berbérati">Roman Catholic Diocese of Berbérati</a>.</p>
