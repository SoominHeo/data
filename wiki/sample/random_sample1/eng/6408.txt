<p><b>Berthe Marie Pauline Morisot</b> (<small>French: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[mɔʁizo]</a></span>; January 14, 1841 – March 2, 1895) was a <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painter</a> and a member of the circle of painters in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> who became known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impressionists" title="Impressionists">Impressionists</a>. She was described by <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Geffroy" title="Gustave Geffroy">Gustave Geffroy</a> in 1894 as one of "les trois grandes dames" of Impressionism alongside <a href="/wiki/Marie_Bracquemond" title="Marie Bracquemond">Marie Bracquemond</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mary_Cassatt" title="Mary Cassatt">Mary Cassatt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1864, she exhibited for the first time in the highly esteemed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris_Salon" title="Paris Salon">Salon de Paris</a>. Sponsored by the government, and judged by <a href="/wiki/Academician" title="Academician">Academicians</a>, the Salon was the official, annual <a href="/wiki/Art_exhibition" title="Art exhibition">exhibition</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_des_beaux-arts" title="Académie des beaux-arts">Académie des beaux-arts</a> in Paris. Her work was selected for exhibition in six subsequent Salons<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> until, in 1874, she joined the <i>"rejected"</i> Impressionists in the first of their own exhibitions, which included <a href="/wiki/Paul_C%C3%A9zanne" title="Paul Cézanne">Paul Cézanne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Degas" title="Edgar Degas">Edgar Degas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Claude_Monet" title="Claude Monet">Claude Monet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camille_Pissarro" title="Camille Pissarro">Camille Pissarro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Auguste_Renoir" title="Pierre-Auguste Renoir">Pierre-Auguste Renoir</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Sisley" title="Alfred Sisley">Alfred Sisley</a>. It was held at the <a href="/wiki/Studio" title="Studio">studio</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Photographer" title="Photographer">photographer</a> <a href="/wiki/Nadar_(photographer)" title="Nadar (photographer)">Nadar</a>.</p>
<p>She was married to <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Manet" title="Eugène Manet">Eugène Manet</a>, the brother of her friend and colleague <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Manet" title="Édouard Manet">Édouard Manet</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berthe_Morisot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Mother_and_Sister_of_the_Artist.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1607" data-file-width="1278" height="314" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/The_Mother_and_Sister_of_the_Artist.JPG/250px-The_Mother_and_Sister_of_the_Artist.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/The_Mother_and_Sister_of_the_Artist.JPG/375px-The_Mother_and_Sister_of_the_Artist.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/The_Mother_and_Sister_of_the_Artist.JPG/500px-The_Mother_and_Sister_of_the_Artist.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Mother_and_Sister_of_the_Artist.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Berthe Morisot, <i>Portrait de Mme Morisot et de sa fille Mme Pontillon ou La lecture</i> (The Mother and Sister of the Artist - Marie-Joséphine &amp; Edma) 1869/70</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Morisot was born in <a href="/wiki/Bourges" title="Bourges">Bourges</a>, France, into an affluent bourgeois family. Her father, Edmé Tiburce Morisot, was the <a href="/wiki/Prefect" title="Prefect">prefect</a> (senior administrator) of the <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_France" title="Departments of France">department</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cher_(d%C3%A9partement)" title="Cher (département)">Cher</a>. Her mother, Marie-Joséphine-Cornélie Thomas, was the great-niece of <a href="/wiki/Jean-Honor%C3%A9_Fragonard" title="Jean-Honoré Fragonard">Jean-Honoré Fragonard</a>, one of the most prolific <a href="/wiki/Rococo" title="Rococo">Rococo</a> painters of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancien_r%C3%A9gime" title="Ancien régime">ancien régime</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> She had two older sisters, Yves (1838–1893) and Edma (1839–1921), plus a younger brother, Tiburce, born in 1848. The family moved to Paris in 1852, when Morisot was a child.</p>
<p>It was common practice for daughters of bourgeois families to receive art education, so Berthe and her sisters Yves and Edma were taught privately by Geoffroy-Alphonse Chocarne and <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Guichard" title="fr:Joseph Guichard">Joseph Guichard</a>. In 1857 Guichard introduced Berthe and Edma to the <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">Louvre</a> gallery where they could learn by looking, and from 1858 they learned by copying paintings. He also introduced them to the works of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gavarni" title="Gavarni">Gavarni</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Higonet_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Higonet-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>As art students, Berthe and Edma worked closely together until Edma married Adolphe Pontillon, a naval officer, moved to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cherbourg" title="Cherbourg">Cherbourg</a>, had children, and had less time to paint. Letters between the sisters show a loving relationship, underscored by Berthe's regret at the distance between them and Edma's withdrawal from painting. Edma wholeheartedly supported Berthe's continued work and their families always remained close. Edma wrote <i>“… I am often with you in thought, dear Berthe. I’m in your studio and I like to slip away, if only for a quarter of an hour, to breathe that atmosphere that we shared for many years…”</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Met_Museum_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Met_Museum-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSL87_16_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-SSL87_16-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Women_in_the_Act_of_Painting_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Women_in_the_Act_of_Painting-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Her sister Yves married Theodore Gobillard, a tax inspector, in 1866, and was painted by <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Degas" title="Edgar Degas">Edgar Degas</a> as <i>Mrs Theodore Gobillard</i> (<a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Met_Museum_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Met_Museum-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSL87_16_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-SSL87_16-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Higonnet_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Higonnet-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Morisot registered as a <a href="/wiki/Copyist" title="Copyist">copyist</a> at the Louvre where she befriended other artists and teachers including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camille_Corot" title="Camille Corot">Camille Corot</a>, the pivotal landscape painter of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barbizon_School" title="Barbizon School">Barbizon School</a> who also excelled in figure painting. In 1860, under Corot's influence she took up the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plein_air" title="Plein air">plein air</a> (outdoors) method of working. By 1863 she was studying under <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achille_Oudinot" title="fr:Achille Oudinot">Achille Oudinot</a>, another Barbizon painter. In the winter of 1863–64 she studied sculpture under <a href="/wiki/Aim%C3%A9_Millet" title="Aimé Millet">Aimé Millet</a>, but none of her sculpture is known to survive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Higonet_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Higonet-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>Morisot's first appearance in the <a href="/wiki/Salon_(Paris)" title="Salon (Paris)">Salon de Paris</a> came at the age of twenty-three in 1864, with the acceptance of two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landscape_art" title="Landscape art">landscape</a> paintings. She continued to show regularly in the Salon, to generally favorable reviews, until 1873, the year before the first Impressionist exhibition. She exhibited with the Impressionists from 1874 onwards, only missing the exhibition in 1878 when her daughter was born.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Morisot's mature career began in 1872. She found an audience for her work with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Durand-Ruel" title="Durand-Ruel">Durand-Ruel</a>, the private dealer, who bought twenty-two paintings. In 1877, she was described by the critic for <i>Le Temps</i> as the "one real Impressionist in this group."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> She chose to exhibit under her full maiden name instead of using a pseudonym or her married name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In the 1880 exhibition, many reviews judged Morisot among the best, including <i>Le Figaro</i> critic <a href="/wiki/Albert_Wolff_(journalist)" title="Albert Wolff (journalist)">Albert Wolff</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Morisot_and_the_Manets">Morisot and the Manets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berthe_Morisot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Morisot and the Manets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Edouard_Manet_-_Berthe_Morisot_With_a_Bouquet_of_Violets_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3552" data-file-width="2617" height="299" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Edouard_Manet_-_Berthe_Morisot_With_a_Bouquet_of_Violets_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/220px-Edouard_Manet_-_Berthe_Morisot_With_a_Bouquet_of_Violets_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Edouard_Manet_-_Berthe_Morisot_With_a_Bouquet_of_Violets_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/330px-Edouard_Manet_-_Berthe_Morisot_With_a_Bouquet_of_Violets_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Edouard_Manet_-_Berthe_Morisot_With_a_Bouquet_of_Violets_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/440px-Edouard_Manet_-_Berthe_Morisot_With_a_Bouquet_of_Violets_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Edouard_Manet_-_Berthe_Morisot_With_a_Bouquet_of_Violets_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Manet" title="Édouard Manet">Édouard Manet</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Berthe_Morisot_with_a_Bouquet_of_Violets" title="Berthe Morisot with a Bouquet of Violets">Berthe Morisot with a Bouquet of Violets</a></i> (in mourning for her father), 1872, <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_d%27Orsay" title="Musée d'Orsay">Musée d'Orsay</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1868 Morisot became friends with <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Manet" title="Édouard Manet">Édouard Manet</a> who painted several portraits of her, including a striking study in a black veil while in mourning for her father. Correspondence between them shows warm affection, and Manet gave her an easel as a Christmas present. To her dismay he interfered with one of her Salon submissions whilst he was engaged to transport it, mistaking her self-criticism as an invitation to add corrections. Manet wrote: "The young Morisot girls are charming. It's annoying that they are not men. However, as women, they could serve the cause of painting by each marrying a member of the French Academy and sowing discord in the camp of those dotards." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although Manet is regarded as the master and Morisot as the follower, there is evidence that their relationship was reciprocal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Records show Manet's appreciation of her distinctive original style and compositional decisions, some of which he incorporated into his own work. It was Morisot who persuaded Manet to attempt <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plein_air" title="Plein air">plein air</a> painting, which she had been practising since having been introduced to it by Corot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Morisot drew Manet into the circle of painters who became known as the Impressionists. In 1874, she married Manet's brother, Eugène, and they had one daughter, <a href="/wiki/Julie_Manet" title="Julie Manet">Julie</a>, who became the subject for many of her mother's paintings. Julie's memoirs, <i>Growing Up with the Impressionists: The Diary of Julie Manet</i>, were published in 1987.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Style_and_technique">Style and technique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berthe_Morisot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Style and technique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jeune_Fille_au_Manteau_Vert_by_Berthe_Morisot.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2500" data-file-width="1688" height="326" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Jeune_Fille_au_Manteau_Vert_by_Berthe_Morisot.jpg/220px-Jeune_Fille_au_Manteau_Vert_by_Berthe_Morisot.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Jeune_Fille_au_Manteau_Vert_by_Berthe_Morisot.jpg/330px-Jeune_Fille_au_Manteau_Vert_by_Berthe_Morisot.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Jeune_Fille_au_Manteau_Vert_by_Berthe_Morisot.jpg/440px-Jeune_Fille_au_Manteau_Vert_by_Berthe_Morisot.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jeune_Fille_au_Manteau_Vert_by_Berthe_Morisot.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Jeune Fille au Manteau Vert by Berthe Morisot. Oil on canvas, circa 1894</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Morisot’s works are almost always small in scale. She worked in oil paint, watercolors, or pastel, and sketched using various drawing media. Around 1880 she began painting on unprimed canvases—a technique Manet and <a href="/wiki/Eva_Gonzal%C3%A8s" title="Eva Gonzalès">Eva Gonzalès</a> also experimented with at the time<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>—and her brushwork became looser. In 1888–89, her brushstrokes transitioned from short, rapid strokes to long, sinuous ones that define form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The outer edges of her paintings were often left unfinished, allowing the canvas to show through and increasing the sense of spontaneity. After 1885, she worked mostly from preliminary drawings before beginning her oil paintings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Charles_F..2CWilliam_P._18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Charles_F..2CWilliam_P.-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among her contemporary art critics such as Gustave Geoffrey in 1881, Morisot was hailed as "no one represents Impressionism with more refined talent or more authority than Morisot" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Morisot creates a sense of space and depth through the use of color. Although her color palette was somewhat limited, her fellow impressionists regarded her as a "virtuoso colorist".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Charles_F..2CWilliam_P._18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Charles_F..2CWilliam_P.-18">[18]</a></sup> She typically made expansive use of white, whether used as a pure white or mixed with other colors. In her large painting, <i>The Cherry Tree</i>, colors are more vivid but are still used to emphasize form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Charles_F..2CWilliam_P._18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Charles_F..2CWilliam_P.-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subjects">Subjects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berthe_Morisot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Subjects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Berthe_Morisot_-_Berg%C3%A8re_nue_couch%C3%A9e.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1309" data-file-width="2000" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Berthe_Morisot_-_Berg%C3%A8re_nue_couch%C3%A9e.jpg/220px-Berthe_Morisot_-_Berg%C3%A8re_nue_couch%C3%A9e.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Berthe_Morisot_-_Berg%C3%A8re_nue_couch%C3%A9e.jpg/330px-Berthe_Morisot_-_Berg%C3%A8re_nue_couch%C3%A9e.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Berthe_Morisot_-_Berg%C3%A8re_nue_couch%C3%A9e.jpg/440px-Berthe_Morisot_-_Berg%C3%A8re_nue_couch%C3%A9e.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Morisot painted what she experienced on a daily basis. Her paintings reflect the 19th-century cultural restrictions of her class and gender. She avoided urban and street scenes and seldom painted the nude figure. Like her fellow Impressionist <a href="/wiki/Mary_Cassatt" title="Mary Cassatt">Mary Cassatt</a>, she focused on domestic life and portraits in which she could use family and personal friends as models, including her daughter Julie and sister Edma. Prior to the 1860s, Morisot painted subjects in line with the Barbizon school before turning to scenes of contemporary femininity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Paintings like <i>The Cradle</i> (1872), in which she depicted current trends for nursery furniture, reflect her sensitivity to fashion and advertising, both of which would have been apparent to her female audience. Her works also include landscapes, portraits, garden settings and boating scenes. Later in her career Morisot worked with more ambitious themes, such as nudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Corresponding with Morisot's interest in nude subjects, Morisot also began to focus more on preliminary drawings, completing many drypoints, charcoal, and color pencil drawings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berthe_Morisot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Morisot was married to <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Manet" title="Eugène Manet">Eugène Manet</a>, the brother of her friend and colleague <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Manet" title="Édouard Manet">Édouard Manet</a>, from 1874 until his death in 1892. In 1878 she gave birth to her only child, <a href="/wiki/Julie_Manet" title="Julie Manet">Julie</a>, who posed frequently for her mother and other Impressionist artists, including Renoir and her uncle Édouard.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berthe_Morisot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Morisot died on 2 March 1895, in Paris, of <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> contracted while attending to her daughter Julie's similar illness, and thus orphaning her at the age of 16. She was interred in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cimeti%C3%A8re_de_Passy" title="Cimetière de Passy">Cimetière de Passy</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture">Popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berthe_Morisot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She was portrayed by actress <a href="/wiki/Marine_Delterme" title="Marine Delterme">Marine Delterme</a> in the eponymous 2012 French biographical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TV_film" title="TV film">TV film</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Champetier" title="Caroline Champetier">Caroline Champetier</a>. The character of Beatrice de Clerval in <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Kostova" title="Elizabeth Kostova">Elizabeth Kostova</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Swan_Thieves" title="The Swan Thieves">The Swan Thieves</a></i> is largerly based on Morisot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_market">Art market</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berthe_Morisot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Art market">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Morisot's work sold comparatively well. She achieved the two highest prices at a <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B4tel_Drouot" title="Hôtel Drouot">Hôtel Drouot</a> auction in 1875, the <i>Interior (Young Woman with Mirror)</i> sold for 480 francs, and her pastel <i>On the Lawn</i> sold for 320 francs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Her works averaged 250 francs, the best relative prices at the auction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2013, Morisot became the highest priced female artist, when <i>After Lunch</i> (1881), a portrait of a young redhead in a straw hat and purple dress, sold for $10.9 million at a <a href="/wiki/Christie%27s" title="Christie's">Christie's</a> auction. The painting achieved roughly three times its upper estimate,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wall_Street_Journal_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_Street_Journal-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Women_on_the_Verge_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Women_on_the_Verge-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Women_on_the_Verge_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Women_on_the_Verge-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> exceeding the $10.7 million for a sculpture by <a href="/wiki/Louise_Bourgeois" title="Louise Bourgeois">Louise Bourgeois</a> in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wall_Street_Journal_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_Street_Journal-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
