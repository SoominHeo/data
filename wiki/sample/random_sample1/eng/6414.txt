<p>The <b>Nazi book burnings</b> were a campaign conducted by the <a href="/wiki/German_Student_Union" title="German Student Union">German Student Union</a> to ceremonially <a href="/wiki/Book_burning" title="Book burning">burn books</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/First_Austrian_Republic" title="First Austrian Republic">Austria</a> in the 1930s. The books targeted for burning were those viewed as being subversive or as representing ideologies opposed to <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazism</a>. These included books written by <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jewish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pacifism" title="Pacifism">pacifist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">Religious</a>, <a href="/wiki/Classical_liberalism" title="Classical liberalism">classical liberal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialist</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communist</a> authors, among others.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_book-burning_campaign">The book-burning campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazi_book_burnings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The book-burning campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_119-2406-01,_Berlin-Lustgarten,_Rede_Joseph_Goebbels.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="557" height="244" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Bundesarchiv_Bild_119-2406-01%2C_Berlin-Lustgarten%2C_Rede_Joseph_Goebbels.jpg/170px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_119-2406-01%2C_Berlin-Lustgarten%2C_Rede_Joseph_Goebbels.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Bundesarchiv_Bild_119-2406-01%2C_Berlin-Lustgarten%2C_Rede_Joseph_Goebbels.jpg/255px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_119-2406-01%2C_Berlin-Lustgarten%2C_Rede_Joseph_Goebbels.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Bundesarchiv_Bild_119-2406-01%2C_Berlin-Lustgarten%2C_Rede_Joseph_Goebbels.jpg/340px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_119-2406-01%2C_Berlin-Lustgarten%2C_Rede_Joseph_Goebbels.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<p>On April 8, 1933, the Main Office for <a href="/wiki/News_media" title="News media">Press</a> and <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">Propaganda</a> of the <a href="/wiki/German_Student_Union" title="German Student Union">German Student Union</a> proclaimed a nationwide "Action against the Un-German Spirit", which was to climax in a literary purge or "cleansing" ("Säuberung") by fire. Local chapters were to supply the press with releases and commissioned articles, sponsor well-known <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi</a> figures to speak at public gatherings, and negotiate for radio broadcast time. On the 8th of April, the Student Union also drafted the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Theses" title="Twelve Theses">Twelve Theses</a> which deliberately evoked <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> and the historic burning of "Un-German" books at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wartburg_festival" title="Wartburg festival">Wartburg festival</a> on the 300th anniversary of the posting of Luther's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Ninety-Five_Theses" title="The Ninety-Five Theses">Ninety-Five Theses</a>. The theses called for a "pure" national language and culture. Placards publicized the theses, which attacked "Jewish intellectualism", asserted the need to "purify" German language and literature, and demanded that universities be centres of German <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a>. The students described the "action" as a response to a worldwide Jewish "smear campaign" against Germany and an affirmation of traditional German values.</p>
<p>On 10 May 1933, in an act of ominous significance, the students <a href="/wiki/Book_burning" title="Book burning">burned</a> upwards of 25,000 volumes of "un-German" books, thereby presaging an era of uncompromising state censorship. In many university towns, nationalist students marched in torch lit parades against the "un-German" spirit. The scripted rituals of this night called for high Nazi officials, professors, rectors, and student leaders to address the participants and spectators. At the meeting places, students threw the pillaged, banned books into the bonfires with a great joyous ceremony that included live music, singing, "fire oaths," and incantations. In Berlin, some 40,000 people gathered in the <a href="/wiki/Bebelplatz" title="Bebelplatz">square at the State Opera</a> to hear <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Goebbels" title="Joseph Goebbels">Joseph Goebbels</a> deliver a fiery address: "No to decadence and moral corruption!" Goebbels enjoined the crowd. “Yes to decency and morality in family and state! I consign to the flames the writings of <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Mann" title="Heinrich Mann">Heinrich Mann</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_Gl%C3%A4ser&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ernst Gläser (page does not exist)">Ernst Gläser</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erich_K%C3%A4stner" title="Erich Kästner">Erich Kästner</a>.”</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The era of extreme Jewish <a href="/wiki/Intellectualism" title="Intellectualism">intellectualism</a> is now at an end. The breakthrough of the German revolution has again cleared the way on the German path...The future German man will not just be a man of books, but a man of character. It is to this end that we want to educate you. As a young person, to already have the courage to face the pitiless glare, to overcome the fear of death, and to regain respect for death - this is the task of this young generation. And thus you do well in this midnight hour to commit to the flames the evil spirit of the past. This is a strong, great and <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbolic</a> deed - a deed which should document the following for the world to know - Here the intellectual foundation of the <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">November Republic</a> is sinking to the ground, but from this wreckage the <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_(mythology)" title="Phoenix (mythology)">phoenix</a> of a new spirit will triumphantly rise.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Joseph Goebbels, Speech to the students in Berlin</cite></div>
</blockquote>

<p>Not all book burnings took place on 10 May as the German Student Union had planned. Some were postponed a few days because of rain. Others, based on local chapter preference, took place on 21 June, the <a href="/wiki/Summer_solstice" title="Summer solstice">summer solstice</a>, a traditional date of celebration. Nonetheless, in 34 university towns across Germany the "Action against the Un-German Spirit" was a success, enlisting widespread newspaper coverage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> And in some places, notably Berlin, radio broadcasts brought the speeches, songs, and ceremonial incantations "live" to countless German listeners.</p>
<p>All of the following types of literature were to be banned.</p>
<ul>
<li>The works of traitors, emigrants and authors from foreign countries who believe they can attack and denigrate the new Germany (H.G. Wells, Rolland);</li>
<li>The literature of Marxism, Communism and Bolshevism;</li>
<li>Pacifist literature;</li>
<li>Literature with liberal, democratic tendencies and attitudes, and writings supporting the Weimar Republic (Rathenau, Heinrich Mann);</li>
<li>All historical writings whose purpose is to denigrate the origin, the spirit and the culture of the German Volk, or to dissolve the racial and structural order of the Volk, or that denies the force and importance of leading historical figures in favor of egalitarianism and the masses, and which seeks to drag them through the mud (Emil Ludwig);</li>
<li>Writings of a philosophical and social nature whose content deals with the false scientific enlightenment of primitive Darwinism and Monism (Hackel, Benedict);</li>
<li>Books that advocate “art” which is decadent, bloodless, or purely constructivist (Grosz, Dix, Bauhaus, Mendelsohn);</li>
<li>Writings on sexuality and sexual education which serve the egocentric pleasure of the individual and thus, completely destroy the principles of race and Volk (Hirschfeld);</li>
<li>The decadent, destructive and Volk-damaging writings of “Asphalt and Civilization” literati: (Graf, H. Mann, Stefan Zweig, Wassermann, Franz Blei);</li>
<li>Literature by Jewish authors, regardless of the field;</li>
<li>Popular entertainment literature that depicts life and life’s goals in a superficial, unrealistic and sickly sweet manner, based on a bourgeois or upper class view of life;</li>
<li>Nationalistic and patriotic kitsch in literature.</li>
<li>Pornography and explicit literature by Jewish authors.</li>
<li>All books degrading German purity.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Responses">Responses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazi_book_burnings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Responses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The blind writer <a href="/wiki/Helen_Keller" title="Helen Keller">Helen Keller</a> published an <i>Open Letter to German Students:</i> 'You may burn my books and the books of the best minds in Europe, but the ideas those books contain have passed through millions of channels and will go on.'”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Persecuted_authors">Persecuted authors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazi_book_burnings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Persecuted authors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Not only German-speaking authors were burned but also French authors like <a href="/wiki/Victor_Hugo" title="Victor Hugo">Victor Hugo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Gide" title="André Gide">André Gide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romain_Rolland" title="Romain Rolland">Romain Rolland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henri_Barbusse" title="Henri Barbusse">Henri Barbusse</a>, American writers such as <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hemingway" title="Ernest Hemingway">Ernest Hemingway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Upton_Sinclair" title="Upton Sinclair">Upton Sinclair</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Dreiser" title="Theodore Dreiser">Theodore Dreiser</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jack_London" title="Jack London">Jack London</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Dos_Passos" title="John Dos Passos">John Dos Passos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Helen_Keller" title="Helen Keller">Helen Keller</a> as well as English authors <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Conrad" title="Joseph Conrad">Joseph Conrad</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D.H._Lawrence" title="D.H. Lawrence">D.H. Lawrence</a>, <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">H.G. Wells</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aldous_Huxley" title="Aldous Huxley">Aldous Huxley</a>, Irish writer <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">James Joyce</a> and Russian authors including <a href="/wiki/Fyodor_Dostoyevsky" title="Fyodor Dostoyevsky">Fyodor Dostoyevsky</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maxim_Gorki" title="Maxim Gorki">Maxim Gorki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Babel" title="Isaac Babel">Isaac Babel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Vladimir Lenin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Nabokov" title="Vladimir Nabokov">Vladimir Nabokov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leo_Tolstoy" title="Leo Tolstoy">Leo Tolstoy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leon_Trotsky" title="Leon Trotsky">Leon Trotsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Mayakovsky" title="Vladimir Mayakovsky">Vladimir Mayakovsky</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ilya_Ehrenburg" title="Ilya Ehrenburg">Ilya Ehrenburg</a>.</p>
<p>The burning of the books represents a culmination of the persecution of those authors whose oral or written opinions were opposed to Nazi ideology. Many artists, writers and scientists were banned from working and publication. Their works could no longer be found in libraries or in the curricula of schools or universities. Some of them were driven to exile (like <a href="/wiki/Walter_Mehring" title="Walter Mehring">Walter Mehring</a> and Arnold Zweig); others were deprived of their citizenship (for example Ernst Toller and Kurt Tucholsky) or forced into a self-imposed exile from society (e.g. Erich Kästner). For other writers the Nazi persecutions ended in death. Some of them died in concentration camps, due to the consequences of the conditions of imprisonment, or were executed (like Carl von Ossietzky, <a href="/wiki/Erich_M%C3%BChsam" title="Erich Mühsam">Erich Mühsam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gertrud_Kolmar" title="Gertrud Kolmar">Gertrud Kolmar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jakob_van_Hoddis" title="Jakob van Hoddis">Jakob van Hoddis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Kornfeld_(playwright)" title="Paul Kornfeld (playwright)">Paul Kornfeld</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arno_Nadel" title="Arno Nadel">Arno Nadel</a> and Georg Hermann, Theodor Wolff, <a href="/wiki/Adam_Kuckhoff" title="Adam Kuckhoff">Adam Kuckhoff</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Hilferding" title="Rudolf Hilferding">Rudolf Hilferding</a>). Exiled authors despaired and committed suicide, for example: <a href="/wiki/Walter_Hasenclever" title="Walter Hasenclever">Walter Hasenclever</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Weiss" title="Ernst Weiss">Ernst Weiss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Einstein" title="Carl Einstein">Carl Einstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Benjamin" title="Walter Benjamin">Walter Benjamin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Toller" title="Ernst Toller">Ernst Toller</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Zweig" title="Stefan Zweig">Stefan Zweig</a>.</p>
<p>Heinrich Heine, whose work was also burned, wrote in his 1820-1821 play <i>Almansor</i> the famous admonition, “Dort, wo man Bücher verbrennt, verbrennt man am Ende auch Menschen": "Where they burn books, they will in the end also burn people."</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Denazification">Denazification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazi_book_burnings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Denazification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Censorship_in_the_Federal_Republic_of_Germany" title="Censorship in the Federal Republic of Germany">Censorship in the Federal Republic of Germany</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allied_Occupation_Zones_in_Germany" title="Allied Occupation Zones in Germany">Allied Occupation Zones in Germany</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Denazification" title="Denazification">Denazification</a></div>
<p>In 1946, the Allied occupation authorities drew up a list of over 30,000 titles, ranging from school books to poetry and including works by such authors as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Von_Clausewitz" title="Von Clausewitz">von Clausewitz</a>. Millions of copies of these books were confiscated and destroyed. The representative of the Military Directorate admitted that the order in principle was no different from the Nazi book burnings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Artworks were under the same censorship as other media;</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>"all collections of works of art related or dedicated to the perpetuation of German militarism or Nazism will be closed permanently and taken into custody.".</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>The directives were very broadly interpreted, leading to the destruction of thousands of paintings and thousands more were shipped to deposits in the U.S. Those confiscated paintings still surviving in U.S. custody include, for example, a painting "depicting a couple of middle aged women talking in a sunlit street in a small town".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Remembrance">Remembrance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazi_book_burnings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Remembrance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Fighting the Fires of Hate: America and the Nazi Book Burnings</i> is a traveling exhibition produced by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Holocaust_Memorial_Museum" title="United States Holocaust Memorial Museum">United States Holocaust Memorial Museum</a>. Through historical photographs, documents, and films, it explores how the book burnings became a potent symbol in America’s battle against Nazism and why they continue to resonate with the public—in film, literature, and political discourse—to this day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2014, the exhibition was displayed in West Fargo, North Dakota; Dallas, Texas; and Missoula, Montana.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
