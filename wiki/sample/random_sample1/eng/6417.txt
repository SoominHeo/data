<p><b>Berry Gordy Jr.</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bayles_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bayles-2">[2]</a></sup> (born November 28, 1929)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-biography-1">[1]</a></sup> is an American record producer and songwriter. He is best known as the founder of the <a href="/wiki/Motown" title="Motown">Motown</a> record label and its subsidiaries, which was the highest-earning <a href="/wiki/African-American_businesses" title="African-American businesses">African-American business</a> for decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berry_Gordy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Berry Gordy Jr. was born to the middle-class <a href="/wiki/Gordy_family" title="Gordy family">family of Berry Gordy II</a> (also known as Berry Gordy, Sr.), who had relocated to Detroit from <a href="/wiki/Oconee,_Georgia" title="Oconee, Georgia">Oconee</a> in Washington County, Georgia, in 1922.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bayles_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bayles-2">[2]</a></sup> The first Berry Gordy was the son of a white plantation owner in Georgia and his female slave. Berry Gordy, Sr. was lured to Detroit by the job opportunities for black people offered by the booming automotive businesses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bayles_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bayles-2">[2]</a></sup> He developed his interest in music by writing songs and opening the 3-D Record Mart, a record store featuring jazz music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DesertIsland_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-DesertIsland-4">[4]</a></sup> The store was unsuccessful, and Gordy sought work at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lincoln-Mercury" title="Lincoln-Mercury">Lincoln-Mercury</a> plant, but his family connections put him in touch with Al Green (no relation to the singer Al Green), owner of the Flame Show Bar Talent Club, where he met the singer <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Wilson" title="Jackie Wilson">Jackie Wilson</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1957 Wilson recorded "<a href="/wiki/Reet_Petite" title="Reet Petite">Reet Petite</a>", a song Gordy had co-written with his sister Gwen and writer-producer <a href="/wiki/Billy_Davis_(songwriter)" title="Billy Davis (songwriter)">Billy Davis</a>. It became a modest hit, but had more success internationally, especially in the UK, where it reached the Top 10 and even later topped the chart on re-issue in 1986. Wilson recorded six more songs co-written by Gordy over the next two years, including "<a href="/wiki/Lonely_Teardrops" title="Lonely Teardrops">Lonely Teardrops</a>", which topped the R&amp;B charts and got to number 7 in the pop chart. Berry and Gwen Gordy also wrote "<a href="/wiki/All_I_Could_Do_Was_Cry" title="All I Could Do Was Cry">All I Could Do Was Cry</a>" for <a href="/wiki/Etta_James" title="Etta James">Etta James</a> at <a href="/wiki/Chess_Records" title="Chess Records">Chess Records</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Motown_Record_Corporation">Motown Record Corporation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berry_Gordy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Motown Record Corporation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Motown" title="Motown">Motown</a></div>
<p>Gordy reinvested the profits from his songwriting success into producing. In 1957, he discovered <a href="/wiki/The_Miracles" title="The Miracles">the Miracles</a> (originally known as the Matadors) and began building a portfolio of successful artists. In 1959, with the encouragement of Miracles leader <a href="/wiki/Smokey_Robinson" title="Smokey Robinson">Smokey Robinson</a>, Gordy borrowed $800 from his family to create an R&amp;B record company. Originally, Gordy wanted to name the new label Tammy Records, after <a href="/wiki/Tammy_(song)" title="Tammy (song)">the song</a> recorded by <a href="/wiki/Debbie_Reynolds" title="Debbie Reynolds">Debbie Reynolds</a>. However, that name was taken, and he chose the name <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tamla_Records" title="Tamla Records">Tamla Records</a>. The company began operating on January 21, 1959. "Come to Me" by Marv Johnson was issued as Tamla 101. <a href="/wiki/United_Artists_Records" title="United Artists Records">United Artists Records</a> picked up "Come to Me" for national distribution, as well as Johnson's more successful follow-up records such as "<a href="/wiki/You_Got_What_It_Takes" title="You Got What It Takes">You Got What It Takes</a>", co-produced and co-written by Gordy. His next release was the only 45 ever issued on his Rayber label, featuring Wade Jones with an unnamed female backup group. The record did not sell well and is now one of the rarest issues from the Motown stable. Berry's third release was "<a href="/wiki/Bad_Girl_(The_Miracles_song)" title="Bad Girl (The Miracles song)">Bad Girl</a>" by <a href="/wiki/The_Miracles" title="The Miracles">the Miracles</a>, the first release on the Motown record label. "Bad Girl" was a solid hit in 1959 after <a href="/wiki/Chess_Records" title="Chess Records">Chess Records</a> picked it up. <a href="/wiki/Barrett_Strong" title="Barrett Strong">Barrett Strong</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Money_(That%27s_What_I_Want)" title="Money (That's What I Want)">Money (That's What I Want)</a>" initially appeared on Tamla and then charted on Gordy's sister's label, <a href="/wiki/Anna_Records" title="Anna Records">Anna Records</a>, in February 1960.</p>
<p>The Tamla and Motown labels were then merged into a new company, <a href="/wiki/Motown" title="Motown">Motown Record Corporation</a>, incorporated on April 14, 1960. In 1960, Gordy signed an unknown singer, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Wells" title="Mary Wells">Mary Wells</a>, who became the fledgling label's first star, with Smokey Robinson penning her hits "<a href="/wiki/You_Beat_Me_to_the_Punch" title="You Beat Me to the Punch">You Beat Me to the Punch</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Two_Lovers_(Mary_Wells_song)" title="Two Lovers (Mary Wells song)">Two Lovers</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/My_Guy" title="My Guy">My Guy</a>". The Miracles' hit "<a href="/wiki/Shop_Around" title="Shop Around">Shop Around</a>" peaked at No. 1 on the national <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%26B" title="R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a> charts in late 1960 and at No. 2 on the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billboard_magazine" title="Billboard magazine">Billboard</a></i> pop charts on January 16, 1961 (No. 1 pop, <a href="/wiki/Cashbox_(magazine)" title="Cashbox (magazine)">Cash Box</a>), which established Motown as an independent company worthy of notice. Later in 1961, <a href="/wiki/The_Marvelettes" title="The Marvelettes">the Marvelettes</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Please_Mr._Postman" title="Please Mr. Postman">Please Mr. Postman</a>" made it to the top of both charts.</p>

<p>Gordy's gift for identifying and bringing together musical talent, along with the careful management of his artists' public image, made Motown initially a major national and then international success. Over the next decade, he signed such artists as <a href="/wiki/The_Supremes" title="The Supremes">the Supremes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marvin_Gaye" title="Marvin Gaye">Marvin Gaye</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Temptations" title="The Temptations">the Temptations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Ruffin" title="Jimmy Ruffin">Jimmy Ruffin</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Contours" title="The Contours">the Contours</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Four_Tops" title="Four Tops">Four Tops</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gladys_Knight_%26_the_Pips" title="Gladys Knight &amp; the Pips">Gladys Knight &amp; the Pips</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Commodores" title="The Commodores">the Commodores</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Velvelettes" title="The Velvelettes">the Velvelettes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martha_and_the_Vandellas" title="Martha and the Vandellas">Martha and the Vandellas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Wonder" title="Stevie Wonder">Stevie Wonder</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Jackson_5" title="The Jackson 5">the Jackson 5</a>. Though he also signed various white acts on the label, he largely promoted African-American artists but carefully controlled their public image, dress, manners and choreography for across-the-board appeal.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relocation_to_Los_Angeles">Relocation to Los Angeles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berry_Gordy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relocation to Los Angeles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1972, Gordy relocated to Los Angeles, where he produced the commercially successful <a href="/wiki/Billie_Holiday" title="Billie Holiday">Billie Holiday</a> biography <i><a href="/wiki/Lady_Sings_the_Blues_(film)" title="Lady Sings the Blues (film)">Lady Sings the Blues</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Diana_Ross" title="Diana Ross">Diana Ross</a> (who was nominated for an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Pryor" title="Richard Pryor">Richard Pryor</a>, and introducing <a href="/wiki/Billy_Dee_Williams" title="Billy Dee Williams">Billy Dee Williams</a> (cast in a role originally for <a href="/wiki/Levi_Stubbs" title="Levi Stubbs">Levi Stubbs</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Four_Tops" title="Four Tops">Four Tops</a>). Initially the studio, over Gordy's objections, rejected Williams after several screen tests. However, Gordy, known for his tenacity, eventually prevailed, and the film established Williams as a major movie star. Berry Gordy soon after produced and directed <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mahogany_(1975_film)" title="Mahogany (1975 film)">Mahogany</a>,</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> also starring Ross and Williams. In 1985, he produced the cult martial arts film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Dragon" title="The Last Dragon">The Last Dragon</a></i>, which starred martial artist <a href="/wiki/Taimak" title="Taimak">Taimak</a> and one of <a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">Prince's</a> proteges, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanity_(performer)" title="Vanity (performer)">Vanity</a>.</p>
<p>Although Motown continued to produce major hits throughout the 1970s and 1980s by artists including the Jacksons, <a href="/wiki/Rick_James" title="Rick James">Rick James</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lionel_Richie" title="Lionel Richie">Lionel Richie</a> and long-term signings, Stevie Wonder and Smokey Robinson, the record company was no longer the major force it had been. Gordy sold his interests in Motown Records to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Music_Corporation_of_America" title="Music Corporation of America">MCA</a> and Boston Ventures on June 28, 1988, for $61 million. He later sold most of his interests in Jobete publishing to <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a> Publishing. Gordy wrote or co-wrote 240 of the approximately 15,000 songs in Motown's Jobete music catalogue. However, the true test of the label's worth would come a few years later, when Polygram paid over $330 million (<a href="/wiki/Diana_Ross" title="Diana Ross">Diana Ross</a> was given shares in this version of the label) for the Motown catalog. (Though the current label bearing its name is a shell of its former self, the <a href="/wiki/Motown" title="Motown">Motown</a> sound is now practically a genre of its own).</p>
<p>Gordy published an autobiography, <i>To Be Loved</i>, in 1994.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_accolades">Awards and accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berry_Gordy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Awards and accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gordy was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rock and Roll Hall of Fame">Rock and Roll Hall of Fame</a> in 1988.</p>
<p>Gordy was inducted into the Junior Achievement U.S. Business Hall of Fame in 1998.</p>
<p>Gordy received the <a href="/wiki/Songwriters_Hall_of_Fame" title="Songwriters Hall of Fame">Songwriters Hall of Fame</a>'s Pioneer Award on June 13, 2013. He is the first living individual to receive the honor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2016, Gordy received the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Medal_of_Arts" title="National Medal of Arts">National Medal of Arts</a> from <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">President Obama</a> for "helping to create a trailblazing new sound in American music. As a record producer and songwriter, he helped build Motown, launching the music careers of countless legendary artists. His unique sound helped shape our Nation's story."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statements_about_Motown_artists">Statements about Motown artists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berry_Gordy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Statements about Motown artists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the funeral of <a href="/wiki/Marvin_Gaye" title="Marvin Gaye">Marvin Gaye</a> on April 5, 1984, Gordy declared Gaye "the greatest of his time" and stated the singer "had no musical equals", comparing his talents to that of <a href="/wiki/Billie_Holiday" title="Billie Holiday">Billie Holiday</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERitz1991336-337_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERitz1991336-337-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>On March 20, 2009, Gordy was in Hollywood to pay tribute to his first group and first million-selling act, <a href="/wiki/The_Miracles" title="The Miracles">the Miracles</a>, when the members received a star on the <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Walk_of_Fame" title="Hollywood Walk of Fame">Hollywood Walk of Fame</a>. Speaking in tribute to the group, Gordy said: "Without the Miracles, Motown would not be the Motown it is today."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the age of 79, Gordy spoke at the <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson_memorial_service" title="Michael Jackson memorial service">memorial service for Michael Jackson</a> in Los Angeles, on July 7, 2009. He suggested that "The King of Pop" was perhaps not the best description for Jackson in light of his achievements, referring to him instead as "the greatest entertainer that ever lived."</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Motown:_The_Musical"><i>Motown: The Musical</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berry_Gordy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Motown: The Musical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On May 15, 2011, it was announced that Gordy was developing a <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a> musical about Motown. The show is said to be an account of events of the 1960s and how they shaped the creation of the label. Gordy hoped that the musical would improve the reputation of Motown Records and clear up any misconceptions regarding the label's demise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Motown:_The_Musical" title="Motown: The Musical">Motown: The Musical</a></i> began previews at the <a href="/wiki/Lunt-Fontanne_Theatre" title="Lunt-Fontanne Theatre">Lunt-Fontanne Theatre</a> on March 11, 2013, and began regular performances there on April 14.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The musical closed in January 2015, with the possibility of returning in the summer of 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The UK version of Motown the Musical opened in the West End in January 2016. Berry Gordy went to the opening night.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berry_Gordy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gordy, who was married and divorced three times, has eight children: Hazel Joy, Berry IV, Terry James, Kerry Ashby, Sherry, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kennedy_William_Gordy" title="Kennedy William Gordy">Kennedy William</a>, Rhonda Suzanne, and <a href="/wiki/Redfoo" title="Redfoo">Stefan Kendal</a>. His publishing company, Jobete, was named after his three eldest children, <b>Jo</b>y, <b>Be</b>rry and <b>Te</b>rry.</p>
<p>He had three children with his first wife, Thelma Coleman, who he married in 1953 (they were divorced in 1959):</p>
<ul>
<li>Hazel Joy (born August 24, 1954)</li>
<li>Berry IV (born October 1955)</li>
<li>Terry James (born August 1956)</li>
</ul>
<p>In the spring of 1960 he married <a href="/wiki/Ray_Singleton" title="Ray Singleton">Raynoma Mayberry Liles</a> (they were divorced in 1964).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> They had one child:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kerry_Gordy" title="Kerry Gordy">Kerry Ashby</a> (born June 25, 1959)</li>
</ul>
<p>With Jeana Jackson, Gordy had one daughter:</p>
<ul>
<li>Sherry (born May 23 [1960?])<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>With his then-mistress Margaret Norton, Gordy had a son who would later become more popularly known as Motown musician <a href="/wiki/Rockwell_(musician)" title="Rockwell (musician)">Rockwell</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rockwell_(musician)" title="Rockwell (musician)">Kennedy William</a> (born March 15, 1964)</li>
</ul>
<p>Gordy had a daughter with Motown artist <a href="/wiki/Diana_Ross" title="Diana Ross">Diana Ross</a>, with whom he had an intimate relationship from 1965 through the 1970s:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rhonda_Ross_Kendrick" title="Rhonda Ross Kendrick">Rhonda Suzanne</a> (born August 14, 1971)</li>
</ul>
<p>Gordy's eighth and youngest child is a son born to Nancy Leiviska. He is known by his stage name, <a href="/wiki/Redfoo" title="Redfoo">Redfoo</a>, as one member of the duo <a href="/wiki/LMFAO" title="LMFAO">LMFAO</a> (the other member is Skyler Gordy, born August 23, 1986, and known professionally as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SkyBlu" title="SkyBlu">SkyBlu</a>; he is the grandson of Gordy and Thelma Coleman through their son Berry IV and his wife, Valerie Robeson):</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Redfoo" title="Redfoo">Stefan Kendal</a> (born September 3, 1975)</li>
</ul>
<p>Berry married Grace Eaton on July 17, 1990; they divorced in 1993.</p>
<p>Gordy is a <a href="/wiki/Veganism" title="Veganism">vegan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berry_Gordy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Gordy was portrayed by Billy Dee Williams (whose career Gordy had helped to jump-start in the 1970s) in the 1992 miniseries <i><a href="/wiki/The_Jacksons:_An_American_Dream" title="The Jacksons: An American Dream">The Jacksons: An American Dream</a></i>.</li>
<li>Gordy was portrayed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obba_Babatunde" title="Obba Babatunde">Obba Babatunde</a> in the 1998 miniseries <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Temptations_(TV_miniseries)" title="The Temptations (TV miniseries)">The Temptations</a></i>.</li>
<li>The character Gordy Berry in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fresh_Prince_of_Bel-Air" title="The Fresh Prince of Bel-Air">The Fresh Prince of Bel-Air</a></i> is a reference to Berry Gordy.</li>
<li>The character of Curtis Taylor Jr., a music executive in the 2006 musical film <i><a href="/wiki/Dreamgirls_(film)" title="Dreamgirls (film)">Dreamgirls</a></i>, has been called "a thinly veiled portrayal" of Gordy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sorry_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-sorry-21">[21]</a></sup> The film was based on the 1981 musical <i><a href="/wiki/Dreamgirls" title="Dreamgirls">Dreamgirls</a></i>, but the film made the connection to Gordy and Motown much more explicit than the musical did, by, among other things, moving the setting of the story from Chicago to Detroit. Taylor appears in the film as unethical and insensitive to his artists, which caused Gordy and others to criticize the film after its release. Gordy called the portrayal "100% wrong," while <a href="/wiki/Smokey_Robinson" title="Smokey Robinson">Smokey Robinson</a> said it "blatantly painted a negative picture of Motown and Berry Gordy and of <a href="/wiki/The_Supremes" title="The Supremes">the Supremes</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In 2007, the producers of the film, <a href="/wiki/DreamWorks" title="DreamWorks">DreamWorks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a>, issued a public apology to Gordy, saying they were sorry "for any confusion that has resulted from our fictional work." Gordy accepted the apology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sorry_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-sorry-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
