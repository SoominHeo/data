<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">VeriChip</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><i><b>VeriChip</b></i> (now re-branding <i><b><a href="/wiki/PositiveID" title="PositiveID">PositiveID</a></b></i>) was the only <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA)-approved <a href="/wiki/Microchip_implant_(human)" title="Microchip implant (human)">human-implantable microchip</a>. It was marketed by <a href="/wiki/PositiveID" title="PositiveID">PositiveID</a>, a subsidiary of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Applied_Digital_Solutions" title="Applied Digital Solutions">Applied Digital Solutions</a>, and it received United States FDA approval in 2004. Its manufacture and marketing were discontinued in 2010. About twice the length of a dime, the device is typically implanted between the shoulder and elbow area of an individual’s right arm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Once scanned at the proper frequency, the VeriChip responds with a unique 16 digit number which could be then linked with information about the user held on a database for identity verification, medical records access and other uses. The insertion procedure is performed under local anesthetic in a physician's office.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As an implanted device used for identification by a third party, it had generated controversy and debate. VeriChip's merger in 2010 officially changed their name to "PositiveID."</p>
<p>Destron Fearing, a subsidiary of Applied Digital Solutions, initially developed the technology for the VeriChip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ComputerBytesMan_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ComputerBytesMan-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Privacy_concerns">Privacy concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=VeriChip&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Privacy concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Certain privacy advocates have raised concerns regarding potential abuse of the VeriChip, with some warning that adoption by governments as a compulsory identification program could lead to erosion of civil liberties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In addition, it has been shown that the VeriChip's lack of security features made it susceptible to cloning,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> which could present a risk of identity theft. At the same time if these security features were to be increased the chips could begin to play a major role in Identity Theft protection. Three states in the United States of America have passed anti-chipping legislation, protecting against mandated implantation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> These states are California, Wisconsin, and North Dakota.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_effects">Health effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=VeriChip&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Health effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to Wired News online,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and the Associated Press,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> there have been research articles over the last ten years that found a connection between the chips and possible cancer. When mice and rats were injected with glass-encapsulated RFID transponders, like those made by VeriChip, they "developed malignant, fast-growing, lethal cancers in up to 1% to 10% of cases" at the site at which the microchip was injected or to which it had migrated. However, the 10% rate was obtained with hemizygous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P53" title="P53">p53</a>-deficient mice, the counterpart of humans with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Li-Fraumeni_syndrome" title="Li-Fraumeni syndrome">Li-Fraumeni syndrome</a>, and rates near 1% were more typical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The Verichip corporation responded to this report, which caused a 40% drop in their stock value, by stating that rodent data had been provided to the FDA and did not reflect the effect of the chips in humans or pets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Dogs, alternatively, are more resistant to the formation of malignant soft tissue tumors in response to foreign body insult. Induction of sarcomas by foreign bodies has been reported in humans,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and has been described as analogous to rodent foreign body-associated sarcomas and is fairly infrequent. Resolution of the question may be hindered by the long delay in onset of sarcoma induction or other deleterious side effects, analogous to the controversy in the mid 20th century over <a href="/wiki/Asbestos" title="Asbestos">asbestos</a> exposure and predisposition to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pleural" title="Pleural">pleural</a> abnormalities such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malignant" title="Malignant">malignant mesothelioma</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tommy_Thompson" title="Tommy Thompson">Tommy Thompson</a>, the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretary_of_Health_and_Human_Services" title="Secretary of Health and Human Services">Secretary of Health and Human Services</a>, supported the VeriChip as a "useful tool in sharing medical information with health care providers in emergency situations". Thompson sat on the board of directors of VeriChip's parent company <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Applied_Digital_Solutions" title="Applied Digital Solutions">Applied Digital Solutions</a> for two years. In June 2007, the American Medical Association declared that "implantable radio frequency identification (RFID) devices may help to identify patients, thereby improving the safety and efficiency of patient care, and may be used to enable secure access to patient clinical information".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Channel" title="Discovery Channel">Discovery Channel</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/MythBusters" title="MythBusters">MythBusters</a></i> explored whether an RFID tag will explode if placed inside an <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">MRI</a>. The Build Team inserted an RFID tag into pig flesh and placed inside the MRI, but failed to get any results. Team member <a href="/wiki/Kari_Byron" title="Kari Byron">Kari Byron</a> then had an RFID tag placed inside her arm and was placed inside the MRI. The RFID tag remained unaffected, and left Kari unharmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_concerns">Religious concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=VeriChip&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Religious concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some activists, including <a href="/wiki/Mark_Dice" title="Mark Dice">Mark Dice</a>, the author of a book titled <i>The Resistance Manifesto</i>, make a link between the VeriChip and the Biblical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mark_of_the_Beast" title="Mark of the Beast">Mark of the Beast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gary_Wohlscheid&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gary Wohlscheid (page does not exist)">Gary Wohlscheid</a>, president of These Last Days Ministries, has argued that "Out of all the technologies with potential to be the mark of the beast, the VeriChip has got the best possibility right now".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
