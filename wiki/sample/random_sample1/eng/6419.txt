<p><b>Berwick-upon-Tweed</b> [<span class="nowrap"><span class="noexcerpt"><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/22/En-uk-Berwick-upon-Tweed.ogg" title="Listen"><img alt="Listen" data-file-height="11" data-file-width="11" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/11px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/22px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a><sup><span class="IPA" style="color:#00e;font:bold 80% sans-serif;padding:0 .1em"><a href="/wiki/File:En-uk-BerwickUponTweed.ogg" title="File:En-uk-BerwickUponTweed.ogg">i</a></span></sup></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA 'tie'">t</span><span title="'w' in 'wind'">w</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span>/</a></span></span>] (<a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a>: <span lang="sco" xml:lang="sco"><i>Sooth Berwick</i></span>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" title="Scottish Gaelic language">Scottish Gaelic</a>: <span lang="gd" xml:lang="gd"><i>Bearaig a Deas</i></span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>) is a <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">town</a> in the <a href="/wiki/County" title="County">county</a> of <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>. It is the <a href="/wiki/North" title="North">northernmost</a> town in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is located <span class="frac nowrap">2<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> miles (4 km) south of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Scottish_Border" title="Anglo-Scottish Border">Scottish border</a>, at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/River_Tweed" title="River Tweed">River Tweed</a> on the east coast. It is about 56 miles (90 km) east-south east of <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>, 65 miles (105 km) north of <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a> and 345 miles (555 km) north of <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2011" title="United Kingdom Census 2011">United Kingdom Census 2011</a> recorded Berwick's population as 12,043.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a> and town council were created in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Berwick was founded as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon" title="Anglo-Saxon">Anglo-Saxon</a> settlement during the time of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Northumbria" title="Kingdom of Northumbria">Kingdom of Northumbria</a>, which was annexed by England in the 10th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lepage_2011_272_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lepage_2011_272-4">[4]</a></sup> The area was for more than 400 years central to historic border wars between the Kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Scotland" title="Kingdom of Scotland">Scotland</a>, and several times possession of Berwick changed hands between the two kingdoms. The last time it changed hands was when England <a href="/wiki/Capture_of_Berwick_(1482)" title="Capture of Berwick (1482)">retook it in 1482</a>. Berwick remains a traditional <a href="/wiki/Market_town" title="Market town">market town</a> and also has some notable architectural features, in particular its medieval <a href="/wiki/Berwick_town_walls" title="Berwick town walls">town walls</a>, its Elizabethan <a href="/wiki/Defensive_wall" title="Defensive wall">ramparts</a> and Britain's earliest <a href="/wiki/Berwick_Barracks" title="Berwick Barracks">barracks</a> buildings (1717–21 by <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Hawksmoor" title="Nicholas Hawksmoor">Nicholas Hawksmoor</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Ordnance" title="Board of Ordnance">Board of Ordnance</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berwick-upon-Tweed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name "Berwick" is of <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> origin, and is derived from the term <i>bere-wīc</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> combining <i>bere</i>, meaning "<a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>", and <i>wīc</i>, referring to a farm or settlement. "Berwick" thus means "barley village" or "barley farm".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berwick-upon-Tweed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berwick-upon-Tweed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the post-Roman period, the area was inhabited by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brython" title="Brython">Brythons</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bryneich" title="Bryneich">Bryneich</a>. Later, the region became part of the <a href="/wiki/Angles" title="Angles">Anglian</a> kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Bernicia" title="Bernicia">Bernicia</a>. Bernicia later united with the kingdom of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deira_(kingdom)" title="Deira (kingdom)">Deira</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Northumbria" title="Kingdom of Northumbria">Northumbria</a>, which in the mid-10th century entered the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a> under <a href="/wiki/Eadred" title="Eadred">Eadred</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cannon474_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cannon474-10">[10]</a></sup> Berwick itself was founded as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon" title="Anglo-Saxon">Anglo-Saxon</a> settlement during the time of the kingdom of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northumbria" title="Northumbria">Northumbria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lepage_2011_272_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lepage_2011_272-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scottish_burgh">Scottish burgh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berwick-upon-Tweed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Scottish burgh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Between the late 10th and early 11th centuries, the land between the rivers <a href="/wiki/River_Forth" title="River Forth">Forth</a> and Tweed came under Scottish control, either through conquest or through <a href="/wiki/Cession" title="Cession">cession</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Berwick was made a <a href="/wiki/Royal_burgh" title="Royal burgh">royal burgh</a> in the reign of <a href="/wiki/David_I_of_Scotland" title="David I of Scotland">David I</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mint_(coin)" title="Mint (coin)">mint</a> was present in the town by 1153.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>While under Scottish control, Berwick was referred to as "South Berwick" in order to differentiate it from the town of <a href="/wiki/North_Berwick" title="North Berwick">North Berwick</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Lothian" title="East Lothian">East Lothian</a>, near Edinburgh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Berwick's strategic position on the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Scottish_border" title="Anglo-Scottish border">Anglo-Scottish border</a> during centuries of war between the two nations and its relatively great wealth led to a succession of raids, sieges and takeovers. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_I_of_Scotland" title="William I of Scotland">William I of Scotland</a> invaded and attempted to capture northern England in 1173-74.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> After his defeat, Berwick was ceded to <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II of England</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It was later sold back to William by <a href="/wiki/Richard_I_of_England" title="Richard I of England">Richard I of England</a> in order to raise funds for his <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusade</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Berwick had become a prosperous town by the middle of the 13th century. According to <a href="/wiki/William_Edington" title="William Edington">William Edington</a>, a bishop and chancellor of Scotland, Berwick was "so populous and of such commercial importance that it might rightly be called another <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a>, whose riches were the sea and the water its walls".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1291–92 Berwick was the site of <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I of England</a>'s arbitration in the <a href="/wiki/Competitors_for_the_Crown_of_Scotland" title="Competitors for the Crown of Scotland">contest for the Scottish crown</a> between <a href="/wiki/John_Balliol" title="John Balliol">John Balliol</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Bruce,_5th_Lord_of_Annandale" title="Robert Bruce, 5th Lord of Annandale">Robert Bruce, 5th Lord of Annandale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dunbar116_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-dunbar116-19">[19]</a></sup> The decision in favour of Balliol was pronounced in the Great Hall of <a href="/wiki/Berwick_Castle" title="Berwick Castle">Berwick Castle</a> on 17 November 1292.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dunbar116_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-dunbar116-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1296 England went to war with <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, with whom Scotland was in alliance. Balliol invaded England in response, sacking <a href="/wiki/Cumberland" title="Cumberland">Cumberland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Edward in turn invaded Scotland and <a href="/wiki/Capture_of_Berwick_(1296)" title="Capture of Berwick (1296)">captured Berwick</a>, destroying much of the town and massacring many of the inhabitants. Edward I went again to Berwick in August 1296 to <a href="/wiki/Ragman_Rolls" title="Ragman Rolls">receive formal homage</a> from some 2,000 Scottish nobles, after defeating the Scots at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dunbar_(1296)" title="Battle of Dunbar (1296)">Battle of Dunbar</a> in April and forcing John Balliol to abdicate at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kincardine_Castle,_Mearns&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kincardine Castle, Mearns (page does not exist)">Kincardine Castle</a> the following July. It was at this time that work began on building the town walls (and rebuilding the earlier Castle); these fortifications were complete by 1318 and subsequently improved under Scottish rule. An arm of <a href="/wiki/William_Wallace" title="William Wallace">William Wallace</a> was displayed at Berwick after his execution and quartering on 23 August 1305. In 1314 <a href="/wiki/Edward_II_of_England" title="Edward II of England">Edward II of England</a> mustered 25,000 men at Berwick, who later fought in (and lost) the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bannockburn" title="Battle of Bannockburn">Battle of Bannockburn</a>.</p>
<p>Between 1315 and 1318 Scottish armies, sometimes with the help of Flemish and German <a href="/wiki/Privateer" title="Privateer">privateers</a>, besieged and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blockaded" title="Blockaded">blockaded</a> the town, finally <a href="/wiki/Capture_of_Berwick_(1318)" title="Capture of Berwick (1318)">invading and capturing it in April 1318</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> England retook Berwick some time shortly after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Halidon_Hill" title="Battle of Halidon Hill">Battle of Halidon Hill</a> in 1333.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In October 1357 a treaty was signed at Berwick by which the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Scotland" title="Parliament of Scotland">Scottish estates</a> undertook to pay 100,000 marks as a ransom for <a href="/wiki/David_II_of_Scotland" title="David II of Scotland">David II of Scotland</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> who had been taken prisoner at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Neville%27s_Cross" title="Battle of Neville's Cross">Battle of Neville's Cross</a> on 17 October 1346.</p>
<p>In 1461 Berwick was ceded back to Scotland by <a href="/wiki/Margaret_of_Anjou" title="Margaret of Anjou">Margaret of Anjou</a> on behalf of her husband, <a href="/wiki/Henry_VI_of_England" title="Henry VI of England">Henry VI</a>, in return for help against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yorkists" title="Yorkists">Yorkists</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Wars_of_the_Roses" title="Wars of the Roses">Wars of the Roses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Robert Lauder of <a href="/wiki/Edrington" title="Edrington">Edrington</a> was put in charge of the castle. He was succeeded in 1474 by <a href="/wiki/David_Lindsay,_1st_Duke_of_Montrose" title="David Lindsay, 1st Duke of Montrose">David, Earl of Crawford</a>. On 3 February 1478, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Lauder_of_The_Bass" title="Robert Lauder of The Bass">Robert Lauder of The Bass</a> and Edrington was again appointed Keeper of the castle, a position that he held until the final year of Scottish occupation, when <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Hepburn" title="Patrick Hepburn">Patrick Hepburn</a>, 1st Lord Hailes, had possession.</p>
<p>Berwick had a mediaeval hospital for the sick and poor which was administered by the Church. A charter under the <a href="/wiki/Great_Seal_of_Scotland" title="Great Seal of Scotland">Great Seal of Scotland</a>, confirmed by King <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_Scotland" title="James I of Scotland">James I of Scotland</a>, grants the king's chaplain "<a href="/wiki/Thomas_Lauder" title="Thomas Lauder">Thomas Lauder</a> of the House of God or Hospital lying in the burgh of Berwick-upon-Tweed, to be held to him for the whole time of his life with all lands, teinds, rents and profits, etc., belonging to the said hospital, as freely as is granted to any other hospital in the Kingdom of Scotland; the king also commands all those concerned to pay to the grantee all things necessary for the support of the hospital. Dated at Edinburgh June 8, in the 20th year of his reign."</p>
<p>In 1482 Richard, <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Gloucester" title="Duke of Gloucester">Duke of Gloucester</a> (later <a href="/wiki/Richard_III_of_England" title="Richard III of England">Richard III</a>) <a href="/wiki/Capture_of_Berwick_(1482)" title="Capture of Berwick (1482)">recaptured the town</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Over the course of a little more than 400 years, Berwick had changed hands more than a dozen times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPevsnerRichmondGrundy1992173_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPevsnerRichmondGrundy1992173-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English_town">English town</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berwick-upon-Tweed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: English town">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 1551 the town was made a <a href="/wiki/County_corporate" title="County corporate">county corporate</a>. During the reign of Queen <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I of England</a>, vast sums – one source reports "£128,648, the most expensive undertaking of the Elizabethan period"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> – were spent on its fortifications, in a new Italian style (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_fort" title="Star fort">trace italienne</a></i>), designed both to withstand artillery and to facilitate its use from within the fortifications. These fortifications have been described as "the only surviving walls of their kind".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cannon474_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cannon474-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lee_(engineer)" title="Richard Lee (engineer)">Sir Richard Lee</a> designed some of the Elizabethan works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1639 the army of <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a> faced that of General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Leslie" title="Alexander Leslie">Alexander Leslie</a> at Berwick in the <a href="/wiki/Bishops%27_Wars" title="Bishops' Wars">Bishops' Wars</a>, which were concerned with bringing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presbyterian" title="Presbyterian">Presbyterian</a> <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scotland" title="Church of Scotland">Church of Scotland</a> under Charles's control. The two sides did not fight, but negotiated a settlement, "Pacification of Berwick", in June.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Holy Trinity Church was built in 1648–52;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> a rare example of a church being built in the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_England" title="Commonwealth of England">Commonwealth period</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_town">British town</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berwick-upon-Tweed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: British town">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In 1707, the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Act of Union</a> between England and Scotland largely ended the contention about which of the countries Berwick belonged to. Since then, Berwick remained within the laws and legal system of <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Wales_and_Berwick_Act_1746" title="Wales and Berwick Act 1746">Wales and Berwick Act 1746</a> (since repealed) deemed that whenever legislation referred to England it applied to Berwick. England now is officially defined as "subject to any alteration of boundaries under Part IV of the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a>, the area consisting of the counties established by section 1 of that Act, Greater London and the Isles of Scilly.",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> which thus includes Berwick.</p>
<p>Berwick remained a county in its own right, and was not included in <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a> for Parliamentary purposes until 1885. In the same year, the <a href="/wiki/Redistribution_of_Seats_Act_1885" title="Redistribution of Seats Act 1885">Redistribution of Seats Act</a> reduced the number of Members of Parliament (MPs) returned by the town from two to one.</p>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">reorganisation of English local government on 1 April 1974</a> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berwick-upon-Tweed_(borough)" title="Berwick-upon-Tweed (borough)">Borough of Berwick-upon-Tweed</a> was created by the merger of the previous borough of Berwick-upon-Tweed with Belford Rural District, Glendale Rural District and <a href="/wiki/Norham_and_Islandshires_Rural_District" title="Norham and Islandshires Rural District">Norham and Islandshires Rural District</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Interpretation_Act_1978" title="Interpretation Act 1978">Interpretation Act 1978</a> provides that in legislation passed between 1967 and 1974, "a reference to England includes Berwick upon Tweed and <a href="/wiki/Monmouthshire_(historic)" title="Monmouthshire (historic)">Monmouthshire</a>".</p>
<p>In 2008 <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_Scottish_Parliament" title="Member of Scottish Parliament">Member of Scottish Parliament</a> (MSP) <a href="/wiki/Christine_Grahame" title="Christine Grahame">Christine Grahame</a> made calls in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a> for Berwick to become part of Scotland again, saying, "Even the Berwick-upon-Tweed Borough Council leader, who is a Liberal Democrat, backs the idea and others see the merits of reunification with Scotland."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The Liberal Democrat MSP <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Purvis" title="Jeremy Purvis">Jeremy Purvis</a>, who was born and brought up in Berwick, also asked for the border to be moved twenty miles south (i.e., a significant distance south of the Tweed) to include Berwick borough council rather than just the town. stating: "There’s a strong feeling that Berwick should be in Scotland. Until recently, I had a gran in Berwick and another in <a href="/wiki/Kelso,_Scottish_Borders" title="Kelso, Scottish Borders">Kelso</a>, and they could see that there were better public services in Scotland. Berwick as a borough council is going to be abolished and it would then be run from <a href="/wiki/Morpeth,_Northumberland" title="Morpeth, Northumberland">Morpeth</a>, more than 30 miles away."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Beith" title="Alan Beith">Alan Beith</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats" title="Liberal Democrats">Liberal Democrat</a> MP for Berwick, said the move would require a massive legal upheaval and is not realistic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The issue was set to be the centre of a new BBC comedy-drama series, <i>A Free Country</i>, commissioned in 2008 from writer <a href="/wiki/Tony_Saint" title="Tony Saint">Tony Saint</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> but to date this project has vanished from view.</p>
<p>In 2009 the Borough of Berwick-upon-Tweed was abolished as part of <a href="/wiki/2009_structural_changes_to_local_government_in_England" title="2009 structural changes to local government in England">wider structural changes to local government in England</a>. All functions previously exercised by Berwick Borough Council were transferred to <a href="/wiki/Northumberland_County_Council" title="Northumberland County Council">Northumberland County Council</a>, which is the <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">unitary authority</a> for the area.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berwick-upon-Tweed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Berwick-upon-Tweed has a typical British <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">marine climate</a> with narrow temperature differences between seasons. Because of its far northern position in England coupled with considerable <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> influence, the area has very cool summers for an English location, with a subdued July (1981-2010) high of 17.9 °C (64.2 °F). January in turn has a high of 6.8 °C (44.2 °F) with a low of 1.7 °C (35.1 °F) with occasional frosts averaging 38.1 times per annum. Precipitation is relatively low by British standards, with 589.2 millimetres (23.20 in) on average. Sunshine is still limited to 1508.5 hours per annum. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berwick-upon-Tweed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Berwick_Town_Hall_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3780" data-file-width="2848" height="292" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Berwick_Town_Hall_2.jpg/220px-Berwick_Town_Hall_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Berwick_Town_Hall_2.jpg/330px-Berwick_Town_Hall_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Berwick_Town_Hall_2.jpg/440px-Berwick_Town_Hall_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Berwick_Town_Hall_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Town Hall, built 1754–60</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During periods of Scottish administration Berwick was the <a href="/wiki/County_town" title="County town">county town</a> of <a href="/wiki/Berwickshire" title="Berwickshire">Berwickshire</a>, to which the town gave its name. Thus at various points in the Middle Ages and from 1482 (when Berwick became administered by England) Berwickshire had the unique distinction of being the only county in the British Isles to be named after a town in another country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>The town of Berwick was a <a href="/wiki/County_corporate" title="County corporate">county corporate</a> for most purposes from 1482, up until 1885, when it was fully incorporated into <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>. Between 1885, and 1974, Berwick (north of the Tweed) was a borough council in its own right, and then on 1 April 1974 it was merged with Belford Rural District, Glendale Rural District and <a href="/wiki/Norham_and_Islandshires_Rural_District" title="Norham and Islandshires Rural District">Norham and Islandshires Rural District</a>.</p>
<p>During these periods, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berwick-upon-Tweed_(borough)" title="Berwick-upon-Tweed (borough)">Berwick Borough Council</a> and Berwickshire County Council (or District Council) existed, both named after the same town, but covering entirely different areas.</p>
<p>The Borough of Berwick-upon-Tweed was abolished on 1 April 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> From that date, <a href="/wiki/Northumberland_County_Council" title="Northumberland County Council">Northumberland County Council</a> assumed its functions, and those of the other districts in its area, to become a unitary authority.</p>
<p>A new Berwick-upon-Tweed Town Council, a <a href="/wiki/Parish_councils_in_England" title="Parish councils in England">parish council</a>, has been created covering Berwick-upon-Tweed, Tweedmouth and Spittal. It is expected to take over the former Borough's mayoralty and regalia.</p>
<p>Berwick-upon-Tweed is in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" title="British House of Commons">parliamentary</a> constituency of <a href="/wiki/Berwick-upon-Tweed_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Berwick-upon-Tweed (UK Parliament constituency)">Berwick-upon-Tweed</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berwick-upon-Tweed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Berwick_Town_In_Winter.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Berwick_Town_In_Winter.jpg/220px-Berwick_Town_In_Winter.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Berwick_Town_In_Winter.jpg/330px-Berwick_Town_In_Winter.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Berwick_Town_In_Winter.jpg/440px-Berwick_Town_In_Winter.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Berwick_Town_In_Winter.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
High Street</div>

</div>
<p>Slightly more than 60% of the population is employed in the service sector, including shops, hotels and catering, financial services and most government activity, including health care. About 13% is in manufacturing, 10% in agriculture, and 8% in construction.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some current and recent Berwick economic activities include <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> fishing, shipbuilding, engineering, <a href="/wiki/Sawmill" title="Sawmill">sawmilling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a> production, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malting" title="Malting">malting</a> and the manufacture of tweed and hosiery.</p>
<p>Berwick town centre comprises the Mary Gate and High Street where many local shops and some retail chains exist. There is a B&amp;M which replaced the Co-Operative. A new office development has been built in the Walker Gate beside the library which combined spaced with the Northumberland Adult Learning Centre and Tourism centre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is a retail park in Tweed mouth consisting of a Home Base, Farm Foods, Marks and Spencer, Argo's, Next, Carpet Right, Curry's PC World, Halfords, and the newly opened Pound Land. Berwick Borough Council refused a proposal from <a href="/wiki/Asda" title="Asda">Asda</a> in 2006 to build a store near the site,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> but in 2008 gave <a href="/wiki/Tesco" title="Tesco">Tesco</a> planning permission for its new store in the town,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> which opened on 13 September 2010. Asda went on to take over the Co-op shop unit in Tweedmouth early 2010. A <a href="/wiki/Morrisons" title="Morrisons">Morrisons</a> supermarket with a petrol station, alongside a branch of <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald's">McDonald's</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Travelodge_UK" title="Travelodge UK">Travelodge UK</a> and an Aldi all exist on Loaning Meadows close to the outskirts of the town near the current A1.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berwick-upon-Tweed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumbcaption">

Berwick breakwater Lighthouse</div>

<p>The old <a href="/wiki/A1_road_(Great_Britain)" title="A1 road (Great Britain)">A1 road</a> passes through Berwick. The modern A1 goes around the town to the west. The town is on the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_Main_Line" title="East Coast Main Line">East Coast Main Line</a> railway, and has a <a href="/wiki/Berwick-upon-Tweed_railway_station" title="Berwick-upon-Tweed railway station">railway station</a>. A small seaport at Tweedmouth facilitates the import and export of goods, but provides no passenger services. The port is protected by a long breakwater built in the 19th century, at the end of which is a red and white <a href="/wiki/Lighthouse" title="Lighthouse">lighthouse</a>. Completed in 1826, the 13 metres (43 ft) tower emits a white light every five seconds from a window overlooking the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rowlett_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-rowlett-43">[43]</a></sup> Seafarers' charity, <a href="/wiki/Apostleship_of_the_Sea" title="Apostleship of the Sea">Apostleship of the Sea</a> has a chaplain to support the needs of mariners arriving at the port.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berwick-upon-Tweed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Berwick_dialect">Berwick dialect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berwick-upon-Tweed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Berwick dialect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The local speech of Berwick-upon-Tweed shares many characteristics with both other rural <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a> dialects and East <a href="/wiki/Central_Scots" title="Central Scots">Central Scots</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> In 1892, <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguist</a> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Oliver_Heslop" title="Richard Oliver Heslop">Richard Oliver Heslop</a> divided the county of Northumberland into four dialect zones and placed the Berwick dialect in the "north-Northumbrian" region, an area extending from Berwick down to the <a href="/wiki/River_Coquet" title="River Coquet">River Coquet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Likewise, Charles Jones (1997) classes the dialect as "predominantly North-Northumbrian" with "a few features shared with <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Features of this dialect include the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northumbrian_Burr" title="Northumbrian Burr">Northumbrian Burr</a>", a distinct pronunciation of the letter <a href="/wiki/R" title="R">R</a> historically common to many dialects of <a href="/wiki/North_East_England" title="North East England">North East England</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> and predominant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhotic_and_non-rhotic_accents" title="Rhotic and non-rhotic accents">non-rhoticity</a>: older speakers tend to be slightly rhotic, while younger speakers are universally non-rhotic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>A sociological study of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English-Scottish_border" title="English-Scottish border">English-Scottish border</a> region conducted in 2000 found that locals of <a href="/wiki/Alnwick" title="Alnwick">Alnwick</a>, 30 miles (48 km) south of Berwick, associated the Berwick accent with Scottish influence. Conversely, those from <a href="/wiki/Eyemouth" title="Eyemouth">Eyemouth</a>, Scotland, 9 miles (14 km) north of Berwick, firmly classed Berwick speech as English, identifying it as "Northumbrian or <a href="/wiki/Geordie" title="Geordie">Geordie</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berwick-upon-Tweed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Berwick_Rangers_F.C." title="Berwick Rangers F.C.">Berwick Rangers Football Club</a> were formed in the town in 1881.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Despite being located in England, the club plays in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_football_league_system" title="Scottish football league system">Scottish football league system</a>. The home stadium of Berwick Rangers is <a href="/wiki/Shielfield_Park" title="Shielfield Park">Shielfield Park</a> and the club currently plays in <a href="/wiki/Scottish_League_Two" title="Scottish League Two">Scottish League Two</a>, the fourth tier of the Scottish football league system.</p>
<p>The town also has a <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby union</a> side, <a href="/wiki/Berwick_RFC" title="Berwick RFC">Berwick RFC</a> who play in <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Rugby_Union" title="Scottish Rugby Union">Scottish Rugby Union</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Regional_Leagues_(Rugby_Union)" title="East Regional Leagues (Rugby Union)">East Regional League Division 1</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_speedway" title="Motorcycle speedway">Speedway</a> has taken place in Berwick in two separate eras. The sport was introduced to Shielfield Park in May 1968. A dispute between the speedway club and the stadium owners ended the first spell. The sport returned to Shielfield Park in the mid-1990s. The lack of a venue in the town saw the team move to a rural location called Berrington Lough. The team, known as the <a href="/wiki/Berwick_Bandits" title="Berwick Bandits">Bandits</a>, have raced at all levels from First Division to Conference League (first to third levels).</p>
<p>Berwick Rangers and Berwick RFC are unique in being English teams that play in Scottish leagues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relations_with_Russia">Relations with Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berwick-upon-Tweed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Relations with Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is an <a href="/wiki/Apocrypha" title="Apocrypha">apocryphal</a> story that Berwick is (or recently was) technically at war with <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The story tells that since Berwick had changed hands several times, it was traditionally regarded as a special, separate entity, and some proclamations referred to "England, Scotland and the town of Berwick-upon-Tweed". One such was the declaration of the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_War" title="Crimean War">Crimean War</a> against Russia in 1853, which <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> supposedly signed as "Victoria, Queen of Great Britain, Ireland, Berwick-upon-Tweed and all British Dominions". When the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1856)" title="Treaty of Paris (1856)">Treaty of Paris</a> was signed to conclude the war, "Berwick-upon-Tweed" was left out. This meant that, supposedly, one of Britain's smallest towns was officially at war with one of the world's largest powers – and the conflict <a href="/wiki/List_of_wars_extended_by_diplomatic_irregularity" title="List of wars extended by diplomatic irregularity">extended by the lack of a peace treaty</a> for over a century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Culture_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Culture-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> programme <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nationwide_(TV_series)" title="Nationwide (TV series)">Nationwide</a></i> investigated this story in the 1970s, and found that while Berwick was not mentioned in the Treaty of Paris, it was not mentioned in the declaration of war either. The question remained as to whether Berwick had ever been at war with Russia in the first place. The true situation is that since the <a href="/wiki/Wales_and_Berwick_Act_1746" title="Wales and Berwick Act 1746">Wales and Berwick Act 1746</a> had already made it clear that all references to England included Berwick, the town had no special status at either the start or end of the war. The grain of truth in this legend could be that some important documents from the 17th century did mention Berwick separately, but this became unnecessary after 1746.</p>
<p>According to a story by George Hawthorne in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> of 28 December 1966, the London correspondent of <i><a href="/wiki/Pravda" title="Pravda">Pravda</a></i> visited the Mayor of Berwick, Councillor <a href="/wiki/Robert_Knox_(politician)" title="Robert Knox (politician)">Robert Knox</a>, and the two made a mutual declaration of peace. Knox said "Please tell the Russian people through your newspaper that they can sleep peacefully in their beds." The same story, cited to the <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">Associated Press</a>, appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Baltimore_Sun" title="The Baltimore Sun">The Baltimore Sun</a></i> of 17 December 1966; <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> of 18 December 1966; and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Christian_Science_Monitor" title="The Christian Science Monitor">The Christian Science Monitor</a></i> of 22 December 1966. At some point in turn the real events seem to have been turned into a story of a "Soviet official" having signed a "peace treaty" with Mayor Knox; Knox's remark to the <i>Pravda</i> correspondent was preserved in this version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Culture_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-Culture-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>

<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Berwick_Castle" title="Berwick Castle">Berwick Castle</a> was built in the 13th century and rebuilt in the 1290s. It was in disrepair by the 17th century and much of it was demolished in the 19th century to make way for the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_Main_Line" title="East Coast Main Line">railway</a>. However, substantial ruins remain, just outside the town's rampart walls to the west by the river.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Berwick_town_walls" title="Berwick town walls">Berwick town walls</a> and Tudor ramparts – some of the finest remaining examples of their type in the country.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Berwick_Bridge" title="Berwick Bridge">Old Bridge</a>, 15-span <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> arch bridge 1,164 feet (355 m) long, built in 1610–24 for £15,000. The bridge continues to carry road traffic, but in one direction only. The bridge, part of the main route from <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> to <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> was ordered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_VI_of_Scotland" title="James VI of Scotland">James VI of Scotland</a>.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Holy_Trinity,_Berwick-on-Tweed" title="Church of the Holy Trinity, Berwick-on-Tweed">Holy Trinity Parish Church</a>, unusual for having been built during the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_England" title="Commonwealth of England">Commonwealth of England</a>. It was built in 1648–52 with stone from the 13th-century castle. It was originally a plain "preaching box", with no steeple, stained glass or other decorations. Contents include a pulpit thought to have been built for John Knox during his stay in the town. The church was much altered in 1855 with many new windows and the addition of a <a href="/wiki/Chancel" title="Chancel">chancel</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Berwick_Barracks" title="Berwick Barracks">Berwick Barracks</a>, built 1717–21, the design attributed to <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Hawksmoor" title="Nicholas Hawksmoor">Nicholas Hawksmoor</a>.</li>
<li>The Town Hall, designed by S&amp;J Worrell and built in 1754–60. The building is <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">neoclassical</a> and originally the town's prison was on the top floor. The tower above the council chamber has a <a href="/wiki/Change_ringing" title="Change ringing">ring of eight bells</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Curfew_bell" title="Curfew bell">curfew bell</a>. Lester and Pack of the <a href="/wiki/Whitechapel_Bell_Foundry" title="Whitechapel Bell Foundry">Whitechapel Bell Foundry</a> cast the tenor, third, fourth and treble bells in 1754 and the fifth and sixth bells in 1759. Charles Carr of <a href="/wiki/Smethwick" title="Smethwick">Smethwick</a> cast the second and curfew bells in 1894. Mears and Stainbank of the Whitechapel Bell Foundry cast the seventh bell in 1901.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></li>
<li>Dewars Lane Granary, built in 1769, now restored as a hotel and art gallery <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marshall_Meadows_Country_House_Hotel" title="Marshall Meadows Country House Hotel">Marshall Meadows Country House Hotel</a>, built in 1780 as a <a href="/wiki/English_country_house" title="English country house">country house</a>, is north of the town. It is the most northern hotel in England, just 275 metres from the Scottish border at <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Meadows_Bay" title="Marshall Meadows Bay">Marshall Meadows Bay</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Union_Bridge_(Tweed)" title="Union Bridge (Tweed)">Union Bridge</a>, 5 miles (8 km) upstream, from Berwick, was built in 1821 and is the World's oldest surviving suspension bridge.</li>
<li>The Kings Arms Hotel on Hide Hill was built in 1782 and rebuilt in 1845. <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a> stayed there in 1861.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Border_Bridge" title="Royal Border Bridge">Royal Border Bridge</a>, designed by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stephenson" title="Robert Stephenson">Robert Stephenson</a> and built in 1847–50 at a cost of £253,000, is a 720-yard-long <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railway" title="Railway">railway</a> <a href="/wiki/Viaduct" title="Viaduct">viaduct</a> with 28 arches, carrying the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_Main_Line" title="East Coast Main Line">East Coast Main Line</a> 126 feet above the River Tweed. It was opened by <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a>.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>St Andrew's Church, Wallace Green was built in 1859 and is one of only eight <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scotland" title="Church of Scotland">Church of Scotland</a> congregations in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.</li>
<li>The Masonic Hall was built in 1872 for the town's St David's <a href="/wiki/Freemasonry" title="Freemasonry">Masonic Lodge</a> for £1,800. The lodge still owns the hall, and is also used by other Masonic lodges and orders. It is one of very few purpose-built Masonic halls in the country and is a very rare example of Victorian Masonic architecture. It has a large pipe organ built in 1895. The Hall contains many artefacts and documents concerning Freemasonry in the town which can be traced back to 1643.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Tweed_Bridge" title="Royal Tweed Bridge">Royal Tweed Bridge</a>, built in 1925 to carry the <a href="/wiki/A1_road_(Great_Britain)" title="A1 road (Great Britain)">A1 road</a> across the Tweed. Its span is 361 feet (110 m), which at the time was the longest concrete span. The A1 now bypasses the town to the west. In the early 2000s the bridge was renovated, the road and pavement layout revised and new street lighting added.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dewars_Lane" title="Dewars Lane">Dewars Lane</a> runs down Back Street just off Bridge Street. Like other Berwick locations it was painted by <a href="/wiki/L._S._Lowry" title="L. S. Lowry">L. S. Lowry</a>, who visited Berwick often, especially in the 1930s, when he stayed at the Castle Hotel.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Torben_Betts" title="Torben Betts">Torben Betts</a>, award-winning playwright, lives in Berwick</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/James_Cockburn_(politician)" title="James Cockburn (politician)">James Cockburn</a>, first speaker of the Canadian House of Commons, born in Berwick</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/George_Frederick_Cooke" title="George Frederick Cooke">George Frederick Cooke</a> (1756–1811), widely called the first Romantic actor in England</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anne_Hepple_Dickinson" title="Anne Hepple Dickinson">Anne Hepple Dickinson</a> (1877 – 1959), writer</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/George_Johnston_(naturalist)" title="George Johnston (naturalist)">George Johnston</a> (1797 - 1855), influential naturalist in the 1800s.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Robert_Lee_(minister)" title="Robert Lee (minister)">Rev Prof Robert Lee DD</a> (1804-1868), raised in Tweedmouth</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alan_Martin_(writer)" title="Alan Martin (writer)">Alan Martin</a>, co-creator of the comic and movie character <a href="/wiki/Tank_Girl" title="Tank Girl">Tank Girl</a>, lives in Berwick</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Purvis" title="Jeremy Purvis">Jeremy Purvis</a>, Liberal Democrat MSP, and youngest person in <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a> at time of election</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/James_Redpath" title="James Redpath">James Redpath</a> American anti-slavery activist and journalist, born in Berwick</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ian_Sarfas" title="Ian Sarfas">Ian Sarfas</a>, English darts player played in the 1994 BDO World Darts Championships</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Smith_(English_soldier)" title="Thomas Smith (English soldier)">Thomas Smith</a>, soldier and writer</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Trevor_Steven" title="Trevor Steven">Trevor Steven</a>, England and Everton footballer, born in Berwick</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stevenson" title="Joseph Stevenson">Joseph Stevenson</a>, prominent English Catholic archivist and Society of Jesus priest, born in Berwick</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Patrick_Tonyn" title="Patrick Tonyn">Patrick Tonyn</a>, born in Berwick in 1725, a military general and Governor of British <a href="/wiki/East_Florida" title="East Florida">East Florida</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Henry_Travers" title="Henry Travers">Henry Travers</a>, grew up in Berwick (although actually born in Prudhoe), was a character actor, most famously as Clarence the angel in <i><a href="/wiki/It%27s_a_Wonderful_Life" title="It's a Wonderful Life">It's a Wonderful Life</a></i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eric_Lomax" title="Eric Lomax">Eric Lomax</a>, the author of <i>The Railway Man</i> lived in Berwick-upon-Tweed</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lucy_Bronze" title="Lucy Bronze">Lucy Bronze</a>, footballer for Manchester City Women and England, born in Berwick-upon-Tweed</li>
<li>Michael Johnson. (b1954) Sculptor was born in Berwick-upon-Tweed</li>
</ul>
