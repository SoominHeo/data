<p>The "<b>51st state</b>", in post-1959 <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_the_United_States" title="Politics of the United States">American political discourse</a>, is a phrase that refers to areas or locales that are – seriously or facetiously – considered candidates for <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. statehood</a>, joining the 50 states that presently compose the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The phrase has been applied to external territories as well as parts of existing states which would be admitted as separate states in their own right.</p>
<p>The phrase "51st state" can be used in a positive sense, meaning that a region or territory is so aligned, supportive, and conducive with the United States, that it is like a U.S. state. It can also be used in a <a href="/wiki/Pejorative" title="Pejorative">pejorative</a> sense, meaning an area or region is perceived to be under excessive American cultural or military influence or control. In various countries around the world, people who believe their local or national culture has become too <a href="/wiki/Americanization" title="Americanization">Americanized</a> sometimes use the term "51st state" in reference to their own countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_requirements">Legal requirements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Legal requirements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Under <a href="/wiki/Article_Four_of_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Article Four of the United States Constitution">Article IV</a>, Section Three of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a>, which outlines the relationship among the states, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> has the power to admit new states to the union. The states are required to give "<a href="/wiki/Full_Faith_and_Credit_Clause" title="Full Faith and Credit Clause">full faith and credit</a>" to the acts of each other's <a href="/wiki/State_legislature_(United_States)" title="State legislature (United States)">legislatures</a> and <a href="/wiki/Court" title="Court">courts</a>, which is generally held to include the recognition of legal contracts, marriages, and criminal judgments. The states are guaranteed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defense_(military)" title="Defense (military)">military</a> and <a href="/wiki/Civil_defense" title="Civil defense">civil defense</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">federal government</a>, which is also obliged by Article IV, Section Four, to "guarantee to every state in this union a republican form of government".</p>
<p>Congress is a highly politicized body, and discussions about the admission of new states, which typically take years before approval, are invariably informed by the political concerns of Congress at the time the proposal is presented. These concerns include or included maintaining a balance between free and slave states, and which faction in Congress (Democrats or Republicans, conservatives or liberals, rural or urban blocks) would benefit, and which lose, if the proposed state were admitted.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_new_states">Possible new states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Possible new states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_status_changes_of_current_U.S._territories">By status changes of current U.S. territories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: By status changes of current U.S. territories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Puerto_Rico">Puerto Rico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Puerto Rico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Proposed_political_status_for_Puerto_Rico" title="Proposed political status for Puerto Rico">Proposed political status for Puerto Rico</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Statehood_movement_in_Puerto_Rico" title="Statehood movement in Puerto Rico">Statehood movement in Puerto Rico</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> has been discussed as a potential 51st state of the United States. In a <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_status_referendum,_2012" title="Puerto Rican status referendum, 2012">2012 status referendum</a> a majority of voters, 54%, expressed dissatisfaction with the current political relationship. In a separate question, 61% of voters supported statehood (excluding the 26% of voters who left this question blank). On December 11, 2012, Puerto Rico's legislature resolved to request that the President and the U.S. Congress act on the results, end the current form of territorial status and begin the process of admitting Puerto Rico to the Union as a state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> On January 4th, 2017, Puerto Rico's new representative to Congress pushed a bill that would turn the island to the 51st state by 2025. <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.newser.com/story/236427/bill-seeks-to-make-puerto-rico-51st-state-by-2025.html" rel="nofollow">[1]</a></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Since 1898, Puerto Rico has had limited representation in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Resident_Commissioner_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Resident Commissioner of Puerto Rico">Resident Commissioner</a>, a nonvoting delegate. The <a href="/wiki/110th_United_States_Congress" title="110th United States Congress">110th Congress</a> returned the Commissioner's power to vote in the <a href="/wiki/Committee_of_the_Whole_(United_States_House_of_Representatives)" title="Committee of the Whole (United States House of Representatives)">Committee of the Whole</a>, but not on matters where the vote would represent a decisive participation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhg_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-rhg-3">[3]</a></sup> Puerto Rico has elections on the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_primary" title="United States presidential primary">United States presidential primary</a> or caucus of the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican Party</a> to select delegates to the respective parties' <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_nominating_convention" title="United States presidential nominating convention">national conventions</a> although presidential electors are not granted on the <a href="/wiki/Electoral_College_(United_States)" title="Electoral College (United States)">Electoral College</a>. As American citizens, Puerto Ricans can vote in U.S. presidential elections, provided they reside in one of the 50 states or the District of Columbia and not in Puerto Rico itself.</p>
<p>Residents of Puerto Rico pay <a href="/wiki/Taxation_in_the_United_States" title="Taxation in the United States">U.S. federal taxes</a>: import/export taxes, federal commodity taxes, social security taxes, therefore contributing to the American Government. Most Puerto Rico residents do not pay <a href="/wiki/Income_tax_in_the_United_States" title="Income tax in the United States">federal income tax</a> but do pay federal <a href="/wiki/Payroll_tax" title="Payroll tax">payroll taxes</a> (<a href="/wiki/Social_Security_(United_States)" title="Social Security (United States)">Social Security</a> and <a href="/wiki/Medicare_(United_States)" title="Medicare (United States)">Medicare</a>). However, federal employees, those who do business with the federal government, Puerto Rico–based corporations that intend to send funds to the U.S. and others do pay federal income taxes. Puerto Ricans may enlist in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_armed_forces" title="United States armed forces">U.S. military</a>. Puerto Ricans have participated in all <a href="/wiki/List_of_wars_involving_the_United_States" title="List of wars involving the United States">American wars</a> since 1898; 52 Puerto Ricans had been killed in the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%9314)" title="War in Afghanistan (2001–14)">War in Afghanistan</a> by November 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Puerto Rico has been under U.S. sovereignty for over a century when it was ceded to the U.S. by Spain following the end of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">Spanish–American War</a>, and Puerto Ricans have been U.S. citizens since 1917. The island's ultimate status has not been determined as of 2012<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, its residents do not have voting representation in their federal government. Puerto Rico has limited representation in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Congress" title="U.S. Congress">U.S. Congress</a> in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Resident_Commissioner_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Resident Commissioner of Puerto Rico">Resident Commissioner</a>, a delegate with limited no voting rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhg_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-rhg-3">[3]</a></sup> Like the states, Puerto Rico has self-rule, a republican form of government organized pursuant to a constitution adopted by its people, and a <a href="/wiki/Bill_of_rights" title="Bill of rights">bill of rights</a>.</p>
<p>This constitution was created when the U.S. Congress directed local government to organize a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_convention_(political_meeting)" title="Constitutional convention (political meeting)">constitutional convention</a> to write the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Constitution" title="Puerto Rico Constitution">Puerto Rico Constitution</a> in 1951. The acceptance of that constitution by Puerto Rico's electorate, the U.S. Congress, and the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">U.S. president</a> occurred in 1952. In addition, the rights, privileges and immunities attendant to United States citizens are "respected in Puerto Rico to the same extent as though Puerto Rico were a state of the union" through the express extension of the <a href="/wiki/Privileges_and_Immunities_Clause" title="Privileges and Immunities Clause">Privileges and Immunities Clause</a> of the U.S. Constitution by the U.S. Congress in 1948.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Puerto Rico is designated in its constitution as the "Commonwealth of Puerto Rico".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Constitution of Puerto Rico">Constitution of Puerto Rico</a> which became effective in 1952 adopted the name of <i>Estado Libre Asociado</i> (literally translated as "Free Associated State"), officially translated into English as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commonwealth_(United_States_insular_area)" title="Commonwealth (United States insular area)">Commonwealth</a>, for its <a href="/wiki/Body_politic" title="Body politic">body politic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The island is under the jurisdiction of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Territorial_Clause" title="Territorial Clause">Territorial Clause</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_United_States" title="Constitution of the United States">U.S. Constitution</a>, which has led to doubts about the finality of the Commonwealth status for Puerto Rico. In addition, all people born in Puerto Rico become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural-born-citizen_clause_of_the_U.S._Constitution" title="Natural-born-citizen clause of the U.S. Constitution">citizens of the U.S. at birth</a> (under provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Jones%E2%80%93Shafroth_Act" title="Jones–Shafroth Act">Jones–Shafroth Act</a> in 1917), but citizens residing in Puerto Rico cannot vote for president nor for full members of either house of Congress. Statehood would grant island residents full voting rights at the Federal level. The <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Democracy_Act" title="Puerto Rico Democracy Act">Puerto Rico Democracy Act</a> (H.R. 2499) was approved on April 29, 2010, by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a> 223–169,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> but was not approved by the Senate before the end of the <a href="/wiki/111th_United_States_Congress" title="111th United States Congress">111th Congress</a>. It would have provided for a federally sanctioned self-determination process for the people of Puerto Rico. This act would provide for <a href="/wiki/Referendum" title="Referendum">referendums</a> to be held in Puerto Rico to determine <a href="/wiki/Political_status_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Political status of Puerto Rico">the island's ultimate political status</a>. It had also been introduced in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crs_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-crs-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Vote_for_statehood">Vote for statehood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vote for statehood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_status_referendum,_2012" title="Puerto Rican status referendum, 2012">November 2012</a>, a referendum resulted in 54 percent of respondents voting to reject the current status under the territorial clause of the U.S. Constitution,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-207.150.251.12_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-207.150.251.12-11">[11]</a></sup> while a second question resulted in 61 percent of voters identifying statehood as the preferred alternative to the current territorial status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OPCIONES_NO_TERRITORIALES_ISLA_PM_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-OPCIONES_NO_TERRITORIALES_ISLA_PM-12">[12]</a></sup> The 2012 referendum was by far the most successful referendum for statehood advocates and support for statehood has risen in each successive popular referendum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-letpuertoricodecide1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-letpuertoricodecide1-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> However, more than one in four voters abstained from answering the question on the preferred alternative status. Statehood opponents have argued that the statehood option garnered only 45 percent of the votes if abstentions are included.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> If abstentions are considered, the result of the referendum is much closer to 44 percent for statehood, a number that falls under the 50 percent majority mark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Post" title="Washington Post">Washington Post</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Boston_Herald" title="Boston Herald">Boston Herald</a></i> have published opinion pieces expressing support for the statehood of Puerto Rico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> On November 8, 2012, Washington, D.C. newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hill_(newspaper)" title="The Hill (newspaper)">The Hill</a></i> published an article saying that Congress will likely ignore the results of the referendum due to the circumstances behind the votes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and U.S. Congressman <a href="/wiki/Luis_Guti%C3%A9rrez" title="Luis Gutiérrez">Luis Gutiérrez</a> U.S. Congresswoman <a href="/wiki/Nydia_Vel%C3%A1zquez" title="Nydia Velázquez">Nydia Velázquez</a>, both of Puerto Rican ancestry, agreed with the <i>The Hill</i> 's statements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Shortly after the results were published Puerto Rico-born U.S. Congressman <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Enrique_Serrano" title="José Enrique Serrano">José Enrique Serrano</a> commented "I was particularly impressed with the outcome of the 'status' referendum in Puerto Rico. A majority of those voting signaled the desire to change the current territorial status. In a second question an even larger majority asked to become a state. This is an earthquake in Puerto Rican politics. It will demand the attention of Congress, and a definitive answer to the Puerto Rican request for change. This is a history-making moment where voters asked to move forward."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several days after the referendum, the <a href="/wiki/Resident_Commissioner_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Resident Commissioner of Puerto Rico">Resident Commissioner</a> <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Pierluisi" title="Pedro Pierluisi">Pedro Pierluisi</a>, Governor <a href="/wiki/Luis_Fortu%C3%B1o" title="Luis Fortuño">Luis Fortuño</a>, and Governor-elect <a href="/wiki/Alejandro_Garc%C3%ADa_Padilla" title="Alejandro García Padilla">Alejandro García Padilla</a> wrote separate letters to the President of the United States <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> addressing the results of the voting. Pierluisi urged Obama to begin legislation in favor of the statehood of Puerto Rico, in light of its win in the referendum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Fortuño urged him to move the process forward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> García Padilla asked him to reject the results because of their ambiguity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The White House stance related to the November 2012 plebiscite was that the results were clear, the people of Puerto Rico want the issue of status resolved, and a majority chose statehood in the second question. Former White House director of Hispanic media stated, "Now it is time for Congress to act and the administration will work with them on that effort, so that the people of Puerto Rico can determine their own future."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 15, 2013, Resident Commissioner Pierluisi introduced H.R. 2000 to Congress to "set forth the process for Puerto Rico to be admitted as a state of the Union," asking for Congress to vote on ratifying Puerto Rico as the 51st state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> On February 12, 2014, Senator <a href="/wiki/Martin_Heinrich" title="Martin Heinrich">Martin Heinrich</a> introduced a bill in the US Senate. The bill would require a binding referendum to be held in Puerto Rico asking whether the territory wants to be admitted as a state. In the event of a yes vote, the president would be asked to submit legislation to Congress to admit Puerto Rico as a state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_funds">Government funds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Government funds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>On January 15, 2014, the United States House of Representatives approved $2.5 million in funding to hold a referendum. This referendum can be held at any time as there is no deadline by which the funds have to be used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-U.S._approves_funds_for_referendum_on_Puerto_Rico.27s_status_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-U.S._approves_funds_for_referendum_on_Puerto_Rico.27s_status-29">[29]</a></sup> The United States Senate then passed the bill which was signed into law on January 17, 2014 by <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a>, the President of the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Make_room_for_51st_star.3F_Spending_bill_includes_.242.5_million_for_vote_on_Puerto_RIco_statehood_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Make_room_for_51st_star.3F_Spending_bill_includes_.242.5_million_for_vote_on_Puerto_RIco_statehood-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="District_of_Columbia">District of Columbia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: District of Columbia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia_statehood_movement" title="District of Columbia statehood movement">District of Columbia statehood movement</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia_retrocession" title="District of Columbia retrocession">District of Columbia retrocession</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> is often mentioned as a candidate for statehood. In Federalist No. 43 of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Federalist_Papers" title="The Federalist Papers">The Federalist Papers</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/James_Madison" title="James Madison">James Madison</a> considered the implications of the definition of the "seat of government" found in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a>. Although he noted potential conflicts of interest, and the need for a "municipal legislature for local purposes",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Madison did not address the district's role in national voting. Legal scholars disagree on whether a simple act of Congress can admit the District as a state, due to its status as the seat of government of the United States, which Article I, Section 8 of the Constitution requires to be under the exclusive jurisdiction of Congress; depending on the interpretation of this text, admission of the full District as a state may require a Constitutional amendment, which is much more difficult to enact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> However, the Constitution does not set a minimum size for the District. Its size has already changed once before, when Virginia <a href="/wiki/Arlington_County,_Virginia#Retrocession" title="Arlington County, Virginia">reclaimed</a> the portion of the District south of the Potomac. So the constitutional requirement for a federal district can be satisfied by reducing its size to the small central core of government buildings and monuments, giving the rest of the territory to the new state.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Washington, D.C. residents who support the statehood movement sometimes use a shortened version of the Revolutionary War protest motto "<a href="/wiki/No_taxation_without_representation" title="No taxation without representation">No taxation without representation</a>", omitting the initial "No", denoting their lack of Congressional representation; the phrase is now printed on newly issued Washington, D.C. license plates (although a driver may choose to have the District of Columbia website address instead). President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presidential_State_Car_(United_States)" title="Presidential State Car (United States)">presidential limousine</a> had the "Taxation without representation" license plate late in his term, while President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> had the vehicle's plates changed shortly after beginning his term in office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> had the license plates changed back to the protest style at the beginning of his second term.</p>
<p>This position was carried by the D.C. Statehood Party, a political party; it has since merged with the local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_Party_(United_States)" title="Green Party (United States)">Green Party</a> affiliate to form the <a href="/wiki/D.C._Statehood_Green_Party" title="D.C. Statehood Green Party">D.C. Statehood Green Party</a>. The nearest this movement ever came to success was in 1978, when Congress passed the <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia_Voting_Rights_Amendment" title="District of Columbia Voting Rights Amendment">District of Columbia Voting Rights Amendment</a>. Two years later in 1980, local citizens passed an <a href="/wiki/Initiative" title="Initiative">initiative</a> calling for a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_convention_(political_meeting)" title="Constitutional convention (political meeting)">constitutional convention</a> for a new state. In 1982, voters ratified the constitution of the state, which was to be called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D.C._statehood_movement" title="D.C. statehood movement">New Columbia</a>. The drive for statehood stalled in 1985, however, when the District of Columbia Voting Rights Amendment failed because not enough states <a href="/wiki/Ratification" title="Ratification">ratified</a> the amendment within the seven-year span specified.</p>
<p>Another proposed option would be to have <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, from which the current land was ceded, <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia_retrocession" title="District of Columbia retrocession">retake the District of Columbia</a>, as <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> has already done for its <a href="/wiki/Arlington_County,_Virginia" title="Arlington County, Virginia">part</a>, while leaving the <a href="/wiki/National_Mall" title="National Mall">National Mall</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">United States Capitol</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> in a truncated District of Columbia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-richards_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-richards-34">[34]</a></sup> This would give residents of the District of Columbia the benefit of statehood while precluding the creation of a 51st state. The requirement that Maryland consent to a change in its borders makes this unlikely.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="2016_statehood_referendum">2016 statehood referendum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2016 statehood referendum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia_statehood_referendum,_2016" title="District of Columbia statehood referendum, 2016">District of Columbia statehood referendum, 2016</a></div>
<p>On April 15, 2016, District Mayor <a href="/wiki/Muriel_Bowser" title="Muriel Bowser">Muriel Bowser</a> called for a citywide vote on whether the nation's capital should become the 51st state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> This was followed by the release of a proposed state constitution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> This constitution would make the <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_the_District_of_Columbia" title="Mayor of the District of Columbia">Mayor of the District of Columbia</a> the governor of the proposed state, while the members of the City Council would make up the proposed House of Delegates. While the name "New Columbia" has long been associated with the movement, community members thought other names, such as Potomac or Douglass, were more appropriate for the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>On November 8, 2016, the voters of the District of Columbia voted overwhelmingly in favor of statehood, with 86% of voters voting to advise approving the proposal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_status_changes_of_former_U.S._territories">By status changes of former U.S. territories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: By status changes of former U.S. territories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippines">Philippines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Philippines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> has had small grassroots movements for U.S. statehood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Originally part of the platform of the <a href="/wiki/Progresista_Party" title="Progresista Party">Progressive Party</a>, then known as the Federalista Party, the party dropped it in 1907, which coincided with the name change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-contstud_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-contstud-41">[41]</a></sup> In 1981, the presidential candidate for the <a href="/wiki/Federalist_Party_(Philippines)#Federalist_Party_of_the_Fourth_Republic" title="Federalist Party (Philippines)">Federal Party</a> ran on a platform of Philippine Statehood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> As recently as 2004, the concept of the Philippines becoming a U.S. state has been part of a political platform in the Philippines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Supporters of this movement include Filipinos who believe that the quality of life in the Philippines would be higher and that there would be less poverty there if the Philippines were an American state or territory. Supporters also include Filipinos that had fought as members of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">United States Armed Forces</a> in various wars during the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_the_Philippines" title="Commonwealth of the Philippines">Commonwealth period</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Philippine statehood movement had a significant impact during the early <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Philippines_(1898%E2%80%931946)" title="History of the Philippines (1898–1946)">American colonial period</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-contstud_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-contstud-41">[41]</a></sup> It is no longer a mainstream movement,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> but is a small social movement that gains interest and talk in that nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_partition_of_or_secession_from_current_U.S._states">By partition of or secession from current U.S. states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: By partition of or secession from current U.S. states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_U.S._state_partition_proposals" title="List of U.S. state partition proposals">List of U.S. state partition proposals</a></div>
<p>There exist several proposals to divide states with regions that are politically or culturally divergent into smaller, more homogeneous, administratively efficient entities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Splitting a state would need to receive the approval of its legislature and the Congress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Proposals of new states by partition include:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Arizona</b>: The secession of Pima County in <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> with the hopes of Cochise, Yuma, and Santa Cruz joining to form a state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Partition_and_secession_in_California" title="Partition and secession in California">California and Oregon</a></b>:
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jefferson_(Pacific_state)" title="Jefferson (Pacific state)">Jefferson</a>, from Northern California and Southern <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>.</li>
<li>Various proposals of <a href="/wiki/Partition_and_secession_in_California" title="Partition and secession in California">partition and secession in California</a>, usually involving splitting the south half from the north or the urban coastline from the rest of the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li>California's Secretary of State allowed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tim_Draper" title="Tim Draper">Tim Draper</a> to start collecting signatures for his petition to split <a href="/wiki/Six_Californias" title="Six Californias">California into six different states</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The initiative drive did not gain sufficient valid signatures to be put on the ballot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Northern_Colorado" title="Northern Colorado">Colorado</a></b>: On June 6, 2013, commissioners in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weld_County" title="Weld County">Weld County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a> announced a proposal to leave Colorado along with neighboring counties and form the state of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Colorado" title="Northern Colorado">North Colorado</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The counties in contention voted to begin plans for secession on November 5, 2013, with mixed results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Delaware, Maryland and Virginia</b>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delmarva" title="Delmarva">Delmarva</a>, from the eastern shores of <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> combining with the state of <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a>, or more often, only <a href="/wiki/Kent_County,_Delaware" title="Kent County, Delaware">Kent County</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sussex_County,_Delaware" title="Sussex County, Delaware">Sussex County, Delaware</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Florida</b>: The secession of <a href="/wiki/South_Florida" title="South Florida">South Florida</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Florida_metropolitan_area" title="South Florida metropolitan area">Greater Miami</a> area to form the state of "South Florida." South Florida has a population of over 7 million, comprising 41% of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>'s population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sun-sentinel.com_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-sun-sentinel.com-57">[57]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Illinois</b>: The secession of <a href="/wiki/Cook_County,_Illinois" title="Cook County, Illinois">Cook County</a>, which contains <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> to form a state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Chicago sits in the northeast corner of the state, with the remainder of the Illinois sometimes referred to as <a href="/wiki/Downstate_Illinois" title="Downstate Illinois">Downstate Illinois</a>. Such proposals have invariably come from the more Republican downstate Illinois, as a way to end the dominance in statewide politics of the overwhelmingly Democratic Chicago area. Stronger moves have been made to form Southern Illinois "The Great Rivers State". With the separation being south of Springfield, the capital would be in Mt. Vernon.</li>
<li><b>Maryland</b>: The secession of five counties on the western side of the state due to political differences with the more liberal central part of the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Superior_(proposed_U.S._state)" title="Superior (proposed U.S. state)">Michigan and Superior</a></b>: The northern part of Michigan known formally as the Upper Peninsula of Michigan, and colloquially known as Yooper Land, the Yooper Peninsula, the UP, or Superiorland. Note that the Upper Peninsula of Michigan is actually north of the region of the Lower Peninsula normally known in lower Michigan as "Northern Michigan", which tends to cause some confusion.</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secession_in_New_York" title="Secession in New York">New York</a></b>: Various proposals <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secession_in_New_York" title="Secession in New York">partitioning</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> into separate states, all of which involve to some degree <a href="/wiki/New_York_City:_the_51st_State" title="New York City: the 51st State">the separation of New York City</a> from the rest of New York state<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyc_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyc-60">[60]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Texas_divisionism" title="Texas divisionism">Texas</a></b>: Under the joint resolution of Congress by which the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Texas" title="Republic of Texas">Republic of Texas</a> was admitted to the Union, it had the right to divide itself into as many as five different states. It is not clear whether this provides any power beyond that already provided by the Constitution. What is clear is that the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Legislature" title="Texas Legislature">Texas Legislature</a> would have to approve any proposal to divide the state using this prerogative. There were a significant number of Texans who supported dividing the state in its early decades. They were generally called <i>divisionists</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The Texas Constitution and the Texas Annexation Act both provide for the possibility of Texas voting to divide into up to five sovereign States of the Union. Current Texas politics and self-image make any tampering with Texas' status as the largest state by land area in the contiguous United States unlikely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Washington</b>: Dividing the state into <a href="/wiki/Western_Washington" title="Western Washington">Western Washington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Washington" title="Eastern Washington">Eastern Washington</a> via the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cascade_Mountains" title="Cascade Mountains">Cascade Mountains</a>. Suggested names include East Washington, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lincoln_(Northwest_state)" title="Lincoln (Northwest state)">Lincoln</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cascadia_(independence_movement)" title="Cascadia (independence movement)">Cascadia</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/National_Movement_for_the_Establishment_of_a_49th_State" title="National Movement for the Establishment of a 49th State">National Movement for the Establishment of a 49th State</a>, founded by <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Brown,_Sr." title="Oscar Brown, Sr.">Oscar Brown, Sr.</a> and Bradley Cyrus and active in Chicago in 1934–37, had the aim of forming an African American state in the South.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aptheker1974_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aptheker1974-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_internationally">Use internationally</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Use internationally">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some countries, because of their cultural similarities and close alliances with the United States, are often described as a 51st state. In other countries around the world, movements with various degrees of support and seriousness have proposed U.S. statehood.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Annexation_movements_of_Canada" title="Annexation movements of Canada">Annexation movements of Canada</a></div>
<p>In Canada, "the 51st state" is a phrase generally used in such a way as to imply that if a certain political course is taken, Canada's destiny will be as little more than a part of the United States. Examples include the <a href="/wiki/Canada%E2%80%93United_States_Free_Trade_Agreement" title="Canada–United States Free Trade Agreement">Canada–United States Free Trade Agreement</a> in 1988, the debate over the creation of a <a href="/wiki/Fortress_North_America" title="Fortress North America">common defense perimeter</a>, and as a potential consequence of not adopting proposals intended to resolve the issue of <a href="/wiki/Quebec_sovereignty_movement" title="Quebec sovereignty movement">Quebec sovereignty</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Charlottetown_Accord" title="Charlottetown Accord">Charlottetown Accord</a> in 1992 and the <a href="/wiki/Clarity_Act" title="Clarity Act">Clarity Act</a> in 1999.</p>
<p>The phrase is usually used in local political debates, in <a href="/wiki/Polemic" title="Polemic">polemic</a> writing or in private conversations. It is rarely used by politicians themselves in a public context, although at certain times in Canadian history political parties have used other similarly loaded imagery. In the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_1988" title="Canadian federal election, 1988">1988 federal election</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Canada" title="Liberal Party of Canada">Liberals</a> asserted that the proposed Free Trade Agreement amounted to an American takeover of Canada<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>—notably, the party ran an ad in which <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Conservative_Party_of_Canada" title="Progressive Conservative Party of Canada">Progressive Conservative</a> (PC) strategists, upon the adoption of the agreement, slowly erased the Canada-U.S. border from a desktop map of North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Within days, however, the PCs responded with an ad which featured the border being drawn back <i>on</i> with a permanent marker, as an announcer intoned "Here's where we draw the line."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>The implication has historical basis and dates to the breakup of <a href="/wiki/British_America" title="British America">British America</a> during the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>. The colonies that had confederated to form the United States invaded Canada (at the time a term referring specifically to the modern-day provinces of <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, which had only been in British hands since <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1763)" title="Treaty of Paris (1763)">1763</a>) at least twice, neither time succeeding in taking control of the territory. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Canada_(1775)" title="Invasion of Canada (1775)">The first invasion</a> was during the Revolution, under the assumption that French-speaking Canadians' presumed hostility towards British colonial rule combined with the <a href="/wiki/Franco-American_alliance" title="Franco-American alliance">Franco-American alliance</a> would make them natural allies to the American cause; the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> successfully recruited <a href="/wiki/2nd_Canadian_Regiment" title="2nd Canadian Regiment">two Canadian regiments</a> for the invasion. That invasion's failure forced the members of those regiments into exile, and they settled mostly in <a href="/wiki/Upstate_New_York" title="Upstate New York">upstate New York</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Articles_of_Confederation" title="Articles of Confederation">Articles of Confederation</a>, written during the Revolution, included a provision for Canada to join the United States, should they ever decide to do so, without needing to seek U.S. permission as other states would.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> The United States again <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Canada_(1812)" title="Invasion of Canada (1812)">invaded Canada</a> during the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a>, but this effort was made more difficult due to the large number of <a href="/wiki/United_Empire_Loyalist" title="United Empire Loyalist">Loyalist</a> Americans that had fled to what is now Ontario and still resisted joining the republic. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hunter_Patriot" title="Hunter Patriot">Hunter Patriots</a> in the 1830s and the <a href="/wiki/Fenian_raids" title="Fenian raids">Fenian raids</a> after the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> were private attacks on Canada from the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> Several U.S. politicians in the 19th century also spoke in favour of annexing Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-J.L._Granatstein.2C_Norman_Hillmer_1991_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-J.L._Granatstein.2C_Norman_Hillmer_1991-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the late 1940s, during the last days of the <a href="/wiki/Dominion_of_Newfoundland" title="Dominion of Newfoundland">Dominion of Newfoundland</a> (at the time a dominion-dependency in the Commonwealth and independent of Canada), there was mainstream support, although not majority, for Newfoundland to form an economic union with the United States, thanks to the efforts of the <a href="/wiki/Economic_Union_Party" title="Economic Union Party">Economic Union Party</a> and significant U.S. investment in Newfoundland stemming from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_relationship" title="Special relationship">U.S.-British alliance</a> in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. The movement ultimately failed when, in a <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_referendums,_1948" title="Newfoundland referendums, 1948">1948 referendum</a>, voters narrowly chose to confederate with Canada (the Economic Union Party supported an independent "responsible government" that they would then push toward their goals).</p>
<p>A few groups in Canada have actively campaigned in favor of joining the United States. These <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annexationist_movements_of_Canada" title="Annexationist movements of Canada">annexationist movements</a> have not attracted large mainstream attention, although surveys have found that a small minority of Canadians expressed support for the concept in surveys done by <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9ger_Marketing" title="Léger Marketing">Léger Marketing</a> in 2001 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> and in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States, the term "the 51st state" when applied to Canada can serve to highlight the similarities and close relationship between the United States and Canada. Sometimes the term is used disparagingly, intended to deride Canada as an unimportant neighbor. In the <a href="/wiki/Quebec_general_election,_1989" title="Quebec general election, 1989">Quebec general election, 1989</a>, the political party <a href="/wiki/Parti_51" title="Parti 51">Parti 51</a> ran 11 candidates on a platform of <a href="/wiki/Quebec_sovereignty_movement" title="Quebec sovereignty movement">Quebec seceding from Canada</a> to join the United States (with its leader, André Perron, claiming Quebec could not survive as an independent nation).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> The party attracted just 3,846 votes across the province, 0.11% of the total votes cast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> In comparison, the other parties in favour of sovereignty of Quebec in that election got 40.16% (PQ) and 1.22% (NPDQ).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latin_America">Latin America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Latin America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_America">Central America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Central America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Due to geographical proximity of the Central American countries to the U.S. which has powerful military, economic, and political influences, there were several movements and proposals by the United States during the 19th and 20th centuries to annex some or all of the Central American republics (<a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a> with the formerly British-ruled Bay Islands, <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>, <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a> which had the U.S.-ruled <a href="/wiki/Panama_Canal_Zone" title="Panama Canal Zone">Canal Zone</a> territory from 1903 to 1979, and <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>, which is a constitutional monarchy and was known as <a href="/wiki/British_Honduras" title="British Honduras">British Honduras</a> until 1981). However, the U.S. never acted on these proposals from some U.S. politicians; some of which were never delivered or considered seriously. In 2001, El Salvador adopted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._dollar" title="U.S. dollar">U.S. dollar</a> as its currency, while Panama has used it for decades due to its ties to the Canal Zone.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuba">Cuba</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Cuba">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1854 the <a href="/wiki/Ostend_Manifesto" title="Ostend Manifesto">Ostend Manifesto</a> was written, outlining the rationale for the U.S. to purchase Cuba from Spain, implying taking the island by force if Spain refused. Once the document became published many northern states denounced the document.</p>
<p>In 1859, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Slidell" title="John Slidell">John Slidell</a> introduced a bill to purchase Cuba from Spain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cuba, like many Spanish territories, wanted to break free from Spain. A pro-independence movement in Cuba was supported by the U.S., and Cuban guerrilla leaders wanted annexation to the United States, but Cuban revolutionary leader <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mart%C3%AD" title="José Martí">José Martí</a> called for Cuban nationhood. When the U.S. battleship <i><a href="/wiki/USS_Maine_(ACR-1)" title="USS Maine (ACR-1)">Maine</a></i> sank in Havana Harbor, the U.S. blamed Spain and the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">Spanish–American War</a> broke out in 1898. After the U.S. won, Spain relinquished claim of sovereignty over territories, including Cuba. The U.S. administered Cuba as a <a href="/wiki/Protectorate" title="Protectorate">protectorate</a> until 1902. Several decades later in 1959, the Cuban government of U.S.-backed <a href="/wiki/Fulgencio_Batista" title="Fulgencio Batista">Fulgencio Batista</a> was overthrown by <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a> who subsequently installed a <a href="/wiki/Marxism%E2%80%93Leninism" title="Marxism–Leninism">Marxist–Leninist</a> government. When the US refused to trade with Cuba, Cuba allied with the Soviet Union who imported Cuban sugar, Cuba's main export. The government installed by Fidel Castro has been in power ever since. Recently the US has relaxed sanctions on Cuba.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (December 2016)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/United_Airlines" title="United Airlines">United Airlines</a> submitted a formal application to the U.S. Department of Transportation (DOT) for authority to provide service from four of its largest U.S. gateway cities – Newark/New York, Houston, Washington, D.C. and Chicago – to Havana's José Martí International Airport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dominica">Dominica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Dominica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1898, one or more news outlets in the Caribbean noted growing sentiments of resentment of British rule in <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">Dominica</a>, including the system of administration over the country. These publications attempted to gauge sentiments of annexation to the United States as a way to change this system of administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dominican_Republic">Dominican Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Dominican Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On June 30, 1870, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> took a vote on an annexation treaty with the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, but it failed to proceed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Haiti">Haiti</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Haiti">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> columnist Mark Thompson suggested that <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> had effectively become the 51st state after the <a href="/wiki/2010_Haiti_earthquake" title="2010 Haiti earthquake">2010 Haiti earthquake</a>, with the widespread destruction prompting a quick and extensive response from the United States, even so far as the stationing of the U.S. military in Haitian air and sea ports to facilitate foreign aid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexico">Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1847–48, with the United States occupying Mexico at the conclusion of the <a href="/wiki/Mexican%E2%80%93American_War" title="Mexican–American War">Mexican–American War</a>, there was talk in Congress of annexing the entirety of Mexico; see <a href="/wiki/All_of_Mexico_Movement" title="All of Mexico Movement">All of Mexico Movement</a>. The result was the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Cession" title="Mexican Cession">Mexican Cession</a>, also called the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Guadalupe_Hidalgo" title="Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo">Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo</a> for the town in which the treaty was signed, in which the U.S. annexed over 40% of Mexico. Talk of annexing all of Mexico disappeared after this time.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia_and_Pacific">Asia and Pacific</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Asia and Pacific">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Australia, the term '51st state' is used as a disparagement of a perceived invasion of American cultural or political influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Iraq">Iraq</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Iraq">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>Several publications suggested that the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a> was a <a href="/wiki/Neocolonialism" title="Neocolonialism">neocolonialist</a> war to make the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Iraq" title="Republic of Iraq">Republic of Iraq</a> into the 51st U.S. state, though such statements are usually made in a facetious manner, as a tongue-in-cheek statement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Israel">Israel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Israel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Several websites assert that Israel is the 51st state due to the annual funding and defense support it receives from the United States. An example of this concept can be found in 2003 when <a href="/wiki/Martine_Rothblatt" title="Martine Rothblatt">Martine Rothblatt</a> published a book called <i><a href="/wiki/Two_Stars_for_Peace_solution" title="Two Stars for Peace solution">Two Stars for Peace</a></i> that argued for the addition of <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palestinian_Territories" title="Palestinian Territories">Palestinian territories</a> surrounding it as the 51st state in the Union. <i>The American State of Canaan</i>, is a book published by Prof. <a href="/wiki/Alfred_de_Grazia" title="Alfred de Grazia">Alfred de Grazia</a>, political science and sociologist, in March 2009, proposing the creation of the 51st and 52nd states from Israel and the Palestinian territories.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Article 3 of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_San_Francisco" title="Treaty of San Francisco">Treaty of San Francisco</a> between the Allied Powers and Japan, which came into force in April 1952, the U.S. put the outlying islands of the <a href="/wiki/Ryukyu_Islands" title="Ryukyu Islands">Ryukyus</a>, including the island of <a href="/wiki/Okinawa_Prefecture" title="Okinawa Prefecture">Okinawa</a>—home to over 1 million <a href="/wiki/Ryukyuan_people" title="Ryukyuan people">Okinawans</a> related to the Japanese—and the <a href="/wiki/Bonin_Islands" title="Bonin Islands">Bonin Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Volcano_Islands" title="Volcano Islands">Volcano Islands</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Iwo_Jima" title="Iwo Jima">Iwo Jima</a> into <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Trusteeship_Council" title="United Nations Trusteeship Council">U.S. trusteeship</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> All these trusteeships were slowly returned to Japanese rule. Okinawa was returned on May 15, 1972, but the U.S. stations <a href="/wiki/United_States_Forces_Japan" title="United States Forces Japan">troops</a> in the island's bases as a defense for Japan.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2010 there was an attempt to register a <i>51st State Party</i> with the New Zealand Electoral Commission. The party advocates New Zealand becoming the 51st state of the United States of America. The party's secretary is Paulus Telfer, a former <a href="/wiki/Christchurch" title="Christchurch">Christchurch</a> mayoral candidate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-logoapp_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-logoapp-92">[92]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> On February 5, 2010, the party applied to register a logo with the Electoral Commission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-logoapp_92-1"><a href="#cite_note-logoapp-92">[92]</a></sup> The logo – a US flag with 51 stars – was rejected by the Electoral Commission on the grounds that it was likely to cause confusion or mislead electors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> As of 2014<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the party remains unregistered and cannot appear on a ballot.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Taiwan">Taiwan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Taiwan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taiwan_independence" title="Taiwan independence">Taiwan independence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Political_status_of_Taiwan" title="Political status of Taiwan">Political status of Taiwan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal_status_of_Taiwan" title="Legal status of Taiwan">Legal status of Taiwan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan%E2%80%93United_States_relations" title="Taiwan–United States relations">Taiwan–United States relations</a></div>
<p>A poll in 2003 among <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwanese</a> residents aged between 13 and 22 found that, when given the options of either becoming a province of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> or a state within the U.S., 55% of the respondents preferred statehood while only 36% chose joining China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> A group called Taiwan Civil Government, established in Taipei in 2008, claims that the <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Taiwan" title="Geography of Taiwan">island of Taiwan</a> and other minor islands are the territory of the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Albania">Albania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Albania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a> has often been called the 51st state for its perceived strongly pro-American positions, mainly because of the United States' <a href="/wiki/Albania%E2%80%93United_States_relations" title="Albania–United States relations">policies towards it</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> In reference to President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>'s 2007 European tour, <a href="/wiki/Edi_Rama" title="Edi Rama">Edi Rama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tirana" title="Tirana">Tirana</a>'s mayor and leader of the opposition Socialists, said: "Albania is for sure the most pro-American country in Europe, maybe even in the world ... Nowhere else can you find such respect and hospitality for the President of the United States. Even in Michigan, he wouldn't be as welcome." At the time of ex-Secretary of State <a href="/wiki/James_Baker" title="James Baker">James Baker</a>'s visit in 1992, there was even a move to hold a referendum declaring the country as the 51st American state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJstates_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-WSJstates-99">[99]</a></sup> In addition to Albania, <a href="/wiki/Kosovo" title="Kosovo">Kosovo</a> which is predominately Albanian is seen as a 51st state due to the heavily presence and influence of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The US has had troops and the largest base outside US territory, <a href="/wiki/Camp_Bondsteel" title="Camp Bondsteel">Camp Bondsteel</a> in the territory since 1999.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Denmark">Denmark</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Denmark">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1989, the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> proclaimed that <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> becomes the 51st state every Fourth of July, because Danish citizens in and around <a href="/wiki/Aalborg" title="Aalborg">Aalborg</a> celebrate the American independence day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenland">Greenland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Greenland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, when <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> was occupied by <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>, the United States briefly controlled <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> for battlefields and protection. In 1946, the United States offered to buy Greenland from Denmark for $100 million ($1.2 billion today) but Denmark refused to sell it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> Several politicians and others have in recent years argued that Greenland could hypothetically be in a better financial situation as a part of the United States; for instance mentioned by professor Gudmundur Alfredsson at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Akureyri" title="University of Akureyri">University of Akureyri</a> in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> One of the actual reasons behind US interest in Greenland could be the vast natural resources of the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikileaks" title="Wikileaks">Wikileaks</a>, the U.S. appears to be highly interested in investing in the resource base of the island and in tapping the vast expected hydrocarbons off the Greenlandic coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Poland">Poland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Poland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Poland has historically been staunchly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poland_%E2%80%93_United_States_relations" title="Poland – United States relations">pro-American</a>, dating back to General <a href="/wiki/Tadeusz_Ko%C5%9Bciuszko" title="Tadeusz Kościuszko">Tadeusz Kościuszko</a> and <a href="/wiki/Casimir_Pulaski" title="Casimir Pulaski">Casimir Pulaski</a>'s involvement in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>. This pro-American stance was reinforced following favorable American intervention in World War I (leading to the creation of an <a href="/wiki/Second_Polish_Republic" title="Second Polish Republic">independent Poland</a>) and the Cold War (culminating in a Polish state independent of Soviet influence). Poland contributed a large force to the "Coalition of the Willing" in Iraq. A quote referring to Poland as "the 51st state" has been attributed to <a href="/wiki/James_Pavitt" title="James Pavitt">James Pavitt</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">Central Intelligence Agency</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deputy_Director_for_Operations" title="Deputy Director for Operations">Deputy Director for Operations</a>, especially in connection to <a href="/wiki/Extraordinary_rendition" title="Extraordinary rendition">extraordinary rendition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sicily_.28Italy.29">Sicily (Italy)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Sicily (Italy)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Party of Reconstruction in <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>, which claimed 40,000 members in 1944, campaigned for Sicily to be admitted as a U.S. state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup> This party was one of several Sicilian <a href="/wiki/Separatism" title="Separatism">separatist</a> movements active after the downfall of <a href="/wiki/Italian_Fascism" title="Italian Fascism">Italian Fascism</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sicily#Demographics" title="Sicily">Sicilians</a> felt neglected or underrepresented by the Italian government after the annexation of 1861 that ended the rule of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Two_Sicilies" title="Kingdom of the Two Sicilies">Kingdom of the Two Sicilies</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>. The large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sicilian_American" title="Sicilian American">population of Sicilians in America</a> and the American-led <a href="/wiki/Allied_invasion_of_Sicily" title="Allied invasion of Sicily">Allied invasion of Sicily</a> in July–August 1943 may have contributed to the sentiment.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom_and_Republic_of_Ireland">United Kingdom and Republic of Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: United Kingdom and Republic of Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> has sometimes been called the 51st state due to the "<a href="/wiki/Special_Relationship" title="Special Relationship">Special Relationship</a>" between the two countries, particularly since the close cooperation between <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> during World War II, and more recently continued during the premierships of <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Promo_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-Promo-110">[110]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a December 29, 2011, column in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/David_Aaronovitch" title="David Aaronovitch">David Aaronovitch</a> said in jest that the UK should consider joining the United States, as the British population <a href="/wiki/Euroscepticism_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Euroscepticism in the United Kingdom">cannot accept union with Europe</a> and the UK would inevitably decline on its own. He also made an alternative case that <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> should be three separate states, with <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> joining the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> and becoming an all-<a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="From_terra_nullius">From terra nullius</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: From terra nullius">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are four categories of <i><a href="/wiki/Terra_nullius" title="Terra nullius">terra nullius</a></i>, land that is unclaimed by any state: the small unclaimed territory of <a href="/wiki/Bir_Tawil" title="Bir Tawil">Bir Tawil</a> between Egypt and Sudan, <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>, the oceans, and celestial bodies such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Moon" title="The Moon">the Moon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>. In the last three of these, international treaties (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antarctic_Treaty" title="Antarctic Treaty">Antarctic Treaty</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea" title="United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea">United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty" title="Outer Space Treaty">Outer Space Treaty</a> respectively) prevent colonization and potential statehood of any of these uninhabited (and, given current technology, not permanently inhabitable) territories.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lagrangian_points_of_Earth.27s_orbit">Lagrangian points of Earth's orbit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Lagrangian points of Earth's orbit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/L5_Society" title="L5 Society">L5 Society</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/L5_Society" title="L5 Society">L<sub>5</sub> Society</a> was founded in 1975 with the intention of constructing a <a href="/wiki/Space_habitat" title="Space habitat">space habitat</a> at one of the <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">Lagrangian points</a> of <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit" title="Earth's orbit">Earth's orbit</a>. Its members successfully lobbied the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> to defeat the <a href="/wiki/Moon_Treaty" title="Moon Treaty">Moon Treaty</a>, a treaty that would have transferred sovereignty of all outer space to an international organization, in 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> The high price such a project would cost compared to previous Earth-based colonies eventually led to the group's demise in the 1980s.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=51st_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_fictional_states_of_the_United_States#51st_state" title="List of fictional states of the United States">List of fictional states of the United States § 51st state</a></div>
<p>Related terms have been used in books and film usually used in a negative sense:</p>
<ul>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Americathon" title="Americathon">Americathon</a></i> (1979), set in a fictional 1998, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a> (renamed as Limeyland) has become the 57th state, and the logo of the <a href="/wiki/Safeway_Inc." title="Safeway Inc.">Safeway</a> grocery chain hangs on the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Palace of Westminster</a>.</li>
<li>In the alternative universe of Alan Moore's graphic novel <i><a href="/wiki/Watchmen" title="Watchmen">Watchmen</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> ends with the conquest of the North and annexation of a united <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> as the 51st state.</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Light_of_Other_Days" title="The Light of Other Days">The Light of Other Days</a></i> (2000), a novel by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">Arthur C. Clarke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Baxter_(author)" title="Stephen Baxter (author)">Stephen Baxter</a>, Britain joins the United States, with the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a> serving as governor and the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Windsor" title="House of Windsor">Royal Family</a> exiled to <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>.</li>
<li>The British film <i><a href="/wiki/The_51st_State" title="The 51st State">The 51st State</a></i> (released in the United States and Canada as <i>Formula 51</i>), makes fun of Anglo-American relations.</li>
</ul>
<p>The term has also been used in music.</p>
<ul>
<li>The 1986 album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ghost_of_Cain" title="The Ghost of Cain">The Ghost of Cain</a></i> by the English rock band <a href="/wiki/New_Model_Army_(band)" title="New Model Army (band)">New Model Army</a> features a track called "<a href="/wiki/51st_State_(song)" title="51st State (song)">51st State</a>", which refers to Britain under <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> for her perceived pro-Americanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup></li>
<li>The 1986 track "Heartland" by English band <a href="/wiki/The_The" title="The The">The The</a> describes an increasingly economically divided Britain as the 51st state of the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
