<p><b>Vesto Melvin Slipher</b> (November 11, 1875 – November 8, 1969) was an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a> who performed the first measurements of radial velocities for galaxies, providing the empirical basis for the expansion of the universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes1969_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes1969-1">[1]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wayhunter_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-wayhunter-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Slipher was born in <a href="/wiki/Mulberry,_Indiana" title="Mulberry, Indiana">Mulberry, Indiana</a>, and completed his doctorate at <a href="/wiki/Indiana_University_Bloomington" title="Indiana University Bloomington">Indiana University</a> in 1909.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes1969_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes1969-1">[1]</a></sup> He spent his entire career at <a href="/wiki/Lowell_Observatory" title="Lowell Observatory">Lowell Observatory</a> in <a href="/wiki/Flagstaff,_Arizona" title="Flagstaff, Arizona">Flagstaff, Arizona</a>, where he was promoted to assistant director in 1915, acting director from 1916, and finally director from 1926 until his retirement in 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes1969_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes1969-1">[1]</a></sup> His brother <a href="/wiki/Earl_C._Slipher" title="Earl C. Slipher">Earl C. Slipher</a> was also an astronomer and a director at the Lowell Observatory.</p>
<p>Slipher used <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopy</a> to investigate the rotation periods of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planets" title="Planets">planets</a> and the composition of planetary atmospheres. In 1912, he was the first to observe the shift of spectral lines of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galaxies" title="Galaxies">galaxies</a>, making him the discoverer of galactic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redshifts" title="Redshifts">redshifts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1914, Slipher also made the first discovery of the rotation of spiral galaxies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He discovered the <a href="/wiki/Sodium_layer" title="Sodium layer">sodium layer</a> in 1929.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He was responsible for hiring <a href="/wiki/Clyde_Tombaugh" title="Clyde Tombaugh">Clyde Tombaugh</a> and supervised the work that led to the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a> in 1930.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes1969_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes1969-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hubble" title="Edwin Hubble">Edwin Hubble</a> is commonly incorrectly credited with discovering the redshift of galaxies;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> these measurements and their significance were understood before 1917 by <a href="/wiki/James_Edward_Keeler" title="James Edward Keeler">James Edward Keeler</a> (Lick &amp; Allegheny), Vesto Melvin Slipher (Lowell), and <a href="/wiki/William_Wallace_Campbell" title="William Wallace Campbell">William Wallace Campbell</a> (Lick) at other observatories.</p>
<p>Combining his own measurements of galaxy distances with Vesto Slipher's measurements of the redshifts associated with the galaxies, Hubble and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milton_Humason" title="Milton Humason">Milton Humason</a> discovered a rough proportionality of the objects' distances with their redshifts. This redshift-distance correlation, nowadays termed <a href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_law" title="Hubble's law">Hubble's law</a>, was formulated by Hubble and Humason in 1929 and became the basis for the modern model of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expanding_universe" title="Expanding universe">expanding universe</a>.</p>
<p>Slipher died in <a href="/wiki/Flagstaff,_Arizona" title="Flagstaff, Arizona">Flagstaff, Arizona</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes1969_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes1969-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and is buried there in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Citizens_Cemetery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Citizens Cemetery (page does not exist)">Citizens Cemetery</a>.</p>
