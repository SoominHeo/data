<p><b>Best Buy Co., Inc.</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational</a> <a href="/wiki/Consumer_electronics" title="Consumer electronics">consumer electronics</a> corporation headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Richfield,_Minnesota" title="Richfield, Minnesota">Richfield, Minnesota</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a> suburb. Internationally, it also operates in Canada and Mexico. It was formerly operational in China until February 2011 (when the faction was merged with Five Star) and in Europe until 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SEC_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-SEC-3">[3]</a></sup> The company was founded by <a href="/wiki/Richard_M._Schulze" title="Richard M. Schulze">Richard M. Schulze</a> and Gary Smoliak in 1966 as an audio specialty store. In 1983, it was renamed and rebranded with more emphasis placed on consumer electronics.</p>
<p>Best Buy's subsidiaries include <a href="/wiki/Geek_Squad" title="Geek Squad">Geek Squad</a>, Magnolia Audio Video, and <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Sales" title="Pacific Sales">Pacific Sales</a>. Best Buy operates under the Best Buy, Best Buy Mobile, Geek Squad, Magnolia Audio Video, Insignia and Pacific Sales brands in the United States; the Best Buy, Geek Squad and Best Buy Mobile brands in Canada; Best Buy Mobile and Five Star in China; and Best Buy, Best Buy Express, and Geek Squad in Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SEC_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-SEC-3">[3]</a></sup> Best Buy sells cellular phones from <a href="/wiki/Verizon_Wireless" title="Verizon Wireless">Verizon Wireless</a>, <a href="/wiki/AT%26T_Mobility" title="AT&amp;T Mobility">AT&amp;T Mobility</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sprint_Corporation" title="Sprint Corporation">Sprint Corporation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boost_Mobile" title="Boost Mobile">Boost Mobile</a>, and <a href="/wiki/T-Mobile_US" title="T-Mobile US">T-Mobile US</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> in regular stores and standalone Best Buy Mobile stores in shopping malls. Best Buy was named "Company of the Year" by <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forbes_(magazine)" title="Forbes (magazine)">Forbes</a></i> magazine in 2004,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> "Specialty Retailer of the Decade" by Discount Store News in 2001,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> ranked in the Top 10 of "America's Most Generous Corporations" by Forbes in 2005 (based on 2004 giving),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and made <i><a href="/wiki/Fortune_(magazine)" title="Fortune (magazine)">Fortune</a></i> magazine's list of "Most Admired Companies" in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hubert_Joly" title="Hubert Joly">Hubert Joly</a> serves as Best Buy's chairman and <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">CEO</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-startribune_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-startribune-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Best_Buy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Best_Buy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In August 1966, Richard M. Schulze and a business partner opened Sound of Music, an electronics store specializing in <a href="/wiki/High_fidelity" title="High fidelity">high fidelity</a> stereos in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Paul,_Minnesota" title="Saint Paul, Minnesota">Saint Paul, Minnesota</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessweek_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-businessweek-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-startribuneone_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-startribuneone-19">[19]</a></sup> Schulze financed the opening of his first store with his personal savings and a second mortgage he took out on his family’s home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessweek_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-businessweek-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-discount_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-discount-20">[20]</a></sup> In 1967, Sound of Music acquired Kencraft Hi-Fi Company and Bergo Company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-discount_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-discount-20">[20]</a></sup> Sound of Music earned $1 million in revenue and made about $58,000 in profits in its first year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessweek_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-businessweek-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1969, Schulze bought out his business partner. Sound of Music had three stores and became a publicly held company listed on the <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> exchange.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessweek_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-businessweek-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-musictrades_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-musictrades-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sound of Music operated nine stores throughout <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a> by 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-startribuneyears_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-startribuneyears-22">[22]</a></sup> In 1981, the <a href="/wiki/Roseville,_Minnesota" title="Roseville, Minnesota">Roseville, Minnesota</a>, Sound of Music location, at the time the largest and most profitable Sound of Music store, was hit by a tornado.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessweek_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-businessweek-18">[18]</a></sup> The store’s roof was sheared off and showroom destroyed, but the storeroom was left intact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessweek_18-5"><a href="#cite_note-businessweek-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessweekbg_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-businessweekbg-23">[23]</a></sup> In response, Schulze decided to have a “Tornado Sale” of damaged and excess stock in the damaged store’s parking lot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessweek_18-6"><a href="#cite_note-businessweek-18">[18]</a></sup> He poured the remainder of his marketing budget into advertising the sale, promising “best buys” on everything.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessweekbg_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-businessweekbg-23">[23]</a></sup> Sound of Music made more money during the four-day sale than it did in a typical month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-startribuneone_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-startribuneone-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1983, with seven stores and $10 million in annual sales, Sound of Music was renamed Best Buy Company, Inc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-startribuneyears_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-startribuneyears-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessweekbg_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-businessweekbg-23">[23]</a></sup> The company also expanded its product offerings to include home appliances and <a href="/wiki/Videocassette_recorder" title="Videocassette recorder">VCRs</a>, in an attempt to expand beyond its then-core customer base of 15-to-18-year-old males.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessweekbg_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-businessweekbg-23">[23]</a></sup> Later that year Best Buy opened its first superstore in <a href="/wiki/Burnsville,_Minnesota" title="Burnsville, Minnesota">Burnsville, Minnesota</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessweekbg_23-4"><a href="#cite_note-businessweekbg-23">[23]</a></sup> The Burnsville location featured a high-volume, low price business model, which was borrowed partially from Schulze’s successful Tornado Sale in 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessweek_18-7"><a href="#cite_note-businessweek-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessweekbg_23-5"><a href="#cite_note-businessweekbg-23">[23]</a></sup> In its first year, the Burnsville store out-performed all other Best Buy stores combined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-startribuneone_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-startribuneone-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-discount_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-discount-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Best Buy debuted on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> in 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chainstore_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-chainstore-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internationaldirectory_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-internationaldirectory-25">[25]</a></sup> In 1989, the company introduced a new store concept dubbed “Concept II.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-discount_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-discount-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chainstore_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-chainstore-24">[24]</a></sup> Concept II replaced dimly lit industrial-style stores with brighter and more fashionably fixtured stores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-discount_20-4"><a href="#cite_note-discount-20">[20]</a></sup> Stores also began placing all stock on the sales floor rather than in a stock room, had fewer salespersons and provided more self-help product information for its customers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chainstore_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-chainstore-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internationaldirectory_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-internationaldirectory-25">[25]</a></sup> Best Buy also did away with commissioned salespeople.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessweek_18-8"><a href="#cite_note-businessweek-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chainstore_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-chainstore-24">[24]</a></sup> The commission-free sales environment “created a more relaxed shopping environment free of the high-pressure sales tactics used in other stores,” but was unpopular with salespersons and suppliers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chainstore_24-4"><a href="#cite_note-chainstore-24">[24]</a></sup> Some suppliers, such as <a href="/wiki/Maytag" title="Maytag">Maytag</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whirlpool_Corporation" title="Whirlpool Corporation">Whirlpool</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a>, were upset that salespeople would no longer be pushing their products and stopped selling their wares in Best Buy stores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessweek_18-9"><a href="#cite_note-businessweek-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-startribuneone_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-startribuneone-19">[19]</a></sup> The suppliers returned after Best Buy’s sales and revenue grew following the roll-out of Concept II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessweekbg_23-6"><a href="#cite_note-businessweekbg-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1992, the company achieved $1 billion in annual revenues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internationaldirectory_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-internationaldirectory-25">[25]</a></sup> In 1995, Best Buy debuted “Concept III” stores, which were larger than its previous stores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internationaldirectory_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-internationaldirectory-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-discountstorenews11_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-discountstorenews11-26">[26]</a></sup> The Concept III stores included expanded product offerings, interactive <a href="/wiki/Touchscreen" title="Touchscreen">touchscreen</a> kiosks that displayed product information for both customers and employees, and demonstration areas for products such as <a href="/wiki/Surround_sound" title="Surround sound">surround sound</a> stereo systems and videogames.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-discountstorenews11_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-discountstorenews11-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-customerelectronics_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-customerelectronics-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Best Buy launched its “Concept IV” stores with its expansion into <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internationaldirectory_25-4"><a href="#cite_note-internationaldirectory-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-discountstorenews26_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-discountstorenews26-28">[28]</a></sup> Concept IV stores included an open layout with products organized by category, cash registers located throughout the store, and slightly smaller stores than Concept III stores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-startribunemoore_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-startribunemoore-29">[29]</a></sup> The stores also had large areas for demonstrating <a href="/wiki/Home_cinema" title="Home cinema">home theater systems</a> and computer software.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internationaldirectory_25-5"><a href="#cite_note-internationaldirectory-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-providencenew_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-providencenew-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1999, Best Buy was added to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_%26_Poor%E2%80%99s" title="Standard &amp; Poor’s">Standard &amp; Poor’s</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S%26P_500" title="S&amp;P 500">S&amp;P 500</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internationaldirectory_25-6"><a href="#cite_note-internationaldirectory-25">[25]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2000, Best Buy formed Redline Entertainment, an independent music label and action-sports video distributor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newswire_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-newswire-31">[31]</a></sup> The company acquired Magnolia Hi-Fi, Inc., an audio-video retailer located in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>, in December 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internationaldirectory_25-7"><a href="#cite_note-internationaldirectory-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2001, Best Buy acquired Musicland Stores Corporation, a <a href="/wiki/Minnetonka,_Minnesota" title="Minnetonka, Minnesota">Minnetonka, Minnesota</a>-based retailer that sold home entertainment products under the Sam Goody, Suncoast Motion Picture Company, Media Play and OnCue brands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internationaldirectory_25-8"><a href="#cite_note-internationaldirectory-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-audioweek_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-audioweek-32">[32]</a></sup> Best Buy purchased the company for $425 million in cash and the assumption of $271 million of Musicland debt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internationaldirectory_25-9"><a href="#cite_note-internationaldirectory-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-audioweek_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-audioweek-32">[32]</a></sup> Later that year, Best Buy acquired the <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>-based electronics-chain <a href="/wiki/Future_Shop" title="Future Shop">Future Shop Ltd.</a>, marking its entrance to the international marketplace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internationaldirectory_25-10"><a href="#cite_note-internationaldirectory-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-discountstorenewscn_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-discountstorenewscn-33">[33]</a></sup> Under the deal, Future Shop was purchased for approximately $377 million and continued to operate as subsidiary independent from Best Buy Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saint_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-saint-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twice_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-twice-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Brad_Anderson_(executive)" title="Brad Anderson (executive)">Brad Anderson</a> succeeded Richard Schulze as Best Buy <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">CEO</a> in July 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-startribunegeek_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-startribunegeek-36">[36]</a></sup> Anderson had begun working at Best Buy in 1973 while attending seminary school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-startribunegeek_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-startribunegeek-36">[36]</a></sup> He was promoted to vice president in 1981 and executive vice president in 1986. Anderson had most recently served as president and <a href="/wiki/Chief_operating_officer" title="Chief operating officer">COO</a> of Best Buy, a position he had held since 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-startribunegeek_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-startribunegeek-36">[36]</a></sup> In September of that year, Best Buy opened the first Canadian Best Buy-branded store in <a href="/wiki/Mississauga" title="Mississauga">Mississauga, Ontario</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-discountstorenewson_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-discountstorenewson-37">[37]</a></sup> In October, Best Buy acquired <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis, Minnesota</a>-based Geek Squad, then a 24-hour residential computer repair business with offices in Minneapolis, <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago, Illinois</a>, <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles, California</a> and <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-startribunegeek_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-startribunegeek-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Best Buy stores in the U.S. surpassed the 600-store mark and the company opened its first global-sourcing office in <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai, China</a> in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-billboard600_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-billboard600-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shanghaidaily_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-shanghaidaily-39">[39]</a></sup> In June, Best Buy divested itself of Musicland in a deal with Sun Capital Partners under which Sun Capital received all of Musicland’s stock and debt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mergersacquis_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-mergersacquis-40">[40]</a></sup> Best Buy launched its “Reward Zone” loyalty program in July following an 8-month test of the program in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-warrens_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-warrens-41">[41]</a></sup> Also in 2003, Best Buy’s corporate offices were consolidated into a single campus in <a href="/wiki/Richfield,_Minnesota" title="Richfield, Minnesota">Richfield, Minnesota</a>.</p>
<p>In January 2004, Best Buy hired Virtucom Group to revamp Best Buy’s website and handle all of the company’s online content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-virtucom_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-virtucom-42">[42]</a></sup> In May, the company launched its “customer centricty” program, which segmented its stores according to customer profiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twice20_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-twice20-43">[43]</a></sup> The program also called for employees to focus on specific customer groups rather than product categories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twice20_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-twice20-43">[43]</a></sup> In October, Best Buy completed rolling out Geek Squad “precincts” in every American Best Buy store.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-financialtimes_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-financialtimes-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2005, Best Buy began eliminating <a href="/wiki/Rebate_(marketing)" title="Rebate (marketing)">mail-in rebates</a> in response to negative customer reaction against them, and instead started giving out instant rebates via notebook computers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twincityjournal_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-twincityjournal-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2006, Best Buy acquired a majority interest in Chinese appliance retailer Jiangsu Five Star Appliance for $180 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xinhua_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-xinhua-46">[46]</a></sup> At the time of the deal, Jiangsu was the fourth-largest appliance chain in China with 193 stores across eight Chinese provinces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xinhua_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-xinhua-46">[46]</a></sup> In June, the company opened Geek Squad precincts at <a href="/wiki/Office_Depot" title="Office Depot">Office Depot</a> in <a href="/wiki/Orlando,_Florida" title="Orlando, Florida">Orlando, Florida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-warrens_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-warrens-41">[41]</a></sup> The market test was later expanded to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denver_Colorado" title="Denver Colorado">Denver Colorado</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-odearnings_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-odearnings-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2007, the first Best Buy-branded store in China officially opened in Shanghai, China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sinocast_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-sinocast-48">[48]</a></sup> In March 2007, Best Buy acquired Speakeasy, a <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle, Washington</a>-based broadband <a href="/wiki/Voice_over_IP" title="Voice over IP">VOIP</a>, data and IT services provider.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twice07_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-twice07-49">[49]</a></sup> The acquisition was worth $80 million and, under terms of the deal, Speakeasy began operating as a wholly owned subsidiary of Best Buy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twice07_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-twice07-49">[49]</a></sup> The company’s products also became part of Best Buy’s For Business program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twice07_49-2"><a href="#cite_note-twice07-49">[49]</a></sup> Best Buy also expanded its Geek Squad market tests in March, opening Geek Squad precincts in <a href="/wiki/FedEx_Office" title="FedEx Office">FedEx Kinkos</a> stores located in <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis" title="Indianapolis">Indianapolis, Indiana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte, North Carolina</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dowjones_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-dowjones-50">[50]</a></sup> In October 2007, Best Buy became the first consumer-electronics retailer to exit the analog television market, carrying only digital products that became mandatory in June 2009 by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">FCC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbc_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-nbc-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2008, Best Buy opened its first store in <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan, Puerto Rico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nuevodia_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-nuevodia-52">[52]</a></sup> Best Buy’s Geek Squad market tests in Office Depot and FedEx Kinkos stores ended by March.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters4_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-reuters4-53">[53]</a></sup> Also in March, the company began promoting the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray</a> optical-disc format over the <a href="/wiki/HD_DVD" title="HD DVD">HD DVD</a> format, a move which ultimately contributed to Toshiba's decision to drop HD DVD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-informationweek_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-informationweek-54">[54]</a></sup> In May, the company agreed to buy 50% of the retail division of The Carphone Warehouse, a <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London, England</a>-based mobile phone retailer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retailweekcw_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-retailweekcw-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-startribunecw_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-startribunecw-56">[56]</a></sup> The deal was worth $2.1 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-startribunecw_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-startribunecw-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2008, Best Buy announced that it would start selling musical instruments and related gear in over 80 of its retail stores, making the company the second-largest musical-instrument distributor in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-associatedpress27_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-associatedpress27-57">[57]</a></sup> Best Buy became the first third-party retail seller of <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple’s</a> <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">iPhone</a> in September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crnnews_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-crnnews-58">[58]</a></sup> Later that month, the company agreed to acquire Napster for $121 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reutersnapster_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-reutersnapster-59">[59]</a></sup> In December, Best Buy opened its first store in Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-associatedpressmex_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-associatedpressmex-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2009, Best Buy leveraged its partnership with The Carphone Warehouse to launch Best Buy Mobile, a Best Buy-branded mobile retailer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-streetjournal_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-streetjournal-61">[61]</a></sup> Best Buy Mobile standalone stores were opened in shopping malls and urban downtowns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-streetjournal_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-streetjournal-61">[61]</a></sup> Best Buy Mobile were also added in all Best Buy-branded stores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-streetjournal_61-2"><a href="#cite_note-streetjournal-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2009, Brian Dunn became Best Buy CEO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-startribune25_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-startribune25-62">[62]</a></sup> Dunn replaced Brad Anderson, who was retiring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-startribune25_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-startribune25-62">[62]</a></sup> Dunn had joined Best Buy in 1985 as a sales associate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twice23_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-twice23-63">[63]</a></sup> In 2000, Dunn became senior vice president of East Coast operations and president of North American retail operations in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twice23_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-twice23-63">[63]</a></sup> He had most recently served as president of Best Buy, a position he had held since 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twice23_63-2"><a href="#cite_note-twice23-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>Best Buy partnered with Roxio's CinemaNow to launch an on-demand movie download service that would allow customers to stream content to any device sold by Best Buy that connected to the Internet in November 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-startribunebus_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-startribunebus-64">[64]</a></sup> In December, the first Turkish Best Buy opened in <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0zmir" title="İzmir">İzmir</a>.</p>

<p>In April 2010, Best Buy opened its first <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>-based Best Buy-branded store in <a href="/wiki/Thurrock" title="Thurrock">Thurrock</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-billboardbiz_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-billboardbiz-65">[65]</a></sup> The company eventually opened 11 Best Buy stores in the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj8_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-wsj8-66">[66]</a></sup> The company closed all UK-based Best Buy stores in November 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj8_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-wsj8-66">[66]</a></sup> That same month Best Buy purchased The Carphone Warehouse’s share of Best Buy Mobile for $1.3 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj8_66-2"><a href="#cite_note-wsj8-66">[66]</a></sup> Best Buy and The Carphone Warehouse maintained their Best Buy Europe joint venture, which at the time operated 2,500 mobile phone stores throughout Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj8_66-3"><a href="#cite_note-wsj8-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>The company closed all of its Best Buy-branded stores in China by February 2011, when it merged Best Buy China’s operations with Jiangsu Five Star, which had become a wholly owned subsidiary of Best Buy in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SEC_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-SEC-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xinhua28_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-xinhua28-67">[67]</a></sup> In December 2011, Best Buy purchased mindSHIFT Technologies, a company that provided IT support for small and medium-sized businesses, for $167 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zdnet_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-zdnet-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, in response to overall revenue decline, Best Buy announced plans to undergo a "transformation strategy". Stores began to adopt a redesigned "Connected Store" format, providing the Geek Squad with a centralized service desk and implementing a "store-within-a-store" concept for Pacific Kitchen &amp; Bath and Magnolia Design Center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2012, Brian Dunn resigned as Best Buy’s CEO during an internal company investigation into allegations of personal misconduct stemming from an inappropriate relationship with a female Best Buy employee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-journaldunn_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-journaldunn-70">[70]</a></sup> Best Buy named Director <a href="/wiki/George_L._Mikan_III" title="George L. Mikan III">George L. Mikan III</a> interim CEO following Dunn’s resignation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-journaldunn_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-journaldunn-70">[70]</a></sup> The internal investigation was released in May 2012 and alleged that Best Buy founder and chairman Richard Schulze knew of Dunn’s inappropriate relationship and failed to notify the Best Buy board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-journaldunn_70-2"><a href="#cite_note-journaldunn-70">[70]</a></sup> Schulze subsequently resigned his chairmanship of the company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-journaldunn_70-3"><a href="#cite_note-journaldunn-70">[70]</a></sup> Best Buy Director Hatim Tyabji replaced Schulze as Best Buy chairman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-journaldunn_70-4"><a href="#cite_note-journaldunn-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hubert_Joly" title="Hubert Joly">Hubert Joly</a> replaced Mikan as Best Buy CEO in September 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessinsider_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-businessinsider-71">[71]</a></sup> Joly had previously served as CEO of <a href="/wiki/Carlson_Companies" title="Carlson Companies">Carlson</a>, a hospitality conglomerate, since 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessinsider_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-businessinsider-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2013, Best Buy exited the European consumer electronics market when it sold its 50% stake in Carphone Warehouse back to the UK-based mobile phone retailer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsjeu_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-wsjeu-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bloombergeu_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-bloombergeu-73">[73]</a></sup> The sale was worth approximately $775 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsjeu_72-1"><a href="#cite_note-wsjeu-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bloombergeu_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-bloombergeu-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>An increasing trend towards online shopping began to erode revenues and profits in the 2010s. A 4% dip in sales for the June 30, 2014 quarter, marked the tenth quarter in a row where Best Buy's sales had declined. The company, in announcing the result, said it was focusing more on digital-media in its marketing, moving away from newspaper, magazine, and television advertising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BestBuyDip_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-BestBuyDip-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>On March 28, 2015, Best Buy announced the shutdown of the Future Shop chain in Canada; 65 of its 131 former locations were converted into Best Buy locations, while the rest (primarily those in close proximity to an existing Best Buy) were shuttered permanently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gandm-fsclosed_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-gandm-fsclosed-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 24, 2016, Best Buy announced Sharon McCollam would step down as Chief Financial Officer on June 14.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>

<p>Best Buy sells <a href="/wiki/Consumer_electronics" title="Consumer electronics">consumer electronics</a> and a variety of related merchandise, including software, video games, music, VHS cassettes and VCRs, mobile phones, digital cameras, car stereos and video cameras, in addition to home appliances (washing machines, dryers, and refrigerators), in a <a href="/wiki/Commission_(remuneration)" title="Commission (remuneration)">non-commissioned</a> sales environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SEC_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-SEC-3">[3]</a></sup> Under the <a href="/wiki/Geek_Squad" title="Geek Squad">Geek Squad</a> brand, Best Buy offers computer repair, warranty service, and accidental service plans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SEC_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-SEC-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The building exteriors of Best Buy-branded stores are typically light brown, with the entrance designed to look like a blue box emerging from the structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-todaysworld_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-todaysworld-77">[77]</a></sup> Corporate employees operated under a <a href="/wiki/ROWE" title="ROWE">results only work environment</a> from 2005 until March 2013, when the management style was abandoned by Best Buy CEO Hubert Joly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-startribunerowe1_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-startribunerowe1-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-startribunerowe2_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-startribunerowe2-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of October 29, 2016, Best Buy operated 1,026 Best Buy, 331 Best Buy Mobile stand-alone stores and 28 stand-alone Pacific Sales stores in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Q3_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Q3-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SEC_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-SEC-3">[3]</a></sup> Best Buy also operated: 135 Best Buy and 53 Best Buy Mobile stand-alone stores in Canada; and 18 Best Buy stores and 5 Best Buy Express stores in Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SEC_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-SEC-3">[3]</a></sup> Best Buy exited the European market in April 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsjeu_72-2"><a href="#cite_note-wsjeu-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bloombergeu_73-2"><a href="#cite_note-bloombergeu-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="House_brands">House brands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Best_Buy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: House brands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Best Buy also produces products under eight <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_brand" title="House brand">house brands</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SEC_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-SEC-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Dynex – Discount electronic and computer equipment such as HDTVs, Blu-ray players, storage media, data and power cables, webcams, and office supplies.</li>
<li>RocketFish – Cables primarily used with home-theater installation and setup as well as on computer and gaming accessories. RocketFish Mobile includes phone cases, gel skins, clips and chargers for cell phones, GPS, and other exclusive high-end products.</li>
<li>Insignia – Electronic equipment such as HDTVs, tablets, home-theater systems, and digital imaging devices.</li>
<li>Modal – Used for style-oriented mobile accessories, including cases, cables, and Bluetooth speakers.</li>
<li>Platinum – Highest-quality in-house brand for the company, producing many products, such as cables, cell phone accessories, tablet accessories, and digital imaging equipment.</li>
<li>Init – Storage products such as media storage, equipment bags, totes and furniture for home theaters.</li>
<li>Pacific Sales – High-end appliance selection inside select Best Buy stores.</li>
<li>Magnolia Home Theater – High-end home theater selection for both audio and visual equipment, including 4K, 3D, and large televisions, projectors, receivers, and speakers. Magnolia Home Theater is found in select Best Buy locations. Magnolia Home Theater also has a group called the Magnolia Home Theater Design Center, where custom home theater setups can be designed and installed.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Best_Buy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Warranty">Warranty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Best_Buy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Warranty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2000, two Florida consumers brought a lawsuit against the company, alleging that it engaged in fraudulent business practices related to the sale of <a href="/wiki/Extended_warranty" title="Extended warranty">extended warranties</a> (or, more accurately, <a href="/wiki/Service_plan" title="Service plan">service plans</a>). The suit claimed that store employees had misrepresented the manufacturer's <a href="/wiki/Warranty" title="Warranty">warranty</a> in order to sell its own Product Service/Replacement Plan and that Best Buy had "entered into a corporate-wide scheme to institute high-pressure sales techniques involving the extended warranties" and that the company used "artificial barriers to discourage consumers who purchased the 'complete extended warranties' from making legitimate claims."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> The company ultimately settled for $200,000 but admitted no wrongdoing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, Best Buy settled for $4.55 million in a class action lawsuit filed against them in April 2010 by consumers who claimed Best Buy was making unsolicited phone calls in contravention of the <a href="/wiki/Telephone_Consumer_Protection_Act_of_1991" title="Telephone Consumer Protection Act of 1991">Telephone Consumer Protection Act</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pricing">Pricing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Best_Buy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Pricing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the second quarter of 2007, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_Attorney_General" title="Connecticut Attorney General">Connecticut Attorney General</a> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Blumenthal" title="Richard Blumenthal">Richard Blumenthal</a> ordered an investigation into the company's use of an in-store website alleged to have misled customers on item sales prices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> In December 2007, the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> reported on the same issue, in which some customers claimed they thought they were surfing the Internet version of bestbuy.com at an in-store kiosk only to learn that the site reflected in-store prices only. In response, company spokesperson Sue Busch indicated the in-store kiosks were not intended for price-match purposes and rather were a means to navigate in-store availability. Since the initial investigation, a banner was placed on the in-store site to make its customers more aware of the difference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>Similar pricing errors occurred in July 2009, when the <a href="/wiki/Palm_Pre" title="Palm Pre">Palm Pre</a> <a href="/wiki/Multimedia" title="Multimedia">multimedia</a> <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphone</a> was mistakenly sold for $99.99 (versus the retail price of $199.99), and Best Buy honored some of the sales.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Best Buy regularly exercises the right to cancel orders associated with errors on its website.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Analog_televisions">Analog televisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Best_Buy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Analog televisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In April 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">U.S. Federal Communications Commission</a> (FCC) fined the company $280,000 for not alerting customers that the <a href="/wiki/Analog_television" title="Analog television">analog televisions</a> it sold would not receive over-the-air stations after the <a href="/wiki/Digital_television_transition" title="Digital television transition">digital transition</a> on June 12, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> The company challenged this ruling in May 2008 by the FCC saying it was and is in compliance with current FCC regulations pertaining to the digital transition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_issues">Environmental issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Best_Buy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Environmental issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It was one of several large companies named by <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> in 2007 for purchasing raw materials or manufactured products derived thereof from logging companies that, in the opinion of Greenpeace, contribute to unethical <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a> in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since that time, however, the company launched what it calls Greener Together to increase the energy efficiency of its products as well as reduce consumer waste through more recyclable packaging and proper disposal of certain electronic components such as rechargeable batteries, and empty ink cartridges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a way to improve its image and past environmental issues, the company introduced a recycling program in 2009 that has since collected nearly half-a-billion pounds of consumer electronics and e-waste and is available at all their stores for free. These items are then handed over to certified recyclers in the U.S. for proper recycling. The company's goal is to collect one billion pounds of recycling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<p>It also has been named to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a> top-50 list of the largest green-power purchasers. In 2011, the company purchased nearly 119 million kilowatt-hours (kWh) of green power –  electricity generated from renewable resources, such as wind, solar, geothermal, biogas, biomass and low-impact hydropower.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
