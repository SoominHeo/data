<p>A <b>baby boom</b> is any period marked by a greatly increased <a href="/wiki/Birth_rate" title="Birth rate">birth rate</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Demography" title="Demography">demographic</a> phenomenon is usually ascribed within certain <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geographical</a> bounds. People born during such a period are often called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baby_boomer" title="Baby boomer">baby boomers</a>; however, some experts distinguish between those born during such demographic baby booms and those who identify with the overlapping <a href="/wiki/Generation" title="Generation">cultural generations</a>. The causes of baby booms may involve various <a href="/wiki/Fertility_factor_(demography)" title="Fertility factor (demography)">fertility factors</a>. One common baby boom was right after World War II during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>.</p>
<p>The U.S. birthrate exploded after World War II. From 1945 to 1961, more than 65 million children were born in the United States. At the height of this baby boom, a child was born every seven seconds. Many factors contributed to the baby boom. First, young couples who had put off getting married during World War II and the Korean War could finally begin their families. Also, the government encouraged the growth of families by offering generous GI benefits for home purchases. Finally, popular culture celebrated pregnancy, parenthood, and large families.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baby_boom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a> crisis in Africa has contributed locally to a population boom. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aid_money&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aid money (page does not exist)">Aid money</a> used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contraceptives" title="Contraceptives">contraceptives</a> has been diverted over the past two decades into fighting HIV, with the number of babies born far outstripping the deaths from AIDS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baby_boom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>France experienced a baby boom after 1945; it reversed a long-term record of low birth rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The sense that the population was too small, especially in regard to more powerful Germany, was a common theme in the early twentieth century. Put in a list policies were proposed in the 1930s, and implemented in the 1940s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, there was a steady immigration, especially from former French colonies in North Africa. The population grew from 41 million in 1946, to 50 million in 1966, and 60 million by 1990. The farm population declined sharply, from 35% of the workforce in 1945 to under 5% by 2000. By 2004, France had the second highest birthrate in Europe, behind only Ireland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Romania">Romania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baby_boom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Romania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decre%C5%A3ei" title="Decreţei">Decreţei</a>: (1967-1989), A baby boom in Romania caused by a ban on abortion and contraception.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baby_boom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "baby boom" most often refers to the <a href="/wiki/Post%E2%80%93World_War_II_baby_boom" title="Post–World War II baby boom">post–World War II baby boom</a> (1946–1964) when the number of annual births exceeded 2 per 100 women (or approximately 1% of the total population size).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> There are an estimated 78.3 million Americans who were born during this period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The term is a general <a href="/wiki/Demography" title="Demography">demographic</a> and is also applicable to other similar population expansions.</p>
