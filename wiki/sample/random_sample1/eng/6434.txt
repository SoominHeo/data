<p><b>Bay City</b> is a city in <a href="/wiki/Bay_County,_Michigan" title="Bay County, Michigan">Bay County, Michigan</a>, located near the base of the <a href="/wiki/Saginaw_Bay" title="Saginaw Bay">Saginaw Bay</a> on <a href="/wiki/Lake_Huron" title="Lake Huron">Lake Huron</a>. As of the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 census</a>, the city's population was 34,932, and is the principal city of the Bay City <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Statistical_Area" title="Metropolitan Statistical Area">Metropolitan Statistical Area</a>, which is included in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saginaw,_Midland,_and_Bay_City_Metropolitan_Area" title="Saginaw, Midland, and Bay City Metropolitan Area">Saginaw-Midland-Bay City</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combined_Statistical_Area" title="Combined Statistical Area">Combined Statistical Area</a>. The city, along with nearby <a href="/wiki/Midland,_Michigan" title="Midland, Michigan">Midland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saginaw,_Michigan" title="Saginaw, Michigan">Saginaw</a>, form the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saginaw,_Midland,_and_Bay_City_Metropolitan_Area" title="Saginaw, Midland, and Bay City Metropolitan Area">Greater Tri-Cities</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Central_Michigan" title="Central Michigan">Central Michigan</a>, which has more recently been called the Great Lakes Bay Region.</p>
<p>The city is geographically divided by the <a href="/wiki/Saginaw_River" title="Saginaw River">Saginaw River</a>, and travel between the east and west sides of the city is made possible by four modern <a href="/wiki/Drawbridge" title="Drawbridge">drawbridges</a>: <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Bridge_(Bay_City,_Michigan)" title="Liberty Bridge (Bay City, Michigan)">Liberty Bridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Veterans_Memorial_Bridge_(Bay_City,_Michigan)" title="Veterans Memorial Bridge (Bay City, Michigan)">Veterans Memorial Bridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Independence_Bridge" title="Independence Bridge">Independence Bridge</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lafayette_Avenue_Bridge" title="Lafayette Avenue Bridge">Lafayette Avenue Bridge</a>, which allow large ships to travel easily down the river. The city is served by <a href="/wiki/MBS_International_Airport" title="MBS International Airport">MBS International Airport</a>, located in nearby <a href="/wiki/Freeland,_Michigan" title="Freeland, Michigan">Freeland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/James_Clements_Municipal_Airport" title="James Clements Municipal Airport">James Clements Municipal Airport</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_City,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Leon Tromble is regarded as the first settler within the limits of Bay County, in an area which would become Bay City. In 1831, he built a log cabin on the east bank of the Saginaw river. Bay City was first established in 1837, and was incorporated as a city in 1865. In 1834 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_B._Trudell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John B. Trudell (page does not exist)">John B. Trudell</a> built a log-cabin near the present corner of Seventeenth and Broadway. Trudell later purchased land that extended from his residence north along the river to what later became the location for the Industrial Brownhoist, making him the first permanent resident of what has become Bay County.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Bay City became the largest community in the county and the location of the county seat of government. Most of the county's agencies and associations are located here. The city shares common borders with <a href="/wiki/Essexville,_Michigan" title="Essexville, Michigan">Essexville</a> and the townships of <a href="/wiki/Bangor_Township,_Bay_County,_Michigan" title="Bangor Township, Bay County, Michigan">Bangor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frankenlust_Township,_Michigan" title="Frankenlust Township, Michigan">Frankenlust</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Township,_Michigan" title="Hampton Township, Michigan">Hampton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merritt_Township,_Michigan" title="Merritt Township, Michigan">Merritt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monitor_Township,_Michigan" title="Monitor Township, Michigan">Monitor</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth_Township,_Michigan" title="Portsmouth Township, Michigan">Portsmouth</a>.</p>
<p>Bay City was originally known as "Lower Saginaw," and fell within the boundaries of <a href="/wiki/Saginaw_County,_Michigan" title="Saginaw County, Michigan">Saginaw County</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> On June 4, 1846, the Hapton, or Hampton, Post Office opened to service Lower Saginaw.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-po_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-po-7">[7]</a></sup> The community was placed in Bay County, when the county was organized in 1857. It was at this time that the name was changed to Bay City.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Post Office changed its name to Bay City on March 22, 1858.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-po_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-po-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>While <a href="/wiki/Saginaw,_Michigan" title="Saginaw, Michigan">Saginaw</a> had the first white settlement in this area in 1819, larger ships had difficulty navigating the shallower water near the Saginaw settlement. Due to this fact, many of the early pioneers moved to Lower Saginaw as it became clear its deeper waters made it a better location for the growth of industry which relied on shipping. By 1860, Lower Saginaw had become a bustling community of about 2,000 people with several mills and many small businesses in operation. In 1865, the village of Bay City was incorporated as a city. Rapid economic growth took place during this time period, with <a href="/wiki/Lumber" title="Lumber">lumbering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sawmill" title="Sawmill">milling</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shipbuilding" title="Shipbuilding">shipbuilding</a> creating many jobs. The early industrialists in the area used the Saginaw River as a convenient means to float lumber to the mills and factories and as a consequence amass large fortunes. Many of the mansions built during this era are registered as historical landmarks by the state and federal government.</p>
<p>In 1873, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_C._Fitzhugh,_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Charles C. Fitzhugh, Jr. (page does not exist)">Charles C. Fitzhugh, Jr.</a>, a Bay City pioneer, and his wife, Jane, purchased land and built a home on property bounded by Washington, Saginaw, Ninth and Tenth Streets, which later became the location for City Hall. Fitzhugh dealt on a large scale in wild lands and farms, being an agent for over 25,000 acres (100 km<sup>2</sup>) of land in Bay County. During this time, Washington Avenue was primarily developed with residential homes. Businesses were concentrated along Water Street near the Saginaw River. As time went on, businesses started to expand along Washington Avenue. In 1891, the Fitzhughs sold the land to the City of Bay City for <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">$</a>8,500 "to be used for the erection of a City Hall and offices and for no other purposes whatever."</p>
<p>Until 1905, the City of Bay City was limited to the east bank of the <a href="/wiki/Saginaw_River" title="Saginaw River">Saginaw River</a>. when <a href="/wiki/West_Bay_City,_Michigan" title="West Bay City, Michigan">West Bay City</a> was annexed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mpn_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-mpn-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the latter half of the 19th century Bay City was the home of several now-closed industries including many sawmills and shipbuilders. The <a href="/wiki/Defoe_Shipbuilding_Company" title="Defoe Shipbuilding Company">Defoe Shipbuilding Company</a>, which ceased operations December 31, 1975 built <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Destroyer_escorts" title="Destroyer escorts">destroyer escorts</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guided_missile_destroyers" title="Guided missile destroyers">guided missile destroyers</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patrol_craft" title="Patrol craft">patrol craft</a> for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Australian_Navy" title="Royal Australian Navy">Royal Australian Navy</a>. To maintain this strong Naval heritage, the <a href="/wiki/Saginaw_Valley_Naval_Ship_Museum" title="Saginaw Valley Naval Ship Museum">Saginaw Valley Naval Ship Museum</a> worked through the Naval Sea Systems Command to bring the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USS_Edson_(DD-946)" title="USS Edson (DD-946)">USS Edson (DD-946)</a> to Bay City as a museum ship. It was finally delivered to its temporary home in <a href="/wiki/Essexville,_Michigan" title="Essexville, Michigan">Essexville, Michigan</a> on August 7, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bctarticle_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-bctarticle-9">[9]</a></sup> Another important part of the city's industrial history is Industrial Brownhoist, which was well known for its construction of large industrial cranes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_events">Notable events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_City,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Notable events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote plainlinks" role="note">This list is <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:WikiProject_Lists#Incomplete_lists" title="Wikipedia:WikiProject Lists">incomplete</a>; you can help by <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bay_City,_Michigan&amp;action=edit">expanding it</a>.</div>
<p>In September 1990, the tankship MV <i>Jupiter</i> was unloading gasoline at the Total Petroleum Terminal. A passing cargo ship, <a href="/wiki/MV_Buffalo" title="MV Buffalo">MV <i>Buffalo</i></a>, moving at excessive speed, created a wake that caused <i>Jupiter</i> to break free of its berth. A fire and explosion ensued, and one man drowned. There was considerable legal action taken, ultimately resulting in an adjudication that was subsequently appealed by the owners of <i>Buffalo</i>. The findings of the Court of Appeals upheld the original decision, which assigned 50% of the responsibility to <i>Buffalo</i> (for her excessive speed), 25% to the dock operator (for rotten wood pilings) and 25% to <i>Jupiter</i> (for improper procedures in unloading her cargo).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2009, Bay City's wholly owned municipal power company, Bay City Electric Light and Power, installed a "limiter" device to restrict the receipt of power to the home of Marvin Schur, a 93-year-old customer who had failed to pay an outstanding bill in excess of $1,000. The Bay City Electric Light and Power policy was to install the limiter, and to notify the customer by trying to collect the amount due. City employees failed to knock on the door, and it was later found that Schur had a check already made out and had failed to mail it. Schur died from hypothermia in his home a few days later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The day following his death, Bay City Electric Light and Power removed the limiters from all households. It was later learned that Schur had willed his estate, estimated by family to be in excess of $500,000, to Bay Regional Medical Center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>On October 12, 2010 the historic 113-year-old City Hall sustained significant damage as the result of an attic fire which caused the sprinkler system to run for nearly two hours. Most of the damage to the building was water damage from the sprinkler system and water used to fight the fire. The fire started in the midst of a $1.6 million roofing project. After an investigation, it was determined that a worker was using a grinder to cut off bolts in the area where the fire started, and sparks from the work started the blaze. Fire crews were on the scene for nearly five hours fighting the hard-to-access fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_City,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the city has a total area of 11.21 square miles (29.03 km<sup>2</sup>), of which 10.17 square miles (26.34 km<sup>2</sup>) is land and 1.04 square miles (2.69 km<sup>2</sup>) is water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gazetteer_files_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gazetteer_files-1">[1]</a></sup> Despite declining population, Bay City remains (by a narrow margin over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_Huron" title="Port Huron">Port Huron</a>) as the largest U.S. city by population on or near <a href="/wiki/Lake_Huron" title="Lake Huron">Lake Huron</a>, much smaller than the largest cities on the other four <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>: (<a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cleveland" title="Cleveland">Cleveland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thunder_Bay" title="Thunder Bay">Thunder Bay</a>).</p>
<p>Bay City, along with <a href="/wiki/Saginaw,_Michigan" title="Saginaw, Michigan">Saginaw</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Midland,_Michigan" title="Midland, Michigan">Midland</a> make up the <a href="/wiki/Tri-Cities_(Michigan)" title="Tri-Cities (Michigan)">Tri-Cities Area</a>, a sub-region of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flint/Tri-Cities" title="Flint/Tri-Cities">Flint/Tri-Cities</a>. Bay City is sometimes regarded as being part of the greater <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_thumb" title="The thumb">Thumb of Michigan Area</a>, which is also a sub-region of the Flint/Tri-Cities.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_City,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climatic</a> region is typified by large seasonal temperature differences, with warm to hot (and often humid) summers and cold (sometimes severely cold) winters. According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_Climate_Classification" title="Köppen Climate Classification">Köppen Climate Classification</a> system, Bay City has a <a href="/wiki/Humid_continental_climate" title="Humid continental climate">humid continental climate</a>, abbreviated "Dfb" on climate maps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bridges">Bridges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_City,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Bridges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Four modern <a href="/wiki/Bascule_bridge" title="Bascule bridge">bascule bridges</a> allow transportation across the Saginaw River, which separates the East and West sides of Bay City. <a href="/wiki/Lafayette_Avenue_Bridge" title="Lafayette Avenue Bridge">Lafayette Avenue Bridge</a>, opened in 1938, carries <a href="/wiki/M-13_(Michigan_highway)" title="M-13 (Michigan highway)">M-13</a> and <a href="/wiki/M-84_(Michigan_highway)" title="M-84 (Michigan highway)">M-84</a> over the river. The <a href="/wiki/Veterans_Memorial_Bridge_(Bay_City,_Michigan)" title="Veterans Memorial Bridge (Bay City, Michigan)">Veterans Memorial Bridge</a>, opened in 1957, carries <a href="/wiki/M-25_(Michigan_highway)" title="M-25 (Michigan highway)">M-25</a> over the river. <a href="/wiki/Independence_Bridge" title="Independence Bridge">Independence Bridge</a>, opened in 1973, carries Truman Parkway over the river, replacing the earlier Belinda Street Bridge (built in 1893). <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Bridge_(Bay_City,_Michigan)" title="Liberty Bridge (Bay City, Michigan)">Liberty Bridge</a>, opened in 1986, connects Vermont Street (on the west side of the river) and Woodside Avenue (on the east side).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010_census">2010 census</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_City,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2010 census">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FactFinder_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FactFinder-2">[2]</a></sup> of 2010, there were 34,932 people, 14,436 households, and 8,546 families residing in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 3,434.8 inhabitants per square mile (1,326.2/km<sup>2</sup>). There were 15,923 housing units at an average density of 1,565.7 per square mile (604.5/km<sup>2</sup>). The racial makeup of the city was 89.7% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 3.5% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_(U.S._Census)" title="Black (U.S. Census)">Black</a>, 0.6% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 0.5% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 1.8% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(U.S._Census)" title="Race (U.S. Census)">other races</a>, and 3.9% from two or more races. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 8.5% of the population.</p>
<p>There were 14,436 households of which 31.7% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 37.0% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 16.6% had a female householder with no husband present, 5.6% had a male householder with no wife present, and 40.8% were non-families. 33.7% of all households were made up of individuals and 11.3% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.38, and the average family size was 3.04.</p>
<p>The median age in the city was 35.8 years. 24.9% of residents were under the age of 18; 9.7% were between the ages of 18 and 24; 27.2% were from 25 to 44; 25.8% were from 45 to 64; and 12.3% were 65 years of age or older. The gender makeup of the city was 48.7% male and 51.3% female.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000_census">2000 census</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_City,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2000 census">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a> of 2000, there were 36,817 people, 15,208 households, and 9,322 families residing in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 3,537.1 per square mile (1,365.5/km²). There were 16,259 housing units at an average density of 1,562.0 per square mile (603.0/km²). The racial makeup of the city was 91.19% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 2.72% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_(U.S._Census)" title="Black (U.S. Census)">Black</a>, 0.74% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 0.53% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 0.01% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 2.47% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 2.33% from two or more races. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 6.72% of the population.</p>
<p>There were 15,208 households out of which 30.2% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 42.4% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 14.7% had a female householder with no husband present, and 38.7% were non-families. 32.9% of all households were made up of individuals and 12.8% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.38 and the average family size was 3.04.</p>
<p>In the city the population was spread out with 25.5% under the age of 18, 9.4% from 18 to 24, 30.5% from 25 to 44, 20.5% from 45 to 64, and 14.1% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 35 years. For every 100 females there were 92.9 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 88.6 males.</p>
<p>The median income for a household in the city was $30,425, and the median income for a family was $38,252. Males had a median income of $32,094 versus $21,494 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the city was $16,550. About 10.3% of families and 14.6% of the population were below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 19.1% of those under age 18 and 10.5% of those age 65 or over.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_City,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bay City has a Commission-Manager form of government. The Mayor, who is elected to a four-year term, is the presiding officer of the City Commission and has the power to appoint some board and commission members, with the approval of the City Commission. The Mayor of Bay City is Christopher Shannon, who has served in that capacity since December 2010. Shannon replaced Charles M. Brunner, who has resigned Nov 30 to prepare for his 96th District State House seat he takes in January. The City Commission has nine members, one from each of the city's nine wards. City Commissioners serve four-year terms. The terms of the Commissioners are staggered; the even-numbered wards are elected together (in years 1999, 2003, 2007, etc.), and the odd-numbered wards are elected together (in years 2001, 2005, 2009, etc.). City operations are managed by the City Manager, who is chosen by the City Commission.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_City,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bay_City_Fireworks_Festival.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8a/Bay_City_Fireworks_Festival.jpg/220px-Bay_City_Fireworks_Festival.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8a/Bay_City_Fireworks_Festival.jpg/330px-Bay_City_Fireworks_Festival.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8a/Bay_City_Fireworks_Festival.jpg/440px-Bay_City_Fireworks_Festival.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Bay City is well known in Mid-Michigan for its numerous festivals and celebrations which take place during the summer months. Among them are the River Roar, <a href="/wiki/Stanislaus_Kostka" title="Stanislaus Kostka">St. Stan</a>'s Polish Festival, the <a href="/wiki/Bay_City_Fireworks_Festival" title="Bay City Fireworks Festival">Bay City Fireworks Festival</a>, and the River of Time living history reenactment. Many of these events take place along one or more banks of the <a href="/wiki/Saginaw_River" title="Saginaw River">Saginaw River</a>, often in Wenonah Park on the east bank or the larger Veterans Memorial Park on the west bank.</p>
<p>The Bay County Historical Museum, located on Washington Avenue, is the designated repository for the records of the Patrol Craft Sailors Association and also contains numerous displays on local and regional history. Over the past several years, the museum has expanded significantly. It is housed in the former armory building on Washington Avenue, adjacent to the historic City Hall.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bay_County_Library_System" title="Bay County Library System">Bay County Library System</a> includes two public libraries located in Bay City.</p>
<p>The official Bay City flag is blue with the city logo on it. It has been changed from the original design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hell%27s_Half_Mile_Film_and_Music_Festival&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hell's Half Mile Film and Music Festival (page does not exist)">Hell's Half Mile Film and Music Festival</a> is held annually in September. The festival features a mix of independent films with live indie music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_City,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bay_City_Rollers" title="Bay City Rollers">Bay City Rollers</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> band, was named after this city after the band randomly threw a dart at a map.</p>
<p>The entertainer <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a>, who was born in Bay City, referred to her birthplace as "a stinky, little town in Northern Michigan" on national television.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_districts">Business districts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_City,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Business districts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Banks Business District – Runs along Marquette Avenue from Ohio Street north to Harry S. Truman Parkway</li>
<li>Broadway Avenue Business District – Extending from Lafayette Avenue south to McGraw Avenue</li>
<li>Columbus Avenue Business District – From Washington Ave to Bay Medical Center Hospital</li>
<li>Downtown Bay City – Between Madison Avenue and the Saginaw River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>Johnson Street Business District – From Center Ave to Woodside St.</li>
<li>Lafayette/Salzburg/Kosciuszko Business District – Extends along Salzburg east to Kosciuszko (Lafayette turns into Kosciuszko).</li>
<li>The Midland Street Historic District – Located on the West side of the city near the banks of the river. Home to many popular bars in the city.</li>
<li>Industrial Districts – Morton Street, Harrison Street, Woodside Avenue, and the Marquette Industrial Center. Home to companies such as: General Motors Powertrain, SC Johnson &amp; Son, Carbone of America/Ultra Carbon Division, Kerkau Manufacturing, Gougeon, and York Electric</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bay_City_Mall" title="Bay City Mall">Bay City Mall</a> area (not located within the city limits, but in the adjoining township of Bangor Township) – Wilder Road at State Street Road. Includes Bay City Mall (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/JCPenney" title="JCPenney">JCPenney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Younkers" title="Younkers">Younkers</a>); other stores in area include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wal-Mart" title="Wal-Mart">Wal-Mart</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Home_Depot" title="The Home Depot">The Home Depot</a></li>
<li>Water Street – home to Michigan's largest antique district</li>
</ul>
