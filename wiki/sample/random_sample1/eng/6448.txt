<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Bechgaard salt</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:340px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tetrathiafulvalene.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="183" data-file-width="338" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Tetrathiafulvalene.svg/338px-Tetrathiafulvalene.svg.png" width="338"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption"><a href="/wiki/Tetrathiafulvalene" title="Tetrathiafulvalene">Tetrathiafulvalene</a> (TTF) center typical in organic superconductors</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A <b>Bechgaard salt</b> is any one of a number of organic <a href="/wiki/Charge-transfer_complex" title="Charge-transfer complex">charge-transfer complexes</a> that exhibit <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconductivity</a> at low temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> They are named for chemist <a href="/wiki/Klaus_Bechgaard" title="Klaus Bechgaard">Klaus Bechgaard</a>, who was one of the first scientists to synthesize them and demonstrate their superconductivity with the help of physicist Denis Jérôme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Most Bechgaard salt superconductors are extremely low temperature, and lose superconductivity above the 1–2 K range, although the most successful compound in this class superconducts up to almost 12 K.</p>
<p>All Bechgaard salts are formed using a small, planar organic molecule as an <a href="/wiki/Electron_donor" title="Electron donor">electron donor</a>, with any of a number of <a href="/wiki/Electron_acceptor" title="Electron acceptor">electron acceptors</a> [such as perchlorate (ClO<sub>4</sub>) or <a href="/wiki/Tetracyanoethylene" title="Tetracyanoethylene">tetracyanoethylene</a> (TCNE)]. All the organic electron donors contain multiply <a href="/wiki/Conjugated_system" title="Conjugated system">conjugated</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterocycle" title="Heterocycle">heterocycles</a> with a number of properties, including planarity, low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionization_potential" title="Ionization potential">ionization potential</a> and good orbital overlap between heteroatoms in neighboring donor molecules. These properties help the final salt conduct electrons by shuttling them through the orbital vacancies left in the donor molecules.</p>
<p>All Bechgaard salts have a variation on a single <a href="/wiki/Tetrathiafulvalene" title="Tetrathiafulvalene">tetrathiafulvalene</a> motif—different superconductors have been made with appendages to the motif, or using a tetraselena<a href="/wiki/Fulvalene" title="Fulvalene">fulvalene</a> center instead (which is a related compound), but all bear this general structural similarity.</p>
<p>There are a wide range of other <a href="/wiki/Organic_superconductor" title="Organic superconductor">organic superconductors</a> including many other charge-transfer complexes.</p>
