<p><b>Betaproteobacteria</b> are a class of <a href="/wiki/Proteobacteria" title="Proteobacteria">Proteobacteria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urlwww.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-urlwww.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov-1">[1]</a></sup> Betaproteobacteria are, like all Proteobacteria, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gram-negative" title="Gram-negative">Gram-negative</a>.</p>
<p>The Betaproteobacteria consist of several groups of <a href="/wiki/Aerobic_organism" title="Aerobic organism">aerobic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Facultative_anaerobic_organism" title="Facultative anaerobic organism">facultative</a> bacteria that are often highly versatile in their degradation capacities, but also contain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemolithotroph" title="Chemolithotroph">chemolithotrophic</a> genera (e.g., the ammonia-oxidising genus <i><a href="/wiki/Nitrosomonas" title="Nitrosomonas">Nitrosomonas</a></i>) and some <a href="/wiki/Phototroph" title="Phototroph">phototrophs</a> (members of the genera <i>Rhodocyclus</i> and <i>Rubrivivax</i>). Betaproteobacteria play a role in nitrogen fixation in various types of plants, oxidizing ammonium to produce nitrite – an important chemical for plant function. Many of them are found in environmental samples, such as waste water or soil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Pathogenic species within this class are the <a href="/wiki/Neisseriaceae" title="Neisseriaceae">Neisseriaceae</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gonorrhea" title="Gonorrhea">gonorrhea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meningitis" title="Meningitis">meningitis</a>) and species of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Burkholderia" title="Burkholderia">Burkholderia</a></i>.</p>
