<p>The <b>General Secretary of the Central Committee of the Communist Party of Vietnam</b> (<a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">Vietnamese</a>: <span lang="vi" xml:lang="vi"><i>Tổng Bí thư Ban Chấp hành Trung ương Đảng Cộng sản Việt Nam</i></span>), known as <b>First Secretary</b> (<a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">Vietnamese</a>: <span lang="vi" xml:lang="vi"><i>Bí thư Thứ nhất</i></span>) from 1951 to 1976, is the highest office within the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Vietnam" title="Communist Party of Vietnam">Communist Party of Vietnam</a>. The General Secretaryship was the second-highest office within the party when <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh" title="Ho Chi Minh">Hô Chí Minh</a> was Chairman, a post which existed from 1951 to 1969. The general secretary is also the Secretary of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Military_Commission_(Vietnam)" title="Central Military Commission (Vietnam)">Central Military Commission</a>, the leading Party organ on military affairs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPorter199383.E2.80.9384_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPorter199383.E2.80.9384-1">[1]</a></sup> For a period in its history, the position of general secretary has been synonymous with leader of Vietnam. The current general secretary is <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_Ph%C3%BA_Tr%E1%BB%8Dng" title="Nguyễn Phú Trọng">Nguyễn Phú Trọng</a>, and he is ranked first in the <a href="/wiki/Politburo_of_the_Communist_Party_of_Vietnam" title="Politburo of the Communist Party of Vietnam">Political Bureau</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tr%E1%BA%A7n_Ph%C3%BA" title="Trần Phú">Trần Phú</a>, one of the founding members of the <a href="/wiki/Indochinese_Communist_Party" title="Indochinese Communist Party">Indochinese Communist Party</a>, was the party's first general secretary. A year after being elected, he was sentenced to prison by the French authorities because of anti-French activities. He died in prison the same year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDoddLewisEmmons2003557_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoddLewisEmmons2003557-2">[2]</a></sup> Trần's <i><a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a></i> successor was <a href="/wiki/L%C3%AA_H%E1%BB%93ng_Phong" title="Lê Hồng Phong">Lê Hồng Phong</a> who led the party through the office of General Secretary of the Overseas Executive Committee (OEC). The OEC general secretary led the party because the <a href="/wiki/Central_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_Vietnam" title="Central Committee of the Communist Party of Vietnam">Central Committee</a> had been all but annihilated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrocheux200760_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrocheux200760-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A0_Huy_T%E1%BA%ADp" title="Hà Huy Tập">Hà Huy Tập</a>, the third general secretary, was removed from his post in March 1938, and was arrested by the authorities in May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESophie_Quinn-Judge2003225_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESophie_Quinn-Judge2003225-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_V%C4%83n_C%E1%BB%AB_(revolutionary)" title="Nguyễn Văn Cừ (revolutionary)">Nguyễn Văn Cừ</a>, the fourth general secretary, was arrested by the authorities in June 1940, and executed by shooting on 25 May 1941. He was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Tr%C6%B0%E1%BB%9Dng_Chinh" title="Trường Chinh">Trường Chinh</a> in May 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECurrey200561_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECurrey200561-5">[5]</a></sup> An article in <i><a href="/wiki/Nh%C3%A2n_D%C3%A2n" title="Nhân Dân">Nhân Dân</a></i> on 25 March 1951 described Trường's role as the "builder and commander" of the revolution, while Hô Chí Minh was referred to as "the soul of the Vietnamese revolution and the VIetnamese resistance".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESophie_Quinn-Judge20031.E2.80.932_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESophie_Quinn-Judge20031.E2.80.932-6">[6]</a></sup> Trường was demoted as First Secretary in 1956 because of his role in the <a href="/wiki/Land_reform_in_North_Vietnam" title="Land reform in North Vietnam">"bloody Land Reform campaign".</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThai198527.E2.80.9329_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThai198527.E2.80.9329-7">[7]</a></sup> Hô took over the office of First Secretary, but quickly appointed <a href="/wiki/L%C3%AA_Du%E1%BA%A9n" title="Lê Duẩn">Lê Duẩn</a> acting First Secretary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOoi2004777_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOoi2004777-8">[8]</a></sup> Lê was elected general secretary in 1960, and was the second only to Hô until the latter's death on 2 September 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrocheux2007174_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrocheux2007174-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 2 September 1969 until his death on 10 July 1986, Lê was the undisputed leader of Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoods200274_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoods200274-10">[10]</a></sup> He died two months before the next <a href="/wiki/National_Congress_of_the_Communist_Party_of_Vietnam" title="National Congress of the Communist Party of Vietnam">National Party Congress</a>, and was succeeded by Trường, the former general secretary who had served as the second-most powerful politician in Vietnam since Hô's death. Trường was demoted from his post at the <a href="/wiki/6th_National_Congress_of_the_Communist_Party_of_Vietnam" title="6th National Congress of the Communist Party of Vietnam">6th National Party Congress</a>, and was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_V%C4%83n_Linh" title="Nguyễn Văn Linh">Nguyễn Văn Linh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECorfield2008111.E2.80.93112_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECorfield2008111.E2.80.93112-11">[11]</a></sup> The Western press called Linh "Vietnam's <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Gorbachev</a>" because of his reformist policies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEM.G._MasonM._Mason1997313_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEM.G._MasonM._Mason1997313-12">[12]</a></sup> Linh resigned because of bad health in 1991, and <a href="/wiki/%C4%90%E1%BB%97_M%C6%B0%E1%BB%9Di" title="Đỗ Mười">Đỗ Mười</a> was appointed to the general secretaryship by the <a href="/wiki/7th_National_Congress_of_the_Communist_Party_of_Vietnam" title="7th National Congress of the Communist Party of Vietnam">7th National Congress</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Mười ruled until 1997, when he was ousted from power by the reformist-wing of the party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELargo200210.E2.80.9313_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELargo200210.E2.80.9313-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/L%C3%AA_Kh%E1%BA%A3_Phi%C3%AAu" title="Lê Khả Phiêu">Lê Khả Phiêu</a> was Mười's successor, and he was elected as a compromise candidate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Phiêu was ousted in 2001, before the <a href="/wiki/10th_National_Congress_of_the_Communist_Party_of_Vietnam" title="10th National Congress of the Communist Party of Vietnam">10th National Party Congress</a>, when the Central Committee overturned a decision of the Politburo; a majority in the Central Committee voted to remove Phiêu as general secretary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCoverruled_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-CCoverruled-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/N%C3%B4ng_%C4%90%E1%BB%A9c_M%E1%BA%A1nh" title="Nông Đức Mạnh">Nông Đức Mạnh</a> succeeded Phiêu, and Manh came to be considered a moderniser. Manh was also the first general secretary with a university degree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Manh retired in 2011, and Nguyễn Phú Trọng succeeded him, and he now is considered the most powerful political figure in Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manhsuccessor_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-manhsuccessor-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The General Secretary presides over the work of the Central Committee, the Political Bureau, the Secretariat, and chairs meetings with key leaders (<i>Working Regulation of the Central Committee</i>, 2011).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Secretary_of_the_Communist_Party_of_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">
<ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-vp-19"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-vp_19-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">These numbers are not official.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-22"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-22">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The Central Committee when it convenes for its first session after being elected by a <a href="/wiki/National_Congress_of_the_Communist_Party_of_Vietnam" title="National Congress of the Communist Party of Vietnam">National Party Congress</a> elects the Politburo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVanCooper198369_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVanCooper198369-20">[19]</a></sup> According to David Koh, in interviews with several high-standing Vietnamese officials, the Politburo ranking is based upon the number of approval votes given by the Central Committee. <a href="/wiki/L%C3%AA_H%E1%BB%93ng_Anh" title="Lê Hồng Anh">Lê Hồng Anh</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Public_Security_(Vietnam)" title="Ministry of Public Security (Vietnam)">Minister of Public Security</a>, was ranked 2nd in the <a href="/wiki/10th_Politburo_of_the_Communist_Party_of_Vietnam" title="10th Politburo of the Communist Party of Vietnam">10th Politburo</a> because he received the second-highest number of approval votes. Another example being <a href="/wiki/T%C3%B4_Huy_R%E1%BB%A9a" title="Tô Huy Rứa">Tô Huy Rứa</a> of the 10th Politburo, he was ranked at the bottom because he received the lowest number of approval votes. This system was implemented at the 1st plenum of the 10th Central Committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKoh2008666_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKoh2008666-21">[20]</a></sup> Before the 10th Party Congress Politburo rankings functioned as the official <a href="/wiki/Order_of_precedence" title="Order of precedence">order of precedence</a>, but it doesn't any longer (however, there are some who disagree with this view).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVanCooper198369_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVanCooper198369-20">[19]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-tc-23"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-tc_23-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">He was ranked #1 until the <a href="/wiki/August_Revolution" title="August Revolution">1945 August Revolution</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh" title="Ho Chi Minh">Hồ Chí Minh</a>.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-24"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-24">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">He was ranked second until the death of Hồ Chí Minh, the CPV Chairman, on 2 September 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrocheux2007174_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrocheux2007174-9">[9]</a></sup></span></li>
</ol>
