<p><b>Traditional Vietnamese music</b> is highly diverse and <a href="/wiki/Syncretism" title="Syncretism">syncretistic</a>, combining native and foreign influences. Throughout its history, <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> has been most heavily influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Music_of_China" title="Music of China">Chinese musical tradition</a>, as an integral part, along with <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Korea" title="Music of Korea">Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Mongolia" title="Music of Mongolia">Mongolia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Japan" title="Music of Japan">Japan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The former <a href="/wiki/Indochina" title="Indochina">Indochinese</a> kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Champa" title="Champa">Champa</a> also exerted some influence (albeit more minor when compared to China) on Vietnam's traditional music.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Imperial_court_music">Imperial court music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Imperial court music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><i><a href="/wiki/Nh%C3%A3_nh%E1%BA%A1c" title="Nhã nhạc">Nhã nhạc</a></i> is the most popular form of imperial court music, specifically referring to the court music played from the <a href="/wiki/Tr%E1%BA%A7n_dynasty" title="Trần dynasty">Trần dynasty</a> to the very last <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_dynasty" title="Nguyễn dynasty">Nguyễn dynasty</a> of Vietnam, being synthesized and most highly developed by the Nguyễn emperors. Along with <i>nhã nhạc</i>, the imperial court of Vietnam in the 19th century also had many <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Vietnamese_dance" title="Traditional Vietnamese dance">royal dances</a> which still exist to this day. The theme of most of these dances is to wish the king longevity and the country wealth.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Orchestre_de_musique_traditionnelle_(Hanoi).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1800" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Orchestre_de_musique_traditionnelle_%28Hanoi%29.jpg/220px-Orchestre_de_musique_traditionnelle_%28Hanoi%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Orchestre_de_musique_traditionnelle_%28Hanoi%29.jpg/330px-Orchestre_de_musique_traditionnelle_%28Hanoi%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Orchestre_de_musique_traditionnelle_%28Hanoi%29.jpg/440px-Orchestre_de_musique_traditionnelle_%28Hanoi%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Classical music is also performed in honour of gods and scholars such as Confucius in temples. These categories are defined as Nhã Nhạc ("elegant music", ritual and ceremonial music), Đại nhạc ("great music"), and Tiểu nhạc ("small music") that was chamber music for the entertainment of the king.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vietnamese_traditional_dance" title="Vietnamese traditional dance">Vietnamese traditional dance</a> court dances were defined as either van vu (civil servant dance) or vo vu (military dance).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Folk_music">Folk music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Folk music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Vietnamese folk music is extremely diverse and includes <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=D%C3%A2n_ca&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dân ca (page does not exist)">dân ca</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Quan_h%E1%BB%8D" title="Quan họ">quan họ</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H%C3%A1t_ch%E1%BA%A7u_v%C4%83n" title="Hát chầu văn">hát chầu văn</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ca_tr%C3%B9" title="Ca trù">ca trù</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=H%C3%B2&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hò (page does not exist)">hò</a></i>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H%C3%A1t_x%E1%BA%A9m" title="Hát xẩm">hát xẩm</a></i>, among other forms.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ch.C3.A8o">Chèo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chèo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Water_Puppet_Theatre_Vietnam(1).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Water_Puppet_Theatre_Vietnam%281%29.jpg/220px-Water_Puppet_Theatre_Vietnam%281%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Water_Puppet_Theatre_Vietnam%281%29.jpg/330px-Water_Puppet_Theatre_Vietnam%281%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Water_Puppet_Theatre_Vietnam%281%29.jpg/440px-Water_Puppet_Theatre_Vietnam%281%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p><i><a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A8o" title="Chèo">Chèo</a></i> is a form of generally satirical <a href="/wiki/Musical_theatre" title="Musical theatre">musical theatre</a>, often encompassing dance, traditionally performed by <a href="/wiki/Peasant" title="Peasant">peasants</a> in northern Vietnam. It is usually performed outdoors by semi-amateur touring groups, stereotypically in a village square or the courtyard of a public building, although today it is also increasingly performed indoors and by professional performers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="X.E1.BA.A9m">Xẩm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Xẩm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vietnamese_xam_artists.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="436" data-file-width="700" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Vietnamese_xam_artists.jpg/220px-Vietnamese_xam_artists.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Vietnamese_xam_artists.jpg/330px-Vietnamese_xam_artists.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Vietnamese_xam_artists.jpg/440px-Vietnamese_xam_artists.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vietnamese_xam_artists.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Blind artists performing xẩm</div>

<p><i><a href="/wiki/X%E1%BA%A9m" title="Xẩm">Xẩm</a></i> or <i>Hát xẩm</i> (Xẩm singing) is a type of Vietnamese <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a> which was popular in the Northern region of Vietnam but is considered nowadays an endangered form of traditional music in Vietnam. In the dynastic time, xẩm was generally performed by blind artists who wandered from town to town and earned their living by singing in common places.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quan_h.E1.BB.8D">Quan họ</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Quan họ">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Quan_h%E1%BB%8D" title="Quan họ">Quan họ</a></i> (<i>alternate singing</i>) is popular in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H%C3%A0_B%E1%BA%AFc" title="Hà Bắc">Hà Bắc</a> (divided into nowadays <a href="/wiki/B%E1%BA%AFc_Ninh_Province" title="Bắc Ninh Province">Bắc Ninh</a> and <a href="/wiki/B%E1%BA%AFc_Giang_Province" title="Bắc Giang Province">Bắc Giang</a> provinces) and across Vietnam; numerous variations exist, especially in the Northern provinces. Sung a cappella, <i>quan họ</i> is improvised and is used in courtship rituals.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="H.C3.A1t_ch.E1.BA.A7u_v.C4.83n">Hát chầu văn</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hát chầu văn">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hat_chau_van" title="Hat chau van">Hát chầu văn</a></i> or <i>hát văn</i> is a spiritual form of music used to invoke spirits during ceremonies. It is highly rhythmic and trance-oriented. Before 1986, the Vietnamese government repressed hát chầu văn and other forms of religious expression. It has since been revived by musicians like <a href="/wiki/Ph%E1%BA%A1m_V%C4%83n_T%E1%BB%B5" title="Phạm Văn Tỵ">Phạm Văn Tỵ</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nh.E1.BA.A1c_d.C3.A2n_t.E1.BB.99c_c.E1.BA.A3i_bi.C3.AAn">Nhạc dân tộc cải biên</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nhạc dân tộc cải biên">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Nh%E1%BA%A1c_d%C3%A2n_t%E1%BB%99c_c%E1%BA%A3i_bi%C3%AAn" title="Nhạc dân tộc cải biên">Nhạc dân tộc cải biên</a></i> is a modern form of Vietnamese <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a> which arose in the 1950s after the founding of the <a href="/wiki/Hanoi_Conservatory_of_Music" title="Hanoi Conservatory of Music">Hanoi Conservatory of Music</a> in 1956. This development involved writing traditional music using Western <a href="/wiki/Musical_notation" title="Musical notation">musical notation</a>, while Western elements of <a href="/wiki/Harmony" title="Harmony">harmony</a> and <a href="/wiki/Musical_instrument" title="Musical instrument">instrumentation</a> were added. <i>Nhạc dân tộc cải biên</i> is often criticized by purists for its watered-down approach to traditional sounds.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ca_tr.C3.B9">Ca trù</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ca trù">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ca_tr%C3%B9" title="Ca trù">Ca trù</a> (also <i>hát ả đào</i>) is a popular folk music which is said to have begun with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=%E1%BA%A2_%C4%90%C3%A0o&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ả Đào (page does not exist)">Ả Đào</a>, a female singer who charmed the enemy with her voice. Most singers remain female, and the genre has been revived since the Communist government loosened its repression in the 1980s, when it was associated with prostitution.</p>
<p>Ca trù, which has many forms, is thought to have originated in the imperial palace, eventually moving predominantly into performances at communal houses for scholars and other members of the elite (this is the type of Ca trù most widely known). It can be referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Geisha" title="Geisha">geisha</a>-type of entertainment where women, trained in music and poetry, entertained rich and powerful men.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="H.C3.B2">Hò</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hò">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Hò" can not be thought of as the southern style of Quan họ. It is improvisational and is typically sung as dialogue between a man and woman. Common themes include love, courtship, the countryside, etc. "Hò" is popular in Cần Thơ - Vietnam.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_music">Classical music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Classical music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vietnamese composers also followed western forms of music, such as <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4_Sao" title="Cô Sao">Cô Sao</a> of <a href="/wiki/%C4%90%E1%BB%97_Nhu%E1%BA%ADn" title="Đỗ Nhuận">Đỗ Nhuận</a>, considered as the first Vietnamese opera. <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_V%C4%83n_Qu%E1%BB%B3" title="Nguyễn Văn Quỳ">Nguyễn Văn Quỳ</a> also wrote 9 sonatas for Violin and Piano, following his French music studies and Vietnamese traditions. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1940s.E2.80.931980s.2C_singer-songwriters">1940s–1980s, singer-songwriters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 1940s–1980s, singer-songwriters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Popular_music_of_Vietnam" title="Popular music of Vietnam">Popular music of Vietnam</a></div>
<p>The Vietnam War, the consequent <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Saigon" title="Fall of Saigon">Fall of Saigon</a>, and the plight of Vietnamese refugees gave rise to a collection of musical pieces that have become "classical" anthems for Vietnamese people both in Vietnam and abroad. Notable writers include <a href="/wiki/Ph%E1%BA%A1m_Duy" title="Phạm Duy">Phạm Duy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tr%E1%BB%8Bnh_C%C3%B4ng_S%C6%A1n" title="Trịnh Công Sơn">Trịnh Công Sơn</a>. Singers include <a href="/wiki/Kh%C3%A1nh_Ly" title="Khánh Ly">Khánh Ly</a> and <a href="/wiki/L%E1%BB%87_Thu" title="Lệ Thu">Lệ Thu</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many of these composers, in the North, also contributed Vietnamese revolutionary songs, known as <a href="/wiki/Nh%E1%BA%A1c_%C4%91%E1%BB%8F" title="Nhạc đỏ">nhạc đỏ</a> "Red Music."</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_music">Modern music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Modern music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/V-pop" title="V-pop">V-pop</a></div>
<p>In Vietnam, there is no official music chart across the country or digital sale, though <i><a href="/wiki/Vietnam_Idol" title="Vietnam Idol">Vietnam Idol</a></i> is reflected in "sales" of <a href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement" title="Copyright infringement">unlicensed CDs</a> and downloads.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pop_music">Pop music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Pop music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The embrace of Modern Pop music culture has increased, as each new generation of people in Vietnam has become more exposed to and influenced by westernized music along with the fashion styles of Hong Kong, Taiwan, Japan, and South Korea. Musical production has improved and expanded over the years as visiting performers and organizers from other countries have helped to stimulate the Vietnamese entertainment industry. Such performances include international stages like the Asia Music Festival in South Korea where popular Vietnamese singers such as <a href="/wiki/H%E1%BB%93_Qu%E1%BB%B3nh_H%C6%B0%C6%A1ng" title="Hồ Quỳnh Hương">Hồ Quỳnh Hương</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%E1%BB%B9_T%C3%A2m" title="Mỹ Tâm">Mỹ Tâm</a>, <a href="/wiki/H%E1%BB%93_Ng%E1%BB%8Dc_H%C3%A0" title="Hồ Ngọc Hà">Hồ Ngọc Hà</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lam_Tr%C6%B0%E1%BB%9Dng" title="Lam Trường">Lam Trường</a>, and others have performed along with other singers from different Asian countries. During the recent years such as 2006 and beyond, Vietnamese pop music has tremendously improved from years past. Vietnamese music has been able to widen its reach to audiences nationally and also overseas. There are many famous underground artists such as Andree Right Hand, Big Daddy, Shadow P (all featured in a popular song called <i>Để anh được yêu</i>) or Lil' Knight and countless others who have risen to fame through the Internet. In addition, there are also other singers that have gone mainstream such as M4U, Hồ Ngọc Hà, <a href="/wiki/B%E1%BA%A3o_Thy" title="Bảo Thy">Bảo Thy</a>, Wanbi Tuấn Anh, <a href="/wiki/Kh%E1%BB%95ng_T%C3%BA_Qu%E1%BB%B3nh" title="Khổng Tú Quỳnh">Khổng Tú Quỳnh</a>, Radio Band, etc. There are also amateur singers whose songs have been hits in Vietnam such as <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%B9y_Chi" title="Thùy Chi">Thùy Chi</a>. These singers tend to view singing as a hobby, therefore not being labeled as mainstream artists. Overall, the quality of recording and the style of music videos in Vietnam has improved a lot compared to the past years due to many private productions and also overseas Vietnamese coming back to produce a combination of Western and Vietnamese music.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rock_and_heavy_metal">Rock and heavy metal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Rock and heavy metal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Introduced by American soldiers, Rock and Roll was popular in Saigon during the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>. This genre has developed strongly in the South and has spread out over the North region after the rise of <a href="/wiki/B%E1%BB%A9c_T%C6%B0%E1%BB%9Dng" title="Bức Tường">Bức Tường</a> in the 90s. For the last 10 years, metal has become more mainstream in Vietnam. Unlimited, <a href="/wiki/Ng%C5%A9_Cung" title="Ngũ Cung">Ngũ Cung</a>, Microwave, and the <a href="/wiki/Black_Infinity" title="Black Infinity">Black Infinity</a> are the current top Vietnamese metal bands in the 21st century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
