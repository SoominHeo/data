<p><b>Benjamin Marshall Wildman-Tobriner</b> (born September 21, 1984) is an American competition swimmer, Olympic gold medalist, and former world record-holder.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_development">Early development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Wildman-Tobriner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wildman-Tobriner was born in San Francisco to parents Michael Tobriner and Stephanie Wildman. He grew up in San Francisco with his parents and older sister, Rebecca. Wildman-Tobriner's paternal grandfather, <a href="/wiki/Mathew_Tobriner" title="Mathew Tobriner">Mathew Tobriner</a>, served as a <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_California" title="Supreme Court of California">California Supreme Court</a> <a href="/wiki/Judge" title="Judge">Justice</a> for 20 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usoc_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-usoc-2">[2]</a></sup> He is currently a radiology resident at <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">Duke University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wilman-Tobriner began his career swimming for a local recreational team called Swimarin in the Marin Swim League until the age of 12. Even at that age, he managed to set several team, pool and league records and become recognized for his talents. Around the age of 12, he joined the Fog City Hammerheads Swim Team under the instruction of the notable coach, Matthew "Berto" Roberto. He attended <a href="/wiki/Lick-Wilmerding_High_School" title="Lick-Wilmerding High School">Lick-Wilmerding High School</a> where he was a four-year varsity swimmer, and three-time MVP. While in high school he received numerous <a href="/wiki/All-America" title="All-America">All-American</a> awards for swimming excellence, as well as setting the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Coast_Section" title="North Coast Section">California North Coast Sectional</a> record in the 50-yard (46 m) freestyle and achieving the second fastest time for a 17- to 18-year-old male in the 50-meter freestyle in the United States. After graduating Lick-Wilmerding, Wildman-Tobriner attended <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> where, as well as swimming all four years, he graduated in 2007 as a Biomechanical Engineer.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Wildman-Tobriner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wildman-Tobriner is most famous for his achievements in the 50-meter freestyle. In the spring on 2007, he competed in the <a href="/wiki/Swimming_at_the_2007_World_Aquatics_Championships" title="Swimming at the 2007 World Aquatics Championships">World Championships</a> where he won the 50-meter freestyle in a time of 21.88 seconds. He had the former American Record in the 50-yard freestyle with a time of 18.87 seconds. He currently has 1 NCAA gold medal for the 400 Medley Relay (2005) as well as more than a dozen All-Americans and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific-10_Conference" title="Pacific-10 Conference">Pac-10</a> championships.</p>
<p>In December 2007, Wildman-Tobriner injured his left <a href="/wiki/Pectoralis_major_muscle" title="Pectoralis major muscle">pectoral</a> tendon, completely separating it from the <a href="/wiki/Humerus" title="Humerus">humerus</a> bone. After surgery, he resumed training, at first swimming using only his right arm, with his left arm in a sling. By May 2008, he had recovered enough to compete again, finishing fourth in a race in Santa Clara.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 3, 2008, Wildman-Tobriner qualified for the Olympic relay team as the second alternate in the 4 × 100 m freestyle relay. In Beijing, during the evening preliminary heats on August 10, he became a member of the World Record 4 × 100 m freestyle relay (3:12.23) by swimming the third leg in a time of 48.03. The next day, Wildman-Tobriner earned a gold medal (but lost the world record) when <a href="/wiki/Jason_Lezak" title="Jason Lezak">Jason Lezak</a> swam down <a href="/wiki/Alain_Bernard" title="Alain Bernard">Alain Bernard</a> in the final leg of the relay's final in world record time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
