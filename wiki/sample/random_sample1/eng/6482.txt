<p>On 1 June 2003, <b><a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a></b> became the second country in the world to legalise <b><a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage" title="Same-sex marriage">same-sex marriage</a></b>, after the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>. "Statutory cohabitation", open to any two legally consenting cohabiting persons, is also possible since 1 January 2000.</p>

<p>In the late 1990s, gay rights organisations in Belgium lobbied for the legalization of same-sex marriage. Belgian civil law did not explicitly require that two people be of opposite gender to be able to marry, as this was considered self-evident. <a href="/wiki/Private_member%27s_bill" title="Private member's bill">Private member's bills</a> in the 1990s by <a href="/wiki/Vlaams_Blok" title="Vlaams Blok">Vlaams Blok</a> senators to add this as an explicit requirement were never considered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1995, a bill was introduced in <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Federal_Parliament" title="Belgian Federal Parliament">Parliament</a> to provide for a legal framework of "cohabitation agreements". It was mostly intended as a response to the lowering marriage rates, rather than giving rights to same-sex couples. In 1998, the bill was changed to "statutory <a href="/wiki/Domestic_partnership" title="Domestic partnership">cohabitation</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <span lang="nl"><i>wettelijke samenwoning</i></span>; <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr"><i>cohabitation légale</i></span>) and finally voted on. The Chamber of Representatives approved it by a 98–10 vote with 32 abstentions and the Senate by a 39–8 vote with 19 abstentions. The Act of 23 November 1998 gives limited rights to <a href="/wiki/Civil_union" title="Civil union">registered</a> same-sex and opposite-sex couples by inserting a Title V-bis on statutory cohabitation in the Belgian Civil Code and by amending certain provisions of the Belgian Civil Code and the Belgian Judicial Code. However, being a couple is not a requirement to make a declaration of statutory cohabitation; relatives can do so too. The law was legally published on 12 January 1999 but not yet in effect.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Verhofstadt_Government">Verhofstadt Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Same-sex_marriage_in_Belgium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Verhofstadt Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The election programmes of the <a href="/wiki/Socialistische_Partij_Anders" title="Socialistische Partij Anders">SP</a> (Flemish social democrats), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agalev" title="Agalev">Agalev</a> (Flemish Greens) and <a href="/wiki/Open_Vlaamse_Liberalen_en_Democraten" title="Open Vlaamse Liberalen en Democraten">VLD</a> (Flemish liberals) for the <a href="/wiki/Belgian_federal_election,_1999" title="Belgian federal election, 1999">13 June 1999 elections</a> included the aim to legalise same-sex marriage. The new <a href="/wiki/Verhofstadt_I_Government" title="Verhofstadt I Government">Verhofstadt I Government</a> was formed, which was notably made up of a coalition of liberal, socialist and green parties and excluded the long-dominant Christian democrats, who lost the elections due to the <a href="/wiki/Dioxin_Affair" title="Dioxin Affair">Dioxin Affair</a>. The coalition agreement included "implementing a full legal partnership scheme" as well as "immediately making the Act of 23 November 1998 enter into force", which had not been done yet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> A royal order signed on 14 December and published on 23 December 1999 made the Act of statutory cohabitation go into effect on 1 January 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>During 1999, the <a href="/wiki/Parti_Socialiste_(Belgium)" title="Parti Socialiste (Belgium)">PS</a> (French-speaking social democrats) and <a href="/wiki/Ecolo" title="Ecolo">Ecolo</a> (French-speaking Greens) also announced they agree to legalise same-sex marriage. At that point, the only remaining party in government that opposed same-sex marriage was the French-speaking liberal <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Reformist_Party" title="Liberal Reformist Party">PRL</a> (later merged into <a href="/wiki/Mouvement_R%C3%A9formateur" title="Mouvement Réformateur">MR</a>), mainly because it was opposed to adoption rights for same-sex couples. PRL agreed not to block same-sex marriage if adoption rights are excluded. As the first <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage_in_the_Netherlands" title="Same-sex marriage in the Netherlands">same-sex marriage in the Netherlands</a> was performed on 1 April 2001, the Belgian government, mostly under the lead of Minister of Health <a href="/wiki/Magda_Aelvoet" title="Magda Aelvoet">Magda Aelvoet</a> (Agalev), began considering it as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> On 22 June, the Council of Ministers formally approved opening marriage to same-sex couples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In September, the largest opposition Christian People's Party (CVP) held a party convention where they rebranded into Christian Democratic &amp; Flemish (CD&amp;V), with a renewed party platform, including the aim to legalise same-sex marriage, put forward by their youth wing.</p>
<p>On 30 November 2001 however, the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_State_(Belgium)" title="Council of State (Belgium)">Council of State</a> gave a negative legal opinion on the bill, saying that "marriage is defined as the union of a man and a woman".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> LGBT organisations and government ministers criticised the opinion and said they would proceed with the legislation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The Council of Ministers formally approved the government bill on 8 December 2001 and in second reading on 30 January 2002 and submitted it to the Chamber of Representatives on 14 March 2002, where it faced a Justice Committee overloaded with bills to consider.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In May 2002, the government bill was then withdrawn from the Chamber and instead introduced as a <a href="/wiki/Private_member%27s_bill" title="Private member's bill">private member's bill</a> (which does not require opinions by the Council of State) in the <a href="/wiki/Senate_(Belgium)" title="Senate (Belgium)">Senate</a> by the group leaders of the majority parties, Jeannine Leduc (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flemish_Liberals_and_Democrats" title="Flemish Liberals and Democrats">VLD</a>), <a href="/wiki/Philippe_Mahoux" title="Philippe Mahoux">Philippe Mahoux</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_(francophone_Belgium)" title="Socialist Party (francophone Belgium)">PS</a>), <a href="/wiki/Philippe_Monfils" title="Philippe Monfils">Philippe Monfils</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reformist_Movement" title="Reformist Movement">MR</a>), <a href="/wiki/Myriam_Vanlerberghe" title="Myriam Vanlerberghe">Myriam Vanlerberghe</a> (<a href="/wiki/SP.A-Spirit" title="SP.A-Spirit">SP.A-Spirit</a>), Marie Nagy (<a href="/wiki/Ecolo" title="Ecolo">Ecolo</a>) and Frans Lozie (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agalev" title="Agalev">Agalev</a>).</p>
<p>As Minister Aelvoet resigned on 28 August 2002 and elections were to be held in <a href="/wiki/Belgian_federal_election,_2003" title="Belgian federal election, 2003">June 2003</a>, the fate of the bill was unclear. Some politicians also accused <a href="/wiki/Philippe_Monfils" title="Philippe Monfils">Philippe Monfils</a> (MR) of deliberately stalling the bill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Nevertheless, new momentum was gained at the start of the new parliamentary year in October 2002. The Senate Justice Committee held hearings and voted 11–4 to approve the bill. It passed in the full Senate on 28 November 2002, with 46 votes to 15 (and 4 abstentions) and on 30 January 2003, the bill passed the <a href="/wiki/Chamber_of_Representatives_(Belgium)" title="Chamber of Representatives (Belgium)">Chamber of Representatives</a> by 91 votes to 22 (and 9 abstentions).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flemish_Liberals_and_Democrats" title="Flemish Liberals and Democrats">Flemish Liberals and Democrats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christen-Democratisch_en_Vlaams" title="Christen-Democratisch en Vlaams">Christian Democratic and Flemish</a>, (Francophone) <a href="/wiki/Parti_Socialiste_(Belgium)" title="Parti Socialiste (Belgium)">Socialist Party</a>, (Flemish) <a href="/wiki/Socialistische_Partij_Anders" title="Socialistische Partij Anders">Socialist Party</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecolo" title="Ecolo">Ecolo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agalev" title="Agalev">Agalev</a> and the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Union_(Belgium)" title="People's Union (Belgium)">People's Union</a> voted generally in favour except for several abstentions, whereas the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flemish_Block" title="Flemish Block">Flemish Block</a> and <a href="/wiki/National_Front_(Belgium)" title="National Front (Belgium)">National Front</a> voted against, the <a href="/wiki/Centre_d%C3%A9mocrate_humaniste" title="Centre démocrate humaniste">Humanist Democratic Centre</a> voted against with several abstentions and the <a href="/wiki/Mouvement_R%C3%A9formateur" title="Mouvement Réformateur">Reformist Movement</a> voted mostly against.</p>

<p>The first female couple married on 6 June 2003 and the first male couple on 13 June 2003, both in <a href="/wiki/Kapellen,_Belgium" title="Kapellen, Belgium">Kapellen</a> near <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2003, opponents of same-sex marriage petitioned the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitutional_Court_of_Belgium" title="Constitutional Court of Belgium">Arbitration Court</a> to invalidate the law as unconstitutional. Their main argument held that treating fundamentally different situations the same way, violates the equality principle of the Constitution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In October 2004, the Arbitration Court, nowadays known as the Constitutional Court, rejected the request.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_changes">Subsequent changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Same-sex_marriage_in_Belgium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Subsequent changes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Originally, Belgium allowed the marriages of foreign same-sex couples only if their country of origin also allowed these unions. A <a href="/wiki/Circulaire" title="Circulaire">circulaire</a> by Minister of Justice <a href="/wiki/Laurette_Onkelinx" title="Laurette Onkelinx">Laurette Onkelinx</a> (PS) of 23 January 2004 however, permits any couple to marry in Belgium if at least one of the spouses has lived in the country for a minimum of three months. This was codified into the Code on International Private Law, which took effect on 1 October 2004.</p>
<p>The same-sex marriage law did not permit <a href="/wiki/LGBT_adoption" title="LGBT adoption">adoption by same-sex partners</a>, and as birth within a same-sex marriage did not imply affiliation, the same-sex spouse of the biological parent had no way to become the legal parent. A proposal to permit adoption was approved 77–62 (with 7 abstentions) by the Chamber of Representatives on 1 December 2005,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and 34–33 (with 2 abstentions) by the Senate on 20 April 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> It received royal assent on 18 May 2006 and went into force on 30 June 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>A legal inequality compared to heterosexual couples still existed with regards to children: the husband of the biological mother is automatically legally recognised as the father (by article 135 of the Civil Code), but this was not the case in a same-sex couple for the wife of the mother. To be recognised as the co-mother, she had to complete an adoption procedure. This accounted for the large majority of adoption cases in Belgium. The <a href="/wiki/Di_Rupo_Government" title="Di Rupo Government">Di Rupo Government</a> promised to fix this, and in 2014, as the Netherlands recently passed similar legislation, LGBT organisations pressured the government about their promise. Subsequently legislators worked to agree on a solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> A bill to this end was approved by the Senate on 3 April 2014 on a 48–2 vote (with one abstention), and by the Chamber of Representatives on 23 April on a 114–10 vote (with one abstention). The bill received royal assent on 5 May and went into effect on 1 January 2015.</p>
<p>Since this change, female same-sex couples are treated equally to heterosexual couples: the co-mother married to the mother is automatically recognised as parent, and an unmarried partner can formally recognise the child at the civil registry. An equivalent solution for male same-sex couples has not been agreed upon, due to the controversy surrounding surrogacy.</p>

<p>Consequently, the share of same-sex marriages among all marriages also differs significantly from region to region. Whereas almost 3% of marriages in both Brussels and Flanders are same-sex marriages, only 1.6% in Wallonia are. There is also a correlation between the share per province and the province's population density. The province with the lowest rate is Luxembourg (1.2%) and the one with the highest rate is Antwerp (3.4%), which are respectively the least and most populous and the least and most densely populated provinces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-statscomparison_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-statscomparison-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_opinion">Public opinion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Same-sex_marriage_in_Belgium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Public opinion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 2006 <a href="/wiki/Eurobarometer" title="Eurobarometer">Eurobarometer</a> found that 62% of Belgian respondents think same-sex marriages should be allowed in Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2008 survey by Delta Lloyd Life found that 76% of Belgians accept same-sex marriage and 46% of Belgians think same-sex couples can raise children just as well as opposite-sex couples can.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>A May 2013 Ipsos poll found that 67% of respondents were in favour of same-sex marriage and another 12% supported other form of recognition for same-sex couples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the Ifop poll, conducted in May 2013, 71% of <a href="/wiki/Belgians" title="Belgians">Belgians</a> supported allowing same-sex couples to marry and adopt children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 2015 Eurobarometer found that 77% of Belgians thought that same-sex marriage should be allowed throughout Europe, 20% were against.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
