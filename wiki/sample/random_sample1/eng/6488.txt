<p><b>Bellagio</b> (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Italian" title="Help:IPA for Italian">[belˈlaːdʒo]</a></span>, <i>Belàs</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[beˈlaːs]</a></span> in <a href="/wiki/Lombard_language" title="Lombard language">Lombard</a>) is a <i><a href="/wiki/Comune" title="Comune">comune</a></i> (municipality) in the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Como" title="Province of Como">Province of Como</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">Lombardy</a>. It is located on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_of_Como" title="Lake of Como">Lake Como</a>, also known by its Latin-derived name, the Lake of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lario" title="Lario">Lario</a>. The arms of the lake form an inverted Y. The triangular land mass at the base of the inverted Y is the <a href="/wiki/Triangolo_lariano" title="Triangolo lariano">Larian Triangle</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Como" title="Como">Como</a> arm of the lake lies to its south west, the <a href="/wiki/Lecco" title="Lecco">Lecco</a> arm of the lake to its south east. At the northern point of the triangle sits Bellagio, looking across to the northern arm of the lake and, behind it, the Alps. It has always been famous for its location.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellagio,_Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BellagioPromontorio.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/BellagioPromontorio.jpg/220px-BellagioPromontorio.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/BellagioPromontorio.jpg/330px-BellagioPromontorio.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/BellagioPromontorio.jpg/440px-BellagioPromontorio.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Bellagio is situated upon the cape of the land mass that divides <a href="/wiki/Lake_Como" title="Lake Como">Lake Como</a> in two. The city centre occupies the tip of the promontory, while other districts are scattered along the lake shores and up the slopes of the hills. The great <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> glaciations with their imposing flows coming from the <a href="/wiki/Valtellina" title="Valtellina">Valtellina</a> and Valchiavenna modelled the actual landscape of Lake Como: at least four times the glaciers went as far as <a href="/wiki/Brianza" title="Brianza">Brianza</a> to the south. From the ancient glacial blanket only the highest tops emerged, one of them Mount St. Primo, which obliged the glaciers to divide into two arms.</p>
<p>Nowadays, a luxuriance of trees and flowers is favoured by a mild and sweet climate. The average daytime temperature during winter is rarely below 6 to 7 °C (43 to 45 °F), while during summer it is around 25 to 28 °C (77 to 82 °F), mitigated during the afternoon by the characteristic <i>breva</i>, the gentle breeze of Lake Como.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Borgo">The Borgo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellagio,_Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Borgo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The historic centre of Bellagio shelters 350m southwest of the promontory of the <a href="/wiki/Triangolo_lariano" title="Triangolo lariano">Larian Triangle</a>, between the Villa Serbelloni on the hill and the Como arm of the lake. At the far tip of the promontory are a park and a marina. Parallel to the shore are three streets, Mazzini, Centrale and Garibaldi in ascending order. Cutting across them to form a sloped grid are seven medieval stone stairs ("salite") running uphill. The Basilica of San Giacomo and a stone tower, sole relic of medieval defences ("Torre delle Arti Bellagio"), sit in a piazza at the top.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ratcliffe_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ratcliffe-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellagio,_Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Before_the_Romans">Before the Romans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellagio,_Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Before the Romans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Even though there are signs of a human presence around Bellagio in the <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a> Period (about 30,000 years ago), it is only in the 7th to 5th centuries BC that there appears on the promontory a <i>castellum</i>, perhaps a place of worship and of exchange which served the numerous small villages on the lake.</p>
<p>The first identifiable inhabitants of the territory of Bellagio, from 400BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> were the <a href="/wiki/Insubres" title="Insubres">Insubres</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> tribe in part of Lombardy and on Lake Como up to the centre of the lake, occupying the western shore (the <a href="/wiki/Orobii" title="Orobii">Orobii</a> had the northern arm of the lake and its east bank). The Insubres lived free and independently until the arrival of the <a href="/wiki/Gauls" title="Gauls">Gauls</a>, led by Belloveso, who, around the year 600 BC, undid the Insubres and settled in Milan and Como, occupying the shores of the lake and creating a garrison at the extreme point of their conquest, Bellagio (fancifully Bellasium, named after their commander Belloveso).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ratcliffe_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ratcliffe-1">[1]</a></sup> The Gauls thus became Gallo-Insubres, merged with the primitive inhabitants and introduced their customs and traditions, leaving traces in local names: Crux Galli (now Grosgalla), on the side of Lezzeno, and Gallo, a small chapel on the old road of Limonta which marks today the border between the two municipalities.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Romans">The Romans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellagio,_Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Romans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 225 BC, the territory of the Gallo-Insubres was occupied by the Romans, in their gradual expansion to the north. The Romans, led by consul <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Claudius_Marcellus" title="Marcus Claudius Marcellus">Marcus Claudius Marcellus</a>, defeated the Gallo-Insubres in a fierce battle near Camerlata, occupying Como and the shores of the lake. Insubre hopes of independence were raised by an alliance with <a href="/wiki/Hannibal" title="Hannibal">Hannibal</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Second_Punic_War" title="Second Punic War">Second Punic War</a>, but dashed by defeat in 104 BC and absorption into a Roman province in 80 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manco_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Manco-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bellagio became both a Roman garrison and a point of passage and wintering for the Roman armies on their way through to the province of <a href="/wiki/Raetia" title="Raetia">Raetia</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spl%C3%BCgen_pass" title="Splügen pass">Splügen pass</a>. Troops wintered at the foot of the present Villa Serbelloni, sheltered from north winds and the Mediterranean climate. Such variant Latin names as Belacius and Bislacus suggest Bellagio was originally Bi-lacus ("between the lakes").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ratcliffe_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ratcliffe-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Between 81 and 77 BC <a href="/wiki/Cornelius_Scipio" title="Cornelius Scipio">Cornelius Scipio</a> brought 3,000 Latin colonists to Lake Como. From 59 BC <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>, as pro-consul, brought up another 5000 colonists, most importantly 500 Greeks from Sicily. Their names are still borne by their descendants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manco_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Manco-4">[4]</a></sup> Bellagio became a mixture of races which became more and more complex in the following centuries. Also it increased its strategic importance because, as well as a place for wintering, it sheltered warships especially at Loppia, where the natural creek made it easy to repair them. Around Loppia there formed one of the first suburbs of Bellagio.</p>
<p>The Romans introduced many Mediterranean crops, including the olive and laurel; from the name of the latter (Laurus) derives the Latin name of Lake Como (Larius). Among the other plant species introduced were the chestnut, already widespread in southern Italy, the cypress, so well naturalised today as to be considered native, and many kinds of herbaceous plants.</p>
<p>In the early decades of the Empire, two great figures brought fame to the lake and Bellagio: <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Younger" title="Pliny the Younger">Pliny the Younger</a>. Virgil, the Latin poet, visited Bellagio and remembered the lake in the second book of the <i>Georgics</i>, verse 155 ("or great Lario"). Pliny the Younger, resident in Como for most of the year, had, among others, a summer villa near the top of the hill of Bellagio; it was known as "Tragedy". Pliny describes in a letter the long periods he spent in his Bellagio villas, not only studying and writing but also hunting and fishing.</p>
<p>Through Bellagio passed, in 9 AD, the Roman legions (partly composed of soldiers from the Bellagio garrison) led by <a href="/wiki/Publius_Quinctilius_Varus" title="Publius Quinctilius Varus">Publius Quinctilius Varus</a>, which had to cross the Splügen pass into Germany against <a href="/wiki/Arminius" title="Arminius">Arminius</a>. They were annihilated in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Teutoburg_Forest" title="Battle of the Teutoburg Forest">Battle of the Teutoburg Forest</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Middle_Ages">The Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellagio,_Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>At the time of the barbarian invasions, <a href="/wiki/Narses" title="Narses">Narses</a>, a general of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Justinian" title="Justinian">Justinian</a>, in his long wanderings through Italy waging war, created along Lake Como a fortified line against the Goths. The line included the fortress of Bellagio, the <a href="/wiki/Isola_Comacina" title="Isola Comacina">Isola Comacina</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castel_Baradello" title="Castel Baradello">Castel Baradello</a>.</p>
<p>Nevertheless, around 568 the Lombards, led by <a href="/wiki/Alboin" title="Alboin">Alboin</a>, poured into the Po Valley and settled in various parts of Lombardy, in the valleys of the Alps and along the lakes. Even the fortress of Bellagio was occupied. In 744 King <a href="/wiki/Liutprand,_King_of_the_Lombards" title="Liutprand, King of the Lombards">Liutprand</a> settled there.</p>
<p>With their arrival in Italy, the Franks of Charlemagne descended on Piedmont and Lombardy and, through the high Alps, defeated the Lombards in the battle of Pavia of 773. The Lombard territory was divided into <a href="/wiki/County" title="County">counties</a> — thus the beginning of feudalism. Bellagio found itself in the county of Milan under the suzerainty of the Frankish kings.</p>
<p>The grandson of Charlemagne, Michel Chehade <a href="/wiki/Lothair_I" title="Lothair I">Lothair</a>, having deposed his father in 834, invested as feudal lords of the territory of Limonta and Civenna the monks of Saint Ambrose of Milan (together with the territory of <a href="/wiki/Campione_d%27Italia" title="Campione d'Italia">Campione d'Italia</a>). The inhabitants of these two places, which later belonged ecclesiastically to the parish of Bellagio ( St. John), were obliged to hand over some of their produce ( olive oil, chestnuts ...) to the monks, an obligation preserved for several centuries.</p>
<p>There followed the rule of the <a href="/wiki/Ottonian_dynasty" title="Ottonian dynasty">Ottonian dynasty</a> of Germany. During the reign of Henry V began a long war over the succession to the bishop of Como between Milan, supporting a bishop imposed by the German Emperor, and Como, which had already designated as bishop Guido Grimoldi, consecrated by the Pope. The war lasted ten years (1117–1127), with a series of small victories and defeats on land and water. Bellagio participated with its fleet as an ally of Milan, Isola Comacina and <a href="/wiki/Gravedona" title="Gravedona">Gravedona</a>. The war ended with the destruction of Como and its subjection to Milan, from which it took decades to recover. It is thought that by 1100 Bellagio was already a free commune and seat of a tribunal and that its dependence on Como was merely formal. However the strategic position of Bellagio was very important for the city of Como, and Bellagio had therefore to suffer more than one incursion from Como and fought numerous naval battles against its neighbour. In 1154, under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frederick_Barbarossa" title="Frederick Barbarossa">Frederick Barbarossa</a>, Bellagio was forced to swear loyalty and pay tribute to Como.</p>
<p>In 1169, after the destruction of Milan by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frederick_Barbarossa" title="Frederick Barbarossa">Frederick Barbarossa</a> (1162), Como attacked Isola Comacina, devastating it and forcing the inhabitants to flee to Varenna and Bellagio, at that time considered impregnable fortresses. The <a href="/wiki/Lombard_League" title="Lombard League">Lombard League</a> was formed, in which Bellagio also participated as an ally of Milan, intervening in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Legnano" title="Battle of Legnano">Battle of Legnano</a> (1176) against Barbarossa and Como.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Renaissance_and_the_Baroque">The Renaissance and the Baroque</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellagio,_Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Renaissance and the Baroque">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Towards the end of the 13th century, Bellagio, which had participated in numerous wars on the side of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ghibellines" title="Ghibellines">Ghibellines</a> (the pro-empire party), became part of the property of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Visconti" title="House of Visconti">House of Visconti</a> and was integrated into the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Milan" title="Duchy of Milan">Duchy of Milan</a>.</p>
<p>In 1440, during the lordship of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Visconti" title="House of Visconti">Visconti</a>, some Cernobbiesi attacked the prison of Bellagio in which the inmates were political prisoners. Liberated, they took flight into mountains of Bellagio, settling in a town that took the name of Cernobbio in memory of the country of origin of their liberators.</p>
<p>With the death of <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Maria_Visconti" title="Filippo Maria Visconti">Filippo Maria</a>, the House of Visconti lost power. For a short time the area was transformed into the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Ambrosian_Republic" title="Golden Ambrosian Republic">Ambrosian Republic</a> (1447–50), until Milan capitulated to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francesco_Sforza" title="Francesco Sforza">Francesco Sforza</a>, who became Duke of Milan and Lombardy. Bellagio, whose territory (and especially the fortress) was occupied by the troops of Sforza in 1449 during the war of succession, was one of the first towns on the lake to take sides and adhere to Sforza rule.</p>
<p>In 1508, under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ludovico_il_Moro" title="Ludovico il Moro">Ludovico il Moro</a> (1479–1508), the estate of Bellagio was taken from the bishop of Como and assigned to the Marquis of Stanga, treasurer, ambassador and friend of il Moro. Stanga built a new villa on Bellagio hill, later ruined in a raid by Cavargnoni.</p>
<p>In 1535, when <a href="/wiki/Francesco_II_Sforza" title="Francesco II Sforza">Francesco II Sforza</a> (the last <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duke_of_Milan" title="Duke of Milan">Duke of Milan</a>) died, there started for Lombardy and the land around the Lake of Lario two centuries of <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish</a> rule (the period in which <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Manzoni" title="Alessandro Manzoni">Alessandro Manzoni</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Betrothed_(Manzoni_novel)" title="The Betrothed (Manzoni novel)">The Betrothed</a></i> is set). The so-called Derta steps that lead from the neighbourhood of Guggiate to that of Suira were built under the Spanish.</p>
<p>In 1533, <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Sfondrati" title="Francesco Sfondrati">Francesco Sfondrati</a>, married to a Visconti, had acquired the fiefdom of Bellagio and for more than 200 years the Sfondrati family, from the highest rank of Milanese society, ruled Bellagio. The ruins of the sumptuous Stanga building were restructured by Francesco and, successively, by Ercole Sfondrati, who spent the last years of his life in pious religious passion in the villa. On the same peninsula he built the church and convent of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Friars_Minor_Capuchin" title="Order of Friars Minor Capuchin">Capuchins</a> (1614), investing enormous capital in the setting, where appeared cypress trees and <a href="/wiki/Osmanthus_fragrans" title="Osmanthus fragrans">sweet olives</a>.</p>
<p>Favoured by Bellagio's ideal position for transport and trade, various small industries flourished, most notably candle-making and silk weaving with its concomitant silk worms and mulberry trees. With the death in 1788 of Carlo, last of the Sfondrati, Bellagio passed to Count Alessandro <a href="/wiki/Serbelloni" title="Serbelloni">Serbelloni</a>, henceforth Serbelloni Sfondrati.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_eighteenth_and_nineteenth_centuries">The eighteenth and nineteenth centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellagio,_Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The eighteenth and nineteenth centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>During the brief <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleonic" title="Napoleonic">Napoleonic</a> period, the port of Bellagio assumed military and strategic importance. A decision, apparently of secondary importance, was to guide the destiny of Bellagio for the two succeeding two centuries: the decision of Count Francesco Melzi d'Eril, Duke of <a href="/wiki/Lodi,_Lombardy" title="Lodi, Lombardy">Lodi</a> and Vice President of the <a href="/wiki/Cisalpine_Republic" title="Cisalpine Republic">Cisalpine Republic</a> to establish here his summer home. Count Melzi proceeded to build on the west bank near Loppia a magnificent villa. That brought to the area the flower of the Milanese nobility and the promontory was transformed into a most elegant and refined court. Roads suitable for carriages were built, first of all to link the villas and the palaces and then towards the town centre; finally the provincial road Erba–Bellagio was completed. The fame of the lakeside town became well known outside the borders of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Lombardy%E2%80%93Venetia" title="Kingdom of Lombardy–Venetia">Kingdom of Lombardy–Venetia</a>: even the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Francis_I" title="Emperor Francis I">Emperor Francis I</a> of Austria visited in 1816 and returned in 1825 to stay in the Villas Serbelloni, Trotti and Melzi.</p>
<p>The Romantic discovery of landscape was changing how the Italian lakes were seen. <a href="/wiki/Stendhal" title="Stendhal">Stendhal</a> had first visited in 1810:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>What can one say about Lake Maggiore, about the Borromean Islands, about Lake Como, unless it be that one pities those who are not madly in love with them … the sky is pure, the air mild, and one recognises the land beloved of the gods, the happy land that neither barbarous invasions nor civil discords could deprive of its heaven-sent blessings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>At Bellagio he was the guest of the Melzi d’Eril, from whose villa he wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I isolate myself in a room on the second floor; there, I lift my gaze to the most beautiful view in the world, after the Gulf of Naples … <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Franz_Liszt" title="Franz Liszt">Franz Liszt</a> and his mistress <a href="/wiki/Marie_d%27Agoult" title="Marie d'Agoult">Comtesse Marie d'Agoult</a> stayed for four months of 1837 on their way from Switzerland to Como and Milan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In Bellagio he wrote many of the piano pieces which became <i>Album d'un voyageur (1835–38)</i>, landscapes seen through the eyes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byron" title="Byron">Byron</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tienne_Pivert_de_Senancour" title="Étienne Pivert de Senancour">Senancour</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> These works contributed much to the image of Bellagio and the lake as a site of Romantic feeling. D'Agoult's letters show they were sadly aware of drawing an age of motorised tourism in their train.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1838, Bellagio received with all honours the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Ferdinand_I" title="Emperor Ferdinand I">Emperor Ferdinand I</a>, the Archduke <a href="/wiki/Archduke_Rainer_Ferdinand_of_Austria" title="Archduke Rainer Ferdinand of Austria">Rainer</a> and the Minister <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metternich" title="Metternich">Metternich</a>, who came from <a href="/wiki/Varenna" title="Varenna">Varenna</a> on the <i>Lario</i>, the first steamboat on the lake, launched in 1826. Bellagio was one of the localities most frequented by the Lombardy nobility and saw the construction of villas and gardens. Luxury shops opened in the village and tourists crowded onto the lakeshore drive. Space was not sufficient and it was decided to cover the old port which came up as far as the arcade in order to construct a large square.</p>
<p>Gustav Flaubert visited Bellagio in 1845. He told his travel diary:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>One could live and die here. The outlook seems designed as a balm to the eyes. … the horizon is lined with snow and the foreground alternates between the graceful and the rugged — a truly Shakespearean landcape, all the forces of nature are brought together, with an overwhelming sense of vastness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Risorgimento">The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Risorgimento" title="Risorgimento">Risorgimento</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellagio,_Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Risorgimento">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1859, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Italian_War_of_Independence" title="Second Italian War of Independence">Second Italian War of Independence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Garibaldi" title="Giuseppe Garibaldi">Garibaldi's</a> <a href="/wiki/Hunters_of_the_Alps" title="Hunters of the Alps">Hunters of the Alps</a> defeated Austrian troops at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Fermo" title="San Fermo">San Fermo</a>, entering Como and bringing the province under <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">Piedmontese</a> rule. Bellagio thus became part of the Kingdom of Italy under the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Savoy" title="House of Savoy">House of Savoy</a> until Germany created in 1943 the puppet <a href="/wiki/Italian_Social_Republic" title="Italian Social Republic">Italian Social Republic</a> under <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a>.</p>
<p>Tourism in the Kingdom of Italy had now become the principal economic resource of the people of Bellagio and from this period on the history of Bellagio coincides with that of its hotels. The first was the present Hotel Metropole, founded in 1825 from the transformation of the old hostelry of Abbondio Genazzini into the first real hotel on the Lario, the Hotel Genazzini. Following this example in the space of a few years came several splendid hotels many of which are still operating, frequently in the hands of the same families who founded them: the Hotel Firenze, built on the old house of the captain of the <i>Lario</i> opened in 1852; the Grand Hotel Bellagio (now the Grand Hotel Villa Serbelloni) opened in 1872. In 1888 the three largest hotels (Genazzini, Grande Bretagne and Grand Hotel Bellagio) first replaced gaslight with electric, and only after this were they followed by many patrician houses. Bellagio was one of the first Italian tourist resorts to become international, but it has never degenerated into a place of mass tourism.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_twentieth_century">The twentieth century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellagio,_Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The twentieth century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bellagio was part of the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Social_Republic" title="Italian Social Republic">Italian Social Republic</a> (RSI) from 1943 to 1945. The <a href="/wiki/Futurism" title="Futurism">Futurist</a> writer and poet <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Tommaso_Marinetti" title="Filippo Tommaso Marinetti">Filippo Tommaso Marinetti</a>, a Mussolini loyalist who had helped shape Fascist philosophy, remained in the RSI as a propagandist until his death from a heart attack at Bellagio in December, 1944.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Luchino_Visconti" title="Luchino Visconti">Luchino Visconti</a> put Bellagio in a scene of his film <i><a href="/wiki/Rocco_and_His_Brothers" title="Rocco and His Brothers">Rocco and His Brothers</a></i> (1960). The scene is on the Europa Promenade, between the pier and the then-functioning Hotel Grande Bretagne.</p>
<p>In 2014, Bellagio merged with the town of <a href="/wiki/Civenna" title="Civenna">Civenna</a>: the new municipality retains the name of Bellagio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Villas">Villas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellagio,_Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Villas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Along the banks of the promontory of Bellagio are many old patrician houses, each surrounded by parks and gardens of trees. Some like Villa Serbelloni and Villa Melzi d'Esti are open to the public.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Villa_Serbelloni">Villa Serbelloni</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellagio,_Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Villa Serbelloni">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Just behind the hill of the promontory into the lake, protected from the winds, is the building complex of Villa Serbelloni. The villa dominates the town's historic centre. It can be reached from Via Garibaldi. It was built in the fifteenth century in place of an old castle razed in 1375. Villa Serbelloni was later rebuilt several times. In 1788 it came into the possession of Alessandro Serbelloni (1745–1826) who enriched it with precious decorations and works of art of the 17th and 18th centuries.Today you can visit only the gardens. The trails, as well as the villa, lead to the remains of the 16th century Capuchin monastery and the Sfondrata, a residence built by the Sfondrati family indeed, overlooking the Lecco branch of the lake.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BellagiodiEst.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/BellagiodiEst.JPG/220px-BellagiodiEst.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/BellagiodiEst.JPG/330px-BellagiodiEst.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/BellagiodiEst.JPG/440px-BellagiodiEst.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BellagiodiEst.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
View of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bellagio_(Italian_region)" title="Bellagio (Italian region)">Bellagio</a>. The building under the crown of the hill is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Villa_Serbelloni" title="Villa Serbelloni">Villa Serbelloni</a>, believed to have been built on the site of Pliny the Younger's villa "Tragedy." His villa "Comedy" was down on the shore.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On the inside, elegant halls with vault and coffered ceilings follow one another accurately decorated in the style of the 17th and 18th centuries. All around, the park develops along most of the promontory of Bellagio with vast tracts of thick woods where the Serbelloni gardeners had traced paths which nowadays still lead the way amongst the small clearings and English style gardens.</p>
<p>As noted by Balbiani, rather than being a garden, it is a real "wood, opened by spacious and comfortable paths, and plants with all generations of high trunk trees”; amongst which, oak trees, conifers, fir trees, holm oaks, osmanti, myrtles and junipers, "… but above all trees, here situated is the pine tree, which, with its gnarled trunk acts as a screen against the storms”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Occasionally, the vegetation thins out at panoramic points which overlook the two branches of the lake, offering a prospect from the slopes of the hill, where the rose bushes flower during the season with their varied colours. The roughness of the rocky plane along the winding path which goes up to the villa has not stopped the construction of terraces and flower beds with yews and boxes trimmed geometrically. Along the upper part of the park is a long row of cypress trees and some palm trees of considerable dimensions.</p>
<p>In 1905, the villa was transformed into a luxury hotel. In 1959 it became the property of the Rockefeller Foundation of New York at the bequest of the American-born Princess of Thurn and Taxis, who had bought it in 1930. Since 1960 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bellagio_Center" title="Bellagio Center">Bellagio Center</a> in the villa has been home to international conferences, often held by US scholars, housed in the former villa or in the grounds.</p>
<p>Quite different is the <a class="external text" href="http://www.villaserbelloni.com/Bellagio/en/" rel="nofollow">Grand Hotel Villa Serbelloni</a>, below on the water's edge. A luxurious neo-classical villa built in the 1850s for an aristocratic Milanese family became the nucleus of the (then-called) Grand Hotel Bellagio, opened in 1873. The hotel retains its original <a href="/wiki/Belle_%C3%89poque" title="Belle Époque">Belle Époque</a> fittings.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Villa_Melzi_d.27Eril">Villa Melzi d'Eril</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellagio,_Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Villa Melzi d'Eril">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Villa_Melzi_(Bellagio)_-_DSC02854.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3240" data-file-width="4320" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Villa_Melzi_%28Bellagio%29_-_DSC02854.JPG/220px-Villa_Melzi_%28Bellagio%29_-_DSC02854.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Villa_Melzi_%28Bellagio%29_-_DSC02854.JPG/330px-Villa_Melzi_%28Bellagio%29_-_DSC02854.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Villa_Melzi_%28Bellagio%29_-_DSC02854.JPG/440px-Villa_Melzi_%28Bellagio%29_-_DSC02854.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Villa_Melzi_(Bellagio)_-_DSC02854.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Villa Melzi d'Eril: the garden front</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This significant building overlooking the lake was built between 1808 and 1815 by the architect <a href="/wiki/Giocondo_Albertolli" title="Giocondo Albertolli">Giocondo Albertolli</a> for Francesco Melzi d'Eril, created Duke of <a href="/wiki/Lodi,_Lombardy" title="Lodi, Lombardy">Lodi</a> by <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a> for whom he filled the role of vice-president of the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Republic_(Napoleonic)" title="Italian Republic (Napoleonic)">Italian Republic</a> from 1802. From 1805, with the advent of the short-lived Napoleonic <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(Napoleonic)" title="Kingdom of Italy (Napoleonic)">Kingdom of Italy</a>, he was its Chancellor.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MelziGarden3.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/MelziGarden3.JPG/220px-MelziGarden3.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/MelziGarden3.JPG/330px-MelziGarden3.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/MelziGarden3.JPG/440px-MelziGarden3.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MelziGarden3.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Villa Melzi: the Moorish Pavilion, furnished with neoclassical busts</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Even after his political career had ended, since this was a Melzi residence, the construction, which he wanted as elegant as the Royal Villa of <a href="/wiki/Monza" title="Monza">Monza</a> and the other villas around Lake of Como, was decorated and furnished by famous artists of the period: painters <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Appiani" title="Andrea Appiani">Appiani</a> and <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Bossi" title="Giuseppe Bossi">Bossi</a>, sculptors <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canova" title="Canova">Canova</a> and <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Comolli" title="Giovanni Battista Comolli">Comolli</a>, and the medalist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Luigi_Manfredini&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Luigi Manfredini (page does not exist)">Luigi Manfredini</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The duke had a collector’s passion which, in the region on Lake Como, had no rival except that of Giovan Battista Sommariva, owner of the villa bearing the same name (nowadays <a href="/wiki/Villa_Carlotta" title="Villa Carlotta">Villa Carlotta</a>) who, politically defeated by Melzi himself (preferred by Napoleon as vice-president), tried to regain lost prestige by assembling an extraordinary art collection.</p>
<p>Villa Melzi is set in English style gardens which develop harmoniously along the banks of the lake, the last reaches of the view from Bellagio towards the hills to the south. Making such a garden required notable changes to the structure of the land and outstanding supporting walls. In such surroundings, enriched by monuments, artefacts (amongst which are a Venetian gondola transported to Bellagio expressly for Napoleon, and two precious Egyptian statues), rare exotic plants, ancient trees, hedges of camellias, groves of azaleas and gigantic rhododendrons, the villa, the chapel and the glass house constitute an ensemble in which the neoclassical style reaches one of its highest peaks.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellagio,_Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rowing">Rowing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellagio,_Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Rowing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rowing is based at the Bellagina Sporting Union, a club specializing in football and especially rowing: world rowing champions <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Enrico_Gandola&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Enrico Gandola (page does not exist)">Enrico Gandola</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alberto_Belgeri&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alberto Belgeri (page does not exist)">Alberto Belgeri</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Igor_Pescialli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Igor Pescialli (page does not exist)">Igor Pescialli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franco_Sancassani" title="Franco Sancassani">Franco Sancassani</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daniele_Gilardoni" title="Daniele Gilardoni">Daniele Gilardoni</a> were born in Bellagio and began their racing careers with Bellagina.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cycling">Cycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellagio,_Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Cycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From Bellagio starts the climb to the Sanctuary of the <a href="/wiki/Madonna_del_Ghisallo" title="Madonna del Ghisallo">Madonna del Ghisallo</a>, the patron saint of cyclists, and therefore an important destination for fans of the sport. The ascent covers a total distance of about 4 km and has a vertical rise of about 500 meters; professional cyclists can do it in 20 minutes. You can also make the climb from <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Onno&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Onno (page does not exist)">Onno</a> to <a href="/wiki/Valbrona" title="Valbrona">Valbrona</a> on the eastern shore of the lake, and the <a href="/wiki/Muro_di_Sormano" title="Muro di Sormano">Wall of Sormano</a> on the road to the western shore. These two climbs, with the ascent to the Sanctuary, are part of the <a class="external text" href="http://www.latrisdellario.it/" rel="nofollow">Tris del Lario</a> competition.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trekking">Trekking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellagio,_Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Trekking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Treks of all degrees of difficulty are possible around and above Bellagio on the Larian Triangle. <a class="external text" href="http://bellagiolifestyle.it/index.php/en/itinerari-e-guide/temi-e-categorie/sentieri-e-trekking" rel="nofollow">Bellagio Lifestyle</a> gives the major treks with maps and route descriptions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuisine">Cuisine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellagio,_Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Cuisine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The traditional Bellagino feast day dish is the Tóch. Eaten with a wooden spoon, it is composed of polenta mixed with butter and cheese and accompanied with dried fish from the lake, cold, stuffed chicken or home-made salami. Red wine is shared from a communal jug. For dessert, <i>miasca</i>—cake made with cornflour and dried fruit; <i>pan mein</i>—made with white and yellow flour, eggs, butter, milk and elder flowers; or <i>paradèl</i>—a wafer of white flour, milk and sugar.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Road">Road</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellagio,_Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Road">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bellagio is accessible on either side of the Larian Triangle by slow, narrow and winding roads from <a href="/wiki/Como" title="Como">Como</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lecco" title="Lecco">Lecco</a>. It is much safer to drive from <a href="/wiki/Como" title="Como">Como</a> up to <a href="/wiki/Cadenabbia" title="Cadenabbia">Cadenabbia</a> and use the reasonably priced car ferry for the quick trip across to Bellagio. An alternative is to take the four-lane road on the eastern shore of the right arm of the lake to <a href="/wiki/Varenna" title="Varenna">Varenna</a>, then take the ferry to Bellagio. Narrow stone stairs make most of the Borgo inaccessible to cars.</p>
<p>Public buses run daily from <a href="/wiki/Como" title="Como">Como</a> to Bellagio.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Twin_towns">Twin towns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellagio,_Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Twin towns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_twin_towns_and_sister_cities_in_Italy" title="List of twin towns and sister cities in Italy">List of twin towns and sister cities in Italy</a></div>
<p>Bellagio is a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/Douzelage" title="Douzelage">Douzelage</a>, a unique <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Town_twinning" title="Town twinning">town twinning</a> association of 24 towns across the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>. This active town twinning began in 1991 and there are regular events, such as a produce market from each of the other countries and festivals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Douzelage_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Douzelage-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DouzelageMembers_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-DouzelageMembers-16">[16]</a></sup> Discussions regarding membership are also in hand with three further towns (<a href="/wiki/Agros,_Cyprus" title="Agros, Cyprus">Agros</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0kofja_Loka" title="Škofja Loka">Škofja Loka</a> in <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tryavna" title="Tryavna">Tryavna</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>).</p>
