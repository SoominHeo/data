<p><b>Bellona</b> was an <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Ancient Roman</a> goddess of war. Her main attribute is the military <a href="/wiki/Helmet" title="Helmet">helmet</a> worn on her head; she often holds a sword, spear or shield, and brandishes a torch or whip as she rides into battle in a four-horse chariot. Her iconography was further extended by painters and sculptors following the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_cult_goddess">Ancient cult goddess</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellona_(goddess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ancient cult goddess">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Originally named Duellona in the <a href="/wiki/Italic_languages" title="Italic languages">Italic languages</a>, Bellona was an ancient <a href="/wiki/Sabines" title="Sabines">Sabine</a> goddess of war, identified with <a href="/wiki/Nerio" title="Nerio">Nerio</a>, the consort of the war god <a href="/wiki/Mars_(mythology)" title="Mars (mythology)">Mars</a>, and later with her Greek equivalent <a href="/wiki/Enyo" title="Enyo">Enyo</a>. Her first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temple_of_Bellona_(Rome)" title="Temple of Bellona (Rome)">temple</a> in Rome was dedicated in 296 BCE, where her festival was celebrated on June 3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Her priests were known as Bellonarii and used to wound their own arms or legs as a blood sacrifice to her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> These rites took place on the 24 March, called the day of blood (<i>dies sanguinis</i>) after the ceremony. In consequence of this practice, which approximated to the rites dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Cybele" title="Cybele">Cybele</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a>, both Enyo and Bellona became identified with her <a href="/wiki/Cappadocia" title="Cappadocia">Cappadocian</a> aspect, <a href="/wiki/Ma_(goddess)" title="Ma (goddess)">Ma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Roman <a href="/wiki/Campus_Martius" title="Campus Martius">Campus Martius</a> area in which Bellonaâs temple was sited had <a href="/wiki/Extraterritoriality" title="Extraterritoriality">extraterritorial status</a>. Ambassadors from foreign states, who were not allowed to enter the city proper, stayed in this complex. The area around the temple of Bellona was considered to symbolize foreign soil, and there the Senate met with ambassadors and received victorious generals prior to their Triumphs. And it was here too that <a href="/wiki/Roman_Senate" title="Roman Senate">Roman Senate</a> meetings relating to foreign war were conducted. Beside the temple was the war column (<i>columna bellica</i>), which represented the boundary of Rome. To declare war on a distant state, a javelin was thrown over the column by one of the priests concerned with diplomacy (<i>fetiales</i>) from Roman territory towards the direction of the enemy land and this symbolical attack was considered the opening of war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the military cult of Bellona, she was associated with <a href="/wiki/Virtus_(deity)" title="Virtus (deity)">Virtus</a>, the personification of valour. She then travelled outside Rome with the imperial legions and her temples have been recorded in France, Germany, Britain and North Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_arts">In the arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellona_(goddess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In the arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poetry">Poetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellona_(goddess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Poetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In poetry the name Bellona is often used simply as a synonym for war, but the goddess often appears as a character in the <a href="/wiki/Thebaid_(Latin_poem)" title="Thebaid (Latin poem)"><i>Thebaid</i></a> of <a href="/wiki/Statius" title="Statius">Statius</a>, symbolising warâs destructive and belligerent aspect. There she is represented as carrying a spear and a flaming torch or waving a blood-stained sword and riding in a chariot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>While she does not figure as a character in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>'s plays, she receives several mentions. In <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_IV,_Part_I" title="Henry IV, Part I">Henry IV, Part I</a></i>, Hotspur describes her as "the fire-eyed maid of smoky war" (IV.i.119). In <a href="/wiki/The_Two_Noble_Kinsmen" title="The Two Noble Kinsmen">The Two Noble Kinsmen</a>, set in pre-Roman Athens, the sister of Hippolyta will solicit her divine aid for Theseus against Thebes (I.iii.13). At the start of the play named after him, <i><a href="/wiki/Macbeth" title="Macbeth">Macbeth</a></i> is introduced as a violent and brave warrior when the Thane of Ross calls him "Bellona's bridegroom" (I.ii.54), that is to say, the equivalent of Mars.</p>
<p>She also does not appear as a character in <a href="/wiki/Paradise_Lost" title="Paradise Lost">Paradise Lost</a> by <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">John Milton</a>; however, she is used in multiple epic similes in the book. The narrator describes Satan's ears as not "less pealed with noises loud and ruinous" than when "Bellona storms with all her battering engines" (II, 920).</p>
<p>In more modern times, <a href="/wiki/Adam_Lindsay_Gordon" title="Adam Lindsay Gordon">Adam Lindsay Gordon</a> dedicated an energetic Swinburnean evocation of the "false goddess" who leads men astray in his poem "Bellona", published in Australia in 1867.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> She also figures in <a href="/wiki/Edgell_Rickword" title="Edgell Rickword">Edgell Rickword</a>âs <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> poem "The Traveller". There the poet describes himself as marching toward the front line in the company of Art, the god <a href="/wiki/Pan_(god)" title="Pan (god)">Pan</a> and the works of <a href="/wiki/Walter_Pater" title="Walter Pater">Walter Pater</a>. Meeting Bellona as they approach the fighting, one by one the pleasurable companions are forced to flee before the violence of war, until the goddess rejoices in having him to herself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cantata_and_opera">Cantata and opera</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellona_(goddess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cantata and opera">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bellona appears in the prologue of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rameau" title="Rameau">Rameau</a>'s opera, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Les_Indes_Galantes" title="Les Indes Galantes">Les Indes Galantes</a></i> (1735), in which the call of love ultimately triumphs over that of war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In a Bach <i>dramma per musica</i> performed two years before, the goddess even quitted her usual ferocity in order to congratulate <a href="/wiki/Maria_Josepha_of_Austria" title="Maria Josepha of Austria">Maria Josepha of Austria</a>, Princess Elector of Saxony and Queen of Poland, on her birthday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, she retains her harsh aspect in "Prometheus Absolved" by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Ambrogio_Migliavacca" title="Giovanni Ambrogio Migliavacca">Giovanni Ambrogio Migliavacca</a> (1718â1795). In this cantata celebrating the birth of the Archduchess Isabella in 1762, the gods sit in judgement on <a href="/wiki/Prometheus" title="Prometheus">Prometheus</a>, some arguing for clemency, while Bellona and others demand rigour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> She also plays her proper part in the âheroic cantataâ created by the composer <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Bianchi_(composer)" title="Francesco Bianchi (composer)">Francesco Bianchi</a> and the librettist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorenzo_da_Ponte" title="Lorenzo da Ponte">Lorenzo da Ponte</a>, titled "The Wedding of the Thames and Bellona" (<i>Le nozze del Tamigi e Bellona</i>). This was performed in London to mark the British naval victory over the Spanish at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cape_St._Vincent_(1797)" title="Battle of Cape St. Vincent (1797)">Battle of Cape St. Vincent (1797)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Painting_and_sculpture">Painting and sculpture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellona_(goddess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Painting and sculpture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Bellona is commonly portrayed wearing a plumed helmet and dressed in armour, or at least a <a href="/wiki/Breastplate" title="Breastplate">breastplate</a> with a skirt beneath. In her hand she carries a spear, shield, or other weapons, and occasionally sounds a <a href="/wiki/Trumpet" title="Trumpet">trumpet</a> for the attack. Anciently she was associated with the winged <a href="/wiki/Victory" title="Victory">Victory</a> holding a laurel crown in her hand, a statue of whom she sometimes carries; when she appears on war memorials she may hold that attribute herself.</p>
<p>Examples of such an armoured figure appear in the 1633 painting attributed to <a href="/wiki/Rembrandt" title="Rembrandt">Rembrandt</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and the statues by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Baptist_Straub" title="Johann Baptist Straub">Johann Baptist Straub</a> (1770) and <a href="/wiki/Johann_Wilhelm_Beyer" title="Johann Wilhelm Beyer">Johann Wilhelm Beyer</a> (1773â80). In the latter she appears with the god <a href="/wiki/Janus" title="Janus">Janus</a>, since both were associated with the Roman ceremonies of declaring war. In the case of Janus, the doors to his temple were left open during the whole period of hostilities.</p>
<p>Straubâs statue has a <a href="/wiki/Gorgon" title="Gorgon">gorgon</a>âs head on her shield to instil terror in her enemies, as does the Rembrandt painting, although this was added later, probably as a response to other examples of this new iconographical departure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In the bust by <a href="/wiki/Bertram_Mackennal" title="Bertram Mackennal">Bertram Mackennal</a> she wears a gorgon mounted on her helmet, while in other depictions it is on the breastplate. Another common innovation was Bellonaâs association with cannons, as in the drawing by Hans Krieg (1590â1645) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and the 1700 ceiling fresco at Hammerschloss SchmidmÃ¼hlen by Hans Georg Asam (1649â1711).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> An early Dutch engraving in a series of prints depicting <i>Personifications of Industrial and Professional Life</i> suggests that it is this goddess who inspires the invention of war material, showing her seated in a factory workshop with all manner of arms at her feet (plate 6, see the Gallery below). In the fresco by Constantino Brumidi in the <a href="/wiki/Brumidi_Corridors" title="Brumidi Corridors">U.S. Capitol</a> (1855â60) her image is updated. There she is shown standing next to an <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">artillery</a> piece and has the stars and stripes on her shield.</p>
<p>Not all representations of Bellona wear armour. The statues by <a href="/wiki/Alvise_Tagliapietra" title="Alvise Tagliapietra">Alvise Tagliapietra</a> at St Petersburg (c.1710) and the <a href="/wiki/J._Paul_Getty_Museum" title="J. Paul Getty Museum">J. Paul Getty Museum</a>âs by <a href="/wiki/Augustin_Pajou" title="Augustin Pajou">Augustin Pajou</a> (1775/85)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> are largely naked, although otherwise wearing or carrying some of the goddessâ other attributes. There are Classical references that sanction this, however. In Valerius Flaccusâ <i><a href="/wiki/Argonautica" title="Argonautica">Argonautica</a></i>, for example, appears the description "Bellona with bare flank, her brazen weapons clanging as she moved" (3. 60).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> A further poetic reference taken up by a painter occurs in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_Jean_Fran%C3%A7ois_Lagren%C3%A9e" title="Louis Jean FranÃ§ois LagrenÃ©e">Louis Jean FranÃ§ois LagrenÃ©e</a>âs "Bellona Presenting the Reins of his Horses to Mars" (1766). This illustrates a speech from <a href="/wiki/Claudian" title="Claudian">Claudian</a>âs <i>In Ruffinum</i> where Mars requests "Let Bellona bring my helmet and Terror guide the reins" (<i>Fer galleam Bellona mihi, nexusque rotarum tende Pavor</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jan_van_Mieris" title="Jan van Mieris">Jan van Mieris</a>â allegorical painting of "Wisdom restraining Bellona" (1685) is also poetic. There the seated figure of Wisdom clasps the right hand of the helmeted goddess, who is turning to leave, her cloak fluttering behind her and her shield held high in her outstretched left hand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_statements">Public statements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bellona_(goddess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Public statements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As well as having a decorative function, representations of the goddess had a public function too. Batholomaeus Sprangerâs "Bellona Leading the Imperial Armies against the Turks" (see above) played its part in Austriaâs anti-Turkish propaganda during the <a href="/wiki/Long_Turkish_War" title="Long Turkish War">Long Turkish War</a>. A later phase of the continuing conflict, culminating in victory at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Zenta" title="Battle of Zenta">battle of Zenta</a> in 1697, is marked by Jean Cosynâs celebratory doorway in Brussels in what is now known as the Maison de Bellone, at the centre of which presides the helmeted bust of the goddess surrounded by military standards and cannons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>A dynastic political statement is made in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marie_de_Medici_Cycle" title="Marie de Medici Cycle">"Marie de Medici as Bellona"</a> (1622/5), designed by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">Peter Paul Rubens</a> for her public rooms in the <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg_Palace" title="Luxembourg Palace">Luxembourg Palace</a>. He represents her there as a wielder of political power at a time when it had actually waned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> She is standing with armour, cannons and muskets at her feet, and her triumphs are underlined by emblems of victory. She carries a small statue of the winged goddess in her right hand, a smaller winged figure is mounted below the plumes of her helmet, while <a href="/wiki/Cupid" title="Cupid">cupids</a> hover above her, holding a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laurel_crown" title="Laurel crown">laurel crown</a>. Her portrayal contrasts with Rembrandtâs depiction of Bellona with the homely features of an ordinary Dutchwoman. This makes an anti-imperial statement, with the assurance that the new <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">Dutch Republic</a> is ready to defend itself, particularly against Spain, during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%E2%80%99_War" title="Thirty Yearsâ War">Thirty Yearsâ War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Auguste_Rodin" title="Auguste Rodin">Auguste Rodin</a>âs sculpture of a head of Bellona (1879) was originally created for a monument to the <a href="/wiki/French_Third_Republic" title="French Third Republic">French Third Republic</a> and shows even more belligerence. Modelled on his mistress Rose Beuret while in a bad mood, the head is drawn back in proud anger, turning in dynamic movement to look along the line of her right shoulder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Defence in actual war is the message of <a href="/wiki/Georg_Kolbe" title="Georg Kolbe">Georg Kolbe</a>âs Bellona fountain in <a href="/wiki/Wuppertal" title="Wuppertal">Wuppertal</a>. It was originally commissioned in 1915 and depicted the helmeted goddess carrying a sword in her left hand and inspiring a kneeling young man. The statue was not erected until 1922, by which time it functioned as a war memorial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The use of Bellona in such structures was well established before this, dating back to her prominent use in Jean Cosynâs doorway. The Temple of Bellona, designed by <a href="/wiki/William_Chambers_(architect)" title="William Chambers (architect)">William Chambers</a> for <a href="/wiki/Kew_Gardens" title="Kew Gardens">Kew Gardens</a> in 1760, was projected as a celebration of the Anglo-Hanoverian war effort during the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a> and eventually housed plaques honouring the regiments that served in it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> These, however, related primarily to remembrance of victory rather than of the fallen. It was not until a century afterwards that the French-Canadian victims of the Seven Years War were commemorated by a monument at <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>. Atop a tall column on the site of the battlefield, Bellona looks down, carrying a shield and laurel crown in her right hand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The statue was presented by <a href="/wiki/Jerome_Napoleon_Bonaparte_II" title="Jerome Napoleon Bonaparte II">JÃ©rÃ´me-NapolÃ©on</a> in 1862 as a gesture of reconciliation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Australian dead during the <a href="/wiki/Gallipoli_Campaign" title="Gallipoli Campaign">Gallipoli Campaign</a> were commemorated by a bronze bust of Bellona by <a href="/wiki/Bertram_Mackennal" title="Bertram Mackennal">Bertram Mackennal</a>, a former student of Rodin. This he presented to the Australian Government in Canberra as a memorial in 1916.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> As in Rodinâs bust, the helmeted head is turned to the right but the breasts are more in evidence. The fallen generally make their appearance later in such structures where Bellona is present. They accompany the sword-wielding goddess in <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Tilden" title="Douglas Tilden">Douglas Tilden</a>'s monument to the California Volunteers during the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="SpanishâAmerican War">SpanishâAmerican War</a> of 1898;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bialystok" title="Bialystok">Bialystok</a> memorial to the dead in the <a href="/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Soviet_War_in_1920" title="PolishâSoviet War in 1920">PolishâSoviet War in 1920</a>, she stands behind a soldier and holds aloft a laurel crown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Bellona on the First World War victory archway at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterloo_Station" title="Waterloo Station">Waterloo Station</a> is particularly memorable, however. Beneath the demonic sword-brandishing wraith with her gorgon necklace, cower and mourn, not the dead but the overlooked living victims of war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
