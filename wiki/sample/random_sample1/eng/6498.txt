<p><b>Flavius Belisarius</b> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Βελισάριος</span>, c. 505<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> – 565) was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General" title="General">general</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>. He was instrumental to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine_Emperor" title="Byzantine Emperor">Emperor</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Justinian" title="Justinian">Justinian</a>'s ambitious project of reconquering much of the Mediterranean territory of the former <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a>, which had been lost less than a century previously.</p>
<p>One of the defining features of Belisarius' career was his success despite varying levels of support from Justinian. His name is frequently given as one of the so-called "<a href="/wiki/Last_of_the_Romans" title="Last of the Romans">Last of the Romans</a>".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_career">Early life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Belisarius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Belisarius was probably born in <i>Germane</i> or <i>Germania</i>, a fortified town (some archaeological remains exist) on the site of present-day <a href="/wiki/Sapareva_Banya" title="Sapareva Banya">Sapareva Banya</a> in south-west <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, in the borders of <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paeonia_(kingdom)" title="Paeonia (kingdom)">Paeonia</a> or in <i><a href="/wiki/Ormenio" title="Ormenio">Germen</a></i> a town in <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a> near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adrianople" title="Adrianople">Adrianople</a>, nowadays in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Born into an <a href="/wiki/Illyrians" title="Illyrians">Illyrian</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Treadgold1997_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Treadgold1997-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barker1966_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barker1966-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Thracians" title="Thracians">Thracian</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> family of possible <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Gothic</a> ancestry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> he spoke Latin as a mother tongue and became a Roman soldier as a young man, serving as bodyguard of Emperor <a href="/wiki/Justin_I" title="Justin I">Justin I</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Treadgold1997_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Treadgold1997-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evans2003_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Evans2003-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>He came to the attention of Justin and his nephew, Justinian, as a promising and innovative officer. He was given permission by the emperor to form a bodyguard regiment (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bucellarii" title="Bucellarii">bucellarii</a></i>), of heavy cavalry, which he later expanded into a personal household regiment, 1,500 strong. Belisarius' <i>bucellarii</i> were the nucleus around which all the armies he would later command were organized. Armed with a lance, (possibly Hunnish style) composite bow, and broadsword, they were fully armoured to the standard of <a href="/wiki/Cataphract" title="Cataphract">heavy cavalry of the day</a>. A multi-purpose unit, they were capable of skirmishing at a distance with bow, like the Huns; or could act as heavy shock cavalry, charging an enemy with lance and sword. In essence, they combined the best and most dangerous aspects of both of Rome's greatest enemies, the Huns and the Goths.</p>

<p>Following Justin's death in 527, the new emperor, <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Justinian I</a>, appointed Belisarius to command the Roman army in the east to deal with incursions from the <a href="/wiki/Sasanian_Empire" title="Sasanian Empire">Sassanid Empire</a>. He quickly proved himself an able and effective commander, defeating the larger Sassanid army through superior generalship. In June/July 530, during the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_War" title="Iberian War">Iberian War</a>, he led the Romans to a stunning victory over the Sassanids in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dara" title="Battle of Dara">Battle of Dara</a>, followed by a tactical defeat at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Callinicum" title="Battle of Callinicum">Battle of Callinicum</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Euphrates" title="Euphrates">Euphrates</a> in 531—this was perhaps a strategic victory in that the Persians retreated to their own borders. This led to the negotiation of an "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eternal_Peace_(532)" title="Eternal Peace (532)">Eternal Peace</a>" with the Persians, and Roman payment of heavy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tributes" title="Tributes">tributes</a> for years in exchange for peace with Persia. This freed resources for redeployment elsewhere.</p>
<p>In 532, he was the highest-ranking military officer in the Imperial capital of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> when the <a href="/wiki/Nika_riots" title="Nika riots">Nika riots</a> broke out in the city (among factions of <a href="/wiki/Chariot_racing" title="Chariot racing">chariot racing</a> fans) and nearly resulted in the overthrow of Justinian. Belisarius, with the help of the <i><a href="/wiki/Magister_militum" title="Magister militum">magister militum</a></i> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Illyricum" title="Diocese of Illyricum">Illyricum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mundus_(general)" title="Mundus (general)">Mundus</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Narses" title="Narses">Narses</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_the_Armenian" title="John the Armenian">John the Armenian</a>, suppressed the rebellion with a bloodbath in the Hippodrome, the gathering place of the rebels, that is said to have claimed the lives of 30,000 people.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_campaigns">Military campaigns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Belisarius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Military campaigns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Against_the_Vandals">Against the Vandals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Belisarius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Against the Vandals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vandalic_War" title="Vandalic War">Vandalic War</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vandalic_War_campaign_map.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1147" data-file-width="1700" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Vandalic_War_campaign_map.png/280px-Vandalic_War_campaign_map.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Vandalic_War_campaign_map.png/420px-Vandalic_War_campaign_map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Vandalic_War_campaign_map.png/560px-Vandalic_War_campaign_map.png 2x" width="280"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vandalic_War_campaign_map.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of the Vandalic War</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>For his efforts, Belisarius was rewarded by Justinian with the command of a land and sea expedition against the Vandal Kingdom, mounted in 533–534. The Romans had political, religious, and strategic reasons for such a campaign. The pro-Roman Vandal king <a href="/wiki/Hilderic" title="Hilderic">Hilderic</a> had been deposed and murdered by the usurper <a href="/wiki/Gelimer" title="Gelimer">Gelimer</a>, giving Justinian a legal pretext. Furthermore, the <a href="/wiki/Arianism" title="Arianism">Arian</a> Vandals had periodically persecuted the <a href="/wiki/Nicene_Creed" title="Nicene Creed">Nicene Christians</a> within their kingdom, many of whom made their way to Constantinople seeking redress. The Vandals had launched many pirate raids on Roman trade interests, hurting commerce in the western areas of the Empire. Justinian also wanted control of the Vandal territory in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">north Africa</a>, which was one of the wealthiest provinces and the breadbasket of the Western Roman Empire and was now vital for guaranteeing Roman access to the western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>.</p>
<p>In the late summer of 533, Belisarius sailed to <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and landed near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caput_Vada" title="Caput Vada">Caput Vada</a> (near Chebba on the coast of Tunisia). He ordered his fleet not to lose sight of the army, then marched along the coastal highway toward the Vandal capital of <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a>. He did this to prevent supplies from being cut off, and to avoid a great defeat such as occurred during <a href="/wiki/Basiliscus" title="Basiliscus">Basiliscus</a>' first attempt to retake northern Africa 65 years before, which had ended in the Roman disaster at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cap_Bon_(468)" title="Battle of Cap Bon (468)">Battle of Cap Bon</a> in 468.</p>
<p>Ten miles from Carthage, the forces of Gelimer (who had just executed Hilderic) and Belisarius finally met at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ad_Decimum" title="Battle of Ad Decimum">Battle of Ad Decimum</a> on September 13, 533. It nearly turned into a defeat for the Romans. Gelimer had chosen his position well and had some success along the main road. The Romans, however, seemed dominant on both sides of the main road to Carthage. At the height of the battle, Gelimer became distraught upon learning of the death of his brother in battle. This gave Belisarius a chance to regroup, and he went on to win the battle and capture Carthage. A second victory at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tricamarum" title="Battle of Tricamarum">Battle of Tricamarum</a> on December 15 of the same year resulted in Gelimer's surrender early in 534 at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Papua&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mount Papua (page does not exist)">Mount Papua</a>, restoring the lost Roman provinces of north Africa to the empire. For this achievement, Belisarius was granted a <a href="/wiki/Roman_triumph" title="Roman triumph">Roman triumph</a> (the last ever given) when he returned to Constantinople. According to <a href="/wiki/Procopius" title="Procopius">Procopius</a> in the procession were paraded the spoils of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temple_of_Jerusalem" title="Temple of Jerusalem">Temple of Jerusalem</a> (the Vandal treasure, including many objects looted from Rome 80 years earlier, the imperial regalia and the menorah of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Temple" title="Second Temple">Second Temple</a> among them) which had been recovered from the Vandal capital along with Gelimer himself before he was sent into peaceful exile. Medals were stamped in his honor with the inscription <i>Gloria Romanorum</i>, though none seem to have survived to modern times. Belisarius was also made sole <a href="/wiki/Roman_consul" title="Roman consul">Consul</a> in 535, being one of the last persons ever to hold this office, which originated in the ancient <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a>.</p>
<p>Nevertheless, the recovery of Africa was not complete; army mutinies and revolts by the native <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_people" title="Berber people">Berbers</a> would plague the new <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_prefecture_of_Africa" title="Praetorian prefecture of Africa">praetorian prefecture of Africa</a> for almost 15 years.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Against_the_Ostrogoths">Against the Ostrogoths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Belisarius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Against the Ostrogoths">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gothic_War_(535%E2%80%93554)" title="Gothic War (535–554)">Gothic War (535–554)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gothic_War_-_First_Phase,_535-540.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="643" data-file-width="1019" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Gothic_War_-_First_Phase%2C_535-540.svg/280px-Gothic_War_-_First_Phase%2C_535-540.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Gothic_War_-_First_Phase%2C_535-540.svg/420px-Gothic_War_-_First_Phase%2C_535-540.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Gothic_War_-_First_Phase%2C_535-540.svg/560px-Gothic_War_-_First_Phase%2C_535-540.svg.png 2x" width="280"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gothic_War_-_First_Phase,_535-540.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Justinian now resolved to restore as much of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a> as he could. In 535, he commissioned Belisarius to attack the <a href="/wiki/Ostrogothic_Kingdom" title="Ostrogothic Kingdom">Ostrogothic Kingdom</a> in Italy. Belisarius landed in Sicily and took the island for use as a base against Italy, while <a href="/wiki/Mundus_(general)" title="Mundus (general)">Mundus</a> recovered <a href="/wiki/Dalmatia" title="Dalmatia">Dalmatia</a>. The preparations for the invasion of the Italian mainland were interrupted in Easter 536, when Belisarius sailed to Africa to counter an uprising of the local army. His reputation made the rebels abandon the siege of Carthage, and Belisarius pursued and defeated them at Membresa. Thereupon he returned to Sicily, and then crossed into mainland <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, where he captured <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Naples_(536)" title="Siege of Naples (536)">Naples</a> in November and <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> in December 536.</p>
<p>In 537–538 he successfully <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_of_Rome_(537-538)" title="Siege of Rome (537-538)">defended Rome</a> against the Goths and moved north to take the Ostrogoth capital of <a href="/wiki/Ravenna" title="Ravenna">Ravenna</a> in 540, where the Goth king <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Witiges" title="Witiges">Witiges</a> was captured. Shortly before the taking of Ravenna, the Ostrogoths had offered to make Belisarius the western emperor. Belisarius feigned acceptance and entered Ravenna via its sole point of entry, a causeway through the marshes, accompanied by a <i><a href="/wiki/Comes" title="Comes">comitatus</a></i> of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bucellarii" title="Bucellarii">bucellarii</a></i>, his personal household regiment. Soon afterwards, he proclaimed the capture of Ravenna in the name of the Emperor Justinian.</p>
<p>The Goths' offer raised suspicions in Justinian's mind and Belisarius was recalled. He returned home with the Gothic treasure, king and warriors.</p>
<p>Belisarius was recalled in part to deal with the Persian conquest of <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, a crucial province of the empire, where he successfully fended off renewed attacks. He defeated the Persian army under Nabades at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nisibis" title="Nisibis">Nisibis</a>, but couldn't take the city because it was fortified and well defended by the Persians. He captured Sisauranon, a small Persian fort to the east. Here Belisarius sent Harith with 1200 Roman troops under John the Glutton and Trajan to plunder Assyria. The expedition was successful, penetrating far into enemy territory and gathering much plunder. In the campaign of 542, Belisarius' presence just to the west of the Euphrates effectively prevented Khusro from advancing further, and the king decided to retreat. Belisarius was nonetheless acclaimed throughout the East for his success in repelling the Persians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Belisarius returned to Italy in 544, where he found that the situation had changed greatly. In 541 the Ostrogoths had elected <a href="/wiki/Totila" title="Totila">Totila</a> as their new leader and had mounted a vigorous campaign against the Romans, recapturing all of northern Italy and even driving the Romans out of Rome. Belisarius managed to recover Rome briefly but his Italian campaign proved unsuccessful, due in no small part to his limited supplies and reinforcements, perhaps as Justinian's empire had suffered from the <a href="/wiki/Plague_of_Justinian" title="Plague of Justinian">plague of 541–542</a>. In 548/9, Justinian relieved him. In 551, after economic recovery (from the effects of the plague) the eunuch <a href="/wiki/Narses" title="Narses">Narses</a> led a large army to bring the campaign to a successful conclusion. For his part, Belisarius retired from military affairs. At the <a href="/wiki/Second_Council_of_Constantinople" title="Second Council of Constantinople">Second Ecumenical Council of Constantinople</a> (553), Belisarius was one of the Emperor's envoys to Pope Vigilius in their tug of war over <a href="/wiki/Three-Chapter_Controversy" title="Three-Chapter Controversy">The Three Chapters</a>. The Patriarch Eutychius, who presided over this council in place of Pope Vigilius, was the son of one of Belisarius' generals.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deposition_of_Pope_Silverius">Deposition of Pope Silverius</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Belisarius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Deposition of Pope Silverius">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the Siege of Rome an incident occurred for which the general would be long condemned: Belisarius, a Byzantine Rite Christian, was commanded by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monophysite" title="Monophysite">monophysite</a> Christian Empress <a href="/wiki/Theodora_(6th_century)" title="Theodora (6th century)">Theodora</a> to depose the reigning Pope, who had been installed by the Goths. This Pope was the former subdeacon <a href="/wiki/Pope_Silverius" title="Pope Silverius">Silverius</a>, the son of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Hormisdas" title="Pope Hormisdas">Pope Hormisdas</a>. Belisarius was to replace him with the Deacon <a href="/wiki/Pope_Vigilius" title="Pope Vigilius">Vigilius</a>, Apocrisarius of Pope John II in Constantinople. Vigilius had in fact been chosen in 531 by Pope Boniface II to be his successor, but this choice was strongly criticised by the Roman clergy and Boniface eventually reversed his decision.</p>
<p>In 537, at the height of the siege, Silverius was accused of conspiring with the Gothic King and several Roman senators to secretly open the gates of the city. Belisarius had him stripped of his vestments and exiled to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patara_(Lycia)" title="Patara (Lycia)">Patara</a> in Lycia in Asia Minor. Following the advocacy of his innocence by the bishop of Patara he was ordered to return to Italy at the command of the Emperor Justinian and, if cleared by investigation, reinstated. However, Vigilius had already been installed in his place and Silverius was intercepted before he could reach Rome and exiled once more, this time on the island of Palmarola (<a href="/wiki/Ponza" title="Ponza">Ponza</a>), where by one account he is said to have starved to death, others say he left for Constantinople. However that may be, he remains the <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saint</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ponza" title="Ponza">Ponza</a> today.</p>
<p>Belisarius, for his part, built a small oratory on the site of the present church of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_in_Trivio" title="Santa Maria in Trivio">Santa Maria in Trivio</a> in Rome as a sign of his repentance. He also built two hospices for pilgrims and a monastery, which have since disappeared. Santa Maria in Trivio is around the corner from the Trevi fountain; a 12th-century inscription is the only surviving monument of the great general.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life_and_campaigns">Later life and campaigns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Belisarius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Later life and campaigns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Justinien_527-565.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>The retirement of Belisarius came to an end in 559, when an army of Kutrigur <a href="/wiki/Bulgars" title="Bulgars">Bulgars</a> under <a href="/wiki/Khan_(title)" title="Khan (title)">Khan</a> <a href="/wiki/Zabergan" title="Zabergan">Zabergan</a> crossed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danube_River" title="Danube River">Danube River</a> to invade Roman territory for the first time and threatened Constantinople itself. Justinian recalled Belisarius to command the Roman army. In his last campaign, Belisarius defeated the <a href="/wiki/Kutrigurs" title="Kutrigurs">Kutrigurs</a> and drove them back across the river with the greatly outnumbered force under his command.</p>
<p>In 562, Belisarius stood trial in Constantinople on a charge of corruption. The charge is presumed to be trumped-up, and modern research suggests that his former secretary <a href="/wiki/Procopius" title="Procopius">Procopius</a> of Caesarea may have judged his case.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Belisarius was found guilty and imprisoned. However, not long after, Justinian pardoned him, ordered his release, and restored him to favour at the imperial court.</p>
<p>In the first five chapters of his <i><a href="/wiki/Procopius#Secret_History" title="Procopius">Secret History</a></i>, Procopius characterises Belisarius as a cuckold husband, who was emotionally dependent on his debauched wife, <a href="/wiki/Antonina_(wife_of_Belisarius)" title="Antonina (wife of Belisarius)">Antonina</a>. According to the historian, Antonina cheated on Belisarius with their adopted son, the young Theodosius. Procopius claims that the love affair was well known in the imperial court and the general was regarded as weak and ridiculous; this view is often considered biased, as Procopius nursed a longstanding hatred of both Belisarius and Antonina. Empress Theodora reportedly helped and saved Antonina when Belisarius tried to charge his wife at last.</p>
<p>Fittingly, Belisarius and Justinian, whose partnership increased the size of the empire by 45%, died within a few months of each other in 565. Belisarius owned the estate of Rufinianae on the Asiatic side of the Constantinople suburbs. He may have died there and been buried near one of the two churches in the area, perhaps Saints Peter and Paul.</p>

<p>According to a story that gained popularity during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, Justinian is said to have ordered Belisarius' eyes to be put out, and reduced him to the status of homeless beggar near the <a href="/wiki/Porta_Pinciana" title="Porta Pinciana">Pincian Gate</a> of Rome, condemned to asking passers-by to "give an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obolus" title="Obolus">obolus</a> to Belisarius" (<i>date obolum Belisario</i>), before pardoning him. Most modern scholars believe the story to be <a href="/wiki/Apocrypha" title="Apocrypha">apocryphal</a>, though <a href="/wiki/Philip_Stanhope,_5th_Earl_Stanhope" title="Philip Stanhope, 5th Earl Stanhope">Philip Stanhope</a>, a 19th-century British <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philologist" title="Philologist">philologist</a> who wrote <i>Life of Belisarius</i>—the only exhaustive biography of the great general—believed the story to be true. Based on a parsing of the available primary sources, Stanhope created an argument for the legend's authenticity.</p>
<p>Though the legend remains of dubious provenance, after the publication of <a href="/wiki/Jean-Fran%C3%A7ois_Marmontel" title="Jean-François Marmontel">Jean-François Marmontel</a>'s novel <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B%C3%A9lisaire_(novel)" title="Bélisaire (novel)">Bélisaire</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/1767_in_literature" title="1767 in literature">1767</a>), this account became a popular subject for progressive painters and their patrons in the later 18th century, who saw parallels between the actions of Justinian and the repression imposed by contemporary rulers. For such subtexts, Marmontel's novel received a public censure by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Legrand_(theologian)" title="Louis Legrand (theologian)">Louis Legrand</a> of the Sorbonne, which contemporary divines regarded as model expositions of theological knowledge and clear thinking (<i>Catholic Encyclopedia</i>: "Louis Legrand"). Marmontel and the painters and sculptors (a bust of Belisarius by the French sculptor <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Stouf" title="Jean-Baptiste Stouf">Jean-Baptiste Stouf</a> is at the <a href="/wiki/J._Paul_Getty_Museum" title="J. Paul Getty Museum">J. Paul Getty Museum</a>) depicted Belisarius as a kind of <a href="/wiki/Secular_saint" title="Secular saint">secular saint</a>, sharing the suffering of the downtrodden poor. The most famous of these paintings, by <a href="/wiki/Jacques-Louis_David" title="Jacques-Louis David">Jacques-Louis David</a>, combines the themes of <a href="/wiki/Charity_(virtue)" title="Charity (virtue)">charity</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Alms" title="Alms">alms</a> giver), <a href="/wiki/Injustice" title="Injustice">injustice</a> (Belisarius), and the radical reversal of power (the soldier who recognises his old commander). Others portray him being helped by the poor after his rejection by the powerful.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_art_and_popular_culture">In art and popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Belisarius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In art and popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Belisarius was featured in several works of art before the 20th century. The oldest of them is the historical treatise by his secretary, <a href="/wiki/Procopius" title="Procopius">Procopius</a>. The <i>Anecdota</i>, commonly referred to as the <i>Arcana Historia</i> or <i>Secret History</i>, is an extended attack on Belisarius and Antonina, and on Justinian and Theodora, indicting Belisarius as a love-blind fool and his wife as unfaithful and duplicitous. <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Belisarius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><i>Bélisaire</i>: a novel by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Fran%C3%A7ois_Marmontel" title="Jean-François Marmontel">Jean-François Marmontel</a> (1767)</li>
<li><i>Belisarius: A Tragedy</i>: by <a href="/wiki/Margaretta_Faug%C3%A8res" title="Margaretta Faugères">Margaretta Faugères</a> (1795). Though she wrote it as a play, Faugères "intended [this work] for the closet," i.e., to be read and not performed. Her preface voices complaints about "maledictions" and long-winded rhetoric in popular tragic drama, which she says tend to bore and even outrage a reader, and announces her intent to "substitute concise narrative and plain sense." The drama's plot and character development are secondary to moral conflicts, mainly between vengeance and mercy/pity, respectively associated with pride and humility.</li>
<li><i>Beliar</i>: 18th-century poem by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friedrich_de_la_Motte_Fouque" title="Friedrich de la Motte Fouque">Friedrich de la Motte Fouque</a>.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/A_Struggle_for_Rome" title="A Struggle for Rome">Ein Kampf um Rom</a></i>: an historical novel by <a href="/wiki/Felix_Dahn" title="Felix Dahn">Felix Dahn</a> (1867)</li>
<li><i>Belisarius</i>, 19th-century poem by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wadsworth_Longfellow" title="Henry Wadsworth Longfellow">Henry Wadsworth Longfellow</a>.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Count_Belisarius" title="Count Belisarius">Count Belisarius</a></i>: a novel by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Graves" title="Robert Graves">Robert Graves</a> (1938); Ostensibly written from the viewpoint of the eunuch Eugenius, servant to Belisarius' wife, but actually based on <a href="/wiki/Procopius" title="Procopius">Procopius</a>'s history, the book portrays Belisarius as a solitary honorable man in a corrupt world, and paints a vivid picture of not only his startling military feats but also the colorful characters and events of his day, such as the savage <a href="/wiki/Hippodrome_of_Constantinople" title="Hippodrome of Constantinople">Hippodrome</a> politics of the Constantinople chariot races, which regularly escalated to open street battles between fans of opposing factions, and the intrigues of the emperor Justinian and the empress Theodora.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Lest_Darkness_Fall" title="Lest Darkness Fall">Lest Darkness Fall</a></i>: an <a href="/wiki/Alternate_history" title="Alternate history">alternative history</a> novel by <a href="/wiki/L._Sprague_de_Camp" title="L. Sprague de Camp">L. Sprague de Camp</a> (1939). Belisarius appears first as the Roman opponent of the <a href="/wiki/Time_travel" title="Time travel">time traveler</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martin_Padway" title="Martin Padway">Martin Padway</a> who tries to spread modern <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> and inventions in Gothic Italy. Eventually Belisarius becomes a general in Padway's army and secures Italy for him.</li>
<li>The character "<a href="/wiki/Bel_Riose" title="Bel Riose">Bel Riose</a>" in <a href="/wiki/Foundation_and_Empire" title="Foundation and Empire">Foundation and Empire</a> by <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">Isaac Asimov</a> is based on Belisarius (1952)</li>
<li><i>A Flame in Byzantium</i>: an historical horror fiction novel by <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_Quinn_Yarbro" title="Chelsea Quinn Yarbro">Chelsea Quinn Yarbro</a> (1987)</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Belisarius_series" title="Belisarius series"><i>Belisarius</i> series</a>: six <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> novels by <a href="/wiki/Eric_Flint" title="Eric Flint">Eric Flint</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Drake" title="David Drake">David Drake</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alternate_history_(fiction)" title="Alternate history (fiction)">Alternate history</a> exploring what might have happened if Belisarius and a rival were granted knowledge of future events and technologies. The first four books are available as free ebooks from the <a href="/wiki/Baen_Free_Library" title="Baen Free Library">Baen Free Library</a>, or all six at The Fifth Imperium website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a class="external text" href="http://www.arxpub.com/literary/Belisarius.html" rel="nofollow">Belisarius: The First Shall Be Last</a></i> (2006) and <i><a class="external text" href="http://www.arxpub.com/literary/Belisarius.html" rel="nofollow">Belisarius: Glory of the Romans</a></i> (2010): novels by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Paolo_Belzoni&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Paolo Belzoni (page does not exist)">Paolo Belzoni</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature_2">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Belisarius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<li><i><a href="/wiki/Mardi" title="Mardi">Mardi</a></i>: novel by <a href="/wiki/Herman_Melville" title="Herman Melville">Herman Melville</a> (1849); Melville playfully assigns the moniker "my Belisarius" to the Samoan Islander first encountered aboard the abandoned vessel "Parki".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jorge_Luis_Borges" title="Jorge Luis Borges">Jorge Luis Borges</a> mentioned the legend of Belisario as a blind beggar in some of his poetic works, for example, "A quien ya no es joven," the first verse of which reads: "Ya puedes ver el tragico escenario y cada cosa en lugar debido; la espada y la ceniza para Dido y la moneda para Belisario."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Foundation_and_Empire" title="Foundation and Empire">Foundation and Empire</a></i>: a <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> novel by Isaac Asimov, second novel in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foundation_Series" title="Foundation Series">Foundation Series</a> (1952). The character <a href="/wiki/Bel_Riose" title="Bel Riose">Bel Riose</a>, based on Belisarius, is the last great general of the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galactic_Empire_(Asimov)" title="Galactic Empire (Asimov)">Galactic Empire</a>, which was modelled on the late Roman Empire.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_General_series" title="The General series">The General</a></i>: a series of <a href="/wiki/Military_science_fiction" title="Military science fiction">military science fiction</a> novels by <a href="/wiki/S._M._Stirling" title="S. M. Stirling">S. M. Stirling</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Drake" title="David Drake">David Drake</a>. The plot draws much from the life and campaigns of Belisarius; the main character, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raj_Whitehall" title="Raj Whitehall">Raj Whitehall</a>, sets out to reunite the planet of Bellevue after the fall of galactic civilization.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Sarantine_Mosaic" title="The Sarantine Mosaic">The Sarantine Mosaic</a></i>: a pair of <a href="/wiki/Alternate_history" title="Alternate history">alternate</a> <a href="/wiki/Historical_fantasy" title="Historical fantasy">historical fantasy</a> novels by <a href="/wiki/Guy_Gavriel_Kay" title="Guy Gavriel Kay">Guy Gavriel Kay</a> which follows the mosaicist Crispin and his entanglement in the affairs of the court under Emperor Valerius II and his general Leontes, loosely based on Justinian and Belisarius in the time immediately before and leading up to the attempt to reclaim the lost Western empire.</li>
<li><i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Buffy_The_Vampire_Slayer:_Immortal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Buffy The Vampire Slayer: Immortal (page does not exist)">Buffy The Vampire Slayer: Immortal</a></i> Belasarius was turned into a Vampire by Veronqiue back in that time period. No one knew and thus he remained General while serving Veronique.</li>
<li>The <b>Belisarius Series</b> is a fictional saga in the <a href="/wiki/Alternate_history" title="Alternate history">alternate history</a> and <a href="/wiki/Military_history" title="Military history">military history</a> subgenres of <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a>, written by American authors <a href="/wiki/David_Drake" title="David Drake">David Drake</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eric_Flint" title="Eric Flint">Eric Flint</a>.</li>
</ul>
