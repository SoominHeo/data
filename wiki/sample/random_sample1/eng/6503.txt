<p>A <b>wall</b> is a structure that defines an area, carries a load, or provides shelter or security. There are many kinds of walls:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Defensive_wall" title="Defensive wall">Defensive walls</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fortification" title="Fortification">fortification</a></li>
<li>Walls in buildings that form a fundamental part of the <a href="/wiki/Superstructure" title="Superstructure">superstructure</a> or separate interior <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Room_(architecture)" title="Room (architecture)">sections</a>, sometimes for <a href="/wiki/Firewall_(construction)" title="Firewall (construction)">fire safety</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Retaining_wall" title="Retaining wall">Retaining walls</a>, which hold back dirt, stone, or water</li>
<li>Walls that protect from oceans (<a href="/wiki/Seawall" title="Seawall">seawalls</a> or rivers <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">levees</a>)</li>
<li>Permanent, solid <a href="/wiki/Fence" title="Fence">fences</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Border_barriers" title="Border barriers">Border barriers</a> between countries</li>
<li>Brick wall</li>
<li>Stone wall</li>
<li>Glass wall (only when most of the wall, in smaller amounts it is called a <a href="/wiki/Window" title="Window">window</a>)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doors" title="Doors">Doors</a> are mobile walls on hinges which open to form a <a href="/wiki/Gate" title="Gate">gateway</a></li>
<li>A Wall of Silence</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Wall</i> comes from Latin <i>vallum</i> meaning "...an earthen wall or rampart set with palisades, a row or line of stakes, a wall, a rampart, fortification..." while the Latin word <i>murus</i> means a defensive stone wall<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> uses the same word to mean an external wall and the internal sides of a room, but this is not universal. Many languages distinguish between the two. In German, some of this distinctions can be seen between <i>Wand</i> and <i>Mauer</i>, in Spanish between <i>pared</i> and <i>muro</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Defensive_wall">Defensive wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Defensive wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Defensive_wall" title="Defensive wall">Defensive wall</a></div>
<p>The word wall originally referred to defensive walls and ramparts.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Building_wall">Building wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Building wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/American_historic_carpentry" title="American historic carpentry">American historic carpentry</a></div>
<p>Building walls purposes are to support <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roofs" title="Roofs">roofs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Floor" title="Floor">floors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ceiling" title="Ceiling">ceilings</a>, enclose a space as part of the <a href="/wiki/Building_envelope" title="Building envelope">building envelope</a>, along with a roof to give buildings form, and to provide shelter and security. In addition, the wall may house various types of utilities such as <a href="/wiki/Electrical_wiring" title="Electrical wiring">electrical wiring</a> or <a href="/wiki/Plumbing" title="Plumbing">plumbing</a>. Wall construction falls into two basic categories: <i>framed walls</i> or <i>mass-walls</i>. In <a href="/wiki/Framing_(construction)" title="Framing (construction)">framed walls</a> the load is transferred to the foundation through posts, columns or studs. Framed walls most often have three or more separate components: the structural elements (such as 2×4 studs in a house wall), <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">insulation</a>, and finish elements or surfaces (such as <a href="/wiki/Drywall" title="Drywall">drywall</a> or <a href="/wiki/Panelling" title="Panelling">panelling</a>). Mass-walls are of a solid material including <a href="/wiki/Masonry" title="Masonry">masonry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> including <a href="/wiki/Slipform_stonemasonry" title="Slipform stonemasonry">slipform stonemasonry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Log_building" title="Log building">log building</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cordwood_construction" title="Cordwood construction">cordwood construction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adobe" title="Adobe">adobe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rammed_earth" title="Rammed earth">rammed earth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cob_(material)" title="Cob (material)">cob</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earthbag_construction" title="Earthbag construction">earthbag construction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bottle_wall" title="Bottle wall">bottles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tin_can_wall" title="Tin can wall">tin cans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Straw-bale_construction" title="Straw-bale construction">straw-bale construction</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ice_palace" title="Ice palace">ice</a>.</p>
<p>There are three basic methods walls control water intrusion: moisture storage, drained cladding, or face-sealed cladding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Moisture storage is typical of stone and brick <i>mass-wall</i> buildings where moisture is absorbed and released by the walls of the structure itself. <i>Drained cladding</i> also known as <i>screened walls</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASTM_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ASTM-3">[3]</a></sup> acknowledges moisture will penetrate the cladding so a <i>moisture barrier</i> such as <a href="/wiki/Housewrap" title="Housewrap">housewrap</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Felt_paper" title="Felt paper">felt paper</a> inside the cladding provides a second line of defense and sometimes a <i>drainage plane</i> or <i>air gap</i> allows a path for the moisture to drain down through and exit the wall. Sometimes ventilation is provided in addition to the drainage plane such as in <a href="/wiki/Rainscreen" title="Rainscreen">rainscreen</a> construction. <i>Face-sealed</i> also called <i>barrier wall</i> or <i>perfect barrier</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASTM_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ASTM-3">[3]</a></sup> cladding relies on maintaining a leak-free surface of the cladding. Examples of face sealed cladding are the early <a href="/wiki/Exterior_insulation_finishing_system" title="Exterior insulation finishing system">exterior insulation finishing systems</a>, structural glazing, metal clad panels, and corrugated metal.</p>
<p>Building walls frequently become works of art, externally and internally, such as when featuring <a href="/wiki/Mosaic" title="Mosaic">mosaic</a> work or when <a href="/wiki/Mural" title="Mural">murals</a> are painted on them; or as design foci when they exhibit textures or painted finishes for effect.</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a>, <i>curtain wall</i> refers to a building <a href="/wiki/Facade" title="Facade">facade</a> that is not <a href="/wiki/Load-bearing_wall" title="Load-bearing wall">load-bearing</a> but provides decoration, finish, front, face, or historical preservation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mullion_wall">Mullion wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mullion wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mullion_wall" title="Mullion wall">Mullion wall</a></div>
<p>Mullion walls are a structural system that carries the load of the floor slab on prefabricated panels around the perimeter.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Partition_wall">Partition wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Partition wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Glass_Partition_Wall.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4822" data-file-width="6256" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Glass_Partition_Wall.jpg/220px-Glass_Partition_Wall.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Glass_Partition_Wall.jpg/330px-Glass_Partition_Wall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Glass_Partition_Wall.jpg/440px-Glass_Partition_Wall.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Glass_Partition_Wall.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Glass Partition Wall</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A partition wall is a wall that separates rooms, or divides a room. Partition walls are usually not <a href="/wiki/Structural_load" title="Structural load">load</a>-bearing. Partition walls are constructed of many materials, including steel panels, bricks, blocks of clay, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terra_cotta" title="Terra cotta">terra-cotta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Concrete_masonry_unit" title="Concrete masonry unit">concrete</a>, or glass blocks.</p>
<p>Some partition walls are made of sheet glass. Glass partition walls are a series of individual <a href="/wiki/Toughened_glass" title="Toughened glass">toughened glass</a> panels mounted in wood or metal framing. They may be suspended from or slide along a robust aluminium ceiling track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The system does not require the use of a floor guide, which allows easy operation and an uninterrupted threshold.</p>
<p>A timber partition consists of a wooden framework, supported on the floor or by side walls. Metal lath and plaster, properly laid, forms a reinforced partition wall. Partition walls constructed from <a href="/wiki/Fibre_cement" title="Fibre cement">fibre cement</a> backer board are popular as bases for tiling in kitchens or in wet areas like bathrooms. Galvanized sheet fixed to wooden or steel members are mostly adopted in works of temporary character. Plain or reinforced partition walls may also be constructed from concrete, including pre-cast concrete blocks. Metal framed partitioning is also available. This partition consists of track (used primarily at the base and head of the partition) and studs (vertical sections fixed into the track typically spaced at 24", 16", or at 12").</p>
<p>Internal wall partitions, also known as office partitioning, is usually made of plasterboard (drywall) or varieties of glass. Toughened glass is a common option, as is low-iron glass (better known as <i>opti-white glass</i>, which increases light and solar heat transmission.</p>
<p>Wall partitions are constructed using beads and tracking that is either hung from the ceiling or fixed into the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The panels are inserted into the tracking and fixed. Some wall partition variations specify their fire resistance and acoustic performance rating.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Party_wall">Party wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Party wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Party_wall" title="Party wall">Party wall</a></div>
<p>Party walls are walls that separate buildings or units within a building. They provide fire resistance and <a href="/wiki/Soundproofing" title="Soundproofing">sound resistance</a> between occupants in a building. The minimum fire resistance and sound resistance required for the party wall is determined by a building code and may be modified to suit a variety of situations. Ownership of such walls can become a legal issue.It is not a load bearing wall and may be owned by different people</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infill_wall">Infill wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Infill wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Infill_wall" title="Infill wall">Infill wall</a></div>
<p>An infill wall is the supported wall that closes the perimeter of a building constructed with a three-dimensional framework structure.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_wall">Fire wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fire wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Firewall_(construction)" title="Firewall (construction)">Firewall (construction)</a></div>
<p>Fire walls resist spread of fire within or sometimes between structures to provide passive fire protection. A delay in the spread of fire gives occupants more time to escape and fire fighters more time to extinguish the fire. Such walls have no windows, and are made of non-combustible material such as concrete, cement block, brick, or fire rated drywall—and have wall penetrations sealed with special materials. A doorway in a firewall must have a rated <a href="/wiki/Fire_door" title="Fire door">fire door</a>. Fire walls provide varying resistance to the spread of fire, some intended to last one to four hours. Firewalls, generally, can also act as smoke barriers when constructed vertically from slab to roof deck and horizontally from an exterior wall to exterior wall subdividing a building into sections. When constructed in this manner the fire wall can also be referred to as an Area Separation Wall.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shear_wall">Shear wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Shear wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Shear_wall" title="Shear wall">Shear wall</a></div>
<p>Shear walls resist lateral forces such as in an earthquake or severe wind. There are different kinds of shear walls such as the <a href="/wiki/Steel_plate_shear_wall" title="Steel plate shear wall">steel plate shear wall</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Knee_wall">Knee wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Knee wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Knee_wall" title="Knee wall">Knee wall</a></div>
<p>Knee walls are short walls that either support rafters or add height in the top floor rooms of houses. In a one-and-one-half story house, the knee wall supports the <i>half story</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cavity_wall">Cavity wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cavity wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cavity_wall" title="Cavity wall">Cavity wall</a></div>
<p>Cavity walls are walls made with a space between two "skins" to inhibit heat transfer.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pony_wall">Pony wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Pony wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pony wall is a general term for short walls, such as:</p>
<ul>
<li>A half wall that only extends partway from floor to ceiling, without supporting anything</li>
<li>A stem wall—a concrete wall that extends from the foundation slab to the cripple wall or floor joists</li>
<li>A cripple wall—a framed wall from the stem wall or foundation slab to the floor joists</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Movable_partitions">Movable partitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Movable partitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Portable_partitions" title="Portable partitions">Portable partitions</a></div>
<p>Movable partitions are walls that open to join two or more rooms into one large floor area. These include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Sliding—a series of panels that slide in tracks fixed to the floor and ceiling, similar sliding doors</li>
<li>Sliding and folding doors —similar to sliding folding doors, these are good for smaller spans</li>
<li>Folding partition walls - a series of interlocking panels suspended from an overhead track that when extended provide an acoustical separation, and when retracted stack against a wall, ceiling, closet, or ceiling pocket.</li>
<li>Screens—usually constructed of a metal or <a href="/wiki/Timber_framing" title="Timber framing">timber frame</a> fixed with plywood and chipboard and supported with legs for free standing and easy movement</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pipe_and_drape" title="Pipe and drape">Pipe and drape</a>—fixed or telescopic uprights and horizontals provide a ground supported drape system with removable panels</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_energy">Solar energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Solar energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Trombe_wall" title="Trombe wall">Trombe wall</a></div>
<p>A trombe wall in <a href="/wiki/Passive_solar_building_design" title="Passive solar building design">passive solar building design</a> acts as a heat sink.</p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Double-skin_facade" title="Double-skin facade">double-skin facade</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shipbuilding">Shipbuilding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Shipbuilding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Shipbuilding" title="Shipbuilding">Shipbuilding</a></div>
<p>On a ship, a wall that separates major compartments is called a <i><a href="/wiki/Bulkhead_(partition)" title="Bulkhead (partition)">bulkhead</a></i>. A thinner wall between cabins is called a <i>partition</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Boundary_wall">Boundary wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Boundary wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stone_wall.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="640" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Stone_wall.jpg/220px-Stone_wall.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Stone_wall.jpg/330px-Stone_wall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Stone_wall.jpg/440px-Stone_wall.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stone_wall.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Stone wall of an English barn</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Boundary walls include privacy walls, boundary-marking walls on property, and town walls. These intergrade into <a href="/wiki/Fence" title="Fence">fences</a>. The conventional differentiation is that a fence is of minimal thickness and often open in nature, while a wall is usually more than a nominal thickness and is completely closed, or opaque. More to the point, an exterior structure of wood or wire is generally called a <a href="/wiki/Fence" title="Fence">fence</a>—but one of <a href="/wiki/Masonry" title="Masonry">masonry</a> is a wall. A common term for both is <a href="/wiki/Border_barrier" title="Border barrier">barrier</a>, which is convenient for structures that are partly wall and partly fence—for example the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Wall" title="Berlin Wall">Berlin Wall</a>. Another kind of wall-fence ambiguity is the <a href="/wiki/Ha-ha" title="Ha-ha">ha-ha</a>—which is set below ground level to protect a view, yet acts as a barrier (to cattle, for example).</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Olditalianwall.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1536" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c7/Olditalianwall.JPG/220px-Olditalianwall.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c7/Olditalianwall.JPG/330px-Olditalianwall.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c7/Olditalianwall.JPG/440px-Olditalianwall.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Olditalianwall.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An old Italian wall surrounded by flowers</div>
</div>

<p>Before the invention of <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">artillery</a>, many of the world's <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">cities</a> and towns, particularly in Europe and Asia, had <a href="/wiki/Defensive_wall" title="Defensive wall">defensive or protective walls</a> (also called town walls or city walls). In fact, the English word "wall" derives from Latin <i><a href="/wiki/Vallum" title="Vallum">vallum</a></i>—a type of fortification wall. These walls are no longer relevant for defense, so such cities have grown beyond their walls, and many fortification walls, or portions of them, have been torn down—for example in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. Examples of protective walls on a much larger scale include the <a href="/wiki/Great_Wall_of_China" title="Great Wall of China">Great Wall of China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_Wall" title="Hadrian's Wall">Hadrian's Wall</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Border_wall">Border wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Border wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Border_barrier" title="Border barrier">Border barrier</a></div>
<p>Some walls formally mark the border between one population and another. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Border_wall" title="Border wall">border wall</a> is constructed to limit the movement of people across a certain line or <a href="/wiki/Border" title="Border">border</a>. These structures vary in placement with regard to international borders and <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a>. The most famous example of border barrier in history is probably the <a href="/wiki/Great_Wall_of_China" title="Great Wall of China">Great Wall of China</a>, a series of walls that separated the Empire of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> from nomadic powers to the north. The most prominent recent example is the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Wall" title="Berlin Wall">Berlin Wall</a>, which surrounded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enclave" title="Enclave">enclave</a> of <a href="/wiki/West_Berlin" title="West Berlin">West Berlin</a> and separated it from <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a> for most of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> era.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Retaining_wall">Retaining wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Retaining wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In areas of rocky soils around the world, farmers have often pulled large quantities of stone out of their fields to make farming easier and have stacked those stones to make walls that either mark the field boundary, or the property boundary, or both.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Retaining_wall" title="Retaining wall">Retaining walls</a> resist movement of earth, stone, or water. They may be part of a building or external. The ground surface or water on one side of a retaining wall is typically higher than on the other side. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dike_(construction)" title="Dike (construction)">dike</a> is a retaining wall, as is a <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">levee</a>, a load-bearing <a href="/wiki/Foundation_(engineering)" title="Foundation (engineering)">foundation</a> wall, and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_wall" title="Sea wall">sea wall</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shared_wall">Shared wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Shared wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Special laws often govern walls that neighbouring properties share. Typically, one neighbour cannot alter the common wall if it is likely to affect the building or property on the other side. A wall may also separate apartment or hotel rooms from each other. Each wall has two sides and breaking a wall on one side will break the wall on the other side.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Portable_wall">Portable wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Portable wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Portable walls, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Room_dividers" title="Room dividers">room dividers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Portable_partitions" title="Portable partitions">portable partitions</a> divide a larger open space into smaller rooms. Portable walls can be static, such as cubicle walls, or can be wall panels mounted on casters to provide an easy way to reconfigure assembly space. They are often found inside schools, churches, convention centers, hotels, and corporate facilities.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Temporary_wall">Temporary wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Temporary wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A temporary wall is constructed for easy removal or demolition. A typical temporary wall can be constructed with 1⁄2" (6 mm) to 5⁄8" (16 mm) sheet rock (plasterboard), metal 2 × 3s (approx. 5 × 7 cm), or 2 × 4s, or taped, plastered and compounded. Most installation companies use lattice (strips of wood) to cover the joints of the temporary wall with the ceiling. These are sometime known as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pressurized_walls&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pressurized walls (page does not exist)">pressurized walls</a> or temporary pressurized walls.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Walls_in_popular_culture">Walls in popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Walls in popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Walls are often seen in many popular cultures, representing barriers preventing progress or entry. For example, the progressive/psychedelic rock band <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a> used a metaphorical wall to represent the isolation felt by the protagonist of their 1979 concept album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall" title="The Wall">The Wall</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Poet_laureate" title="Poet laureate">poet laureate</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Frost" title="Robert Frost">Robert Frost</a> describes a pointless rock wall as a metaphor for the myopia of the culture-bound in his poem "<a href="/wiki/Mending_Wall" title="Mending Wall">Mending Wall</a>", published in 1914. In a real-life example, the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Wall" title="Berlin Wall">Berlin Wall</a>, constructed by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> to divide Berlin into <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">Warsaw Pact</a> zones of occupation, became a worldwide symbol of oppression and isolation.</p>
<p>In some cases, a wall may refer to an individual's debilitating mental or physical condition, seen as an impassable barrier.</p>
<p>Another common usage is as a communal surface to write upon. For instance the social networking site <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> previously used an electronic "wall" to log the scrawls of friends until it was replaced by the "timeline" feature.</p>
