<p><b>Constipation</b> refers to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bowel_movement" title="Bowel movement">bowel movements</a> that are infrequent or hard to pass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review09_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Review09-2">[2]</a></sup> Constipation is a common cause of painful <a href="/wiki/Defecation" title="Defecation">defecation</a>. Severe constipation includes <b>obstipation</b> (failure to pass stools or gas) and <a href="/wiki/Fecal_impaction" title="Fecal impaction">fecal impaction</a>, which can progress to <a href="/wiki/Bowel_obstruction" title="Bowel obstruction">bowel obstruction</a> and become life-threatening.</p>
<p>Constipation is a <a href="/wiki/Symptom" title="Symptom">symptom</a> with many causes. These causes are of two types: <a href="/wiki/Obstructed_defecation" title="Obstructed defecation">obstructed defecation</a> and colonic slow transit (or hypomobility). About 50 percent of people evaluated for constipation at <a href="/wiki/Health_care#Tertiary_care" title="Health care">tertiary</a> referral hospitals have obstructed defecation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16677147_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16677147-3">[3]</a></sup> This type of constipation has mechanical and functional causes. Causes of colonic slow transit constipation include <a href="/wiki/Diet_(nutrition)" title="Diet (nutrition)">diet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Endocrine_disease" title="Endocrine disease">hormonal disorders</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Hypothyroidism" title="Hypothyroidism">hypothyroidism</a>, side effects of medications, and rarely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_(chemistry)" title="Heavy metal (chemistry)">heavy metal</a> toxicity. Because constipation is a symptom, not a disease, effective treatment of constipation may require first determining the cause. Treatments include changes in dietary habits, <a href="/wiki/Laxative" title="Laxative">laxatives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Enema" title="Enema">enemas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biofeedback" title="Biofeedback">biofeedback</a>, and in particular situations surgery may be required.</p>
<p>Constipation is common; in the general population rates of constipation varies from 2–30 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16677147_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16677147-3">[3]</a></sup> In elderly people living in care homes the rate of constipation is 50–75 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In the United States expenditures on medications for constipation are greater than <span style="white-space: nowrap"><a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>250<span class="nowrap"> </span>million</span> per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:372px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BristolStoolChart.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="617" data-file-width="1025" height="223" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/BristolStoolChart.png/370px-BristolStoolChart.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/BristolStoolChart.png/555px-BristolStoolChart.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/BristolStoolChart.png/740px-BristolStoolChart.png 2x" width="370"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BristolStoolChart.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bristol stool chart</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The definition of constipation includes the following:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walia_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-walia-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mccallum_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-mccallum-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emmanuel_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-emmanuel-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>infrequent bowel movements (typically three times or fewer per week)</li>
<li>difficulty during <a href="/wiki/Defecation" title="Defecation">defecation</a> (straining during more than 25% of bowel movements or a subjective sensation of hard stools; straining in this context is a strong effort to push out stool often by holding one's breath and by pushing the respective muscles in the abdominal area hard), or</li>
<li>the sensation of incomplete bowel evacuation.</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rome_process" title="Rome process">Rome III criteria</a> are widely used to diagnose chronic constipation, and are helpful in separating cases of chronic functional constipation from less-serious instances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap01_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap01-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another definition states that less than three bowel movements per week and straining on more than 75% of occasions represents constipation in clinical surveys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Children">Children</span></h3>
<p>Constipation in children usually occurs at three distinct points in time: after starting formula or processed foods (while an infant), during <a href="/wiki/Toilet_training" title="Toilet training">toilet training</a> in toddlerhood, and soon after starting school (as in at kindergarten).</p>
<p>After birth, most infants pass 4-5 soft liquid bowel movements a day. <a href="/wiki/Breastfeeding" title="Breastfeeding">Breast-fed</a> infants usually tend to have more bowel movements compared to <a href="/wiki/Infant_formula" title="Infant formula">formula</a>-fed infants. Some breastfed infants have a bowel movement after each feed, whereas others have only one every 2–3 days. Infants who are breastfed rarely develop constipation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> By the age of two years, a child will usually have 1–2 bowel movements per day and by four years of age, a child will have one bowel movement per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postpartum_women">Postpartum women</span></h3>
<p>The six-week period after pregnancy is called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postpartum" title="Postpartum">postpartum</a> stage. During this time, women are at increased risk of being constipated. Constipation can cause discomfort for women, as they are still recovering from the delivery process especially if they have had a <a href="/wiki/Perineal_tear" title="Perineal tear">perineal tear</a> or underwent a <a href="/wiki/Episiotomy" title="Episiotomy">episiotomy</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hemorrhoid" title="Hemorrhoid">Hemorrhoids</a> are common in pregnancy and also may get exacerbated when constipated. Women sometimes get enemas during labor that can also alter bowel movements in the days after having given birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tur2015_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tur2015-14">[14]</a></sup> However, there is insufficient evidence to make conclusions about the effectiveness and safety of laxatives in this group of people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tur2015_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tur2015-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span></h2>
<p>The causes of constipation can be divided into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congenital" title="Congenital">congenital</a>, primary, and secondary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review09_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Review09-2">[2]</a></sup> The most common cause is primary and not life-threatening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AGA2000_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-AGA2000-15">[15]</a></sup> In the elderly, causes include: insufficient dietary fiber intake, inadequate fluid intake, decreased <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_activity" title="Physical activity">physical activity</a>, side effects of medications, <a href="/wiki/Hypothyroidism" title="Hypothyroidism">hypothyroidism</a>, and obstruction by <a href="/wiki/Colorectal_cancer" title="Colorectal cancer">colorectal cancer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Constipation with no known organic cause, i.e. no medical <a href="/wiki/Explanation" title="Explanation">explanation</a>, exhibits <a href="/wiki/Gender" title="Gender">gender</a> differences in <a href="/wiki/Prevalence" title="Prevalence">prevalence</a>: females are more often affected than males.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chang_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-chang-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary">Primary</span></h3>
<p>Primary or functional constipation is ongoing symptoms for greater than six months not due to any underlying cause such as medication <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Side_effects" title="Side effects">side effects</a> or an underlying medical condition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review09_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Review09-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP05_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-AFP05-18">[18]</a></sup> It is not associated with abdominal pain, thus distinguishing it from <a href="/wiki/Irritable_bowel_syndrome" title="Irritable bowel syndrome">irritable bowel syndrome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review09_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Review09-2">[2]</a></sup> It is the most common cause of constipation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review09_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Review09-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span></h3>
<p>Constipation can be caused or exacerbated by a low-fiber diet, low liquid intake, or dieting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walia_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-walia-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mccallum_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-mccallum-8">[8]</a></sup> Childhood chronic constipation can be manifested as an intolerance to <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">cow's milk</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medication">Medication</span></h3>
<p>Many medications have constipation as a side effect. Some include (but are not limited to) <a href="/wiki/Opioid" title="Opioid">opioids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diuretics" title="Diuretics">diuretics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antidepressant" title="Antidepressant">antidepressants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antihistamine" title="Antihistamine">antihistamines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antispasmodic" title="Antispasmodic">antispasmodics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anticonvulsant" title="Anticonvulsant">anticonvulsants</a>, and aluminum <a href="/wiki/Antacid" title="Antacid">antacids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walia_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-walia-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap01_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-ap01-10">[10]</a></sup> Certain <a href="/wiki/Calcium_channel_blocker" title="Calcium channel blocker">calcium channel blockers</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Nifedipine" title="Nifedipine">nifedipine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Verapamil" title="Verapamil">verapamil</a> can cause severe constipation due to dysfunction of motility in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rectosigmoid_colon" title="Rectosigmoid colon">rectosigmoid colon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metabolic_and_muscular">Metabolic and muscular</span></h3>
<p>Metabolic and endocrine problems which may lead to constipation include: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypercalcemia" title="Hypercalcemia">hypercalcemia</a>, hypothyroidism, <a href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus" title="Diabetes mellitus">diabetes mellitus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cystic_fibrosis" title="Cystic fibrosis">cystic fibrosis</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celiac_disease" title="Celiac disease">celiac disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walia_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-walia-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AGA2000_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-AGA2000-15">[15]</a></sup> Constipation is also common in individuals with muscular and myotonic dystrophy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walia_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-walia-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structural_and_functional_abnormalities">Structural and functional abnormalities</span></h3>
<p>Constipation has a number of structural (mechanical, morphological, anatomical) causes, including: spinal cord lesions, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parkinsons" title="Parkinsons">Parkinsons</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colon_cancer" title="Colon cancer">colon cancer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anal_fissure" title="Anal fissure">anal fissures</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proctitis" title="Proctitis">proctitis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pelvic_floor_dysfunction" title="Pelvic floor dysfunction">pelvic floor dysfunction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AGA2000_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-AGA2000-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Constipation also has functional (neurological) causes, including <a href="/wiki/Anismus" title="Anismus">anismus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Descending_perineum_syndrome" title="Descending perineum syndrome">descending perineum syndrome</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hirschsprung%27s_disease" title="Hirschsprung's disease">Hirschsprung's disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16677147_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16677147-3">[3]</a></sup> In infants, Hirschsprung's disease is the most common medical disorder associated with constipation. Anismus occurs in a small minority of persons with chronic constipation or obstructed defecation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychological">Psychological</span></h3>
<p>Voluntary withholding of the stool is a common cause of constipation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walia_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-walia-7">[7]</a></sup> The choice to withhold can be due to factors such as fear of pain, fear of public restrooms, or laziness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walia_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-walia-7">[7]</a></sup> When a child holds in the stool a combination of encouragement, <a href="/wiki/Drink" title="Drink">fluids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dietary_fiber" title="Dietary fiber">fiber</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Laxative" title="Laxative">laxatives</a> may be useful to overcome the problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">Diagnosis</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Constipation(lots).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1099" data-file-width="868" height="279" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Constipation%28lots%29.png/220px-Constipation%28lots%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Constipation%28lots%29.png/330px-Constipation%28lots%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Constipation%28lots%29.png/440px-Constipation%28lots%29.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Constipation(lots).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Significant constipation in an 8 year old as seen on plain X ray</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The diagnosis is essentially made from the patient's description of the symptoms. Bowel movements that are difficult to pass, very firm, or made up of small hard pellets (like those excreted by rabbits) qualify as constipation, even if they occur every day. Other symptoms related to constipation can include bloating, <a href="/wiki/Abdominal_distension" title="Abdominal distension">distension</a>, abdominal pain, headaches, a feeling of fatigue and nervous exhaustion, or a sense of incomplete emptying.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Inquiring about dietary habits will often reveal a low intake of dietary fiber, inadequate amounts of fluids, poor ambulation or immobility, or medications that are associated with constipation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walia_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-walia-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mccallum_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-mccallum-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/Physical_examination" title="Physical examination">physical examination</a>, <i>scybala</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> (manually palpable lumps of stool) may be detected on palpation of the abdomen. <a href="/wiki/Rectal_examination" title="Rectal examination">Rectal examination</a> gives an impression of the anal sphincter <a href="/wiki/Muscle_tone" title="Muscle tone">tone</a> and whether the lower rectum contains any feces or not. Rectal examination also gives information on the consistency of the stool, the presence of hemorrhoids, admixture of blood and whether any tumors, <a href="/wiki/Polyp" title="Polyp">polyps</a> or abnormalities are present. Physical examination may be done manually by the physician, or by using a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonoscope" title="Colonoscope">colonoscope</a>. <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-rays</a> of the abdomen, generally only performed if bowel obstruction is suspected, may reveal extensive impacted fecal matter in the colon, and confirm or rule out other causes of similar symptoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walia_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-walia-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mccallum_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-mccallum-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chronic constipation (symptoms present at least three days per month for more than three months) associated with abdominal discomfort is often diagnosed as irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) when no obvious cause is found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Colonic propagating pressure wave sequences (PSs) are responsible for discrete movements of the bowel contents and are vital for normal defecation. Deficiencies in PS frequency, amplitude, and extent of propagation are all implicated in severe defecatory dysfunction (SDD). Mechanisms that can normalize these aberrant motor patterns may help rectify the problem. Recently the novel therapy of <a href="/wiki/Sacral_nerve_stimulation" title="Sacral nerve stimulation">sacral nerve stimulation</a> (SNS) has been utilized for the treatment of severe constipation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criteria">Criteria</span></h2>
<p>The Rome II Criteria for constipation require at least two of the following symptoms for 12 weeks or more over the period of a year:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid12074527_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid12074527-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Straining with more than one-fourth of defecations</li>
<li>Hard <a href="/wiki/Human_feces" title="Human feces">stool</a> with more than one-fourth of defecations</li>
<li>Feeling of incomplete evacuation with more than one-fourth of defecations</li>
<li>Sensation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anorectal" title="Anorectal">anorectal</a> obstruction with more than one-fourth of defecations</li>
<li>Manual maneuvers to facilitate more than one-fourth of defecations</li>
<li>Fewer than three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bowel" title="Bowel">bowel</a> movements per week</li>
<li>Insufficient criteria for irritable bowel syndrome</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention">Prevention</span></h2>
<p>Constipation is usually easier to prevent than to treat. Following the relief of constipation, maintenance with adequate exercise, fluid intake, and high-fiber diet is recommended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walia_7-9"><a href="#cite_note-walia-7">[7]</a></sup> Children benefit from scheduled toilet breaks, once early in the morning and 30 minutes after meals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walia_7-10"><a href="#cite_note-walia-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bharucha_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-bharucha-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Bowel_management" title="Bowel management">Bowel management</a></div>
<p>The treatment of constipation should focus on the underlying cause if known. The National Institute of Health and Care Excellence (NICE) break constipation in adults into two categories - chronic constipation of unknown cause and constipation due to opiates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In chronic constipation of unknown cause, the main treatment involves the increased intake of water and fiber (either dietary or as supplements).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AGA2000_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-AGA2000-15">[15]</a></sup> The routine use of laxatives is discouraged, as having bowel movements may come to be dependent upon their use. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enemas" title="Enemas">Enemas</a> can be used to provide a form of mechanical stimulation. However, enemas are generally useful only for stool in the rectum, not in the intestinal tract.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laxatives">Laxatives</span></h3>
<p>If <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laxatives" title="Laxatives">laxatives</a> are used, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milk_of_magnesia" title="Milk of magnesia">milk of magnesia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene_glycol" title="Polyethylene glycol">polyethylene glycol</a> are recommended as first-line agents due to their low cost and safety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AGA2013_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-AGA2013-30">[30]</a></sup> Stimulants should only be used if this is not effective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AGA2000_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-AGA2000-15">[15]</a></sup> In cases of chronic constipation, polyethylene glycol appears superior to <a href="/wiki/Lactulose" title="Lactulose">lactulose</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Prokinetic_agent" title="Prokinetic agent">Prokinetics</a> may be used to improve gastrointestinal motility. A number of new agents have shown positive outcomes in chronic constipation; these include <a href="/wiki/Prucalopride" title="Prucalopride">prucalopride</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Lubiprostone" title="Lubiprostone">lubiprostone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_intervention">Physical intervention</span></h3>
<p>Constipation that resists the above measures may require physical intervention such as manual disimpaction (the physical removal of impacted stool using the hands; see <a href="/wiki/Fecal_impaction" title="Fecal impaction">fecal impaction</a>). Regular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exercise" title="Exercise">exercise</a> can help improve chronic constipation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pediatric">Pediatric</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lactulose" title="Lactulose">Lactulose</a> and milk of magnesia have been compared with polyethylene glycol (PEG) in children. All had similar side effects, but PEG was more effective at treating constipation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Osmotic laxatives are recommended over stimulant laxatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prognosis">Prognosis</span></h2>
<p>Complications that can arise from constipation include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemorrhoids" title="Hemorrhoids">hemorrhoids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anal_fissures" title="Anal fissures">anal fissures</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rectal_prolapse" title="Rectal prolapse">rectal prolapse</a>, and fecal impaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walia_7-11"><a href="#cite_note-walia-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mccallum_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-mccallum-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bharucha_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-bharucha-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-niddk_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-niddk-38">[38]</a></sup> Straining to pass stool may lead to hemorrhoids. In later stages of constipation, the abdomen may become distended, hard and diffusely tender. Severe cases ("fecal impaction" or <i>malignant constipation</i>) may exhibit symptoms of bowel obstruction (<a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">vomiting</a>, very tender abdomen) and <a href="/wiki/Encopresis" title="Encopresis">encopresis</a>, where soft stool from the small intestine bypasses the mass of impacted fecal matter in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colon_(anatomy)" title="Colon (anatomy)">colon</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epidemiology">Epidemiology</span></h2>
<p>Constipation is the most common digestive complaint in the United States as per survey data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Depending on the definition employed, it occurs in 2% to 20% of the population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AGA2000_15-5"><a href="#cite_note-AGA2000-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epi89_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Epi89-40">[40]</a></sup> It is more common in women, the elderly and children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epi89_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Epi89-40">[40]</a></sup> The reasons it occurs more frequently in the elderly is felt to be due to an increasing number of health problems as humans age and decreased physical activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP05_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-AFP05-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>12% of the population worldwide reports having constipation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
<li>Chronic constipation accounts for 3% of all visits annually to pediatric outpatient clinics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walia_7-12"><a href="#cite_note-walia-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>Constipation-related health care costs total $6.9 billion in the US annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AGA2000_15-6"><a href="#cite_note-AGA2000-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>More than four million Americans have frequent constipation, accounting for 2.5 million physician visits a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-niddk_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-niddk-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li>Around $725 million is spent on laxative products each year in America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-niddk_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-niddk-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
