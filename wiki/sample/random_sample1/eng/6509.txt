<p><b>Fecal incontinence</b> (<b>FI</b>), also called <b>faecal incontinence</b>, <b>bowel incontinence</b>, <b>anal incontinence</b>, <b>accidental bowel leakage</b>, or (in some forms) <a href="/wiki/Encopresis" title="Encopresis">encopresis</a>, is a lack of control over <a href="/wiki/Defecation" title="Defecation">defecation</a>, leading to involuntary loss of bowel contents—including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flatus" title="Flatus">flatus</a> (gas), liquid stool elements and <a href="/wiki/Mucus" title="Mucus">mucus</a>, or solid <a href="/wiki/Human_feces" title="Human feces">feces</a>. FI is a <a href="/wiki/Medical_sign" title="Medical sign">sign</a> or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_symptom" title="Medical symptom">symptom</a>, not a <a href="/wiki/Medical_diagnosis" title="Medical diagnosis">diagnosis</a>. Incontinence can result from different causes and might occur with either <a href="/wiki/Constipation" title="Constipation">constipation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhea</a>. Continence is maintained by several inter-related factors, and usually there is more than one deficiency of these mechanisms for incontinence to develop. The most common causes are thought to be immediate or delayed <a href="/wiki/Childbirth#Maternal_complications" title="Childbirth">damage from childbirth</a>, complications from prior <a href="/wiki/Colorectal_surgery#Surgical_treatment_and_diagnostic_procedures" title="Colorectal surgery">anorectal surgery</a> (especially involving the anal sphincters or hemorrhoidal vascular cushions) and altered bowel habits (e.g., caused by <a href="/wiki/Irritable_bowel_syndrome" title="Irritable bowel syndrome">irritable bowel syndrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crohn%27s_disease" title="Crohn's disease">Crohn's disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ulcerative_colitis" title="Ulcerative colitis">ulcerative colitis</a>, food intolerance, or constipation with overflow incontinence).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI-1">[1]</a></sup> An estimated 2.2% of community dwelling adults are affected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fecal incontinence has three main consequences: local reactions of the perianal skin and urinary tract, including <a href="/wiki/Cutaneous_condition#Secondary_lesions" title="Cutaneous condition">maceration</a> (softening and whitening of skin due to continuous moisture), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urinary_tract_infections" title="Urinary tract infections">urinary tract infections</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decubitus_ulcers" title="Decubitus ulcers">decubitus ulcers</a> (pressure sores);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI-1">[1]</a></sup> a financial expense for individuals (due to cost of medication and incontinence products, and loss of productivity), employers (days off), and <a href="/wiki/Health_insurance" title="Health insurance">medical insurers</a> and society generally (health care costs, unemployment); and an associated decrease in <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamada_textbook_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yamada_textbook-3">[3]</a></sup> There is often reduced self-esteem, shame, humiliation, <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a>, a need to organize life around easy access to bathroom and avoidance of enjoyable activities. FI is an example of a <a href="/wiki/Social_stigma" title="Social stigma">stigmatized</a> medical condition, which creates barriers to successful management. People may be too embarrassed to seek medical help, and attempt to self-manage the symptom in secrecy from others.</p>
<p>FI is one of the most psychologically and socially debilitating conditions in an otherwise healthy individual, but it is generally treatable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup> Management may be achieved through an individualized mix of dietary, pharmacologic, and surgical measures. Health care professionals are often poorly informed about treatment options,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup> and may fail to recognize the effect of FI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamada_textbook_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Yamada_textbook-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">Signs and symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Signs and symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>FI affects virtually all aspects of peoples' lives, greatly diminishing physical and mental health, and affect personal, social and professional life. Emotional effects may include stress, fearfulness, anxiety, exhaustion, fear of public humiliation, feeling dirty, poor body-image, reduced desire for sex, anger, humiliation, depression, isolation, secrecy, frustration and embarrassment. Some people may need to be in control of life outside of FI as means of compensation. The physical symptoms such as skin soreness, pain and odor may also affect quality of life. Physical activity such as shopping or exercise is often affected. Travel may be affected, requiring careful planning. Working is also affected for most. Relationships, social activities and self-image likewise often suffer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NICE_guidelines_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NICE_guidelines-4">[4]</a></sup> Symptoms may worsen over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>FI is a sign or a symptom, not a diagnosis,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NICE_guidelines_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-NICE_guidelines-4">[4]</a></sup> and represents an extensive list of causes. Usually, it is the result of a complex interplay of several coexisting factors, many of which may be simple to correct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NICE_guidelines_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-NICE_guidelines-4">[4]</a></sup> Up to 80% of people may have more than one abnormality that is contributing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Deficits of individual functional components of the continence mechanism can be partially compensated for a certain period of time, until the compensating components themselves fail. For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obstetric" title="Obstetric">obstetric</a> injury may precede onset by decades, but <a href="/wiki/Menopause" title="Menopause">postmenopausal</a> changes in the tissue strength reduce in turn the competence of the compensatory mechanisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coloproctology_textbook_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coloproctology_textbook-6">[6]</a></sup> The most common factors in the development are thought to be obstetric injury and after effects of anorectal surgery, especially those involving the anal sphincters and hemorrhoidal vascular cushions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI-1">[1]</a></sup> The majority of incontinent persons over the age of 18 fall into one of several groups. These are: those with structural anorectal abnormalities (sphincter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trauma_(medicine)" title="Trauma (medicine)">trauma</a>, sphincter degeneration, <a href="/wiki/Anal_fistula" title="Anal fistula">perianal fistula</a>, rectal prolapse), <a href="/wiki/Neurological_disorder" title="Neurological disorder">neurological disorders</a> (<a href="/wiki/Multiple_sclerosis" title="Multiple sclerosis">multiple sclerosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">spinal cord</a> injury, <a href="/wiki/Spina_bifida" title="Spina bifida">spina bifida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>, etc.), constipation/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fecal_loading" title="Fecal loading">fecal loading</a> (presence of a large amount of feces in the rectum with stool of any consistency), cognitive and/or behavioral dysfunction (<a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">dementia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Learning_disability" title="Learning disability">learning disabilies</a>), diarrhea, <a href="/wiki/Inflammatory_bowel_disease" title="Inflammatory bowel disease">inflammatory bowel diseases</a> (e.g. ulcerative colitis, Crohn's disease), irritable bowel syndrome, <a href="/wiki/Disability" title="Disability">disability</a> related (people who are frail, acutely unwell, or have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronic_(medicine)" title="Chronic (medicine)">chronic</a>/<a href="/wiki/Acute_(medicine)" title="Acute (medicine)">acute</a> disabilities), and those cases which are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Idiopathic" title="Idiopathic">idiopathic</a> (of unknown cause).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NICE_guidelines_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-NICE_guidelines-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nusrat_2012_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nusrat_2012-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus" title="Diabetes mellitus">Diabetes mellitus</a> is also known to be a cause, but the mechanism of this relationship is not well understood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rodrigues_2012_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rodrigues_2012-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Congenital">Congenital</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Congenital">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Anorectal anomalies and spinal cord defects may be a cause in children. These are usually picked up and operated upon during early life, but continence is often imperfect thereafter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anal_canal">Anal canal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Anal canal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The functioning of the anal canal can be damaged, traumatically or atraumatically. The resting tone of the anal canal is not the only factor which is important, both the length of the high pressure zone and its radial translation of force are required for continence. This means that even with normal anal canal pressure, focal defects such as the keyhole deformity can be the cause of substantial symptoms. External anal sphincter (EAS) dysfunction is associated with impaired voluntary control, whereas internal anal sphincter (IAS) dysfunction is associated with impaired fine tuning of fecal control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI-1">[1]</a></sup> Lesions which mechanically interfere with, or prevent the complete closure of the anal canal can cause a liquid stool or mucous rectal discharge. Such lesions include <a href="/wiki/Hemorrhoid" title="Hemorrhoid">piles</a> (inflamed hemorrhoids), <a href="/wiki/Anal_fissure" title="Anal fissure">anal fissures</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anal_cancer" title="Anal cancer">anal cancer</a> or fistulae. Obstetric injury may tear the anal sphincters, and some of these injuries may be occult (undetected). The risk of injury is greatest when labor has been especially difficult or prolonged, when <a href="/wiki/Forceps_in_childbirth" title="Forceps in childbirth">forceps</a> are used, with higher <a href="/wiki/Birth_weight" title="Birth weight">birth weights</a> or when an <a href="/wiki/Episiotomy" title="Episiotomy">episiotomy</a> is performed. Only when there is post operative investigation of FI such as <a href="/wiki/Endoanal_ultrasound" title="Endoanal ultrasound">endoanal ultrasound</a> is the injury discovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup> FI is a much under-reported complication of surgery. The IAS is easily damaged with an anal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surgical_retractor" title="Surgical retractor">retractor</a> (especially the Park's anal retractor), leading to reduced resting pressure postoperatively. Since the hemorrhoidal vascular cushions contribute 15% of the resting anal tone, surgeries involving these structures may affect continence status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup> Partial internal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sphincterotomy" title="Sphincterotomy">sphincterotomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fistulotomy" title="Fistulotomy">fistulotomy</a>, anal stretch (Lord's operation), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemorrhoidectomy" title="Hemorrhoidectomy">hemorrhoidectomy</a> or transanal advancement flaps may all lead to FI post operatively, with soiling being far more common than solid FI. The "keyhole deformity" refers to scarring within the anal canal and is another cause of mucus leakage and minor incontinence. This defect is also described as a groove in the anal canal wall, and may occur after posterior midline <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fissurectomy" title="Fissurectomy">fissurectomy</a> or fistulotomy, or with lateral IAS defects. Rare causes of traumatic injury to the anal sphincters include military or traffic accidents complicated by <a href="/wiki/Pelvic_fracture" title="Pelvic fracture">pelvic fractures</a>, spine injuries or <a href="/wiki/Perineum" title="Perineum">perineal</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lacerations" title="Lacerations">lacerations</a>, insertion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreign_bodies" title="Foreign bodies">foreign bodies</a> in the rectum, and sexual abuse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup> Nontraumatic conditions causing anal sphincter weakness include <a href="/wiki/Scleroderma" title="Scleroderma">scleroderma</a>, damage to the <a href="/wiki/Pudendal_nerve" title="Pudendal nerve">pudendal nerves</a> and IAS degeneration of unknown etiology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamada_textbook_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Yamada_textbook-3">[3]</a></sup> Radiation induced FI may involve the anal canal as well as the rectum, when <a href="/wiki/Proctitis" title="Proctitis">proctitis</a>, anal fistula formation and diminished function of internal and external sphincter occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup> Irradiation may occur during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiotherapy" title="Radiotherapy">radiotherapy</a>, e.g. for <a href="/wiki/Prostate_cancer" title="Prostate cancer">prostate cancer</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pelvic_floor">Pelvic floor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pelvic floor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many people with FI have a generalized weakness of the pelvic floor, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puborectalis" title="Puborectalis">puborectalis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamada_textbook_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Yamada_textbook-3">[3]</a></sup> A weakened puborectalis leads to widening of the anorectal angle, and impaired barrier to stool in the rectum entering the anal canal, and this is associated with incontinence to solids. Abnormal descent of the pelvic floor can also be a sign of pelvic floor weakness. Abnormal descent manifests as <a href="/wiki/Descending_perineum_syndrome" title="Descending perineum syndrome">descending perineum syndrome</a> (&gt;4 cm perineal descent).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamada_textbook_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Yamada_textbook-3">[3]</a></sup> This syndrome initially gives constipation, and later FI. The pelvic floor is innervated by the pudendal nerve and the <a href="/wiki/Sacral_spinal_nerve_3" title="Sacral spinal nerve 3">S3</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sacral_spinal_nerve_4" title="Sacral spinal nerve 4">S4</a> branches of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelvic_plexus" title="Pelvic plexus">pelvic plexus</a>. With recurrent straining, e.g. during difficult labour or long term constipation, then stretch injury can damage the nerves supplying <a href="/wiki/Levator_ani" title="Levator ani">levator ani</a>. The pudendal nerve is especially vulnerable to irreversible damage, (stretch induced pudendal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neuropathy" title="Neuropathy">neuropathy</a>) which can occur with a 12% stretch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup> If the pelvic floor muscles lose their innervation, they cease to contract and their muscle fibres are in time replaced by fibrous tissue, which is associated with pelvic floor weakness and incontinence. Increased pudendal nerve terminal motor latency may indicate pelvic floor weakness. The various types of pelvic organ prolapse (e.g. external rectal prolapse, mucosal prolapse and internal rectal intussusception &amp; solitary rectal ulcer syndrome) may also cause coexisting <a href="/wiki/Obstructed_defecation" title="Obstructed defecation">obstructed defecation</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rectum">Rectum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rectum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The rectum needs to be of a sufficient volume to store stool until defecation. The rectal walls need to be "compliant" i.e. able to distend to an extent to accommodate stool. Rectal sensation is required to detect the presence, nature and amount of rectal contents. The rectum must also be able to evacuate its contents fully. There must also be efficient co-ordination of rectal sensation and relaxation of the anal canal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hoffmann_1995_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-hoffmann_1995-9">[9]</a></sup> <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">Rectal storage capacity (i.e. rectal volume + rectal compliance) may be affected in the following ways. Surgery involving the rectum (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Lower_anterior_resection" title="Lower anterior resection">lower anterior resection</a>, often performed for colorectal cancer), radiotherapy directed at the rectum, and inflammatory bowel disease can cause scarring, which may result in the walls of the rectum becoming stiff and inelastic, reducing compliance. Reduced rectal storage capacity may lead to urge incontinence, where there is an urgent need to defecate as soon as stool enters the rectum, where normally stool would be stored until there was enough to distend the rectal walls and initiate the defecation cycle. Tumors and strictures also may impair reservoir function. Conversely, increased rectal volume (<a href="/wiki/Megarectum" title="Megarectum">megarectum</a>), may cause fecal loading and overflow FI. Reduced rectal sensation may be a contributory factor.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> If the sensory nerves are damaged, detection of stool in the rectum is dulled or absent, and the person will not feel the need to defecate until too late. Rectal hyposensitivity may manifest as constipation, FI, or both. Rectal hyposensitivty was reported to be present in 10% of people with FI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burgell_2012_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burgell_2012-10">[10]</a></sup> Pudendal neuropathy is one cause of rectal hyposensitivity, and may lead to fecal loading/impaction, megarectum and overflow FI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rao_2004_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rao_2004-11">[11]</a></sup> Normal evacuation of rectal contents is 90-100%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup> If there is incomplete evacuation during defecation, residual stool will be left in the rectum and threaten continence once defecation is finished. This is a feature of people with soiling secondary to obstructed defecation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Obstructed defecation is often due to <a href="/wiki/Anismus" title="Anismus">anismus</a> (paradoxical contraction or relaxation failure of the puborectalis).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:38</sup> Whilst anismus is largely a <a href="/wiki/Functional_disorder" title="Functional disorder">functional disorder</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_disease" title="Organic disease">organic</a> pathologic lesions may mechanically interfere with rectal evacuation. Other causes of incomplete evacuation include non-emptying defects like a <a href="/wiki/Rectocele" title="Rectocele">rectocele</a>. Straining to defecate pushes stool into the rectocele, which acts like a <a href="/wiki/Diverticulum" title="Diverticulum">diverticulum</a> and causes stool sequestration. Once the voluntary attempt to defecate, albeit dysfunctional, is finished, the voluntary muscles relax, and residual rectal contents are then able to descend into the anal canal and cause leaking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:37</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_nervous_system">Central nervous system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Central nervous system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Continence requires conscious and subconscious networking of information from and to the anorectum. Defects/brain damage may affect the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a> focally (e.g. stroke, tumor e.g. spinal cord lesions, trauma, multiple sclerosis) or diffusely (e.g. dementia, multiple sclerosis, infection, <a href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Parkinson's disease">Parkinson's disease</a> or drug-induced).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salat-Foix_2012_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Salat-Foix_2012-14">[14]</a></sup> FI (and <a href="/wiki/Urinary_incontinence" title="Urinary incontinence">urinary incontinence</a>) may also occur during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epileptic_seizures" title="Epileptic seizures">epileptic seizures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dural_ectasia" title="Dural ectasia">Dural ectasia</a> is an example of a spinal cord lesion that may affect continence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nallamshetty_2002_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nallamshetty_2002-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diarrhea">Diarrhea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Diarrhea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Liquid stool is more difficult to control than formed, solid stool. Hence, FI can be exacerbated by diarrhea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NICE_guidelines_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-NICE_guidelines-4">[4]</a></sup> Some consider diarrhea to be the most common aggravating factor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup> <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">Where diarrhea is caused by temporary problems such as mild infections or food reactions, incontinence tends to be short lived. Chronic conditions, such as irritable bowel syndrome or <a href="/wiki/Crohn%27s_disease" title="Crohn's disease">Crohn's disease</a>, can cause severe diarrhea lasting for weeks or months. Diseases, drugs, and indigestible dietary fats that interfere with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intestine" title="Intestine">intestineal</a> absorption may cause <a href="/wiki/Steatorrhea" title="Steatorrhea">steatorrhea</a> (oily rectal discharge &amp; fatty diarrhea) and degrees of FI. Respective examples include <a href="/wiki/Cystic_fibrosis" title="Cystic fibrosis">cystic fibrosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orlistat" title="Orlistat">orlistat</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Olestra" title="Olestra">olestra</a>. <a href="/wiki/Postcholecystectomy_syndrome" title="Postcholecystectomy syndrome">Postcholecystectomy diarrhea</a> is diarrhea that occurs following gall bladder removal, due to excess <a href="/wiki/Bile_acid" title="Bile acid">bile acid</a>.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Orlistat is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-obesity" title="Anti-obesity">anti-obesity</a> (weight loss) drug that blocks the absorption of fats. This may give side effects of FI, diarrhea and steatorrhea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overflow_incontinence">Overflow incontinence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Overflow incontinence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This may occur when there is a large mass of feces in the rectum (fecal loading), which may become hardened (<a href="/wiki/Fecal_impaction" title="Fecal impaction">fecal impaction</a>). Liquid stool elements are able to pass around the obstruction, leading to incontinence. Megarectum (enlarged rectal volume) and rectal hyposensitivity are associated with overflow incontinence. Hospitalized patients and care home residents may develop FI via this mechanism,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NICE_guidelines_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-NICE_guidelines-4">[4]</a></sup> possibly a result of lack of mobility, reduced alertness, constipating effect of medication and/or dehydration.</p>

<p>The mechanisms and factors contributing to normal continence are multiple and inter-related. The puborectalis sling, forming the anorectal angle (see diagram), is responsible for gross continence of solid stool.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamada_textbook_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Yamada_textbook-3">[3]</a></sup> The IAS is an involuntary muscle, contributing about 55% of the resting anal pressure. Together with the hemorrhoidal vascular cushions, the IAS maintains continence of flatus and liquid during rest. The EAS is a voluntary muscle, doubling the pressure in the anal canal during contraction, which is possible for a short time. The rectoanal inhibitory reflex (RAIR) is an involuntary IAS relaxation in response to rectal distension, allowing some rectal contents to descend into the anal canal where it is brought into contact with specialized sensory mucosa to detect consistency. The rectoanal excitatory reflex (RAER) is an initial, semi-voluntary contraction of the EAS and puborectalis which in turn prevents incontinence following the RAIR. Other factors include the specialized anti-peristaltic function of the last part of the sigmoid colon, which keeps the rectum empty most of the time, sensation in the lining of the rectum and the anal canal to detect when there is stool present, its consistency and quantity, and the presence of normal rectoanal reflexes and defecation cycle which completely evacuates stool from the rectum and anal canal. Problems affecting any of these mechanisms and factors may be involved in the cause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-13"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnostic_approach">Diagnostic approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Diagnostic approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Identification of the exact causes usually begins with a thorough <a href="/wiki/Medical_history" title="Medical history">medical history</a>, including detailed questioning about symptoms, bowel habits, diet, medication and other medical problems. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_rectal_examination" title="Digital rectal examination">Digital rectal examination</a> is performed to assesses resting pressure and voluntary contraction (maximum squeeze) of the sphincter complex and puborectalis. Anal sphincter defects, rectal prolapse, and abnormal perineal descent may be detected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamada_textbook_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-Yamada_textbook-3">[3]</a></sup> Anorectal physiology tests assess the functioning of the anorectal anatomy. <a href="/wiki/Anorectal_manometry" title="Anorectal manometry">Anorectal manometry</a> records the pressure exerted by the anal sphincters and puborectalis during rest and during contraction. The procedure is also able to assess sensitivity of the anal canal and rectum. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anal_electromyography" title="Anal electromyography">Anal electromyography</a> tests for nerve damage, which is often associated with obstetric injury. Pudendal nerve terminal motor latency tests for damage to the pudendal motor nerves. Proctography, also known as <a href="/wiki/Defecography" title="Defecography">defecography</a>, shows how much stool the rectum can hold, how well the rectum holds it, and how well the rectum can evacuate the stool. It will also highlight defects in the structure of the rectum such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internal_rectal_intussusception" title="Internal rectal intussusception">internal rectal intussusception</a>. Dynamic pelvic <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">MRI</a>, also called MRI defecography is an alternative which is better for some problems but not as good for other problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proctosigmoidoscopy" title="Proctosigmoidoscopy">Proctosigmoidoscopy</a> involves the insertion of an <a href="/wiki/Endoscope" title="Endoscope">endoscope</a> (a long, thin, flexible tube with a camera) into the anal canal, rectum and sigmoid colon. The procedure allows for visualization of the interior of the gut, and may detect signs of disease or other problems that could be a cause, such as inflammation, tumors, or scar tissue. Endoanal ultrasound, which some consider to be the gold standard for detection of anal canal lesions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abdool_2012_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Abdool_2012-19">[19]</a></sup> evaluates the structure of the anal sphincters, and may detect occult sphincter tears that otherwise would go unseen.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Functional_disorder" title="Functional disorder">Functional</a> FI is common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bharucha_2006_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bharucha_2006-20">[20]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Rome_process" title="Rome process">Rome process</a> published <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diagnostic_criteria" title="Diagnostic criteria">diagnostic criteria</a> for functional FI, which they defined as "recurrent uncontrolled passage of fecal material in an individual with a developmental age of at least 4 years". The diagnostic criteria are, one or more of the following factors present for the last 3 months: abnormal functioning of normally innervated and structurally intact muscles, minor abnormalities of sphincter structure/innervation (nerve supply), normal or disordered bowel habits, (i.e., fecal retention or diarrhea), and psychological causes. Furthermore, exclusion criteria are given. These are factors which all must be excluded for a diagnosis of functional FI, and are abnormal innervation caused by lesion(s) within the brain (e.g., dementia), spinal cord (at or below <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thoracic_vertebra_12" title="Thoracic vertebra 12">T12</a>), or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacral_nerve" title="Sacral nerve">sacral nerve</a> roots, or mixed lesions (e.g., multiple sclerosis), or as part of a generalized peripheral or autonomic neuropathy (e.g., due to diabetes), anal sphincter abnormalities associated with a multisystem disease (e.g., scleroderma), and structural or neurogenic abnormalities that are the major cause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rome_iii_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rome_iii-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is no globally accepted definition,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI-1">[1]</a></sup> but fecal incontinence is generally defined as the recurrent inability to voluntarily control the passage of bowel contents through the anal canal and expel it at a socially acceptable location and time, occurring in individuals over the age of four.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-14"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamada_textbook_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-Yamada_textbook-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NICE_guidelines_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-NICE_guidelines-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coloproctology_textbook_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Coloproctology_textbook-6">[6]</a></sup> "Social continence" has been given various precise definitions for the purposes of research, however generally it refers to symptoms being controlled to an extent that is acceptable to the individual in question, with no significant effect on their life. There is no consensus about the best way to classify FI,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NICE_guidelines_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-NICE_guidelines-4">[4]</a></sup> and several methods are used.</p>
<p>Symptoms can be directly or indirectly related to the loss of bowel control. The direct (primary) symptom is a lack of control over bowel contents which tends to worsen without treatment. Indirect (secondary) symptoms, which are the result of leakage, include <a href="/wiki/Pruritus_ani" title="Pruritus ani">pruritus ani</a> (an intense itching sensation from the anus), perianal <a href="/wiki/Dermatitis" title="Dermatitis">dermatitis</a> (irritation and inflammation of the skin around the anus), and urinary tract infections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI-1">[1]</a></sup> Due to embarrassment, people may only mention secondary symptoms rather than acknowledge incontinence. Any major underlying cause will produce additional signs and symptoms, such as protrusion of mucosa in external <a href="/wiki/Rectal_prolapse" title="Rectal prolapse">rectal prolapse</a>. Symptoms of fecal leakage (FL) are similar, and may occur after defecation. There may be loss of small amounts of brown fluid and staining of the underwear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-15"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>FI can be divided into those people who experience a defecation urge before leakage (urge incontinence), and those who experience no sensation before leakage (passive incontinence or soiling).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NICE_guidelines_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-NICE_guidelines-4">[4]</a></sup> Urge incontinence is characterized by a sudden need to defecate, with little time to reach a toilet. Urge and passive FI may be associated with weakness of the <a href="/wiki/External_anal_sphincter" title="External anal sphincter">external anal sphincter</a> (EAS) and <a href="/wiki/Internal_anal_sphincter" title="Internal anal sphincter">internal anal sphincter</a> (IAS) respectively. Urgency may also be associated with reduced rectal volume, reduced ability of the rectal walls to distend and accommodate stool, and increased rectal sensitivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamada_textbook_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-Yamada_textbook-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is a continuous spectrum of different clinical presentations from incontinence of flatus (gas), through incontinence of mucus or liquid stool, to solids. The term <i>anal incontinence</i> often is used to describe flatus incontinence,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NICE_guidelines_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-NICE_guidelines-4">[4]</a></sup> however it is also used as a synonym for FI generally. It may occur together with incontinence of liquids or solids, or it may present in isolation. Flatus incontinence may be the first sign of FI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-16"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup> Once continence to flatus is lost, it is rarely restored.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NICE_guidelines_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-NICE_guidelines-4">[4]</a></sup> Anal incontinence may be equally disabling as the other types.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4th_ICI_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-4th_ICI-22">[22]</a></sup> Fecal leakage, fecal soiling and fecal seepage are minor degrees of FI, and describe incontinence of liquid stool, mucus, or very small amounts of solid stool. They cover a spectrum of increasing symptom severity (staining, soilage, seepage and accidents).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI-1">[1]</a></sup> Rarely, minor FI in adults may be described as <a href="/wiki/Encopresis" title="Encopresis">encopresis</a>. Fecal leakage is a related topic to <a href="/wiki/Rectal_discharge" title="Rectal discharge">rectal discharge</a>, but this term does not necessarily imply any degree of incontinence. Discharge generally refers to conditions where there is <a href="/wiki/Pus" title="Pus">pus</a> or increased mucus production, or anatomical <a href="/wiki/Lesion" title="Lesion">lesions</a> that prevent the anal canal from closing fully, whereas fecal leakage generally concerns disorders of IAS function and functional evacuation disorders which cause a solid fecal mass to be retained in the <a href="/wiki/Rectum" title="Rectum">rectum</a>. Solid stool incontinence may be called complete (or major) incontinence, and anything less as partial (or minor) incontinence (i.e. incontinence of flatus (gas), liquid stool and/or mucus).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-17"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In children over the age of four who have been toilet trained, a similar condition is generally termed encopresis (or soiling), which refers to the voluntary or involuntary loss of (usually soft or semi-liquid) stool.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The term pseudoincontinence is used when there is FI in children who have anatomical defects (e.g. enlarged <a href="/wiki/Sigmoid_colon" title="Sigmoid colon">sigmoid colon</a> or anal <a href="/wiki/Stenosis" title="Stenosis">stenosis</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-18"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup> Encopresis is a term that is usually applied when there are no such anatomical defects present. The <a href="/wiki/ICD-10" title="ICD-10">ICD-10</a> classifies nonorganic encopresis under "behavioural and emotional disorders with onset usually occurring in childhood and adolescence" and organic causes of encopresis along with FI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> FI can also be classified according to gender, since the cause in females may be different from males, for example it may develop following radical <a href="/wiki/Prostatectomy" title="Prostatectomy">prostatectomy</a> in males,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shamliyan_2009_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shamliyan_2009-25">[25]</a></sup> whereas females may develop FI as an immediate or delayed consequence of damage whilst giving birth. <a href="/wiki/Pelvic_cavity" title="Pelvic cavity">Pelvic anatomy</a> is also different according to gender, with a wider <a href="/wiki/Pelvic_outlet" title="Pelvic outlet">pelvic outlet</a> in females.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_measurement">Clinical measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Clinical measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several severity scales exist. The <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Clinic" title="Cleveland Clinic">Cleveland Clinic</a> (Wexner) fecal incontinence score takes into account five parameters that are scored on a scale from zero (absent) to four (daily) frequency of incontinence to gas, liquid, solid, of need to wear pad, and of lifestyle changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI-1">[1]</a></sup> The Park's incontinence score uses four categories:</p>
<dl>
<dd>1 - those continent for solid and liquid stool and also for flatus.</dd>
<dd>2 - those continent for solid and liquid stool but incontinent for flatus (with or without urgency).</dd>
<dd>3 - those continent for solid stool but incontinent for liquid stool or flatus.</dd>
<dd>4 - those incontinent to formed stool (complete incontinence).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GB-Ratto_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-GB-Ratto-26">[26]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>The fecal incontinence severity index is based on four types of leakage (gas, mucus, liquid stool, solid stool) and five frequencies (once to three times per month, once per week, twice per week, once per day, twice or more per day). Other severity scales include: AMS, Pescatori, Williams score, Kirwan, Miller score, Saint Mark's score and the Vaizey scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-19"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Differential_diagnosis">Differential diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Differential diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>FI may present with signs similar to rectal discharge (e.g. fistulae, proctitis or rectal prolapse), pseudoincontinence, encopresis (with no organic cause) and irritable bowel syndrome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-20"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>FI is generally treatable with conservative management, surgery or both.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-21"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup> The success of treatment depends upon the exact causes and how easily these are corrected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NICE_guidelines_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-NICE_guidelines-4">[4]</a></sup> Treatment choice depends on the cause and severity of disease, and the motivation and general health of the person effected. Commonly, conservative measures are used together, and if appropriate surgery carried out. Treatments may be attempted until symptoms are satisfactorily controlled. A treatment algorithm based upon the cause has been proposed, including conservative, non-operative and surgical measures (neosphincter refers to either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dynamic_graciloplasty" title="Dynamic graciloplasty">dynamic graciloplasty</a> or artificial bowel sphincter, lavage refers to retrograde rectal irrigation).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-22"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup> Conservative measures include dietary modification, drug treatment, retrograde anal irrigation, biofeedback retraining anal sphincter exercises. Incontinence products refer to devices such as anal plugs and perineal pads and garments such as diapers/nappies. Perineal pads are efficient and acceptable for only minor incontinence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-23"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup> If all other measures are ineffective <a href="/wiki/Colectomy" title="Colectomy">removing the entire colon</a> may be an option.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dietary modification may be important for successful management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamada_textbook_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-Yamada_textbook-3">[3]</a></sup> Both diarrhea and constipation can contribute to different cases, so dietary advice must be tailored to address the underlying cause or it may be ineffective or counter productive. In persons with disease aggravated by diarrhea or those with rectal loading by soft stools, the following suggestions may be beneficial: increase <a href="/wiki/Dietary_fiber" title="Dietary fiber">dietary fiber</a>; reduce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wholegrain" title="Wholegrain">wholegrain</a> <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereals</a>/<a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a>; reduce fruit and vegetables which contain natural laxative compounds (<a href="/wiki/Rhubarb" title="Rhubarb">rhubarb</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Figs" title="Figs">figs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prunes" title="Prunes">prunes</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plums" title="Plums">plums</a>); limit <a href="/wiki/Bean" title="Bean">beans</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulses" title="Pulses">pulses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cabbage" title="Cabbage">cabbage</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brussels_sprouts" title="Brussels sprouts">sprouts</a>; reduce <a href="/wiki/Spice" title="Spice">spices</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Chili_pepper" title="Chili pepper">chilli</a>); reduce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_sweeteners" title="Artificial sweeteners">artificial sweeteners</a> (e.g. sugar free <a href="/wiki/Chewing_gum" title="Chewing gum">chewing gum</a>); reduce <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Stout" title="Stout">stout</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ale" title="Ale">ale</a>); reduce <a href="/wiki/Lactose" title="Lactose">lactose</a> if there is some degree of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lactase_deficiency" title="Lactase deficiency">lactase deficiency</a>; and reduce <a href="/wiki/Caffeine" title="Caffeine">caffeine</a>. Caffeine lowers the resting tone of the anal canal and also causes diarrhea. Excessive doses of <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">vitamin C</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> supplements may increase FI. Reducing olestra fat substitute, which can cause diarrhea, may also help.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medication">Medication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Medication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pharmacological management may include anti-diarrheal/constipating agents and laxatives/stool bulking agents Stopping or substituting any previous medication that causes diarrhea may be helpful in some (see <a href="/wiki/Fecal_incontinence#Diarrhea" title="Fecal incontinence">table</a>). There is not good evidence for the use of any medications however.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Omar2013_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Omar2013-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In people who have undergone <a href="/wiki/Cholecystectomy" title="Cholecystectomy">gallbladder removal</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bile_acid_sequestrant" title="Bile acid sequestrant">bile acid sequestrant</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cholestyramine" title="Cholestyramine">cholestyramine</a> may help minor degrees of FI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Bulking agents also absorb water, so may be helpful for those with diarrhea. A common side effect is <a href="/wiki/Bloating" title="Bloating">bloating</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flatulence" title="Flatulence">flatulence</a>. <a href="/wiki/Topical_medication" title="Topical medication">Topical agents</a> to treat and prevent dermatitis may also be used, such as topical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antifungal_medication" title="Antifungal medication">antifungals</a> when there is evidence of perianal <a href="/wiki/Candidiasis" title="Candidiasis">candidiasis</a> or occasionally mild topical anti-inflammatory medication. Prevention of secondary lesions is carried out by perineal cleansing, <a href="/wiki/Moisturizer" title="Moisturizer">moisturization</a>, and use of a skin protectant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gray_2012_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gray_2012-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_measures">Other measures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Other measures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Evacuation aids (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suppositories" title="Suppositories">suppositories</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enemas" title="Enemas">enemas</a>) e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glycerine" title="Glycerine">glycerine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bisacodyl" title="Bisacodyl">bisacodyl</a> suppositories may be prescribed. People may have poor resting tone of the anal canal, and consequently may not be able to retain an enema, in which case <a href="/wiki/Transanal_irrigation" title="Transanal irrigation">transanal irrigation</a> (retrograde anal irrigation) may be a better option, as this equipment utilizes an inflatable catheter to prevent loss of the irrigation tip and to provide a water tight seal during irrigation. A volume of luke warm water is gently pumped into the colon via the anus. People can be taught how to perform this treatment in their own homes, but it does require special equipment. If the irrigation is efficient, stool will not reach the rectum again for up to 48 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emmanuel2013_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emmanuel2013-31">[31]</a></sup> By regularly emptying the bowel using transanal irrigation, controlled bowel function is often re-established to a high degree in patients with bowel incontinence and/or constipation. This enables control over the time and place of evacuation and development of a consistent bowel routine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emmanuel2013_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Emmanuel2013-31">[31]</a></sup> However, persistent leaking of residual irrigation fluid during the day may occur and make this option unhelpful, particularly in persons with obstructed defecation syndrome who may have incomplete evacuation of any rectal contents. Consequently, the best time to carry out the irrigation is typically in the evening, allowing any residual liquid to be passed the next morning before leaving the home. Complications such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrolyte_imbalance" title="Electrolyte imbalance">electrolyte imbalance</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bowel_perforation" title="Bowel perforation">perforation</a> are rare. The effect of transanal irrigation varies considerably. Some individuals experience complete control of incontinence, and other report little or no benefit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emmanuel2013_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-Emmanuel2013-31">[31]</a></sup> It has been suggested that if appropriate, people be offered home retrograde anal irrigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NICE_guidelines_4-12"><a href="#cite_note-NICE_guidelines-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biofeedback#Fecal_incontinence_and_anismus" title="Biofeedback">Biofeedback</a> (the use equipment to record or amplify and then feed back activities of the body) is a commonly used and researched treatment, but the benefits are uncertain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norton_2012_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Norton_2012-32">[32]</a></sup> Biofeedback therapy varies in the way it is delivered, but it is unknown if one type has benefits over another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norton_2012_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Norton_2012-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The role of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelvic_floor_exercise" title="Pelvic floor exercise">pelvic floor exercises</a> and anal sphincter exercises in FI is poorly determined. While there may be some benefit they appear less useful than implanted sacral nerve stimulators. These exercises aim to increase the strength of the pelvic floor muscles (mainly levator ani). The anal sphincters are not technically part of the pelvic floor muscle group, but the EAS is a voluntary, striated muscle which therefore can be strengthened in a similar manner. It has not been established whether pelvic floor exercises can be distinguished from anal sphincter exercises in practice by the people doing them. This kind of exercise is more commonly used to treat urinary incontinence, for which there is a sound evidence base for effectiveness. More rarely are they used in FI. The effect of anal sphincter exercises are variously stated as an increase in the strength, speed or endurance of voluntary contraction (EAS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norton_2012_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Norton_2012-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Electrical stimulation can also be applied to the anal sphincters and pelvic floor muscles, inducing muscle contraction without traditional exercises (similar to <a href="/wiki/Transcutaneous_electrical_nerve_stimulation" title="Transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation">transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation</a>, TENS). The evidence supporting its use is limited, and any benefit is tentative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hosker_2007_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hosker_2007-33">[33]</a></sup> In light of the above, intra-anal electrical stimulation (using an anal probe as electrode) appears to be more efficacious than intra-vaginal (using a vaginal probe as electrode).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hosker_2007_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hosker_2007-33">[33]</a></sup> Rarely, skin reactions may occur where the electrodes are placed, but these issues typically resolve when the stimulation is stopped. Surgically implanted <a href="/wiki/Sacral_nerve_stimulation" title="Sacral nerve stimulation">sacral nerve stimulation</a> may be more effective than exercises, and electrical stimulation and biofeedback may be more effective than exercises or electrical stimulation by themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norton_2012_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-Norton_2012-32">[32]</a></sup> TENS is also sometimes used to treat FI by <a href="/wiki/Percutaneous_tibial_nerve_stimulation#Transcutaneous_tibial_nerve_stimulation" title="Percutaneous tibial nerve stimulation">transcutaneous tibial nerve stimulation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a minority of people, anal plugs may be useful for either standalone therapy or in concert with other treatments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deutekom_2012_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deutekom_2012-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="Updated version https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26193665 (June 2016)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup> Anal plugs (sometimes termed tampons) aim to block involuntary loss of fecal material, and they vary in design and composition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NICE_guidelines_4-13"><a href="#cite_note-NICE_guidelines-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">Polyurethane</a> plugs were reported to perform better than those made of polyvinyl-alcohol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deutekom_2012_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Deutekom_2012-35">[35]</a></sup> Plugs are less likely to help those with frequent bowel movements,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-24"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup> and many find them difficult to tolerate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deutekom_2012_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-Deutekom_2012-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In women, a device that functions as an inflatable balloon in the vagina, has been approved for use in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surgery">Surgery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Surgery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Surgical_management_of_fecal_incontinence" title="Surgical management of fecal incontinence">Surgical management of fecal incontinence</a></div>
<p>Surgery may be carried out if conservative measures alone are not sufficient to control incontinence. There are many surgical options, and their relative effectiveness is debated due to a lack of good quality evidence. The optimal treatment regime may be a both surgical and non-surgical treatments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_2015_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown_2015-37">[37]</a></sup> The surgical options can be considered in four categories: restoration and improvement of residual sphincter function (sphincteroplasty, sacral nerve stimulation, tibial nerve stimulation, correction of anorectal deformity), replacement / imitation of the sphincter or its function (anal encirclement, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SECCA_procedure" title="SECCA procedure">SECCA procedure</a>, non-dynamic graciloplasty, perianal injectable bulking agents), dynamic sphincter replacement (artificial bowel sphincter, dynamic graciloplasty), antegrade continence enema (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malone_procedure" title="Malone procedure">Malone procedure</a>), and finally fecal diversion (e.g. colostomy).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI-1">[1]</a></sup> A surgical treatment algorithm has been proposed. Isolated sphincter defects (IAS/EAS) may be initially treated with sphincteroplasty and if this fails, the person can be assessed for sacral nerve stimulation. Functional deficits of the EAS and/or IAS (i.e. where there is no structural defect, or only limited EAS structural defect, or with neurogenic incontinence) may be assessed for sacral nerve stimulation. If this fails, neosphincter with either dynamic graciloplasty or artificial anal sphincter may be indicated. Substantial muscular and/or neural defects may be treated with neosphincter initially.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coloproctology_textbook_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Coloproctology_textbook-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epidemiology">Epidemiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Epidemiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>FI is thought to be very common,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI-1">[1]</a></sup> but much under-reported due to embarrassment. One study reported a prevalence of 2.2% in the general population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_2-25"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-2">[2]</a></sup> It affects people of all ages, but is more common in older adults (but it should not be considered a normal part of aging).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shah_2012_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shah_2012-38">[38]</a></sup> Females are more likely to develop it than males (63% of those with FI over 30 may be female).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI-1">[1]</a></sup> In 2014, the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Health_Statistics" title="National Center for Health Statistics">National Center for Health Statistics</a> reported that one out of every six seniors in the U.S. who lived in their own home or apartment had FI. Men and women were equally affected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> 45–50% of people with FI have severe physical and/or mental disabilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_core_subjects_FI-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Risk factors include age, female gender, urinary incontinence, history of vaginal delivery (non-<a href="/wiki/Caesarean_section" title="Caesarean section">Caesarean section</a> childbirth), obesity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4th_ICI_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-4th_ICI-22">[22]</a></sup> prior anorectal surgery, poor general health and physical limitations. Combined urinary and fecal incontinence is sometimes termed double incontinence, and it is more likely to be present in those with urinary incontinence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lacima_2003_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lacima_2003-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Traditionally, FI was thought to be an insignificant complication of surgery, but it is now known that a variety of different procedures are associated with this possible complication, and sometimes at high levels. Examples are midline internal sphincterotomy (8% risk), lateral internal sphincterotomy, <a href="/wiki/Fistulectomy" title="Fistulectomy">fistulectomy</a>, fistulotomy (18-52%), hemorrhoidectomy (33%), <a href="/wiki/Ileo-anal_pouch" title="Ileo-anal pouch">ileo-anal reservoir reconstruction</a>, Lower anterior resection, total abdominal colectomy, <a href="/wiki/Ureterosigmoidostomy" title="Ureterosigmoidostomy">ureterosigmoidostomy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4th_ICI_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-4th_ICI-22">[22]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Anal_fissure#Anal_dilation" title="Anal fissure">anal dilation</a> (Lord's procedure, 0-50%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ommer_2008_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ommer_2008-41">[41]</a></sup> Some authors consider obstetric trauma to be the most common cause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rieger_1999_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rieger_1999-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the first mention of urinary incontinence occurs in 1500 BC in the <a href="/wiki/Ebers_Papyrus" title="Ebers Papyrus">Ebers Papyrus</a>, the first mention of FI in a medical context is unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Treatment_of_FI_thesis_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Treatment_of_FI_thesis-43">[43]</a></sup> For many centuries, colonic irrigation was the only treatment available. Stoma creation was described in 1776, FI associated with rectal prolapse in 1873 and anterior sphincter repair in 1875. During the mid 20th Century, several operations were developed for instances where the sphincters were intact but weakened.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Surgery_for_FI_chapter_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Surgery_for_FI_chapter-44">[44]</a></sup> Muscle transpositions using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gluteus_maximus" title="Gluteus maximus">gluteus maximus</a> or the gracilis were devised, but did not become used widely until later. End-to-end sphincteroplasty is shown to have a high failure rate in 1940. In 1971 Parks and McPartlin first describe an overlapping sphincteroplasty procedure. Biofeedback is first introduced in 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> In 1975, Parks describes post anal repair, a technique to reinforce the pelvic floor and EAS to treat idiopathic cases. Endoanal ultrasound is invented in 1991, which starts to demonstrate the high number of occult sphincter tears following vaginal deliveries. In 1994, the use of an endoanal coil during pelvic MRI shows greater detail of the anal canal than previously. During the last 20 years, dynamic graciliplasty, sacral nerve stimulation, injectable perianal bulking agents and radiofrequency ablation have been devised, mainly due to the relatively poor success rates and high morbidity associated with the earlier procedures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Surgery_for_FI_chapter_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Surgery_for_FI_chapter-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society_and_culture">Society and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Society and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Persons with this symptom are frequently ridiculed and ostracized in public. It has been described as one of the most psychologically and socially debilitating conditions in an otherwise healthy individual. In older people, it is one of the most common reasons for admission into a care home. Persons who develop FI earlier in life are less likely to marry and obtain employment. Often, people will go to great lengths to keep their condition secret. It has been termed "the silent affliction" since many do not discuss the problem with their close family, employers or clinicians. They may be subject to gossip, hostility, and other forms of social exclusion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The economic cost has not received much attention. In the Netherlands, outpatients were reported to have total costs of €2169 annually, and over half of this was productivity loss in work. In the USA, the average lifetime cost (treatment and follow-up) was $17,166 per person in 1996. The average hospital charges for sphincteroplasty was $8555 per procedure. Overall, in the USA, the total charges associated with surgery increased from $34 million in 1998 to $57.5 million in 2003. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacral_nerve" title="Sacral nerve">Sacral nerve</a> stimulation, dynamic <a href="/wiki/Gracilis_muscle" title="Gracilis muscle">graciloplasty</a> and colostomy were all shown to be cost effective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Among the most offensive insults in Japan (on the same level as <i>fuck</i> in English) relate to incontinence, such as <i>kusotare/kusottare</i> and <i>shikkotare</i> which mean <i>shit hanger/leaker/oozer</i> and <i>piss leaker/oozer</i> respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fecal_incontinence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tissue_engineering" title="Tissue engineering">Engineered</a> anal sphincters grown from <a href="/wiki/Stem_cell" title="Stem cell">stem cells</a> have been successfully implanted in mice. New blood vessels developed and the tissue displayed normal contraction and relaxation. In the future, these methods may become part of the management of FI, replacing the need for high morbidity implanted devices such as the artificial bowel sphincter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
