<p>A <b>poikilotherm</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/ɔɪ/ 'oy' in 'boy'">ɔɪ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/θ/ 'th' in 'thigh'">θ</span><span title="/ɜːr/ 'ur' in 'fur'">ɜːr</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span></span>, <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/ɔɪ/ 'oy' in 'boy'">ɔɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/θ/ 'th' in 'thigh'">θ</span><span title="/ɜːr/ 'ur' in 'fur'">ɜːr</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is an <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organism</a> whose internal temperature varies considerably. It is the opposite of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeotherm" title="Homeotherm">homeotherm</a>, an organism which maintains <a href="/wiki/Thermal" title="Thermal">thermal</a> <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostasis</a>. Usually the variation is a consequence of variation in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ambient_temperature" title="Ambient temperature">ambient environmental temperature</a>. Many terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Ectotherm" title="Ectotherm">ectotherms</a> are poikilothermic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hilde_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-hilde-1">[1]</a></sup> However some ectotherms remain in temperature-constant environments to the point that they are actually able to maintain a constant internal temperature (i.e. are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeotherm" title="Homeotherm">homeothermic</a>). It is this distinction that often makes the term "poikilotherm" more useful than the vernacular "cold-blooded", which is sometimes used to refer to <a href="/wiki/Ectotherm" title="Ectotherm">ectotherms</a> more generally.</p>
<p>Poikilothermic animals include types of vertebrate animals, specifically fish, amphibians, and reptiles, as well as a large number of <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrate</a> animals. The <a href="/wiki/Naked_mole-rat" title="Naked mole-rat">naked mole-rat</a> is the only mammal that is currently thought to be poikilothermic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daly_et_al..2C_.281997.29_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Daly_et_al..2C_.281997.29-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherwin.2C_.282010.29_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sherwin.2C_.282010.29-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poikilotherm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term derives from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> <i>poikilos</i> (<span lang="el" xml:lang="el">ποικίλος</span>), meaning "varied," ultimately from "thousand-making" and <i>thermos</i> (<span lang="el" xml:lang="el">θερμός</span>), meaning "heat".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physiology">Physiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poikilotherm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Homeothermy-poikilothermy.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="1000" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Homeothermy-poikilothermy.png/250px-Homeothermy-poikilothermy.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Homeothermy-poikilothermy.png/375px-Homeothermy-poikilothermy.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Homeothermy-poikilothermy.png/500px-Homeothermy-poikilothermy.png 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Homeothermy-poikilothermy.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sustained energy output of a poikilotherm (a <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizard</a>) and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeotherm" title="Homeotherm">homeotherm</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Mouse" title="Mouse">mouse</a>) as a function of core body temperature. The homeotherm has a much higher output, but can only function over a very narrow range of body temperatures.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>For an important chemical reaction, poikilotherms may have four to ten <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a> systems that operate at different temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> As a result, poikilotherms often have larger, more complex <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genomes</a> than homeotherms in the same <a href="/wiki/Ecological_niche" title="Ecological niche">ecological niche</a>. <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">Frogs</a> are a notable example of this effect, though their complex development is likely more important.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because their metabolism is variable and generally below that of homeothermic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animals" title="Animals">animals</a>, sustained high-energy activities like powered <a href="/wiki/Flight" title="Flight">flight</a> in large animals or maintaining a large <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a> is generally beyond poikilotherm animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The metabolism of poikilotherms favors strategies such as sit-and-wait hunting over chasing prey for larger animals with high movement cost. As they do not use their <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolisms</a> to heat or cool themselves, total energy requirement over time is low. For the same body weight, poikilotherms need only 5 to 10% of the energy of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeotherm" title="Homeotherm">homeotherms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations_in_poikilotherms">Adaptations in poikilotherms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poikilotherm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Adaptations in poikilotherms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Some adaptations are behavioral. Lizards and <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakes</a> bask in the sun in the early morning and late evening, and seek shelter around noon.</li>
<li>The eggs of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yellow-faced_bumblebee" title="Yellow-faced bumblebee">yellow-faced bumblebee</a> are unable to regulate heat. A behavioral adaptation to combat this is incubation, where to maintain the internal temperatures of eggs, the queen and her workers will incubate the brood almost constantly, by warming their abdomens and touching them to the eggs.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">Termite</a> mounds are usually oriented in a north-south direction so that they absorb as much heat as possible around dawn and dusk and minimise heat absorption around noon.</li>
<li>Tuna are able to warm their entire bodies through a heat exchange mechanism called the <a href="/wiki/Rete_mirabile" title="Rete mirabile">rete mirabile</a>, which helps keep heat inside the body, and minimises the loss of heat through the <a href="/wiki/Gill" title="Gill">gills</a>. They also have their swimming muscles near the center of their bodies instead of near the surface, which minimises heat loss.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gigantothermy" title="Gigantothermy">Gigantothermy</a> means using a low ratio of surface area to volume to minimise heat loss, such as in <a href="/wiki/Sea_turtle" title="Sea turtle">sea turtles</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Camel" title="Camel">Camel</a>s use a method of heat storage in a desert environment, allowing their body temperatures to rise during the day to counteract the extremely low temperatures of night, and vice versa.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poikilotherm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is comparatively easy for a poikilotherm to accumulate enough energy to reproduce. Poikilotherms at the same <a href="/wiki/Trophic_level" title="Trophic level">trophic level</a> often have much shorter generations than homeotherms: weeks rather than years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Such applies even to animals with similar ecological roles such as <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakes</a>.</p>
<p>This difference in energy requirement also means that a given food source can support a greater density of poikilothermic animals than homeothermic animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This is reflected in the predator-prey ratio which is usually higher in poikilothermic fauna compared to homeothermic ones. However, when homeotherms and poikilotherms have similar niches, and compete, the homeotherm can often drive poikilothermic competitors to extinction, because homeotherms can gather food for a greater fraction of each day.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_medicine">In medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poikilotherm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In medicine, loss of normal thermoregulation in humans is referred to as "poikilothermia". This is usually seen with sedative and hypnotic drugs or in '<a href="/wiki/Compartment_syndrome" title="Compartment syndrome">compartment syndrome</a>'. For example, <a href="/wiki/Barbiturate" title="Barbiturate">barbiturates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chloral_hydrate" title="Chloral hydrate">chloral hydrate</a> may precipitate this effect.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/REM_sleep" title="REM sleep">REM sleep</a> is also considered a poikilothermic state in humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
