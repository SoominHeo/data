<p>A <b>displacement</b> <i>is a vector that is the shortest <a href="/wiki/Distance" title="Distance">distance</a> from the initial to the final <a href="/wiki/Position_(vector)" title="Position (vector)">position</a> of a point P.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It quantifies both the distance and direction of an imaginary motion along a straight line from the initial position to the final position of the point.</i></p>
<p>A displacement may be also described as a 'relative position': the final position of a point (<i><b>R<sub>f</sub></b></i>) relative to its initial position (<i><b>R<sub>i</sub></b></i>), and a displacement vector can be mathematically defined as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vector_subtraction" title="Vector subtraction">difference</a> between the final and initial position vectors:</p>
<dl>
<dd><span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y mw-math-element" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">

<p>In considering motions of objects over time the instantaneous <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a> of the object is the rate of change of the displacement as a function of time. The velocity then is distinct from the instantaneous <a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">speed</a> which is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_rate" title="Time rate">time rate</a> of change of the distance traveled along a specific path. The velocity may be equivalently defined as the time rate of change of the position vector. If one considers a moving initial position, or equivalently a moving origin (e.g. an initial position or origin which is fixed to a train wagon, which in turn moves with respect to its rail track), the velocity of P (e.g. a point representing the position of a passenger walking on the train) may be referred to as a relative velocity, as opposed to an absolute velocity, which is computed with respect to a point which is considered to be 'fixed in space' (such as, for instance, a point fixed on the floor of the train station).</p>
<p>For motion over a given interval of time, the displacement divided by the length of the time interval defines the average velocity. (Note that the average <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a>, as a vector, differs from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Average_speed" title="Average speed">average speed</a> that is the ratio of the path length — a scalar — and the time interval.)</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rigid_body">Rigid body</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Displacement_(vector)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Rigid body">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In dealing with the motion of a <a href="/wiki/Rigid_body" title="Rigid body">rigid body</a>, the term <i>displacement</i> may also include the <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">rotations</a> of the body. In this case, the displacement of a particle of the body is called <b>linear displacement</b> (displacement along a line), while the rotation of the body is called <b><a href="/wiki/Angular_displacement" title="Angular displacement">angular displacement</a></b>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Derivatives">Derivatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Displacement_(vector)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Derivatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For a position vector <i><b>s</b></i> that is a function of time <i>t</i>, the derivatives can be computed with respect to <i>t</i>. These derivatives have common utility in the study of <a href="/wiki/Kinematics" title="Kinematics">kinematics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Control_theory" title="Control theory">control theory</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vibration_sensor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vibration sensor (page does not exist)">vibration sensing</a> and other sciences and engineering disciplines.</p>

<p>These common names correspond to terminology used in basic kinematics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stewart_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-stewart-2">[2]</a></sup> By extension, the higher order derivatives can be computed in a similar fashion. Study of these higher order derivatives can improve approximations of the original displacement function. Such higher-order terms are required in order to accurately represent the displacement function as <a href="/wiki/Taylor_series" title="Taylor series">a sum of an infinite series</a>, enabling several analytical techniques in engineering and physics. The fourth order derivative is called <a href="/wiki/Jounce" title="Jounce">jounce</a>, the fifth <a href="/wiki/Crackle_(physics)" title="Crackle (physics)">crackle</a>, and the sixth <a href="/wiki/Pop_(physics)" title="Pop (physics)">pop</a>.</p>
