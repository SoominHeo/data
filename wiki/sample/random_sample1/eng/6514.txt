<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Perversion</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">For other uses of "pervert" or "perversion", see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Perversion_(disambiguation)" title="Perversion (disambiguation)">Perversion (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Perversion</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Human_behavior" title="Human behavior">human behavior</a> that <a href="/wiki/Deviance_(sociology)" title="Deviance (sociology)">deviates</a> from that which is understood to be <a href="/wiki/Orthodoxy" title="Orthodoxy">orthodox</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normal_(behavior)" title="Normal (behavior)">normal</a>. Although the term <i>perversion</i> can refer to a variety of forms of deviation, it is most often used to describe <a href="/wiki/Human_sexual_activity" title="Human sexual activity">sexual behaviors</a> that are considered particularly abnormal, repulsive or <a href="/wiki/Sexual_obsessions" title="Sexual obsessions">obsessive</a>. Perversion differs from deviant behavior, in that the latter covers areas of behavior (such as petty crime) for which <i>perversion</i> would be too strong a term. It is often considered <a href="/wiki/Pejorative" title="Pejorative">derogatory</a>, and, in psychological literature, the term <i><a href="/wiki/Paraphilia" title="Paraphilia">paraphilia</a></i> has been used as a replacement,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martins_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Martins-1">[1]</a></sup> though this term is controversial, and <i>deviation</i> is sometimes used in its place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_concept">History of concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perversion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One view is that the concept of perversion is subjective,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martins_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Martins-1">[1]</a></sup> and its application varies depending on the individual. Another view considers that perversion is a degradation of an objectively true morality. Originating in the 1660s a pervert was originally defined as "one who has forsaken a doctrine or system regarded as true, apostate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-etymonlinepervert_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-etymonlinepervert-3">[3]</a></sup> The sense of a pervert as a sexual term was derived in 1896, and applied originally to variants of sexualities or sexual behavior believed harmful by the individual or group using the term.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-sexual_usages">Non-sexual usages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perversion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Non-sexual usages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The verb <i>pervert</i> is less narrow in reference than the related nouns, and can be used without any sexual connotations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dicpervert_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-dicpervert-4">[4]</a></sup> It is used in English law for the crime of <a href="/wiki/Perverting_the_course_of_justice" title="Perverting the course of justice">perverting the course of justice</a> which is a common law offence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> There is a transition to the sexual in 'the technique of purposeful perversion' of conversational remarks: "Purposeful perversion of what a woman has said ... is a long step closer to a direct attempt at seduction or rape."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The noun sometimes occurs in abbreviated <a href="/wiki/Slang" title="Slang">slang</a> form as "perv" and used as a verb meaning "to act like a pervert", and the adjective "pervy" also occurs. All are often, but not exclusively, used non-seriously.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> the term "<a href="/wiki/Perverse_incentive" title="Perverse incentive">perverse incentive</a>" means a policy that results in an effect contrary to the policymakers' intention.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sexual_usages">Sexual usages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perversion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sexual usages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Freud_on_the_role_of_perversion">Freud on the role of perversion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perversion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Freud on the role of perversion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Freud's didactic strategy in his <i><a href="/wiki/Three_Essays_on_the_Theory_of_Sexuality" title="Three Essays on the Theory of Sexuality">Three Essays on the Theory of Sexuality</a></i> was to construct a bridge between the "perversions" and "normal" sexuality. Clinically exploring 'a richly diversified collection of erotic endowments and inclinations: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermaphroditism" title="Hermaphroditism">hermaphroditism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pedophilia" title="Pedophilia">pedophilia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodomy" title="Sodomy">sodomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fetishism" title="Fetishism">fetishism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exhibitionism" title="Exhibitionism">exhibitionism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sadomasochism" title="Sadomasochism">sadism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sadomasochism" title="Sadomasochism">masochism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coprophilia" title="Coprophilia">coprophilia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Necrophilia" title="Necrophilia">necrophilia</a>' among them, Freud concluded that 'all humans are innately perverse'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He found the roots of such perversions in infantile sexuality—in 'the child's "polymorphously perverse" inclinations ... the "aptitude" for such perversity is innate'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The 'crucial irony of Freud's account in the <i>Three Essays</i> was that perversion in childhood <i>was</i> the norm'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Refining his analysis a decade later, Freud stressed that while childhood sexuality involved a wide and unfocused range of perverse activities, by contrast with adult perversion there was 'an important difference between them. Perverse sexuality is as a rule excellently centred: all its activities are directed to an aim—usually a single one; one component instinct has gained the upper hand...In that respect there is no difference between perverse and normal sexuality other than the fact that their dominating component instincts and consequently their sexual aims are different. In both of them, one might say, a well-organized tyranny has been established, but in each of the two a different family has seized the reins of power'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>A few years later, in "A Child is Being Beaten" (1919), Freud laid greater stress on the fact that perversions 'go through a process of development, that they represent an end-product and not an initial manifestation ... that the sexual aberrations of childhood, as well as those of mature life, are ramifications of the same complex'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>—the <a href="/wiki/Oedipus_complex" title="Oedipus complex">Oedipus complex</a>. <a href="/wiki/Otto_Fenichel" title="Otto Fenichel">Otto Fenichel</a> took up the point about the defensive function of perversions—of 'experiences of sexual satisfactions which simultaneously gave a feeling of security by denying or contradicting some fear';<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> adding that while 'some people think that perverts are enjoying some kind of more intense sexual pleasure than normal people. This is not true ... [though] neurotics, who have repressed perverse longings, may envy the perverts who express the perverse longings openly'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arlene_Richards_on_the_role_of_perversion_in_women">Arlene Richards on the role of perversion in women</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perversion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Arlene Richards on the role of perversion in women">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Freud wrote extensively on perversion in men. However, he and his successors paid scant attention to perversion in women. In 2003, psychologist, psychoanalyst and feminist Arlene Richards published a seminal paper on female perversion, "A Fresh look at Perversion", in the Journal of the American Psychoanalytic Association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In 2015, psychoanalyst Lynn Friedman, in a review of The Complete Works of Arlene Richards in the Journal of the American Psychoanalytic Association, noted prior to that time, "virtually no analysts were writing about female perversion. This pioneering work undoubtedly paved the way for others, including Louise Kaplan (1991), to explore this relatively uncharted territory."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_permissive_society">The permissive society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perversion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The permissive society">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Permissive_society" title="Permissive society">Permissive society</a></div>
<p>With the <a href="/wiki/Sexual_revolution" title="Sexual revolution">Sexual revolution</a> of the later twentieth century, much that Freud had argued for became part of a new, wide-ranging liberal consensus, a quasi-normative belief that 'everyone's entitled to his own sex life...Some people fancy black rubber clothes. Consenting adults and all that'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> At times this might lead to a kind of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panglossianism" title="Panglossianism">Panglossian</a> world view where every <a href="/wiki/Sexual_fetishism" title="Sexual fetishism">fetishist</a> has his 'fetishera ... for every man who is hung up on shoes, there is a woman ready to cater for and groove with him, and for every man who gets his thrills from hair, there is a woman who gets hers from having her <a href="/wiki/The_Rape_of_the_Lock" title="The Rape of the Lock">locks raped</a>. <a href="/wiki/Havelock_Ellis" title="Havelock Ellis">Havelock Ellis</a> has many cases of this meeting of the minds: the man who yearns to get pressed on by high heels sooner or later meets the woman who has daydreamed all her life of heel-pressing'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Where internal controversy did arise in the liberal consensus was about the exact relation of variations to normal development - some considering in the wake of Freud that 'these different sexual orientations can best be explained and understood by comparison with normal development',<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and highlighting the <a href="/wiki/Fear_of_intimacy" title="Fear of intimacy">fear of intimacy</a> in perversion as 'a kind of sex ... which is hedged about with special conditions...puts <i>a vast distance</i> between the partners'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> From such a standpoint, 'whatever the deviant impulse or fantasy may be, that's where the real, true, loving sexuality is hidden'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>—a point of transition perhaps to some of the bleaker post-permissive visions of perversion.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_views">Critical views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perversion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Critical views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For some participants, 'Liberation, at least in its sexual form, was a new kind of imposed morality, quite as restricting' as what had gone before—one that 'took very little account of the complexity of human emotional connections'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and was driven by 'the superego injunction to enjoy that permeates our discourse'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> New, more sceptical currents of disenchantment with perversion emerged as a result (alongside more traditional condemnations) in both the French-speaking and English-speaking worlds.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jacques_Lacan" title="Jacques Lacan">Lacan</a> had early highlighted 'the <a href="/wiki/Ambivalence" title="Ambivalence">ambivalence</a> proper to the "partial drives" of scoptophilia, <a href="/wiki/Sadomasochism" title="Sadomasochism">sadomasochism</a> ... the often very little "realised" aspect of the apprehension of others in the practice of certain of these perversions'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In his wake, others would stress how 'there is always, in any perverse act, an aspect of rape, in the sense that the Other must find himself drawn into the experience despite himself ... a loss or abandonment of subjectivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Object_relations_theory" title="Object relations theory">object relations theory</a> would point to the way 'in perversion there is the refusal, the terror of strangeness'; to the way 'the "pervert"...attacks imaginative elaboration through compulsive action with an accomplice; and this is done to mask psychic pain'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Empirical studies would find 'in the perverse relationships described...an absolute absence of any shared pleasures';<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> while at the theoretical level 'perversions involve - the theory tells us—an attempted denial of the difference between the sexes and the generations', and include 'the wish to damage and dehumanize ... the misery of the driven, damaging life'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
