<p>Many <b>variants of <a href="/wiki/Shogi" title="Shogi">shogi</a></b> have been developed over the centuries, ranging from some of the largest <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a>-type games ever played to some of the smallest. A few of these variants are still regularly played, though none are nearly as popular as shogi itself.</p>
<p>The drop rule, often considered the most notable feature of shogi, is absent from most shogi variants, which therefore play more like other forms of chess, with the board becoming less crowded as pieces are exchanged.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predecessors_of_modern_shogi">Predecessors of modern shogi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shogi_variant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Predecessors of modern shogi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some form of chess had almost certainly reached Japan by the 9th century, if not earlier, but the earliest surviving Japanese description of the rules of chess dates from the early 12th century, during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heian_Period" title="Heian Period">Heian period</a>. Unfortunately, this description does not give enough information to play the game, but this has not stopped people from trying to reconstruct this early form of shogi, which is usually referred to as <a href="/wiki/Heian_shogi" title="Heian shogi">Heian shogi</a> (平安将棋). Piece movements were as in modern shogi, but there was no rook or bishop. The board appears to have been 9×8 or 8×8. The setup is unknown, but can reasonably be assumed to have been the same as in modern shogi (minus the rook and bishop, and minus a gold general in the 8×8 case), but possibly the pawns started on the second rank rather than the third. It can safely be assumed that the game was played without drops.</p>
<p>By the 16th century the game had taken a form closer to the modern game: it was played on a 9×9 board with the same setup as in modern shogi except that an extra piece (a drunken elephant) stood in front of the king. This form of the game is known as <a href="/wiki/Sho_shogi" title="Sho shogi">sho shogi</a> (小将棋), which means "little shogi". (While 9×9 may not seem 'little', it was smaller than the other shogi variants prevalent at the time.) The drunken elephant was eliminated by the <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Go-Nara" title="Emperor Go-Nara">Emperor Go-Nara</a> (reign 1526-1557), and it is assumed that the drop rule was introduced at about the same time, giving rise to shogi as we know it today.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Large-board_variants">Large-board variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shogi_variant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Large-board variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are a number of shogi variants played on boards larger than 9×9. These variants are all quite old, and were probably all played without drops. Michael C. Vanier says, "It is thought that the really huge games (dai-dai and up) were never really played to any significant extent [...] and were devised merely so that the creators could have the fun of inventing enormous games, amazing their friends and confounding their enemies. However, the games up to Tenjiku shogi at least appear to be quite playable, assuming one has the time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The same 12th-century document which describes the Heian form of shogi also describes a variant played on a 13×13 board, which is now called <a href="/wiki/Heian_dai_shogi" title="Heian dai shogi">Heian dai shogi</a> (平安大将棋). As with the smaller Heian shogi, the rules for this game have not been completely preserved.</p>
<p>The most popular large-board variant is <a href="/wiki/Chu_shogi" title="Chu shogi">chu shogi</a> (中将棋), played on a 12×12 board. The name means <b>middle shogi</b>, and the game is sometimes so called in English. Chu shogi has existed since at least the 14th century; there are earlier references, but it is not clear that they refer to the game as we now know it. Chu shogi is best known for a very powerful piece called the lion, which moves like a king but twice per turn. The game was still commonly played in Japan in the early 20th century, but has now largely died out. It has, however, gained some adherents in the West. The main reference work in English is the <i>Middle Shogi Manual</i> by George Hodges.</p>
<p>Other large medieval shogi variants were <a href="/wiki/Wa_shogi" title="Wa shogi">wa shogi</a> (11×11, possibly played with drops), <a href="/wiki/Dai_shogi" title="Dai shogi">dai shogi</a> (大将棋, "great shogi", 15×15), <a href="/wiki/Tenjiku_shogi" title="Tenjiku shogi">tenjiku shogi</a> (天竺将棋, literally "Indian shogi", but probably meant in the sense of "exotic shogi", 16×16), <a href="/wiki/Dai_dai_shogi" title="Dai dai shogi">dai-dai shōgi</a> (大大将棋, "great great shogi", 17×17), <a href="/wiki/Maka_dai_dai_shogi" title="Maka dai dai shogi">maka dai-dai shōgi</a> (摩訶大大将棋, "ultra great great shogi", 19×19) and <a href="/wiki/Tai_shogi" title="Tai shogi">tai shogi</a> (泰将棋, "grand shogi", 25×25). These variants date back at least to the 17th century. Tai shogi was thought to be the world's largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chess_variant" title="Chess variant">chess variant</a>, but recently records of an even larger variant, <a href="/wiki/Taikyoku_shogi" title="Taikyoku shogi">taikyoku shogi</a> (大局将棋, "ultimate shogi", 36×36), was discovered. However, there is no evidence that any of them were commonly played apart from wa shogi and dai shogi. Furthermore, the sources for the rules of the larger variants tend to disagree with each other on many particular issues, such that only wa shogi, chu shogi, dai shogi, and tenjiku shogi can be said to have one single canonical set of rules (and some lacunae in the latter two need to be reconstructed analogously to chu shogi).</p>
<p>The most recent large board variant is <a href="/wiki/Ko_shogi" title="Ko shogi">kō shōgi</a> (廣将棋 or 廣象棋 "wide (elephant) chess", 19×19), which is played on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Go_(board_game)" title="Go (board game)">go</a> board and incorporates elements of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_chess" title="Chinese chess">Chinese chess</a>. Ko shogi is unusual for the interdependence of its pieces and the complex rules of promotion.</p>
<p>Of the historical large-board variants, only wa (11×11), chu (12×12), tenjiku (16×16), and maka dai-dai shogi (19×19) have gained a limited following today. There is a society for chu shogi in Japan, as well as some efforts to revive maka dai-dai, though both efforts have changed the rules slightly from the historical ones. Tenjiku shogi gained some Western following around the turn of the millennium, but due to a misinterpretation of the rules it was then abandoned as being an immediate win for the first player. This is not the case under the corrected rules.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_variants">Modern variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shogi_variant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>These are some of the new and old shogi variants which have been invented. Time will show which if any of the many recently invented variants stand the test of usage and competition from other games, and stay in use.</p>
