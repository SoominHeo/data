<p><b>Astrocytes</b> (Astro from Greek <i>astron</i> = star and <i>cyte</i> from Greek "kyttaron" = cell), also known collectively as <b>astroglia</b>, are characteristic star-shaped <a href="/wiki/Neuroglia" title="Neuroglia">glial</a> <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">spinal cord</a>. The proportion of astrocytes in the brain is not well defined. Depending on the counting technique used, studies have found that the astrocyte proportion varies by region and ranges from 20% to 40% of all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glia" title="Glia">glia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> They perform many functions, including biochemical support of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothelial_cell" title="Endothelial cell">endothelial cells</a> that form the <a href="/wiki/Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier" title="Blood–brain barrier">blood–brain barrier</a>, provision of nutrients to the nervous tissue, maintenance of extracellular ion balance, and a role in the repair and scarring process of the brain and spinal cord following traumatic injuries.</p>
<p>Research since the mid-1990s has shown that astrocytes propagate intercellular <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">Ca<sup>2+</sup></a> waves over long distances in response to stimulation, and, similar to neurons, release transmitters (called <a href="/wiki/Gliotransmitter" title="Gliotransmitter">gliotransmitters</a>) in a Ca<sup>2+</sup>-dependent manner. Data suggest that astrocytes also signal to neurons through Ca<sup>2+</sup>-dependent release of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glutamate" title="Glutamate">glutamate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18834310_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18834310-2">[2]</a></sup> Such discoveries have made astrocytes an important area of research within the field of <a href="/wiki/Neuroscience" title="Neuroscience">neuroscience</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrocyte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Astrocytes are a sub-type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glial_cells" title="Glial cells">glial cells</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a>. They are also known as <i>astrocytic glial cells.</i> Star-shaped, their many processes envelop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synapses" title="Synapses">synapses</a> made by neurons. Astrocytes are classically identified using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Histological" title="Histological">histological</a> analysis; many of these cells express the intermediate filament <a href="/wiki/Glial_fibrillary_acidic_protein" title="Glial fibrillary acidic protein">glial fibrillary acidic protein</a> (GFAP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Several forms of astrocytes exist in the central nervous system including <i>fibrous</i> (in white matter), <i>protoplasmic</i> (in grey matter), and <i>radial</i>. The fibrous glia are usually located within white matter, have relatively few <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organelles" title="Organelles">organelles</a>, and exhibit long unbranched cellular processes. This type often has "vascular feet" that physically connect the cells to the outside of <a href="/wiki/Capillary" title="Capillary">capillary</a> walls when they are in proximity to them. The protoplasmic glia are the most prevalent and are found in grey matter tissue, possess a larger quantity of organelles, and exhibit short and highly branched tertiary processes. The radial glia are disposed in planes perpendicular to the axes of <a href="/wiki/Ventricular_system" title="Ventricular system">ventricles</a>. One of their processes abuts the <a href="/wiki/Pia_mater" title="Pia mater">pia mater</a>, while the other is deeply buried in gray matter. Radial glia are mostly present during development, playing a role in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neuron_migration" title="Neuron migration">neuron migration</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C3%BCller_cells" title="Müller cells">Müller cells</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">retina</a> and Bergmann glia cells of the cerebellar cortex represent an exception, being present still during adulthood. When in proximity to the pia mater, all three forms of astrocytes send out processes to form the pia-glial membrane.</p>

<p>Astrocytes are macroglial cells in the central nervous system. Astrocytes are derived from heterogeneous populations of progenitor cells in the neuroepithelium of the developing central nervous system. Recent works, summarized in a review by Rowitch and Kriegstein,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> indicate that there is remarkable similarity between the well known genetic mechanisms that specify the lineage of diverse <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neuron</a> subtypes and that of macroglial cells. Just as with neuronal cell specification, canonical signaling factors like <a href="/wiki/Sonic_hedgehog" title="Sonic hedgehog">Sonic hedgehog</a> (SHH), <a href="/wiki/Fibroblast_growth_factor" title="Fibroblast growth factor">Fibroblast growth factor</a> (FGFs), <a href="/wiki/Wnt_signaling_pathway" title="Wnt signaling pathway">WNTs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bone_morphogenetic_protein" title="Bone morphogenetic protein">bone morphogenetic proteins</a> (BMPs), provide positional information to developing macroglial cells through morphogen gradients along the dorsal–ventral, anterior–posterior and medial–lateral axes. The resultant patterning along the neuraxis leads to segmentation of the neuroepithelium into progenitor domains (p0, p1 p2, p3 and pMN) for distinct neuron types in the developing spinal cord. On the basis of several studies it is now believed that this model also applies to macroglial cell specification. Studies carried out by Hochstim and colleagues have demonstrated that three distinct populations of astrocytes arise from the p1, p2 and p3 domains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> These subtypes of astrocytes can be identified on the basis of their expression of different transcription factors (PAX6, <a href="/wiki/NKX6-1" title="NKX6-1">NKX6.1</a>) and cell surface markers (<a href="/wiki/Reelin" title="Reelin">reelin</a> and <a href="/wiki/SLIT1" title="SLIT1">SLIT1</a>). The three populations of astrocyte subtypes which have been identified are 1) dorsally located VA1 astrocytes, derived from p1 domain, express <a href="/wiki/PAX6" title="PAX6">PAX6</a> and reelin 2) ventrally located VA3 astrocytes, derived from p3, express NKX6.1 and SLIT1 and 3) and intermediate white-matter located VA2 astrocyte, derived from the p2 domain, which express PAX6, NKX6.1, reelin and SLIT1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> After astrocyte specification has occurred in the developing CNS, it is believed that astrocyte precursors migrate to their final positions within the nervous system before the process of terminal <a href="/wiki/Cellular_differentiation" title="Cellular differentiation">differentiation</a> occurs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrocyte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:652px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Metabolic_interactions_between_astrocytes_and_neurons_with_major_reactions.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="665" data-file-width="995" height="434" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Metabolic_interactions_between_astrocytes_and_neurons_with_major_reactions.png/650px-Metabolic_interactions_between_astrocytes_and_neurons_with_major_reactions.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Metabolic_interactions_between_astrocytes_and_neurons_with_major_reactions.png/975px-Metabolic_interactions_between_astrocytes_and_neurons_with_major_reactions.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3e/Metabolic_interactions_between_astrocytes_and_neurons_with_major_reactions.png 2x" width="650"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Metabolic_interactions_between_astrocytes_and_neurons_with_major_reactions.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Metabolic interactions between astrocytes and neurons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Previously in medical science, the neuronal network was considered the only important function of astrocytes, and they were looked upon as <i>gap fillers</i>. More recently, the function of astrocytes has been reconsidered, and they are now thought to play a number of active roles in the brain, including the secretion or absorption of neural transmitters and maintenance of the blood–brain barrier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Following on this idea the concept of a "tripartite synapse" has been proposed, referring to the tight relationship occurring at synapses among a presynaptic element, a postsynaptic element and a glial element.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Araque_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Araque-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Structural</b>: They are involved in the physical structuring of the brain. Astrocytes get their name because they are "star-shaped". They are the most abundant glial cells in the brain that are closely associated with neuronal synapses. They regulate the transmission of electrical impulses within the brain.</li>
<li><b>Glycogen fuel reserve buffer</b>: Astrocytes contain <a href="/wiki/Glycogen" title="Glycogen">glycogen</a> and are capable of <a href="/wiki/Glycogenesis" title="Glycogenesis">glycogenesis</a>. The astrocytes next to neurons in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frontal_cortex" title="Frontal cortex">frontal cortex</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hippocampus" title="Hippocampus">hippocampus</a> store and release glycogen. Thus, astrocytes can fuel neurons with glucose during periods of high rate of glucose consumption and glucose shortage. Recent research suggests there may be a connection between this activity and exercise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Metabolic support</b>: They provide neurons with nutrients such as <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactate</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier" title="Blood–brain barrier">Blood–brain barrier</a></b>: The astrocyte end-feet encircling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothelial_cell" title="Endothelial cell">endothelial cells</a> were thought to aid in the maintenance of the blood–brain barrier, but recent research indicates that they do not play a substantial role; instead, it is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tight_junctions" title="Tight junctions">tight junctions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Basal_lamina" title="Basal lamina">basal lamina</a> of the cerebral endothelial cells that play the most substantial role in maintaining the barrier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> However, it has recently been shown that astrocyte activity is linked to blood flow in the brain, and that this is what is actually being measured in fMRI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Transmitter uptake and release</b>: Astrocytes express plasma membrane transporters such as <a href="/wiki/Glutamate_transporter" title="Glutamate transporter">glutamate transporters</a> for several neurotransmitters, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glutamate" title="Glutamate">glutamate</a>, ATP, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GABA" title="GABA">GABA</a>. More recently, astrocytes were shown to release glutamate or <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> in a vesicular, Ca<sup>2+</sup>-dependent manner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> (This has been disputed for hippocampal astrocytes.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Potassium_spatial_buffering" title="Potassium spatial buffering">Regulation of ion concentration in the extracellular space</a></b>: Astrocytes express <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potassium_channels" title="Potassium channels">potassium channels</a> at a high density. When neurons are active, they release potassium, increasing the local extracellular concentration. Because astrocytes are highly permeable to potassium, they rapidly clear the excess accumulation in the extracellular space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> If this function is interfered with, the extracellular concentration of potassium will rise, leading to neuronal depolarization by the <a href="/wiki/Goldman_equation" title="Goldman equation">Goldman equation</a>. Abnormal accumulation of extracellular potassium is well known to result in epileptic neuronal activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Modulation of synaptic transmission</b>: In the <a href="/wiki/Supraoptic_nucleus" title="Supraoptic nucleus">supraoptic nucleus</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Hypothalamus" title="Hypothalamus">hypothalamus</a>, rapid changes in astrocyte morphology have been shown to affect heterosynaptic transmission between neurons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Hippocampus" title="Hippocampus">hippocampus</a>, astrocytes suppress synaptic transmission by releasing ATP, which is <a href="/wiki/ATP_hydrolysis" title="ATP hydrolysis">hydrolyzed</a> by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ectonucliotidase&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ectonucliotidase (page does not exist)">ectonucliotidases</a> to yield <a href="/wiki/Adenosine" title="Adenosine">adenosine</a>. Adenosine acts on neuronal <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_receptor" title="Adenosine receptor">adenosine receptors</a> to inhibit synaptic transmission, thereby increasing the <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_range" title="Dynamic range">dynamic range</a> available for <a href="/wiki/Long-term_potentiation" title="Long-term potentiation">LTP</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Vasomodulation</b>: Astrocytes may serve as intermediaries in neuronal regulation of blood flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Promotion of the myelinating activity of <a href="/wiki/Oligodendrocyte" title="Oligodendrocyte">oligodendrocytes</a></b>: Electrical activity in neurons causes them to release ATP, which serves as an important stimulus for myelin to form. However, the ATP does not act directly on oligodendrocytes. Instead, it causes astrocytes to secrete cytokine leukemia inhibitory factor (LIF), a regulatory protein that promotes the myelinating activity of oligodendrocytes. This suggests that astrocytes have an executive-coordinating role in the brain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Nervous system repair</b>: Upon injury to nerve cells within the central nervous system, astrocytes fill up the space to form a <a href="/wiki/Glial_scar" title="Glial scar">glial scar</a>, repairing the area by transformation into neurons<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and replacing the CNS cells that cannot regenerate.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><b>Long-term potentiation</b>: Scientists debate whether astrocytes integrate learning and memory in the hippocampus. Recently it has been shown that engrafting human glial progenitor cell in the nascent mice brains will cause the cells to differentiate into astrocytes. After differentiation these cells increase LTP and improve memory performance in the mice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Astrocytes are linked by <a href="/wiki/Gap_junction" title="Gap junction">gap junctions</a>, creating an electrically coupled (functional) <a href="/wiki/Syncytium" title="Syncytium">syncytium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Because of this ability of astrocytes to communicate with their neighbors, changes in the activity of one astrocyte can have repercussions on the activities of others that are quite distant from the original astrocyte.</p>
<p>An influx of Ca<sup>2+</sup> ions into astrocytes is the essential change that ultimately generates calcium waves. Because this influx is directly caused by an increase in blood flow to the brain, calcium waves are said to be a kind of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemodynamic_response" title="Hemodynamic response">hemodynamic response function</a>. An increase in intracellular calcium concentration can propagate outwards through this functional syncytium. Mechanisms of calcium wave propagation include diffusion of calcium ions and <a href="/wiki/Inositol_trisphosphate" title="Inositol trisphosphate">IP3</a> through gap junctions and <a href="/wiki/P2RX7#Recruitment_of_pannexin" title="P2RX7">extracellular ATP signalling</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Calcium elevations are the primary known axis of activation in astrocytes, and are necessary and sufficient for some types of astrocytic glutamate release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Given the importance of calcium signaling in astrocytes, tight regulatory mechanisms for the progression of the spatio-temporal calcium signaling have been developed. Via mathematical analysis it has been shown that localized inflow of Ca<sup>2+</sup> ions yields a localized raise in the cytosolic concentration of Ca<sup>2+</sup> ions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spatial_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spatial-27">[27]</a></sup> Moreover, cytosolic Ca<sup>2+</sup> accumulation is independent of every intracellular calcium flux and depends on the Ca<sup>2+</sup> exchange across the membrane, cytosolic calcium diffusion, geometry of the cell, extracellular calcium perturbation, and initial concentrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spatial_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spatial-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bergmann_glia">Bergmann glia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrocyte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bergmann glia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><i>Bergmann glia</i>, a type of glia<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> also known as radial epithelial cells (as named by <a href="/wiki/Camillo_Golgi" title="Camillo Golgi">Camillo Golgi</a>) or Golgi epithelial cells (GCEs; not to be mixed up with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golgi_cells" title="Golgi cells">Golgi cells</a>), are astrocytes in the <a href="/wiki/Cerebellum" title="Cerebellum">cerebellum</a> that have their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cell_body" title="Cell body">cell bodies</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Purkinje_cell" title="Purkinje cell">Purkinje cell</a> layer and processes that extend into the molecular layer, terminating with bulbous endfeet at the <a href="/wiki/Pia_mater" title="Pia mater">pial</a> surface. Bergmann glia express high densities of <a href="/wiki/Glutamate_transporter" title="Glutamate transporter">glutamate transporters</a> that limit diffusion of the <a href="/wiki/Neurotransmitter" title="Neurotransmitter">neurotransmitter</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glutamate" title="Glutamate">glutamate</a> during its release from synaptic terminals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Besides their role in early development of the cerebellum, Bergmann glia are also required for the pruning or addition of <a href="/wiki/Synapse" title="Synapse">synapses</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tripartite_synapse">Tripartite synapse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrocyte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tripartite synapse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Within the dorsal horn of the <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">spinal cord</a>, activated astrocytes have the ability to respond to almost all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurotransmitters" title="Neurotransmitters">neurotransmitters</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haydon2001_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haydon2001-31">[31]</a></sup> and, upon activation, release a multitude of neuroactive molecules such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glutamate" title="Glutamate">glutamate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a> (NO), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prostaglandins" title="Prostaglandins">prostaglandins</a> (PG), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D-serine" title="D-serine">D-serine</a>, which in turn influences neuronal excitability. The close association between astrocytes and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presynaptic" title="Presynaptic">presynaptic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postsynaptic" title="Postsynaptic">postsynaptic</a> terminals as well as their ability to integrate synaptic activity and release neuromodulators has been termed the "tripartite synapse".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Araque_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Araque-9">[9]</a></sup> Synaptic modulation by astrocytes takes place because of this 3-part association.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrocyte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Clinical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Astrocytomas">Astrocytomas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrocyte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Astrocytomas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Astrocytoma" title="Astrocytoma">Astrocytomas</a> are primary intracranial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tumor" title="Tumor">tumors</a> derived from astrocytes cells of the brain. It is also possible that glial progenitors or neural stem cells give rise to astrocytomas.</p>
<p>Astrocytomas are brain tumors that develop from astrocytes. They may occur in many parts of the brain and sometimes in the spinal cord. They can occur at any age and they primarily occur in males. Astrocytomas are divided into two categories: Low grade (I and II) and High Grade (III and IV). Low grade tumors are more common in children and high grade tumors are more common in adults.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref32_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref32-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pilocytic_astrocytoma" title="Pilocytic astrocytoma">Pilocytic astrocytoma</a> are Grade I tumors. They are considered benign and slow growing tumors. Pilocytic astrocytomas frequently have cystic portions filled with fluid and a nodule, which is the solid portion. Most are located in the cerebellum. Therefore, most symptoms are related to balance or coordination difficulties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref32_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-ref32-32">[32]</a></sup> They also occur more frequently in children and teens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref33_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref33-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Grade II Tumors grow relatively slow but invade surrounding healthy tissue. Usually considered benign but can grow into malignant tumors. Other names that are used are Fibrillary or Protoplasmic astrocytomas. They are prevalent in younger people who are often present with seizures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref33_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-ref33-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anaplastic astrocytoma is classified as grade III and are malignant tumors. They grow more rapidly than lower grade tumors and tend to invade nearby healthy tissue. Anaplastic astrocytomas recur more frequently than lower grade tumors because of their tendency to spread into surrounding tissue makes them difficult to completely remove surgically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref32_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-ref32-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Glioblastoma Multiforme is also a malignant tumor and classified as a grade IV. Glioblastomas can contain more than one cell type (i.e., astrocytes, oligondroctyes). Also, while one cell type may die off in response to a particular treatment, the other cell types may continue to multiply. Glioblastomas are the most invasive type of glial tumors as they grow rapidly and spread to nearby tissue. Approximately 50% of astrocytomas are glioblastomas and are very difficult to treat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref32_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-ref32-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neurodevelopment_disorders">Neurodevelopment disorders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrocyte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Neurodevelopment disorders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A relatively new direction has emerged in the field of neurodevelopmental disorders. This view states that astrocyte dysfunction may play a role in the aberration of neuronal circuitry that underlies certain psychiatric disorders, including the autism spectrum disorders (ASDs) and schizophrenia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chronic_pain">Chronic pain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrocyte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Chronic pain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Under normal conditions, pain conduction begins with some noxious signal followed by an action potential carried by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nociceptive" title="Nociceptive">nociceptive</a> (pain sensing) afferent neurons, which elicit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Excitatory_postsynaptic_potentials" title="Excitatory postsynaptic potentials">excitatory postsynaptic potentials</a> (EPSP) in the dorsal horn of the spinal cord. That message is then relayed to the <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex" title="Cerebral cortex">cerebral cortex</a>, where we translate those EPSPs into "pain." Since the discovery of astrocytic influence, our understanding of the conduction of pain has been dramatically complicated. Pain processing is no longer seen as a repetitive relay of signals from body to brain, but as a complex system that can be up- and down-regulated by a number of different factors. One factor at the forefront of recent research is in the pain-potentiating synapse located in the dorsal horn of the spinal cord and the role of astrocytes in encapsulating these synapses. Garrison and co-workers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> were the first to suggest association when they found a correlation between astrocyte <a href="/wiki/Hypertrophy" title="Hypertrophy">hypertrophy</a> in the dorsal horn of the spinal cord and hypersensitivity to pain after peripheral nerve injury, typically considered an indicator of glial activation after injury. Astrocytes detect neuronal activity and can release chemical transmitters, which in turn control synaptic activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haydon2001_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Haydon2001-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In the past, <a href="/wiki/Hyperalgesia" title="Hyperalgesia">hyperalgesia</a> was thought to be modulated by the release of <a href="/wiki/Substance_P" title="Substance P">substance P</a> and excitatory amino acids (EAA), such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glutamate" title="Glutamate">glutamate</a>, from the presynaptic afferent nerve terminals in the spinal cord dorsal horn. Subsequent activation of <a href="/wiki/AMPA" title="AMPA">AMPA</a> (α-amino-3-hydroxy-5-methyl-4-isoxazole propionic acid), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NMDA" title="NMDA">NMDA</a> (N-methyl-D-aspartate) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kainate" title="Kainate">kainate</a> subtypes of ionotropic <a href="/wiki/Glutamate_receptor" title="Glutamate receptor">glutamate receptors</a> follows. It is the activation of these receptors that potentiates the pain signal up the spinal cord. This idea, although true, is an oversimplification of pain transduction. A litany of other neurotransmitter and neuromodulators, such as <a href="/wiki/Calcitonin_gene-related_peptide" title="Calcitonin gene-related peptide">calcitonin gene-related peptide</a> (CGRP), <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">adenosine triphosphate</a> (ATP), <a href="/wiki/Brain-derived_neurotrophic_factor" title="Brain-derived neurotrophic factor">brain-derived neurotrophic factor</a> (BDNF), <a href="/wiki/Somatostatin" title="Somatostatin">somatostatin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vasoactive_intestinal_peptide" title="Vasoactive intestinal peptide">vasoactive intestinal peptide</a> (VIP), <a href="/wiki/Galanin" title="Galanin">galanin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vasopressin" title="Vasopressin">vasopressin</a> are all synthesized and released in response to <a href="/wiki/Noxious_stimulus" title="Noxious stimulus">noxious stimuli</a>. In addition to each of these regulatory factors, several other interactions between pain-transmitting neurons and other neurons in the dorsal horn have added impact on pain pathways.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Two_states_of_persistent_pain">Two states of persistent pain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrocyte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Two states of persistent pain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>After persistent peripheral tissue damage there is a release of several factors from the injured tissue as well as in the spinal dorsal horn. These factors increase the responsiveness of the dorsal horn pain-projection neurons to ensuing stimuli, termed "spinal sensitization," thus amplifying the pain impulse to the brain. Release of glutamate, substance P, and calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP) mediates NMDAR activation (originally silent because it is plugged by Mg2+), thus aiding in depolarization of the postsynaptic pain-transmitting neurons (PTN). In addition, activation of IP3 signaling and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MAPK" title="MAPK">MAPKs</a> (mitogen-activated protein kinases) such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extracellular_signal-regulated_kinases" title="Extracellular signal-regulated kinases">ERK</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/JNK" title="JNK">JNK</a>, bring about an increase in the synthesis of inflammatory factors that alter glutamate transporter function. ERK also further activates AMPARs and NMDARs in neurons. <a href="/wiki/Nociception" title="Nociception">Nociception</a> is further sensitized by the association of ATP and substance P with their respective receptors (P<sub>2</sub>X<sub>3</sub>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurokinin_1_receptor" title="Neurokinin 1 receptor">neurokinin 1 receptor</a> (NK1R), as well as activation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metabotropic_glutamate_receptors" title="Metabotropic glutamate receptors">metabotropic glutamate receptors</a> and release of BDNF. Persistent presence of glutamate in the synapse eventually results in dysregulation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GLT1" title="GLT1">GLT1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glutamate_aspartate_transporter" title="Glutamate aspartate transporter">GLAST</a>, crucial transporters of glutamate into astrocytes. Ongoing excitation can also induce ERK and JNK activation, resulting in release of several inflammatory factors.</p>
<p>As noxious pain is sustained, spinal sensitization creates <a href="/wiki/Transcriptional_regulation" title="Transcriptional regulation">transcriptional changes</a> in the neurons of the dorsal horn that lead to altered function for extended periods. Mobilization of Ca<sup>2+</sup> from internal stores results from persistent synaptic activity and leads to the release of glutamate, ATP, tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α), interleukin 1β (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interleukin_1_family" title="Interleukin 1 family">IL-1β</a>), IL-6, nitric oxide (NO), and prostaglandin E2 (PGE2). Activated astrocytes are also a source of <a href="/wiki/Matrix_metalloproteinase" title="Matrix metalloproteinase">matrix metalloproteinase</a> 2 (<a href="/wiki/MMP2" title="MMP2">MMP2</a>), which induces <a href="/wiki/Pyroptosis" title="Pyroptosis">pro-IL-1β</a> cleavage and sustains astrocyte activation. In this chronic signaling pathway, p38 is activated as a result of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interleukin_1_family" title="Interleukin 1 family">IL-1β</a> signaling, and there is a presence of chemokines that trigger their receptors to become active. In response to nerve damage, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_shock_proteins" title="Heat shock proteins">heat shock proteins</a> (HSP) are released and can bind to their respective <a href="/wiki/Toll-like_receptor" title="Toll-like receptor">TLRs</a>, leading to further activation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_pathologies">Other pathologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrocyte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other pathologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Neuroimmune_system" title="Neuroimmune system">Neuroimmune system</a></div>
<p>Other clinically significant pathologies involving astrocytes include <a href="/wiki/Astrogliosis" title="Astrogliosis">astrogliosis</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrocytopathy" title="Astrocytopathy">astrocytopathy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_studies">Recent studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrocyte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Recent studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A recent study, done in November 2010 and published March 2011, was done by a team of scientists from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Rochester" title="University of Rochester">University of Rochester</a> and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Colorado_School_of_Medicine" title="University of Colorado School of Medicine">University of Colorado School of Medicine</a>. They did an experiment to attempt to repair <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trauma_(medicine)" title="Trauma (medicine)">trauma</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Nervous_System" title="Central Nervous System">Central Nervous System</a> of an adult rat by replacing the glial cells. When the glial cells were injected into the injury of the adult rat’s spinal cord, astrocytes were generated by exposing human glial precursor cells to bone morphogenetic protein (Bone morphogenetic protein is important because it is considered to create <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissue</a> architecture throughout the body). So, with the bone protein and human glial cells combined, they promoted significant recovery of conscious foot placement, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axonal" title="Axonal">axonal</a> growth, and obvious increases in neuronal survival in the spinal cord <a href="/wiki/Basal_lamina" title="Basal lamina">laminae</a>. On the other hand, human glial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precursor_cells" title="Precursor cells">precursor cells</a> and astrocytes generated from these cells by being in contact with <a href="/wiki/Cilium" title="Cilium">ciliary</a> neurotrophic factors, failed to promote neuronal survival and support of axonal growth at the spot of the injury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref34_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref34-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>One study done in <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a> had two types of <a href="/wiki/Hippocampus" title="Hippocampus">hippocampal</a> neuronal cultures: In one culture, the neuron was grown from a layer of astrocytes and the other culture was not in contact with any astrocytes, but they were instead fed a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Glial_conditioned_medium&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Glial conditioned medium (page does not exist)">glial conditioned medium</a> (GCM), which inhibits the rapid growth of cultured astrocytes in the brains of rats in most cases. In their results they were able to see that astrocytes had a direct role in <a href="/wiki/Long-term_potentiation" title="Long-term potentiation">Long-term potentiation</a> with the mixed culture (which is the culture that was grown from a layer of astrocytes) but not in GCM cultures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref36_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref36-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recent studies have shown that astrocytes play an important function in the regulation of neural <a href="/wiki/Stem_cell" title="Stem cell">stem cells</a>. Research from the Schepens Eye Research Institute at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvard" title="Harvard">Harvard</a> shows the human brain to abound in neural stem cells, which are kept in a dormant state by <a href="/wiki/Neurotransmitter" title="Neurotransmitter">chemical signals</a> (ephrin-A2 and ephrin-A3) from the astrocytes. The astrocytes are able to activate the stem cells to transform into working neurons by dampening the release of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ephrin-A2" title="Ephrin-A2">ephrin-A2</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ephrin-A3" title="Ephrin-A3">ephrin-A3</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In a study published in a 2011 issue of <i>Nature Biotechnology</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> a group of researchers from the University of Wisconsin reports that it has been able to direct embryonic and induced human <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stem_cells" title="Stem cells">stem cells</a> to become astrocytes.</p>
<p>A 2012 study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> of the effects of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marijuana" title="Marijuana">marijuana</a> on short term memories found that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/THC" title="THC">THC</a> activates <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cannabinoid_receptor_1" title="Cannabinoid receptor 1">CB1</a> receptors of astrocytes which cause receptors for <a href="/wiki/AMPA" title="AMPA">AMPA</a> to be removed from the membranes of associated neurons.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrocyte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are several different ways to classify astrocytes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lineage_and_antigenic_phenotype">Lineage and antigenic phenotype</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrocyte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Lineage and antigenic phenotype">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These have been established by classic work by Raff et al. in early 1980s on Rat optic nerves.</p>
<ul>
<li>Type 1: Antigenically Ran2<sup>+</sup>, GFAP<sup>+</sup>, FGFR3<sup>+</sup>, A2B5<sup>−</sup>, thus resembling the "type 1 astrocyte" of the postnatal day 7 rat optic nerve. These can arise from the tripotential glial restricted precursor cells (GRP), but not from the bipotential O2A/OPC (oligodendrocyte, type 2 astrocyte precursor, also called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oligodendrocyte_precursor_cell" title="Oligodendrocyte precursor cell">Oligodendrocyte progenitor cell</a></i>) cells.</li>
<li>Type 2: Antigenically A2B5<sup>+</sup>, GFAP<sup>+</sup>, FGFR3<sup>−</sup>, Ran 2<sup>−</sup>. These cells can develop <i>in vitro</i> from the either tripotential GRP (probably via O2A stage) or from bipotential O2A cells (which some people<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (March 2009)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> think may in turn have been derived from the GRP) <b>or</b> in vivo when these progenitor cells are transplanted into lesion sites (but <i>probably not in normal development, at least not in the rat optic nerve</i>). Type-2 astrocytes are the major astrocytic component in postnatal optic nerve cultures that are generated by O2A cells grown in the presence of fetal calf serum but are not thought to exist <i>in vivo</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anatomical_classification">Anatomical classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrocyte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Anatomical classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Protoplasmic: found in <a href="/wiki/Grey_matter" title="Grey matter">grey matter</a> and have many branching processes whose end-feet envelop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synapses" title="Synapses">synapses</a>. Some protoplasmic astrocytes are generated by multipotent <a href="/wiki/Subventricular_zone" title="Subventricular zone">subventricular zone</a> progenitor cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid8439409_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid8439409-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid7472478_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid7472478-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li>Gömöri-positive astrocytes. These are a subset of protoplasmic astrocytes that contain numerous cytoplasmic inclusions, or granules, that stain positively with <a href="/wiki/Gy%C3%B6rgy_G%C3%B6m%C3%B6ri" title="György Gömöri">Gömöri</a>'s chrome-alum hematoxylin stain. It is now known that these granules are formed from the remnants of degenerating mitochondria engulfed within lysosomes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Some type of oxidative stress appears to be responsible for the mitochondrial damage within these specialized astrocytes. Gömöri-positive astrocytes are much more abundant within the <a href="/wiki/Arcuate_nucleus" title="Arcuate nucleus">arcuate nucleus</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Hypothalamus" title="Hypothalamus">hypothalamus</a> and in the hippocampus than in other brain regions. They may have a role in regulating the response of the hypothalamus to glucose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Fibrous: found in <a href="/wiki/White_matter" title="White matter">white matter</a> and have long thin unbranched processes whose end-feet envelop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nodes_of_Ranvier" title="Nodes of Ranvier">nodes of Ranvier</a>. Some fibrous astrocytes are generated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radial_glia" title="Radial glia">radial glia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid77708_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid77708-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid111580_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid111580-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid3069243_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid3069243-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid2808761_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid2808761-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid8844514_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid8844514-54">[54]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transporter.2Freceptor_classification">Transporter/receptor classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrocyte&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Transporter/receptor classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>GluT type: these express <a href="/wiki/Glutamate_transporter" title="Glutamate transporter">glutamate transporters</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EAAT1" title="EAAT1">EAAT1</a>/<i><a class="external text" href="http://www.genenames.org/data/hgnc_data.php?match=SLC1A3" rel="nofollow">SLC1A3</a></i> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EAAT2" title="EAAT2">EAAT2</a>/<i><a class="external text" href="http://www.genenames.org/data/hgnc_data.php?match=SLC1A2" rel="nofollow">SLC1A2</a></i>) and respond to synaptic release of glutamate by transporter currents. The function and availability of EAAT2 is modulated by <a href="/wiki/TAAR1" title="TAAR1">TAAR1</a>, an intracellular receptor in human astrocytes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Astrocyte-localized_TAAR1_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Astrocyte-localized_TAAR1-55">[55]</a></sup></li>
<li>GluR type: these express <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glutamate_receptors" title="Glutamate receptors">glutamate receptors</a> (mostly <a href="/wiki/Metabotropic_glutamate_receptor" title="Metabotropic glutamate receptor">mGluR</a> and <a href="/wiki/AMPA_receptor" title="AMPA receptor">AMPA</a> type) and respond to synaptic release of glutamate by channel-mediated currents and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inositol_triphosphate" title="Inositol triphosphate">IP3</a>-dependent Ca<sup>2+</sup> transients.</li>
</ul>
