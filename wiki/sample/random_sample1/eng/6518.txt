<p><i><b>Kirby's Dream Land</b></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[a]</a></sup> is an <a href="/wiki/Action_game" title="Action game">action</a>-<a href="/wiki/Platform_game" title="Platform game">platform</a> video game developed by <a href="/wiki/HAL_Laboratory" title="HAL Laboratory">HAL Laboratory</a> and published by <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy" title="Game Boy">Game Boy</a>, which released in 1992. It is both the first video game in the <a href="/wiki/Kirby_(series)" title="Kirby (series)"><i>Kirby</i> series</a> and the debut of <a href="/wiki/Kirby_(character)" title="Kirby (character)">Kirby</a> himself. Being the inaugural <i>Kirby</i> title, it created many conventions that would appear in later games in the series, including Kirby's basic moves. However, Kirby's trademark "copy" ability would not appear until <i><a href="/wiki/Kirby%27s_Adventure" title="Kirby's Adventure">Kirby's Adventure</a></i>, released less than one year later.</p>
<p><i>Kirby's Dream Land</i> was designed by <a href="/wiki/Masahiro_Sakurai" title="Masahiro Sakurai">Masahiro Sakurai</a>, who intended it to be a simple game that could be easy to pick up and play even by those unfamiliar with action games. He offered additional optional challenges for more advanced gamers such as an unlockable hard mode and the ability to edit Kirby's maximum HP and starting number of lives. <i>Kirby's Dream Land</i> was re-released on the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_3DS" title="Nintendo 3DS">Nintendo 3DS</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Virtual_Console" title="Virtual Console">Virtual Console</a> in 2011 and was also one of the games included in the compilation game <i><a href="/wiki/Kirby%27s_Dream_Collection" title="Kirby's Dream Collection">Kirby's Dream Collection</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/Wii" title="Wii">Wii</a>, released to celebrate Kirby's 20th anniversary.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gameplay">Gameplay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kirby%27s_Dream_Land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gameplay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kirbys_Dream_Land_Whispy.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="144" data-file-width="160" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/4/41/Kirbys_Dream_Land_Whispy.png" width="160"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kirbys_Dream_Land_Whispy.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Kirby battling the first stage's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boss_(video_games)" title="Boss (video games)">boss</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whispy_Woods" title="Whispy Woods">Whispy Woods</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The gluttonous <a href="/wiki/King_Dedede" title="King Dedede">King Dedede</a> has stolen all of the food from the inhabitants of Dream Land for a midnight feast, so Kirby, a resident of Dream Land, goes to retrieve the food and stop Dedede. <i>Kirby's Dream Land</i> plays like other platformers of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_video_game_consoles_(third_generation)" title="History of video game consoles (third generation)">8-bit</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_video_game_consoles_(fourth_generation)" title="History of video game consoles (fourth generation)">16-bit era of video games</a>: Kirby must use various natural abilities and occasionally items or <a href="/wiki/Power-up" title="Power-up">power-ups</a> while heading toward the goal at the end of each level where a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boss_(video_games)" title="Boss (video games)">boss</a> is fought. All levels are played on a <a href="/wiki/Dimension#Spatial_dimensions" title="Dimension">two-dimensional</a> plane, letting Kirby move only left, right, up, and down. Kirby can walk, jump, and fly. Kirby can also inhale objects and enemies, swallowing them or spitting them out as projectiles. However, in contrast to later games in the series, enemies' powers cannot be copied.</p>
<p>Levels are made up of a series of large "rooms" rather than the single long corridors typical of platformers at the time and the doors between these rooms act as checkpoints returning the player to the beginning of a "room" when they die rather than starting the level over. Like many 1980s-era platformers, the player can accumulate points, with an <a href="/wiki/1-up" title="1-up">extra life</a> granted when the player has enough points. However, because Kirby lacks a save function, scores are not recorded. Also, there are no save files, so the player has to start over again when the Game Boy is turned off, if the player chooses to return to the title screen after a Game Over, or if the player resets the game. Once the game has been completed, a code is offered to play an optional Extra Game, in which the difficulty is significantly increased. Completing the extra game offered a second code that lets the player adjust lives and vitality settings to play an even easier or harder game, and listen to music and sound effects freely. Since there is no save function, these codes are allowed to be entered even on a new session where the game has not been beaten yet.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kirby%27s_Dream_Land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Popopo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="186" data-file-width="300" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f9/Popopo.jpg/220px-Popopo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/f/f9/Popopo.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Popopo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An early magazine scan, still referring to Kirby as Popopo.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Kirby's Dream Land</i> was developed by <a href="/wiki/Masahiro_Sakurai" title="Masahiro Sakurai">Masahiro Sakurai</a> of <a href="/wiki/HAL_Laboratory" title="HAL Laboratory">HAL Laboratory</a>. Kirby was a dummy character that the developers used until they could define a more sophisticated image. The designers grew to like Kirby so much that they decided to keep him instead of using a more advanced character. He was originally named Popopo <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">ポポポ</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, and <i>Kirby's Dream Land</i> was titled <i>Twinkle Popo</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">ティンクル・ポポ</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Tinkuru Popo</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>. Eventually, he was renamed Kirby and the game was renamed <i>Kirby of the Stars</i>. During the development of what was then referred to as <i>Twinkle Popopo</i>, Popopo/Kirby's color had not been decided upon. <a href="/wiki/Masahiro_Sakurai" title="Masahiro Sakurai">Masahiro Sakurai</a> wanted him to be pink, while <a href="/wiki/Shigeru_Miyamoto" title="Shigeru Miyamoto">Shigeru Miyamoto</a> felt that he should be yellow. Eventually, Nintendo decided to make him pink. Although pink is still Kirby's main color, later games have shown Kirby in a wide variety of colors, including yellow. When <i>Kirby of the Stars</i> was first released in Japan, it featured Kirby as being pink. However, Nintendo of America designed the North American box art and advertisements with a white Kirby based on the game's grey-scale visuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespyclassic_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamespyclassic-2">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The music was composed by Jun Ishikawa. He was the only composer for this game,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mobygames_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-mobygames-3">[2]</a></sup> and has been a regular composer in the <i>Kirby</i> series. His most recent work was on <i><a href="/wiki/Kirby:_Triple_Deluxe" title="Kirby: Triple Deluxe">Kirby: Triple Deluxe</a></i> in 2014. Certain songs were also reused in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super_Smash_Bros._(series)" title="Super Smash Bros. (series)"><i>Super Smash Bros.</i> series</a> as the background music of the Dream Land stage in <i><a href="/wiki/Super_Smash_Bros." title="Super Smash Bros.">Super Smash Bros.</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Save_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Save-4">[3]</a></sup> and the Green Greens stage in <i><a href="/wiki/Super_Smash_Bros._Melee" title="Super Smash Bros. Melee">Super Smash Bros. Melee</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phdreams_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-phdreams-5">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p><i>Kirby's Dream Land</i> has received generally positive reception. It holds an aggregate score of 62.22% on <a href="/wiki/GameRankings" title="GameRankings">GameRankings</a> with nine reviews, making it the 17th best Game Boy game and the 12,566th best video game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> By May 28, 1995 <i>Kirby's Dream Land</i> had sold 1.3 million copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> To date, it has sold in excess of 5 million copies worldwide; <a href="/wiki/Gamasutra" title="Gamasutra">Gamasutra</a> cited its new style of gameplay for its success. At the time, it was <a href="/wiki/HAL_Laboratory" title="HAL Laboratory">HAL Laboratory</a>'s most successful game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra3_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra3-9">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gamasutra" title="Gamasutra">Gamasutra</a>'s Osamu Inoue attributed the game's success to <a href="/wiki/Satoru_Iwata" title="Satoru Iwata">Satoru Iwata</a>, formerly an employee of <a href="/wiki/HAL_Laboratory" title="HAL Laboratory">HAL Laboratory</a>, who Inoue comments has a "simple-minded passion for creating games".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra3_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra3-9">[8]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Game_Informer" title="Game Informer">Game Informer</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Ben Reeves called it the 14th best Game Boy game and felt that it was a relaxing game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> Author Wendy Despain used <i>Kirby's Dream Land</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s plot as an example of how early platform games' plots progressed, which she collectively described as "the main character needing to reach a villain to put right a wrong."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (March 2015)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Humongous Life's Jonathan Wahlgren called it a strong game, but felt it to be "too elementary".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GamesRadar" title="GamesRadar">GamesRadar</a>'s Brett Elston did an article on its music, specifically giving praise to the final boss music, stating that it is the "only song in the original Kirby that had a sense of challenge or conflict instead of skipping through a field of floating cakes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> Nadia Oxford of <a href="/wiki/1UP.com" title="1UP.com">1UP.com</a> praised it for its unique platforming mechanics, describing it as the start of a "gluttonous legend".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allgame" title="Allgame">Allgame</a>'s Joshua Crystal called it a "great game for beginner players and ones that enjoy a fun, but short, experience.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an article detailing various <i>Kirby</i> series video games, <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> stated that it was a decent platformer, but also very basic compared to later titles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirbycompared_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-kirbycompared-16">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a>'s Lucas M. Thomas and Craig Harris included <i>Kirby's Dream Land</i> in their wishlist for a hypothetical "<a href="/wiki/Virtual_Console" title="Virtual Console">Virtual Console</a>" for the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_3DS" title="Nintendo 3DS">Nintendo 3DS</a>, commenting that its inclusion would be based on nostalgia rather than it feeling "new and sensational". They also added that original characters like <a href="/wiki/Kirby_(character)" title="Kirby (character)">Kirby</a> were the stars of the <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy" title="Game Boy">Game Boy</a> rather than established characters such as <a href="/wiki/Link_(The_Legend_of_Zelda)" title="Link (The Legend of Zelda)">Link</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mario" title="Mario">Mario</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samus_Aran" title="Samus Aran">Samus Aran</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pit_(Kid_Icarus)" title="Pit (Kid Icarus)">Pit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> They would again praise <i>Kirby's Dream Land</i> for being an original game in their "History of the Game Boy" article, adding that while "attitude" was common in new platforming mascots, Kirby was cheery and adorable, while the game featured "breezy, casual gameplay and lighthearted atmosphere".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/GameSpy" title="GameSpy">GameSpy</a>'s Gerald Villoria, Brian Altano, and Ryan Scott called it "basic" compared to later titles in the series, adding that it lacked a sense of danger because Kirby could fly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Power" title="Nintendo Power">Nintendo Power</a></i> editors George and Bob shared roughly similar opinions of the game; George stated that it is a really fun game, owing its quality to its excellent play control and well thought out concept, while Bob stated that it is deceptively simple looking, when it in fact features a decent challenge for more experienced gamers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirbynp_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-kirbynp-20">[19]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GamesRadar" title="GamesRadar">GamesRadar</a> listed <i>Kirby's Dream Land</i> and its sequel as two of the titles they want in the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_3DS" title="Nintendo 3DS">3DS</a> <a href="/wiki/Virtual_Console" title="Virtual Console">Virtual Console</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kirby%27s_Dream_Land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Kirby_media" title="List of Kirby media">List of Kirby media</a></div>
<p><i>Kirby's Dream Land</i> has since spawned dozens of sequels across more than a half-dozen video game consoles. The first direct sequel <i><a href="/wiki/Kirby%27s_Adventure" title="Kirby's Adventure">Kirby's Adventure</a></i>, was released on the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Entertainment_System" title="Nintendo Entertainment System">Nintendo Entertainment System</a>. It introduced the ability to steal powers from enemies, an ability which would become a staple of the series following it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> The series has featured several spin-offs, in differing genres including a racing game <i><a href="/wiki/Kirby_Air_Ride" title="Kirby Air Ride">Kirby Air Ride</a></i> and pinball game <i><a href="/wiki/Kirby%27s_Pinball_Land" title="Kirby's Pinball Land">Kirby's Pinball Land</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> Two named sequels to <i>Kirby's Dream Land</i> would be released: <i><a href="/wiki/Kirby%27s_Dream_Land_2" title="Kirby's Dream Land 2">Kirby's Dream Land 2</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Kirby%27s_Dream_Land_3" title="Kirby's Dream Land 3">Kirby's Dream Land 3</a></i>, however these aren't direct sequels and have more in common with each other than with the first <i>Dream Land</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although <i>Kirby's Dream Land</i> has never received a stand-alone remake the <a href="/wiki/Super_Nintendo_Entertainment_System" title="Super Nintendo Entertainment System">Super NES</a> game <i><a href="/wiki/Kirby_Super_Star" title="Kirby Super Star">Kirby Super Star</a></i> contains "Spring Breeze", a shortened remake of <i>Kirby's Dream Land</i>, missing the Castle Lololo level and Kaboola boss fight but adding Kirby's copy ability from later titles. <i>Kirby Super Star Ultra</i> features "Revenge of The King", a harder version of "Spring Breeze" that takes heavy inspiration from <i>Kirby's Dream Land's</i> extra mode. Kirby and King Dedede appear as playable characters in the <a href="/wiki/Super_Smash_Bros." title="Super Smash Bros.">Super Smash Bros.</a> series and the stages "Dream Land" and "Green Greens" take elements from the game's first level "Green Greens".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup></p>
