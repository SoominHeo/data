<p>The <b>Terracotta Army</b> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">兵马俑</span>; literally: "Soldier-and-horse funerary statues") is a collection of <a href="/wiki/Terracotta" title="Terracotta">terracotta</a> sculptures depicting the armies of <a href="/wiki/Qin_Shi_Huang" title="Qin Shi Huang">Qin Shi Huang</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_China" title="Emperor of China">Emperor of China</a>. It is a form of <a href="/wiki/Funerary_art" title="Funerary art">funerary art</a> buried with the emperor in 210–209 BCE and whose purpose was to protect the emperor in his afterlife.</p>
<p>The figures, dating from approximately the late third century BCE,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> were discovered in 1974 by local farmers in <a href="/wiki/Lintong_District" title="Lintong District">Lintong District</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xi%27an" title="Xi'an">Xi'an</a>, Shaanxi province. The figures vary in height according to their roles, with the tallest being the generals. The figures include warriors, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chariot_(Ancient_China)" title="Chariot (Ancient China)">chariots</a> and horses. Estimates from 2007 were that the three pits containing the Terracotta Army held more than 8,000 soldiers, 130 chariots with 520 horses and 150 cavalry horses, the majority of which remained buried in the pits nearby <a href="/wiki/Mausoleum_of_the_First_Qin_Emperor" title="Mausoleum of the First Qin Emperor">Qin Shi Huang's mausoleum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPortal2007167_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPortal2007167-2">[2]</a></sup> Other terracotta non-military figures were found in other pits, including officials, acrobats, strongmen and musicians.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tomb_of_Emperor_Qin_Shi_Huang.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1855" data-file-width="2512" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Tomb_of_Emperor_Qin_Shi_Huang.jpg/220px-Tomb_of_Emperor_Qin_Shi_Huang.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Tomb_of_Emperor_Qin_Shi_Huang.jpg/330px-Tomb_of_Emperor_Qin_Shi_Huang.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Tomb_of_Emperor_Qin_Shi_Huang.jpg/440px-Tomb_of_Emperor_Qin_Shi_Huang.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tomb_of_Emperor_Qin_Shi_Huang.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The mound where the tomb is located</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The construction of the tomb was described by historian <a href="/wiki/Sima_Qian" title="Sima Qian">Sima Qian</a> (145–90 BCE) in his most noted work <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">Shiji</a></i>, written a century after the mausoleum's completion. Work on the mausoleum began in 246 BCE soon after Emperor Qin (then aged 13) ascended the throne, and the project eventually involved 700,000 workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chinesetext_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-chinesetext-3">[3]</a></sup> Geographer <a href="/wiki/Li_Daoyuan" title="Li Daoyuan">Li Daoyuan</a>, writing six centuries after the First Emperor's death, recorded in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shui_Jing_Zhu" title="Shui Jing Zhu">Shui Jing Zhu</a></i> that Mount Li was a favoured location due to its auspicious geology, "famed for its jade mines, its northern side was rich in gold, and its southern side rich in beautiful jade; the First Emperor, covetous of its fine reputation, therefore chose to be buried there".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEClements2007158_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEClements2007158-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Sima Qian wrote that the First Emperor was buried with palaces, towers, officials, valuable artifacts and wondrous objects. According to this account, 100 flowing rivers were simulated using mercury, and above them the ceiling was decorated with heavenly bodies below which were the features of the land. Some translations of this passage refer to "models" or "imitations;" however, those words were not used in the original text, which makes no mention of the terracotta army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chinesetext_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-chinesetext-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPortal200717_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPortal200717-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>High levels of mercury were found in the soil of the tomb mound, giving credence to Sima Qian's account.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPortal2007202_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPortal2007202-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later historical accounts suggested that the tomb had been looted by <a href="/wiki/Xiang_Yu" title="Xiang Yu">Xiang Yu</a>, a contender for the throne after the death of the first emperor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> However, there are indications that the tomb may not have been plundered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span></h3>
<p><span class="nowrap">The Terracotta Army</span> was discovered on 29 March 1974<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Agnew2010_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Agnew2010-12">[12]</a></sup> to the east of <a href="/wiki/Xi%27an" title="Xi'an">Xi'an</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shaanxi_province" title="Shaanxi province">Shaanxi province</a> by farmers digging a water well approximately 1.6 kilometres (0.99 mi) east of the Qin Emperor's tomb mound at <a href="/wiki/Mount_Li" title="Mount Li">Mount Li</a> (Lishan),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> a region riddled with underground springs and watercourses. For centuries, occasional reports mentioned pieces of terracotta figures and fragments of the Qin <a href="/wiki/Necropolis" title="Necropolis">necropolis</a> – roofing tiles, bricks and chunks of masonry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEClements2007155.2C_157.2C_158.2C_160.E2.80.93161.2C_166_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEClements2007155.2C_157.2C_158.2C_160.E2.80.93161.2C_166-15">[15]</a></sup> This discovery prompted Chinese archaeologists to investigate, revealing the largest pottery figurine group ever found in China.</p>
<p>A museum complex has since been constructed over the area, with the largest pit enclosed within with a large structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Necropolis">Necropolis</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Terracotta_Army-China2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Terracotta_Army-China2.jpg/220px-Terracotta_Army-China2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Terracotta_Army-China2.jpg/330px-Terracotta_Army-China2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Terracotta_Army-China2.jpg/440px-Terracotta_Army-China2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Terracotta_Army-China2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
View of the Terracotta Army</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Terracotta Army is part of a much larger <a href="/wiki/Necropolis" title="Necropolis">necropolis</a>. Ground-penetrating radar and core sampling have measured the area to be approximately 38 square miles (98 square kilometers).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The necropolis was constructed as a microcosm of the emperor's imperial palace or compound, and covers a large area around the tomb mound of the first emperor. The earthen tomb mound is located at the foot of Mount Li and built in a <a href="/wiki/Chinese_pyramids" title="Chinese pyramids">pyramidal</a> shape,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and is surrounded by two solidly built <a href="/wiki/Rammed_earth" title="Rammed earth">rammed earth</a> walls with gateway entrances. The necropolis consists of several offices, halls, stables, other structures as well as an imperial park placed around the tomb mound.</p>
<p>The warriors stand guard to the east of the tomb. Up to 5 metres (16 ft) of reddish, sandy soil had accumulated over the site in the two millennia following its construction, but archaeologists found evidence of earlier disturbances at the site. During the excavations near the Mount Li burial mound, archaeologists found several graves dating from the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, where diggers had apparently struck terracotta fragments. These were discarded as worthless and used along with soil to back fill the excavations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEClements2007160_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEClements2007160-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tomb">Tomb</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mausoleum_of_the_First_Qin_Emperor" title="Mausoleum of the First Qin Emperor">Mausoleum of the First Qin Emperor</a></div>
<p>The tomb appears to be a hermetically-sealed space the size of a football pitch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-channel4_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-channel4-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The tomb remains unopened, possibly due to concerns over preservation of its artifacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-channel4_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-channel4-20">[20]</a></sup> For example, after the excavation of the Terracotta Army, the painted surface present on some terracotta figures began to flake and fade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The lacquer covering the paint can curl in fifteen seconds once exposed to Xi'an's dry air and can flake off in just four minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pits">Pits</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Terracotta_Army_Pit_1_-_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2336" data-file-width="3504" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Terracotta_Army_Pit_1_-_2.jpg/220px-Terracotta_Army_Pit_1_-_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Terracotta_Army_Pit_1_-_2.jpg/330px-Terracotta_Army_Pit_1_-_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Terracotta_Army_Pit_1_-_2.jpg/440px-Terracotta_Army_Pit_1_-_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Terracotta_Army_Pit_1_-_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
View of Pit 1, the largest excavation pit of the Terracotta Army</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Four main pits approximately 7 metres (23 ft) deep have been excavated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lecture_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-lecture-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lothar_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-lothar-25">[25]</a></sup> These are located approximately 1.5 kilometres (0.93 mi) east of the burial mound. The soldiers within were laid out as if to protect the tomb from the east, where all the Qin Emperor's conquered states lay.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pit_one">Pit one</span></h4>
<p>Pit one, which is 230 metres (750 ft) long and 62 metres (203 ft) wide,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lothar_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lothar-26">[26]</a></sup> contains the main army of more than 6,000 figures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Pit one has 11 corridors, most of which are more than 3 metres (9.8 ft) wide and paved with small bricks with a wooden ceiling supported by large beams and posts. This design was also used for the tombs of nobles and would have resembled palace hallways when built. The wooden ceilings were covered with reed mats and layers of clay for waterproofing, and then mounded with more soil raising them about 2 to 3 metres (6 ft 7 in to 9 ft 10 in) above the surrounding ground level when completed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPortal2007_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPortal2007-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span></h4>
<p>Pit two has cavalry and infantry units as well as <a href="/wiki/Chariot_(China)" title="Chariot (China)">war chariots</a> and is thought to represent a military guard. Pit three is the command post, with high-ranking officers and a war chariot. Pit four is empty, perhaps left unfinished by its builders.</p>
<p>Some of the figures in pit one and two show fire damage, while remains of burnt ceiling rafters have also been found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> These, together with the missing weapons, have been taken as evidence of the reported looting by Xiang Yu and the subsequent burning of the site, which is thought to have caused the roof to collapse and crush the army figures below. The terracotta figures currently on display have been restored from the fragments.</p>
<p>Other pits that formed the necropolis also have been excavated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> These pits lie within and outside the walls surrounding the tomb mound. They variously contain bronze carriages, terracotta figures of entertainers such as acrobats and strongmen, officials, stone armour suits, burials sites of horses, rare animals and labourers, as well as bronze cranes and ducks set in an underground park.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Warrior_figures">Warrior figures</span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_and_appearance">Types and appearance</span></h3>
<p>The terracotta figures are life-sized. They vary in height, uniform, and hairstyle in accordance with rank. The faces appear different for each individual figure, scholars however have identified 10 basic face shapes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-national_geographic_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-national_geographic-32">[32]</a></sup> The figures are of these general types: armored warriors; unarmored <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infantrymen" title="Infantrymen">infantrymen</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cavalrymen" title="Cavalrymen">cavalrymen</a> who wear a pillbox hat; helmeted driver of chariots with more armor protection; spear-carrying charioteers; kneeling archers who are armored; standing archers who are not; as well as generals and other lower-ranking officers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> There are however many variations in the uniforms within the ranks, for example, some may wear shin pads while others not; they may wear either long or short trousers, some of which may be padded; and their body armors vary depending on rank, function, and position in formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> There are also terracotta horses placed among the warrior figures.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Recreated_colored_terracotta_warriors.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3056" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Recreated_colored_terracotta_warriors.jpg/220px-Recreated_colored_terracotta_warriors.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Recreated_colored_terracotta_warriors.jpg/330px-Recreated_colored_terracotta_warriors.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Recreated_colored_terracotta_warriors.jpg/440px-Recreated_colored_terracotta_warriors.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Recreated_colored_terracotta_warriors.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Recreated figures of an archer and an officer, showing how they would have looked when painted</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Originally, the figures were painted with bright pigments, variously coloured pink, red, green, blue, black, brown, white and lilac.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The coloured lacquer finish and individual facial features would have given the figures a realistic feel. However, much of the colour coating had flaked off or become greatly faded.</p>
<p>Some scholars have speculated a possible <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic" title="Hellenistic">Hellenistic</a> link to these sculptures, due to the lack of life-sized and realistic sculptures prior to the Qin dynasty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> They argued that potential Greek influence is particularly evident in some terracotta figures such as those of acrobats, as well as the technique used for casting bronze sculptures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span></h3>
<p>The terracotta army figures were manufactured in workshops by government laborers and local craftsmen using local materials. Heads, arms, legs, and torsos were created separately and then assembled by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luting" title="Luting">luting</a> the pieces together. When completed, the terracotta figures were placed in the pits in precise military formation according to rank and duty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>The faces were created using <a href="/wiki/Molding_(process)" title="Molding (process)">molds</a>, and at least ten face molds may have been used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-national_geographic_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-national_geographic-32">[32]</a></sup> Clay was then added after assembly to provide individual facial features to make each figure appear different.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPortal2007170_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPortal2007170-42">[42]</a></sup> It is believed that the warriors' legs were made in much the same way that terracotta drainage pipes were manufactured at the time. This would classify the process as <a href="/wiki/Assembly_line" title="Assembly line">assembly line</a> production, with specific parts manufactured and assembled after being fired, as opposed to crafting one solid piece and subsequently firing it. In those times of tight imperial control, each workshop was required to inscribe its name on items produced to ensure quality control. This has aided modern historians in verifying which workshops were commandeered to make tiles and other mundane items for the terracotta army.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weaponry">Weaponry</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bronze_jian_of_the_Terracotta_Army.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1572" data-file-width="2994" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Bronze_jian_of_the_Terracotta_Army.jpg/260px-Bronze_jian_of_the_Terracotta_Army.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Bronze_jian_of_the_Terracotta_Army.jpg/390px-Bronze_jian_of_the_Terracotta_Army.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Bronze_jian_of_the_Terracotta_Army.jpg/520px-Bronze_jian_of_the_Terracotta_Army.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bronze_jian_of_the_Terracotta_Army.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bronze <i><a href="/wiki/Jian" title="Jian">jian</a></i> sword with chrome plating</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Most of the figures originally held real weapons such as spears, swords, or crossbows, and the use of actual weapons would have increased the figures' realism. Most of the original weapons, however, were looted shortly after the creation of the army, or have rotted away. Nevertheless, many weapons such as swords, spears, lances, battle-axes, scimitars, shields, crossbows, and arrowheads have been found in the pits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lecture_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-lecture-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Over 40,000 bronze items of weaponry have been recovered from the pits, with most of these arrowheads which are usually found in bundles of 100 units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Studies of these arrowheads suggests that they were produced in small units of self-sufficient, autonomous workshops that produced finished items in a production process referred to as cellular production or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toyotism" title="Toyotism">Toyotism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> There are also hundreds of crossbow triggers, and smaller number of other weapons such as bronze swords, and daggers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>

<p>Some of these weapons, such as the swords are sharp and were coated with a 10–15 micrometre layer of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromium_dioxide" title="Chromium dioxide">chromium dioxide</a> that kept the swords rust-free for 2,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The swords contain an alloy of copper, tin, and other elements including nickel, magnesium, and cobalt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Some carry inscriptions that date their manufacture to between 245 and 228 BCE, indicating that they were used as weapons before their burials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bm_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-bm-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>An important element of the army is the <a href="/wiki/Chariot" title="Chariot">chariot</a>, of which four types were found. In battle the fighting chariots formed pairs at the head of a unit of infantry. The principal weapon of the charioteers was the <i>ge</i> or <a href="/wiki/Dagger-axe" title="Dagger-axe">dagger-axe</a>, an L-shaped bronze blade mounted on a long shaft used for sweeping and hooking at the enemy. Infantrymen also carried <i>ge</i> on shorter shafts, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ji_(halberd)" title="Ji (halberd)">ji</a></i> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halberds" title="Halberds">halberds</a> and spears and lances. For close fighting and defence, both charioteers and infantrymen carried double-edged straight swords. The archers carried crossbows, with sophisticated trigger mechanisms, capable of shooting arrows farther than 800 metres (2,600 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bm_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-bm-50">[50]</a></sup></p>

<p>A collection of 120 objects from the mausoleum and 12 terracotta warriors were displayed at the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a> in London as its special the British Museum's most successful year and made the British Museum the United Kingdom's top cultural attraction between 2007 and 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The exhibition brought the most visitors to the museum since the <a that the 400,000 advance tickets sold out so fast that the museum extended its opening hours until midnight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greatexhibitions_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-greatexhibitions-54">[54]</a></sup> According to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i>, many people had to be turned away, despite the extended hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thetimes_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-thetimes-55">[55]</a></sup> During the day of events to mark the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_New_Year" title="Chinese New Year">Chinese New Year</a>, the crush was so intense that the gates to the museum had to be shut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thetimes_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-thetimes-55">[55]</a></sup> The Terracotta Army has been described as the only other set of historic artifacts (along with the remnants of wreck of the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">RMS <i>Titanic</i></a>) that can draw a crowd by the name alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greatexhibitions_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-greatexhibitions-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>Warriors and other artifacts were exhibited to the public at the Forum de Barcelona in <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a> between 9 May and 26 September 2004. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_research">Scientific research</span></h2>
<p>In 2007, scientists at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Light_Source" title="Advanced Light Source">Advanced Light Source</a> facility in Berkeley, California reported that <a href="/wiki/Powder_diffraction" title="Powder diffraction">powder diffraction</a> experiments combined with <a href="/wiki/Energy-dispersive_X-ray_spectroscopy" title="Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy">energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Micro-X-ray_fluorescence" title="Micro-X-ray fluorescence">micro-X-ray fluorescence</a> analysis showed that the process of producing Terracotta figures colored with <a href="/wiki/Han_purple_and_Han_blue" title="Han purple and Han blue">Chinese purple</a> dye consisting of <a href="/wiki/Barium" title="Barium">barium</a> <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicate</a> was derived from the knowledge gained by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taoist" title="Taoist">Taoist</a> <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemists</a> in their attempts to synthesize <a href="/wiki/Jade" title="Jade">jade</a> <a href="/wiki/Ornament_(art)" title="Ornament (art)">ornaments</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 2006, an international team of researchers at the <a href="/wiki/UCL_Institute_of_Archaeology" title="UCL Institute of Archaeology">UCL Institute of Archaeology</a> have been using <a href="/wiki/Analytical_chemistry" title="Analytical chemistry">analytical chemistry</a> techniques to uncover more details about the production techniques employed in the creation of the Terracotta Army. Using <a href="/wiki/X-ray_fluorescence" title="X-ray fluorescence">X-ray fluorescence</a> spectrometry of 40,000 bronze <a href="/wiki/Arrowhead" title="Arrowhead">arrowheads</a> bundled in groups of 100, the researchers reported that the arrowheads within a single bundle formed a relatively tight cluster that was different from other bundles. In addition, the presence or absence of metal impurities was consistent within bundles. Based on the arrows’ chemical compositions, the researchers concluded that a <a href="/wiki/Cellular_manufacturing" title="Cellular manufacturing">cellular manufacturing</a> system similar to the one used in a modern <a href="/wiki/Toyota" title="Toyota">Toyota</a> factory, as opposed to a continuous <a href="/wiki/Assembly_line" title="Assembly line">assembly line</a> in the early days of automobile industry, was employed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rsc_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-rsc-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>Grinding and polishing marks visible under a <a href="/wiki/Scanning_electron_microscope" title="Scanning electron microscope">scanning electron microscope</a> provide evidence for the earliest industrial use of <a href="/wiki/Lathe" title="Lathe">lathes</a> for polishing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rsc_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-rsc-70">[70]</a></sup></p>

<li id="cite_note-chinesetext-3"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-chinesetext_3-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-chinesetext_3-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text"><a class="external text" href="http://ctext.org/pre-qin-and-han?filter=d270" rel="nofollow">Sima Qian – Shiji Volume 6</a> 《史记·秦始皇本纪》 <b>Original text:</b> <span lang="zh-hans" xml:lang="zh-hans">始皇初即位，穿治郦山，及并天下，天下徒送诣七十余万人，穿三泉，下铜而致椁，宫观百官奇器珍怪徙臧满之。令匠作机驽矢，有所穿近者辄射之。以水银为百川江河大海，机相灌输，上具天文，下具地理。以人鱼膏为烛，度不灭者久之。二世曰："先帝后宫非有子者，出焉不宜。" 皆令从死，死者甚众。葬既已下，或言工匠为机，臧皆知之，臧重即泄。大事毕，已臧，闭中羡，下外羡门，尽闭工匠臧者，无复出者。树草木以象山。</span> <b>Translation:</b> When the First Emperor ascended the throne, the digging and preparation at Mount Li began. After he unified his empire, 700,000 men were sent there from all over his empire. They dug down deep to underground springs, pouring copper to place the outer casing of the coffin. Palaces and viewing towers housing a hundred officials were built and filled with treasures and rare artifacts. Workmen were instructed to make automatic crossbows primed to shoot at intruders. Mercury was used to simulate the hundred rivers, the Yangtze and Yellow River, and the great sea, and set to flow mechanically. Above, the heaven is depicted, below, the geographical features of the land. Candles were made of "mermaid"'s fat which is calculated to burn and not extinguish for a long time. The Second Emperor said: "It is inappropriate for the wives of the late emperor who have no sons to be free", ordered that they should accompany the dead, and a great many died. After the burial, it was suggested that it would be a serious breach if the craftsmen who constructed the tomb and knew of its treasure were to divulge those secrets. Therefore, after the funeral ceremonies had completed, the inner passages and doorways were blocked, and the exit sealed, immediately trapping the workers and craftsmen inside. None could escape. Trees and vegetation were then planted on the tomb mound such that it resembled a hill.</span></li>
