<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Anointing of the Sick</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Anointing of the sick</b>, known also by other names, is a form of religious <a href="/wiki/Anointing" title="Anointing">anointing</a> or "unction" (an older term with the same meaning) for the benefit of a sick person. It is practiced by many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_church" title="Christian church">Christian churches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian_denomination" title="Christian denomination">denominations</a>.</p>
<p>Anointing of the sick was a customary practice in many civilizations, including among the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greeks</a> and early <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Jewish</a> communities. The use of oil for healing purposes is referred to in the writings of <a href="/wiki/Hippocrates" title="Hippocrates">Hippocrates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anointing of the sick should be distinguished from other religious anointings that occur in relation to other <a href="/wiki/Sacrament" title="Sacrament">sacraments</a>, in particular <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Confirmation" title="Confirmation">confirmation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ordination" title="Ordination">ordination</a>, and also in the <a href="/wiki/Coronation" title="Coronation">coronation</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarch</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODCC_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ODCC-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names">Names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anointing_of_the_Sick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 1972, the Roman Catholic Church uses the name "Anointing of the Sick" both in the English translations issued by the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> of its official documents in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and in the English official documents of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Episcopal_conference" title="Episcopal conference">Episcopal conferences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It does not, of course, forbid the use of other names, for example the more archaic term "Unction of the Sick" or the term "Extreme Unction". Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Walter_Kasper" title="Walter Kasper">Walter Kasper</a> used the latter term in his intervention at the 2005 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assembly_of_the_Synod_of_Bishops" title="Assembly of the Synod of Bishops">Assembly of the Synod of Bishops</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> However, the Church declared that "'Extreme unction' ... may also <i>and more fittingly</i> be called 'anointing of the sick'" (emphasis added),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and has itself adopted the latter term, while not outlawing the former. This is to emphasize that the sacrament is available, and recommended, to all those suffering from any serious illness, and to dispel the common misconception that it is exclusively for those at or very near the point of death.</p>
<p><b>Extreme Unction</b> was the usual name for the sacrament in the West from the late twelfth century until 1972, and was thus used at the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trent" title="Council of Trent">Council of Trent</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and in the 1913 <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Encyclopedia" title="Catholic Encyclopedia">Catholic Encyclopedia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-CE-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Lombard" title="Peter Lombard">Peter Lombard</a> (died 1160) is the first writer known to have used the term,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODCC_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ODCC-3">[3]</a></sup> which did not become the usual name in the West till towards the end of the twelfth century, and never became current in the East.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-CE-9">[9]</a></sup> The word "extreme" (final) indicated either that it was the last of the sacramental unctions (after the anointings at Baptism, Confirmation and, if received, Holy Orders) or because at that time it was normally administered only when a patient was <i>in extremis</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODCC_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-ODCC-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other names used in the West include <b>the unction or blessing of consecrated oil</b>, <b>the unction of God</b>, and <b>the office of the unction</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-CE-9">[9]</a></sup> Among some <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> bodies, who do not consider it a sacrament, but instead as a practice suggested rather than commanded by Scripture, it is called <b>anointing with oil</b>.</p>
<p>In the Greek Church the sacrament is called <b>Euchelaion</b> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> Εὐχέλαιον, from εὐχή, "prayer", and ἔλαιον, "oil").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODCC_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-ODCC-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-CE-9">[9]</a></sup> Other names are also used, such as ἅγιον ἔλαιον (holy oil), ἡγιασμένον ἔλαιον (consecrated oil), and χρῖσις or χρῖσμα (anointing).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-CE-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Community_of_Christ" title="Community of Christ">Community of Christ</a> uses the term <b>administration to the sick</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term "<a href="/wiki/Last_rites" title="Last rites">last rites</a>" refers to administration to a dying person not only of this sacrament but also of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacrament_of_Penance_(Catholic_Church)" title="Sacrament of Penance (Catholic Church)">Penance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Holy Communion</a>, the last of which, when administered in such circumstances, is known as "<a href="/wiki/Viaticum" title="Viaticum">Viaticum</a>", a word whose original meaning in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> was "provision for the journey". The normal order of administration is: first Penance (if the dying person is physically unable to <a href="/wiki/Confession_(religion)" title="Confession (religion)">confess</a>, absolution, conditional on the existence of contrition, is given); next, Anointing; finally, Viaticum (if the person can receive it).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biblical_texts">Biblical texts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anointing_of_the_Sick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biblical texts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The chief <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Biblical</a> text concerning the rite is <a class="external text" href="http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=James+5:14–15:14&amp;version=nrsv" rel="nofollow">James 5:14-15</a>: "Is any among you sick? Let him call for the elders of the church, and let them pray over him, anointing him with oil in the name of the Lord; and the prayer of faith will save the sick man, and the Lord will raise him up; and if he has committed sins, he will be forgiven." (RSV)</p>
<p><a class="external text" href="http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Matthew+10:8–10:8&amp;version=nrsv" rel="nofollow">Matthew 10:8</a>, <a class="external text" href="http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Luke+10:8–9:8&amp;version=nrsv" rel="nofollow">Luke 10:8-9</a> and <a class="external text" href="http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Mark+6:13–6:13&amp;version=nrsv" rel="nofollow">Mark 6:13</a> are also quoted in this regard.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sacramental_beliefs">Sacramental beliefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anointing_of_the_Sick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sacramental beliefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coptic_Orthodox_Church_of_Alexandria" title="Coptic Orthodox Church of Alexandria">Coptic</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Old_Catholic_Church" title="Old Catholic Church">Old Catholic</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Churches consider this anointing to be a sacrament. Other Christians too, in particular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglican" title="Anglican">Anglicans</a>, Lutherans and some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> and other Christian communities use a rite of anointing the sick, without necessarily classifying it as a sacrament.</p>
<p>In the Churches mentioned here by name, the oil used (called "oil of the sick" in both West and East)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> is blessed specifically for this purpose.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_Catholic_Church">Roman Catholic Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anointing_of_the_Sick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Roman Catholic Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anointing_of_the_Sick_(Catholic_Church)" title="Anointing of the Sick (Catholic Church)">Anointing of the Sick (Catholic Church)</a></div>
<p>An extensive account of the teaching of the Catholic Church on Anointing of the Sick is given in <a class="external text" href="http://www.usccb.org/catechism/text/pt2sect2chpt2art5.shtml" rel="nofollow"><i>Catechism of the Catholic Church</i>, 1499–1532.</a></p>
<p>Anointing of the Sick is one of the seven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacraments_(Catholic_Church)" title="Sacraments (Catholic Church)">Sacraments</a> recognized by the Catholic Church, and is associated with not only bodily healing but also forgiveness of sins. Only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Orders_(Catholic_Church)" title="Holy Orders (Catholic Church)">ordained priests</a> can administer it,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and "any priest may carry the holy oil with him, so that in a case of necessity he can administer the sacrament of anointing of the sick."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sacramental_graces">Sacramental graces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anointing_of_the_Sick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sacramental graces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Catholic Church sees the effects of the sacrament as follows. As the sacrament of Marriage gives grace for the married state, the sacrament of Anointing of the Sick gives grace for the state into which people enter through sickness. Through the sacrament a gift of the Holy Spirit is given, that renews confidence and faith in God and strengthens against temptations to discouragement, despair and anguish at the thought of death and the struggle of death; it prevents from losing Christian hope in God's justice, truth and salvation.</p>
<p>The special grace of the sacrament of the Anointing of the Sick has as its effects:</p>
<ul>
<li>the uniting of the sick person to the passion of Christ, for his own good and that of the whole Church;</li>
<li>the strengthening, peace, and courage to endure, in a Christian manner, the sufferings of illness or old age;</li>
<li>the forgiveness of sins, if the sick person was not able to obtain it through the sacrament of penance;</li>
<li>the restoration of health, if it is conducive to the salvation of his soul;</li>
<li>the preparation for passing over to eternal life."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sacramental_oil">Sacramental oil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anointing_of_the_Sick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sacramental oil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The duly blessed oil used in the sacrament is, as laid down in the Apostolic Constitution <a class="external text" href="http://www.vatican.va/holy_father/paul_vi/apost_constitutions/documents/hf_p-vi_apc_19721130_sacram-unctionem_en.html" rel="nofollow">Sacram unctionem infirmorum</a>, pressed from olives or from other plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> It is blessed by the bishop of the diocese at the <a href="/wiki/Chrism" title="Chrism">Chrism</a> Mass he celebrates on <a href="/wiki/Maundy_Thursday" title="Maundy Thursday">Holy Thursday</a> or on a day close to it. If oil blessed by the bishop is not available, the priest administering the sacrament may bless the oil, but only within the framework of the celebration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_liturgical_form_.281972.29">Current liturgical form (1972)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anointing_of_the_Sick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Current liturgical form (1972)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Rite" title="Roman Rite">Roman Rite</a> Anointing of the Sick, as revised in 1972, puts greater stress than in the immediately preceding centuries on the sacrament's aspect of healing, and points to the place sickness holds in the normal life of Christians and its part in the redemptive work of the Church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODCC_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-ODCC-3">[3]</a></sup> Canon law permits its administration to any Catholic who has reached the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Use_of_reason" title="Use of reason">use of reason</a> and is beginning to be put in danger by illness or old age,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> unless the person in question obstinately persists in a manifestly grave sin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> "If there is any doubt as to whether the sick person has reached the use of reason, or is dangerously ill, or is dead, this sacrament is to be administered".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> There is an obligation to administer it to the sick who, when they were in possession of their faculties, at least implicitly asked for it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> A new illness or a renewal or worsening of the first illness enables a person to receive the sacrament a further time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ritual book on pastoral care of the sick provides three rites:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A more detailed citation is required. (September 2015)">full citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> anointing outside Mass,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> anointing within Mass,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and anointing in a hospital or institution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The rite of anointing outside Mass begins with a greeting by the priest, followed by sprinkling of all present with holy water, if deemed desirable, and a short instruction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> There follows a penitential act, as at the beginning of Mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> If the sick person wishes to receive the sacrament of penance, it is preferable that the priest make himself available for this during a previous visit; but if the sick person must confess during the celebration of the sacrament of anointing, this confession replaces the penitential rite<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> A passage of Scripture is read, and the priest may give a brief explanation of the reading, a short litany is said, and the priest lays his hands on the head of the sick person and then says a prayer of thanksgiving over the already blessed oil or, if necessary, blesses the oil himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The actual anointing of the sick person is done on the forehead, with the prayer "Through this holy anointing may the Lord in his love and mercy help you with the grace of the Holy Spirit", and on the hands, with the prayer "May the Lord who frees you from sin save you and raise you up". To each prayer the sick person, if able, responds: "Amen."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pastoral_Care_of_the_Sick.2C_124_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pastoral_Care_of_the_Sick.2C_124-32">[32]</a></sup> It is permitted, in accordance with local culture and traditions and the condition of the sick person, to anoint other parts of the body in addition, such as the area of pain or injury, but without repeating the sacramental form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pastoral_Care_of_the_Sick.2C_124_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pastoral_Care_of_the_Sick.2C_124-32">[32]</a></sup> In case of emergency, a single anointing, not necessarily on the forehead, is sufficient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_liturgical_form">Historical liturgical form</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anointing_of_the_Sick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Historical liturgical form">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>From the early <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> until after the Second Vatican Council the sacrament was administered, within the <a href="/wiki/Latin_Church" title="Latin Church">Latin Church</a>, only when <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">death</a> was approaching and, in practice, bodily recovery was not ordinarily looked for,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODCC_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-ODCC-3">[3]</a></sup> giving rise, as mentioned above to the name "Extreme Unction" (i.e. final anointing). The form used in the Roman Rite included anointing of seven parts of the body while saying (in Latin): "Through this holy unction and His own most tender mercy may the Lord pardon thee whatever sins or faults thou hast committed [quidquid deliquisti] by sight [by hearing, smell, taste, touch, walking, carnal delectation]", the last phrase corresponding to the part of the body that was touched; however, in the words of the 1913 Catholic Encyclopedia, "the unction of the loins is generally, if not universally, omitted in English-speaking countries, and it is of course everywhere forbidden in case of women".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-CE-9">[9]</a></sup> Use of this form is still permitted under the conditions mentioned in article 9 of the 2007 <a href="/wiki/Motu_proprio" title="Motu proprio">motu proprio</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Summorum_Pontificum" title="Summorum Pontificum">Summorum Pontificum</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Liturgical rites of the Catholic Church, both Western and Eastern, other than the Roman, have a variety of other forms for celebrating the sacrament.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_Orthodox_Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anointing_of_the_Sick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Eastern Orthodox Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The teaching of the Eastern Orthodox Church on the Holy Mystery (sacrament) of Unction is similar to that of the Roman Catholic Church. However, the reception of the Mystery is not limited to those who are enduring physical illness. The Mystery is given for healing (both physical and spiritual) and for the forgiveness of sin. For this reason, it is normally required that one go to confession before receiving Unction. Because it is a Sacred Mystery of the Church, only Orthodox Christians may receive it.</p>
<p>The solemn form of Eastern Christian anointing requires the ministry of seven priests. A table is prepared, upon which is set a vessel containing <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>. Into the wheat has been placed an empty shrine-lamp, seven candles, and seven anointing brushes. Candles are distributed for all to hold during the service. The rite begins with reading <a href="/wiki/Psalm_51" title="Psalm 51">Psalm 50</a> (the great penitential psalm), followed by the chanting of a special <a href="/wiki/Canon_(hymnography)" title="Canon (hymnography)">canon</a>. After this, the senior priest (or bishop) pours pure olive oil and a small amount of wine into the shrine lamp, and says the "Prayer of the Oil", which calls upon God to "...sanctify this Oil, that it may be effectual for those who shall be anointed therewith, unto healing, and unto relief from every passion, every malady of the flesh and of the spirit, and every ill..." Then follow seven series of <a href="/wiki/Epistle" title="Epistle">epistles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gospel" title="Gospel">gospels</a>, long prayers, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ektenia" title="Ektenia">Ektenias</a> (litanies) and anointings. Each series is served by one of the seven priests in turn. The afflicted one is anointed with the <a href="/wiki/Sign_of_the_cross" title="Sign of the cross">sign of the cross</a> on seven places: the forehead, the nostrils, the cheeks, the lips, the breast, the palms of both hands, and the back of the hands. After the last anointing, the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_Book" title="Gospel Book">Gospel Book</a> is opened and placed with the writing down upon the head of the one who was anointed, and the senior priest reads the "Prayer of the Gospel". At the end, the anointed kisses the Gospel, the Cross and the right hands of the priests, receiving their blessing.</p>
<p>Anointing is considered to be a public rather than a private sacrament, and so as many of the faithful who are able are encouraged to attend. It should be celebrated in the church when possible, but if this is impossible, it may be served in the home or hospital room of the afflicted.</p>
<p>Unction in the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Church" title="Greek Orthodox Church">Greek Orthodox Church</a> and Churches of Hellenic custom (<a href="/wiki/Melkite" title="Melkite">Melkite</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiochian_Orthodox" title="Antiochian Orthodox">Antiochian Orthodox</a>, etc.) is usually given with a minimum of ceremony.</p>
<p>Anointing may also be given during <a href="/wiki/Clean_Monday" title="Clean Monday">Forgiveness Vespers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holy_Week" title="Holy Week">Great Week</a>, on <a href="/wiki/Holy_Wednesday" title="Holy Wednesday">Great and Holy Wednesday</a>, to all who are prepared. Those who receive Unction on Holy Wednesday should go to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Communion" title="Holy Communion">Holy Communion</a> on Maundy Thursday. The significance of receiving Unction on Holy Wednesday is shored up by the hymns in the <a href="/wiki/Triodion" title="Triodion">Triodion</a> for that day, which speak of the sinful woman who anointed the feet of Christ (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Matthew#26:6" title="s:Bible (King James)/Matthew">Matthew 26:6-16</a>). Just as her sins were forgiven because of her penitence, so the faithful are exhorted to repent of their sins. In the same narrative, Jesus says, "in that she hath poured this ointment on my body, she did it for my burial" (Id., v. 12), linking the unction with Christ's death and resurrection.</p>
<p>In some <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">dioceses</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church" title="Russian Orthodox Church">Russian Orthodox Church</a> it is customary for the bishop to visit each parish or region of the diocese some time during <a href="/wiki/Great_Lent" title="Great Lent">Great Lent</a> and give Anointing for the faithful, together with the local clergy.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anglican_churches">Anglican churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anointing_of_the_Sick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Anglican churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1552 and later editions of the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Book of Common Prayer</a> omitted the form of anointing given in the original (1549) version in its Order for the Visitation of the Sick, but most twentieth-century Anglican prayer books do have anointing of the sick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODCC_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-ODCC-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some Anglicans accept that anointing of the sick has a sacramental character and is therefore a channel of God's grace, seeing it as an "outward and visible sign of an inward and spiritual grace" which is the definition of a sacrament. The Catechism of the Episcopal Church of the United States of America includes Unction of the Sick as among the "other sacramental rites" and it states that unction can be done with oil or simply with laying on of hands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The rite of anointing is included in the Episcopal Church's "Ministration to the Sick" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Article 25 of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thirty-Nine_Articles" title="Thirty-Nine Articles">Thirty-Nine Articles</a>, which are one of the historical formularies of the Church of England (and as such, the Anglican Communion), speaking of the sacraments, says: "Those five commonly called Sacraments, that is to say, Confirmation, Penance, Orders, Matrimony, and extreme Unction, are not to be counted for Sacraments of the Gospel, being such as have grown partly of the corrupt following of the Apostles, partly are states of life allowed in the Scriptures; but yet have not like nature of Sacraments with Baptism, and the Lord's Supper, for that they have not any visible sign or ceremony ordained of God."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lutheran_churches">Lutheran churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anointing_of_the_Sick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Lutheran churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Anointing of the sick has been retained in some <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheran</a> churches since the Reformation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Although it is not considered a <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_sacraments" title="Lutheran sacraments">sacrament</a> like <a href="/wiki/Baptism#Reformation" title="Baptism">baptism</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eucharist_in_the_Lutheran_Church" title="Eucharist in the Lutheran Church">Eucharist</a>, it is known as a <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritual</a> in the same respect as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Confession_in_the_Lutheran_Church" title="Confession in the Lutheran Church">confession</a>, <a href="/wiki/Confirmation_(Lutheran_Church)" title="Confirmation (Lutheran Church)">confirmation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Orders" title="Holy Orders">holy orders</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_views_of_marriage#Protestants" title="Christian views of marriage">matrimony</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Liturgy">Liturgy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anointing_of_the_Sick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Liturgy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>After the penitent has received <a href="/wiki/Absolution" title="Absolution">absolution</a> following confession, the presiding minister recites <a class="external text" href="http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=James+5:14–16:14&amp;version=nrsv" rel="nofollow">James 5:14-16</a>. He goes on to recite the following:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcc_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-pcc-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>[Name], you have confessed your sins and received Holy Absolution. In remembrance of the grace of God given by the Holy Spirit in the waters of Holy Baptism, I will anoint you with oil. Confident in our Lord and in love for you, we also pray for you that you will not lose faith. Knowing that in Godly patience the Church endures with you and supports you during this affliction. We firmly believe that this illness is for the glory of God and that the Lord will both hear our prayer and work according to His good and gracious will.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>He anoints the person on the forehead and says this blessing:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Almighty God, the Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, who has given you the new birth of water and the Spirit and has forgiven you all your sins, strengthen you with His grace to life everlasting. Amen.</p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_Protestant_communities">Other Protestant communities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anointing_of_the_Sick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Other Protestant communities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Among Protestants, anointing is provided in a wide variety of formats but, for the most part, however, it has fallen into disuse. Protestant communities generally vary widely on the sacramental character of anointing. Most Mainline Protestants recognize only two sacraments, the Eucharist and baptism, deeming Anointing only a humanly-instituted rite. Non-traditional Protestant communities generally use the term "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ordinance_(Christian)" title="Ordinance (Christian)">ordinance</a>" rather than "Sacrament".</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mainline_beliefs">Mainline beliefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anointing_of_the_Sick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Mainline beliefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liturgical" title="Liturgical">Liturgical</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mainline_Protestant" title="Mainline Protestant">Mainline Protestant</a> communities (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presbyterian" title="Presbyterian">Presbyterian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Congregational_church" title="Congregational church">Congregationalist</a>/<a href="/wiki/United_Church_of_Christ" title="United Church of Christ">United Church of Christ</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methodist" title="Methodist">Methodist</a>, etc.) all have official yet often optional liturgical rites for the anointing of the sick partly on the model of Western pre-Reformation rites. Anointing need not be associated with grave illness or imminent danger of death.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Charismatic_and_Pentecostal_beliefs">Charismatic and Pentecostal beliefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anointing_of_the_Sick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Charismatic and Pentecostal beliefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charismatic_(Christians)" title="Charismatic (Christians)">Charismatic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentecostal" title="Pentecostal">Pentecostal</a> communities, anointing of the sick is a frequent practice and has been an important ritual in these communities since the respective movements were founded in the 19th and 20th centuries. These communities use extemporaneous forms of administration at the discretion of the minister, who need not be a pastor. There is minimal ceremony attached to its administration. Usually, several people physically touch (laying on of hands) the recipient during the anointing. It may be part of a worship service with the full assembly of the congregation present, but may also be done in more private settings, such as homes or hospital rooms. Some Pentecostals believe that physical healing is within the anointing and so there is often great expectation or at least great hope that a miraculous cure or improvement will occur when someone is being prayed over for healing.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Evangelical_and_fundamentalist_beliefs">Evangelical and fundamentalist beliefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anointing_of_the_Sick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Evangelical and fundamentalist beliefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Evangelicalism" title="Evangelicalism">Evangelical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fundamentalism" title="Fundamentalism">Fundamentalist</a> communities, anointing of the sick is performed with varying degrees of frequency, although laying on of hands may be more common than anointing. The rite would be similar to that of Pentecostals in its simplicity, but would usually not have the same emotionalism attached to it. Unlike some Pentecostals, Evangelicals and Fundamentalists generally do not believe that physical healing is within the anointing. Therefore, God may or may not grant physical healing to the sick. The healing conferred by anointing is thus a spiritual event that may not result in physical recovery.</p>
<p>The Church of the Brethren practices Anointing with Oil as an ordinance along with Baptism, Communion, Laying on of Hands, and the Love Feast.</p>
<p>Evangelical Protestants who use anointing differ about whether the person doing the anointing must be an ordained member of the <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a>, whether the oil must necessarily be olive oil and have been previously specially consecrated, and about other details. Several Evangelical groups reject the practice so as not to be identified with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charismatic_movement" title="Charismatic movement">charismatic</a> and Pentecostal groups, which practice it widely.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_Catholic_rite_among_Protestants">Use of Catholic rite among Protestants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anointing_of_the_Sick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Use of Catholic rite among Protestants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some Protestant US military chaplains carry the Roman Rite version of the Anointing of the Sick with them for use if called upon to assist wounded or dying soldiers who are Catholics. The Catholic and Orthodox Churches consider invalid <i>as a sacrament</i> the administration of Anointing of the Sick by such chaplains, who in the eyes of those Churches are not validly ordained priests. The rite performed by them is thus seen as having the same by no means negligible value of any other form of prayer offered for the sick or dying.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latter_Day_Saint_movement">Latter Day Saint movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anointing_of_the_Sick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Latter Day Saint movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anointing_of_the_Sick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Latter_Day_Saint_movement" title="Latter Day Saint movement">Latter-day Saints</a>, who consider themselves <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Restorationism_(Christian_primitivism)" title="Restorationism (Christian primitivism)">restorationists</a>, also practice ritual anointing of the sick, as well as other forms of anointing. Members of <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a> (LDS Church) consider anointing to be an <a href="/wiki/Ordinance_(Latter_Day_Saints)" title="Ordinance (Latter Day Saints)">ordinance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-613_Administering_to_the_Sick_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-613_Administering_to_the_Sick-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Members of the LDS Church who hold the <a href="/wiki/Melchizedek_priesthood_(Latter_Day_Saints)" title="Melchizedek priesthood (Latter Day Saints)">Melchizedek priesthood</a> may use consecrated oil in performing the ordinance of <a href="/wiki/Anointing_of_the_Sick#Latter_Day_Saints" title="Anointing of the Sick">blessing of the "sick or afflicted"</a>, though oil is not required if it is unavailable. The priesthood holder anoints the recipient's head with a drop of oil, then <a href="/wiki/Laying_on_of_hands" title="Laying on of hands">lays hands upon that head</a> and declare their act of anointing. Then another priesthood holder joins in, if available, and pronounces a "sealing" of the anointing and other words of blessing, as he feels inspired. Melchizedek priesthood holders are also authorized to consecrate any pure olive oil and often carry a personal supply in case they have need to perform a blessing. Oil is not used in other <a href="/wiki/Priesthood_blessing" title="Priesthood blessing">blessings</a>, such as for people seeking comfort or counsel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-613_Administering_to_the_Sick_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-613_Administering_to_the_Sick-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/James#5:14" title="s:Bible (King James)/James">James 5:14-15</a> reference, the <a href="/wiki/Doctrine_and_Covenants" title="Doctrine and Covenants">Doctrine and Covenants</a> contains numerous references to the anointing and healing of the sick by those with authority to do so.</p>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacraments_(Community_of_Christ)#Administration_to_the_sick" title="Sacraments (Community of Christ)">Administration to the sick</a> is one of the eight <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacraments_(Community_of_Christ)" title="Sacraments (Community of Christ)">sacraments of the Community of Christ</a>, in which it has also been used for people seeking spiritual, emotional or mental healing.</p>
