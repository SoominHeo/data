<p><b>Bonnie Lynn Raitt</b> (born November 8, 1949) is an American <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a> singer-songwriter, musician, and activist.</p>
<p>During the 1970s, Raitt released a series of <a href="/wiki/Americana_(music)" title="Americana (music)">roots</a>-influenced albums which incorporated elements of <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a>, rock, <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country</a>. In 1989, after several years of critical acclaim but little commercial success, she had a major hit with the album <i><a href="/wiki/Nick_of_Time_(album)" title="Nick of Time (album)">Nick of Time</a></i>. The following two albums, <i><a href="/wiki/Luck_of_the_Draw_(album)" title="Luck of the Draw (album)">Luck of the Draw</a></i> (1991) and <i><a href="/wiki/Longing_in_Their_Hearts" title="Longing in Their Hearts">Longing in Their Hearts</a></i> (1994), were also multimillion sellers, generating several hit singles, including "<a href="/wiki/Something_to_Talk_About_(Bonnie_Raitt_song)" title="Something to Talk About (Bonnie Raitt song)">Something to Talk About</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Love_Sneakin%27_Up_On_You" title="Love Sneakin' Up On You">Love Sneakin' Up on You</a>", and the ballad "<a href="/wiki/I_Can%27t_Make_You_Love_Me" title="I Can't Make You Love Me">I Can't Make You Love Me</a>" (with Bruce Hornsby on piano).</p>
<p>Raitt has received 10 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammy_Awards" title="Grammy Awards">Grammy Awards</a>. She is listed as number 50 in <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine's list of the "100 Greatest Singers of All Time"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and number 89 on their list of the "100 Greatest Guitarists of All Time".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonnie_Raitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Raitt was born in Burbank, California. She is the daughter of the <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a> musical star <a href="/wiki/John_Raitt" title="John Raitt">John Raitt</a> and his first wife, the pianist Marjorie Haydock, and was raised in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quaker" title="Quaker">Quaker</a> tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oprah_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-oprah-3">[3]</a></sup> She began playing guitar at Camp Regis-Apple Jack in Paul Smiths, NY, an early age. Later she gained notice for her <a href="/wiki/Slide_guitar" title="Slide guitar">bottleneck-style guitar</a> playing. Raitt says she played "a little at school and at [a summer] camp", <a href="/wiki/Camp_Regis-Applejack" title="Camp Regis-Applejack">Camp Regis-Applejack</a>, in New York. Raitt is of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_American" title="Scottish American">Scottish</a> ancestry, with her ancestors being those who constructed <a href="/wiki/Rait_Castle" title="Rait Castle">Rait Castle</a> near <a href="/wiki/Nairn" title="Nairn">Nairn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>After graduating from <a href="/wiki/Oakwood_Friends_School" title="Oakwood Friends School">Oakwood Friends School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Poughkeepsie,_New_York" title="Poughkeepsie, New York">Poughkeepsie, New York</a>, in 1967 Raitt entered <a href="/wiki/Radcliffe_College" title="Radcliffe College">Radcliffe College</a>, majoring in <a href="/wiki/Social_relation" title="Social relation">social relations</a> and <a href="/wiki/African_studies" title="African studies">African studies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> She said her "plan was to travel to <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>, where President <a href="/wiki/Julius_Nyerere" title="Julius Nyerere">Julius Nyerere</a> was creating a government based on democracy and socialism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oprah_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-oprah-3">[3]</a></sup> Raitt became friends with blues promoter <a href="/wiki/Dick_Waterman" title="Dick Waterman">Dick Waterman</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oprah_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-oprah-3">[3]</a></sup> During her second year of college Raitt took a semester off and moved to <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> with Waterman and other local musicians. Raitt says it was an "opportunity that changed everything."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oprah_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-oprah-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonnie_Raitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970.E2.80.9376">1970–76</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonnie_Raitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1970–76">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the fall of 1970, while opening for <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Fred_McDowell" title="Mississippi Fred McDowell">Mississippi Fred McDowell</a> at <a href="/wiki/The_Gaslight_Cafe" title="The Gaslight Cafe">the Gaslight Cafe</a> in New York, she was seen by a reporter from <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i> magazine, who began to spread the word about her performance. Scouts from major record companies were soon attending her shows to watch her play. She eventually accepted an offer from <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a>, which soon released her debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Bonnie_Raitt_(album)" title="Bonnie Raitt (album)">Bonnie Raitt</a></i>, in 1971. The album was warmly received by the music press, with many writers praising her skills as an interpreter and as a bottleneck guitarist; at the time, few women in popular music had strong reputations as guitarists.</p>
<p>While admired by those who saw her perform, and respected by her peers, Raitt gained little public acclaim for her work. Her critical stature continued to grow but record sales remained modest. Her second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Give_It_Up_(Bonnie_Raitt_album)" title="Give It Up (Bonnie Raitt album)">Give It Up</a></i>, was released in 1972 to universal <a class="external text" href="http://www.allmusic.com/album/give-it-up-mw0000196849" rel="nofollow">"acclaim"</a>; though many critics<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (March 2015)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> still regard it as her best work, it did not change her commercial fortunes. 1973's <i><a href="/wiki/Takin%27_My_Time" title="Takin' My Time">Takin' My Time</a></i> was also met with critical acclaim, but these notices were not matched by the sales.</p>
<p>Raitt was beginning to receive greater press coverage, including a 1975 cover story for <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone_Magazine" title="Rolling Stone Magazine">Rolling Stone Magazine</a></i>, but with <a href="/wiki/1974_in_music" title="1974 in music">1974's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Streetlights_(Bonnie_Raitt_album)" title="Streetlights (Bonnie Raitt album)">Streetlights</a></i>, reviews for her work were becoming increasingly mixed. By now, Raitt was already experimenting with different producers and different styles, and she began to adopt a more mainstream sound that continued through 1975's <i><a href="/wiki/Home_Plate_(album)" title="Home Plate (album)">Home Plate</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1976, Raitt made an appearance on <a href="/wiki/Warren_Zevon" title="Warren Zevon">Warren Zevon</a>'s eponymous album with his friend <a href="/wiki/Jackson_Browne" title="Jackson Browne">Jackson Browne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fleetwood_Mac" title="Fleetwood Mac">Fleetwood Mac</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Lindsey_Buckingham" title="Lindsey Buckingham">Lindsey Buckingham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Nicks" title="Stevie Nicks">Stevie Nicks</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1977.E2.80.9388">1977–88</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonnie_Raitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1977–88">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>1977's <i><a href="/wiki/Sweet_Forgiveness" title="Sweet Forgiveness">Sweet Forgiveness</a></i> album gave Raitt her first commercial breakthrough when it yielded a hit single in her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cover_song" title="Cover song">cover</a> of <a href="/wiki/Runaway_(Del_Shannon_song)" title="Runaway (Del Shannon song)">"Runaway."</a> Recast as a heavy <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">rhythm and blues</a> recording based on a rhythmic groove inspired by <a href="/wiki/Al_Green" title="Al Green">Al Green</a>, Raitt's version of "Runaway" was disparaged by many critics. However, the song's commercial success prompted a bidding war for Raitt between <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a>. "There was this big Columbia–Warner war going on at the time", recalled Raitt in a 1990 interview. "<a href="/wiki/James_Taylor" title="James Taylor">James Taylor</a> had just left Warner Bros. and made a big album for Columbia...And then, Warner signed <a href="/wiki/Paul_Simon" title="Paul Simon">Paul Simon</a> away from Columbia, and they didn't want me to have a hit record for Columbia – no matter what! So, I renegotiated my contract, and they basically matched Columbia's offer. Frankly the deal was a really big deal."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Warner Brothers held higher expectations for Raitt's next album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Glow" title="The Glow">The Glow</a></i>, in 1979, but it was released to poor reviews as well as modest sales. Raitt would have one commercial success in 1979 when she helped organize the five <a href="/wiki/Musicians_United_for_Safe_Energy" title="Musicians United for Safe Energy">Musicians United for Safe Energy</a> (MUSE) concerts at <a href="/wiki/Madison_Square_Garden" title="Madison Square Garden">Madison Square Garden</a> in New York City. The shows spawned the three-record gold album <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/No_Nukes_(album)" title="No Nukes (album)">No Nukes</a></i>, as well as a Warner Brothers feature film <a href="/wiki/No_Nukes_(film)" title="No Nukes (film)">of the same name</a>. The shows featured co-founders <a href="/wiki/Jackson_Browne" title="Jackson Browne">Jackson Browne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graham_Nash" title="Graham Nash">Graham Nash</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Hall_(New_York)" title="John Hall (New York)">John Hall</a>, and Raitt as well as <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Springsteen" title="Bruce Springsteen">Bruce Springsteen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Petty_and_the_Heartbreakers" title="Tom Petty and the Heartbreakers">Tom Petty and the Heartbreakers</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Doobie_Brothers" title="The Doobie Brothers">the Doobie Brothers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carly_Simon" title="Carly Simon">Carly Simon</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Taylor" title="James Taylor">James Taylor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gil_Scott-Heron" title="Gil Scott-Heron">Gil Scott-Heron</a>, and numerous others.</p>
<p>For her next record, <a href="/wiki/1982_in_music" title="1982 in music">1982's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Green_Light_(Bonnie_Raitt_album)" title="Green Light (Bonnie Raitt album)">Green Light</a></i>, Raitt made a conscious attempt to revisit the sound of her earlier records. However, to her surprise, many of her peers and the media compared her new sound to the burgeoning <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">new wave</a> movement. The album received her strongest reviews in years, but her sales did not improve and this would have a severe impact on her relationship with Warner Brothers.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Drop_from_Warner_Brothers">Drop from Warner Brothers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonnie_Raitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Drop from Warner Brothers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1983, as Raitt was finishing work on her follow-up album, entitled <i>Tongue &amp; Groove</i>, Warner Brothers "cleaned house", dropping a number of major artists such as <a href="/wiki/Van_Morrison" title="Van Morrison">Van Morrison</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arlo_Guthrie" title="Arlo Guthrie">Arlo Guthrie</a> from their roster. The day after mastering was completed on <i>Tongue &amp; Groove</i>, the record label dropped Raitt also. The album was shelved indefinitely, and Raitt was left without a record label. By then, Raitt was also struggling with alcohol and drug abuse problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-notbroken_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-notbroken-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite her personal and professional problems, Raitt continued to tour and participate in political activism. In 1985, she sang and appeared in the video of "<a href="/wiki/Sun_City_(song)" title="Sun City (song)">Sun City</a>", the anti-<a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">apartheid</a> record written and produced by guitarist <a href="/wiki/Steven_Van_Zandt" title="Steven Van Zandt">Steven Van Zandt</a>. Along with her participation in <a href="/wiki/Farm_Aid" title="Farm Aid">Farm Aid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a> concerts, Raitt traveled to Moscow in 1987 to participate in the first joint Soviet/American Peace Concert, later shown on the <a href="/wiki/Showtime_(TV_network)" title="Showtime (TV network)">Showtime</a> television network. Also in 1987, Raitt organized a benefit in Los Angeles for Countdown '87 to Stop <a href="/wiki/Contras" title="Contras">Contra</a> Aid. The benefit featured herself along with musicians <a href="/wiki/Don_Henley" title="Don Henley">Don Henley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herbie_Hancock" title="Herbie Hancock">Herbie Hancock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holly_Near" title="Holly Near">Holly Near</a> and others.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tongue_and_Groove.27s_name_change_and_release"><i>Tongue and Groove's</i> name change and release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonnie_Raitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tongue and Groove's name change and release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Two years after dropping her from their label, Warner Brothers notified Raitt of their plans to release <i>Tongue &amp; Groove</i>. "I said it wasn't really fair," recalled Raitt. "I think at this point they felt kind of bad. I mean, I was out there touring on my savings to keep my name up, and my ability to draw was less and less. So they agreed to let me go in and recut half of it, and that's when it came out as <i><a href="/wiki/Nine_Lives_(Bonnie_Raitt_album)" title="Nine Lives (Bonnie Raitt album)">Nine Lives</a></i>." A critical and commercial disappointment, <a href="/wiki/1986_in_music" title="1986 in music">1986's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Nine_Lives_(Bonnie_Raitt_album)" title="Nine Lives (Bonnie Raitt album)">Nine Lives</a></i> would be Raitt's last new recording for Warner Brothers.</p>
<p>In late 1987, Raitt joined singers <a href="/wiki/K.d._lang" title="K.d. lang">k.d. lang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Warnes" title="Jennifer Warnes">Jennifer Warnes</a> as female background vocalists for <a href="/wiki/Roy_Orbison" title="Roy Orbison">Roy Orbison</a>'s television special, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roy_Orbison_and_Friends,_A_Black_and_White_Night" title="Roy Orbison and Friends, A Black and White Night">Roy Orbison and Friends, A Black and White Night</a></i>. Following this highly acclaimed broadcast, Raitt began working on new material. By then, she was clean and sober, having resolved her <a href="/wiki/Substance_abuse" title="Substance abuse">substance abuse</a> problem. She later credited <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Ray_Vaughan" title="Stevie Ray Vaughan">Stevie Ray Vaughan</a> for his help in a <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_State_Fair" title="Minnesota State Fair">Minnesota State Fair</a> concert<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> the night after Vaughan's 1990 death. During this time, Raitt considered signing with the <a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">Prince</a>-owned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paisley_Park" title="Paisley Park">Paisley Park</a> label, but negotiations ultimately fell through. Instead she began recording a bluesy mix of pop and rock under the production guidance of <a href="/wiki/Don_Was" title="Don Was">Don Was</a> at <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol Records</a>.</p>
<p>Raitt had met Was through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hal_Wilner" title="Hal Wilner">Hal Wilner</a>, who was putting together <i><a href="/wiki/Stay_Awake:_Various_Interpretations_of_Music_from_Vintage_Disney_Films" title="Stay Awake: Various Interpretations of Music from Vintage Disney Films">Stay Awake</a></i>, a tribute album to <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">Disney</a> music for <a href="/wiki/A%26M_Records" title="A&amp;M Records">A&amp;M</a>. Was and Wilner both wanted Raitt to sing lead on an adult-contemporary arrangement created by Was for "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baby_Mine_(song)" title="Baby Mine (song)">Baby Mine</a>", the <a href="/wiki/Lullaby" title="Lullaby">lullaby</a> from <i><a href="/wiki/Dumbo" title="Dumbo">Dumbo</a></i>. Raitt was very pleased with the sessions, and she asked Was to produce her next album.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1989.E2.80.9399:_Commercial_breakthrough">1989–99: Commercial breakthrough</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonnie_Raitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1989–99: Commercial breakthrough">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>After working with Was on the <i>Stay Awake</i> album, Raitt's management, Gold Mountain, approached numerous labels about a new record deal, and she was signed to Capitol by a&amp;r executive <a href="/wiki/Tim_Devine" title="Tim Devine">Tim Devine</a>. At Capitol, after nearly 20 years, Raitt achieved belated commercial success with her tenth album, <i><a href="/wiki/Nick_of_Time_(album)" title="Nick of Time (album)">Nick of Time</a></i>. Released in the spring of 1989, <i>Nick of Time</i> went to the top of the U.S. charts following Raitt's Grammy sweep in early 1990. This album has been voted number 230 in the Rolling Stone magazine list of 500 Greatest Albums of All Time. Raitt herself pointed out that her 10th try was "my first sober album."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the same time, Raitt received a fourth <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> for her duet "In the Mood" with <a href="/wiki/John_Lee_Hooker" title="John Lee Hooker">John Lee Hooker</a> on his album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Healer_(album)" title="The Healer (album)">The Healer</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Nick_of_Time_(album)" title="Nick of Time (album)">Nick of Time</a> was also the first of many of her recordings to feature her longtime rhythm section of <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Fataar" title="Ricky Fataar">Ricky Fataar</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_%22Hutch%22_Hutchinson" title='James "Hutch" Hutchinson'>James "Hutch" Hutchinson</a> (Although previously Fataar had played on her Green Light album and Hutchinson had worked on Nine Lives), both of whom record and tour with her to this day. <i>Nick of Time</i> has sold over six million copies in the US alone.</p>
<p>Raitt followed up this success with three more Grammy Awards for her 1991 album <i><a href="/wiki/Luck_of_the_Draw_(album)" title="Luck of the Draw (album)">Luck of the Draw</a></i> which sold nearly 8 million copies in the United States. Three years later, in 1994, she added two more Grammys with her album <i><a href="/wiki/Longing_in_Their_Hearts" title="Longing in Their Hearts">Longing in Their Hearts</a></i>, her second no. 1 album. Both of these albums were multi-platinum successes. Raitt's collaboration with Was would amicably come to an end with <a href="/wiki/1995_in_music" title="1995 in music">1995's</a> live release, <i><a href="/wiki/Road_Tested" title="Road Tested">Road Tested</a></i>. Released to solid reviews, it sold well enough to be certified gold.</p>
<p>"Rock Steady" was a hit written by Bryan Adams and Gretchen Peters in 1995. The song was written as a duet with Bryan Adams and Bonnie Raitt for her Road Tested tour, which also became one of her albums. The original demo version of the song appears on Adams' 1996 single "Let's Make a Night to Remember".</p>
<p>For her next studio album, Raitt hired <a href="/wiki/Mitchell_Froom" title="Mitchell Froom">Mitchell Froom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tchad_Blake" title="Tchad Blake">Tchad Blake</a> as her producers. "I loved working with <a href="/wiki/Don_Was" title="Don Was">Don Was</a> but I wanted to give myself and my fans a stretch and do something different," Raitt said. Her work with Froom and Blake was released on <i><a href="/wiki/Fundamental_(Bonnie_Raitt_album)" title="Fundamental (Bonnie Raitt album)">Fundamental</a></i> in 1998.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000.E2.80.932007">2000–2007</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonnie_Raitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2000–2007">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In March 2000, Raitt was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rock and Roll Hall of Fame">Rock and Roll Hall of Fame</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleveland,_Ohio" title="Cleveland, Ohio">Cleveland, Ohio</a>.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Silver_Lining_(album)" title="Silver Lining (album)">Silver Lining</a></i> was released in 2002. In the US, it reached 13 on the Billboard chart and was later certified Gold. It contains the 3 singles "I Can't Help You Now", "Time of Our Lives" and the title track. All 3 singles charted within the top 40 of the US A/C chart.</p>
<p>In 2003 Capitol Records released the compilation album <i>The Best of Bonnie Raitt</i>. It contains songs from her prior Capitol albums from 1989 – 2002 including <i>Nick of Time</i>, <i>Luck of the Draw</i>, <i>Longing in Their Hearts</i>, <i>Road Tested</i>, <i>Fundamental</i>, and <i>Silver Lining</i>.</p>
<p>Raitt was featured on the album <a href="/wiki/True_Love_(Toots_%26_the_Maytals_album)" title="True Love (Toots &amp; the Maytals album)"><i>True Love</i></a> by <a href="/wiki/Toots_and_the_Maytals" title="Toots and the Maytals">Toots and the Maytals</a>, which won the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> in 2004 for Best Reggae Album, and showcased many notable musicians including <a href="/wiki/Willie_Nelson" title="Willie Nelson">Willie Nelson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Clapton" title="Eric Clapton">Eric Clapton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Beck" title="Jeff Beck">Jeff Beck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trey_Anastasio" title="Trey Anastasio">Trey Anastasio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gwen_Stefani" title="Gwen Stefani">Gwen Stefani</a> / <a href="/wiki/No_Doubt" title="No Doubt">No Doubt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ben_Harper" title="Ben Harper">Ben Harper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manu_Chao" title="Manu Chao">Manu Chao</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Roots" title="The Roots">The Roots</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Adams" title="Ryan Adams">Ryan Adams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Keith_Richards" title="Keith Richards">Keith Richards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toots_Hibbert" title="Toots Hibbert">Toots Hibbert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Douglas_(musician)" title="Paul Douglas (musician)">Paul Douglas</a>, Jackie Jackson, <a href="/wiki/Ken_Boothe" title="Ken Boothe">Ken Boothe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_Skatalites" title="The Skatalites">The Skatalites</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Souls_Alike" title="Souls Alike">Souls Alike</a></i> followed and was released in September 2005. In the US, it reached the top 20 on the Billboard chart. It contains the singles "I Will Not Be Broken" and "I Don't Want Anything to Change", which both charted in the top 40 of the US A/C chart.</p>
<p>In 2006 she released the live DVD/CD <i>Bonnie Raitt and Friends</i>, which was filmed as part of the critically acclaimed VH1 Classic Decades Rock Live Concert Series, featuring special guests Keb Mo', Alison Krauss, Ben Harper, Jon Cleary and Norah Jones. The DVD was released by Capitol Records on August 15. Bonnie Raitt and Friends, which was recorded live at the Trump Taj Mahal in Atlantic City, NJ on September 30, 2005, features never-before-seen performance and interview footage, including four duets not included in the VH1 Classic broadcast of the concert. With two hours of concert and interview footage, the concert which was filmed in Hi-Definition and is presented in 5.1 audio, features Raitt performing 17 songs with her longtime band – George Marinelli (guitar), James "Hutch" Hutchinson (bass), Ricky Fataar (drums) and Jon Cleary (keyboards). Included are such classic Raitt hits as "Something To Talk About," "Love Letter" (with Mo'), "You" (with Krauss) and a knock-out encore of "Love Sneakin' Up on You" with Raitt, Jones, Harper, Krauss and Mo' as well as highlights from her latest studio album, Souls Alike, including "I Will Not Be Broken," "God Was in the Water", "I Don't Want Anything To Change" (with Jones) and "Unnecessarily Mercenary" (a duet with keyboardist Cleary, who wrote the song). The accompanying CD features 11 tracks, including the radio single "Two Lights in the Nighttime" (featuring Ben Harper). The Bonnie Raitt and Friends TV Special and DVD/CD was produced by Barry Summers of World Productions and Rock Fuel Media.</p>
<p>Australian Country Music Artist <a href="/wiki/Graeme_Connors" title="Graeme Connors">Graeme Connors</a> has said, "Bonnie Raitt does something with a lyric no one else can do; she bends it and twists it right into your heart." (ABC Radio NSW Australia interview with Interviewer Chris Coleman on January 18, 2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, Raitt contributed to <i><a href="/wiki/Goin%27_Home:_A_Tribute_to_Fats_Domino" title="Goin' Home: A Tribute to Fats Domino">Goin' Home: A Tribute to Fats Domino</a></i>. With <a href="/wiki/Jon_Cleary_(musician)" title="Jon Cleary (musician)">Jon Cleary</a>, she sang a medley of "<a href="/wiki/I%27m_in_Love_Again" title="I'm in Love Again">I'm in Love Again</a>" and "All by Myself" by <a href="/wiki/Fats_Domino" title="Fats Domino">Fats Domino</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008.E2.80.93present">2008–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonnie_Raitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2008–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Raitt appeared on the June 7, 2008, broadcast of <a href="/wiki/Garrison_Keillor" title="Garrison Keillor">Garrison Keillor</a>'s radio program <i><a href="/wiki/A_Prairie_Home_Companion" title="A Prairie Home Companion">A Prairie Home Companion</a></i>. She performed two blues songs with Kevin "<a href="/wiki/Keb%27_Mo%27" title="Keb' Mo'">Keb' Mo'</a>" Moore: "No Getting Over You" and "There Ain't Nothin' in Ramblin'". Raitt also sang "<a href="/wiki/Dimming_of_the_Day" title="Dimming of the Day">Dimming of the Day</a>" with <a href="/wiki/Richard_Thompson_(musician)" title="Richard Thompson (musician)">Richard Thompson</a>. This show, along with another one with Raitt and her band in October 2006, is archived on the <i>Prairie Home Companion</i> website.</p>
<p>In April 2011, the <i>Bonnie Raitt and Friends</i> DVD officially became RIAA certified gold.</p>
<p>Raitt appeared in the 2011 documentary “Reggae Got Soul: The Story of <a href="/wiki/Toots_and_the_Maytals" title="Toots and the Maytals">Toots and the Maytals</a>” which was featured on <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> and described as “The untold story of one of the most influential artists ever to come out of Jamaica”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2012, Raitt performed a duet with <a href="/wiki/Alicia_Keys" title="Alicia Keys">Alicia Keys</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/54th_Grammy_Awards" title="54th Grammy Awards">54th Grammy Awards</a> in 2012 honoring <a href="/wiki/Etta_James" title="Etta James">Etta James</a>.</p>
<p>In April 2012, Raitt released her first studio album since 2005, entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Slipstream_(Bonnie_Raitt_album)" title="Slipstream (Bonnie Raitt album)">Slipstream</a></i>. It charted at Number 6 on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart marking her first top ten album since 1994's <i><a href="/wiki/Longing_in_Their_Hearts" title="Longing in Their Hearts">Longing in Their Hearts</a></i>. The album was described as "one of the best of her 40-year career" by <i><a href="/wiki/American_Songwriter" title="American Songwriter">American Songwriter</a></i> magazine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2012, Raitt was featured in a campaign called "30 Songs / 30 Days" to support <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half_the_Sky:_Turning_Oppression_into_Opportunity_for_Women_Worldwide" title="Half the Sky: Turning Oppression into Opportunity for Women Worldwide">Half the Sky: Turning Oppression into Opportunity for Women Worldwide</a>, a multi-platform media project inspired by a project outlined in a book by Nicholas Kristof and Sheryl WuDunn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In 2013, she appeared on <a href="/wiki/Foy_Vance" title="Foy Vance">Foy Vance</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/Joy_of_Nothing" title="Joy of Nothing">Joy of Nothing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></i></p>
<p>Raitt is listed at Number 50 in <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine's list of the 100 Greatest Singers of All Time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> She is also listed at number 89 in the <i>Rolling Stone</i> list of the 100 Greatest Guitarists of All Time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2016, Raitt released her seventeenth studio album <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dig_in_Deep" title="Dig in Deep">Dig in Deep</a></i>. The album charted at number 11 on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and received favorable reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The album features the single "Gypsy in Me" as well as a cover of the <a href="/wiki/INXS" title="INXS">INXS</a> song "<a href="/wiki/Need_You_Tonight" title="Need You Tonight">Need You Tonight</a>"</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Drug_and_alcohol_use_and_recovery">Drug and alcohol use and recovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonnie_Raitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Drug and alcohol use and recovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Raitt used alcohol and drugs, but began <a href="/wiki/Psychotherapy" title="Psychotherapy">psychotherapy</a> and joined <a href="/wiki/Alcoholics_Anonymous" title="Alcoholics Anonymous">Alcoholics Anonymous</a> in the late 1980s. She has said "I thought I had to live that partying lifestyle in order to be authentic, but in fact if you keep it up too long, all you're going to be is sloppy or dead".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ontheredcarpet1_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-ontheredcarpet1-21">[21]</a></sup> She became clean in 1987. She has credited <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Ray_Vaughan" title="Stevie Ray Vaughan">Stevie Ray Vaughan</a> for breaking her substance abuse, saying that what gave her the courage to admit her alcohol problem and stop drinking was seeing that Stevie Ray Vaughan was an even better musician when sober.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> She has also said that she stopped because she realized that the "late night life" was not working for her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In 1989 she said "I really feel like some angels have been carrying me around. I just have more focus and more discipline, and consequently more self-respect."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonnie_Raitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Raitt has taken sabbaticals, including after the deaths of her parents, brother and best friend. She has said "When I went through a lot of loss, I took a hiatus."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ontheredcarpet1_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-ontheredcarpet1-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Raitt and actor <a href="/wiki/Michael_O%27Keefe" title="Michael O'Keefe">Michael O'Keefe</a> were married on April 27, 1991. They announced their divorce on November 9, 1999,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> with a causal factor appearing to be that their careers caused considerable time apart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_activism">Political activism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonnie_Raitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Political activism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Raitt's political involvement goes back to the early 1970s. Her 1972 album <i>Give It Up</i> had a dedication "to the people of <a href="/wiki/North_Vietnam" title="North Vietnam">North Vietnam</a> ..." printed on the back.</p>
<p>Raitt's web site urges fans to learn more about preserving the environment. She was a founding member of <a href="/wiki/Musicians_United_for_Safe_Energy" title="Musicians United for Safe Energy">Musicians United for Safe Energy</a> in 1979 and a catalyst for the larger <a href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear_movement" title="Anti-nuclear movement">anti-nuclear movement</a>, becoming involved with groups like the <a href="/wiki/Abalone_Alliance" title="Abalone Alliance">Abalone Alliance</a> and Alliance for Survival.</p>
<p>In 1994 at the urging of writer <a href="/wiki/Dick_Waterman" title="Dick Waterman">Dick Waterman</a>, Raitt funded the replacement of a headstone for one of her mentors, blues guitarist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fred_McDowell" title="Fred McDowell">Fred McDowell</a> through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mt._Zion_Memorial_Fund" title="Mt. Zion Memorial Fund">Mt. Zion Memorial Fund</a>. Raitt later financed memorial headstones in Mississippi for musicians <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Minnie" title="Memphis Minnie">Memphis Minnie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sam_Chatmon" title="Sam Chatmon">Sam Chatmon</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tommy_Johnson_(blues_musician)" title="Tommy Johnson (blues musician)">Tommy Johnson</a> again with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mt._Zion_Memorial_Fund" title="Mt. Zion Memorial Fund">Mt. Zion Memorial Fund</a>.</p>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_Jazz_Festival" title="Stockholm Jazz Festival">Stockholm Jazz Festival</a> in July 2004, Raitt dedicated a classic to sitting (and later re-elected) U.S. President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>. She was quoted as saying, "We're gonna sing this for George Bush because he's out of here, people!" before she launched into the opening licks of "Your Good Thing (Is About to End)", a cover that was featured on her <a href="/wiki/1979_in_music" title="1979 in music">1979 album</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Glow" title="The Glow">The Glow</a></i>. In 2002, Raitt signed on as an official supporter of Little Kids Rock, a nonprofit organization that provides free musical instruments and free lessons to children in public schools throughout the U.S.A. She has visited children in the program and sits on the organization's board of directors as an honorary member.</p>
<p>In 2008, Raitt donated a song to the <a href="/wiki/Aid_Still_Required" title="Aid Still Required">Aid Still Required</a>'s CD to assist with relief efforts in Southeast Asia from the 2004 tsunami.</p>
<p>Raitt worked with <a href="/wiki/Reverb_(non-profit)" title="Reverb (non-profit)">Reverb</a>, a non-profit environmental organization, for her 2005 fall/winter and 2006 spring/summer/fall tours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Raitt is part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/No_Nukes_group" title="No Nukes group">No Nukes group</a>, which opposes the expansion of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a>. In 2007, No Nukes recorded a music video of a new version of the <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Springfield" title="Buffalo Springfield">Buffalo Springfield</a> song "<a href="/wiki/For_What_It%27s_Worth_(Buffalo_Springfield_song)" title="For What It's Worth (Buffalo Springfield song)">For What It's Worth</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 2008 Democratic primary campaign Raitt, along with <a href="/wiki/Jackson_Browne" title="Jackson Browne">Jackson Browne</a> and bassist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_%22Hutch%22_Hutchinson" title='James "Hutch" Hutchinson'>James "Hutch" Hutchinson</a>, performed at campaign appearances for candidate <a href="/wiki/John_Edwards" title="John Edwards">John Edwards</a>.</p>
