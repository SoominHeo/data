<p><b>Bonnie Kathleen Blair</b> (born March 18, 1964) is a retired American <a href="/wiki/Speed_skating" title="Speed skating">speedskater</a>. She is one of the top skaters of her era, and one of the most decorated athletes in <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic</a> history. Blair competed for the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> in four Olympics, winning five gold medals and one bronze medal.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonnie_Blair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Blair was born in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall,_New_York" title="Cornwall, New York">Cornwall, New York</a> to Charlie and Eleanor Blair. She was the youngest of six children and was raised in <a href="/wiki/Champaign,_Illinois" title="Champaign, Illinois">Champaign, Illinois</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> She attended Jefferson Middle School. After graduating from <a href="/wiki/Centennial_High_School_(Champaign,_Illinois)" title="Centennial High School (Champaign, Illinois)">Centennial High School</a> in Champaign,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> she moved to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milwaukee,_Wisconsin" title="Milwaukee, Wisconsin">Milwaukee</a> area to train with the United States national speed skating team.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonnie_Blair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1984.2C_1988.2C_and_1992_Olympics">1984, 1988, and 1992 Olympics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonnie_Blair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1984, 1988, and 1992 Olympics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Blair appeared at her first Olympic games at age 19 in <a href="/wiki/1984_Winter_Olympics" title="1984 Winter Olympics">Sarajevo</a> in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> She failed to medal but finished eighth in the 500 meters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/1988_Winter_Olympics" title="1988 Winter Olympics">1988 Winter Olympics</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calgary,_Alberta" title="Calgary, Alberta">Calgary, Alberta</a>, Blair had her best start ever in the 500 meters, winning the gold medal in world record time of 39.10 seconds. She also won the bronze in the 1,000 meters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Blair won the gold in both the 500 and 1,000 meters (1:21.90) at the <a href="/wiki/1992_Winter_Olympics" title="1992 Winter Olympics">1992 Winter Olympics</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albertville,_France" title="Albertville, France">Albertville, France</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1994_Olympics">1994 Olympics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonnie_Blair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1994 Olympics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Blair took advantage of a change of Olympic rules. In 1986, the <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> voted to stage the <a href="/wiki/Winter_Olympic_Games" title="Winter Olympic Games">Winter Olympics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Summer_Olympic_Games" title="Summer Olympic Games">Summer Olympics</a> in alternating four year cycles. Thus, the next Winter Games would be held in February 1994 rather than in February 1996. The <a href="/wiki/1994_Winter_Olympics" title="1994 Winter Olympics">1994 Winter Olympics</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lillehammer,_Norway" title="Lillehammer, Norway">Lillehammer, Norway</a>, were a coronation of sorts for Blair: She again won gold in the 500 meters (39.25) and 1,000 (1:18.74) meters races, in dominating fashion. Blair finished 0.36 seconds ahead of the second best time in the 500 meters, and her 1.38 second margin in the 1,000 meters race is the largest margin of victory in the history of the event. In the process she became the first American woman to win five gold medals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> She also was the only American to have 6 medals at any Winter Olympics, a record that stood until <a href="/wiki/Apolo_Ohno" title="Apolo Ohno">Apolo Ohno</a> tied it and then surpassed it at the 2010 Winter Olympics when he won a silver and two bronze medals in <a href="/wiki/Short_track_speed_skating" title="Short track speed skating">short track speed skating</a> for eight career medals.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Outside_the_Olympics">Outside the Olympics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonnie_Blair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Outside the Olympics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the 1994 Olympics, Blair continued to compete. In March 1994, Blair set another world record in the 500 meters, becoming the first female to complete the race in under 39 seconds (38.99). On March 18, 1995, she retired. Blair won several prestigious awards, including the 1992 <a href="/wiki/James_E._Sullivan_Award" title="James E. Sullivan Award">James E. Sullivan Award</a>, the 1992 <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Mathisen_Award" title="Oscar Mathisen Award">Oscar Mathisen Award</a> (being the first female winner of this award), the 1992 <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Wide_World_of_Sports_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Wide World of Sports (U.S. TV series)">Wide World of Sports</a> Athlete of the Year, and <a href="/wiki/Sports_Illustrated" title="Sports Illustrated">Sports Illustrated</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sportsman_of_the_Year" title="Sportsman of the Year">Sportsman of the Year</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Johann_Olav_Koss" title="Johann Olav Koss">Johann Olav Koss</a>, in 1994. She also was Female Athlete of the Year as selected by the <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">Associated Press</a> in 1994. Blair also won the World Cup points championship 11 times.</p>
<p>Blair also tried track cycle racing, and was coached by former speed skater and cycling world champion <a href="/wiki/Connie_Paraskevin" title="Connie Paraskevin">Connie Paraskevin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Blair also competed in short-track speed skating, becoming the Overall Short-track World Champion in 1986.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recognition.2C_personal_life">Recognition, personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonnie_Blair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Recognition, personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>She is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Chicagoland_Sports_Hall_of_Fame" title="Chicagoland Sports Hall of Fame">Chicagoland Sports Hall of Fame</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_Athletic_Hall_of_Fame" title="Wisconsin Athletic Hall of Fame">Wisconsin Athletic Hall of Fame</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 2004, she was elected to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Olympic_Hall_of_Fame" title="United States Olympic Hall of Fame">United States Olympic Hall of Fame</a>. At the time of her induction, Blair was the most decorated United States Winter Olympian of all time with 5 gold and one bronze (she is currently third to <a href="/wiki/Apolo_Ohno" title="Apolo Ohno">Apolo Ohno</a> who has 2 gold, 2 silver and 4 bronze if equality of medals irrespective of colour is applied). She was awarded a star (#7) on The Flag for Hope on September 29, 2015 in recognition of her outstanding Speed Skating Career and philanthropic efforts. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flag_for_Hope_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flag_for_Hope-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> She is married to fellow Olympic speedskater <a href="/wiki/Dave_Cruikshank" title="Dave Cruikshank">Dave Cruikshank</a>, with whom she has a son, Grant, and daughter, Blair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
