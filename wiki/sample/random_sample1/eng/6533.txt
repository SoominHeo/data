<p>The <b>Vodafone Arena</b> is an <a href="/wiki/All-seater_stadium" title="All-seater stadium">all-seater</a>, multi-purpose <a href="/wiki/Stadium" title="Stadium">stadium</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Be%C5%9Fikta%C5%9F" title="Beşiktaş">Beşiktaş</a> district of <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>, Turkey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It is the home ground of <a href="/wiki/Be%C5%9Fikta%C5%9F_J.K." title="Beşiktaş J.K.">Beşiktaş J.K.</a> The stadium was built on the site of Beşiktaş's former home, <a href="/wiki/BJK_%C4%B0n%C3%B6n%C3%BC_Stadium" title="BJK İnönü Stadium">BJK İnönü Stadium</a>. It has a capacity of approximately 43,500 spectators, after initially being planned for 41,903.</p>
<p>Vodafone Arena houses 144 executive suites, and one "1903 Lounge" which will entertain 1,903 spectators in total. The "1903 stand" has a capacity of 636 spectators. The new stadium also has 2,123 square metres of restaurants, 2,520 square metres of terrace restaurants and a VIP parking capacity of 600 vehicles. The ground is a 'smart stadium', where fans enjoy StadiumVision and high-speed Wi-Fi technology, being planned in conjunction with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cisco" title="Cisco">Cisco</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Demolition works on the <a href="/wiki/BJK_%C4%B0n%C3%B6n%C3%BC_Stadium" title="BJK İnönü Stadium">BJK İnönü Stadium</a> started on 2 June 2013, following the end of promotion play-offs for the <a href="/wiki/TFF_First_League" title="TFF First League">TFF First League</a>. The estimated cost of the project was around $80 million. The new stadium was designed by DB architects. The old stadium was demolished, except for the Eski Açık stand, since this stand and its towers are considered historical monuments by the government. The Eski Açık stand is re-arranged to resemble the look of an antique amphitheatre. The new stadium is "in harmony with the natural and historic landscape of the Bosphorus" when seen from the sea.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vodafone_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vodafone_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1936, the French architect and city planner <a href="/wiki/Henri_Prost" title="Henri Prost">Henri Prost</a> (1874–1959) was invited to Turkey by President <a href="/wiki/Mustafa_Kemal_Atat%C3%BCrk" title="Mustafa Kemal Atatürk">Mustafa Kemal Atatürk</a>. He was tasked with the preparation of Istanbul's rough-cut urban planning and rebuilding, which lasted until 1951. In the first plan, Prost thought that the district of Şişli might be a good location for a city stadium. However, the governor of Istanbul, Muhittin Üstündağ, asked Prost to put the stadium to Dolmabahçe district instead of the stable of the Dolmabahçe Palace as a fait accompli in his <a href="/wiki/Henri_Prost#Istanbul.27s_redevelopment" title="Henri Prost">master city plan</a>, which came into force in 1939.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vodafone_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/BJK_%C4%B0n%C3%B6n%C3%BC_Stadium" title="BJK İnönü Stadium">BJK İnönü Stadium</a>, originally named the Dolmabahçe Stadium, was designed by Italian architect <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Vietti-Violi" title="Paolo Vietti-Violi">Paolo Vietti-Violi</a>, who collaborated with Turkish architects Şinasi Şahingiray and Fazıl Aysu for preparing the project. The first foundation was laid on May 19, 1939, but construction was halted due to the outbreak of the Second World War. The stadium was inaugurated on May 19, 1947, by <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0smet_%C4%B0n%C3%B6n%C3%BC" title="İsmet İnönü">İsmet İnönü</a>, the second President of Turkey and himself a Beşiktaş fan, and <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCtfi_K%C4%B1rdar" title="Lütfi Kırdar">Lütfi Kırdar</a>, the Governor of Istanbul.</p>
<p>The initial capacity was 16,000. In the original project plan, there were two bronze statues of athlete figures at the Eski Açık stand: one throwing a discus, and the other throwing a javelin. However, the statues were never built due to financial concerns. The oil factory which was found behind the stadium was demolished to construct the Yeni Açık stand at the west part of the stadium in 1950 (hence the name Yeni Açık, meaning the New Open-top; referring to the two covered stands (Numaralı and Kapalı) and the two open-top stands (Eski Açık and Yeni Açık).) In 1952, the stadium was renamed as the Mithat Paşa Stadium, and later in 1973, it was renamed as the İnönü Stadium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> A leasing contract was signed between Beşiktaş J.K. and the Ministry of Youth and Sports in February 1998 which gave all usage rights of the İnönü Stadium to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Be%C5%9Fikta%C5%9F_JK" title="Beşiktaş JK">Beşiktaş JK</a> for 49 years.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Football_Match">First Football Match</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vodafone_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First Football Match">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first football match at the Dolmabahçe Stadium took place between Beşiktaş J.K. and <a href="/wiki/AIK_Fotboll" title="AIK Fotboll">AIK Stockholm</a> of Sweden, on November 27, 1947, and the first goal in the stadium was scored by <a href="/wiki/S%C3%BCleyman_Seba" title="Süleyman Seba">Süleyman Seba</a>; the most famous and longest-presiding Chairman of the club (in the 1980s and 1990s) when he used to be a player of Beşiktaş JK. Beşiktaş lost this match 3–2.</p>
<p>Galatasaray S.K. and Fenerbahce S.K. shared the stadium with Beşiktaş JK for many years, before the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Ali_Sami_Yen_Stadium" title="Ali Sami Yen Stadium">Ali Sami Yen Stadium</a> for Galatasaray in 1964, and the renovation of the <a href="/wiki/%C5%9E%C3%BCkr%C3%BC_Saraco%C4%9Flu_Stadium" title="Şükrü Saracoğlu Stadium">Şükrü Saracoğlu Stadium</a> for Fenerbahçe S.K. in 1982.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renovation">Renovation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vodafone_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Renovation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Renovation work took place at the BJK İnönü Stadium in 2004. The tartan track was removed within the scope of these activities and the ground level was lowered by 4 metres to increase the capacity of the stadium to 32,145 spectators. The press seats were relocated to the Numaralı tribune from the Kapalı tribune. The lounges that were found in the middle of the Kapalı tribune were demolished. The number of gates was doubled. A press centre for Beşiktaş TV was built inside the stadium. The restrooms and food counters were renewed. The Yeni Açık stand was covered with a metallic structure. The stadium complies with UEFA standards.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Stadium_Project">New Stadium Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vodafone_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: New Stadium Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to the unique location of the <a href="/wiki/BJK_%C4%B0n%C3%B6n%C3%BC_Stadium" title="BJK İnönü Stadium">BJK İnönü Stadium</a>, which is considered as one of the best stadiums in the world,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and its legal status as a "historic monument" protected by the High Council of Monuments of Turkey, the renewal project had to be modified and postponed several times and all official requests, efforts, renewal attempts were denied. In 2008, former president Yildirim Demirören launched a project designed by HOK sports, with a design capacity of 42,000 spectators. However, this project was also cancelled because of concerns regarding how the new project didn't suit the historical environment of Beşiktaş. Finally, in 2013, current president Fikret Orman completed the administrative procedures after long extended bureaucratic exchanges, and got all the required permissions by proposing specific design goals.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="December_2016_bombing">December 2016 bombing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vodafone_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: December 2016 bombing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 10 December 2016, <a href="/wiki/December_2016_Istanbul_bombings" title="December 2016 Istanbul bombings">two bombs exploded</a> outside the stadium, killing at least 46 (38 police officer, 8 civilian) and injuring 136. The first and larger explosion took place about 7:30 p.m. after the home team Besiktas beat visitor Bursaspor 2-1 in the Turkish Super League. An official with knowledge of the attack said that the timing of the bombing aimed to maximize the loss of life.The civilian death toll was lower because fans had already left the newly built Vodafone Arena Stadium after the soccer match when the blasts occurred. Witnesses also heard gunfire after the explosions. Officials have said that the first explosion was caused by a passing vehicle that detonated in an area where police special forces were located at the stadium exit. A riot police bus appears to have been the target. Turkish officials said a person who had been stopped in nearby Macka Park committed suicide by triggering explosives moments later.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vodafone_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vodafone Arena was designed by the architect firm DB Architects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Bünyamin Derman served as the project <a href="/wiki/Architect_of_record" title="Architect of record">Architect of record</a>. The stadium meets <a href="/wiki/UEFA_stadium_categories" title="UEFA stadium categories">UEFA 4-category criteria</a>, the strictest in the ranking defined by UEFA regulations for stadium infrastructure.</p>
<p>Vodafone Arena differs from modern stadiums by 3 design goals imposed by the authority and nature of the construction site. Thanks to these design goals the architecture of the stadium reflects history, heritage and prosperity.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colosseum_.28Coliseum.29_Architecture">Colosseum (Coliseum) Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vodafone_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Colosseum (Coliseum) Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to existence of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dolmabah%C3%A7e" title="Dolmabahçe">Dolmabahçe Palace</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dolmabah%C3%A7e_Clock_Tower" title="Dolmabahçe Clock Tower">Dolmabahçe Clock Tower</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dolmabah%C3%A7e_Mosque" title="Dolmabahçe Mosque">Dolmabahçe Mosque</a> and of course the <a href="/wiki/Bosphorus" title="Bosphorus">Bosphorus</a>, which are very close to Vodafone Arena, the Turkish High Council of Monuments demanded an ancient look which should fit the surrounding historical area. Most of the proposed architectural designs for new stadium were modern structures, with glossy extern revetment made from metal, glass or composite materials. Hence to accomplish the first design goal, a <a href="/wiki/Colosseum" title="Colosseum">colosseum</a> architecture was proposed to and accepted by the high council. Colosseum architecture was previously used in stadiums such as the (<a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Memorial_Coliseum" title="Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum">Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Busch_Memorial_Stadium" title="Busch Memorial Stadium">Busch Memorial Stadium</a>, etc.), but mostly several decades ago.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Waveform_Stands">Waveform Stands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vodafone_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Waveform Stands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In modern stadiums the height of the stands are mostly at the same level. Since the construction site of Vodafone Arena is too narrow and surrounded by roads, the height of the stands decrease specifically at the corners and widen at the edges. The varying elevation creates a significant waveform-shape in the stands and gives a traditional look. The height of the east stand is very low which is another design goal imposed by the High Council so as not affect the silhouette of the <a href="/wiki/Bosphorus" title="Bosphorus">Bosphorus</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elliptical_Design">Elliptical Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vodafone_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Elliptical Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The design of the majority of modern football stadiums are mostly square, rectangular with rounded corners, or circular. The stadiums, which were built during the 20th century, include tartan tracks, especially Olympic stadiums. Due to the tartan tracks, the old stadiums have elliptical forms like the old Inönü stadium. To be faithful to the previous architecture which was designed in 1939 and coherent with the remaining historical two towers of the old Inönü stadium, Vodafone Arena also has an elliptical form, although there is no tartan track inside the stadium. Proposed design repeats the same idea of perfect symmetry reached in 1939 by extending the main arc between the two old towers through all structure.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vodafone_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_Compatibility">Environmental Compatibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vodafone_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Environmental Compatibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The new stadium project aimed to ensure a low environmental impact to the nature and its surroundings via the use of advanced state of the art sustainable technologies. This stadium will be constructed to reduce energy consumption from non-renewable energy sources by reducing waste and optimizing the resources available.The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_Building" title="Green Building">Green Building</a> concept of Vodafone Arena ensures environment friendly and resource efficient processes at each stage of construction, right from site selection and designing to construction by using Green Building certified construction materials, operation followed by maintenance, renovation or even demolition to seek minimum possible impact on environment.</p>
<p>The stadium will produce the electricity it needs using solar energy captured through photovoltaic panels<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> which can produce 500 kVA per year. This corresponds to an annual electricity consumption of about 100 average households and a CO2 saving of around 250 tonnes. The stadium will store rainwater in cisterns to use later for watering the pitch and other purposes. These alternative energy sources are aimed at helping stadium meet the criteria dictated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kyoto_protocol" title="Kyoto protocol">Kyoto protocol</a> by generating multiple results:</p>
<ul>
<li>Intensive exploitation of solar energy through solar tracker tools</li>
<li>Reductions of greenhouse gases<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>No production of chemical or acoustic emissions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>No air pollution</li>
<li>No risk of fire</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting" title="Rainwater harvesting">Rainwater harvesting</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>All concrete and metal parts from the old demolished BJK Inönü Stadium have been separated and reused; other materials left have been divided into categories, in order to be recycled, resold, or reused.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_Monument">Historical Monument</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vodafone_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Historical Monument">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The stands behind the goal areas were called <i>Eski Açık</i> (Old Tribune) with a <a href="/wiki/Seating_capacity" title="Seating capacity">seating capacity</a> of 7,962. There are two towers on the left and right side of the <i>Eski Açık</i> stands and an iron gate in the middle of 2 towers. The towers and the gate were preserved as a hictorical monument during the construction of the new site.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_Rights">Naming Rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vodafone_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Naming Rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Beşiktaş signed a $145 million deal with <a href="/wiki/Vodafone" title="Vodafone">Vodafone</a> on 21 August 2013. The deal includes shirt sponsorship for 3 (+2 optional) years, stadium naming rights (to be named 'Vodafone Arena'), advertising, and stadium technology infrastructure investment rights for 10 (+5 optional) years.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roof">Roof</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vodafone_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Roof">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The lightweight cable roof is designed as a classic looped cable roof containing one compression and one tension ring. While the majority of the roof is covered with a membrane, the inner edge has a glass roof. The roof of Vodafone Arena is composed of 260 tons of 246 rope assemblies. The inner tension ring, which is made of 8 Ø110 mm full locked coil ropes with a single length of nearly 200 meters, is connected to the outer compression ring by radial cables that consist of full locked coil ropes with Ø110, Ø85, Ø65 and Ø45 mm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<li>The stadium hosted the heavy metal <a href="/wiki/Sonisphere_Festival" title="Sonisphere Festival">Sonisphere Festival</a> in 2010, featuring the "Big Four" of thrash metal: <a href="/wiki/Metallica" title="Metallica">Metallica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slayer" title="Slayer">Slayer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Megadeth" title="Megadeth">Megadeth</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anthrax_(American_band)" title="Anthrax (American band)">Anthrax</a>, in addition to <a href="/wiki/Manowar" title="Manowar">Manowar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rammstein" title="Rammstein">Rammstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Chains" title="Alice in Chains">Alice in Chains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Accept_(band)" title="Accept (band)">Accept</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stone_Sour" title="Stone Sour">Stone Sour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volbeat" title="Volbeat">Volbeat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pentagram_(band)" title="Pentagram (band)">Pentagram</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hayko_Cepkin" title="Hayko Cepkin">Hayko Cepkin</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rihanna" title="Rihanna">Rihanna</a> performed as part of the <a href="/wiki/Diamonds_World_Tour" title="Diamonds World Tour">Diamonds World Tour</a> on 30 May 2013.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Iron_Maiden" title="Iron Maiden">Iron Maiden</a> performed as part of the <a href="/wiki/Maiden_England_World_Tour" title="Maiden England World Tour">Maiden England World Tour</a> on 26 July 2013.</li>
</ul>

<p>The opening ceremony of the stadium was held on 11 April 2016,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> with a Süper Lig match against <a href="/wiki/Bursaspor" title="Bursaspor">Bursaspor</a>. Beşiktaş fans walked though the wooded road down the red carpet laid between Beşiktaş and <a href="/wiki/Kabata%C5%9F,_Istanbul" title="Kabataş, Istanbul">Kabataş</a>; Fans had been launching flares since 16:00 in the Beşiktaş district, well before the opening ceremony. The game ended 3–2 with two goals from <a href="/wiki/Mario_G%C3%B3mez" title="Mario Gómez">Mario Gómez</a> and one from <a href="/wiki/Alexis_(footballer)" title="Alexis (footballer)">Alexis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In the 22nd minute of the game, during an attack initiated by <a href="/wiki/O%C4%9Fuzhan_%C3%96zyakup" title="Oğuzhan Özyakup">Oğuzhan Özyakup</a>, the ball met <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Sosa" title="José Sosa">José Sosa</a>, who sent a fine pass behind the defense outside the penalty area. Gómez controlled it in the penalty area, slipped away from goalkeeper <a href="/wiki/Harun_Tekin_(footballer)" title="Harun Tekin (footballer)">Harun Tekin</a> and scored the very first goal at Vodafone Arena.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport_Connections">Transport Connections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vodafone_Arena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Transport Connections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vodafone Arena is situated in the heart of the city centre surrounded by many transport facilities. A 5-minute walk is enough to reach to the stadium from <a href="/wiki/Taksim_Square" title="Taksim Square">Taksim</a> square which is the most important transportation hub of Istanbul served by many bus lines, metro (M2) and the Kabataş-Taksim Funicular (F1). The Kabataş tram (T1) can also be used to plan your journey to Vodafone Arena. Sea transport is also available for visitors who want to arrive from the Anatolian Side of Istanbul by using Beşiktaş-Kadıköy, Beşiktaş-Üsküdar ferries or Bostanci-Kabataş Fast Ferry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
