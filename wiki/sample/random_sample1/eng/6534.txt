<p><b>Bodo Ramelow</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[ˈboːdoː ˈʁaməloː]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata"> (<a href="/wiki/File:De-Bodo_Ramelow.ogg" title="File:De-Bodo Ramelow.ogg"><img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a> <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/10/De-Bodo_Ramelow.ogg" title="De-Bodo Ramelow.ogg">listen</a>)</small>; born 16 February 1956 in <a href="/wiki/Osterholz-Scharmbeck" title="Osterholz-Scharmbeck">Osterholz-Scharmbeck</a>) is a <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> politician of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left_Party_(Germany)" title="Left Party (Germany)">Left Party</a> who has been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister_President" title="Minister President">Minister President</a> of <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a> since 2014. Previously he was chairman of his party's group in the <a href="/wiki/Landtag_of_Thuringia" title="Landtag of Thuringia">Landtag of Thuringia</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_career">Political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodo_Ramelow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ramelow was born and raised in <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a>. He is a trained retail salesman and became an official in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gewerkschaft_Handel,_Banken_und_Versicherungen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gewerkschaft Handel, Banken und Versicherungen (page does not exist)">HBV</a>, the union for trade, bank and insurance employees during the 1980s. He moved to Thuringia, in former <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a>, after the unification of Germany in 1990. There he joined the post-Communist <a href="/wiki/Party_of_Democratic_Socialism_(Germany)" title="Party of Democratic Socialism (Germany)">Party of Democratic Socialism</a> (PDS). He was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Landtag_of_Thuringia" title="Landtag of Thuringia">Landtag of Thuringia</a> in 1999. He became deputy chairman and in 2001 chairman of the party's parliamentary group in the Landtag (state parliament).</p>
<p>In February 2004, Ramelow was elected top candidate of the PDS in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thuringia_state_election,_2004" title="Thuringia state election, 2004">Thuringian state elections</a>. In June 2004 the party gained its best result in Thuringia since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_unification" title="German unification">German unification</a> with 26.1% of the votes. Ramelow was re-elected as the PDS chairman in Thuringia.</p>
<p>Starting in June 2005, Ramelow was chief negotiator during unification talks between the PDS and <a href="/wiki/Labour_and_Social_Justice_%E2%80%93_The_Electoral_Alternative" title="Labour and Social Justice – The Electoral Alternative">WASG</a>, a unification that resulted in the new party <a href="/wiki/The_Left_(Germany)" title="The Left (Germany)">The Left</a>. In September 2005 he was elected deputy chairman of The Left in the Bundestag. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thuringia_state_election,_2009" title="Thuringia state election, 2009">Thuringia state election in September 2009</a> he led <a href="/wiki/The_Left_(Germany)" title="The Left (Germany)">The Left</a> to become the second biggest party with 27.4% of the votes, making him a competitor for the post of minister-president.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Illegal_observation_by_the_Verfassungsschutz">Illegal observation by the <i>Verfassungsschutz</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodo_Ramelow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Illegal observation by the Verfassungsschutz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2003 it became publicly known that Germany's domestic intelligence service, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bundesamt_f%C3%BCr_Verfassungsschutz" title="Bundesamt für Verfassungsschutz">Bundesamt für Verfassungsschutz</a>, had been observing Ramelow and had opened a file on him because of his alleged contacts with the <a href="/wiki/German_Communist_Party" title="German Communist Party">German Communist Party</a> (DKP) during the 1980s. Supposedly the observation had stopped following Ramelow's entry into the Landtag in 1999, but in May 2006 the <a href="/wiki/Administrative_court" title="Administrative court">Administrative court</a> of <a href="/wiki/Weimar" title="Weimar">Weimar</a> decreed that the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Th%C3%BCringer_Landesamt_f%C3%BCr_Verfassungsschutz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thüringer Landesamt für Verfassungsschutz (page does not exist)">Thuringian state Verfassungsschutz</a> had to reveal the file and the stored data. It became known that the federal Verfassungsschutz had observed Ramelow for many years. Ramelow sued the authorities, but in 2010 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Administrative_Court_of_Germany" title="Federal Administrative Court of Germany">Federal Administrative Court of Germany</a> ruled that the Verfassungsschutz is entitled to observe politicians of the Left Party due to "reasonable suspicion of anti-constitutional activity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>This ruling was overturned in 2013 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Constitutional_Court_of_Germany" title="Federal Constitutional Court of Germany">Federal Constitutional Court</a>, which decided that the monitoring had been illegal. It stated that monitoring lawmakers may be acceptable, but only in exceptional circumstances, "if there is an indication that a legislator has abused his or her mandate in the fight against the democratic constitutional order, or actively or aggressively fought against that order." The court found no grounds to suspect Ramelow, who is considered one of the more moderate voices within his party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The decision was widely seen as a major victory for Ramelow's party as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="State_premier_of_Thuringia">State premier of Thuringia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodo_Ramelow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: State premier of Thuringia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_Ramelow" title="Cabinet Ramelow">Cabinet Ramelow</a></div>
<p>Following <a href="/wiki/Thuringian_state_election,_2014" title="Thuringian state election, 2014">elections in September 2014</a>, Ramelow was elected by the Landtag as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister-President_of_Thuringia" title="Minister-President of Thuringia">Minister-President of Thuringia</a> on 5 December 2014 with the support of the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">Social Democratic Party</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" title="Alliance '90/The Greens">Greens</a>, which had joined the Left in a coalition. This vote, which Ramelow won in the second round, marked the first time the Left had won the leadership of any of Germany's states since the <a href="/wiki/Unification_of_Germany" title="Unification of Germany">unification of Germany</a> in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
