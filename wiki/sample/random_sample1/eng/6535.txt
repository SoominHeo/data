<p><b>Bodo</b> (बर' <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Tibetan" title="Help:IPA for Tibetan">[bɔɽo]</a></span>), or <b>Mech</b>, is the <a href="/wiki/Sino-Tibetan_languages" title="Sino-Tibetan languages">Sino-Tibetan language</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Bodo_people" title="Bodo people">Bodo people</a> of north-eastern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bengal" title="Bengal">Bengal</a>. It is one of the official languages of the Indian state of <a href="/wiki/Assam" title="Assam">Assam</a>, and is one of the <a href="/wiki/Languages_with_official_status_in_India#Eighth_Schedule_to_the_Constitution" title="Languages with official status in India">22 scheduled languages</a> that is given a special <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_India" title="Constitution of India">constitutional</a> status in India. Bodo language is written using <a href="/wiki/Devanagari" title="Devanagari">Devanagari</a> script since 1963. It was formerly written using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assamese_script" title="Assamese script">Assamese script</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_linguistic_classification">History and linguistic classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and linguistic classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bodo is a <a href="/wiki/Sino-Tibetan_languages" title="Sino-Tibetan languages">Sino-Tibetan language</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Bodo%E2%80%93Garo_languages" title="Bodo–Garo languages">Bodo group</a>. It is closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Dimasa_language" title="Dimasa language">Dimasa language</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tiwa_language_(India)" title="Tiwa language (India)">Tiwa (Lalung) Language</a> of Assam, the <a href="/wiki/Garo_language" title="Garo language">Garo language</a> of <a href="/wiki/Meghalaya" title="Meghalaya">Meghalaya</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kokborok_language" title="Kokborok language">Kokborok language</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tripura" title="Tripura">Tripura</a>. The Bodo speaking areas of Assam stretch from <a href="/wiki/Dhubri" title="Dhubri">Dhubri</a> in the west to <a href="/wiki/Sadiya" title="Sadiya">Sadiya</a> in the east. In <a href="/wiki/Alipurduar" title="Alipurduar">Alipurduar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cooch_Behar" title="Cooch Behar">Cooch Behar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jalpaiguri" title="Jalpaiguri">Jalpaiguri</a> and other adjacent districts of Bengal, the Boros are known as "Mech". The population of Boro speakers according to 1991 census report was 1,984,569 (Bodo 1,324,748), (<a href="/wiki/Mech_tribe" title="Mech tribe">Mech</a> 659,821). The census reports of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bodo_tribe" title="Bodo tribe">Bodo tribe</a>, however, comprises only the Bodos, excluding Mech tribe. The word Boro denotes the language and the community and it is pronounced with a high tone on the second syllable.</p>
<p>The dialects spoken in this area could be broadly sub-divided into three main groups:</p>
<ol>
<li>The Western Boro dialect, {(Sønabari) WBD}:</li>
<li>The Eastern Boro dialect, {(Sanzari) EBD} and</li>
<li>The Southern Boro dialect, {(Hazari) SBD}.</li>
</ol>
<p>The Western Boro dialects are spoken in the districts of Kokrajhar, Chirang, Baksa and Bongaigaon and the Eastern Bodo dialects are found mainly in the districts of Barpeta, Nalbari and Kamrup and some parts of Darrang as well. It is worthwhile to mention that the Western Boro dialect has gained the status of Standard Dialect and has developed a written form as well. The variations between these two dialect groups are mainly phonological and lexical.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_Grants_Commission_(India)" title="University Grants Commission (India)">University Grants Commission</a> has included Bodo as subject in UPSC and <a href="/wiki/National_Eligibility_Test" title="National Eligibility Test">NET</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> examination.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_beginning">The beginning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The beginning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the Bodo language is a rich and ancient language, it did not have written literature until the second decade of the twentieth century. Christian missionaries, who entered the Bodo speaking areas with an intent to preach their religion, published some books on religion, tales, rhymes and songs. These missionaries also published some books on grammar and dictionary. Reverend <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sidney_Endle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sidney Endle (page does not exist)">Sidney Endle</a> compiled <i>An Outline of the Kachari Grammar</i> in 1884. The grammar is based on the dialect of Darrang district. Endle also wrote an important monograph on the Bodos. The monograph is entitled <i>The Kacharis</i>. The book was published in 1911 and it contains chapters on social customs, agriculture practices, festivities, food habits, life cycle rituals, crafts and textiles of the Bodos. The book has also incorporated specimens of Bodo folktales, rhymes and grammars. J.D. Anderson's <i>Collection of Bodo Folktales and Rhymes</i> (1895) incorporated seventeen Bodo folktales translated into English, besides the original versions in Bodo language.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the aftermath of socio-political awakening and movement launched by the Bodo organisations since 1913, the language was introduced as the medium of instruction (1963) in the primary schools in Bodo dominated areas. The Bodo language serves as a medium of instruction up to the secondary level and an associated official language in the state of Assam. The language has attained a position of pride with the opening of the post-graduate course in Bodo language and literature in the <a href="/wiki/Gauhati_University" title="Gauhati University">University of Guwahati</a> in 1996. The Bodo language has to its credit large number of books of poetry, drama, short stories, novels, biography, travelogues, children's literature and literary criticism. Though the spoken language has been affected by other communities, especially the Assamese, in and around Kokrajhar, it is still to be heard in its pure form, in and around Udalguri district.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_system">Writing system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Writing system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The language is officially written using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Devanagari_script" title="Devanagari script">Devanagari script</a>, although it was also historically written in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assamese_script" title="Assamese script">Assamese script</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin script</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Some researchers have suggested that the language used to use a now-lost script called <a href="/wiki/Deodhai_script" title="Deodhai script">Deodhai</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bodo_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-bodo-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>But there is a difference in using the letters in Bodo than the Devanagari. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishnuprasad_Rabha" title="Bishnuprasad Rabha">Kalaguru Bishnu Prasad Rabha</a> gathered a few specimen of the Deodhai alphabet from an informant of <a href="/wiki/Dimapur" title="Dimapur">Dimapur</a> area which was noted for the <a href="/wiki/Kachari_Kingdom" title="Kachari Kingdom">Kachari reign</a> and remains representing the art and architecture.</p>
