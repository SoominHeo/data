<p>A <b>board game</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Tabletop_game" title="Tabletop game">tabletop game</a> that involves counters or <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_board_games#piece" title="Glossary of board games"><span class="" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of board games § piece">pieces</span></a></dfn> moved or placed on a pre-marked surface or "board", according to a set of rules. Some games are based on pure <a href="/wiki/Strategy_game" title="Strategy game">strategy</a>, but many contain an element of <a href="/wiki/Game_of_chance" title="Game of chance">chance</a>; and some are purely chance, with no element of skill.</p>
<p>Games usually have a goal that a player aims to achieve. Early board games represented a battle between two armies, and most modern board games are still based on defeating opposing players in terms of counters, winning position, or accrual of points.</p>
<p>There are many varieties of board games. Their representation of real-life situations can range from having no inherent theme, like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Checkers" title="Checkers">checkers</a>, to having a specific theme and narrative, like <i><a href="/wiki/Cluedo" title="Cluedo">Cluedo</a></i>. Rules can range from the very simple, like <a href="/wiki/Tic-tac-toe" title="Tic-tac-toe">Tic-tac-toe</a>, to those describing a game universe in great detail, like <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></i> – although most of the latter are <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_game" title="Role-playing game">role-playing games</a> where the board is secondary to the game, serving to help visualize the game <a href="/wiki/Scenario" title="Scenario">scenario</a>.</p>
<p>The time required to learn to play or master a game varies greatly from game to game, but is not necessarily correlated with the number or complexity of rules; games like <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Go_(board_game)" title="Go (board game)">Go</a> possess relatively simple rulesets, but have great strategic depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>Board games have been played in most cultures and societies throughout history. A number of important historical sites, artifacts, and documents shed light on early board games such as:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jiroft_civilization" title="Jiroft civilization">Jiroft civilization</a> gameboards<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Senet" title="Senet">Senet</a>, found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predynastic_Egypt" title="Predynastic Egypt">Predynastic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_dynasty_of_Egypt" title="First dynasty of Egypt">First Dynasty</a> burials of Egypt, c. 3500 BC and 3100 BC respectively;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-senet1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-senet1-3">[3]</a></sup> the oldest board game known to have existed, Senet was pictured in a <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">fresco</a> found in Merknera's tomb (3300–2700 BC)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-senet2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-senet2-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pivotto_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pivotto-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mehen_(game)" title="Mehen (game)">Mehen</a>, also from Predynastic Egypt</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Go_(board_game)" title="Go (board game)">Go</a>, originating in China</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Board_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In seventeenth and eighteenth century colonial America, the agrarian life of the country left little time for game playing though <a href="/wiki/Draughts" title="Draughts">draughts</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Checkers" title="Checkers">checkers</a>), <a href="/wiki/Bowling" title="Bowling">bowling</a>, and card games were not unknown. The Pilgrims and Puritans of New England frowned on game playing and viewed dice as instruments of the devil. When the Governor William Bradford discovered a group of non-Puritans playing stool-ball, pitching the bar, and pursuing other sports in the streets on Christmas Day, 1622, he confiscated their implements, reprimanded them, and told them their devotion for the day should be confined to their homes.</p>
<p>In <i>Thoughts on Lotteries</i> (1826) <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a> wrote:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Almost all these pursuits of chance [i.e., of human industry] produce something useful to society. But there are some which produce nothing, and endanger the well-being of the individuals engaged in them or of others depending on them. Such are games with cards, dice, billiards, etc. And although the pursuit of them is a matter of natural right, yet society, perceiving the irresistible bent of some of its members to pursue them, and the ruin produced by them to the families depending on these individuals, consider it as a case of insanity, quoad hoc, step in to protect the family and the party himself, as in other cases of insanity, infancy, imbecility, etc., and suppress the pursuit altogether, and the natural right of following it. There are some other games of chance, useful on certain occasions, and injurious only when carried beyond their useful bounds. Such are insurances, lotteries, raffles, etc. These they do not suppress, but take their regulation under their own discretion.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The board game <i>Traveller's Tour Through the United States</i> and its sister game <i>Traveller’s Tour Through Europe</i> were published by New York City bookseller <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=F._%26_R._Lockwood&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="F. &amp; R. Lockwood (page does not exist)">F. &amp; R. Lockwood</a> in 1822 and today claims the distinction of being the first board game published in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fv_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-fv-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the United States shifted from agrarian to urban living in the nineteenth century, greater leisure time and a rise in income became available to the middle class. The American home, once the center of economic production, became the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/locus" title="wikt:locus">locus</a> of entertainment, enlightenment, and education under the supervision of mothers. Children were encouraged to play board games that developed literacy skills and provided moral instruction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jensen_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jensen-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FirstAmericanPrintrunOfThe_MansionOfHappiness.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="992" data-file-width="1200" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/FirstAmericanPrintrunOfThe_MansionOfHappiness.jpg/220px-FirstAmericanPrintrunOfThe_MansionOfHappiness.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/FirstAmericanPrintrunOfThe_MansionOfHappiness.jpg/330px-FirstAmericanPrintrunOfThe_MansionOfHappiness.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/FirstAmericanPrintrunOfThe_MansionOfHappiness.jpg/440px-FirstAmericanPrintrunOfThe_MansionOfHappiness.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FirstAmericanPrintrunOfThe_MansionOfHappiness.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>The Mansion of Happiness</i> (1843)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The earliest board games published in the United States were based upon Christian morality. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mansion_of_Happiness" title="The Mansion of Happiness">The Mansion of Happiness</a></i> (1843), for example, sent players along a path of virtues and vices that led to the Mansion of Happiness (Heaven).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jensen_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jensen-7">[7]</a></sup> <i>The Game of Pope or Pagan, or The Siege of the Stronghold of Satan by the Christian Army</i> (1844) pitted an image on its board of a <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> woman committing <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suttee" title="Suttee">suttee</a></i> against missionaries landing on a foreign shore. The missionaries are cast in white as "the symbol of innocence, temperance, and hope" while the pope and pagan are cast in black, the color of "gloom of error, and...grief at the daily loss of empire".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Commercially produced board games in the mid-nineteenth century were monochrome prints laboriously hand-colored by teams of low paid young factory women. Advances in paper making and printmaking during the period enabled the commercial production of relatively inexpensive board games. The most significant advance was the development of <a href="/wiki/Chromolithography" title="Chromolithography">chromolithography</a>, a technological achievement that made bold, richly colored images available at affordable prices. Games cost as little as US$.25 for a small boxed card game to $3.00 for more elaborate games.</p>
<p>American Protestants believed a virtuous life led to success, but the belief was challenged mid-century when Americans embraced materialism and capitalism. The accumulation of material goods was viewed as a divine blessing. In 1860, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Checkered_Game_of_Life" title="The Checkered Game of Life">The Checkered Game of Life</a></i> rewarded players for mundane activities such as attending college, marrying, and getting rich. Daily life rather than eternal life became the focus of board games. The game was the first to focus on secular virtues rather than religious virtues,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jensen_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jensen-7">[7]</a></sup> and sold 40,000 copies its first year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hofer_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hofer-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:District_Messenger_Boy_Box_Cover_1886.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="282" height="301" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/District_Messenger_Boy_Box_Cover_1886.jpg/170px-District_Messenger_Boy_Box_Cover_1886.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/District_Messenger_Boy_Box_Cover_1886.jpg/255px-District_Messenger_Boy_Box_Cover_1886.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/42/District_Messenger_Boy_Box_Cover_1886.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:District_Messenger_Boy_Box_Cover_1886.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Game of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telegraph_boys" title="Telegraph boys">District Messenger Boy</a></i> (1886)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Game of the District Messenger Boy, or Merit Rewarded</i>, published in 1886 by the New York City firm of <a href="/wiki/McLoughlin_Brothers" title="McLoughlin Brothers">McLoughlin Brothers</a>, was one of the first board games based on materialism and capitalism published in the United States. The game is a typical <a href="/wiki/Category:Roll-and-move_board_games" title="Category:Roll-and-move board games">roll-and-move</a> track board game. Players move their tokens along the track at the spin of the arrow toward the goal at track's end. Some spaces on the track will advance the player while others will send him back.</p>
<p>In the affluent 1880s, Americans witnessed the publication of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horatio_Alger,_Jr." title="Horatio Alger, Jr.">Algeresque</a> <a href="/wiki/Rags_to_riches" title="Rags to riches">rags to riches</a> games that permitted players to emulate the capitalist heroes of the age. One of the first such games, <i>The Game of the District Messenger Boy</i>, encouraged the idea that the lowliest messenger boy could ascend the corporate ladder to its topmost rung. Such games insinuated that the accumulation of wealth brought increased social status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jensen_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Jensen-7">[7]</a></sup> Competitive capitalistic games culminated in 1935 with <i><a href="/wiki/Monopoly_(game)" title="Monopoly (game)">Monopoly</a></i>, the most commercially successful board game in United States history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>McLoughlin Brothers published similar games based on the telegraph boy theme including <i>Game of the Telegraph Boy, or Merit Rewarded</i> (1888). Greg Downey notes in his essay, "Information Networks and Urban Spaces: The Case of the Telegraph Messenger Boy" that families who could afford the deluxe version of the game in its <a href="/wiki/Chromolithography" title="Chromolithography">chromolithographed</a>, wood-sided box would not "have sent their sons out for such a rough apprenticeship in the working world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Margaret_Hofer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Margaret Hofer (page does not exist)">Margaret Hofer</a> described the period of 1880s–1920s as "The Golden Age" of board gaming in America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fv_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-fv-6">[6]</a></sup> Board games popularity was boosted, like that of many items, through <a href="/wiki/Mass_production" title="Mass production">mass production</a>, which made them cheaper and more easily available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker2014_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walker2014-12">[12]</a></sup> Although there are no detailed statistics, some scholars suggest that the 20th century saw a decline in the popularity of the hobby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker2014_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Walker2014-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Board_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The late 1990s onwards have seen substantial growth in the reach and market of board games. This has been attributed to, among other factors, the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>, which has made it easier for people to find out about games and to find opponents to play against.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker2014_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Walker2014-12">[12]</a></sup> Around the year 2000 the board gaming industry began significant growth with companies producing a rising number of new games to be sold to a growing worldwide audience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-board_game_resurgence_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-board_game_resurgence-13">[13]</a></sup> In the 2010s, a number of publications referred to the board games as having a new Golden Age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-board_game_resurgence_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-board_game_resurgence-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian2014oct_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian2014oct-14">[14]</a></sup> Board game venues are also growing in popularity; for example the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Board_game_cafe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Board game cafe (page does not exist)">board game cafes</a> are reported to be very popular in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Luck.2C_strategy.2C_and_diplomacy">Luck, strategy, and diplomacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Board_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Luck, strategy, and diplomacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some games, such as chess, depend completely on player skill, while many children's games such as <i><a href="/wiki/Candy_Land" title="Candy Land">Candy Land</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Snakes_and_Ladders" title="Snakes and Ladders">Snakes and Ladders</a></i> require no decisions by the players and are decided purely by luck.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many games require some level of both skill and luck. A player may be hampered by bad luck in <a href="/wiki/Backgammon" title="Backgammon">backgammon</a>, <i>Monopoly</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Risk_(game)" title="Risk (game)">Risk</a></i>; but over many games a skilled player will win more often<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and the elements of luck can make for more excitement, and more diverse and multifaceted strategies, as concepts such as <a href="/wiki/Expected_value" title="Expected value">expected value</a> and <a href="/wiki/Risk_management" title="Risk management">risk management</a> must be considered.</p>
<p>Luck may be introduced into a game by a number of methods. The use of <a href="/wiki/Dice" title="Dice">dice</a> of various sorts goes back to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Game_of_Ur" title="Royal Game of Ur">earliest board games</a>. These can decide everything from how many steps a player moves their token, as in <i>Monopoly</i>, to how their forces fare in battle, as in <i>Risk</i>, or which resources a player gains, as in <i>The Settlers of Catan</i>. Other games such as <i><a href="/wiki/Sorry!_(game)" title="Sorry! (game)">Sorry!</a></i> use a deck of special <a href="/wiki/Playing_card" title="Playing card">cards</a> that, when shuffled, create randomness. <i><a href="/wiki/Scrabble" title="Scrabble">Scrabble</a></i> does something similar with randomly picked letters. Other games use spinners, timers of random length, or other sources of randomness. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German-style_board_game" title="German-style board game">German-style board games</a> are notable for often having less luck element than many North American board games.</p>
<p>Another important aspect of some games is <a href="/wiki/Diplomacy" title="Diplomacy">diplomacy</a>, that is, players making deals with one another. Negotiation generally features only in games with three or more players, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cooperative_games" title="Cooperative games">cooperative games</a> being the exception. An important facet of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Settlers_of_Catan" title="The Settlers of Catan">The Settlers of Catan</a></i>, for example, is convincing players to trade with you rather than with opponents. In <i>Risk</i>, two or more players may team up against others. <i>Easy</i> diplomacy involves convincing other players that someone else is winning and should therefore be teamed up against. <i>Advanced</i> diplomacy (e.g. in the aptly named game <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diplomacy_(board_game)" title="Diplomacy (board game)">Diplomacy</a></i>) consists of making elaborate plans together, with the possibility of betrayal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a <i><a href="/wiki/Perfect_information" title="Perfect information">perfect information</a></i> games like chess each player has complete information on the state of the game, but in other games, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Tigris_and_Euphrates" title="Tigris and Euphrates">Tigris and Euphrates</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Stratego" title="Stratego">Stratego</a></i>, some information is hidden from players. This makes finding the best move more difficult, and may involve estimating probabilities by the opponents.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Board_games_and_other_media">Board games and other media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Board_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Board games and other media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many board games are now available as <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video games</a>, which can include the computer itself as one of several players, or as a sole opponent. Many board games can now be played <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">online</a> against a computer and/or other players. Some <a href="/wiki/Website" title="Website">websites</a> allow play in <a href="/wiki/Real_time_(media)" title="Real time (media)">real time</a> and immediately show the opponents' moves, while others use <a href="/wiki/Email" title="Email">email</a> to notify the players after each move.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chessbyemail_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-chessbyemail-19">[19]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">internet</a> and cheaper home printing has also influenced board games via print-and-play games that may be purchased and printed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-printplay_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-printplay-20">[20]</a></sup> Some games use external media such as audio cassettes or <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVDs</a> in accompaniment to the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvdgames_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-dvdgames-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cassettegames_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-cassettegames-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Market">Market</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Board_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Market">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

The modern German board game <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Settlers_of_Catan" title="The Settlers of Catan">The Settlers of Catan</a></i> is printed in 30 languages and sold 15 million by 2009.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>While the board gaming market is estimated to be smaller than that for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_games" title="Video games">video games</a>, it has also experienced significant growth from the late 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian2014oct_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-guardian2014oct-14">[14]</a></sup> A 2012 article in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> described board games as "making a comeback".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Another from 2014 gave an estimate that put the growth of the board game market at "between 25% and 40% annually" since 2010, and described the current time as the "golden era for board games".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian2014oct_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-guardian2014oct-14">[14]</a></sup> The rise in board game popularity has been attributed to quality improvement (more elegant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Game_mechanic" title="Game mechanic">mechanics</a> and graphics) as well as increased availability thanks to sales through the Internet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian2014oct_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-guardian2014oct-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 1991 estimate for the global board game market was over $1.2 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BrowneBrowne2001_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-BrowneBrowne2001-24">[24]</a></sup> A 2001 estimate for the United States "board games and puzzle" market gave the value of under $400 million, and for United Kingdom, of about £50 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> A 2009 estimate for the Korean market was put at 800 million won,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and another estimate for the American board game market for the same year was at about $800 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> A 2011 estimate for the Chinese board game market was at over 10 billion <a href="/wiki/Chinese_yuan" title="Chinese yuan">yuan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> (Some estimates may split board games from collectible card, miniature and role-playing games; for example another 2014 estimate distinguishing board games from other types of hobby games gave the estimate for US and Canada market at only $75 million, with the total size of what it defined as the hobby game market at over $700 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> with a 2015 estimate suggesting a value of almost $900 million<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>) A 2013 estimate put the size of the German toy market at 2.7 billion euro (out of which, board games and puzzle market is worth about 375 million euro), and Polish markets, at 2 billion and 280 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Z%C5%82oty" title="Złoty">złoties</a>, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Per capita, in 2009 Germany was considered to be the best market, with the highest number of games sold per individual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_into_gaming">Research into gaming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Board_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Research into gaming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>A dedicated field of research into gaming exists, known as <a href="/wiki/Game_studies" title="Game studies">game studies</a> or ludology.</p>
<p>While there has been a fair amount of scientific research on the psychology of older board games (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Go_(board_game)" title="Go (board game)">Go</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mancala" title="Mancala">mancala</a>), less has been done on contemporary board games such as <i><a href="/wiki/Monopoly_(game)" title="Monopoly (game)">Monopoly</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Scrabble" title="Scrabble">Scrabble</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Risk_(game)" title="Risk (game)">Risk</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Much research has been carried out on chess, in part because many tournament players are publicly ranked in national and international lists, which makes it possible to compare their levels of expertise. The works of <a href="/wiki/Adriaan_de_Groot" title="Adriaan de Groot">Adriaan de Groot</a>, William Chase, <a href="/wiki/Herbert_A._Simon" title="Herbert A. Simon">Herbert A. Simon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fernand_Gobet" title="Fernand Gobet">Fernand Gobet</a> have established that knowledge, more than the ability to anticipate moves, plays an essential role in chess-playing.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Linearly arranged board games have been shown to improve children's spatial numerical understanding. This is because the game is similar to a <a href="/wiki/Number_line" title="Number line">number line</a> in that they promote a linear understanding of numbers rather than the innate logarithmic one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Research studies show that board games such as Chutes and Ladders result in children showing significant improvements in aspects of basic number skills such as counting, recognizing numbers, numerical estimation and number comprehension. They also practice fine motor skills each time they grasp a game piece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Playing board games has also been tied to improving children's <a href="/wiki/Executive_functions" title="Executive functions">executive functions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Additionally, board games can be therapeutic. <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Barrymore_Halpenny" title="Bruce Barrymore Halpenny">Bruce Halpenny</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Games_inventor" title="Games inventor">games inventor</a> said when interviewed about his game, <a href="/wiki/The_Great_Train_Robbery_(board_game)" title="The Great Train Robbery (board game)"><i>The Great Train Robbery</i></a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>With crime you deal with every basic human emotion and also have enough elements to combine action with melodrama. The player’s imagination is fired as they plan to rob the train. Because of the gamble they take in the early stage of the game there is a build up of tension, which is immediately released once the train is robbed. Release of tension is therapeutic and useful in our society, because most jobs are boring and repetitive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TRN1976_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-TRN1976-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Playing games have been suggested as a viable addition to traditional educational curriculum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Categories">Categories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Board_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Categories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are a number of ways in which board games can be classified,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fv_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-fv-6">[6]</a></sup> and considerable overlap may exist, so that a game belong in several categories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fv_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-fv-6">[6]</a></sup> The following is a list of some of the most common:</p>
<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 25em; -webkit-column-width: 25em; column-width: 25em;">
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Abstract_strategy_game" title="Abstract strategy game">Abstract strategy games</a> – e.g. chess, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Checkers" title="Checkers">checkers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Go_(board_game)" title="Go (board game)">Go</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reversi" title="Reversi">Reversi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tafl_games" title="Tafl games">tafl games</a>, or modern games such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abalone_(game)" title="Abalone (game)">Abalone</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Halma" title="Halma">Halma</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Stratego" title="Stratego">Stratego</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hive_(game)" title="Hive (game)">Hive</a></i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/GIPF_(game)" title="GIPF (game)">GIPF</a></i></li>
<li>Alignment games – e.g. <a href="/wiki/Renju" title="Renju">Renju</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gomoku" title="Gomoku">Gomoku</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connect6" title="Connect6">Connect6</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nine_Men%27s_Morris" title="Nine Men's Morris">Nine Men's Morris</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Tic-tac-toe" title="Tic-tac-toe">Tic-tac-toe</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Category:Auction_board_games" title="Category:Auction board games">Auction games</a> – e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Hoity_Toity" title="Hoity Toity">Hoity Toity</a></i></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chess_variant" title="Chess variant">Chess variants</a> – traditional variants e.g. <a href="/wiki/Shogi" title="Shogi">shogi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xiangqi" title="Xiangqi">xiangqi</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Janggi" title="Janggi">janggi</a>; modern variants e.g. <a href="/wiki/Chess960" title="Chess960">Chess960</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_chess" title="Grand chess">Grand chess</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hexagonal_chess" title="Hexagonal chess">Hexagonal chess</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Alice_chess" title="Alice chess">Alice chess</a></li>
<li>Configuration games – e.g. <a href="/wiki/Lines_of_Action" title="Lines of Action">Lines of Action</a>, Hexade, or <i><a href="/wiki/Entropy_(1977_board_game)" title="Entropy (1977 board game)">Entropy</a></i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Connection_game" title="Connection game">Connection games</a> – e.g. <a href="/wiki/TwixT" title="TwixT">TwixT</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hex_(board_game)" title="Hex (board game)">Hex</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Havannah" title="Havannah">Havannah</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cooperative_board_game" title="Cooperative board game">Cooperative games</a> – e.g. <i>Max the Cat</i>, <i>Caves and Claws</i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Pandemic_(board_game)" title="Pandemic (board game)">Pandemic</a></i></li>
<li>Count and capture games – e.g. <a href="/wiki/Mancala" title="Mancala">mancala</a> games</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cross_and_circle_game" title="Cross and circle game">Cross and circle games</a> – e.g. <a href="/wiki/Yut" title="Yut">Yut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ludo_(board_game)" title="Ludo (board game)">Ludo</a>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Aggravation_(board_game)" title="Aggravation (board game)">Aggravation</a></i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Category:Deduction_board_games" title="Category:Deduction board games">Deduction games</a> – e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Mastermind_(board_game)" title="Mastermind (board game)">Mastermind</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Box_(game)" title="Black Box (game)">Black Box</a></i></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dexterity" title="Dexterity">Dexterity</a> games – e.g. <i>Tumblin' Dice</i> or <i>Pitch Car</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Category:Economic_simulation_board_games" title="Category:Economic simulation board games">Economic simulation games</a> – e.g. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Business_Game" title="The Business Game">The Business Game</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Monopoly_(game)" title="Monopoly (game)">Monopoly</a></i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/The_Game_of_Life" title="The Game of Life">The Game of Life</a></i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Educational_game" title="Educational game">Educational games</a> – e.g. <i>Arthur Saves the Planet</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cleopatra_and_the_Society_of_Architects" title="Cleopatra and the Society of Architects">Cleopatra and the Society of Architects</a></i>, or <i>Shakespeare: The Bard Game</i></li>
<li>Elimination games – e.g. <a href="/wiki/Draughts" title="Draughts">draughts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alquerque" title="Alquerque">Alquerque</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fanorona" title="Fanorona">Fanorona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yot%C3%A9" title="Yoté">Yoté</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Surakarta_(game)" title="Surakarta (game)">Surakarta</a></li>
<li>Family games – e.g. <i>Roll Through the Ages</i>, <i>Birds on a Wire</i>, or <i>For Sale</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Category:Fantasy_board_games" title="Category:Fantasy board games">Fantasy games</a> – e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Shadows_Over_Camelot" title="Shadows Over Camelot">Shadows Over Camelot</a></i></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German-style_board_game" title="German-style board game">German-style board games</a> or <i>Eurogames</i> – e.g. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Settlers_of_Catan" title="The Settlers of Catan">The Settlers of Catan</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Carcassonne_(board_game)" title="Carcassonne (board game)">Carcassonne</a></i>, Decatur • The Game, Carson City, or <i><a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_(board_game)" title="Puerto Rico (board game)">Puerto Rico</a></i></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guessing_game" title="Guessing game">Guessing games</a> – e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Pictionary" title="Pictionary">Pictionary</a></i> or <a href="/wiki/Battleship_(game)" title="Battleship (game)">Battleship</a></li>
<li>Hidden-role games – e.g. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mafia_(game)" title="Mafia (game)">Mafia</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/The_Resistance_(game)" title="The Resistance (game)">The Resistance</a></i></li>
<li>Historical simulation games – e.g. <i>Through the Ages</i> or <i>Railways of the World</i></li>
<li>Large multiplayer games – e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Take_It_Easy_(game)" title="Take It Easy (game)">Take It Easy</a></i> or <i>Swat</i> (2010)</li>
<li>Learning/communication non-competitive games – e.g. <a href="/wiki/The_Ungame" title="The Ungame">The Ungame</a> (1972)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_mancala_games" title="List of mancala games">Mancala games</a> – e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wari_(game)" title="Wari (game)">Wari</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oware" title="Oware">Oware</a>, or The Glass Bead Game</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Multiplayer_game" title="Multiplayer game">Multiplayer games</a> – e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Risk_(game)" title="Risk (game)">Risk</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Monopoly_(game)" title="Monopoly (game)">Monopoly</a></i>, or <a href="/wiki/Four-player_chess" title="Four-player chess">Four-player chess</a></li>
<li>Musical games – e.g. <i>Spontuneous</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Category:Negotiation_tabletop_games" title="Category:Negotiation tabletop games">Negotiation games</a> – e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Diplomacy_(game)" title="Diplomacy (game)">Diplomacy</a></i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Paper-and-pencil_game" title="Paper-and-pencil game">Paper-and-pencil games</a> – e.g. <a href="/wiki/Tic-tac-toe" title="Tic-tac-toe">Tic-tac-toe</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dots_and_Boxes" title="Dots and Boxes">Dots and Boxes</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Category:Board_games_of_physical_skill" title="Category:Board games of physical skill">Physical skill games</a> – e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Camp_Granada" title="Camp Granada">Camp Granada</a></i></li>
<li>Position games (no captures; win by leaving the opponent unable to move) – e.g. <a href="/wiki/Konane" title="Konane">Konane</a>, <a href="/wiki/M%C5%AB_t%C5%8Drere" title="Mū tōrere">mū tōrere</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/L_game" title="L game">L game</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Race_game" title="Race game">Race games</a> – e.g. <a href="/wiki/Pachisi" title="Pachisi">Pachisi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Backgammon" title="Backgammon">backgammon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snakes_and_Ladders" title="Snakes and Ladders">Snakes and Ladders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyena_chase" title="Hyena chase">Hyena chase</a>, or <i>Worm Up</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tabletop_role-playing_game" title="Tabletop role-playing game">Role-playing games</a> – e.g. <a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Category:Roll-and-move_board_games" title="Category:Roll-and-move board games">Roll-and-move</a> games – e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Monopoly_(game)" title="Monopoly (game)">Monopoly</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/The_Game_of_Life" title="The Game of Life">Life</a></i></li>
<li>Share-buying games (games in which players buy stakes in each other's positions) – typically longer economic-management games, e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Acquire" title="Acquire">Acquire</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Panamax" title="Panamax">Panamax</a></i></li>
<li>Single-player <a href="/wiki/Puzzle" title="Puzzle">puzzle</a> games – e.g. <a href="/wiki/Peg_solitaire" title="Peg solitaire">peg solitaire</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sudoku" title="Sudoku">Sudoku</a></li>
<li>Spiritual development games (games with no winners or losers) – e.g. <i>Transformation Game</i> or <i>Psyche's Key</i></li>
<li>Story telling games – e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Dixit_(card_game)" title="Dixit (card game)">Dixit</a></i> or <i>Tales of the Arabian Nights</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_abstract_strategy_games#Stacking_games" title="List of abstract strategy games">Stacking games</a> – e.g. <a href="/wiki/Lasca" title="Lasca">Lasca</a> or <i><a href="/wiki/DVONN" title="DVONN">DVONN</a></i></li>
<li>Territory games – e.g. <a href="/wiki/Go_(game)" title="Go (game)">Go</a> or <a href="/wiki/Reversi" title="Reversi">Reversi</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Category:Tile-based_board_games" title="Category:Tile-based board games">Tile-based games</a> – e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Carcassonne_(board_game)" title="Carcassonne (board game)">Carcassonne</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Scrabble" title="Scrabble">Scrabble</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Tigris_and_Euphrates" title="Tigris and Euphrates">Tigris and Euphrates</a></i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Evo_(board_game)" title="Evo (board game)">Evo</a></i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Train_game" title="Train game">Train games</a> – e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Ticket_to_Ride_(board_game)" title="Ticket to Ride (board game)">Ticket to Ride</a></i>, <i>Steam</i>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/18xx" title="18xx">18xx</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Trivia" title="Trivia">Trivia</a> games – e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Trivial_Pursuit" title="Trivial Pursuit">Trivial Pursuit</a></i></li>
<li>Two-player-only themed games – e.g. <i>En Garde</i> or <i>Dos de Mayo</i></li>
<li>Unequal forces (or "hunt") games – e.g. <a href="/wiki/Fox_games" title="Fox games">Fox and Geese</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tafl_games" title="Tafl games">Tablut</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Board_wargame" title="Board wargame">Wargames</a> – ranging from <i><a href="/wiki/Risk_(game)" title="Risk (game)">Risk</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Diplomacy_(game)" title="Diplomacy (game)">Diplomacy</a></i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Axis_%26_Allies" title="Axis &amp; Allies">Axis &amp; Allies</a></i>, to <i><a href="/wiki/Attack!_(board_game)" title="Attack! (board game)">Attack!</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_the_Empire" title="Conquest of the Empire">Conquest of the Empire</a></i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Word_game" title="Word game">Word games</a> – e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Scrabble" title="Scrabble">Scrabble</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Boggle" title="Boggle">Boggle</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Anagrams" title="Anagrams">Anagrams</a>, or <i>What's My Word?</i> (2010)</li>
</ul>
