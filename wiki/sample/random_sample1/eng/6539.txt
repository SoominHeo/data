<p><b>Boracay</b> is a small <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">island</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> located approximately 315 km (196 mi) south of <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a> and 2 km off the northwest tip of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panay_Island" title="Panay Island">Panay Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/Western_Visayas" title="Western Visayas">Western Visayas</a> region of the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>. Boracay Island and its beaches have received awards from numerous travel publications and agencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[Note 1]</a></sup> The island comprises the <a href="/wiki/Barangay" title="Barangay">barangays</a> of Manoc-Manoc, Balabag, and Yapak in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippine_municipality" title="Philippine municipality">municipality</a> of <a href="/wiki/Malay,_Aklan" title="Malay, Aklan">Malay</a>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aklan_Province" title="Aklan Province">Aklan Province</a>. The island is administered by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippine_Tourism_Authority" title="Philippine Tourism Authority">Philippine Tourism Authority</a> and the provincial government of Aklan. Apart from its white sand beaches, Boracay is also famous for being one of the world's top destinations for relaxation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> It is also emerging among the top destinations for tranquility and nightlife.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Boracay was awarded as the 2012 best island in the world from the international travel magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Travel_%2B_Leisure" title="Travel + Leisure">Travel + Leisure</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> In 2014, the resort island was at the top of the <i>Best Islands in the World</i> list published by the international magazine <a href="/wiki/Cond%C3%A9_Nast_Traveler" title="Condé Nast Traveler">Condé Nast Traveler</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> In 2016, Boracay headed the magazine's list of <i>Top 10 destinations to watch</i> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boracay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name <i>Boracay</i> is attributed to different origins. One story says that it is derived from the local word "borac" which means white cotton with characteristics close to the color and texture of Boracay's white sugary and powdery sand. Another credits the name to local words "bora," meaning bubbles, and "bocay," meaning white. Yet another version dating back to the Spanish era says the name is derived from "sagay," the word for a shell, and "boray," the word for seed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boracay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-colonial_period">Pre-colonial period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boracay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pre-colonial period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Boracay was originally home to the <a href="/wiki/Ati_people" title="Ati people">Ati</a> people. Boracay Island was already an inhabited place before the Spaniards came to the Philippines. It was known to the Iberian conquerors as <i>Buracay</i>. At the time of contact with the Europeans, <i>Buracay</i> had a population of one hundred people, who cultivated rice on the island and augmented their income by raising goats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_period">Contemporary period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boracay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Contemporary period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Boracay is part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aklan_Province" title="Aklan Province">Aklan Province</a>, which became an independent province on April 25, 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-panublion_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-panublion-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sofia Gonzales Tirol and her husband Lamberto Hontiveros Tirol, a town judge on nearby Panay island, took ownership of substantial properties on the island around 1900 and planted coconuts, fruit trees, and greenery on the island. Others followed the Tirols, and cultivation and development of the island gradually spread from this initial beginning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tourism came to the island beginning in about the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> The movie <i><a href="/wiki/Too_Late_the_Hero_(film)" title="Too Late the Hero (film)">Too Late the Hero</a></i> was filmed in 1970 on locations in Boracay and Caticlan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup> In the 1980s, the island became popular as a budget destination for <a href="/wiki/Backpacking_(travel)" title="Backpacking (travel)">backpackers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-panublion_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-panublion-21">[20]</a></sup> By the 1990s, Boracay's beaches were being acclaimed as the best in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Tourism_(Philippines)" title="Department of Tourism (Philippines)">Philippine Department of Tourism</a> reported that Boracay had been named the world's second best beach after <a href="/wiki/Providenciales" title="Providenciales">Providenciales</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos_Islands" title="Turks and Caicos Islands">Turks and Caicos Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boracay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Boracay Island is located off the northwest corner of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panay_Island" title="Panay Island">Panay Island</a>, and belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Western_Visayas" title="Western Visayas">Western Visayas</a> island-group, or Region VI, of the Philippines. The island is approximately seven kilometers long, dog-bone shaped with the narrowest spot being less than one kilometer wide, and has a total land area of 10.32 square kilometers.</p>
<p>South-facing Cagban Beach is located across a small strait from the jetty port at <a href="/wiki/Malay,_Aklan" title="Malay, Aklan">Caticlan</a> on Panay island, and the Cagban jetty port serves as Boracay's main entry and exit point during most of the year. When wind and sea conditions dictate, east-facing Tambisaan Beach serves as an alternative entry and exit point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup> Boracay's two primary tourism beaches, White Beach and Bulabog Beach, are located on opposite sides of the island's narrow central area. White Beach faces westward and Bulabog Beach faces eastward. The island also has several other beaches.</p>
<p>White Beach, the main tourism beach, is about four kilometers long and is lined with resorts, hotels, lodging houses, restaurants, and other tourism-related businesses. In the central portion, for about two kilometers, there is a footpath known as the Beachfront Path separating the beach itself from the establishments located along it. North and south of the Beachfront Path, beachfront establishments do literally front along the beach itself. Several roads and paths connect the Beachfront Path with Boracay's Main Road, a vehicular road which runs the length of the island. At the extreme northern end of White Beach, a footpath runs around the headland there and connects White Beach with Diniwid Beach.</p>
<p>Bulabog Beach, across the island from White Beach, is the second most popular tourism beach on the island and Boracay's main <a href="/wiki/Windsurfing" title="Windsurfing">windsurfing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kitesurfing" title="Kitesurfing">kiteboarding</a> area.</p>
<p>Boracay is divided for land use and conservation purposes into 400 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hectares" title="Hectares">hectares</a> of preserved forestland and 628.96 hectares of agricultural land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup></p>

<p>Weather in Boracay is generally divided into two seasonal weather patterns known locally as the <a href="/wiki/Amihan" title="Amihan">Amihan</a> and Habagat seasons. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visayan_language" title="Visayan language">Visayan language</a>, <i>Amihan</i> means <i>a cool northeast wind</i>, and <i>Habagat</i> means <i>west or southwest wind; southwest monsoon</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L_EnglishDict_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-L_EnglishDict-38">[37]</a></sup> The Amihan season is characterized by moderate temperatures, little or no rainfall, and a prevailing wind from the northeast. The Habagat season is characterized by hot and humid weather, frequent heavy rainfall, and a prevailing wind from the west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>On Boracay, the main indicator of the switch between the Amihan and Habagat seasonal patterns is the switch in wind direction. In most years this transition is abrupt and occurs overnight. In some years there is a period of perhaps a week or two where the wind will switch between Amihan and Habagat patterns several times before settling into the pattern for the new season. As a rule of thumb, Boracay will be in the Amihan weather pattern from sometime in October to sometime in June and in the Habagat weather pattern for the remainder of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Daytime temperatures on Boracay generally range from 77–90 °F (25–32 °C) from the beginning of the Amihan season into February or March, and increase to the 82–100 °F (28–38 °C) range with the onset of the Habagat season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup> During <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_low" title="Tropical low">Tropical Storm</a> periods, temperatures can fall below 68 °F (20 °C). Tropical Storms can impact Boracay at any time of year, but are most likely to be seen during the Habagat season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boracay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Partly because of its wind and weather patterns, <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a> in Boracay is at its peak during the <i><a href="/wiki/Amihan" title="Amihan">amihan</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">season</a> (start from September or October and ends sometime in May or June) . During <i>amihan</i>, the prevailing wind blows from the east. Boracay's main tourism area, White Beach, is on the western side of the island and is sheltered from the wind. During the Amihan season, the water off White Beach is often glassy-smooth. On the eastern side of the island, hills on the northern and southern ends of the island channel the Amihan season wind from the east onshore, onto Bulabog Beach in the central part of the island's eastern side. This makes the <a href="/wiki/Reef" title="Reef">reef</a>-protected waters off that beach ideal for <a href="/wiki/Windsurfing" title="Windsurfing">windsurfing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kitesurfing" title="Kitesurfing">kiteboarding / kitesurfing</a>.</p>
<p>In June 2011, it was reported that <a href="/wiki/Megaworld_Corporation" title="Megaworld Corporation">Megaworld Corporation</a>, a real estate development group led by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Tan" title="Andrew Tan">Andrew Tan</a> had earmarked <a href="/wiki/Philippine_peso" title="Philippine peso">PHP</a>20 <a href="/wiki/Billion" title="Billion">billion</a> to develop tourism estates "featuring an integrated, master-planned layout and world-class resort offerings and amenities" in Boracay and <a href="/wiki/Cavite" title="Cavite">Cavite</a>. The planned Boracay project, Boracay Newcoast, involves four hotels with 1,500 rooms, a plaza and entertainment center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup></p>

<p>Leisure activities available on or near Boracay include horseback riding, <a href="/wiki/Scuba_diving" title="Scuba diving">scuba diving</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diving_helmet" title="Diving helmet">diving helmet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snorkeling" title="Snorkeling">snorkeling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Windsurfing" title="Windsurfing">windsurfing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kitesurfing" title="Kitesurfing">kiteboarding</a>, cliff diving, <a href="/wiki/Parasailing" title="Parasailing">parasailing</a> and beach relaxation.</p>
<p>Boracay is the site of an 18-hole par 72 <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a> course designed by <a href="/wiki/Graham_Marsh" title="Graham Marsh">Graham Marsh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup> In addition, as of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Boracay&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Boracay has in excess of 350 beach resorts offering more than 2,000 rooms ranging in quality from five-star to budget accommodation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup> In addition, Boracay offers a wide range of restaurants, bars, pubs, and nightclubs.</p>
<p>Boracay may be a small island, but it is huge in its offering of a variety of activities—from just swimming in its pristine blue water, strolling along the sugary white sanded beach, enjoying the nightlife, making choices on restaurants featuring multi-ethnic foods, and just relaxing in wonderment of the island's amazing scenery viewed from a range of affordable to five-star hotels. A <a href="/wiki/Landmark" title="Landmark">landmark</a> natural rock formation, Willy's Rock, juts prominently directly in front of Willy's Beach Resort.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boracay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Boracay is one competitive venue for the Asian Windsurfing Tour,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> with the week-long <a href="/wiki/Boracay_International_Funboard_Cup" title="Boracay International Funboard Cup">Boracay International Funboard Cup</a> competition usually held in January on Bulabog Beach. In 2010, the event dates are January 25 – 31<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Boracay&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup> CNNGo, a division of <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a> focused on travel/lifestyle/entertainment, selected the Boracay International Funboard Competition on the weekend of January 22–24 as one of its 52 weekend recommendations for 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dragon_boat" title="Dragon boat">Dragon boat</a> races are held annually on Boracay under the auspices of the <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Dragon_Boat_Federation" title="Philippine Dragon Boat Federation">Philippine Dragon Boat Federation</a>, with teams coming from around the Philippines and from other Asian nations to compete. The races usually take place sometime in April or May. The 2012 Boracay Edition of the PDBF International Club Crew Challenge to is scheduled for April 26–28, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Boracay Open Asian Beach Ultimate Tournament, an <a href="/wiki/Ultimate_(sport)" title="Ultimate (sport)">ultimate frisbee</a> event, has been held annually since 2003, usually in March or April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asian_Games_Centennial_Festival">Asian Games Centennial Festival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boracay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Asian Games Centennial Festival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Boracay will host a special multi-sport event called the <i><a href="/wiki/Asian_Games" title="Asian Games">Asian Games</a> Centennial Festival</i>. On its 31st General Assembly in <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Council_of_Asia" title="Olympic Council of Asia">Olympic Council of Asia</a> (OCA) has decided to create the <i>Asian Games Centennial Festival</i> in celebration of the 100th anniversary of the Oriental Games (later became <a href="/wiki/Far_Eastern_Championship_Games" title="Far Eastern Championship Games">Far Eastern Championship Games</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup> OCA has awarded the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> the hosting rights as it was the same host 100 years ago in the first <a href="/wiki/Far_Eastern_Championship_Games" title="Far Eastern Championship Games">Far Eastern Championship Games</a> held in <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a>. The <i>Asian Games Centennial Festival</i> was to be held in Boracay on November 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup> However, due to <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Haiyan" title="Typhoon Haiyan">Typhoon Haiyan</a>, it was postponed to January 17, 2014, at the Sofitel Plaza in Manila.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup> The 32nd OCA General Assembly was to be held in conjunction with the games.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippine_Swimming_League">Philippine Swimming League</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boracay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Philippine Swimming League">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On April 27, 2014, the <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Swimming_League" title="Philippine Swimming League">Philippine Swimming League</a> was held at Boracay Island for their open water swim after the competition in Aklan Sports Complex in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Makato" title="Makato">Makato</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aklan" title="Aklan">Aklan</a>. Many swimming teams joined the competition including <a href="/wiki/Aklan" title="Aklan">Aklan</a> Swimming Club based in <a href="/wiki/Kalibo" title="Kalibo">Kalibo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aklan" title="Aklan">Aklan</a> and John B. Lacson Swimming Team which it is based in <a href="/wiki/Iloilo_City" title="Iloilo City">Iloilo City</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boracay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first settlers of Boracay were a <a href="/wiki/Negrito" title="Negrito">Negrito</a> people called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ati_(Philippines)" title="Ati (Philippines)">Ati</a>, and who spoke a <a href="/wiki/Visayan_languages" title="Visayan languages">Visayan language</a> called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ati_(Philippines)#Language" title="Ati (Philippines)">Inati</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup> Later settlers brought other languages to the island, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aklanon_languages" title="Aklanon languages">Aklanon</a> (as Boracay is part of <a href="/wiki/Aklan" title="Aklan">Aklan province</a>), <a href="/wiki/Hiligaynon_language" title="Hiligaynon language">Hiligaynon (Ilonggo)</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kinaray-a_language" title="Kinaray-a language">Kinaray-a</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capiznon_language" title="Capiznon language">Capiznon</a>, other <a href="/wiki/Visayan_languages" title="Visayan languages">Visayan languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Filipino_language" title="Filipino language">Filipino</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Philippine_English" title="Philippine English">English</a>.</p>
<p>The well-known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ati-Atihan_Festival" title="Ati-Atihan Festival">Ati-Atihan Festival</a> takes place each January in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kalibo,_Aklan" title="Kalibo, Aklan">Kalibo</a> on nearby <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panay_Island" title="Panay Island">Panay Island</a>. A much smaller Ati-Atihan festival is celebrated on Boracay, usually in the second or third week of January.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boracay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Boracay_Tricycles.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Boracay_Tricycles.jpg/220px-Boracay_Tricycles.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Boracay_Tricycles.jpg/330px-Boracay_Tricycles.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Boracay_Tricycles.jpg/440px-Boracay_Tricycles.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

The <a href="/wiki/Motorized_tricycle_(Philippines)" title="Motorized tricycle (Philippines)">tricycle</a> functions like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxi" title="Taxi">taxis</a> and are the primary mode of transportation in Boracay</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Boracay island is separated from Panay island by a narrow strait. The island is located opposite the barangay of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caticlan" title="Caticlan">Caticlan</a> in the municipality of Malay, Aklan. Transportation across the strait is provided by boats operating from the Caticlan jetty port.</p>
<p>Boracay is served by two airports in Aklan: the <a href="/wiki/Kalibo_International_Airport" title="Kalibo International Airport">Kalibo International Airport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Godofredo_P._Ramos_Airport" title="Godofredo P. Ramos Airport">Godofredo P. Ramos Airport</a> commonly referred to as the Caticlan airport.</p>
<p>The western part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_Republic_Nautical_Highway" title="Strong Republic Nautical Highway">Strong Republic Nautical Highway</a> (SRNH) passes through Caticlan, with car ferries from <a href="/wiki/Roxas,_Oriental_Mindoro" title="Roxas, Oriental Mindoro">Roxas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Mindoro" title="Oriental Mindoro">Oriental Mindoro</a> docking at the Caticlan jetty port. Several bus companies operate provincial bus routes from Manila which pass through Caticlan via the SRNH.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/2GO" title="2GO">2GO</a>, the largest ferry company in the Philippines, offers regular overnight passenger and cargo service between <a href="/wiki/Batangas_City" title="Batangas City">Batangas City</a> and Caticlan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>The two main modes of transport are via motor-tricycles along the main road or by walking along the beaches. Pedicabs, known as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sikad&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sikad (page does not exist)">sikads</a>, are also available along the Beachfront Path. Other means of transportation include <a href="/wiki/Mountain_bike" title="Mountain bike">mountain bikes</a>, <a href="/wiki/All-terrain_vehicle" title="All-terrain vehicle">quadbikes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle" title="Motorcycle">motorbikes</a>, all of which can be rented.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modernization">Modernization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boracay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Modernization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In February 2016 Boracay got a huge boost in payment-modernization and safety. Although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automatic_Teller_Machine" title="Automatic Teller Machine">ATMs</a> are now quite common on the island, Boracay now features a cashless payment-system called "BoraPay". Upon arrival on the island, selected hotels give out bracelets featuring <a href="/wiki/Near_field_communication#Commerce" title="Near field communication">NFC</a> technology that can be loaded with any international debit or credit card, making frustrating walks to the ATM a thing of the past. Accounts can be funded from overseas before tourists start their trip allowing travelers to budget in advance. Most establishments along the famous white beach are equipped with mobile terminals where you can now pay with a simple swipe. BoraPay's application also features an up-to-date map of the island helping "newcomers" maximize their time.</p>
