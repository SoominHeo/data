<p>The <b>bodhrán</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ɔːr/ 'or' in 'abhor'">ɔːr</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/aʊ/ 'ou' in 'pout'">aʊ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>; plural <i>bodhráin</i> or <i>bodhráns</i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Irish</a> <a href="/wiki/Frame_drum" title="Frame drum">frame drum</a> ranging from 25 to 65 cm (10 to 26 in) in diameter, with most drums measuring 35 to 45 cm (14 to 18 in). The sides of the drum are 9 to 20 cm (<span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap">3<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> to 8 in) deep. A <a href="/wiki/Goatskin_(material)" title="Goatskin (material)">goatskin</a> head is tacked to one side (synthetic heads or other animal skins are sometimes used). The other side is open-ended for one hand to be placed against the inside of the drum head to control the <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">pitch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Timbre" title="Timbre">timbre</a>.</p>
<p>One or two crossbars, sometimes removable, may be inside the frame, but this is increasingly rare on modern instruments. Some professional modern bodhráns integrate mechanical tuning systems similar to those used on drums found in <a href="/wiki/Drum_kit" title="Drum kit">drum kits</a>. It is usually with a hex key that the bodhrán skins are tightened or loosened depending on the atmospheric conditions.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodhr%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to musician Ronan Nolan, former editor of <i>Irish Music</i> magazine, the bodhrán evolved in the mid-19th century from the tambourine, which can be heard on some Irish music recordings dating back to the 1920s and viewed in a pre-Famine painting. However, in remote parts of the south-west, the "poor man's tambourine" – made from farm implements and without the cymbals – was in popular use among mummers, or wren boys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A large oil painting on canvas by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Maclise" title="Daniel Maclise">Daniel Maclise</a> (1806–1870) depicts a large Halloween house party in which a bodhrán features clearly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tab_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-tab-3">[3]</a></sup> That painting, produced c. 1842, shows a flautist accompanied by a tambourine player who, in an Arabic style in contrast to standard bodhrán technique, used his fingers rather than a tipper. It is known that by the early 20th century, home-made frame drums were constructed using willow branches as frames, leather as drumheads, and pennies as jingles. In photographs from the 1940s and videos from the 1950s, jingles remained part of the bodhrán construction like a tambourine, yet were played with <i>cipín</i>, also known in English as "tipper".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Se%C3%A1n_%C3%93_Riada" title="Seán Ó Riada">Seán Ó Riada</a> declared the bodhrán to be the native drum of the Celts as did Paraic McNeela (The Bodhrán Maker) possibly used originally for <a href="/wiki/Winnowing" title="Winnowing">winnowing</a> or the dying of wool, with a musical history that predated Christianity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> native to southwest Ireland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KF_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-KF-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodhr%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Irish word <i>bodhrán</i> (<small>pronounced </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Irish" title="Help:IPA for Irish">[ˈbˠəuɾˠaːn̪ˠ]</a></span>, plural <i>bodhráin</i>), indicating a drum, is first mentioned in a translated English document in the 17th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tab_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-tab-3">[3]</a></sup> It appears in Jacob Pool's list of words from the Baronies of Forth and Bargy in county Wexford (collected in the late 18th century), meaning "A drum, tambourine...also a sieve used in winnowing corn".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Third-generation bodhrán maker Caramel Tobin suggests that the name <i>bodhrán</i> means "skin tray". He also suggests a link with the <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a> word <i>bodhar</i>, meaning, among other things, a drum or a dull sound (it also means <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deaf" title="Deaf">deaf</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tab_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-tab-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drivertutor_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-drivertutor-8">[8]</a></sup> A relatively new introduction to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_music" title="Irish music">Irish music</a>, the bodhrán has largely replaced the role of the <a href="/wiki/Tambourine" title="Tambourine">tambourine</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_antecedents">Possible antecedents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodhr%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Possible antecedents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bodhrán is one of the most basic of drums and as such it is similar to the frame drums distributed widely across <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Africa" title="Northern Africa">northern Africa</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, and has cognates in instruments used for <a href="/wiki/Arabic_music" title="Arabic music">Arabic music</a> and the musical traditions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> region (see <a href="/wiki/Music_of_North_Africa" title="Music of North Africa">Music of North Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Greece" title="Music of Greece">Music of Greece</a> etc.). A larger form is found in the Iranian <i>daff</i>, which is played with the fingers in an upright position, without a stick. Traditional skin drums made by some <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native Americans</a> are very close in design to the bodhrán as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drivertutor_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-drivertutor-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>It has also been suggested that the origin of the instrument may be the skin trays used in Ireland for carrying <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a>; the earliest bodhrán may have simply been a skin stretched across a wood frame without any means of attachment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drivertutor_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-drivertutor-8">[8]</a></sup> The Cornish frame drum <a href="/wiki/Crowdy-crawn" title="Crowdy-crawn">crowdy-crawn</a>, which was also used for harvesting grain, was known as early as 1880.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Peter Kennedy observed a similar instrument in <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a> in the 1950s, where it was known as a "<a href="/wiki/Riddle_drum" title="Riddle drum">riddle drum</a>", a <a href="/wiki/Riddle_(tool)" title="Riddle (tool)">riddle</a> being a large sieve for separating soil particles from stones etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dorothea Hast has stated that until the mid-twentieth century the bodhrán was mainly used as a tray for separating chaff, in baking, as a food server, and for storing food or tools. She argues that its use as musical instrument was restricted to ritual use in rural areas. She claims that while the earliest evidence of its use beyond ritual occurs in 1842, its use as a general instrument did not become widespread until the 1960s, when <a href="/wiki/Se%C3%A1n_%C3%93_Riada" title="Seán Ó Riada">Seán Ó Riada</a> used it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Popularity">Popularity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodhr%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Popularity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are no known references to this particular name for a drum prior to the 17th century. Although various drums (played with either hands or sticks) have been used in Ireland since ancient times, the bodhrán itself did not gain wide recognition as a legitimate musical instrument until the <a href="/wiki/Folk_music_of_Ireland" title="Folk music of Ireland">Irish traditional music</a> resurgence in the 1960s in which it became known through the music of Seán Ó Riada<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KF_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-KF-6">[6]</a></sup> and others.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The second wave <a href="/wiki/Roots_revival" title="Roots revival">roots revival</a> of Irish Traditional music in the 1960s and 1970s brought virtuoso bodhrán playing to the forefront, when it was further popularized by bands such as <a href="/wiki/Ceolt%C3%B3ir%C3%AD_Chualann" title="Ceoltóirí Chualann">Ceoltóirí Chualann</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Chieftains" title="The Chieftains">The Chieftains</a>.</p>
<p>Growing interest led to internationally available <a href="/wiki/Gramophone_record" title="Gramophone record">LP</a> recordings, at which time the bodhrán became a globally recognized instrument. In the 1970s, virtuoso players such as <a href="/wiki/The_Boys_of_the_Lough" title="The Boys of the Lough">The Boys of the Lough</a>'s Robin Morton, <a href="/wiki/The_Chieftains" title="The Chieftains">The Chieftains</a>' Peadar Mercier, <a href="/wiki/Planxty" title="Planxty">Planxty</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Christy_Moore" title="Christy Moore">Christy Moore</a>, and <a href="/wiki/De_Dannan" title="De Dannan">De Dannan</a>'s Johnny "Ringo" McDonagh further developed playing techniques.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_use">International use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodhr%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: International use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although most common in Ireland, the bodhrán has gained popularity throughout the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_music" title="Celtic music">Celtic music</a> world, especially in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cape_Breton_Island" title="Cape Breton Island">Cape Breton</a>, North mainland <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a>. In Southern England tambourines were a popular accompaniment to traditional dance music. In the South West of England a similar instrument made from the frame of a garden sieve was once popular and known as a Riddle Drum. In <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Cornwall" title="Music of Cornwall">Cornish traditional music</a> they are called a <a href="/wiki/Crowdy-crawn" title="Crowdy-crawn">Crowdy-crawn</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corollyn_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Corollyn-12">[12]</a></sup> the use of this instrument to store odds and ends led to the name also being used to mean "miscellaneous". The bodhran has also found application within the Celtic music of Galicia, often accompanying the <i>gaita gallega</i> (galician bagpipes).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beaters">Beaters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodhr%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Beaters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The drum is struck either with the bare hand or with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lathe_(tool)" title="Lathe (tool)">lathe</a>-<a href="/wiki/Woodturning" title="Woodturning">turned</a> piece of wood called a <i>bone</i>, <i>tipper</i>, <i>beater</i>, or <i>cipín</i>.</p>
<p>Tippers were originally fashioned from a double-ended knuckle bone, but are now commonly made from ash, holly,or hickory wood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drivertutor_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-drivertutor-8">[8]</a></sup> Brush-ended beaters, and a "rim shot" (striking the rim) technique for contrast, were introduced by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Johnny_McDonagh&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Johnny McDonagh (page does not exist)">Johnny McDonagh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ceolasplayers_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ceolasplayers-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Playing">Playing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodhr%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Playing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The drum is usually played in a seated position, held vertically on the player's thigh and supported by his or her upper body and arm (usually on the left side, for a right-handed player), with the hand placed on the inside of the skin where it is able to control the tension (and therefore the pitch and timbre) by applying varying amounts of pressure and also the amount of surface area being played, with the back of the hand against the crossbar, if present. The drum is struck with the other arm (usually the right) and is played either with the bare hand or with a tipper. There are numerous playing styles, mostly named after the region of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> in which they originated. The most common is <a href="/wiki/County_Kerry" title="County Kerry">Kerry</a> style, which uses a two-headed tipper; the <a href="/wiki/County_Limerick" title="County Limerick">West Limerick</a> style uses only one end of the tipper.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:203-0046-bodhran_top-end_hinnerk-ruemenapf-v01-i01-h3000.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="2008" height="329" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/203-0046-bodhran_top-end_hinnerk-ruemenapf-v01-i01-h3000.jpg/220px-203-0046-bodhran_top-end_hinnerk-ruemenapf-v01-i01-h3000.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/203-0046-bodhran_top-end_hinnerk-ruemenapf-v01-i01-h3000.jpg/330px-203-0046-bodhran_top-end_hinnerk-ruemenapf-v01-i01-h3000.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/203-0046-bodhran_top-end_hinnerk-ruemenapf-v01-i01-h3000.jpg/440px-203-0046-bodhran_top-end_hinnerk-ruemenapf-v01-i01-h3000.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:203-0046-bodhran_top-end_hinnerk-ruemenapf-v01-i01-h3000.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bodhrán, top-end style</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Later players such as Robbie Breathnach, Tommy Hayes, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aidan_%22Scobie%22_McDonnell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title='Aidan "Scobie" McDonnell (page does not exist)'>Aidan "Scobie" McDonnell</a>, Abe Doron, and Damien Quinn developed sophisticated pitch-varying techniques which allow players to follow the tune being played. This was the birth of the "top-end" style. Their breakthrough in this style has achieved local and international acclaim with many beginners now being educated in this manner. This "top-end" style, is often played on a smaller (14–15 inch) and deeper (4–6 inch) drum with a thinner resonant skin, prepared like the skin of a <a href="/wiki/Lambeg_drum" title="Lambeg drum">Lambeg drum</a>. The tipper in this style is usually straight and most of the expressive action is focused on the top end of the drum. The concept involves allowing a greater vs. lesser amount of the skin to resonate, with the "skin hand" acting as a moving <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bearing_edge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bearing edge (page does not exist)">bearing edge</a>. Top-end players move the skin hand from the bottom and towards the top of the drum to generate increasingly high pitches. By making a "C" shape with the skin hand, the player can help enhance and even amplify the sound. The same concept can be employed while playing at the front of the drum (the skin hand moving towards and away from the player) or in a "bottom end" style, which is essentially top end, but upside down, with the majority of tipper strikes at the bottom of the head. In any of these styles, crossbars are most often absent, allowing a more unrestricted access for the left hand to modify the tone. This enables a more <a href="/wiki/Melody" title="Melody">melodic</a> approach to this rhythm instrument, with a wide range of tones being employed.</p>
<p>When playing the bodhrán as an accompaniment to Irish music, different beats may be used. For example, reels have a 4/4 time. The bodhrán player must stick to this rhythm but is free to improvise within the structure: most simply, s/he may enunciate the first beat of four, making a sound like ONE two three four ONE two three four; but s/he can syncopate, put in double pulses, according to the rhythmic characteristics of the tunes being played. This is the difference between sensitive and insensitive playing, a matter of much concern to other traditional musicians. Because the bodhrán typically plays 16th notes (Kerry style), a great deal of variety can be introduced by these syncopations and the use of rests. Combined with manual pitch changes and naturally occurring tonal variations in an animal skin drumhead, the bodhrán can almost sound as melodically expressive as other non-percussive instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drivertutor_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-drivertutor-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_techniques">New techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodhr%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: New techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Playing styles have all been affected by the introduction of the internal tone ring, driven against the skin to tension/loosen it by screws. This was invented by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Seamus_O%27Kane&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Seamus O'Kane (page does not exist)">Seamus O'Kane</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Dungiven" title="Dungiven">Dungiven</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Londonderry" title="County Londonderry">County Londonderry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, to combat the damp conditions of Donegal in 1976. This system was copied from banjo design but adapted for bodhráns. For a few years only about six drums of this type were made, so it was not until the idea was taken and refined by makers that it caught on. This system is now being used by makers from many diverse cultures worldwide. It has revolutionized the making and playing of bodhráns by removing the threat of atmospheric conditions to the tension of the drumhead. The accepted philosophy of thick skins was challenged also at this time by O'Kane's introduction of thin Lambeg skins. This allowed the bodhrán to achieve both higher and lower crisp notes and allowed the players to become more musical and delicate in their playing.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_modifications">Common modifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodhr%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Common modifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is currently not unusual for the rim of a bodhran to be covered with electrical tape, either by the drum-maker or the owner. This both reduces "edge-loading" (where the vibrations in the skin hit the rim and bounce back toward the center of the drum), and dampens unwanted overtones, allowing for greater control of the drum's sound. Electrical tape is preferred because the adhesive is rubber-based and will stretch with the skin even after bonding to it, lessening the likelihood of bubbles and other changes in the tape occurring when the skin tension is changed by tuning or atmospheric conditions. Owners of lower quality drums, with thick and rough skins, may also choose to sand the skin very lightly to reduce the rasp when the tipper strikes the face of the drum. Many effects of these and other modifications to the drum-skin, especially high quality skins, can also be achieved through regular use of the drum over time.</p>

<p>As world music in general has become more popular, techniques once associated with other ethnic drumming traditions have become widespread in bodhrán playing.</p>
<p>The World Bodhrán Championships are held in Milltown, County Kerry, Ireland each year.</p>
