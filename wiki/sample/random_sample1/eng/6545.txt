As of 2014, its population was of 4,931.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Borja,_Zaragoza&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The municipality borders with <a href="/wiki/Ablitas" title="Ablitas">Ablitas</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Navarre" title="Navarre">Navarre</a>) <a href="/wiki/Ag%C3%B3n" title="Agón">Agón</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ainz%C3%B3n" title="Ainzón">Ainzón</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alberite_de_San_Juan" title="Alberite de San Juan">Alberite de San Juan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albeta" title="Albeta">Albeta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ambel" title="Ambel">Ambel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulbuente" title="Bulbuente">Bulbuente</a>, <a href="/wiki/El_Buste" title="El Buste">El Buste</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9scano" title="Fréscano">Fréscano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magall%C3%B3n" title="Magallón">Magallón</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malej%C3%A1n" title="Maleján">Maleján</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mall%C3%A9n" title="Mallén">Mallén</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tabuenca" title="Tabuenca">Tabuenca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tarazona" title="Tarazona">Tarazona</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vera_de_Moncayo" title="Vera de Moncayo">Vera de Moncayo</a>. It is the administrative seat of the comarca of <a href="/wiki/Campo_de_Borja" title="Campo de Borja">Campo de Borja</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Borja,_Zaragoza&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The town's origins date back to the 5th century BC, when a <a href="/wiki/Celtiberians" title="Celtiberians">Celtiberian</a> settlement, known as <b>Bursau</b> or <b>Bursao</b>, existed near the current ruins of the castle. After the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> conquest (1st century BC) also the slopes of the hill were populated, though the town started to expand significantly only after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_Spain" title="Muslim conquest of Spain">Muslim conquest</a> in the 8th century AD.</p>
<p>In the 12th century it was conquered by the Christians from the north, and in the 15th/16th centuries it was converted into a military fortress against the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Castile" title="Kingdom of Castile">Castillan</a> invasions, but at the same time received much of its historical architectural heritage, with numerous churches and palaces. It received the title of "city" by King <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_V_of_Aragon" title="Alfonso V of Aragon">Alfonso V of Aragon</a> in 1438. During the reign of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">Catholic Monarchs</a>, the Jews, forming an important part of the Borjan community, were expelled.</p>
<p>Borja lived through a period of recession and plagues in the 17th and 18th centuries. It recovered economically starting from the 19th century, when a railway connecting the city to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cortes_de_Navarra" title="Cortes de Navarra">Cortes de Navarra</a> was inaugurated. In the 20th century agriculture, traditionally the mainstay of Borja's economy, started to lose its importance, and, without a consistent industrial base, the city lost economical and political importance in the area: much of the population therefore emigrated to other more developed areas. The industrial sector is intended to be boosted by businesses being attracted to the ongoing development "Polígono Industrial Barbalanca", the Barbalanca Industrial Estate.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fresco_restoration">Fresco restoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Borja,_Zaragoza&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fresco restoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2012, octogenarian amateur painter Cecilia Giménez botched a partial restoration of an unremarkable <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecce_Homo_(El%C3%ADas_Garc%C3%ADa_Mart%C3%ADnez)" title="Ecce Homo (Elías García Martínez)">Ecce Homo</a></i> fresco (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 1930) depicting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christ" title="Christ">Christ</a> by <a href="/wiki/El%C3%ADas_Garc%C3%ADa_Mart%C3%ADnez" title="Elías García Martínez">Elías García Martínez</a>. The spectacularly bad results garnered worldwide attention,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and has been called one of "the worst art restoration projects of all time"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
