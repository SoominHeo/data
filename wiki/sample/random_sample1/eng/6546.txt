<p>A <b>full moon</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">lunar phase</a> that occurs when the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> is completely illuminated as seen from <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. This occurs when Earth is located directly between the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> and the Moon (more exactly, when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecliptic_coordinates" title="Ecliptic coordinates">ecliptic longitudes</a> of the Sun and Moon differ by 180 degrees). This means that the hemisphere of the Moon that is facing Earth (the <a href="/wiki/Near_side_of_the_Moon" title="Near side of the Moon">near side</a>) is almost fully illuminated by the Sun and appears round (while the <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">far side</a> is almost completely unilluminated). On some occasions at the time of full moon there is also a <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">lunar eclipse</a> so the moon's face appears reddish due to the <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">rayleigh scattering</a> of blue light in Earth's <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">Lunar eclipses</a> can occur only at full moon, where the Moon's orbit allows it to pass through Earth's shadow. Lunar eclipses do not occur every month because the Moon usually passes above or below Earth's shadow (which is mostly restricted to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plane_of_the_ecliptic" title="Plane of the ecliptic">ecliptic plane</a>). Lunar eclipses can occur only when the full moon occurs near the two <a href="/wiki/Orbital_node" title="Orbital node">nodes of the orbit</a>, either the ascending or descending node. This causes eclipses to only occur about every 6 months, and often 2 weeks before or after a <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">solar eclipse</a> at <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">new moon</a> at the opposite node.</p>
<p>The time interval between similar lunar phasesâthe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synodic_month" title="Synodic month">synodic month</a>âaverages about 29.53 days. Therefore, in those <a href="/wiki/Lunar_calendar" title="Lunar calendar">lunar calendars</a> in which each month begins on the new moon, the full moon falls on either the 14th or 15th of the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month" title="Lunar month">lunar month</a>. Because calendar months have a whole number of days, lunar months may be either 29 or 30 days long.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Full_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A full moon is often thought of as an event of a full night's duration. This is somewhat misleading because the Moon seen from Earth is continuously becoming larger and/or smaller (though much too slowly to notice with the <a href="/wiki/Naked_eye" title="Naked eye">naked eye</a>). Its absolute maximum size occurs at the moment expansion has stopped. For any given location, about half of these absolute maximum full moons will be potentially visible, as the other half occur during the day, when the full moon is below the horizon. Many <a href="/wiki/Almanac" title="Almanac">almanacs</a> list full moons not just by date, but by their exact time as well, usually in <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">Coordinated Universal Time</a> (UTC). Typical monthly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calendars" title="Calendars">calendars</a> that include phases of the Moon may be offset by one day if intended for use in a different <a href="/wiki/Time_zone" title="Time zone">time zone</a>.</p>
<p>Full moons are generally a poor time to conduct <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomical</a> observations, because the bright reflected sunlight from the Moon overwhelms the dimmer light from stars.</p>
<p>On 12 December 2008, the full moon occurred closer to the Earth than it had been at any time for the previous 15 years, called a <a href="/wiki/Supermoon" title="Supermoon">supermoon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 19 March 2011, another super full moon occurred, closer to the Earth than at any time for the previous ~18 years. (cycle)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 14 November 2016, a super full moon occurred closer to the Earth than at any time for the previous 68 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>Full moons are traditionally associated with temporal <a href="/wiki/Insomnia" title="Insomnia">insomnia</a> (inability to sleep), <a href="/wiki/Insanity" title="Insanity">insanity</a> (hence the terms <i>lunacy</i> and <i>lunatic</i>) and various "magical phenomena" such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lycanthropy" title="Lycanthropy">lycanthropy</a>. Psychologists, however, have found that there is no strong evidence for effects on human behavior around the time of a full moon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> They find that studies are generally not consistent, with some showing a positive effect and others showing a negative effect. In one instance, the 23 December 2000 issue of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Medical_Journal" title="British Medical Journal">British Medical Journal</a></i> published two studies on dog bite admission to hospitals in England and Australia. The study of the <a href="/wiki/Bradford_Royal_Infirmary" title="Bradford Royal Infirmary">Bradford Royal Infirmary</a> found that dog bites were twice as common during a full moon, whereas the study conducted by the public hospitals in Australia found that they were less likely. All this is relevant to canine rather than human behavior.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Full_moon_names">Full moon names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Full_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Full moon names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Historically, <a href="/wiki/Month" title="Month">month</a> names are names of moons (lunations, not necessarily full moons) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunisolar_calendars" title="Lunisolar calendars">lunisolar calendars</a>. Since the introduction of the solar <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian calendar</a> in the Roman Empire, and later the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a> worldwide, month names have ceased to be perceived as "moon names". The traditional <a href="/wiki/Germanic_calendar#Month_names" title="Germanic calendar">Old English month names</a> were equated with the names of the Julian calendar from an early time (soon after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christianization_of_the_Anglo-Saxons" title="Christianization of the Anglo-Saxons">Christianization</a>, according to the testimony of <a href="/wiki/Bede" title="Bede">Bede</a> ca. AD 700).</p>
<p>Some full moons have developed new names in modern times, e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Blue_moon" title="Blue moon">blue moon</a>, and the names "harvest moon" and "hunter's moon" for the full moons of autumn.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Harvest_and_hunter.27s_moons">Harvest and hunter's moons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Full_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Harvest and hunter's moons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><span id="Harvest_moon"></span><span id="Hunter.27s_moon"></span></p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">"Harvest moon" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Harvest_moon_(disambiguation)" title="Harvest moon (disambiguation)">Harvest moon (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">"Hunter's moon" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Hunter%27s_moon_(disambiguation)" title="Hunter's moon (disambiguation)">Hunter's moon (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Harvest_moon.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="372" data-file-width="723" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Harvest_moon.jpg/300px-Harvest_moon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Harvest_moon.jpg/450px-Harvest_moon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Harvest_moon.jpg/600px-Harvest_moon.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Harvest_moon.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A harvest moon. Its orange color is due to its closeness to the horizon, rather than being unique to harvest moons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The "harvest moon" and "hunter's moon" are traditional terms for the full moons occurring during late summer and in the <a href="/wiki/Autumn" title="Autumn">autumn</a>, in the northern hemisphere usually in August, September and October respectively. The "harvest moon" is the full moon closest to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autumnal_equinox" title="Autumnal equinox">autumnal equinox</a> (22 or 23 September), and the "hunter's moon" is the one following it. The names are recorded from the early 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OED" title="OED">Oxford English Dictionary</a> entry for "harvest moon" cites a 1706 reference, and for "hunter's moon" a 1710 edition of <i>The British Apollo</i>, where the term is attributed to "the country people" (<i>The Country People call this the Hunters-Moon.</i>) The names became traditional in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_folklore" title="American folklore">American folklore</a>, where they are now often popularly attributed to <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">the Native Americans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Feast_of_the_Hunters%27_Moon" title="Feast of the Hunters' Moon">Feast of the Hunters' Moon</a> is a yearly festival in <a href="/wiki/West_Lafayette,_Indiana" title="West Lafayette, Indiana">West Lafayette, Indiana</a>, held in late September or early October each year since 1968.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In 2010, the Harvest moon occurred on the night of equinox itself (some 5<span class="nowrap">â</span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>â<sub>2</sub></span> hours after the point of equinox) for the first time since 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>All full moons rise around the time of sunset. Because the moon moves eastward among the stars faster than the sun its meridian passage is delayed, causing it to rise later each day â on average by about 50.47 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The harvest moon and hunter's moon are unique because the time difference between moonrises on successive evenings is much shorter than average. The moon rises approximately 30 minutes later from one night to the next, as seen from about 40 degrees N or S latitude. (This is because a full moon in September appears to move not straight east but north-east in the sky.) Thus, there is no long period of darkness between sunset and moonrise for several days following the actual date of the full moon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Farmers.27_Almanacs">Farmers' Almanacs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Full_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Farmers' Almanacs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maine_Farmers%27_Almanac" title="Maine Farmers' Almanac">Maine Farmers' Almanac</a></i> from c. the 1930s began to publish Native American "Indian" full moon names. The <i><a href="/wiki/Farmers%27_Almanac" title="Farmers' Almanac">Farmers' Almanac</a></i> (since 1955 published in Maine, but not the same publication as the <i>Maine Farmers' Almanac</i>) continues to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>An early list of "Indian month names" was published in 1918 by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Carter_Beard" title="Daniel Carter Beard">Daniel Carter Beard</a> in his <i>The American Boy's Book of Signs, Signals and Symbols</i> for use by the <a href="/wiki/Boy_Scouts_of_America" title="Boy Scouts of America">boy scouts</a>. Beard's "Indian" month names were:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>January: Difficulty, Black Smoke</li>
<li>February: Raccoon, Bare Spots on the Ground</li>
<li>March: Wind, Little Grass, Sore-Eye</li>
<li>April: Ducks, Goose-Eggs</li>
<li>May: Green Grass, Root-Food</li>
<li>June: Corn-Planting, Strawberry</li>
<li>July: Buffalo (Bull), Hot Sun</li>
<li>August: Harvest, Cow Buffalo</li>
<li>September: Wild Rice, Red Plum</li>
<li>October: Leaf-Falling, Nuts</li>
<li>November: Deer-Mating, Fur-Pelts</li>
<li>December: Wolves, Big Moon</li>
</ul>
<p>Such names have gained currency in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_folklore" title="American folklore">American folklore</a>. They appear in print more widely outside of the almanac tradition from the 1990s in popular publications about the Moon. <i>Mysteries of the Moon</i> by Patricia Haddock ("Great Mysteries Series", Greenhaven Press, 1992) gave an extensive list of such names along with the individual tribal groups they were supposedly associated with.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Haddock supposes that certain "Colonial American" moon names were adopted from <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian languages</a> (which were formerly spoken in the territory of New England), while others are based in European tradition (e.g., the Colonial American names for the May moon, "Milk Moon", "Mother's Moon", "Hare Moon" have no parallels in the supposed native names, while the name of November, "Beaver Moon" is supposedly based in the Algonquin).</p>
<p>The individual names given in <i>Farmers' Almanac</i> include:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul>
<li>January: "Wolf Moon" (this is the name of December in Beard 1918)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> also "Old Moon"</li>
<li>February: "Snow Moon", also "Hunger Moon"</li>
<li>March: "Worm Moon", "Crow Moon", "Sap Moon", "Lenten Moon"</li>
<li>April: "Seed Moon", "Pink Moon", "Sprouting Grass Moon", "Egg Moon" (c.f. "Goose-Egg" in Beard 1918), "Fish Moon"</li>
<li>May: "Milk Moon", "Flower Moon", "Corn Planting Moon"</li>
<li>June: "Mead Moon", "Strawberry Moon" (c.f. Beard 1918), "Rose Moon", "Thunder Moon"</li>
<li>July: "Hay Moon", "Buck Moon", "Elk Moon", "Thunder Moon"</li>
<li>August: "Corn Moon", "Sturgeon Moon", "Red Moon", "Green Corn Moon", "Grain Moon"</li>
<li>September: "Harvest Moon", "Full Corn Moon",</li>
<li>October: "Hunter's moon", "Blood Moon"/"Sanguine Moon"</li>
<li>November: "Beaver Moon", "Frosty Moon"</li>
<li>December: "Oak Moon", "Cold Moon", "Long Night's Moon"</li>
</ul>
<p>The Long Night's Moon is the last of the year and the closest to the <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">winter solstice</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2016 the so-called "Strawberry Moon" coincided with the <a href="/wiki/Summer_solstice" title="Summer solstice">Summer solstice</a> for the first time since 1967, and would not return for another 46 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_and_lunisolar_calendars">Lunar and lunisolar calendars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Full_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lunar and lunisolar calendars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Christmas_full_moons_1977-2091.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="479" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Christmas_full_moons_1977-2091.gif/220px-Christmas_full_moons_1977-2091.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Christmas_full_moons_1977-2091.gif/330px-Christmas_full_moons_1977-2091.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Christmas_full_moons_1977-2091.gif/440px-Christmas_full_moons_1977-2091.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Christmas_full_moons_1977-2091.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The December 2015 <i>full moon</i> coincided with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christmas_day" title="Christmas day">Christmas day</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> This last occurred in 1977, with a Christmas Eve full moon between in 1996 and a full moon the day after Christmas in 2004 and before Christmas Eve in 2007 (both not shown here). A small horizontal <a href="/wiki/Libration" title="Libration">libration</a> is visible comparing their appearances. By the 19-year <a href="/wiki/Metonic_cycle" title="Metonic cycle">metonic cycle</a> the full moon will repeat again on Christmas day in 2034, 2053, 2072, and 2091.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></div>
</div>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lunar_calendar" title="Lunar calendar">Lunar calendar</a></div>
<p>Most pre-modern calendars the world over were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunisolar" title="Lunisolar">lunisolar</a>, combining the solar year with the lunation by means of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intercalary_month" title="Intercalary month">intercalary months</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The Julian calendar abandoned this method in favour of a purely solar reckoning while conversely the 7th-century <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">Islamic calendar</a> opted for a purely lunar one.</p>
<p>A continuing lunisolar calendar is in the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">Hebrew calendar</a>. Evidence of this is noted in the dates of <a href="/wiki/Passover" title="Passover">Passover</a> and <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a> in Judaism and Christianity, respectively. The date of the Jewish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rosh_Hashana" title="Rosh Hashana">Rosh Hashana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sukkot" title="Sukkot">Sukkot</a> festivals along with all other Jewish holidays are dependent on the dates of the new moons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intercalary_months">Intercalary months</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Full_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Intercalary months">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intercalary_month" title="Intercalary month">Intercalary month</a></div>
<p>In lunisolar calendars, an intercalary month occurs 7 times in the 19 years of the <a href="/wiki/Metonic_cycle" title="Metonic cycle">Metonic cycle</a>, or on average every 2.7 years (19/7). In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew_Calendar" title="Hebrew Calendar">Hebrew Calendar</a> this is noted with a periodic extra month of <a href="/wiki/Adar" title="Adar">Adar</a> in the early spring.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Blue_moon">Blue moon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Full_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Blue moon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Blue_moon" title="Blue moon">Blue moon</a></div>
<p>In the modern system of "traditional" full moon names tied to the <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">solstice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">equinox</a> points, a supernumerary full moon in such a period is called a blue moon. The term "blue moon" used in this sense may date to as early as the 16th century, but it became well known in the United States due to the <i>Farmers' Almanac</i> (published since 1818).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the <i>Farmers' Almanac</i>, a "blue moon" is the third full moon in any period between either solstice and equinox, or between equinox and solstice, (calculated using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_tropical_year" title="Mean tropical year">mean tropical year</a>), which contains four full moons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> These seasons are equal in length, unlike the astronomical ones, which vary in length depending on the Earth's speed in its elliptical orbit round the sun. To compare, in 1983 the equal length seasons began at 1.48 AM on 23 March, 9.15 AM on 22 June, 4.42 PM on 21 September and 12.10 AM on 22 December, while the astronomical seasons began at 4.39 AM on 21 March, 11.09 PM on 21 June, 2.42 PM on 23 September and 10.30 AM on 22 December (all times GMT). Due to a misinterpretation of this definition in the March 1946 <i><a href="/wiki/Sky_%26_Telescope" title="Sky &amp; Telescope">Sky &amp; Telescope</a></i> magazine, "blue moon" has also been used in the sense of "the second full moon in any month which contains two full moons (this usage has been noted as "erroneous" by <i>Sky &amp; Telescope</i> in 1999).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> According to either definition, "blue moons" occur with the average frequency of intercalary months, seven times in 19 years, the <i>Farmers' Almanac</i> system of "full moon names" effectively defining a lunisolar calendar.</p>
