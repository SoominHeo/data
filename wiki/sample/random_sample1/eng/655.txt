<p>The <b>Gwangju Uprising</b> , alternatively called <b>May 18 Democratic Uprising</b> by UNESCO,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and also known as <b>Gwangju Democratization Movement</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>: <span lang="ko-Hang">, 광주 민주화 운동</span>; <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">Hanja</a>: <span lang="ko-Hani">光州民主化運動</span>; <a href="/wiki/Revised_Romanization_of_Korean" title="Revised Romanization of Korean">RR</a>: <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Korean transliteration">Gwangju Minjuhwa Undong</span></i>), was a popular uprising in the city of <a href="/wiki/Gwangju" title="Gwangju">Gwangju, South Korea</a>, from May 18 to 27, 1980. Estimates suggest up to 606 people may have died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> During this period, Gwangju citizens took up arms (by robbing local armories and police stations) when local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeonnam_University" title="Jeonnam University">Jeonnam University</a> students – who were demonstrating against the Chun Doo-hwan government – were fired upon, killed, and beaten in an unprecedented attack by government troops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The uprising eventually ended in defeat on May 27, 1980. The event is sometimes called <b>5·18</b> (May 18; <a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>: <span lang="ko-Hang">오일팔</span>; <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">Hanja</a>: <span lang="ko-Hani">五一八</span>; <a href="/wiki/Revised_Romanization_of_Korean" title="Revised Romanization of Korean">RR</a>: <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Korean transliteration">Oilpal</span></i>), in reference to the date the movement began.</p>
<p>Some critics of the event point to the fact that it occurred before <a href="/wiki/Chun_Doo-hwan" title="Chun Doo-hwan">Chun Doo-hwan</a> officially took office, and so contend that it could not really have been a simple student protest against him that started it; however, Chun Doo-hwan had become the default leader of South Korea at that time since coming into power on December 12, 1979, after leading a successful military coup of the previous South Korean government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/Chun_Doo-hwan" title="Chun Doo-hwan">Chun Doo-hwan</a>'s presidency, the authorities used to define the incident as a rebellion instigated by Communist sympathizers and rioters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> By 1997, a national cemetery and day of commemoration (May 18), along with acts to "compensate, and restore honor" to victims, were established.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, 1980 Archives for the May 18th Democratic Uprising against Military Regime located in Gwangju city hall were inscribed on the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Memory_of_the_World_Register" title="Memory of the World Register">Memory of the World Register</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gwangju_Uprising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The series of turmoils in South Korea began with the <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_Park_Chung-hee" title="Assassination of Park Chung-hee">assassination of President Park Chung-hee</a> on October 26, 1979. The abrupt termination of Park's 18-year authoritarian rule left a power vacuum and led to political and social instability. While President <a href="/wiki/Choi_Kyu-hah" title="Choi Kyu-hah">Choi Kyu-hah</a>, the successor to the Presidency after Park's death, had no dominant control over the government, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Korea_Army" title="Republic of Korea Army">South Korean Army</a> major general <a href="/wiki/Chun_Doo-hwan" title="Chun Doo-hwan">Chun Doo-hwan</a>, the chief of the <a href="/wiki/Defense_Security_Command" title="Defense Security Command">Defense Security Command</a>, seized military power through the <a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat_of_December_Twelfth" title="Coup d'état of December Twelfth">Coup d'état of December Twelfth</a> and tried to intervene in the domestic issues. The military however could not explicitly reveal its political ambitions and had no obvious influence over the civil administration before the mass civil unrest in May 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The nation's democratization movements, which had been suppressed during Park's tenure, were being revived. With the beginning of a new semester in March 1980, professors and students expelled for pro-democracy activities returned to their universities, and student unions were formed. These unions led nationwide demonstrations for reforms, including an end to <a href="/wiki/Martial_law" title="Martial law">martial law</a> (declared after Park's assassination), democratization, minimum wage demands, and freedom of press.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> These activities culminated in the anti-martial law demonstration at <a href="/wiki/Seoul_Station" title="Seoul Station">Seoul Station</a> on May 15, 1980 in which about 100,000 students and citizens participated.</p>
<p>In response, Chun Doo-hwan took several suppressive measures. On May 17, Chun Doo-hwan forced the Cabinet to extend martial law to the whole nation, which had previously not applied to <a href="/wiki/Jeju_Province" title="Jeju Province">Jeju Province</a>. The extended martial law closed universities, banned political activities and further curtailed the press. To enforce martial law, troops were dispatched to various<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (June 2015)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> parts of the country. On the same day, The Defense Security Command raided a national conference of student union leaders from 55 universities, who were gathered to discuss their next moves in the wake of the May 15 demonstration. Twenty-six politicians, including <a href="/wiki/South_Jeolla_Province" title="South Jeolla Province">South Jeolla Province</a> native <a href="/wiki/Kim_Dae-jung" title="Kim Dae-jung">Kim Dae-jung</a>, were also arrested on charges of instigating demonstrations.</p>
<p>Ensuing strife focused in the South Jeolla Province, particularly in the then-provincial capital, <a href="/wiki/Gwangju" title="Gwangju">Gwangju</a>, for complex political and geographical reasons. These factors were both deep and contemporary:</p>
<dl>
<dd>[The Jeolla, or Honam] region is the granary of Korea. However, due to its abundant natural resources, the Jeolla area has historically been the target for exploitation by both domestic and foreign powers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Oppositional protest has existed in Korea historically—especially in the South Cheolla Province region—during the <a href="/wiki/Donghak_Peasant_Revolution" title="Donghak Peasant Revolution">Donghak Peasant Revolution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gwangju_Students_Movement" title="Gwangju Students Movement">Gwangju Students Movement</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yeosu%E2%80%93Suncheon_Rebellion" title="Yeosu–Suncheon Rebellion">Yeosu–Suncheon Rebellion</a>, regional resistance to the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_invasions_of_Korea_(1592%E2%80%9398)" title="Japanese invasions of Korea (1592–98)">Japanese invasions of Korea (1592–98)</a>, and more recently under the <a href="/wiki/Third_Republic_of_South_Korea" title="Third Republic of South Korea">Third Republic of South Korea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Republic_of_South_Korea" title="Fourth Republic of South Korea">Fourth Republic of South Korea</a>, as can be seen by the three excerpts below:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Park Chung Hee's dictatorship had showered economic and political favors on his native <a href="/wiki/Gyeongsang_Province" title="Gyeongsang Province">Gyeongsang Province</a> in the southeast, at the expense of the Jeolla region of the southwest. The latter became the real hotbed of political opposition to the dictatorship, which in turn led to more discrimination from the centre. Finally, in May 1980 the city of Gwangju in South Jeolla province exploded in a popular uprising against the new military strongman, General Chun Doo Hwan, who responded with a bloodbath that killed hundreds of Gwangju's citizens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>The city of Kwangju was subject to particularly severe and violent repression by the military after [nationwide] martial law was imposed. The denial of democracy and the heightening authoritarianism that accompanied the coming to power of Chun Doo Hwan to replace Park prompted nation-wide protests which, because of Cholla's [Jeolla's] historical legacy of dissent and radicalism, were most intense in that region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>"The Kwangju incident is not a communist riot but a righteous movement against oppression of democracy and freedom. Thus, the main force behind this noble movement is neither mobs nor communists. It was we, the democracy-loving Chonnam people who rose to protect our rights in the name of democracy." [Citizen's testimony]<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Timeline">Timeline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gwangju_Uprising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Timeline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="May_18.E2.80.9321">May 18–21</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gwangju_Uprising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: May 18–21">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Korea-Gwangju-The_former_South_Jeolla_provincial_office_building-01A.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="658" data-file-width="987" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Korea-Gwangju-The_former_South_Jeolla_provincial_office_building-01A.jpg/220px-Korea-Gwangju-The_former_South_Jeolla_provincial_office_building-01A.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Korea-Gwangju-The_former_South_Jeolla_provincial_office_building-01A.jpg/330px-Korea-Gwangju-The_former_South_Jeolla_provincial_office_building-01A.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Korea-Gwangju-The_former_South_Jeolla_provincial_office_building-01A.jpg/440px-Korea-Gwangju-The_former_South_Jeolla_provincial_office_building-01A.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Korea-Gwangju-The_former_South_Jeolla_provincial_office_building-01A.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>On the morning of May 18, students gathered at the gate of <a href="/wiki/Chonnam_National_University" title="Chonnam National University">Chonnam National University</a>, in defiance of its closing. By 9:30 am, around 200 students had arrived; they were opposed by 30 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paratroopers" title="Paratroopers">paratroopers</a>. At around 10 am, soldiers and students clashed: soldiers charged the students; students threw stones. The protest then moved to the downtown, Geumnamno (the street leading to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeollanamdo" title="Jeollanamdo">Jeollanamdo</a> Provincial Office), area. There the conflict broadened, to around 2000 participants by afternoon. Initially, police handled the Geumnamno protests; at 4 pm, though, the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Korea_Army_Special_Warfare_Command" title="Republic of Korea Army Special Warfare Command">ROK Special Warfare Command</a> (SWC) sent paratroopers to take over. The arrival of these 686 soldiers, from 33rd and 35th battalions of the 7th Airborne Brigade, marked a new, violent, and now infamous phase of suppression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Witnesses say soldiers clubbed both demonstrators and onlookers. Testimonies, photographs, and internal records attest to the use of bayonets. The first known fatality was a 29-year-old deaf man named Kim Gyeong-cheol, who was clubbed to death on May 18 while passing by the scene. As citizens were infuriated by the violence, the number of protesters rapidly increased and exceeded 10,000 by May 20.</p>
<p>As the conflict escalated, the army began to fire on citizens, killing an unknown number near <a href="/wiki/Gwangju_Station" title="Gwangju Station">Gwangju Station</a> on May 20. That same day, angered protesters burned down the local <a href="/wiki/Munhwa_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Munhwa Broadcasting Corporation">MBC</a> station, which had misreported the situation then unfolding in Gwangju (acknowledging only 1 civilian casualty, for example).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Four policemen were killed at a police barricade near the Provincial Government Building after a car rammed into them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the night of May 20, hundreds of taxis led a large parade of buses, large trucks and cars toward the Provincial Office to meet the protest. As the drivers drove in the demonstration, the troops used tear gas, pulled them out of the cars and beat them. These “drivers of democracy” showed up to support the citizens and the demonstration because of troop brutality witnessed earlier in the day, as well as out of anger after many taxi drivers were assaulted when trying to assist the injured and while taking people to the hospital. Some were shot after the drivers attempted to use the vehicles to block soldiers or as weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The violence climaxed on May 21. At about 1 pm, the army fired at a protesting crowd gathered in front of the Jeonnam Provincial Office, causing casualties. In response, some protesters raided armories and police stations in nearby towns and armed themselves with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M1_Garand_rifle" title="M1 Garand rifle">M1 rifles</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbines" title="Carbines">carbines</a>. Later that afternoon, bloody gunfights between civilian militias and the army broke out in the Provincial Office Square. By 5:30 pm, militias had acquired two light machine guns and used them against the army, which began to retreat from the downtown area.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="May_22.E2.80.9325">May 22–25</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gwangju_Uprising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: May 22–25">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Blockade_of_Gwangju.2C_and_further_atrocities">Blockade of Gwangju, and further atrocities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gwangju_Uprising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Blockade of Gwangju, and further atrocities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>At this point, all troops retreated to suburban areas, waiting for reinforcements. During this period the army blocked all routes and communications leading into and out of the city. Although there was a lull in fighting between militias and the army, more casualties were incurred when soldiers fired at a bus that attempted to break-out of the city in Jiwon-dong, killing 17 of the 18 passengers, on May 23. The following day soldiers mistook boys swimming in Wonje reservoir for attempted crossing and opened fire at them resulting in one death. Later that day the army suffered its heaviest casualties, when troops mistakenly fired at each other in Songam-dong.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Settlement_Committees">Settlement Committees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gwangju_Uprising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Settlement Committees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Meanwhile, in the "liberated" city of Gwangju, the Citizens' Settlement Committee and the Students' Settlement Committee were formed. The former was composed of about 20 preachers, lawyers and professors. They negotiated with the army demanding the release of arrested citizens, compensation for victims and prohibition of retaliation in exchange for disarmament of militias. The latter was formed by university students, and took charge of funerals, public campaigns, traffic control, withdrawal of weapons, and medical aid.</p>
<p>The city's order was well maintained, but negotiations came to a deadlock as the army urged the militias to immediately disarm themselves. This issue caused division within the Settlement Committees; some wanted immediate surrender, while others called for continued resistance until their demands were met. After heated debates, eventually those calling for continued resistance took control.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Protests_in_other_regions">Protests in other regions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gwangju_Uprising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Protests in other regions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As the news of the bloody crackdown spread, further protests against the government broke out in nearby regions including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hwasun" title="Hwasun">Hwasun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Naju" title="Naju">Naju</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haenam" title="Haenam">Haenam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mokpo" title="Mokpo">Mokpo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yeongam" title="Yeongam">Yeongam</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gangjin" title="Gangjin">Gangjin</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muan" title="Muan">Muan</a>. While protests ended peacefully in most regions, in Haenam there were gunfights between armed protesters and troops.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> By May 24, most of these protests had died down, except for Mokpo where protests continued until May 28.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="May_26">May 26</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gwangju_Uprising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: May 26">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By May 26, the army was ready to reenter the city. Members of the Citizens' Settlement Committee unsuccessfully tried to block the army's advance by lying down on the street. As the news of the imminent attack spread, civil militias gathered in the Provincial Office, preparing for the last stand.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="May_27">May 27</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gwangju_Uprising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: May 27">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At 4:00 am, troops from five divisions moved into the downtown area and defeated the civil militias within 90 minutes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Casualties">Casualties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gwangju_Uprising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Casualties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mangwol-dong-cemetery.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Mangwol-dong-cemetery.JPG/220px-Mangwol-dong-cemetery.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Mangwol-dong-cemetery.JPG/330px-Mangwol-dong-cemetery.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Mangwol-dong-cemetery.JPG/440px-Mangwol-dong-cemetery.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mangwol-dong-cemetery.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Mangwol-dong cemetery in Gwangju where victims' bodies were buried</div>

<p>There is no universally accepted death toll for the 1980 Gwangju Uprising. Official figures released by the Martial Law Command<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (April 2013)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> put the death toll at 144 civilians, 22 troops and 4 police killed, with 127 civilians, 109 troops and 144 police wounded. Individuals who attempted to dispute these figures were liable for arrest for "spreading false rumors."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the May 18 Bereaved Family Association, at least 165 people died between May 18 and 27. Another 76 are still missing and presumed dead. 23 soldiers and 4 policemen were killed during the uprising, including 13 soldiers killed in the friendly-fire incident between troops in Songam-dong. Figures for police casualties are likely to be higher, due to reports of several policemen being killed by soldiers for releasing captured rioters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The official figures have been criticized by some as being too low. Based on reports by foreign press sources and critics of the Chun Doo-hwan administration, it has been argued that the actual death toll was in the 1,000 to 2,000 range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gwangju_Uprising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The government denounced the uprising as a rebellion instigated by <a href="/wiki/Kim_Dae-jung" title="Kim Dae-jung">Kim Dae-jung</a> and his followers. In subsequent trials, Kim was convicted and sentenced to death, although his punishment was later reduced in response to international outcries. Overall 1,394 people were arrested for some involvement in the Gwangju incident and 427 were indicted. Among them, 7 received death sentences and 12 received life sentences.</p>
<p>The Gwangju Uprising had a profound impact on South Korean politics and history. Chun Doo-hwan already had popularity problems because he took power through a military coup, but after authorizing the dispatch of Special Forces upon citizens, his legitimacy was further damaged. The movement also paved the way for later movements in the 1980s that eventually brought democracy to South Korea. The Gwangju Uprising has become a symbol of South Koreans' struggle against authoritarian regimes and their fight for democracy.</p>
<p>Beginning in 2000, the May 18 Memorial Foundation has offered an annual <a href="/wiki/Gwangju_Prize_for_Human_Rights" title="Gwangju Prize for Human Rights">Gwangju Prize for Human Rights</a> to a notable human rights defender in memory of the uprising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GP_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-GP-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-American_sentiment">Anti-American sentiment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gwangju_Uprising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Anti-American sentiment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1980s marked a surge in <a href="/wiki/Anti-American_sentiment_in_Korea" title="Anti-American sentiment in Korea">Anti-Americanism in Korea</a>, widely traced to the events of May 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eroseffect.com_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-eroseffect.com-27">[27]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Cumings" title="Bruce Cumings">Bruce Cumings</a>:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Gwangju convinced a new generation of young [Koreans] that the democratic movement had developed not with the support of Washington, as an older generation of more conservative Koreans thought, but in the face of daily American support for any dictator who could quell the democratic aspirations of the Korean people. The result was an anti-American movement in the 1980s that threatened to bring down the whole structure of American support for the ROK. American cultural centers were burned to the ground (more than once in Gwangju); students immolated themselves in protest of Reagan's support for Chun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Fundamental to this movement was a perception of U.S. complicity in Chun's rise to power, and, more particularly, in the Gwangju Uprising itself. These matters remain controversial. It is clear, for example, that the U.S. authorized the ROK Army's 20th Division to re-take Gwangju – as acknowledged in a 1982 letter to the New York Times by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Ambassador_to_Korea" title="United States Ambassador to Korea">then-Ambassador</a> William H. Gleysteen.</p>
<dl>
<dd>[General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_A._Wickham,_Jr." title="John A. Wickham, Jr.">John A. Wickham</a>], with my concurrence, permitted transfer of well-trained troops of the twentieth ROKA Division from martial-law duty in Seoul to Gwangju because law and order had to be restored in a situation that had run amok following the outrageous behavior of the Korean Special Forces, which had never been under General Wickham's command.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>However, as Gwangju Uprising editors Scott-Stokes and Lee note, whether the expulsion of government troops left the situation lawless or "amok" is open to dispute. But the gravest questions pertain to the initial, triggering use of South Korean special forces. The United States has always denied foreknowledge of their deployment, most definitively in a June 19, 1989 white paper; that report additionally downplays Gleysteen's and others' characterizations of the U.S. actions.</p>
<dl>
<dd>...Ambassador Gleysteen has stated that the U.S. "approved" the movement of the 20th Division, and a U.S. Department of Defense spokesman on May 23, 1980 stated that the U.S. had "agreed" to release from OPCON [operational control] of the troops sent to Gwangju. Irrespective of the terminology, under the rights of national sovereignty the ROKG had the authority to deploy the 20th Division as it saw fit, once it had OPCON, regardless of the views of the U.S. Government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>However, the report is problematic in two respects: (1) Declassified documents (e.g. the "Cherokee files") contradict its claims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> (2) Its judicial focus skirts larger issues of the United States' support of the Chun regime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eroseffect.com_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-eroseffect.com-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reevaluation">Reevaluation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gwangju_Uprising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Reevaluation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mangwol-dong&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mangwol-dong (page does not exist)">Mangwol-dong</a> cemetery in Gwangju where victims' bodies were buried, survivors of the democratization movement and bereaved families have held an annual memorial service on May 18 every year since 1983. Many pro-democracy demonstrations in the 1980s demanded official recognition of the truth of the uprising and punishment for those responsible.</p>
<p>Official reevaluation began after the reinstatement of direct presidential elections in 1987. In 1988, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Korea" title="National Assembly of Korea">National Assembly</a> held a public hearing on the Gwangju Uprising, and officially renamed the incident as the Gwangju Uprising. While this official renaming occurred in 1987, it can also be found translated into English as "Gwangju People's Uprising".</p>
<p>In 1995, as public pressure mounted, the National Assembly passed the Special Law on May 18 Democratization Movement, which enabled prosecution of those responsible for the December 12 coup d'état and Gwangju Uprising despite the fact that the statute of limitations had run out. Subsequently, 8 politicians were indicted for high treason and the massacre in 1996. Their punishments were settled in 1997, including an initial death sentence, changed to a life sentence for Chun Doo-hwan. Former President Roh Tae-Woo, Chun's successor and fellow participant in the December 12 coup, was also sentenced to life in prison. But all convicts were pardoned in the name of national reconciliation on December 22 by President <a href="/wiki/Kim_Young-sam" title="Kim Young-sam">Kim Young-sam</a>, based on advice from then president-elect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kim_Dae-Jung" title="Kim Dae-Jung">Kim Dae-Jung</a>.</p>
<p>In 1997, May 18 was declared an official memorial day. In 2002, a law privileging bereaved families took effect, and the Mangwol-dong cemetery was elevated to the status of a national cemetery.</p>
<p>On May 18, 2013, President <a href="/wiki/Park_Geun-hye" title="Park Geun-hye">Park Geun-hye</a> attended the 33rd anniversary of the Gwangju uprising, and said "I feel the sorrow of family members and the city of Gwangju every time I visit the National May 18 Cemetery," "I believe achieving a more mature democracy is a way to repay the sacrifice paid by those [killed in the massacre.]"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jooogang20130520_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-jooogang20130520-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
