<p><b>Boris III, Tsar of Bulgaria</b> (<a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a>: <span lang="bg" xml:lang="bg">Борѝс III Обединител</span>; 30 January [<a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">O.S.</a> 18 January] 1894 – 28 August 1943), originally <b>Boris Klemens Robert Maria Pius Ludwig Stanislaus Xaver</b> (<b>Boris Clement Robert Mary Pius Louis Stanislaus Xavier</b>), son of <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I_of_Bulgaria" title="Ferdinand I of Bulgaria">Ferdinand I</a>, came to the throne in 1918 upon the <a href="/wiki/Abdication" title="Abdication">abdication</a> of his father, following the defeat of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bulgaria" title="Kingdom of Bulgaria">Kingdom of Bulgaria</a> during World War I. This was the country's second major defeat in only five years, after the disastrous <a href="/wiki/Second_Balkan_War" title="Second Balkan War">Second Balkan War</a> (1913). Under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Neuilly" title="Treaty of Neuilly">Treaty of Neuilly</a>, Bulgaria was forced to cede new territories and pay crippling reparations to its neighbours, thereby threatening political and economic stability. Two political forces, the Agrarian Union and the Communist Party, were calling for the overthrowing of the monarchy and the change of the government. It was in these circumstances that Boris succeeded to the throne. He distinguished himself during the Second World War by opposing attempts by <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> to deport the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Bulgaria" title="History of the Jews in Bulgaria">Jewish</a> population of his country.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boris_III_of_Bulgaria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:QG_Triplice_Macedoine.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="869" data-file-width="999" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/QG_Triplice_Macedoine.jpg/250px-QG_Triplice_Macedoine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/QG_Triplice_Macedoine.jpg/375px-QG_Triplice_Macedoine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/QG_Triplice_Macedoine.jpg/500px-QG_Triplice_Macedoine.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:QG_Triplice_Macedoine.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Boris (2nd from right) with German and Bulgarian commanders during World War I.</div>

<p>Boris was born on 30 January 1894 in <a href="/wiki/Sofia" title="Sofia">Sofia</a>. He was the first son of Prince <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I_of_Bulgaria" title="Ferdinand I of Bulgaria">Ferdinand of Bulgaria</a> and his wife Princess <a href="/wiki/Princess_Marie_Louise_of_Bourbon-Parma" title="Princess Marie Louise of Bourbon-Parma">Marie Louise</a>.</p>
<p>In February 1896, his father paved the way for the reconciliation of Bulgaria and <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russia</a> with the conversion of the infant Prince Boris from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" title="Roman Catholicism">Roman Catholicism</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Christianity" title="Eastern Orthodox Christianity">Eastern Orthodox Christianity</a>, a move that earned Ferdinand the frustration of his wife, the animosity of his Catholic Austrian relatives (particularly his uncle <a href="/wiki/Franz_Joseph_I_of_Austria" title="Franz Joseph I of Austria">Franz Joseph I of Austria</a>) and excommunication from the Catholic Church. In order to remedy this difficult situation, Ferdinand christened all his remaining children as Catholics. <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_II_of_Russia" title="Nicholas II of Russia">Nicholas II of Russia</a> stood as godfather to Boris and met the young boy during Ferdinand's official visit to <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a> in July 1898.</p>
<p>He received his initial education in the so-called Palace Secondary School, which Ferdinand had created in 1908 solely for his sons. Later, Boris graduated from the Military School in <a href="/wiki/Sofia" title="Sofia">Sofia</a>, then took part in the <a href="/wiki/Balkan_Wars" title="Balkan Wars">Balkan Wars</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a>, he served as <a href="/wiki/Liaison_officer" title="Liaison officer">liaison officer</a> of the General Staff of the Bulgarian Army on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macedonian_front" title="Macedonian front">Macedonian front</a>. In 1916, he was promoted to <a href="/wiki/Colonel" title="Colonel">colonel</a> and attached again as liaison officer to <a href="/wiki/Army_Group_Mackensen_(Romania)" title="Army Group Mackensen (Romania)">Army Group Mackensen</a> and the Bulgarian <a href="/wiki/Third_Army_(Bulgaria)" title="Third Army (Bulgaria)">Third Army</a> for the operations against <a href="/wiki/Romania_during_World_War_I" title="Romania during World War I">Romania</a>. Boris worked hard to smooth the sometimes difficult relations between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Field_Marshal" title="Field Marshal">Field Marshal</a> <a href="/wiki/August_von_Mackensen" title="August von Mackensen">Mackensen</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant_General" title="Lieutenant General">Lieutenant General</a> <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Toshev" title="Stefan Toshev">Stefan Toshev</a>, the commander of the Third Army. Through his courage and personal example, he earned the respect of the troops and the senior Bulgarian and German commanders, even that of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generalquartiermeister" title="Generalquartiermeister">Generalquartiermeister</a> of the German Army, <a href="/wiki/Erich_Ludendorff" title="Erich Ludendorff">Erich Ludendorff</a>, who preferred dealing personally with Boris and described him as excellently trained, a thoroughly soldierly person and mature beyond his years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ludendorf_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ludendorf-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1918, Boris was made a <a href="/wiki/Major_general" title="Major general">major general</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_reign">Early reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boris_III_of_Bulgaria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

The Royal Sceptre of Boris III</div>

<p>With the abdication of his father, he acceded to the throne as Tsar Boris III on 3 October 1918.</p>
<p>One year after Boris's accession, <a href="/wiki/Aleksandar_Stamboliyski" title="Aleksandar Stamboliyski">Aleksandar Stamboliyski</a> (or <i>Stambolijski</i>) of the Bulgarian People's Agrarian Union was elected prime minister. Though popular with the large peasant class, Stambolijski earned the animosity of the middle class and military, which led to his toppling in a military coup on 9 June 1923, and his subsequent assassination. On 14 April 1925, an anarchist group attacked Boris's cavalcade as it passed through the <a href="/wiki/Arabakonak" title="Arabakonak">Arabakonak</a> Pass. Two days later, a bomb killed 150 members of the Bulgarian political and military elite in Sofia as they attended the funeral of a murdered general (see <a href="/wiki/St_Nedelya_Church_assault" title="St Nedelya Church assault">St Nedelya Church assault</a>). Following a further attempt on Boris's life the same year, military reprisals killed several thousand communists and agrarians, including representatives of the intelligentsia. Finally, in October 1925, there was a short border war with Greece, known as the <a href="/wiki/Incident_at_Petrich" title="Incident at Petrich">Incident at Petrich</a>, which was resolved with the help of the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a>.</p>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat_of_1934" title="Bulgarian coup d'état of 1934">coup on 19 May 1934</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Zveno" title="Zveno">Zveno</a> military organisation established a dictatorship and abolished political parties in Bulgaria. King Boris was reduced to the status of a puppet king as a result of the coup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The following year, he staged a counter-coup and assumed control of the country. The political process was controlled by the Tsar, but a form of parliamentary rule was re-introduced, without the restoration of the political parties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> With the rise of the "King's government" in 1935, Bulgaria entered an era of prosperity and astounding growth, which deservedly qualifies it as the Golden Age of the Third Bulgarian Kingdom. It lasted nearly five years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_and_issue">Marriage and issue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boris_III_of_Bulgaria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marriage and issue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Boris married <a href="/wiki/Giovanna_of_Italy" title="Giovanna of Italy">Giovanna of Italy</a>, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Victor_Emmanuel_III_of_Italy" title="Victor Emmanuel III of Italy">Victor Emmanuel III of Italy</a>, first in a Catholic ceremony in <a href="/wiki/Assisi" title="Assisi">Assisi</a>, Italy on 25 October 1930 (attended by <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a>), and then at an Orthodox ceremony in Sofia. The marriage produced a daughter, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marie_Louise_of_Bulgaria" title="Marie Louise of Bulgaria">Maria Louisa</a>, in January 1933, and a son and heir to the throne, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simeon_II_of_Bulgaria" title="Simeon II of Bulgaria">Simeon</a>, in 1937.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_World_War">Second World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boris_III_of_Bulgaria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Second World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early days of the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a>, Bulgaria was neutral, but powerful groups in the country swayed its politics towards Germany (with which Bulgaria had been allied in the First World War). As a result of peace treaties that ended the First World War (the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Versailles" title="Treaty of Versailles">Treaty of Versailles</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Neuilly" title="Treaty of Neuilly">Treaty of Neuilly</a>), Bulgaria, which had fought on the losing side, lost two important territories to neighboring countries: the northern plain of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dobrudja" title="Dobrudja">Dobrudja</a> to Romania, and <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a> to Greece. The Bulgarians considered these treaties an insult and wanted the lands restored. When <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> rose to power, he tried to win Bulgarian King Boris III’s allegiance. In the summer of 1940, after a year of war, Hitler hosted diplomatic talks between Bulgaria and Romania in Vienna. On 7 September, an agreement was signed for the return of South <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dobrudja" title="Dobrudja">Dobrudja</a> to Bulgaria. The Bulgarian nation rejoiced. In March 1941, Boris allied himself with the <a href="/wiki/Axis_powers" title="Axis powers">Axis powers</a>, thus recovering most of <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(region)" title="Macedonia (region)">Macedonia</a> and Aegean <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a>, as well as protecting his country from being crushed by the German <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a> like neighboring <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>. For recovering these territories, Tsar Boris was called the Unifier (Bulgarian: Цар Обединител). Tsar Boris appeared on the cover of <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> on 20 January 1941 wearing a full military uniform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite this alliance, and the German presence in <a href="/wiki/Sofia" title="Sofia">Sofia</a> and along the railway line which passed through the Bulgarian capital to Greece, Boris was not willing to provide full and unconditional cooperation with Germany. He refused to send regular Bulgarian troops to fight the Soviet Union on the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Front_(World_War_II)" title="Eastern Front (World War II)">Eastern Front</a> alongside Germany and the other Axis belligerents, and also refused to allow unofficial volunteers (such as Spain's <a href="/wiki/Blue_Division" title="Blue Division">Blue Division</a>) to participate either, although the German legation in Sofia received 1,500 requests from young Bulgarian men who wanted to fight against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolshevism" title="Bolshevism">Bolshevism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>But there was a price to be paid for the return of Dobrudja. This was the adoption of the anti-Jewish "<a href="/wiki/Law_for_Protection_of_the_Nation" title="Law for Protection of the Nation">Law for Protection of the Nation</a>" (Закон за защита на нацията — ЗЗН) on 24 December 1940. This law was in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Laws" title="Nuremberg Laws">Nuremberg Laws</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> and the rest of Hitler's occupied Europe. Bulgarian Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Bogdan_Filov" title="Bogdan Filov">Bogdan Filov</a> and Interior Minister <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petur_Gabrovski" title="Petur Gabrovski">Petur Gabrovski</a>, both Nazi sympathisers, were the architects of this law, which restricted Jewish rights, imposed new taxes, and established a quota for Jews in some professions. Many Bulgarians protested in letters to their government. In March 1941, Bulgaria signed the Tripartite Pact and joined the Axis in hopes of regaining the territories of Macedonia and Thrace. Tsar Boris signed it into law on 21 January 1941.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Law_for_Protection_of_the_Nation.jpg">[1]</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Holocaust">The Holocaust</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boris_III_of_Bulgaria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Holocaust">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In early 1943, Hitler's emissary, <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Dannecker" title="Theodor Dannecker">Theodor Dannecker</a>, arrived in Bulgaria. Dannecker was an SS Hauptsturmführer and one of <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Eichmann" title="Adolf Eichmann">Adolf Eichmann</a>'s associates who guided the campaign for the deportation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Jews" title="French Jews">French Jews</a> to death camps. In February 1943, Dannecker met with the Commissar for Jewish Affairs in Bulgaria, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Belev" title="Alexander Belev">Alexander Belev</a>, famous for his antisemitic and strong nationalist views. They held closed-door meetings and ended with a secret agreement signed on 22 February 1943 for the deportations of 20,000 Jews from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aegean_Thrace" title="Aegean Thrace">Aegean Thrace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vardar_Macedonia" title="Vardar Macedonia">Vardar Macedonia</a>. These were the territories conquered by Germany and legally not under Bulgarian jurisdiction at the time. The Jewish people in these territories were citizens of <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a>. Several days later, it became clear that the number of Jews in Aegean Thrace and Vardar Macedonia was 11,343, which was short of the 20,000 quota. The revised pact called for making up this shortfall with another 8,000 from Bulgaria proper. The remaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Jews" title="Bulgarian Jews">Bulgarian Jews</a> were to be deported later.</p>
<p>The initial roundups were to begin on 9 March 1943. Boxcars were lined up in <a href="/wiki/Kyustendil" title="Kyustendil">Kyustendil</a>, a town on the western border. But as the news about the imminent deportations leaked out, protests arose throughout Bulgaria. On the morning of 9 March, a delegation from Kyustendil, composed of eminent public figures and headed by <a href="/wiki/Dimitar_Peshev" title="Dimitar Peshev">Dimitar Peshev</a>, the deputy speaker of the National Assembly, met with Interior Minister <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petur_Gabrovski" title="Petur Gabrovski">Petur Gabrovski</a>. Facing strong opposition within the country, Gabrovski relented. The same day, he sent telegrams to the roundup centers cancelling the deportations.</p>
<p>In a report of 5 April 1943, Adolph Hoffman, a German government adviser and police attache at the German legation in Sofia (1943–44) wrote: "The Minister of Interior has received instruction from the highest place to stop the planned deportation of Jews from the old borders of Bulgaria". In fact, Gabrovski's decision was not taken on his own "personal initiative", but had come from the highest authority—King Boris III, who at the risk of direct confrontation with the Reich, refused to deport the Jews. Four hours before the deadline, the order was cancelled. While Jews living in <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a> proper were saved, the 11,343 Jews from Vardar Macedonia and Thrace were deported to the death camps of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treblinka" title="Treblinka">Treblinka</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Majdanek" title="Majdanek">Majdanek</a>. The Jewish subjects of these new territories were considered exiles under Hitler's military command and direct jurisdiction. Bulgaria administered these lands, but Nazi Germany had not formally annexed them to Bulgaria and their status was to be resolved only after the war.</p>
<p>Still reluctant to comply with the German deportation request, the royal palace utilised Swiss diplomatic channels to inquire whether it was possible to deport the Jews to British-controlled Palestine by ship rather than to concentration camps in Poland by train. This was blocked by the British Foreign Secretary, <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Eden" title="Anthony Eden">Anthony Eden</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Aware of Bulgaria's unreliability on the Jewish matter, the Nazis grew more suspicious about the quiet activities in aid of European Jewry of an old friend of King Boris, Monsignor Angelo Roncalli (the future <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XXIII" title="Pope John XXIII">Pope John XXIII</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Papal_diplomacy" title="Papal diplomacy">Papal Nuncio</a> in <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>. Reporting on the humanitarian efforts of Roncalli, his secretary in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Vatican</a>, Monsignor <a href="/wiki/Loris_Francesco_Capovilla" title="Loris Francesco Capovilla">Loris F. Capovilla</a> writes: "Through his intervention, and with the help of King Boris III of Bulgaria, thousands of Jews from <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>, who had first been sent to <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> and then to Bulgaria, and who were in danger of being sent to <a href="/wiki/Nazi_concentration_camps" title="Nazi concentration camps">Nazi concentration camps</a>, obtained transit visas for <a href="/wiki/Palestine_(region)" title="Palestine (region)">Palestine</a> signed by him." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meetings_with_Hitler">Meetings with Hitler</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boris_III_of_Bulgaria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Meetings with Hitler">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nazi pressure on King Boris III continued for the deportation of the Bulgarian Jewry. At the end of March, Hitler invited the King to visit him. Upon returning home, King Boris ordered able-bodied Jewish men to join hard labor units to build roads within the interior of his kingdom. It is widely believed this was the King's attempt to avoid deporting them. In May 1943, Dannecker and Belev, the Commissar for Jewish Affairs, planned the deportation of 50,000 Bulgarian Jews, to be loaded on steamers on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_Danube" title="River Danube">River Danube</a>. Boris III continued the cat-and-mouse game that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Jews" title="Bulgarian Jews">Bulgarian Jews</a> were needed for the construction of roads and railway lines inside his kingdom. Nazi officials requested that Bulgaria deport its Jewish population to German-occupied <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>. The request caused a public outcry, and a campaign whose most prominent leaders were Parliament vice-chairman <a href="/wiki/Dimitar_Peshev" title="Dimitar Peshev">Dimitar Peshev</a> and the head of the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Orthodox_Church" title="Bulgarian Orthodox Church">Bulgarian Orthodox Church</a>, Archbishop Stefan, was organised. Following this campaign, Boris III refused to permit the extradition of Bulgaria's 50,000 Jews.</p>
<p>On 30 June 1943, Apostolic Delegate Angelo Roncalli, the future <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XXIII" title="Pope John XXIII">Pope John XXIII</a>, wrote to King Boris, asking for mercy for "the sons of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_people" title="Jewish people">Jewish people</a>." He wrote that King Boris should on no account agree to that dishonorable action. On the copy of the letter, the future pope noted, by hand, that the King replied verbally to his message. The note states "Il Re ha fatto qualche cosa" ("The King did something") and also noting the difficult situation of the monarch, Mgr. Roncalli stressed once again "Però, ripeto, ha fatto" ("But I repeat, he has acted").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>An excerpt from the diary of Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Zion" title="Daniel Zion">Daniel Zion</a>, the spiritual leader of the Jewish community in Bulgaria during the war years, reads:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Do not be afraid, dear brothers and sisters! Trust in the Holy Rock of our salvation ... Yesterday I was informed by Bishop Stephen about his conversation with the Bulgarian king. When I went to see Bishop Stephen, he said: "Tell your people, the King has promised, that the Bulgarian Jews shall not leave the borders of Bulgaria ...". When I returned to the synagogue, silence reigned in anticipation of the outcome of my meeting with Bishop Stephen. When I entered, my words were: "Yes, my brethren, God heard our prayers ...<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Most irritating for Hitler was the Tsar's refusal to declare war on the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> or send Bulgarian troops to the Eastern Front. On 9 August 1943, Hitler summoned Boris to a stormy meeting at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rastenburg" title="Rastenburg">Rastenburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Prussia" title="East Prussia">East Prussia</a>. Tsar Boris arrived by plane from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vrazhdebna" title="Vrazhdebna">Vrazhdebna</a> on 14 August. The King asserted his stance once again not to send Bulgarian Jews to death camps in Poland and Germany. While Bulgaria had declared a 'symbolic' war on the distant United Kingdom and United States, at that meeting Boris once again refused to get involved in the war against the Soviet Union, giving two major reasons for his unwillingness to send troops to Russia. First, many ordinary Bulgarians had strong pro-Russian sentiments; and second, the political and military position of Turkey remained unclear. The 'symbolic' war against the Western Allies turned into a disaster for the citizens of <a href="/wiki/Sofia" title="Sofia">Sofia</a>, as the city was heavily bombarded by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Army_Air_Force" title="US Army Air Force">US Army Air Force</a> and the British <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> in 1943 and 1944. Nevertheless, the bombardments started only after Boris's death.</p>
<p>Bulgaria’s opposition came to a head at this last official meeting between Hitler and King Boris III in August 1943. Reports of the meeting indicate that Hitler was furious with the King for refusing to join the war against the USSR and to deport the Jews within his kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> At the end of the meeting, it was agreed that "the Bulgarian Jews were not to be deported for King Boris had insisted that the Jews were needed for various laboring tasks including road maintenance." This act of bravery displayed by King Boris saved all 50,000 Jews of Bulgaria.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boris_III_of_Bulgaria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Shortly after returning to Sofia from a meeting with Hitler, Boris died of apparent heart failure on 28 August 1943.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> According to the diary of the German attache in Sofia at the time, Colonel von Schoenebeck, the two German doctors who attended the King – Sajitz and <a href="/wiki/Hans_Eppinger" title="Hans Eppinger">Hans Eppinger</a> – both believed that he had died from the same poison that Dr. Eppinger had allegedly found two years earlier in the postmortem examination of the Greek Prime Minister, <a href="/wiki/Ioannis_Metaxas" title="Ioannis Metaxas">Ioannis Metaxas</a>, a slow poison which takes weeks to do its work and which causes the appearance of blotches on the skin of its victim before death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Boris was succeeded by his six-year-old son <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simeon_II_of_Bulgaria" title="Simeon II of Bulgaria">Simeon II</a>, under a Regency Council headed by Boris's brother <a href="/wiki/Prince_Kiril_of_Bulgaria" title="Prince Kiril of Bulgaria">Prince Kiril of Bulgaria</a>.</p>
<p>Following a large, impressive state funeral at the <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Nevsky_Cathedral,_Sofia" title="Alexander Nevsky Cathedral, Sofia">Alexander Nevsky Cathedral, Sofia</a>, where the streets were lined with weeping crowds, the coffin of Tsar Boris III was taken by train to the mountains and buried in Bulgaria's largest and most important monastery, the <a href="/wiki/Rila_Monastery" title="Rila Monastery">Rila Monastery</a>. After taking power in September 1944, the Communist-dominated government had his body exhumed and secretly buried in the courtyard of <a href="/wiki/Vrana_Palace" title="Vrana Palace">Vrana Palace</a>, near Sofia. At a later time, the Communist authorities moved the zinc coffin from Vrana to a secret location, which remains unknown to this day. After the fall of communism, an excavation was made at Vrana Palace. Only Boris's heart was found, as it had been put in a glass cylinder outside the coffin. The heart was taken by his widow in 1993 to Rila Monastery, where it was reinterred.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> proclaimed King Boris III savior of 50,000 Bulgarian Jews on 12 May 1994.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>King Boris III was posthumously awarded the Jewish National Fund's Medal of the Legion of Honor Award, the first non-Jew to receive one of the Jewish community's highest honors.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Anti-Defamation_League" title="Anti-Defamation League">Anti-Defamation League</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chabad" title="Chabad">Chabad</a> have also honored King Boris III for refusing to sacrifice his Jewish subjects to the Nazis.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tsar_Boris_III_Boulevard&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tsar Boris III Boulevard (page does not exist)">Tsar Boris III Boulevard</a> is one of the main boulevards in <a href="/wiki/Sofia" title="Sofia">Sofia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Varna" title="Varna">Varna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plovdiv" title="Plovdiv">Plovdiv</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Borisova_gradina" title="Borisova gradina">Borisova gradina</a> is the largest park in Sofia.</p>
<p>In 1998, to thank Tsar Boris, Bulgarian Jews in the United States and the Jewish National Fund erected a monument in "The Bulgarian Forest" in Israel, honoring Tsar Boris as a savior of Bulgarian Jews. In July 2003, a public committee headed by Israeli Chief Justice Dr. Moshe Beiski decided to remove the memorial from "The Bulgarian Forest," because Bulgaria had consented to the delivery of the Jews from occupied territory of Macedonia and Thrace to the Germans.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.yumpu.com/en/document/view/12340342/clarifying-70-years-of-whitewashing-and-shelomo-alfassa" rel="nofollow">[2]</a></p>
