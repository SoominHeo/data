<p><b>Robert Henry</b> "<b>Bobby</b>" <b>Timmons</b> (December 19, 1935 – March 1, 1974) was an American <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> pianist and composer. He was a <a href="/wiki/Sideman" title="Sideman">sideman</a> in <a href="/wiki/Art_Blakey" title="Art Blakey">Art Blakey</a>'s Jazz Messengers for two periods (July 1958 to September 1959; February 1960 to June 1961), between which he was part of <a href="/wiki/Cannonball_Adderley" title="Cannonball Adderley">Cannonball Adderley</a>'s band. Several of Timmons' compositions written when part of these bands – including "<a href="/wiki/Moanin%27_(song)" title="Moanin' (song)">Moanin'</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Dat_Dere" title="Dat Dere">Dat Dere</a>", and "This Here" – enjoyed commercial success and brought him more attention. In the early and mid-1960s he led a series of piano trios that toured and recorded extensively.</p>
<p>Timmons was strongly associated with the <a href="/wiki/Soul_jazz" title="Soul jazz">soul jazz</a> style that he helped initiate; this link to apparently simple writing and playing, coupled with drug and alcohol addiction, led to a decline in his career. Timmons died, aged 38, from <a href="/wiki/Cirrhosis" title="Cirrhosis">cirrhosis</a>. Several critics have commented that his contribution to jazz remains undervalued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[a]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bobby_Timmons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Timmons was born in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, the son of a minister.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grove_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grove-2">[1]</a></sup> He had a sister, Eleanor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TP2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-TP2-3">[2]</a></sup> Both of his parents, and several aunts and uncles, played the piano.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Comp_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Comp-4">[3]</a></sup> From an early age Timmons studied music with an uncle, Robert Habershaw, who also taught <a href="/wiki/McCoy_Tyner" title="McCoy Tyner">McCoy Tyner</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Comp_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Comp-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> Timmons first played at the church where his grandfather was minister;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TP1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-TP1-6">[5]</a></sup> this influenced his later jazz playing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grove_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grove-2">[1]</a></sup> He grew up in the same area as other future musicians, including the Heath brothers, <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Heath" title="Jimmy Heath">Jimmy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Percy_Heath" title="Percy Heath">Percy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Albert_Heath" title="Albert Heath">Tootie</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TP1_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-TP1-6">[5]</a></sup> Timmons' first professional performances were in his local area,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feather_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Feather-7">[6]</a></sup> often as a trio that included Tootie Heath on drums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TP2_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-TP2-3">[2]</a></sup> After graduating from high school Timmons was awarded a scholarship to study at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_the_Arts_(Philadelphia)" title="University of the Arts (Philadelphia)">Philadelphia Musical Academy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Comp_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Comp-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1954_to_spring_1961">1954 to spring 1961</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bobby_Timmons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1954 to spring 1961">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Timmons moved to New York in 1954.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grove_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Grove-2">[1]</a></sup> He played with <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Dorham" title="Kenny Dorham">Kenny Dorham</a> in 1956,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feather_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Feather-7">[6]</a></sup> making his recording debut with the trumpeter in a live set in May of that year. He went on to play and record with <a href="/wiki/Chet_Baker" title="Chet Baker">Chet Baker</a> in 1956–57 (bassist <a href="/wiki/Scott_LaFaro" title="Scott LaFaro">Scott LaFaro</a> was part of this band for a time<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup>), <a href="/wiki/Sonny_Stitt" title="Sonny Stitt">Sonny Stitt</a> in 1957, and <a href="/wiki/Maynard_Ferguson" title="Maynard Ferguson">Maynard Ferguson</a> in 1957–58.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grove_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Grove-2">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feather_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Feather-7">[6]</a></sup> He also recorded as a <a href="/wiki/Sideman" title="Sideman">sideman</a> with hornmen <a href="/wiki/Curtis_Fuller" title="Curtis Fuller">Curtis Fuller</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hank_Mobley" title="Hank Mobley">Hank Mobley</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Lee_Morgan" title="Lee Morgan">Lee Morgan</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> all for <a href="/wiki/Blue_Note_Records" title="Blue Note Records">Blue Note Records</a> in 1957.</p>
<p>Timmons became best known as a member of <a href="/wiki/Art_Blakey" title="Art Blakey">Art Blakey</a>'s band the Jazz Messengers, which he was first part of from July 1958 to September 1959, including for a tour of Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grove_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Grove-2">[1]</a></sup> He was recruited for the Messengers by saxophonist <a href="/wiki/Benny_Golson" title="Benny Golson">Benny Golson</a>, who said that "He was inventive, [...] He could play <a href="/wiki/Bebop" title="Bebop">bebop</a> and he could play funky – he could play a lot of things, and I thought it was the element that Art needed. He hadn't had anybody quite like Bobby, who could go here or go there, rather than walking in a single corridor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TP1_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-TP1-6">[5]</a></sup> By late 1958 Timmons was sharing bandmate Morgan's East Sixth Street apartment and the pair had bought a piano, allowing Timmons to practice and Morgan to work on composing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSM_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-JSM-12">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:88</sup> From around the time he joined Blakey, Timmons, along with some of his fellow band members, was a heroin user.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSM_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-JSM-12">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:90</sup> After leaving Blakey, Timmons joined <a href="/wiki/Cannonball_Adderley" title="Cannonball Adderley">Cannonball Adderley</a>'s band, in October 1959.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grove_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Grove-2">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Timmons was also known as a composer during this period: <i>The Encyclopedia of Jazz</i> states that his compositions "<a href="/wiki/Moanin%27_(song)" title="Moanin' (song)">Moanin'</a>" (from <a href="/wiki/Moanin%27" title="Moanin'">the 1958 album of the same title</a>), "This Here", and "<a href="/wiki/Dat_Dere" title="Dat Dere">Dat Dere</a>" "helped generate the gospel-tinged '<a href="/wiki/Soul_jazz" title="Soul jazz">soul jazz</a>' style of [the] late '50s and early '60s."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feather_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Feather-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:646</sup> The first was written when Timmons was first with Blakey; the others were composed when he was with Adderley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-All_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-All-13">[12]</a></sup> "This Here" (sometimes "Dis Here") was a surprise commercial success for Adderley: recorded in concert in 1959, it was released as part of the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cannonball_Adderley_Quintet_in_San_Francisco" title="The Cannonball Adderley Quintet in San Francisco">The Cannonball Adderley Quintet in San Francisco</a></i> album while the band was still on tour, and they discovered its popularity only when they arrived back in New York and found crowds outside the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Village_Gate" title="The Village Gate">Village Gate</a>, where they were due to play.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dis_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dis-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Timmons was reported to be dissatisfied with the money he had received from "This Here", and was enticed in February 1960 into leaving Adderley and returning to Blakey's band by the offer of more pay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grove_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Grove-2">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dis_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dis-14">[13]</a></sup> Timmons then appeared on further well-known albums with the drummer, including <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_Night_in_Tunisia_(1960_album)" title="A Night in Tunisia (1960 album)">A Night in Tunisia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Freedom_Rider" title="The Freedom Rider">The Freedom Rider</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Witch_Doctor_(album)" title="The Witch Doctor (album)">The Witch Doctor</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TP1_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-TP1-6">[5]</a></sup> His own recording debut as sole leader was <i><a href="/wiki/This_Here_Is_Bobby_Timmons" title="This Here Is Bobby Timmons">This Here Is Bobby Timmons</a></i> in 1960, which contained his first versions of his best-known compositions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Myers_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Myers-15">[14]</a></sup> In the same year, he played on recordings led by <a href="/wiki/Nat_Adderley" title="Nat Adderley">Nat Adderley</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Penguin_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Penguin-16">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:13</sup> <a href="/wiki/Arnett_Cobb" title="Arnett Cobb">Arnett Cobb</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Penguin_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Penguin-16">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:307</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Griffin" title="Johnny Griffin">Johnny Griffin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Penguin_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Penguin-16">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:670</sup> among others; on the first of these, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Work_Song_(Nat_Adderly_album)" title="Work Song (Nat Adderly album)">Work Song</a></i>, Timmons did not appear on all of the tracks, because he had been drinking heavily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Summer_1961_to_1974">Summer 1961 to 1974</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bobby_Timmons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Summer 1961 to 1974">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Timmons left Blakey for the second time in June 1961,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grove_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Grove-2">[1]</a></sup> encouraged by the success of his compositions, including <a href="/wiki/Jukebox" title="Jukebox">jukebox</a> plays of "Dat Dere", which <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Brown" title="Oscar Brown">Oscar Brown</a> had recorded after adding lyrics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TP1_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-TP1-6">[5]</a></sup> Timmons then formed his own bands, initially with <a href="/wiki/Ron_Carter" title="Ron Carter">Ron Carter</a> on bass and Tootie Heath on drums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> They toured around the US, including the West Coast, but played most in and around New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TP2_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-TP2-3">[2]</a></sup> In the initial stages of this trio, Timmons liked the group sounds of the trios led by <a href="/wiki/Red_Garland" title="Red Garland">Red Garland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ahmad_Jamal" title="Ahmad Jamal">Ahmad Jamal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TP3_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-TP3-19">[18]</a></sup> According to Tootie Heath, Timmons was at the peak of his fame at that point, but was addicted to heroin, and used a lot of the money that the band was paid maintaining his habit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EI_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-EI-20">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1963 Timmons' playing, with Lewis Powers on bass and Ron McCurdy on drums, was described by a <i>Washington Post</i> reviewer as "flexible and adventuresome [...] Glossing over everything is an undeniable sheen of church music and spirituals."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> In 1965 the same reviewer commented that Timmons was employing musicians who were of much lower ability: "Timmons lacks a certain passion but I wonder if this is not the fault of his sidemen."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> Timmons started playing <a href="/wiki/Vibraphone" title="Vibraphone">vibes</a> in the mid-1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP74_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-WP74-23">[22]</a></sup> He occasionally played organ, but recorded only one track on that instrument – a 1964 version of "Moanin'" on <i><a href="/wiki/From_the_Bottom" title="From the Bottom">From the Bottom</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-All_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-All-13">[12]</a></sup> Recordings as a leader continued, usually as part of a trio or quartet, but, after joining <a href="/wiki/Milestone_Records" title="Milestone Records">Milestone Records</a> around 1967,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> Timmons' album <i><a href="/wiki/Got_to_Get_It!" title="Got to Get It!">Got to Get It!</a></i> featured him as part of a nonet, playing arrangements by <a href="/wiki/Tom_McIntosh" title="Tom McIntosh">Tom McIntosh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-All_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-All-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Timmons' career declined quickly in the 1960s, in part because of drug abuse<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GG_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-GG-25">[24]</a></sup> and alcoholism and partly as a result of frustration at being typecast as a composer and player of seemingly simple pieces of music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grove_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-Grove-2">[1]</a></sup> In 1968 he made his second, final, recording for Milestone, <i><a href="/wiki/Do_You_Know_the_Way%3F" title="Do You Know the Way?">Do You Know the Way?</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-All_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-All-13">[12]</a></sup> In the following year he played in a quartet led by <a href="/wiki/Sonny_Red" title="Sonny Red">Sonny Red</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Dexter_Gordon" title="Dexter Gordon">Dexter Gordon</a> on one of the saxophonist's temporary returns to the US from Europe,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogle_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bogle-27">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> and in a trio backing vocalist <a href="/wiki/Etta_Jones" title="Etta Jones">Etta Jones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup> Timmons continued to play in the early 1970s, mostly in small groups or in combination with other pianists, and mainly in the New York area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to saxophonist Jimmy Heath, Timmons joined <a href="/wiki/Clark_Terry" title="Clark Terry">Clark Terry</a>'s big band for a tour of Europe in 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heath_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heath-34">[33]</a></sup> He was unwell and drank on the plane to Sweden, and fell while drinking at the bar before the band's first concert, in <a href="/wiki/Malm%C3%B6" title="Malmö">Malmö</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heath_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Heath-34">[33]</a></sup> Susceptible to blood clotting, he was flown back to the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heath_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-Heath-34">[33]</a></sup> On March 1, 1974, he died from <a href="/wiki/Cirrhosis" title="Cirrhosis">cirrhosis</a>, at the age of 38, at St Vincent's Hospital in New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feather_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Feather-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT74_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT74-35">[34]</a></sup> He had been in hospital for a month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP74_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-WP74-23">[22]</a></sup> He was buried in Philadelphia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup> and was survived by his wife, Estelle, and son, also Bobby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT74_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT74-35">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Playing_style_and_influence">Playing style and influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bobby_Timmons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Playing style and influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Timmons was known for using <a href="/wiki/Block_chord" title="Block chord">block chords</a>, "a style in which the right hand creates the melody and the left hand moves with the rhythm of the right hand, but does not change voicing except to accommodate the chord changes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Champ_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Champ-37">[36]</a></sup> His use of them was more aggressive, and less melodic, than that of Garland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Champ_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Champ-37">[36]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Penguin_Guide_to_Jazz" title="The Penguin Guide to Jazz">The Penguin Guide to Jazz</a></i> suggested that "Timmons' characteristic style was a rolling, gospelly funk, perhaps longer on sheer energy than on harmonic sophistication."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Penguin_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Penguin-16">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:1574</sup> In the opinion of <a href="/wiki/Scott_Yanow" title="Scott Yanow">Scott Yanow</a>, stylistically, "somehow Bobby Timmons never grew beyond where he was in 1960."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gary_Giddins" title="Gary Giddins">Gary Giddins</a>, however, highlighted other facets of Timmons' playing: the "lush <a href="/wiki/Bud_Powell" title="Bud Powell">[Bud] Powell</a>-inspired ballads, his clear, sharp, unsentimental long lines."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GG_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-GG-25">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:50</sup> Carter also identified Powell as a primary influence on Timmons, and commented that his partner in the trio "was very giving, very loyal, played every night like it was his last chance to get it right."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TP3_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-TP3-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The funky aspects of Timmons' playing influenced fellow pianists, including <a href="/wiki/Les_McCann" title="Les McCann">Les McCann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ramsey_Lewis" title="Ramsey Lewis">Ramsey Lewis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Benny_Green_(pianist)" title="Benny Green (pianist)">Benny Green</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> Timmons is often mentioned as being under-rated;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup> jazz writer <a href="/wiki/Marc_Myers" title="Marc Myers">Marc Myers</a> commented in 2008 that "today, Timmons' contribution to jazz – as an accompanist, writer, leader and innovator of a new sound – is vastly overlooked and undervalued."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Myers_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Myers-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compositions">Compositions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bobby_Timmons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Compositions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Timmons wrote "a steady stream of infectious funky tunes", stated Giddins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GG_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-GG-25">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:50</sup> Timmons dismissed the idea that he was deliberately a composer: "I'm a dilettante as a composer. I have never consciously sat down and tried to write a song."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Comp_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Comp-4">[3]</a></sup> He stated that his method of composing a new song might involve "whistling, playing around with the notes, or at a club. I'll tell one musician to play this note, another that note, and we kick it around."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Comp_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Comp-4">[3]</a></sup> One account of the creation of "Moanin'" was given by Golson: Timmons had the opening eight bars, which he often played between tunes, but formed the complete song only after Golson encouraged him to add a <a href="/wiki/Bridge_(music)" title="Bridge (music)">bridge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TP1_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-TP1-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personality">Personality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bobby_Timmons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Personality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tootie Heath reported that, when they were on tour and Timmons was addicted to heroin, the pianist would routinely lie and sometimes pull out a knife to threaten people with.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EI_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-EI-20">[19]</a></sup> Carter, the bassist from that tour, stated that Timmons offered his bandmates a lot of encouragement to experiment and improve from performance to performance, and that he "was a really multi-talented person and he was just a real sweetheart, a sweetheart of a man."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> In Golson's words, Timmons "had no ego about him, [...] He was always upbeat, never downbeat, and he never maligned anybody unless it was in a humorous way."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TP1_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-TP1-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
