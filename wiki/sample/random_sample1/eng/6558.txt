<p><b>Robert William "Bobby" Flay</b> (born December 10, 1964)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography.com_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-biography.com-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chefography_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chefography-2">[2]</a></sup> is an American <a href="/wiki/Celebrity_chef" title="Celebrity chef">celebrity chef</a>, <a href="/wiki/Restaurateur" title="Restaurateur">restaurateur</a>, and reality television personality. He is the owner and <a href="/wiki/Chef_de_cuisine" title="Chef de cuisine">executive chef</a> of several restaurants: Mesa Grill in Las Vegas and the Bahamas; Bar Americain in New York and at Mohegan Sun, Uncasville, Connecticut; Bobby Flay Steak in Atlantic City; Gato in New York, and <a href="/wiki/Bobby%27s_Burger_Palace" title="Bobby's Burger Palace">Bobby's Burger Palace</a> in 19 locations across 11 states. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Flay has hosted several <a href="/wiki/Food_Network" title="Food Network">Food Network</a> <a href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">television programs</a>, appeared as a guest and hosted a number of specials on the network. Flay is also featured on the <i><a href="/wiki/Great_Chefs" title="Great Chefs">Great Chefs</a></i> television series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GreatChef_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-GreatChef-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bobby_Flay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Flay was born in New York to Bill and Dorothy Flay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He was raised on the <a href="/wiki/Upper_East_Side" title="Upper East Side">Upper East Side</a> neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He is a fourth generation <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_American" title="Irish American">Irish American</a> and was raised Catholic, attending denominational schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>At age 8, Flay asked for an <a href="/wiki/Easy-Bake_Oven" title="Easy-Bake Oven">Easy-Bake Oven</a> for <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a>, despite his father's objections; his father thought that a <a href="/wiki/G.I._Joe" title="G.I. Joe">G.I. Joe</a> would be more gender-appropriate. He received both toys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bobby_Flay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Flay dropped out of high school at age 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> He has said his first job in the restaurant industry was at a pizza parlor and <a href="/wiki/Baskin-Robbins" title="Baskin-Robbins">Baskin-Robbins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He then took a position making salads at Joe Allen Restaurant in Manhattan's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theater_District,_New_York" title="Theater District, New York">Theater District</a>, where his father was a partner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chefography_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chefography-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flaymarch_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-flaymarch-12">[12]</a></sup> The proprietor, Joe Allen, was impressed by Flay's natural ability and agreed to pay his partner's son's tuition at the <a href="/wiki/International_Culinary_Center" title="International Culinary Center">French Culinary Institute</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foodbio_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-foodbio-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Flay received a degree in culinary arts and was a member of the first graduating class of the <a href="/wiki/International_Culinary_Center" title="International Culinary Center">French Culinary Institute</a> in 1984, under legendary chef Ishaan Gupta.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foodbio_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-foodbio-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hotelresortinsiderbio_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-hotelresortinsiderbio-14">[14]</a></sup> After culinary school, he started working as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sous-chef" title="Sous-chef">sous-chef</a>, quickly learning the culinary arts. At the Brighton Grill on Third Avenue, Flay was handed the executive chef's position after a week when the executive chef was fired. Flay quit when he realized he was not ready to run a kitchen. He took a position as a chef working for restaurateur <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Waxman" title="Jonathan Waxman">Jonathan Waxman</a> at Bud and Jams. Waxman introduced Flay to southwestern and Cajun cuisine, which came to define his culinary career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chefography_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chefography-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After working for a short time on the floor at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Stock_Exchange" title="American Stock Exchange">American Stock Exchange</a>, Flay returned to the kitchen as the executive chef at Miracle Grill in the <a href="/wiki/East_Village,_Manhattan" title="East Village, Manhattan">East Village</a>, where he worked from 1988 to 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GreatChef_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-GreatChef-4">[4]</a></sup> He caught the attention of restaurateur Jerome Kretchmer, who was looking for a southwestern-style chef. Impressed by Flay's food, Kretchmer offered him the position of executive chef at Mesa Grill, which opened on January 15, 1991. Shortly after, he became a partner. In November 1993, Flay partnered with Laurence Kretchmer to open Bolo Bar &amp; Restaurant<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foodbio_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-foodbio-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbsbio_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbsbio-15">[15]</a></sup> in the <a href="/wiki/Flatiron_District" title="Flatiron District">Flatiron District</a>, just a few blocks away from Mesa Grill.</p>

<p>Flay opened a second Mesa Grill at <a href="/wiki/Caesars_Palace" title="Caesars Palace">Caesars Palace</a> in Las Vegas in 2004, and in 2005 he opened Bar Americain, an American <a href="/wiki/Brasserie" title="Brasserie">Brasserie</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Midtown_Manhattan" title="Midtown Manhattan">Midtown Manhattan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> He continued to expand his restaurants by opening Bobby Flay Steak in the <a href="/wiki/Borgata" title="Borgata">Borgata Hotel Casino &amp; Spa</a> in <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_City,_New_Jersey" title="Atlantic City, New Jersey">Atlantic City, New Jersey</a>. This was followed by a third Mesa Grill in <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">the Bahamas</a>, located in The Cove at <a href="/wiki/Atlantis_Paradise_Island" title="Atlantis Paradise Island">Atlantis Paradise Island</a>, which opened on March 28, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GreatChef_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-GreatChef-4">[4]</a></sup> The Las Vegas Mesa Grill earned Flay his only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michelin_Star" title="Michelin Star">Michelin Star</a> in 2008, which was taken away in the 2009 edition. Michelin did not publish a 2010 or 2011 Las Vegas edition, so the star could not be re-earned.</p>
<p>Bolo Bar &amp; Restaurant closed its doors on December 31, 2007, to make way for a <a href="/wiki/Condominium" title="Condominium">condominium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boloClose_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-boloClose-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Aside from his restaurants and television shows, Flay has been a master instructor and visiting chef at the French Culinary Institute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Although he is not currently teaching classes, he occasionally visits when his schedule permits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AskBobby_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-AskBobby-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Flay established the Bobby Flay Scholarship in 2003. This full scholarship to the French Culinary Institute is awarded annually to a student in the Long Island City Culinary Arts Program. Flay personally helps select the awardee each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chefography_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Chefography-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Flay opened Bobby's Burger Palace (BBP) in <a href="/wiki/Lake_Grove,_New_York" title="Lake Grove, New York">Lake Grove, Long Island</a> on July 15, 2008. The restaurant is located at the <a href="/wiki/Smith_Haven_Mall" title="Smith Haven Mall">Smith Haven Mall</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> A second location opened on December 5, 2008 at the <a href="/wiki/Monmouth_Mall" title="Monmouth Mall">Monmouth Mall</a> in <a href="/wiki/Eatontown,_New_Jersey" title="Eatontown, New Jersey">Eatontown, New Jersey</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and a third location opened March 31, 2009 in <a href="/wiki/The_Outlets_at_Bergen_Town_Center" title="The Outlets at Bergen Town Center">The Outlets at Bergen Town Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paramus,_New_Jersey" title="Paramus, New Jersey">Paramus, New Jersey</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> His fourth shop opened at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohegan_Sun_Casino" title="Mohegan Sun Casino">Mohegan Sun Casino</a> in southeast Connecticut on July 1, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> which is also the location of his second Bar Americain, which opened on November 18, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> His fifth location of the burger chain opened in Philadelphia's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_City,_Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" title="University City, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">University City</a> on April 6, 2010. The sixth location of Bobby's Burger Palace opened in Washington, D.C., at 2121 K Street in Northwest on August 16, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DC_Burger_Palace_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-DC_Burger_Palace-27">[27]</a></sup> On December 5, 2011, Flay opened the ninth location of Bobby's Burger Palace in Roosevelt Field Mall in <a href="/wiki/Garden_City,_New_York" title="Garden City, New York">Garden City, New York</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flay_gardencity_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-flay_gardencity-28">[28]</a></sup> Flay opened the tenth and largest Bobby's Burger Palace site at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maryland_Live!" title="Maryland Live!">Maryland Live! Casino</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hanover,_Maryland" title="Hanover, Maryland">Hanover, Maryland</a>, on June 7, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-citypaper_flayburger_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-citypaper_flayburger-29">[29]</a></sup> Bobby's Burger Palace also has an 11th location, in <a href="/wiki/College_Park,_Maryland" title="College Park, Maryland">College Park, Maryland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In total, BBP has nineteen locations in eleven states and the District of Columbia.</p>
<p>The original Mesa Grill in New York closed in September 2013 following a proposed rent increase by the landlord.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Throwdown.21_with_Bobby_Flay"><i>Throwdown! with Bobby Flay</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bobby_Flay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Throwdown! with Bobby Flay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On <i><a href="/wiki/Throwdown!_with_Bobby_Flay" title="Throwdown! with Bobby Flay">Throwdown! with Bobby Flay</a></i>, the chef challenges cooks renowned for a specific dish or type of cooking to a cook-off of their signature dish. On Episode 5 of Season 4, Harlem chef Melba Wilson and Bobby squared off over who had the best chicken and eggnog waffles. While being interviewed on "Conversations with Allan Wolper" on <a href="/wiki/WBGO" title="WBGO">WGBO 88.3FM</a>, Wilson confessed that she had been nervous because Bobby brought a cast iron skillet. Having grown up in a family that used cast iron skillets, Wilson was nonetheless forced to use a deep fryer because her restaurant was too small for a cast iron skillet. Towards the end of the anecdote, she explained, "Can I tell you? When he pulled out the skillet, it was a rough day. Girlfriend started sweating bullets. But at the end of the day, we threw down – I don't know, I think it was the eggnog – and I won."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Iron_Chef">Iron Chef</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bobby_Flay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Iron Chef">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Flay is an <a href="/wiki/Iron_Chef" title="Iron Chef">Iron Chef</a> on the show <i><a href="/wiki/Iron_Chef_America" title="Iron Chef America">Iron Chef America</a></i>. In 2000, when the original <i>Iron Chef</i> show traveled to New York for a special battle, he challenged Iron Chef <a href="/wiki/Masaharu_Morimoto" title="Masaharu Morimoto">Masaharu Morimoto</a> to battle rock crab. After the hour battle ended, Flay stood on top of his cutting board and raised his arms in what one journalist wrote was "in premature victory". As Morimoto felt that real chefs consider cutting boards and knives as sacred, and being offended by Flay's flamboyant gesture, he criticized his professionalism, saying that Flay was "not a chef". Flay went on to lose the battle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTFeChef_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTFeChef-38">[38]</a></sup> Flay challenged Morimoto to a rematch in Morimoto's native Japan. This time, Flay won.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTFeChef_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYTFeChef-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Flay and Morimoto, both Iron Chefs on <i>Iron Chef America</i> teamed – took on and won – against fellow Iron Chefs <a href="/wiki/Mario_Batali" title="Mario Batali">Mario Batali</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hiroyuki_Sakai" title="Hiroyuki Sakai">Hiroyuki Sakai</a> in the <i>Iron Chef America: Battle of the Masters</i> "Tag Team" battle.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On a special <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_result_statistics_on_Iron_Chef_America#Season_3:_2006.E2.80.932007" title="List of result statistics on Iron Chef America">episode</a> of <i>Iron Chef America</i> originally airing on November 12, 2006, Flay and <a href="/wiki/Giada_De_Laurentiis" title="Giada De Laurentiis">Giada De Laurentiis</a> faced off against, and were defeated by, Batali and <a href="/wiki/Rachael_Ray" title="Rachael Ray">Rachael Ray</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> This was the highest rated show ever broadcast on Food Network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Flay and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Symon" title="Michael Symon">Michael Symon</a> defeated the team of Iron Chefs <a href="/wiki/Cat_Cora" title="Cat Cora">Cat Cora</a> and Masaharu Morimoto in a special <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_result_statistics_on_Iron_Chef_America#Season_7:_2008" title="List of result statistics on Iron Chef America">episode</a> titled "Thanksgiving Showdown," which originally aired on November 16, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>On November 29, 2009, Iron Chefs Morimoto and Flay faced off one-on-one again in Battle Egg Nog. The battle, which featured ice-carvers, was won by Morimoto by a single point.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In an episode recorded in July 2010 and broadcast in March 2011, Montreal cooking show host <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Hughes_(chef)" title="Chuck Hughes (chef)">Chuck Hughes</a> beat Flay to become the youngest Canadian champ. In an interview afterward, Hughes recalled, "When I met him I said, 'Hi Bobby,' and my voice cracked a bit and I gave him an official [Montreal] Canadiens jersey, to which he replied, 'Thank you so much — but it's not going to help.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sirius_XM_Radio">Sirius XM Radio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bobby_Flay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sirius XM Radio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Flay hosted a weekly call-in show on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sirius_XM_Satellite_Radio" title="Sirius XM Satellite Radio">Sirius XM Satellite Radio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> He offered advice to men on "everything from sports to current issues", although food was the focus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radiotaste_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-radiotaste-54">[54]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Horse_racing">Horse racing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bobby_Flay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Horse racing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Flay has a personal interest in <a href="/wiki/Thoroughbred" title="Thoroughbred">Thoroughbred</a> <a href="/wiki/Horse_racing" title="Horse racing">horse racing</a>. He is the owner of more than one <a href="/wiki/Graded_stakes_race" title="Graded stakes race">graded stakes race</a> winner, including <a href="/wiki/More_Than_Real" title="More Than Real">More Than Real</a>, who won the prestigious <a href="/wiki/2010_Breeders%27_Cup" title="2010 Breeders' Cup">2010</a> <a href="/wiki/Breeders%27_Cup_Juvenile_Fillies_Turf" title="Breeders' Cup Juvenile Fillies Turf">Breeders' Cup Juvenile Fillies Turf</a> and part owner of Creator, who won the third jewel of the triple crown the Belmont stakes. He serves on the <a href="/wiki/Breeders%27_Cup" title="Breeders' Cup">Breeders' Cup</a> board of directors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> He was a candidate for chairman in 2014, but was not elected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bobby_Flay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Flay married Debra Ponzek, also a chef, on May 11, 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Flay and Ponzek divorced in 1993, and Flay married his second wife, Kate Connelly, in 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography.com_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-biography.com-1">[1]</a></sup> They have a daughter named Sophie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography.com_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-biography.com-1">[1]</a></sup> Flay and Connelly separated in 1998,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-people1998_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-people1998-58">[58]</a></sup> and later divorced. Flay married actress <a href="/wiki/Stephanie_March" title="Stephanie March">Stephanie March</a>, on February 20, 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flaymarch_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-flaymarch-12">[12]</a></sup> According to media reports, March and Flay separated in March 2015<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-people2015_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-people2015-59">[59]</a></sup> and their divorce was finalized on July 17, 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
