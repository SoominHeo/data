<p><b>Boss</b> is a manufacturer of <a href="/wiki/Effects_unit" title="Effects unit">effects pedals</a> for <a href="/wiki/Electric_guitar" title="Electric guitar">electric guitar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bass_guitar" title="Bass guitar">bass guitar</a>. It is a division of the <a href="/wiki/Roland_Corporation" title="Roland Corporation">Roland Corporation</a>, a Japanese manufacturer that specializes in musical equipment and accessories. For many years Boss has manufactured a wide range of products related to effects processing for guitars, including "compact" and "twin" effects pedals, multi-effect pedals, <a href="/wiki/Electronic_tuner" title="Electronic tuner">electronic tuners</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guitar_pedalboard" title="Guitar pedalboard">pedal boards</a>. In more recent times, Boss expanded their product line by including Digital Studios, rhythm machines and other electronic music equipment.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boss_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:157px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Boss_DS-2_Distortion_Pedal.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="251" data-file-width="155" height="251" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/6/6b/Boss_DS-2_Distortion_Pedal.jpg" width="155"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Boss_DS-2_Distortion_Pedal.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A typical BOSS compact effects pedal</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The earliest Boss product was called <i>B-100 The Boss</i>, released in 1974. This came with a clip-on pre-amp and a pickup to amplify acoustic guitars. At this point the Boss company had not been formed and it was still technically a Beckmen Musical Instruments product (as seen on the B-100 box <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>).</p>
<p>The first proper Boss foot pedal effect, the CE-1 Chorus Ensemble, was released June, 1976, which was a stand-alone unit of the chorus/vibrato circuit found in the Roland JC-120 amplifier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It was a fairly large, AC-powered unit.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BossHM2HeavyMetal.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="519" data-file-width="391" height="292" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/BossHM2HeavyMetal.jpg/220px-BossHM2HeavyMetal.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/BossHM2HeavyMetal.jpg/330px-BossHM2HeavyMetal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c9/BossHM2HeavyMetal.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BossHM2HeavyMetal.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Boss HM-2 Heavy Metal distortion pedal</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Boss's line of compact pedals began in 1977 with the release of three pedals: an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overdrive_(music)" title="Overdrive (music)">overdrive</a> pedal (OD-1), a <a href="/wiki/Phaser_(effect)" title="Phaser (effect)">phaser</a> pedal (PH-1) and a parametric equalizer called the Spectrum (SP-1). The <a href="/wiki/Boss_DS-1" title="Boss DS-1">Boss DS-1</a> was released the next year, in 1979. Also released in this year is the T Wah (TW-1) pedal. Their first compact <a href="/wiki/Chorus_effect" title="Chorus effect">chorus</a> pedal (CE-2) came the next year (1979), and their first <a href="/wiki/Flanging" title="Flanging">flanger</a> pedal (BF-2) in 1981. In 1983 Boss released the DD-2 Digital Delay, the first mass-produced digital delay in a compact pedal format. The Metal Zone (MT-2) was released in 1991. In 1992 Boss released nine new pedals, including the Turbo Distortion (DS-2). The Heavy Metal (HM-2) distortion pedal was an integral part of the guitar sound of many styles of heavy metal music ever since.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The pedals all share the same 'footprint', for compatibility with pedal boards.</p>
<p>Boss introduced COSM (Composite Object Sound Modeling), Roland's proprietary version of digital <a href="/wiki/Amplifier_modeling" title="Amplifier modeling">modeling</a> technology, into their AC-3 Acoustic Simulator pedal in 2006. Boss has since released several pedals using COSM, including the FBM-1 '59 Fender Bassman pedal and FDR-1 '65 Fender Deluxe Reverb pedal, introduced at the Winter NAMM show in January 2007.</p>
<p>All Boss compact pedals use a "buffered bypass" type of silent foot switching utilizing Field Effect Transistors (FETs) to avoid clicks and pops. While not "true" bypass, the buffered bypass has the advantage of preventing signal loss due to long runs of cable, while keeping original guitar tone intact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GEOFEX_-_Ins_and_Outs_of_Effect_Bypassing_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-GEOFEX_-_Ins_and_Outs_of_Effect_Bypassing-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan.2FTaiwan">Japan/Taiwan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boss_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Japan/Taiwan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BossFX.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4272" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/BossFX.JPG/220px-BossFX.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/BossFX.JPG/330px-BossFX.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/BossFX.JPG/440px-BossFX.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BossFX.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
BOSS effect pedals</div>

<p>Boss compact pedals were originally produced in Japan, until circa 1990 when production moved to Taiwan. Earlier units came with a metal screw securing the battery compartment, later models retained the metal screw, adding a plastic knob for tool-less battery removal. The labels on the bottom of the pedals come in several different colours including black, silver, green, pink and blue. Apart from this the basic design has remained unchanged for over 25 years.</p>
<p>The DS-1 Distortion, however, is an exception; the design has changed significantly twice throughout its lifetime. The first time was around 1994 when the Toshiba TA7136AP op-amp was replaced with the Rohm BA728N. In 2000 the op-amp was again changed. This time it was replaced with the Mitsubishi M5223AL. The latest op-amp change occurred in 2007, when new DS-1 pedals began shipping with the NJM3404AL op-amp.</p>
<p>In recent years older Boss compact pedals have begun to command a high premium on the used market.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some pedals, such as the relatively rare VB-2 Vibrato, SG-1 Slow Gear, SP-1 Spectrum, DC-2 Dimension C, PS-3 Digital Pitch Shifter/Delay, and either of the two analog delay pedals, the DM-2 and DM-3, are highly sought after by collectors.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Boss_Doctor_Rhythm_DR-110" title="Boss Doctor Rhythm DR-110">Boss Doctor Rhythm DR-110</a> drum machine was the last drum machine out of Roland to use analog sound synthesis to generate the drum sounds. After the DR-110, all Roland drum machines used samples of drums to produce sounds. The DR-110 is not easily incorporated into a modernized studio setup running MIDI but there are modifications available to sync the DR-110 to other gear. There are also modifications available in DIY formats to add tonal controls to the analog drum sounds on board the DR-110 that will give the user a wider range of sonic ability.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
