<p><b>Boston</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="noexcerpt"><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1c/En-uk-Boston.ogg" title="Listen"><img alt="Listen" data-file-height="11" data-file-width="11" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/11px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/17px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/22px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a><sup><span class="IPA" style="color:#00e;font:bold 80% sans-serif;padding:0 .1em"><a href="/wiki/File:En-uk-Boston.ogg" title="File:En-uk-Boston.ogg">i</a></span></sup></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ən/ 'on' in 'button'">ən</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a town and small port in <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>, on the east coast of England. It is the largest town of the wider <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_(borough)" title="Boston (borough)">Borough of Boston</a> local government district. The borough had a total population of 66,900, at the ONS mid 2015 estimates,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> while the town itself had a population of 35,124 at the 2001 census.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It is due north of <a href="/wiki/Greenwich" title="Greenwich">Greenwich</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Meridian" title="Prime Meridian">Prime Meridian</a>.</p>
<p>Boston's most notable landmark is <a href="/wiki/St_Botolph%27s_Church,_Boston" title="St Botolph's Church, Boston">St Botolph's Church ("The Stump")</a>, said to be the largest <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parish church</a> in England,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> with one of the taller towers in England<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> visible for miles around from the flat lands of Lincolnshire. Residents of Boston are known as Bostonians. Emigrants from Boston named several other settlements after the town, most notably <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, in the United States.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EB9_Boston_1.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="840" data-file-width="844" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/EB9_Boston_1.png/200px-EB9_Boston_1.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/EB9_Boston_1.png/300px-EB9_Boston_1.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/EB9_Boston_1.png/400px-EB9_Boston_1.png 2x" width="200"/></a>

</div>
<p>The name "Boston" is said to be a contraction of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Botolph" title="Saint Botolph">Saint Botolph</a>'s town",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271878_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271878-5">[5]</a></sup> "stone", or "<i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">tun</span></i>" (<a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> and present <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a>) for a <a href="/wiki/Hamlet_(place)" title="Hamlet (place)">hamlet</a> or <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farm</a>, hence the Latin <i>villa Sancti Botulfi</i> "St. Botulf's village").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The town's link to the saint's life is probably apocryphal, but the name may have arisen from confusion as to the site of his monastery or owing to his patronage of the parish church.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The town was once held to have been a <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman</a> settlement, but there is no evidence that this is the case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271878_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271878-5">[5]</a></sup> Similarly, it is often linked to the <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastery</a> established by the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Saxon</a> <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">monk</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Botolph" title="Saint Botolph">Botolph</a> at "<b>Icanhoe</b>" on the <a href="/wiki/River_Witham" title="River Witham">Witham</a> in <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span> 654 and destroyed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking_invasions_of_England" title="Viking invasions of England">Vikings</a> in 870,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271878_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271878-5">[5]</a></sup> but this is now doubted by modern historians. The early medieval geography of <a href="/wiki/The_Fens" title="The Fens">The Fens</a> was much more fluid than it is today and, at that time, the Witham did not flow near the site of Boston. Botolph's establishment is most likely to have been in <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a>. However, he was a popular <a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">missionary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saint</a>, to whom many churches between <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sussex" title="Sussex">Sussex</a> (including that Boston's) are dedicated.</p>
<p>The 1086 <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i> does not mention Boston by name,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271911290_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271911290-7">[7]</a></sup> but nearby settlements of the tenant-in-chief Count <a href="/wiki/Alan_Rufus" title="Alan Rufus">Alan Rufus</a> of Brittany are covered. Its present territory was probably then part of the grant of <a href="/wiki/Skirbeck" title="Skirbeck">Skirbeck</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271911290_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271911290-7">[7]</a></sup> part of the very wealthy manor of Drayton, which before 1066 had been owned by <a href="/wiki/Ralph_the_Staller" title="Ralph the Staller">Ralph the Staller</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Confessor" title="Edward the Confessor">Edward the Confessor</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_East_Anglia" title="Earl of East Anglia">Earl of East Anglia</a>. Skirbeck had two churches and one is likely to have been that dedicated to St Botolph, in what was consequently Botolph's town. Skirbeck (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion" style="white-space: nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://tools.wmflabs.org/os/coor_g/?pagename=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;params=TF339433_region%3AGB_scale%3A200000">map</a></span>) is now considered part of Boston, but the name remains, as a church parish and an electoral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ward_(country_subdivision)" title="Ward (country subdivision)">ward</a>.</p>
<p>The order of importance was the other way round, when the Boston quarter of Skirbeck developed at the head of <a href="/wiki/The_Haven,_Boston" title="The Haven, Boston">the Haven</a>, which lies under the present Market Place. At that stage, The Haven was the <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tidal</a> part of the stream, now represented by the Stone Bridge Drain (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion" style="white-space: nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://tools.wmflabs.org/os/coor_g/?pagename=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;params=TF347490_region%3AGB_scale%3A200000">map</a></span>), which carried the water from the East and West Fens. The line of the road through Wide Bargate, to <a href="/wiki/A52_road" title="A52 road">A52</a> and <a href="/wiki/A16_road_(England)" title="A16 road (England)">A16</a>, is likely to have developed on its marine silt levees.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It led, as it does now, to the relatively high ground at Sibsey (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion" style="white-space: nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://tools.wmflabs.org/os/coor_g/?pagename=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;params=TF350500_region%3AGB_scale%3A200000">map</a></span>), and thence to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lindsey_(government_district)" title="Lindsey (government district)">Lindsey</a>.</p>
<p>The reason for the original development of the town, away from the centre of Skirbeck, was that Boston lay on the point where navigable tidal water was alongside the land route, which used the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">Devensian</a> terminal <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">moraine</a> ridge at Sibsey, between the upland of East Lindsey and the three routes to the south of Boston:</p>
<ul>
<li>The coastal route, on the marine silts, crossed the mouth of Bicker Haven towards <a href="/wiki/Spalding,_Lincolnshire" title="Spalding, Lincolnshire">Spalding</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Sleaford" title="Sleaford">Sleaford</a> route, into <a href="/wiki/Kesteven" title="Kesteven">Kesteven</a>, passed via Swineshead (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion" style="white-space: nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://tools.wmflabs.org/os/coor_g/?pagename=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;params=TF237402_region%3AGB_scale%3A200000">map</a></span>), thence following the old course of the River Slea, on its marine <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a> <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">levee</a>.</li>
<li>The Salters' Way route into Kesteven, left Holland from <a href="/wiki/Donington,_Lincolnshire" title="Donington, Lincolnshire">Donington</a>. This route was much more thoroughly developed, in the later Medieval period, by Bridge End Priory (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion" style="white-space: nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://tools.wmflabs.org/os/coor_g/?pagename=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;params=TF148361_region%3AGB_scale%3A200000">map</a></span>).</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/River_Witham" title="River Witham">River Witham</a> seems to have joined The Haven after the flood of September 1014, having abandoned the port of Drayton, on what subsequently became known as Bicker Haven.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The predecessor of Ralph the Staller owned most of both Skirbeck and Drayton, so it was a relatively simple task to transfer his business from Drayton, but the <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i> of 1086 still records his source of income in Boston under the heading of Drayton, so Boston's name is famously not mentioned. The Town Bridge still maintains the pre-flood route, along the old Haven bank.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth">Growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Blackfriars_Arts_Centre_October_2004.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="597" height="101" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d6/Blackfriars_Arts_Centre_October_2004.JPG/150px-Blackfriars_Arts_Centre_October_2004.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d6/Blackfriars_Arts_Centre_October_2004.JPG/225px-Blackfriars_Arts_Centre_October_2004.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d6/Blackfriars_Arts_Centre_October_2004.JPG/300px-Blackfriars_Arts_Centre_October_2004.JPG 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Blackfriars_Arts_Centre_October_2004.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Blackfriars Arts Centre</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman Conquest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_the_Staller" title="Ralph the Staller">Ralph the Staller</a>'s property was taken over by <a href="/wiki/Alan_Rufus" title="Alan Rufus">Count Alan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorris1986Landowner_12.2C_.C2.A767_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorris1986Landowner_12.2C_.C2.A767-8">[8]</a></sup> It subsequently came to be attached to the Earldom of <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_North_Yorkshire" title="Richmond, North Yorkshire">Richmond, North Yorkshire</a>, and known as the Richmond Fee. It lay on the left bank of The Haven.</p>
<p>During the eleventh and twelfth centuries, Boston grew into a notable town and port.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1204, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Lackland" title="John Lackland">King John</a> vested sole control over the town in his <a href="/wiki/Bailiff" title="Bailiff">bailiff</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271911290_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271911290-7">[7]</a></sup> That year<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271878_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271878-5">[5]</a></sup> or the next,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271911290_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271911290-7">[7]</a></sup> he levied a "fifteenth" tax (<i><span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">quinzieme</span></i>) of 6.67% on the moveable goods of merchants in the ports of England: the merchants of Boston paid £780, the highest in the kingdom after London's £836.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271878_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271878-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1856Div._VIII_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1856Div._VIII-10">[10]</a></sup> Thus by the opening of the thirteenth century, it was already significant in trade with the <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continent</a> of Europe and ranked as a port of the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Edward_III_of_England" title="Edward III of England">Edward III</a> named it a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Staple_port" title="Staple port">staple port</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wool_trade" title="Wool trade">wool trade</a> in 1369.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271911290_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271911290-7">[7]</a></sup> Apart from wool, Boston also exported <a href="/wiki/Sea_salt" title="Sea salt">salt</a>, produced locally on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holland,_Lincolnshire" title="Holland, Lincolnshire">Holland</a> coast, <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">grain</a>, produced up-river, and lead, produced in <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a> and brought via <a href="/wiki/Lincoln,_England" title="Lincoln, England">Lincoln</a>, up-river.</p>
<p>A quarrel between the local and foreign merchants led to the withdrawal of the Hansards<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271878_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271878-5">[5]</a></sup> around 1470.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271911290_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271911290-7">[7]</a></sup> Around the same time, the decline of the local guilds<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271911290_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271911290-7">[7]</a></sup> and shift towards domestic <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">weaving</a> of English wool (conducted in other areas of the country)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> led to a near-complete collapse of the town's foreign trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271911290_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27EB.27.271911290-7">[7]</a></sup> The silting of the Haven only furthered the town's decline.</p>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Monasteries" title="Dissolution of the Monasteries">Dissolution of the Monasteries</a> by <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> during the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">English Reformation</a>, Boston's <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominican</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franciscan" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carmelite" title="Carmelite">Carmelite</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustinian" title="Augustinian">Augustinian</a> friaries—erected during the boom years of the 13th and 14th centuries—were all expropriated. The <a href="/wiki/Refectory" title="Refectory">refectory</a> of the Dominican friary was eventually converted into a theatre in 1965 and now houses the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackfriars_Arts_Centre" title="Blackfriars Arts Centre">Blackfriars Arts Centre</a>.</p>
<p>Henry VIII granted the town its charter in 1545<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1856_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1856-13">[13]</a></sup> and Boston had two Members of Parliament from 1552.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="17th_and_18th_centuries">17th and 18th centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 17th and 18th centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pilgrim_Fathers_Memorial.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="597" data-file-width="400" height="224" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Pilgrim_Fathers_Memorial.JPG/150px-Pilgrim_Fathers_Memorial.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Pilgrim_Fathers_Memorial.JPG/225px-Pilgrim_Fathers_Memorial.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Pilgrim_Fathers_Memorial.JPG/300px-Pilgrim_Fathers_Memorial.JPG 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pilgrim_Fathers_Memorial.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Pilgrim_Fathers_Memorial" title="Pilgrim Fathers Memorial">Pilgrim Fathers Memorial</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The staple trade made Boston a centre of intellectual influence from the Continent, including the teachings of <a href="/wiki/John_Calvin" title="John Calvin">John Calvin</a> that became known as <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinism</a>. This, in turn, revolutionised the Christian beliefs and practices of many Bostonians and residents of the neighbouring shires of England. In 1607 a group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilgrims" title="Pilgrims">pilgrims</a> from <a href="/wiki/Nottinghamshire" title="Nottinghamshire">Nottinghamshire</a> led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Brewster_(pilgrim)" title="William Brewster (pilgrim)">William Brewster</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Bradford_(Plymouth_governor)" title="William Bradford (Plymouth governor)">William Bradford</a> attempted to escape pressure to conform with the teaching of the <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">English church</a> by going to <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">the Netherlands</a> from Boston. At that time unsanctioned emigration was illegal, and they were brought before the court in the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Guildhall" title="Boston Guildhall">Guildhall</a>. Most of the pilgrims were released fairly soon and the following year, set sail for the Netherlands, settling in <a href="/wiki/Leiden" title="Leiden">Leiden</a>. In 1620, several of these were among the group who moved to <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mayflower" title="Mayflower">Mayflower</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Boston remained a hotbed of religious dissent. In 1612 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Cotton_(Puritan)" title="John Cotton (Puritan)">John Cotton</a> became the Vicar of St Botolph's and, although viewed askance by the Church of England for his non-conformist preaching, became responsible for a large increase in Church attendance. He encouraged those who disliked the lack of religious freedom in England to join the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Bay_Colony" title="Massachusetts Bay Colony">Massachusetts Bay Company</a>, and later helped to found the city of Boston, Massachusetts, which he was instrumental in naming. Unable to tolerate the religious situation any longer he eventually emigrated himself in 1633.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>At the same time, work on draining the fens to the west of Boston was begun, a scheme which displeased many whose livelihoods were at risk. (One of the sources of livelihood obtained from the fen was fowling, supplying ducks and geese for meat and in addition the processing of their feathers and down for use in mattresses and pillows. The feathery aspect of this is still reflected in the presence of the bedding company named Fogarty, nearby in <a href="/wiki/Fishtoft" title="Fishtoft">Fishtoft</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>) This and the religious friction put Boston into the <a href="/wiki/Roundhead" title="Roundhead">parliamentarian</a> camp in the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">Civil War</a> which in England began in 1642. The chief backer of the drainage locally, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bertie,_1st_Earl_of_Lindsey" title="Robert Bertie, 1st Earl of Lindsey">Lord Lindsey</a>, was shot in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Edgehill" title="Battle of Edgehill">first battle</a> and the fens returned to their accustomed dampness until after 1750.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The later eighteenth century saw a revival when the Fens began to be effectively drained. The <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act of Parliament</a> permitting the embanking and straightening of the fenland Witham was dated 1762. A sluice, called for in the Act, was designed to help scour out The Haven. The land proved to be fertile, and Boston began exporting cereals to London. In 1774 the first financial bank was opened, and in 1776 an Act of Parliament allowed watchmen to begin patrolling the streets at night.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_history">Modern history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Modern history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 19th century, the names of Howden, a firm located near the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Sluice&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Grand Sluice (page does not exist)">Grand Sluice</a>; and Tuxford, near the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Maud_Foster_Sluice&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Maud Foster Sluice (page does not exist)">Maud Foster Sluice</a>, were respected among engineers for their steam road locomotives, threshing engines and the like. Howden developed his business from making steam engines for river boats while Tuxford began as a miller and millwright. His mill was once prominent near Skirbeck Church, just to the east of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Maud_Foster_Drain&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Maud Foster Drain (page does not exist)">Maud Foster Drain</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The railway reached the town in 1848, and it was briefly on the main line from London to the North. The area between the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sluice" title="Black Sluice">Black Sluice</a> and the railway station was mainly railway yard and the railway company's main depôt. The latter facility moved to <a href="/wiki/Doncaster" title="Doncaster">Doncaster</a> when the modern main line was opened. Boston remained something of a local railway hub well into the 20th century, moving the produce of the district and the trade of the dock, plus the excursion trade to <a href="/wiki/Skegness" title="Skegness">Skegness</a> and similar places. But it was much quieter by the time of the <a href="/wiki/Richard_Beeching" title="Richard Beeching">Beeching</a> cuts of the 1960s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Boston once again became a significant port in trade and fishing in 1884, when the new <a href="/wiki/Dock_(maritime)" title="Dock (maritime)">dock</a> with its associated <a href="/wiki/Wharf" title="Wharf">wharves</a> on The Haven were constructed. It continued as a working port, exporting grain, fertiliser, and importing timber, although much of the fishing trade was moved out in the inter-war period.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a> many of the town's trawlermen, together with those from <a href="/wiki/Grimsby" title="Grimsby">Grimsby</a>, were taken prisoner after their ships were sunk by German raiders in the North Sea. Their families did not know what had happened to them until late September 1914. The men were taken to <a href="/wiki/Sennelager" title="Sennelager">Sennelager</a> camp, then on to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruhleben_POW_camp" title="Ruhleben POW camp">Ruhleben POW camp</a> where most remained till repatriated in 1918. There is a full report of their homecoming in the <i>Lincolnshire Standard</i> newspaper, January 1918. Meanwhile, the port was used by hospital ships and some 4,000 sick or wounded troops passed through Boston.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cwgc1_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-cwgc1-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first cinema opened in 1910, and the town was used by film makers during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> to represent the Netherlands when the real thing was not available for filming. In 1913 a new Town Bridge was constructed. Central Park was purchased in 1919, and is now one of the focal points of the town. Electricity came to Boston during the early part of the century, and electrical <a href="/wiki/Street_light" title="Street light">street lighting</a> was provided from 1924.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> the borough lost 17 civilian dead through enemy air raids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> A memorial in <a href="/wiki/Boston_Cemetery" title="Boston Cemetery">Boston Cemetery</a> commemorates them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cwgc1_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-cwgc1-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Haven_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Haven Bridge (page does not exist)">Haven Bridge</a>, which now carries the two trunk roads over the river, was opened in 1966, and the new road built in the early 1970s rather separated <a href="/wiki/Skirbeck" title="Skirbeck">Skirbeck</a> from Boston; but the town largely avoided the development boom of the 1960s. A shopping centre, named the Pescod Centre, opened in 2004, bringing many new shops into the town. Further development was planned as of 2012<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>There had been widespread disillusionment with what was perceived as the somewhat apathetic and incompetent running of the local council. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_Borough_Council_elections,_2007" title="Boston Borough Council elections, 2007">Local Elections of 2007</a> this contributed to the overwhelming victory of a small <a href="/wiki/Boston_Bypass_Independents" title="Boston Bypass Independents">bypass pressure group</a> over the traditional political parties. Describing their victory, the new council leader Richard Austin said: "We knew that the mood of the people of Boston was very black and they really do want something to happen to Boston that isn't happening at the moment. "It's only a reflection of this black mood of the people of Boston."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_United" title="Boston United">Boston United</a> football club was relegated after losing its final match of the 2006/7 season against Wrexham, and following some financial irregularities during Steve Evans' time as manager of the club. A report, cited in <i>The Sun</i> in 2008, highlighted that Boston has the highest number of immigrants per capita of any town in Britain (an estimated one-quarter of the population are immigrants).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Railway">Railway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Railway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The railways came to Boston in 1848 following the building of The East Lincolnshire Railway from Grimsby to Boston and the simultaneous building of the Lincolnshire Loop Line by the Great Northern Railway which ran between Peterborough and York via Boston, Lincoln and Doncaster. This line was built before the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_Main_Line" title="East Coast Main Line">East Coast Main Line</a> and for a short while put Boston on the map as the GNR's main Locomotive Works before it was relocated to Doncaster in 1852.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Boston_railway_station" title="Boston railway station">Boston railway station</a> is served by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Midland_Trains" title="East Midland Trains">East Midland Trains</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Poacher_Line" title="Poacher Line">Poacher Line</a> from <a href="/wiki/Grantham" title="Grantham">Grantham</a> to <a href="/wiki/Skegness" title="Skegness">Skegness</a>. It was the southern terminus of the <a href="/wiki/East_Lincolnshire_Railway" title="East Lincolnshire Railway">East Lincolnshire Line</a> to <a href="/wiki/Louth,_Lincolnshire" title="Louth, Lincolnshire">Louth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grimsby" title="Grimsby">Grimsby</a> until closure in 1970.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Boston residents voted strongly (76% with 77% participation) in favour of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_withdrawal_from_the_European_Union" title="United Kingdom withdrawal from the European Union">leaving the European Union</a> on <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_European_Union_membership_referendum,_2016" title="United Kingdom European Union membership referendum, 2016">23 June 2016</a>, this was the strongest such vote in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brexitland_versus_Londonia_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brexitland_versus_Londonia-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boston_bypass">Boston bypass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Boston bypass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Boston_Bypass_Independents" title="Boston Bypass Independents">Boston Bypass Independents</a></div>
<p>In May 2007 a <a href="/wiki/Single-issue_politics" title="Single-issue politics">single-issue</a> political party, the <i>Independent Bypass Group</i>, campaigning for a <a href="/wiki/Bypass_(road)" title="Bypass (road)">bypass</a> to be built around Boston, <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>, took control of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_(borough)" title="Boston (borough)">Boston Borough Council</a>, removing the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> majority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Boston received its charter in 1545. It is the main settlement in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_(borough)" title="Boston (borough)">Boston</a> local government district of <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a> which includes the unparished town of Boston and eighteen other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parishes</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electoral_wards">Electoral wards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Electoral wards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In the local elections of 2007, many local councillors from the major parties were displaced by independent candidates whose main issue as a group is the construction of a road bypass which they believe is being deliberately denied by the Lincoln-centric members of the <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a> County Council. However, in 2011, the Conservative party took control with a majority,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> effectively removing the previous administration.</p>
<p>Nationally Boston is in the <a href="/wiki/East_Midlands_(European_Parliament_constituency)" title="East Midlands (European Parliament constituency)">East Midlands</a> European Parliament constituency, which elects six members. At the national level, the town is part of <a href="/wiki/Boston_and_Skegness_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Boston and Skegness (UK Parliament constituency)">the Boston and Skegness parliamentary constituency</a>, currently represented by <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> Matt Warman.The town was previously represented in parliament for 35 years by Conservative Sir Richard Body.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demography">Demography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Demography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the 2001 census, there were 35,124 people residing in Boston town, of whom 48.2% were male and 51.8% were female. Children under five accounted for approximately 5% of the population. 23% of the resident population in Boston were of retirement age. In the 2011 census the Borough of Boston had a population of 64,600 with 15% of the population having been born outside of the UK and 11% having been born in <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> accession countries (2001-2011) such as <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-research-lincs.org.uk_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-research-lincs.org.uk-23">[23]</a></sup> The non-white population made up 2.4% of the total population in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-research-lincs.org.uk_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-research-lincs.org.uk-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>80% of the population are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_(word)" title="Christian (word)">Christians</a>, the next highest religious minority were Hindus making up 0.7%.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arts_and_culture">Arts and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Arts and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Boston has historically had strong cultural connections to <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">the Netherlands</a>, and Dutch influence can be found in its <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brexitland_versus_Londonia_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brexitland_versus_Londonia-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Landmarks">Landmarks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Landmarks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some of the most interesting things to be seen in Boston lie not in the usual list of tourist features, but in the area of <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a>. However, there are remarkable sights of the more usual sort:</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parish church</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Botolph" title="Saint Botolph">Saint Botolph</a> is known locally as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_Stump" title="Boston Stump">The Stump</a></i> and is renowned for its lantern interior and 52 <a href="/wiki/Misericord" title="Misericord">misericords</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>The Grand Sluice is disguised by railway and road bridges, but it is there, keeping the tide out of the Fens and twice a day, allowing the water from the upland to scour the Haven. Not far away, in the opposite direction, was the boyhood home of <a href="/wiki/John_Foxe" title="John Foxe">John Foxe</a>, the author of <a href="/wiki/Foxe%27s_Book_of_Martyrs" title="Foxe's Book of Martyrs">Foxe's Book of Martyrs</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Town Bridge maintains the line of the road to Lindsey and from its western end, looking at the river side of the Exchange Building to the right, it is possible to see how the two ends of the building, founded on the natural levees of The Haven, have stood firm while the middle has sunk into the infill of the former river.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>From 1552, the Bostonians used to have their jail near The Stump (about where the red car in the photograph is located). This is likely to be where the <a href="/wiki/Scrooby" title="Scrooby">Scrooby</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilgrims" title="Pilgrims">Pilgrims</a> were imprisoned in 1607. The lawyers' quarter is still in use, just to the north of the church.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A statue of the founder of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Illustrated_London_News" title="The Illustrated London News">The Illustrated London News</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Ingram" title="Herbert Ingram">Herbert Ingram</a> is now located in front of The Stump (see photograph). The statue was designed by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Munro_(sculptor)" title="Alexander Munro (sculptor)">Alexander Munro</a> and was unveiled in October 1862. The allegorical figure, at the base of the monument, is a reference to Ingram's efforts to bring the first piped water to the town. He was also instrumental in bringing the railways to Boston. Born in nearby Paddock Grove, son of a butcher,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> he was also MP for <a href="/wiki/Boston_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Boston (UK Parliament constituency)">Boston</a>, from 1856, until his death in 1860, in a shipping accident on <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Lake Michigan</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The market, held on Saturdays and Wednesdays, in the Market Place and also on Wide Bargate on Wednesday. Market Place and Strait Bargate are the retail hub of the town centre. Coincidentally, No.1 Market Place and No.1 Strait Bargate are the same building.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>The seven-storeyed <a href="/wiki/Maud_Foster_Windmill" title="Maud Foster Windmill">Maud Foster Tower Windmill</a>, completed in 1819, by millwrights Norman &amp; Smithson of <a href="/wiki/Kingston_upon_Hull" title="Kingston upon Hull">Kingston upon Hull</a> for Issac and Thomas Reckitt, is currently the tallest operating <a href="/wiki/Windmill" title="Windmill">windmill</a> in England (80 feet or 24.4 metres to the top of the cap), following extensive restoration during the 1980s and early 1990s and is now a working museum. The tall mill, without the usual tar coating in Lincs, stands on the dyke above the drain it is named after and is unusual in having an odd number (five) of sails.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Boston Guildhall in which the Pilgrim Fathers were tried, on the <a href="/wiki/Storey" title="Storey">first floor</a>, by the <a href="/wiki/Magistrate" title="Magistrate">magistrates</a>, was converted into a museum in 1929. The American Room was opened by the U.S. Ambassador, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_P._Kennedy,_Sr." title="Joseph P. Kennedy, Sr.">Joseph P. Kennedy, Sr.</a>, in 1938. The cells in which the pilgrims are said to have been held at the time of their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trial_(law)" title="Trial (law)">trial</a> are on the <a href="/wiki/Storey" title="Storey">ground floor</a>. In 2005 it was closed for repair and refurbishment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pilgrim_Fathers_Memorial" title="Pilgrim Fathers Memorial">Pilgrim Fathers Memorial</a> is located on the north bank of The Haven a few miles outside the town. It was here at Scotia Creek, that the pilgrims made their first attempt to leave for the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">Dutch Republic</a> in 1607.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>The ruined Hussey Tower is all that remains of a medieval brick fortified house.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> 2 miles (3 km) east, Rochford Tower is another medieval tower house.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Skirbeck Quarter, on the right bank of The Haven, is the Black Sluice, the <a href="/wiki/Outfall" title="Outfall">outfall</a> of the <a href="/wiki/South_Forty-Foot_Drain" title="South Forty-Foot Drain">South Forty-Foot Drain</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Meridian" title="Prime Meridian">Prime Meridian</a> passes through the eastern side of Boston, marked by the fairly modern, suburban Meridian Road (<a class="external text" href="https://www.bing.com/mapspreview?where1=PE21%200NB,GB" rel="nofollow">PE21 0NB</a>), which straddles the line the road was named after.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>The oldest landmark is the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Boston_May_Fair&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Boston May Fair (page does not exist)">Boston May Fair</a> which has been held in the town every year since at least 1125. This fair is held during the first week of May, and is one of the largest outdoor fairs in the country. By tradition, the fair was officially opened by the incumbent mayor at 11 am on the May Day <a href="/wiki/Bank_holiday" title="Bank holiday">bank holiday</a>. However this is now not the case.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a class="external text" href="http://www.thehavengallery.org.uk/" rel="nofollow">Haven Gallery</a>, opened in 2005, was closed to the public in 2010 in a cost-cutting measure by Boston Borough Council.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frampton_Marsh" title="Frampton Marsh">Frampton Marsh</a> and Freiston Shore are two nature reserves, managed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RSPB" title="RSPB">RSPB</a>, which lie on <a href="/wiki/The_Wash" title="The Wash">The Wash</a> coast on either side of the mouth of <a href="/wiki/The_Haven,_Boston" title="The Haven, Boston">The Haven</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Dickens" title="The Last Dickens">The Last Dickens</a></i> is set in Boston.</li>
<li>Boston is often assumed to be the prototype for <a href="/wiki/Flaxborough" title="Flaxborough">Flaxborough</a>, in the detective novels of <a href="/wiki/Colin_Watson_(writer)" title="Colin Watson (writer)">Colin Watson</a>.</li>
<li>Boston was the birthplace of the 19th century author and poet <a href="/wiki/Jean_Ingelow" title="Jean Ingelow">Jean Ingelow</a>.</li>
<li>Boston was also the Dutch town in <i><a href="/wiki/One_of_Our_Aircraft_Is_Missing" title="One of Our Aircraft Is Missing">One of Our Aircraft Is Missing</a></i>, with scenes showing the Stump, externally and internally, and also the dock swing bridge on the River Haven.</li>
<li>Boston is mentioned several times in the English band <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/To_Kill_A_King_(band)" title="To Kill A King (band)">To Kill a King</a>'s EP <i>My Crooked Saint</i>.</li>
<li>Boston may have been used in Anthony Horowitz' book <i>Oblivion</i> in which he describes the village with the church of St Botolphs.</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_economy">Local economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Local economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Boston's most important industries are food production, including vegetables and potatoes; road haulage and logistics companies that carry the food; the Port of Boston which handles more than one million tons of cargo per year including the import of steel and timber and the export of grain and recyclable materials; and also shellfishing, other light industry and tourism. The port is connected by rail, with steel imports going by rail each day to Washwood Heath in Birmingham, and the port and town are also connected by trunk roads including the A16 and the A52.</p>
<p>Boston has two weekly newspapers, <i>The Boston Standard</i> and <i>The Boston Target</i>, and a community radio station called Endeavour Radio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Boston's market<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> is held every Wednesday and Saturday<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> in one of England's largest market places, with an additional market and outside auction held on Wednesdays on Bargate Green.</p>
<p>Boston has a theatre and arts centre called Blackfriars<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> which is housed in the oldest building in Boston town centre, which was formerly the refectory of the Benedictine friary, built in the 13th century and once visited by King Edward I.</p>
<p>Work was due to commence in 2014 on a new marina of the river Witham which would offer moorings, a restaurant and other facilities. The town is also set to be a major part of the Fens waterway project which will be an equivalent of the Norfolk Broads. This is scheduled to be completed in 2018.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In late 2013, a £100 million development was announced for the outskirts of town on the A16 towards Kirton. This development, named the Quadrant, is split in two phases. Phase one consists of a new football ground for <a href="/wiki/Boston_United_F.C." title="Boston United F.C.">Boston United F.C.</a>, 500 new homes, retail and business outlets and a possible supermarket. This development also includes the beginning of a relief road and would eventually link the A52 Grantham Road and the A16 together. Phase two, still in the development stage, consists of a possible second new marina, more new homes and retail units.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Princess Royal Arena is located on The Boardsides, just outside Boston. The stadium is unique owing to the attention given to the disabled.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rugby">Rugby</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Rugby">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Boston_Rugby_Club" title="Boston Rugby Club">Boston Rugby Club</a> is based at the Princess Royal Arena. The club was established in 1927 by Ernst Clark, who had an interest in providing activity for boys.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Football">Football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The town has two non-league football clubs. The more senior <a href="/wiki/Boston_United_F.C." title="Boston United F.C.">Boston United</a>, nicknamed The Pilgrims, plays in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conference_North" title="Conference North">Conference North</a>. The stadium is currently located on York Street in the centre of the town and has an approximate capacity of 6,200. The town's second club, <a href="/wiki/Boston_Town_F.C." title="Boston Town F.C.">Boston Town</a>, nicknamed The Poachers, plays in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Counties_Football_League" title="United Counties Football League">United Counties Football League</a>. Its home games are played at their stadium on Tattershall Road, on the outskirts of Boston. The two teams traditionally play each other at the beginning of each season.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rowing">Rowing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Rowing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Boston Rowing Club, near Carlton Road, hosts the annual 33 miles (53 km) <a href="/wiki/Boston_Rowing_Marathon" title="Boston Rowing Marathon">Boston Rowing Marathon</a> each year in mid-September. Crews from throughout the world compete, starting at <a href="/wiki/Brayford_Pool" title="Brayford Pool">Brayford Pool</a> in Lincoln, and finishing in times from three to six hours.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Speedway">Speedway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Speedway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Speedway racing was staged at a stadium in New Hammond Beck Road in the 1970s and 1980s. The Boston Barracudas raced in the British League Division Two, (now the Premier League) and in 1973 won the League and the Knock-out Cup, with one member winning the League Individual Championship.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> After the New Hammond Beck Road Stadium was sold for re-development in 1988, there were failed attempts to secure a new venue in the 1990s. A team, known as Boston, raced in the Conference League at King's Lynn.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Swimming">Swimming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Swimming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Boston Amateur Swimming Club holds galas and open meets, including the Boston Open, and two yearly club championship events. It trains at the Geoff Moulder Swimming Pool.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sailing">Sailing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Sailing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Witham Sailing Club is a based on the banks of the Witham, with its own clubhouse.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the mid-2000s Boston was shown to have the highest obesity rate of any town in the United Kingdom, with one-third of its adults (31%) considered clinically obese. Obesity has been linked to social deprivation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Independent_schools">Independent schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Independent schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>St George's Preparatory School, established in 2011, is housed in a Grade II listed building, the former home of the town architect William Wheeler. The school, which follows the Forest School Ethos,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> caters for the 3–11 year age group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secondary_schools">Secondary schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Secondary schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Boston_Grammar_School" title="Boston Grammar School">Boston Grammar School</a>, an all-male selective school, is on South End, near the John Adams Way (A52/A16), <a class="external text" href="http://www.boston.gov.uk/leisurepool" rel="nofollow">Geoff Moulder Leisure Centre</a> and River Witham. Its female counterpart, <a class="external text" href="http://www.bostonhighschool.co.uk" rel="nofollow">Boston High School</a> is on Spilsby Road (A16), in the north of the town next to the <a class="external text" href="http://www.ulh.nhs.uk/Contact" rel="nofollow">Pilgrim Hospital</a>. They were planning to join schools and make a federation but continued pressure and protests prevented that from happening. <a href="/wiki/Haven_High_Academy" title="Haven High Academy">Haven High Academy</a> is on Marian Road to the north of the town. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_College,_Lincolnshire" title="Boston College, Lincolnshire">Boston College</a> is on Skirbeck Road. Sixth forms in the town are located at the Grammar School, the High School and Haven High Academy.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kitwood_Boys_School" title="Kitwood Boys School">Kitwood Boys School</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kitwood_Girls%27_School&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kitwood Girls' School (page does not exist)">Kitwood Girls' School</a> were both examples of the <a href="/wiki/Post-war" title="Post-war">post-war</a>, <a href="/wiki/Secondary_modern_school" title="Secondary modern school">secondary modern school</a> system. The boys' school located in Mill Road was closed in 1993 and now forms part of Boston College. The former girls' school has now become <a href="/wiki/Haven_High_Academy" title="Haven High Academy">Haven High Academy</a></p>
<p>Boston currently has one lowest standard of education in Lincolnshire, with only 72% of GCSE students receiving grades above C. However Boston High School is in the top 5% in the whole country as all students achieved at least a grade of A* to C.</p>
