<p>The <b>Boston Tea Party</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Direct_action#Violent_direct_action" title="Direct action">political protest</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Sons_of_Liberty" title="Sons of Liberty">Sons of Liberty</a> in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, on December 16, 1773. The demonstrators, some disguised as <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans</a>, in defiance of the <a href="/wiki/Tea_Act" title="Tea Act">Tea Act</a> of May 10, 1773, destroyed an entire shipment of <a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">tea</a> sent by the <a href="/wiki/East_India_Company" title="East India Company">East India Company</a>. They boarded the ships and threw the chests of tea into <a href="/wiki/Boston_Harbor" title="Boston Harbor">Boston Harbor</a>. The British government responded harshly and the episode escalated into the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>. The Tea Party became an iconic event of American history, and other political protests such as the <a href="/wiki/Tea_Party_movement" title="Tea Party movement">Tea Party movement</a> after 2010 explicitly refer to it.</p>
<p>The Tea Party was the culmination of a resistance movement throughout <a href="/wiki/British_America" title="British America">British America</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Tea_Act" title="Tea Act">Tea Act</a>, which had been passed by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Great_Britain" title="Parliament of Great Britain">British Parliament</a> in 1773. Colonists objected to the Tea Act because they believed that it violated their <a href="/wiki/Rights_of_Englishmen" title="Rights of Englishmen">rights as Englishmen</a> to "<a href="/wiki/No_taxation_without_representation" title="No taxation without representation">No taxation without representation</a>", that is, be taxed only by their own elected representatives and not by a British parliament in which they were not represented. Protesters had successfully prevented the unloading of taxed tea in three other colonies, but in Boston, embattled Royal Governor <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hutchinson_(governor)" title="Thomas Hutchinson (governor)">Thomas Hutchinson</a> refused to allow the tea to be returned to Britain.</p>
<p>The Boston Tea Party was a significant event in the growth of the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>. Parliament responded in 1774 with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coercive_Acts" title="Coercive Acts">Coercive Acts</a>, or Intolerable Acts, which, among other provisions, ended local self-government in Massachusetts and <a href="/wiki/Boston_Port_Act" title="Boston Port Act">closed Boston's commerce</a>. Colonists up and down the <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies" title="Thirteen Colonies">Thirteen Colonies</a> in turn responded to the Coercive Acts with additional acts of protest, and by convening the <a href="/wiki/First_Continental_Congress" title="First Continental Congress">First Continental Congress</a>, which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petition_to_the_King_(1774)" title="Petition to the King (1774)">petitioned the British monarch</a> for repeal of the acts and coordinated colonial resistance to them. The crisis escalated, and the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a> began near Boston in 1775.</p>

<p>The Boston Tea Party arose from two issues confronting the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> in 1765: the financial problems of the <a href="/wiki/East_India_Company" title="East India Company">British East India Company</a>; and an ongoing dispute about the extent of Parliament's authority, if any, over the British American colonies without seating any elected representation. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Ministry" title="North Ministry">North Ministry</a>'s attempt to resolve these issues produced a showdown that would eventually result in revolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tea_trade_to_1767">Tea trade to 1767</span></h3>
<p>As Europeans developed a taste for tea in the 17th century, rival companies were formed to import the product from <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In England, Parliament gave the East India Company a monopoly on the importation of tea in 1698.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> When tea became popular in the British colonies, Parliament sought to eliminate foreign competition by passing an act in 1721 that required colonists to import their tea only from Great Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The East India Company did not export tea to the colonies; by law, the company was required to sell its tea <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wholesale" title="Wholesale">wholesale</a> at auctions in England. British firms bought this tea and exported it to the colonies, where they resold it to merchants in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Until 1767, the <a href="/wiki/East_India_Company" title="East India Company">East India Company</a> paid an <a href="/wiki/Ad_valorem_tax" title="Ad valorem tax"><i>ad valorem</i> tax</a> of about 25% on tea that it imported into Great Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Parliament laid additional taxes on tea sold for consumption in Britain. These high taxes, combined with the fact that tea imported into the Dutch Republic was not taxed by the Dutch government, meant that Britons and British Americans could buy <a href="/wiki/Smuggling" title="Smuggling">smuggled</a> Dutch tea at much cheaper prices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The biggest market for illicit tea was England—by the 1760s the East India Company was losing £400,000 per year to smugglers in Great Britain<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>—but Dutch tea was also smuggled into British America in significant quantities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1767, to help the East India Company compete with smuggled Dutch tea, Parliament passed the Indemnity Act, which lowered the tax on tea consumed in Great Britain, and gave the East India Company a refund of the 25% duty on tea that was re-exported to the colonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> To help offset this loss of government revenue, Parliament also passed the <a href="/wiki/Townshend_Acts" title="Townshend Acts">Townshend Revenue Act</a> of 1767, which levied new taxes, including one on tea, in the colonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Instead of solving the smuggling problem, however, the Townshend duties renewed a controversy about Parliament's right to tax the colonies.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Townshend_duty_crisis">Townshend duty crisis</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/Townshend_Acts" title="Townshend Acts">Townshend Acts</a>.</div>
<p>Controversy between Great Britain and the colonies arose in the 1760s when Parliament sought, for the first time, to impose a direct tax on the colonies for the purpose of raising revenue. Some colonists, known in the colonies as <a href="/wiki/Patriot_(American_Revolution)" title="Patriot (American Revolution)">Whigs</a>, objected to the new tax program, arguing that it was a violation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Constitution" title="British Constitution">British Constitution</a>. Britons and British Americans agreed that, according to the constitution, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_subjects" title="British subjects">British subjects</a> could not be <a href="/wiki/No_taxation_without_representation" title="No taxation without representation">taxed without the consent</a> of their elected representatives. In Great Britain, this meant that taxes could only be levied by Parliament. Colonists, however, did not elect members of Parliament, and so American Whigs argued that the colonies could not be taxed by that body. According to Whigs, colonists could only be taxed by their own colonial assemblies. Colonial protests resulted in the repeal of the <a href="/wiki/Stamp_Act_1765" title="Stamp Act 1765">Stamp Act</a> in 1766, but in the 1766 <a href="/wiki/Declaratory_Act" title="Declaratory Act">Declaratory Act</a>, Parliament continued to insist that it had the right to legislate for the colonies "in all cases whatsoever".</p>
<p>When new taxes were levied in the Townshend Revenue Act of 1767, Whig colonists again responded with protests and boycotts. Merchants organized a non-importation agreement, and many colonists pledged to abstain from drinking <a href="/wiki/Tea_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Tea in the United Kingdom">British tea</a>, with activists in New England promoting alternatives, such as domestic <a href="/wiki/Labrador_tea" title="Labrador tea">Labrador tea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Smuggling continued apace, especially in New York and Philadelphia, where tea smuggling had always been more extensive than in Boston. Dutied British tea continued to be imported into Boston, however, especially by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Clarke_(merchant)" title="Richard Clarke (merchant)">Richard Clarke</a> and the sons of Massachusetts Governor <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hutchinson_(governor)" title="Thomas Hutchinson (governor)">Thomas Hutchinson</a>, until pressure from Massachusetts Whigs compelled them to abide by the non-importation agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Parliament finally responded to the protests by repealing the Townshend taxes in 1770, except for the tea duty, which Prime Minister <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_North" title="Lord North">Lord North</a> kept to assert "the right of taxing the Americans".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This partial repeal of the taxes was enough to bring an end to the non-importation movement by October 1770.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> From 1771 to 1773, British tea was once again imported into the colonies in significant amounts, with merchants paying the Townshend duty of three pence per pound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Boston was the largest colonial importer of legal tea; smugglers still dominated the market in New York and Philadelphia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tea_Act_of_1773">Tea Act of 1773</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tea_Act" title="Tea Act">Tea Act</a></div>
<p>The Indemnity Act of 1767, which gave the East India Company a refund of the duty on tea that was re-exported to the colonies, expired in 1772. Parliament passed a new act in 1772 that reduced this refund, effectively leaving a 10% duty on tea imported into Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The act also restored the tea taxes within Britain that had been repealed in 1767, and left in place the three <a href="/wiki/Penny" title="Penny">pence</a> Townshend duty in the colonies. With this new tax burden driving up the price of British tea, sales plummeted. The company continued to import tea into Great Britain, however, amassing a huge surplus of product that no one would buy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> For these and other reasons, by late 1772 the East India Company, one of Britain's most important commercial institutions, was in a serious financial crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eliminating some of the taxes was one obvious solution to the crisis. The East India Company initially sought to have the Townshend duty repealed, but the North ministry was unwilling because such an action might be interpreted as a retreat from Parliament's position that it had the right to tax the colonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> More importantly, the tax collected from the Townshend duty was used to pay the salaries of some colonial governors and judges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> This was in fact the purpose of the Townshend tax: previously these officials had been paid by the colonial assemblies, but Parliament now paid their salaries to keep them dependent on the British government rather than allowing them to be accountable to the colonists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another possible solution for reducing the growing mound of tea in the East India Company warehouses was to sell it cheaply in Europe. This possibility was investigated, but it was determined that the tea would simply be smuggled back into Great Britain, where it would undersell the taxed product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The best market for the East India Company's surplus tea, so it seemed, was the American colonies, if a way could be found to make it cheaper than the smuggled Dutch tea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The North ministry's solution was the <a href="/wiki/Tea_Act" title="Tea Act">Tea Act</a>, which received the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Assent" title="Royal Assent">assent</a> of <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George III of the United Kingdom">King George</a> on May 10, 1773.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> This act restored the East India Company's full refund on the duty for importing tea into Britain, and also permitted the company, for the first time, to export tea to the colonies on its own account. This would allow the company to reduce costs by eliminating the middlemen who bought the tea at wholesale auctions in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Instead of selling to middlemen, the company now appointed colonial merchants to receive the tea on <a href="/wiki/Consignment" title="Consignment">consignment</a>; the consignees would in turn sell the tea for a commission. In July 1773, tea consignees were selected in New York, Philadelphia, Boston, and Charleston.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Tea Act retained the three pence Townshend duty on tea imported to the colonies. Some members of Parliament wanted to eliminate this tax, arguing that there was no reason to provoke another colonial controversy. Former Chancellor of the Exchequer <a href="/wiki/William_Dowdeswell_(Chancellor)" title="William Dowdeswell (Chancellor)">William Dowdeswell</a>, for example, warned Lord North that the Americans would not accept the tea if the Townshend duty remained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> But North did not want to give up the revenue from the Townshend tax, primarily because it was used to pay the salaries of colonial officials; maintaining the right of taxing the Americans was a secondary concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> According to historian Benjamin Labaree, "A stubborn Lord North had unwittingly hammered a nail in the coffin of the old British Empire."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Even with the Townshend duty in effect, the Tea Act would allow the East India Company to sell tea more cheaply than before, undercutting the prices offered by smugglers, but also undercutting colonial tea importers, who paid the tax and received no refund. In 1772, legally imported <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bohea" title="Bohea">Bohea</a>, the most common variety of tea, sold for about 3 <a href="/wiki/Shilling" title="Shilling">shillings</a> (3s) per pound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> After the Tea Act, colonial consignees would be able to sell it for 2 shillings per pound (2s), just under the smugglers' price of 2 shillings and 1 penny (2s 1d).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Realizing that the payment of the Townshend duty was politically sensitive, the company hoped to conceal the tax by making arrangements to have it paid either in London once the tea was landed in the colonies, or have the consignees quietly pay the duties after the tea was sold. This effort to hide the tax from the colonists was unsuccessful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resisting_the_Tea_Act">Resisting the Tea Act</span></h2>

<p>In September and October 1773, seven ships carrying East India Company tea were sent to the colonies: four were bound for Boston, and one each for New York, Philadelphia, and Charleston.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In the ships were more than 2,000 chests containing nearly 600,000 pounds of tea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Americans learned the details of the Tea Act while the ships were en route, and opposition began to mount.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Whigs, sometimes calling themselves <a href="/wiki/Sons_of_Liberty" title="Sons of Liberty">Sons of Liberty</a>, began a campaign to raise awareness and to convince or compel the consignees to resign, in the same way that stamp distributors had been forced to resign in the 1765 Stamp Act crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>The protest movement that culminated with the Boston Tea Party was not a dispute about high taxes. The price of legally imported tea was actually reduced by the Tea Act of 1773. Protesters were instead concerned with a variety of other issues. The familiar "no taxation without representation" argument, along with the question of the extent of Parliament's authority in the colonies, remained prominent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Samuel Adams considered the British tea monopoly to be "equal to a tax" and to raise the same representation issue whether or not a tax was applied to it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Some regarded the purpose of the tax program—to make leading officials independent of colonial influence—as a dangerous infringement of colonial rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> This was especially true in Massachusetts, the only colony where the Townshend program had been fully implemented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Colonial merchants, some of them smugglers, played a significant role in the protests. Because the Tea Act made legally imported tea cheaper, it threatened to put smugglers of Dutch tea out of business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Legitimate tea importers who had not been named as consignees by the East India Company were also threatened with financial ruin by the Tea Act.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Another major concern for merchants was that the Tea Act gave the East India Company a monopoly on the tea trade, and it was feared that this government-created monopoly might be extended in the future to include other goods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>South of Boston, protesters successfully compelled the tea consignees to resign. In Charleston, the consignees had been forced to resign by early December, and the unclaimed tea was seized by customs officials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> There were mass protest meetings in Philadelphia. <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Rush" title="Benjamin Rush">Benjamin Rush</a> urged his fellow countrymen to oppose the landing of the tea, because the cargo contained "the seeds of slavery".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> By early December, the Philadelphia consignees had resigned and the tea ship <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Tea_Party" title="Philadelphia Tea Party">returned to England with its cargo</a> following a confrontation with the ship's captain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The tea ship bound for New York City was delayed by bad weather; by the time it arrived, the consignees had resigned, and the ship returned to England with the tea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standoff_in_Boston">Standoff in Boston</span></h2>
<p>In every colony except Massachusetts, protesters were able to force the tea consignees to resign or to return the tea to England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In Boston, however, Governor Hutchinson was determined to hold his ground. He convinced the tea consignees, two of whom were his sons, not to back down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BostonTeaPartyJoyceNotice.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="388" data-file-width="619" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/BostonTeaPartyJoyceNotice.jpg/220px-BostonTeaPartyJoyceNotice.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/BostonTeaPartyJoyceNotice.jpg/330px-BostonTeaPartyJoyceNotice.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/BostonTeaPartyJoyceNotice.jpg/440px-BostonTeaPartyJoyceNotice.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BostonTeaPartyJoyceNotice.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
This notice from the "Chairman of the Committee for <a href="/wiki/Tarring_and_feathering" title="Tarring and feathering">Tarring and Feathering</a>" in Boston denounced the tea consignees as "traitors to their country".</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>When the tea ship <i>Dartmouth</i> arrived in the Boston Harbor in late November, Whig leader <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Adams" title="Samuel Adams">Samuel Adams</a> called for a mass meeting to be held at <a href="/wiki/Faneuil_Hall" title="Faneuil Hall">Faneuil Hall</a> on November 29, 1773. Thousands of people arrived, so many that the meeting was moved to the larger <a href="/wiki/Old_South_Meeting_House" title="Old South Meeting House">Old South Meeting House</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> British law required the <i>Dartmouth</i> to unload and pay the duties within twenty days or customs officials could confiscate the cargo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The mass meeting passed a resolution, introduced by Adams and based on a <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Tea_Party" title="Philadelphia Tea Party">similar set of resolutions promulgated earlier in Philadelphia</a>, urging the captain of the <i>Dartmouth</i> to send the ship back without paying the import duty. Meanwhile, the meeting assigned twenty-five men to watch the ship and prevent the tea—including a number of chests from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Davison,_Newman_and_Co." title="Davison, Newman and Co.">Davison, Newman and Co.</a> of London—from being unloaded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Governor Hutchinson refused to grant permission for the <i>Dartmouth</i> to leave without paying the duty. Two more tea ships, the <i>Eleanor</i> and the <i>Beaver</i>, arrived in Boston Harbor (there was another tea ship headed for Boston, the <i>William</i>, but it encountered a storm and was destroyed before it could reach its destination<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>). On December 16—the last day of the <i>Dartmouth's</i> deadline—about 7,000 people had gathered around the Old South Meeting House.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> After receiving a report that Governor Hutchinson had again refused to let the ships leave, Adams announced that "This meeting can do nothing further to save the country." According to a popular story, Adams's statement was a prearranged signal for the "tea party" to begin. However, this claim did not appear in print until nearly a century after the event, in a biography of Adams written by his great-grandson, who apparently misinterpreted the evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> According to eyewitness accounts, people did not leave the meeting until ten or fifteen minutes after Adams's alleged "signal", and Adams in fact tried to stop people from leaving because the meeting was not yet over.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Destruction_of_the_tea">Destruction of the tea</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Boston_Tea_Party-Cooper.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="560" data-file-width="896" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Boston_Tea_Party-Cooper.jpg/220px-Boston_Tea_Party-Cooper.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Boston_Tea_Party-Cooper.jpg/330px-Boston_Tea_Party-Cooper.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Boston_Tea_Party-Cooper.jpg/440px-Boston_Tea_Party-Cooper.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

1789 engraving of the destruction of the tea</div>

<p>While Samuel Adams tried to reassert control of the meeting, people poured out of the Old South Meeting House to prepare to take action. In some cases, this involved donning what may have been elaborately prepared <a href="/wiki/Mohawk_people" title="Mohawk people">Mohawk</a> costumes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> While disguising their individual faces was imperative, because of the illegality of their protest, dressing as Mohawk warriors was a specific and symbolic choice. It showed that the Sons of Liberty identified with America, over their official status as subjects of Great Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>That evening, a group of 30 to 130 men, some dressed in the Mohawk warrior disguises, boarded the three vessels and, over the course of three hours, dumped all 342 chests of tea into the water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> The precise location of the Griffin's Wharf site of the Tea Party has been subject to prolonged uncertainty; a comprehensive study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> places it near the foot of Hutchinson Street (today's Pearl Street).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction">Reaction</span></h2>
<p>Whether or not Samuel Adams helped plan the Boston Tea Party is disputed, but he immediately worked to publicize and defend it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> He argued that the Tea Party was not the act of a lawless mob, but was instead a principled protest and the only remaining option the people had to defend their constitutional rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>By "constitution" he referred to the idea that all governments have a constitution, written or not, and that the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Constitution of the United Kingdom">constitution of Great Britain</a> could be interpreted as banning the levying of taxes without representation. For example, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_of_Rights_of_1689" title="Bill of Rights of 1689">Bill of Rights of 1689</a> established that long-term taxes could not be levied without Parliament, and other precedents said that Parliament must actually represent the people it ruled over, in order to "count".</p>
<p>Governor Thomas Hutchinson had been urging London to take a hard line with the Sons of Liberty. If he had done what the other royal governors had done and let the ship owners and captains resolve the issue with the colonists, the <i>Dartmouth</i>, <i>Eleanor</i> and the <i>Beaver</i> would have left without unloading any tea.</p>
<p>In Britain, even those politicians considered friends of the colonies were appalled and this act united all parties there against the colonies. The Prime Minister <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_North" title="Lord North">Lord North</a> said, "Whatever may be the consequence, we must risk something; if we do not, all is over".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> The British government felt this action could not remain unpunished, and responded by closing the port of Boston and putting in place other laws known as the "<a href="/wiki/Intolerable_Acts" title="Intolerable Acts">Coercive Acts</a>." <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a> stated that the destroyed tea must be paid for, all ninety thousand pounds (which, at two shillings per pound, came to £9,000, or £1.03 million [2014, approx. $1.7 million US]).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inflation-UK_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-UK-67">[67]</a></sup> Robert Murray, a New York merchant, went to Lord North with three other merchants and offered to pay for the losses, but the offer was turned down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>The incident resulted in a similar effect in America when news of the Boston Tea Party reached London in January and Parliament responded with a series of acts known collectively in the colonies as the <a href="/wiki/Intolerable_Acts" title="Intolerable Acts">Intolerable Acts</a>. These were intended to punish Boston for the destruction of private property, restore British authority in Massachusetts, and otherwise reform colonial government in America. Although the first two, the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Port_Act" title="Boston Port Act">Boston Port Act</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Government_Act" title="Massachusetts Government Act">Massachusetts Government Act</a>, applied only to Massachusetts, colonists outside that colony feared that their governments could now also be changed by legislative fiat in England. The Intolerable Acts were viewed as a violation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Constitution" title="British Constitution">constitutional rights</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_rights" title="Natural rights">natural rights</a>, and colonial charters, and united many colonists throughout America,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> exemplified by the calling of the <a href="/wiki/First_Continental_Congress" title="First Continental Congress">First Continental Congress</a> in September 1774.</p>
<p>A number of colonists were inspired by the Boston Tea Party to carry out similar acts, such as the burning of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMS_Peggy_Stewart" title="HMS Peggy Stewart"><i>Peggy Stewart</i></a>. The Boston Tea Party eventually proved to be one of the many reactions that led to the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In his December 17, 1773 entry in his diary, John Adams wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Last Night 3 Cargoes of Bohea Tea were emptied into the Sea. This Morning a Man of War sails.</p>
<p>This is the most magnificent Movement of all. There is a Dignity, a Majesty, a Sublimity, in this last Effort of the Patriots, that I greatly admire. The People should never rise, without doing something to be remembered—something notable And striking. This Destruction of the Tea is so bold, so daring, so firm, intrepid and inflexible, and it must have so important Consequences, and so lasting, that I cant but consider it as an Epocha in History.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>There was a repeat performance on March 7, 1774, but it was much less destructive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 1775, Britain passed the <a href="/wiki/Conciliatory_Resolution" title="Conciliatory Resolution">Conciliatory Resolution</a>, which ended taxation for any colony that satisfactorily provided for the imperial defense and the upkeep of imperial officers. The tax on tea was repealed with the <a href="/wiki/Taxation_of_Colonies_Act_1778" title="Taxation of Colonies Act 1778">Taxation of Colonies Act 1778</a>, part of another Parliamentary attempt at conciliation that failed.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span></h2>

<p>John Adams and many other Americans considered tea drinking to be unpatriotic following the Boston Tea Party. Tea drinking declined during and after the Revolution, resulting in a shift to <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">coffee</a> as the preferred hot drink.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to historian Alfred Young, the term "Boston Tea Party" did not appear in print until 1834.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> Before that time, the event was usually referred to as the "destruction of the tea". According to Young, American writers were for many years apparently reluctant to celebrate the destruction of property, and so the event was usually ignored in histories of the American Revolution. This began to change in the 1830s, however, especially with the publication of biographies of <a href="/wiki/George_Robert_Twelves_Hewes" title="George Robert Twelves Hewes">George Robert Twelves Hewes</a>, one of the few still-living participants of the "tea party", as it then became known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>The issue was never the tax but how the tax was passed without American input; United States Congress taxed tea from 1789 to 1872.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Boston Tea Party has often been referenced in other political protests. When <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohandas_Karamchand_Gandhi" title="Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi">Mohandas K. Gandhi</a> led a mass burning of Indian registration cards in South Africa in 1908, a British newspaper compared the event to the Boston Tea Party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> When Gandhi met with the British viceroy in 1930 after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salt_Satyagraha" title="Salt Satyagraha">Indian salt protest</a> campaign, Gandhi took some duty-free salt from his shawl and said, with a smile, that the salt was "to remind us of the famous Boston Tea Party."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<p>American activists from a variety of political viewpoints have invoked the Tea Party as a symbol of protest. In 1973, on the 200th anniversary of the Tea Party, a mass meeting at <a href="/wiki/Faneuil_Hall" title="Faneuil Hall">Faneuil Hall</a> called for the impeachment of President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> and protested oil companies in the ongoing <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">oil crisis</a>. Afterwards, protesters boarded a replica ship in Boston Harbor, hanged Nixon in effigy, and dumped several empty oil drums into the harbor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> In 1998, two conservative US Congressmen put the <a href="/wiki/Internal_Revenue_Code" title="Internal Revenue Code">federal tax code</a> into a chest marked "tea" and dumped it into the harbor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, a <a href="/wiki/Libertarianism" title="Libertarianism">libertarian</a> political party called the "<a href="/wiki/Boston_Tea_Party_(political_party)" title="Boston Tea Party (political party)">Boston Tea Party</a>" was founded. In 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Ron_Paul" title="Ron Paul">Ron Paul</a> "Tea Party" <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Money_bomb" title="Money bomb">money bomb</a>, held on the 234th anniversary of the Boston Tea Party, broke the one-day fund-raising record by raising $6.04 million in 24 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> Subsequently, these fund-raising "Tea parties" grew into the <a href="/wiki/Tea_Party_movement" title="Tea Party movement">Tea Party movement</a>, which dominated politics for the next two years, culminating in a voter victory for the Republicans in 2010 who were widely elected to seats in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boston_Tea_Party_Ships_and_Museum">Boston Tea Party Ships and Museum</span></h3>
<p>The Boston Tea Party Museum is located on the Congress Street Bridge in Boston. It features reenactments, a documentary, and a number of interactive exhibits. The museum features two replica ships of the period, the <i>Eleanor</i> and the <i>Beaver</i>. Additionally, the museum possesses one of two known tea chests from the original event, part of its permanent collection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
