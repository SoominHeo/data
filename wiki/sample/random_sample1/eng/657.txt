<p><b>Fifth Avenue</b> is a major <a href="/wiki/Thoroughfare" title="Thoroughfare">thoroughfare</a> going through the <a href="/wiki/Borough_(New_York_City)" title="Borough (New York City)">borough</a> of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, United States. It stretches from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/143rd_Street_(Manhattan)" title="143rd Street (Manhattan)">West 143rd Street</a> in <a href="/wiki/Harlem" title="Harlem">Harlem</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waverly_Place_(Manhattan)" title="Waverly Place (Manhattan)">Washington Square North</a> at <a href="/wiki/Washington_Square_Park" title="Washington Square Park">Washington Square Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Village" title="Greenwich Village">Greenwich Village</a>. It is considered among the most expensive and best shopping streets in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-huffpost_20100921_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-huffpost_20100921-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_19970429_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes_19970429-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fifth_Avenue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:327px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fifth_Avenue_after_a_snow_storm.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="763" data-file-width="952" height="260" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Fifth_Avenue_after_a_snow_storm.jpg/325px-Fifth_Avenue_after_a_snow_storm.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Fifth_Avenue_after_a_snow_storm.jpg/488px-Fifth_Avenue_after_a_snow_storm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Fifth_Avenue_after_a_snow_storm.jpg/650px-Fifth_Avenue_after_a_snow_storm.jpg 2x" width="325"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fifth_Avenue_after_a_snow_storm.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Fifth Avenue after a snow storm in 1905</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The lower stretch of Fifth Avenue extended the stylish neighborhood of Washington Square northwards. The high status of Fifth Avenue was confirmed in 1862, when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caroline_Webster_Schermerhorn_Astor" title="Caroline Webster Schermerhorn Astor">Caroline Schermerhorn Astor</a> settled on the southwest corner of 34th Street, and the beginning of the end of its reign as a residential street was symbolized by the erection, in 1893, of the Astoria Hotel on the site of her house, later linked to its neighbor as the <a href="/wiki/Waldorf%E2%80%93Astoria_(New_York,_1893)" title="Waldorf–Astoria (New York, 1893)">Waldorf–Astoria Hotel</a> (now the site of the <a href="/wiki/Empire_State_Building" title="Empire State Building">Empire State Building</a>). Fifth Avenue is the central scene in <a href="/wiki/Edith_Wharton" title="Edith Wharton">Edith Wharton</a>'s 1920 Pulitzer Prize winning novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Age_of_Innocence" title="The Age of Innocence">The Age of Innocence</a></i>. The novel describes New York's social elite in the 1870s and provides historical context to Fifth Avenue and New York's aristocratic families.</p>
<p>Originally a narrower thoroughfare, much of Fifth Avenue south of <a href="/wiki/Central_Park" title="Central Park">Central Park</a> was widened in 1908, sacrificing its wide sidewalks to accommodate the increasing traffic. The midtown blocks, now famously commercial, were largely a residential district until the start of the 20th century. The first commercial building on Fifth Avenue was erected by <a href="/wiki/B._Altman_and_Company" title="B. Altman and Company">Benjamin Altman</a> who bought the corner lot on the northeast corner of <a href="/wiki/34th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="34th Street (Manhattan)">34th Street</a> in 1896, and demolished the <a href="/wiki/280_Broadway" title="280 Broadway">"Marble Palace"</a> of his arch-rival, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Turney_Stewart" title="Alexander Turney Stewart">A. T. Stewart</a>. In 1906 his department store, <a href="/wiki/B._Altman_and_Company" title="B. Altman and Company">B. Altman and Company</a>, occupied the whole of its block front. The result was the creation of a high-end shopping district that attracted fashionable women and the upscale stores that wished to serve them. <a href="/wiki/Lord_%26_Taylor" title="Lord &amp; Taylor">Lord &amp; Taylor</a>'s flagship store is still located on Fifth Avenue near the <a href="/wiki/Empire_State_Building" title="Empire State Building">Empire State Building</a> and the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Public_Library" title="New York Public Library">New York Public Library</a>.</p>
<div style="clear:left;"></div>
<p>In the 1920s traffic towers controlled important intersections from 14th to 59th Streets.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fifth_Avenue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fifth Avenue originates at <a href="/wiki/Washington_Square_Park" title="Washington Square Park">Washington Square Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Village" title="Greenwich Village">Greenwich Village</a> and runs northwards through the heart of <a href="/wiki/Midtown_Manhattan" title="Midtown Manhattan">Midtown</a>, along the eastern side of <a href="/wiki/Central_Park" title="Central Park">Central Park</a>, where it forms the boundary of the <a href="/wiki/Upper_East_Side" title="Upper East Side">Upper East Side</a> and through <a href="/wiki/Harlem" title="Harlem">Harlem</a>, where it terminates at the <a href="/wiki/Harlem_River" title="Harlem River">Harlem River</a> at 142nd Street. Traffic crosses the river on the <a href="/wiki/Madison_Avenue_Bridge" title="Madison Avenue Bridge">Madison Avenue Bridge</a>. Fifth Avenue serves as the dividing line for <a href="/wiki/House_numbering" title="House numbering">house numbering</a> and west-east streets in Manhattan, just as <a href="/wiki/Jerome_Avenue" title="Jerome Avenue">Jerome Avenue</a> does in <a href="/wiki/The_Bronx" title="The Bronx">the Bronx</a>. It separates, for example, East 59th Street from West 59th Street. From this zero point for <i>street</i> addresses, numbers increase in both directions as one moves away from Fifth Avenue, with 1 West 58th Street on the corner at Fifth Avenue, and 300 West 58th Street located three blocks to the west of it.</p>
<p>The section of Fifth Avenue that crosses <a href="/wiki/Midtown_Manhattan" title="Midtown Manhattan">Midtown Manhattan</a>, especially that between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/49th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="49th Street (Manhattan)">49th Street</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/60th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="60th Street (Manhattan)">60th Street</a>, is lined with prestigious shops and is consistently ranked among the most expensive shopping streets in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-huffpost_20100921_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-huffpost_20100921-3">[3]</a></sup> The "most expensive street in the world" moniker changes depending on currency fluctuations and local economic conditions from year to year. For several years starting in the mid-1990s, the shopping district between 49th and <a href="/wiki/57th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="57th Street (Manhattan)">57th Streets</a> was ranked as having the world's most expensive retail spaces on a cost per square foot basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_19970429_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes_19970429-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2008, <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i> magazine ranked Fifth Avenue as being the most expensive street in the world. Some of the most coveted real estate on Fifth Avenue are the penthouses perched atop the buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Planning_Association" title="American Planning Association">American Planning Association</a> (APA) compiled a list of "2012 Great Places in America" and declared Fifth Avenue to be one of the greatest streets to visit in America. This historic street has many world-renowned museums, businesses and stores, parks, luxury apartments, and historical landmarks that are reminiscent of its history and vision for the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traffic_pattern">Traffic pattern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fifth_Avenue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Traffic pattern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fifth Avenue from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/142nd_Street_(Manhattan)" title="142nd Street (Manhattan)">142nd Street</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/135th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="135th Street (Manhattan)">135th Street</a> carries <a href="/wiki/Bidirectional_traffic" title="Bidirectional traffic">two-way traffic</a>. Fifth Avenue carries <a href="/wiki/One-way_traffic" title="One-way traffic">one-way traffic</a> southbound from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/135th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="135th Street (Manhattan)">135th Street</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waverly_Place_(Manhattan)" title="Waverly Place (Manhattan)">Washington Square North</a>. The changeover to one-way traffic south of 135th Street took place on January 14, 1966, at which time <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madison_Avenue_(Manhattan)" title="Madison Avenue (Manhattan)">Madison Avenue</a> was changed to one way uptown (northbound).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> From 124th Street to 120th Street, Fifth Avenue is cut off by <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Garvey_Park" title="Marcus Garvey Park">Marcus Garvey Park</a>, with southbound traffic diverted around the park via Mount Morris Park West.</p>
<p>Fifth Avenue is one of the few major streets in Manhattan along which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Streetcar" title="Streetcar">streetcars</a> did not operate. Instead, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fifth_Avenue_Coach_Company_and_Surface_Transit" title="Fifth Avenue Coach Company and Surface Transit">Fifth Avenue Coach</a> offered a service more to the taste of fashionable gentlefolk, at twice the fare. On May 23, 2008, <i>The New York Times</i> reported that the New York City area <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Transportation_Authority" title="Metropolitan Transportation Authority">Metropolitan Transportation Authority's</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_City_Transit_buses" title="New York City Transit buses">bus division</a> is considering the use of <a href="/wiki/Double-decker_bus" title="Double-decker bus">double-decker buses</a> on Fifth Avenue once again, where they were operated by the Fifth Avenue Coach Company until 1953,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and again by MTA from 1976 to 1978.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Parade_route">Parade route</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fifth_Avenue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Parade route">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Fifth Avenue is the traditional route for many celebratory <a href="/wiki/Parade" title="Parade">parades</a> in New York City; thus, it is closed to traffic on numerous Sundays in warm weather. The longest running parade is the annual St. Patrick's Day Parade. Parades held are distinct from the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ticker-tape_parade" title="Ticker-tape parade">ticker-tape parades</a></i> held on the <i>"<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canyon_of_Heroes" title="Canyon of Heroes">Canyon of Heroes</a>"</i> on lower <a href="/wiki/Broadway_(Manhattan)" title="Broadway (Manhattan)">Broadway</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Macy%27s_Thanksgiving_Day_Parade" title="Macy's Thanksgiving Day Parade">Macy's Thanksgiving Day Parade</a> held on Broadway from the <a href="/wiki/Upper_West_Side" title="Upper West Side">Upper West Side</a> downtown to <a href="/wiki/Herald_Square" title="Herald Square">Herald Square</a>. Fifth Avenue parades usually proceed from south to north, with the exception of the <a href="/wiki/LGBT_Pride_March_(New_York_City)" title="LGBT Pride March (New York City)">LGBT Pride March</a>, which goes north to south to end in <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Village" title="Greenwich Village">Greenwich Village</a>. The Latino literary classic by New Yorker <a href="/wiki/Giannina_Braschi" title="Giannina Braschi">Giannina Braschi</a>, entitled "Empire of Dreams," takes place on the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_Day_Parade" title="Puerto Rican Day Parade">Puerto Rican Day Parade</a> on Fifth Avenue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bicycling_route">Bicycling route</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fifth_Avenue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Bicycling route">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cycling_in_New_York_City" title="Cycling in New York City">Bicycling on Fifth Avenue</a> ranges from segregated with a <a href="/wiki/Bike_lane" title="Bike lane">bike lane</a> south of <a href="/wiki/23rd_Street_(Manhattan)" title="23rd Street (Manhattan)">23rd Street</a>, to scenic along <a href="/wiki/Central_Park" title="Central Park">Central Park</a>, to dangerous through Midtown with very heavy traffic during rush hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> There is no dedicated bike lane along Fifth Avenue.</p>
<p>In July 1987, then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_City_Mayor" title="New York City Mayor">New York City Mayor</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_Koch" title="Edward Koch">Edward Koch</a> proposed banning bicycling on Fifth, Park, and Madison Avenues during weekdays, but many bicyclists protested and had the ban overturned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> When the trial was started on Monday, August 24, 1987 for 90 days to ban bicyclists from these three avenues from 31st Street to 59th Street between 10 a.m. and 4 p.m. on weekdays, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mopeds" title="Mopeds">mopeds</a> would not be banned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> On Monday, August 31, 1987, a state appeals court judge halted the ban for at least a week pending a ruling after opponents against the ban brought a lawsuit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nicknames">Nicknames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fifth_Avenue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nicknames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Upper_Fifth_Avenue.2FMillionaire.27s_Row">Upper Fifth Avenue/Millionaire's Row</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fifth_Avenue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Upper Fifth Avenue/Millionaire's Row">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the late 19th century, the very rich of New York began building mansions along the stretch of Fifth Avenue between <a href="/wiki/59th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="59th Street (Manhattan)">59th Street</a> and <a href="/wiki/96th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="96th Street (Manhattan)">96th Street</a>, looking onto <a href="/wiki/Central_Park" title="Central Park">Central Park</a>. By the early 20th century, this portion of Fifth Avenue had been nicknamed "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millionaire%27s_Mile" title="Millionaire's Mile">Millionaire's Row</a>", with mansions such as the <a href="/wiki/Mrs._William_B._Astor_House" title="Mrs. William B. Astor House">Mrs. William B. Astor House</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_A._Clark_House" title="William A. Clark House">William A. Clark House</a>, <a href="/wiki/Felix_M._Warburg_House" title="Felix M. Warburg House">Felix M. Warburg House</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Morton_F._Plant_House" title="Morton F. Plant House">Morton F. Plant Houses</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_B._Duke_House" title="James B. Duke House">James B. Duke House</a> and numerous others (see Category:Fifth Avenue, below). Entries to Central Park along this stretch include Inventor's Gate at 72nd Street, which gave access to the park's carriage drives, and Engineers' Gate at 90th Street, used by equestrians.</p>
<p>A milestone change for Fifth Avenue came in 1916, when the grand corner mansion at 72nd Street and Fifth Avenue that <a href="/wiki/James_A._Burden_II" title="James A. Burden II">James A. Burden II</a> had erected in 1893 became the first private mansion on Fifth Avenue above 59th Street to be demolished to make way for a grand apartment house. The building at <a href="/wiki/907_Fifth_Avenue" title="907 Fifth Avenue">907 Fifth Avenue</a> began a trend, with its 12 stories around a central court, with two apartments to a floor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Its strong cornice above the fourth floor, just at the eaves height of its neighbors, was intended to soften its presence.</p>
<p>In January 1922, the city reacted to complaints about the ongoing replacement of Fifth Avenue's mansions by apartment buildings by restricting the height of future structures to 75 feet (23 m), about half the height of a ten-story apartment building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-19">[19]</a></sup> Architect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._E._R._Carpenter" title="J. E. R. Carpenter">J. E. R. Carpenter</a> brought suit, and won a verdict overturning the height restriction in 1923. Carpenter argued that "the avenue would be greatly improved in appearance when deluxe apartments would replace the old-style mansions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-19">[19]</a></sup> Led by real estate investors <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benjamin_Winter,_Sr." title="Benjamin Winter, Sr.">Benjamin Winter, Sr.</a> and Frederick Brown, the old mansions were quickly torn down and replaced with apartment buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>This area contains many notable apartment buildings, including <a href="/wiki/810_Fifth_Avenue" title="810 Fifth Avenue">810 Fifth Avenue</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Park_Cinq" title="Park Cinq">Park Cinq</a>, many of them built in the 1920s by architects such as <a href="/wiki/Rosario_Candela" title="Rosario Candela">Rosario Candela</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._E._R._Carpenter" title="J. E. R. Carpenter">J. E. R. Carpenter</a>. A very few post-<a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> structures break the unified limestone frontage, notably the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_R._Guggenheim_Museum" title="Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum">Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum</a> between 88th and 89th Streets</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Museum_Mile">Museum Mile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fifth_Avenue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Museum Mile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Museum Mile is the name for a section of Fifth Avenue running from 82nd to 105th streets on the <a href="/wiki/Upper_East_Side" title="Upper East Side">Upper East Side</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> in an area sometimes called Upper <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Hill" title="Carnegie Hill">Carnegie Hill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The Mile, which contains one of the densest displays of culture in the world, is actually three blocks longer than one mile (1.6 km). Nine museums occupy the length of this section of Fifth Avenue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mmilemus_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-mmilemus-24">[24]</a></sup> A ninth museum, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Museum_for_African_Art" title="Museum for African Art">Museum for African Art</a>, joined the ensemble in 2009; its Museum at <a href="/wiki/110th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="110th Street (Manhattan)">110th Street</a>, the first new museum constructed on the Mile since the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_R._Guggenheim_Museum" title="Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum">Guggenheim</a> in 1959,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mafr_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-mafr-25">[25]</a></sup> opened in late 2012.</p>
<p>In addition to other programming, the museums collaborate for the annual Museum Mile Festival to promote the museums and increase visitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drv_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-drv-26">[26]</a></sup> The Museum Mile Festival traditionally takes place here on the second Tuesday in June from 6 – 9 p.m. It was established in 1979 to increase public awareness of its member institutions and promote public support of the arts in New York City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The first festival was held on June 26, 1979<span style="display:none"> (<span class="bday dtstart published updated">1979-06-26</span>)</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The nine museums are open free that evening to the public. Several of the participating museums offer outdoor art activities for children, live music and street performers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> During the event, Fifth Avenue is closed to traffic.</p>
<p>Museums on the mile include:</p>
<ul>
<li>110th Street – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Museum_for_African_Art" title="Museum for African Art">Museum for African Art</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afart_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-afart-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>105th Street – <a href="/wiki/El_Museo_del_Barrio" title="El Museo del Barrio">El Museo del Barrio</a></li>
<li>103rd Street – <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_the_City_of_New_York" title="Museum of the City of New York">Museum of the City of New York</a></li>
<li>92nd Street – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_Museum_(New_York)" title="Jewish Museum (New York)">The Jewish Museum</a></li>
<li>91st Street – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cooper-Hewitt,_National_Design_Museum" title="Cooper-Hewitt, National Design Museum">Cooper-Hewitt, National Design Museum</a> (part of the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">Smithsonian Institution</a>)</li>
<li>89th Street – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Design" title="National Academy of Design">National Academy Museum</a> and School of Fine Arts</li>
<li>88th Street – <a href="/wiki/Solomon_R._Guggenheim_Museum" title="Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum">Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum</a></li>
<li>86th Street – <a href="/wiki/Neue_Galerie_New_York" title="Neue Galerie New York">Neue Galerie New York</a></li>
<li>82nd Street – <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">The Metropolitan Museum of Art</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Additionally, on the corner of Fifth Avenue and 70th Street lies the <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clay_Frick_House" title="Henry Clay Frick House">Henry Clay Frick House</a> which houses the <a href="/wiki/Frick_Collection" title="Frick Collection">Frick Collection</a>, though this is not part of Museum Mile.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fifth_Avenue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/49th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="49th Street (Manhattan)">49th Street</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/60th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="60th Street (Manhattan)">60th Street</a>, is lined with prestigious boutiques and flagship stores and is consistently ranked among the most expensive shopping streets in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Luxury_goods" title="Luxury goods">luxury goods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fashion" title="Fashion">fashion</a>, toys, and sport <a href="/wiki/Brand" title="Brand">brand</a> boutiques are located on Fifth Avenue, including <a href="/wiki/Louis_Vuitton" title="Louis Vuitton">Louis Vuitton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tiffany_%26_Co." title="Tiffany &amp; Co.">Tiffany &amp; Co.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gucci" title="Gucci">Gucci</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prada" title="Prada">Prada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bottega_Veneta" title="Bottega Veneta">Bottega Veneta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armani" title="Armani">Armani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fendi" title="Fendi">Fendi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Versace" title="Versace">Versace</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tommy_Hilfiger_Corporation" title="Tommy Hilfiger Corporation">Tommy Hilfiger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cartier_(jeweler)" title="Cartier (jeweler)">Cartier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Omega_SA" title="Omega SA">Omega</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Lauren_Corporation" title="Ralph Lauren Corporation">Ralph Lauren</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chanel" title="Chanel">Chanel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harry_Winston,_Inc." title="Harry Winston, Inc.">Harry Winston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salvatore_Ferragamo_S.p.A." title="Salvatore Ferragamo S.p.A.">Salvatore Ferragamo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nike,_Inc." title="Nike, Inc.">Nike</a>, <a href="/wiki/Escada" title="Escada">Escada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swarovski" title="Swarovski">Swarovski</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgari" title="Bulgari">Bvlgari</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emilio_Pucci" title="Emilio Pucci">Emilio Pucci</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ermenegildo_Zegna" title="Ermenegildo Zegna">Ermenegildo Zegna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diesel_(brand)" title="Diesel (brand)">Diesel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lacoste" title="Lacoste">Lacoste</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brooks_Brothers" title="Brooks Brothers">Brooks Brothers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abercrombie_%26_Fitch" title="Abercrombie &amp; Fitch">Abercrombie &amp; Fitch</a>, <a href="/wiki/De_Beers" title="De Beers">De Beers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emanuel_Ungaro" title="Emanuel Ungaro">Emanuel Ungaro</a>, <a href="/wiki/FAO_Schwarz" title="FAO Schwarz">FAO Schwarz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gap_Inc." title="Gap Inc.">Gap</a> , Lindt Chocolate Shop, <a href="/wiki/Henri_Bendel" title="Henri Bendel">Henri Bendel</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_Association" title="National Basketball Association">NBA</a> Store, <a href="/wiki/Oxxford_Clothes" title="Oxxford Clothes">Oxxford Clothes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sephora" title="Sephora">Sephora</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Colors_of_Benetton" title="United Colors of Benetton">United Colors of Benetton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zara_(retailer)" title="Zara (retailer)">Zara</a>, <a href="/wiki/H%26M" title="H&amp;M">H&amp;M</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BCBG_Max_Azria" title="BCBG Max Azria">BCBG Max Azria</a> etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Luxury department stores include <a href="/wiki/Lord_%26_Taylor" title="Lord &amp; Taylor">Lord &amp; Taylor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saks_Fifth_Avenue" title="Saks Fifth Avenue">Saks Fifth Avenue</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bergdorf_Goodman" title="Bergdorf Goodman">Bergdorf Goodman</a>. Fifth Avenue is home to New York's fifth most photographed building, the <a href="/wiki/Apple_Store" title="Apple Store">Apple Store</a>, and the largest <a href="/wiki/Build-A-Bear_Workshop" title="Build-A-Bear Workshop">Build-A-Bear Workshop</a> in the world.</p>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Airline" title="Airline">airlines</a> at one time had ticketing offices along Fifth Avenue. In the years leading up to 1992, the number of ticketing offices along Fifth Avenue decreased. <a href="/wiki/Pan_American_World_Airways" title="Pan American World Airways">Pan American World Airways</a> went out of business, while <a href="/wiki/Air_France" title="Air France">Air France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finnair" title="Finnair">Finnair</a>, and <a href="/wiki/KLM" title="KLM">KLM</a> moved their ticket offices to other areas in <a href="/wiki/Midtown_Manhattan" title="Midtown Manhattan">Midtown Manhattan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
