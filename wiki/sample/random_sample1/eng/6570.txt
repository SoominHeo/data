<p>The <b>Boston Terrier</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Dog_breed" title="Dog breed">breed</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dog</a> originating in the United States. This "American Gentleman" was accepted in 1893 by the <a href="/wiki/American_Kennel_Club" title="American Kennel Club">American Kennel Club</a> as a non-sporting breed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meade_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meade-2">[2]</a></sup> Color and markings are important when distinguishing this breed to the AKC standard. They should be either black, brindle or seal with white markings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AKC_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-AKC-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CKC_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-CKC-4">[4]</a></sup> Bostons are small and compact with a short tail and erect ears. The AKC says they are highly intelligent and very easily trained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> They are friendly and can be stubborn at times. The average life span of a Boston is around 11 to 13 years, though some can live well into their teens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The American Kennel Club ranked the Boston Terrier as the 23rd most popular pure-breed in the United States in 2012 and 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Boston_Terrier_male.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1134" data-file-width="850" height="294" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Boston_Terrier_male.jpg/220px-Boston_Terrier_male.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Boston_Terrier_male.jpg/330px-Boston_Terrier_male.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Boston_Terrier_male.jpg/440px-Boston_Terrier_male.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Boston_Terrier_male.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Young Boston Terrier</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Boston terrier breed originated around 1870, when <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_C._Hooper&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Robert C. Hooper (page does not exist)">Robert C. Hooper</a> of Boston, purchased from <a href="/wiki/Edward_Burnett" title="Edward Burnett">Edward Burnett</a> a dog named Judge (known later as Hooper's Judge), which was of a Bull and Terrier type lineage. Hooper's Judge is either directly related to the original Bull and Terrier breeds of the 19th and early 20th centuries, or Judge is the result of modern English Bulldogs being crossed into terriers created in the 1860s for show purposes, like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_English_Terrier" title="White English Terrier">White English Terrier</a>. The <a href="/wiki/American_Kennel_Club" title="American Kennel Club">American Kennel Club</a> cites Hooper's Judge as the ancestor of almost all true modern Boston Terriers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Get_to_Know_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Get_to_Know-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Judge weighed over 27.5 pounds (12.5 kg). The offspring interbred with one or more <a href="/wiki/French_Bulldog" title="French Bulldog">French Bulldogs</a>, providing the foundation for the Boston Terrier. Bred down in size from <a href="/wiki/List_of_dog_fighting_breeds" title="List of dog fighting breeds">fighting dogs</a> of the Bull and Terrier types, the Boston Terrier originally weighed up to 44 pounds (20 kg) (Olde Boston Bulldogge).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meade_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Meade-2">[2]</a></sup> The breed was first shown in Boston in 1870. By 1889 the breed had become sufficiently popular in Boston that fanciers formed the American Bull Terrier Club, the breed's nickname, "roundheads". Shortly after, at the suggestion of James Watson (a noted writer and authority), the club changed its name to the Boston Terrier Club and in 1893 it was admitted to membership in the American Kennel Club, thus making it the first US breed to be recognized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Get_to_Know_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Get_to_Know-8">[8]</a></sup> It is one of a small number of breeds to have originated in the United States. The Boston Terrier was the first non-sporting dog bred in the US.</p>
<p>In the early years, the color and markings were not very important. By the 20th century the breed's distinctive markings and color were written into the standard, becoming an essential feature. The Boston Terrier has lost most of its aggressive nature, preferring the company of humans, although some males will still challenge other dogs if they feel their territory is being invaded. <a href="/wiki/Boston_University" title="Boston University">Boston University</a>'s mascot is <a href="/wiki/Rhett_the_Boston_Terrier" title="Rhett the Boston Terrier">Rhett the Boston Terrier</a>. The Boston Terrier is also the mascot of <a href="/wiki/Wofford_College" title="Wofford College">Wofford College</a> in Spartanburg, S.C.</p>

<p>The Boston Terrier is a compactly-built, well-proportioned dog. It has a square-looking head with erect ears and a slightly arched neck. The <a href="/wiki/Snout" title="Snout">muzzle</a> is short and generally <a href="/wiki/Wrinkle" title="Wrinkle">wrinkle</a>-free, with an even or a slightly undershot bite. The chest is broad and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tail_(anatomy)" title="Tail (anatomy)">tail</a> is short.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CKC_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-CKC-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> According to international breed standards, the dog should weigh no more than 25 pounds (11 kg). Boston Terriers usually stand up to 15-17 inches at the <a href="/wiki/Withers" title="Withers">withers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AKC_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-AKC-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The American Kennel Club divides the breed into three classes: under 15 pounds, 15 pounds and under 20 pounds, 20 pounds and not exceeding 25 pounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Get_to_Know_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Get_to_Know-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coat_and_color">Coat and color</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Coat and color">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Boston Terrier is characteristically marked with white in proportion to either black, <a href="/wiki/Brindle" title="Brindle">brindle</a>, seal (color of a wet seal, a very dark brown that looks black except in the bright sun), or a combination of the three.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Get_to_Know_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Get_to_Know-8">[8]</a></sup> Any other color is not accepted as a Boston Terrier by the American Kennel Club, as they are usually obtained by crossbreeding with other breeds and the dog loses its characteristic "tuxedo" appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AKC_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-AKC-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CKC_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-CKC-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the American Kennel Club, an ideal Boston Terrier should have white that covers its chest, muzzle, band around the neck, half way up the <a href="/wiki/Forelimb" title="Forelimb">forelegs</a>, up to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hock_(zoology)" title="Hock (zoology)">hocks</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hind_limb" title="Hind limb">rear legs</a>, and a white blaze between (but not touching) the eyes. For <a href="/wiki/Conformation_show" title="Conformation show">conformation showing</a>, symmetrical markings are preferred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AKC_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-AKC-3">[3]</a></sup> Due to the Boston Terrier's markings resembling <a href="/wiki/Formal_wear" title="Formal wear">formal wear</a>, in addition to its refined and pleasant personality, the breed is commonly referred to as the "American Gentleman."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meade_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Meade-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Get_to_Know_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Get_to_Know-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperament">Temperament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Temperament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Boston Terrier is a gentle breed that typically has a strong, happy-go-lucky, and friendly personality with a merry sense of humour. Bostons are generally eager to please their owner and can be easily trained. They can be very protective of their owners, which may result in aggressive and territorial behavior toward other pets and strangers. The breed requires only a minimum amount of grooming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Get_to_Know_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-Get_to_Know-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>While originally bred for fighting as well as hunting rats in garment factories, they were later down bred for companionship. They are not considered terriers by the American Kennel Club, however, but are part of the non-sporting group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Get_to_Know_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-Get_to_Know-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both females and males are generally quiet and bark only when necessary,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cline_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cline-10">[10]</a></sup> though early training in this regard is essential.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Their usually sensible attitude towards barking makes them excellent choices for apartment dwellers. They enjoy being around people, get along well with children, the elderly, other canines, and non-canine pets, if properly socialized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meade_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Meade-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>Curvature of the back, called roaching, might be caused by <a href="/wiki/Patella" title="Patella">patella</a> problems with the rear legs, which in turn causes the dog to lean forward onto the forelegs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meade_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Meade-2">[2]</a></sup> This might also just be a structural fault with little consequence to the dog. Many Bostons cannot tolerate excessive heat and also extremely cold weather, due to the shortened muzzle, so hot or cold weather combined with demanding exercise can bring harm to a Boston Terrier. A sensitive digestive system is also typical of Boston Terriers. In the absence of good quality, proper diet, flatulence is associated with the breed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Their large and prominent eyes make Boston Terriers prone to corneal ulcers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bostons are <a href="/wiki/Cephalic_index#Brachycephalic_animals" title="Cephalic index">brachycephalic</a> breeds. The word comes from Greek roots "Brachy," meaning short and "cephalic," meaning head. This anatomy can cause tiny nostrils, long palates and a narrow trachea. Bostons may be prone to snoring and <a href="/wiki/Reverse_sneezing" title="Reverse sneezing">reverse sneeze</a>—a rapid and repeated forced inhalation through the nose—accompanied by snorting or gagging sounds used to clear the palate of mucus, which does not harm the dog in any way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Brachycephalic dogs may be prone to complications with general anesthesia. Bostons frequently require <a href="/wiki/Caesarean_section" title="Caesarean section">caesarean section</a> to give birth, with over 90% of litters in a UK Kennel Club survey delivered this way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph-CSection_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-CSection-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the past, Bostons were primarily pit-fighting dogs, but with their friendly and happy temperament they became a companion and rat hunter.</p>
<p>In modern days, aside from being an excellent companion, the Boston Terrier also excels in all sorts of canine sports. The breed is increasingly popular in <a href="/wiki/Dog_agility" title="Dog agility">dog agility</a> competitions, <a href="/wiki/Obedience_training" title="Obedience training">obedience training</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rally_obedience" title="Rally obedience">rally obedience</a>, tracking, <a href="/wiki/Dock_jumping" title="Dock jumping">dock diving</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flyball" title="Flyball">flyball</a>, weight-pulling, barn hunt and <a href="/wiki/Lure_coursing" title="Lure coursing">lure coursing</a>. Being such a versatile breed and with their outgoing personality and eagerness to meet new acquaintances, the Boston Terrier is a popular <a href="/wiki/Therapy_dog" title="Therapy dog">therapy dog</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
