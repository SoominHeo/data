<p><b>Boann</b> or <b>Boand</b> (modern spelling: <b>Bóinn</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Irish_mythology" title="Irish mythology">Irish</a> goddess of the <a href="/wiki/River_Boyne" title="River Boyne">River Boyne</a>, a river in <a href="/wiki/Leinster" title="Leinster">Leinster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>. According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Lebor_Gab%C3%A1la_%C3%89renn" title="Lebor Gabála Érenn">Lebor Gabála Érenn</a></i> she was the daughter of Delbáeth, son of <a href="/wiki/Elatha" title="Elatha">Elada</a>, of the <a href="/wiki/Tuatha_D%C3%A9_Danann" title="Tuatha Dé Danann">Tuatha Dé Danann</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Her husband is variously <a href="/wiki/Nechtan_(mythology)" title="Nechtan (mythology)">Nechtan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elcmar" title="Elcmar">Elcmar</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nuada_Airgetl%C3%A1m" title="Nuada Airgetlám">Nuada Airgetlám</a>. Her lover is her uncle, <a href="/wiki/The_Dagda" title="The Dagda">the Dagda</a>, by whom she had her son, <a href="/wiki/Aengus" title="Aengus">Aengus</a>. In order to hide their affair, the Dagda made the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a> stand still for nine months; therefore, Aengus was conceived, gestated and born in one day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>As told in the <a href="/wiki/Dindsenchas" title="Dindsenchas">Dindsenchas</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Boann created the Boyne. Though forbidden to by her husband, Nechtan, Boann approached the magical <a href="/wiki/Connla%27s_Well" title="Connla's Well">Well of Segais</a> (also known as the Connla's Well), which was surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Hazel" title="Hazel">hazels</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hazelnut" title="Hazelnut">Hazelnuts</a> were known to fall into the Well, where they were eaten by the speckled <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> (who, along with hazelnuts, also embody and represent <a href="/wiki/Wisdom" title="Wisdom">wisdom</a> in <a href="/wiki/Irish_mythology" title="Irish mythology">Irish mythology</a>). Boann challenged the power of the well by walking around it <a href="/wiki/Widdershins" title="Widdershins">widdershins</a>; this caused the waters to surge up violently and rush down to the sea, creating the Boyne. In this catastrophe, she was swept along in the rushing waters, and lost an arm, leg and eye, and ultimately her life, in the flood. The poem equates her with famous rivers in other countries, including the <a href="/wiki/River_Severn" title="River Severn">River Severn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tiber" title="Tiber">Tiber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jordan_River" title="Jordan River">Jordan River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tigris" title="Tigris">Tigris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Euphrates" title="Euphrates">Euphrates</a>.</p>
<p>She also appears in <i>Táin Bó Fraích</i> as the maternal aunt and protector of the mortal <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A1ech" title="Fráech">Fráech</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Her name is interpreted as "white cow" (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>bó fhionn</i></span>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Irish_language" title="Old Irish language">Old Irish</a>: <span lang="sga" xml:lang="sga"><i>bó find</i></span>) in the <i>dinsenchas</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>'s 2nd century <i><a href="/wiki/Geography_(Ptolemy)" title="Geography (Ptolemy)">Geography</a></i> shows that in antiquity the river's name was <i>Bubindas</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> which may derive from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Celtic_language" title="Proto-Celtic language">Proto-Celtic</a> <i>*Bou-vindā</i>, "white cow".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern-day commentators and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_paganism" title="Modern paganism">modern paganism</a> sometimes identify Boann with the goddess <a href="/wiki/Brigid" title="Brigid">Brigid</a> or believe Boann to be Brigid's mother;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> however there are no <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> sources that describe her as such. It is also speculated by some modern writers that, as the more well-known goddess, and later saint, the legends of numerous "minor" goddesses with similar associations may have over time been incorporated into the symbology, worship and tales of Brigid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Condren_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Condren-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
