<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Bohr model</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

</div>
</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Atomic_physics" title="Atomic physics">atomic physics</a>, the <b>Rutherford–Bohr model</b> or <b>Bohr model</b> or <b>Bohr diagram</b>, introduced by <a href="/wiki/Niels_Bohr" title="Niels Bohr">Niels Bohr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford" title="Ernest Rutherford">Ernest Rutherford</a> in 1913, depicts the <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a> as a small, positively charged <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nucleus</a> surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> that travel in circular orbits around the nucleus—similar in structure to the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, but with attraction provided by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrostatic_force" title="Electrostatic force">electrostatic forces</a> rather than <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a>. After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cubic_atom" title="Cubic atom">cubic model</a> (1902), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plum-pudding_model" title="Plum-pudding model">plum-pudding model</a> (1904), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturnian_model" title="Saturnian model">Saturnian model</a> (1904), and the <a href="/wiki/Rutherford_model" title="Rutherford model">Rutherford model</a> (1911) came the <i>Rutherford–Bohr model</i> or just <i>Bohr model</i> for short (1913). The improvement to the Rutherford model is mostly a quantum physical interpretation of it. The model's key success lay in explaining the <a href="/wiki/Rydberg_formula" title="Rydberg formula">Rydberg formula</a> for the spectral <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_spectral_series" title="Hydrogen spectral series">emission lines</a> of atomic <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>. While the Rydberg formula had been known experimentally, it did not gain a theoretical underpinning until the Bohr model was introduced. Not only did the Bohr model explain the reason for the structure of the Rydberg formula, it also provided a justification for its empirical results in terms of fundamental physical constants.</p>
<p>The Bohr model is a relatively primitive model of the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_atom" title="Hydrogen atom">hydrogen atom</a>, compared to the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_orbital" title="Atomic orbital">valence shell atom</a>. As a theory, it can be derived as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orders_of_approximation#First-order" title="Orders of approximation">first-order approximation</a> of the hydrogen atom using the broader and much more accurate <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> and thus may be considered to be an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obsolete_scientific_theory" title="Obsolete scientific theory">obsolete scientific theory</a>. However, because of its simplicity, and its correct results for selected systems (see below for application), the Bohr model is still commonly taught to introduce students to <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Energy_level#Energy_level_diagrams" title="Energy level">energy level diagrams</a> before moving on to the more accurate, but more complex, valence shell atom. A related model was originally proposed by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Erich_Haas" title="Arthur Erich Haas">Arthur Erich Haas</a> in 1910, but was rejected. The quantum theory of the period between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planck#Black-body_radiation" title="Planck">Planck's discovery of the quantum</a> (1900) and the advent of a full-blown <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> (1925) is often referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Old_quantum_theory" title="Old quantum theory">old quantum theory</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohr_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atome_bohr_couches_electroniques_KLM.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="124" data-file-width="128" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Atome_bohr_couches_electroniques_KLM.svg/220px-Atome_bohr_couches_electroniques_KLM.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Atome_bohr_couches_electroniques_KLM.svg/330px-Atome_bohr_couches_electroniques_KLM.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Atome_bohr_couches_electroniques_KLM.svg/440px-Atome_bohr_couches_electroniques_KLM.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In the early 20th century, experiments by <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford" title="Ernest Rutherford">Ernest Rutherford</a> established that <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> consisted of a diffuse cloud of negatively charged <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> surrounding a small, dense, positively charged <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nucleus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bohr1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-bohr1-2">[2]</a></sup> Given this experimental data, Rutherford naturally considered a planetary-model atom, the <a href="/wiki/Rutherford_model" title="Rutherford model">Rutherford model</a> of 1911 – electrons orbiting a solar nucleus – however, said planetary-model atom has a technical difficulty. The laws of classical mechanics (i.e. the <a href="/wiki/Larmor_formula" title="Larmor formula">Larmor formula</a>), predict that the electron will release <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> while orbiting a nucleus. Because the electron would lose energy, it would rapidly spiral inwards, collapsing into the nucleus on a timescale of around 16 picoseconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This atom model is disastrous, because it predicts that all atoms are unstable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also, as the electron spirals inward, the emission would rapidly increase in frequency as the orbit got smaller and faster. This would produce a continuous smear, in frequency, of electromagnetic radiation. However, late 19th century experiments with <a href="/wiki/Electric_discharge" title="Electric discharge">electric discharges</a> have shown that atoms will only emit light (that is, electromagnetic radiation) at certain discrete frequencies.</p>
<p>To overcome this difficulty, <a href="/wiki/Niels_Bohr" title="Niels Bohr">Niels Bohr</a> proposed, in 1913, what is now called the <i>Bohr model of the atom</i>. He suggested that electrons could only have certain <i>classical</i> motions:</p>
<ol>
<li>Electrons in atoms orbit the nucleus.</li>
<li>The electrons can only orbit stably, without radiating, in certain orbits (called by Bohr the "stationary orbits"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>) at a certain discrete set of distances from the nucleus. These orbits are associated with definite energies and are also called energy shells or <a href="/wiki/Energy_level" title="Energy level">energy levels</a>. In these orbits, the electron's acceleration does not result in radiation and energy loss as required by classical electromagnetics. The Bohr model of an atom was based upon Planck's quantum theory of radiation.</li>
<li>Electrons can only gain and lose energy by jumping from one allowed orbit to another, absorbing or emitting electromagnetic radiation with a frequency <i>ν</i> determined by the energy difference of the levels according to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planck_relation" title="Planck relation">Planck relation</a>:

<p>where <i>n</i> = 1, 2, 3, ... is called the <a href="/wiki/Principal_quantum_number" title="Principal quantum number">principal quantum number</a>, and <span class="texhtml"><i>ħ</i> = <i>h</i>/2π</span>. The lowest value of <i>n</i> is 1; this gives a smallest possible orbital radius of 0.0529 nm known as the <a href="/wiki/Bohr_radius" title="Bohr radius">Bohr radius</a>. Once an electron is in this lowest orbit, it can get no closer to the proton. Starting from the angular momentum quantum rule, Bohr<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bohr1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-bohr1-2">[2]</a></sup> was able to calculate the <a href="/wiki/Bohr_model#Electron_energy_levels" title="Bohr model">energies of the allowed orbits</a> of the hydrogen atom and other <a href="/wiki/Bohr_model#Shell_model_of_the_atom" title="Bohr model">hydrogen-like</a> atoms and ions.</p>
<p>Other points are:</p>
<ol>
<li>Like Einstein's theory of the <a href="/wiki/Photoelectric_effect" title="Photoelectric effect">Photoelectric effect</a>, Bohr's formula assumes that during a quantum jump a <i>discrete</i> amount of energy is radiated. However, unlike Einstein, Bohr stuck to the <i>classical</i> <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations" title="Maxwell's equations">Maxwell theory</a> of the electromagnetic field. Quantization of the electromagnetic field was explained by the discreteness of the atomic <a href="/wiki/Bohr_model#Electron_energy_levels" title="Bohr model">energy levels</a>; Bohr did not believe in the existence of <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a>.</li>
<li>According to the Maxwell theory the frequency <i>ν</i> of classical radiation is equal to the rotation frequency <i>ν</i><sub>rot</sub> of the electron in its orbit, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harmonics" title="Harmonics">harmonics</a> at integer multiples of this frequency. This result is obtained from the Bohr model for jumps between energy levels <i>E</i><sub><i>n</i></sub> and <i>E</i><sub><i>n</i>−<i>k</i></sub> when <i>k</i> is much smaller than <i>n</i>. These jumps reproduce the frequency of the <i>k</i>-th harmonic of orbit <i>n</i>. For sufficiently large values of <i>n</i> (so-called <a href="/wiki/Rydberg_state" title="Rydberg state">Rydberg states</a>), the two orbits involved in the emission process have nearly the same rotation frequency, so that the classical orbital frequency is not ambiguous. But for small <i>n</i> (or large <i>k</i>), the radiation frequency has no unambiguous classical interpretation. This marks the birth of the <a href="/wiki/Correspondence_principle" title="Correspondence principle">correspondence principle</a>, requiring quantum theory to agree with the classical theory only in the limit of large quantum numbers.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/BKS_theory" title="BKS theory">Bohr-Kramers-Slater theory</a> (BKS theory) is a failed attempt to extend the Bohr model, which violates the <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_energy" title="Conservation of energy">conservation of energy</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_of_linear_momentum" title="Conservation of linear momentum">momentum</a> in quantum jumps, with the conservation laws only holding on average.</li>
</ol>

<p>Substituting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/De_Broglie_wavelength" title="De Broglie wavelength">de Broglie's wavelength</a> of <span class="texhtml"><i>λ</i> = <i>h</i>/<i>p</i></span> reproduces Bohr's rule. In 1913, however, Bohr justified his rule by appealing to the correspondence principle, without providing any sort of wave interpretation. In 1913, the wave behavior of matter particles such as the electron (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Matter_wave" title="Matter wave">matter waves</a>) was not suspected.</p>
<p>In 1925, a new kind of mechanics was proposed, <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>, in which Bohr's model of electrons traveling in quantized orbits was extended into a <a href="/wiki/Matrix_mechanics" title="Matrix mechanics">more accurate model</a> of electron motion. The new theory was proposed by <a href="/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg" title="Werner Heisenberg">Werner Heisenberg</a>. <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation" title="Schrödinger equation">Another form</a> of the same theory, wave mechanics, was discovered by the Austrian physicist <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Schr%C3%B6dinger" title="Erwin Schrödinger">Erwin Schrödinger</a> independently, and by different reasoning. Schrödinger employed de Broglie's matter waves, but sought wave solutions of a three-dimensional wave equation describing electrons that were constrained to move about the nucleus of a <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen-like_atom" title="Hydrogen-like atom">hydrogen-like atom</a>, by being trapped by the potential of the positive nuclear charge.</p>

<p>The Bohr model gives almost exact results only for a system where two charged points orbit each other at speeds much less than that of light. This not only includes one-electron systems such as the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_atom" title="Hydrogen atom">hydrogen atom</a>, singly ionized <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>, and doubly ionized <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a>, but it includes <a href="/wiki/Positronium" title="Positronium">positronium</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rydberg_states" title="Rydberg states">Rydberg states</a> of any atom where one electron is far away from everything else. It can be used for <a href="/wiki/K-line_(spectrometry)" title="K-line (spectrometry)">K-line</a> X-ray transition calculations if other assumptions are added (see <a href="#Moseley.27s_law_and_calculation_of_K-alpha_X-ray_emission_lines">Moseley's law</a> below). In high energy physics, it can be used to calculate the masses of <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">heavy quark</a> <a href="/wiki/Meson" title="Meson">mesons</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rydberg_formula">Rydberg formula</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohr_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rydberg formula">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rydberg_formula" title="Rydberg formula">Rydberg formula</a>, which was known empirically before Bohr's formula, is seen in Bohr's theory as describing the energies of transitions or <a href="/wiki/Atomic_electron_transition" title="Atomic electron transition">quantum jumps</a> between one orbital energy levels. Bohr's formula gives the numerical value of the already-known and measured <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rydberg%27s_constant" title="Rydberg's constant">Rydberg's constant</a>, but in terms of more fundamental constants of nature, including the electron's charge and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planck%27s_constant" title="Planck's constant">Planck's constant</a>.</p>
<p>When the electron gets moved from its original energy level to a higher one, it then jumps back each level until it comes to the original position, which results in a <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> being emitted. Using the derived formula for the different energy levels of hydrogen one may determine the wavelengths of light that a hydrogen atom can emit.</p>

This was established empirically before Bohr presented his model.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shell_model_of_heavier_atoms">Shell model of heavier atoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohr_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Shell model of heavier atoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bohr extended the model of hydrogen to give an approximate model for heavier atoms. This gave a physical picture that reproduced many known atomic properties for the first time.</p>
<p>Heavier atoms have more protons in the nucleus, and more electrons to cancel the charge. Bohr's idea was that each discrete orbit could only hold a certain number of electrons. After that orbit is full, the next level would have to be used. This gives the atom a <a href="/wiki/Electron_configuration" title="Electron configuration">shell structure</a>, in which each shell corresponds to a Bohr orbit.</p>
<p>This model is even more approximate than the model of hydrogen, because it treats the electrons in each shell as non-interacting. But the repulsions of electrons are taken into account somewhat by the phenomenon of <a href="/wiki/Shielding_effect" title="Shielding effect">screening</a>. The electrons in outer orbits do not only orbit the nucleus, but they also move around the inner electrons, so the effective charge Z that they feel is reduced by the number of the electrons in the inner orbit.</p>
<p>For example, the lithium atom has two electrons in the lowest 1s orbit, and these orbit at Z=2. Each one sees the nuclear charge of Z=3 minus the screening effect of the other, which crudely reduces the nuclear charge by 1 unit. This means that the innermost electrons orbit at approximately 1/4 the Bohr radius. The outermost electron in lithium orbits at roughly Z=1, since the two inner electrons reduce the nuclear charge by 2. This outer electron should be at nearly one Bohr radius from the nucleus. Because the electrons strongly repel each other, the effective charge description is very approximate; the effective charge Z doesn't usually come out to be an integer. But <a href="/wiki/Moseley%27s_law" title="Moseley's law">Moseley's law</a> experimentally probes the innermost pair of electrons, and shows that they do see a nuclear charge of approximately Z−1, while the outermost electron in an atom or ion with only one electron in the outermost shell orbits a core with effective charge Z−k where k is the total number of electrons in the inner shells.</p>
<p>The shell model was able to qualitatively explain many of the mysterious properties of atoms which became codified in the late 19th century in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periodic_table_of_the_elements" title="Periodic table of the elements">periodic table of the elements</a>. One property was the size of atoms, which could be determined approximately by measuring the <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> of gases and density of pure crystalline solids. Atoms tend to get smaller toward the right in the periodic table, and become much larger at the next line of the table. Atoms to the right of the table tend to gain electrons, while atoms to the left tend to lose them. Every element on the last column of the table is chemically inert (<a href="/wiki/Noble_gas" title="Noble gas">noble gas</a>).</p>
<p>In the shell model, this phenomenon is explained by shell-filling. Successive atoms become smaller because they are filling orbits of the same size, until the orbit is full, at which point the next atom in the table has a loosely bound outer electron, causing it to expand. The first Bohr orbit is filled when it has two electrons, which explains why helium is inert. The second orbit allows eight electrons, and when it is full the atom is neon, again inert. The third orbital contains eight again, except that in the more correct Sommerfeld treatment (reproduced in modern quantum mechanics) there are extra "d" electrons. The third orbit may hold an extra 10 d electrons, but these positions are not filled until a few more orbitals from the next level are filled (filling the n=3 d orbitals produces the 10 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transition_elements" title="Transition elements">transition elements</a>). The irregular filling pattern is an effect of interactions between electrons, which are not taken into account in either the Bohr or Sommerfeld models and which are difficult to calculate even in the modern treatment.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Moseley.27s_law_and_calculation_of_K-alpha_X-ray_emission_lines">Moseley's law and calculation of K-alpha X-ray emission lines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohr_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Moseley's law and calculation of K-alpha X-ray emission lines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Niels Bohr said in 1962, "You see actually the Rutherford work [the nuclear atom] was not taken seriously. We cannot understand today, but it was not taken seriously at all. There was no mention of it any place. The great change came from Moseley."</p>
<p>In 1913 <a href="/wiki/Henry_Moseley" title="Henry Moseley">Henry Moseley</a> found an empirical relationship between the strongest X-ray line emitted by atoms under electron bombardment (then known as the <a href="/wiki/K-alpha" title="K-alpha">K-alpha</a> line), and their atomic number <span class="texhtml"><i>Z</i></span>. Moseley's empiric formula was found to be derivable from Rydberg and Bohr's formula (Moseley actually mentions only <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford" title="Ernest Rutherford">Ernest Rutherford</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antonius_Van_den_Broek" title="Antonius Van den Broek">Antonius Van den Broek</a> in terms of models). The two additional assumptions that <b>[1]</b> this X-ray line came from a transition between energy levels with quantum numbers 1 and 2, and <b>[2]</b>, that the atomic number <span class="texhtml"><i>Z</i></span> when used in the formula for atoms heavier than hydrogen, should be diminished by 1, to <span class="texhtml">(<i>Z</i>−1)<sup>2</sup></span>.</p>
<p>Moseley wrote to Bohr, puzzled about his results, but Bohr was not able to help. At that time, he thought that the postulated innermost "K" shell of electrons should have at least four electrons, not the two which would have neatly explained the result. So Moseley published his results without a theoretical explanation.</p>
<p>Later, people realized that the effect was caused by charge screening, with an inner shell containing only 2 electrons. In the experiment, one of the innermost electrons in the atom is knocked out, leaving a vacancy in the lowest Bohr orbit, which contains a single remaining electron. This vacancy is then filled by an electron from the next orbit, which has n=2. But the n=2 electrons see an effective charge of Z−1, which is the value appropriate for the charge of the nucleus, when a single electron remains in the lowest Bohr orbit to screen the nuclear charge +Z, and lower it by −1 (due to the electron's negative charge screening the nuclear positive charge). <p>The <a href="/wiki/K-alpha" title="K-alpha">K-alpha</a> line of Moseley's time is now known to be a pair of close lines, written as (<b>Kα<sub>1</sub></b> and <b>Kα<sub>2</sub></b>) in <a href="/wiki/Siegbahn_notation" title="Siegbahn notation">Siegbahn notation</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shortcomings">Shortcomings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohr_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Shortcomings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Bohr model gives an incorrect value <span class="texhtml"><i>L</i>=<i>ħ</i></span> for the ground state orbital angular momentum: The angular momentum in the true ground state is known to be zero from experiment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Although mental pictures fail somewhat at these levels of scale, an electron in the lowest modern "orbital" with no orbital momentum, may be thought of as not to rotate "around" the nucleus at all, but merely to go tightly around it in an ellipse with zero area (this may be pictured as "back and forth", without striking or interacting with the nucleus). This is only reproduced in a more sophisticated semiclassical treatment like Sommerfeld's. Still, even the most sophisticated semiclassical model fails to explain the fact that the lowest energy state is spherically symmetric - it doesn't point in any particular direction.</p>
<p>Nevertheless, in the modern <a href="/wiki/Phase_space_formulation" title="Phase space formulation"><i>fully quantum treatment in phase space</i></a>, the proper deformation (careful full extension) of the semi-classical result adjusts the angular momentum value to the correct effective one. As a consequence, the physical ground state expression is obtained through a shift of the vanishing quantum angular momentum expression, which corresponds to spherical symmetry.</p>
<p>In modern quantum mechanics, the electron in hydrogen is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_cloud" title="Electron cloud">spherical cloud of probability</a> that grows denser near the nucleus. The rate-constant of probability-decay in hydrogen is equal to the inverse of the Bohr radius, but since Bohr worked with circular orbits, not zero area ellipses, the fact that these two numbers exactly agree is considered a "coincidence". (However, many such coincidental agreements are found between the semiclassical vs. full quantum mechanical treatment of the atom; these include identical energy levels in the hydrogen atom and the derivation of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fine_structure_constant" title="Fine structure constant">fine structure constant</a>, which arises from the relativistic Bohr–Sommerfeld model (see below) and which happens to be equal to an entirely different concept, in full modern quantum mechanics).</p>
<p>The Bohr model also has difficulty with, or else fails to explain:</p>
<ul>
<li>Much of the spectra of larger atoms. At best, it can make predictions about the <a href="/wiki/K-alpha" title="K-alpha">K-alpha</a> and some L-alpha X-ray emission spectra for larger atoms, if <i>two</i> additional ad hoc assumptions are made (see <a href="/wiki/Moseley%27s_law" title="Moseley's law">Moseley's law</a> above). Emission spectra for atoms with a single outer-shell electron (atoms in the <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a> group) can also be approximately predicted. Also, if the empiric electron–nuclear screening factors for many atoms are known, many other spectral lines can be deduced from the information, in similar atoms of differing elements, via the Ritz–Rydberg combination principles (see <a href="/wiki/Rydberg_formula" title="Rydberg formula">Rydberg formula</a>). All these techniques essentially make use of Bohr's Newtonian energy-potential picture of the atom.</li>
<li>the relative intensities of spectral lines; although in some simple cases, Bohr's formula or modifications of it, was able to provide reasonable estimates (for example, calculations by Kramers for the <a href="/wiki/Stark_effect" title="Stark effect">Stark effect</a>).</li>
<li>The existence of <a href="/wiki/Fine_structure" title="Fine structure">fine structure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hyperfine_structure" title="Hyperfine structure">hyperfine structure</a> in spectral lines, which are known to be due to a variety of relativistic and subtle effects, as well as complications from electron spin.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Zeeman_effect" title="Zeeman effect">Zeeman effect</a> – changes in spectral lines due to external <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic fields</a>; these are also due to more complicated quantum principles interacting with electron spin and orbital magnetic fields.</li>
<li>The model also violates the <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty_principle" title="Uncertainty principle">uncertainty principle</a> in that it considers electrons to have known orbits and locations, two things which can not be measured simultaneously.</li>
<li>Doublets and Triplets: Appear in the spectra of some atoms: Very close pairs of lines. Bohr’s model cannot say why some energy levels should be very close together.</li>
<li>Multi-electron Atoms: don’t have energy levels predicted by the model. It doesn’t work for (neutral) helium.</li>
<li>A rotating charge, such as the electron classically orbiting around the nucleus, would constantly lose energy in form of electromagnetic radiation (via various mechanisms: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dipole_radiation" title="Dipole radiation">dipole radiation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bremsstrahlung" title="Bremsstrahlung">Bremsstrahlung</a>,...). But such radiation is not observed.</li>
</ul>

<p>Several enhancements to the Bohr model were proposed, most notably the <b><a href="/wiki/Old_quantum_theory" title="Old quantum theory">Sommerfeld model</a></b> or <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bohr%E2%80%93Sommerfeld_theory" title="Bohr–Sommerfeld theory">Bohr–Sommerfeld model</a></b>, which suggested that electrons travel in elliptical orbits around a nucleus instead of the Bohr model's circular orbits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Akhlesh_Lakhtakia_Ed._1996_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Akhlesh_Lakhtakia_Ed._1996-1">[1]</a></sup> This model supplemented the quantized angular momentum condition of the Bohr model with an additional radial quantization condition, the <b><a href="/wiki/Arnold_Sommerfeld" title="Arnold Sommerfeld">Sommerfeld</a>–<a href="/wiki/William_Wilson_(English_academic)" title="William Wilson (English academic)">Wilson</a> quantization condition</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>where <i>p<sub>r</sub></i> is the radial momentum canonically conjugate to the coordinate <i>q</i> which is the radial position and <i>T</i> is one full orbital period. The integral is the <a href="/wiki/Action_(physics)" title="Action (physics)">action</a> of <a href="/wiki/Action-angle_coordinates" title="Action-angle coordinates">action-angle coordinates</a>. This condition, suggested by the <a href="/wiki/Correspondence_principle" title="Correspondence principle">correspondence principle</a>, is the only one possible, since the quantum numbers are <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_invariant" title="Adiabatic invariant">adiabatic invariants</a>.</p>
<p>The Bohr–Sommerfeld model was fundamentally inconsistent and led to many paradoxes. The <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_quantum_number" title="Magnetic quantum number">magnetic quantum number</a> measured the tilt of the orbital plane relative to the <i>xy</i>-plane, and it could only take a few discrete values. This contradicted the obvious fact that an atom could be turned this way and that relative to the coordinates without restriction. The Sommerfeld quantization can be performed in different canonical coordinates and sometimes gives different answers. The incorporation of radiation corrections was difficult, because it required finding action-angle coordinates for a combined radiation/atom system, which is difficult when the radiation is allowed to escape. The whole theory did not extend to non-integrable motions, which meant that many systems could not be treated even in principle. In the end, the model was replaced by the modern <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanical</a> treatment of the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_atom" title="Hydrogen atom">hydrogen atom</a>, which was first given by <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Pauli" title="Wolfgang Pauli">Wolfgang Pauli</a> in 1925, using <a href="/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg" title="Werner Heisenberg">Heisenberg</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Matrix_mechanics" title="Matrix mechanics">matrix mechanics</a>. The current picture of the hydrogen atom is based on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_orbitals" title="Atomic orbitals">atomic orbitals</a> of <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation" title="Schrödinger equation">wave mechanics</a> which <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Schr%C3%B6dinger" title="Erwin Schrödinger">Erwin Schrödinger</a> developed in 1926.</p>
<p>However, this is not to say that the Bohr-Sommerfeld model was without its successes. Calculations based on the Bohr–Sommerfeld model were able to accurately explain a number of more complex atomic spectral effects. For example, up to first-order <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_theory" title="Perturbation theory">perturbations</a>, the Bohr model and quantum mechanics make the same predictions for the spectral line splitting in the <a href="/wiki/Stark_effect" title="Stark effect">Stark effect</a>. At higher-order perturbations, however, the Bohr model and quantum mechanics differ, and measurements of the Stark effect under high field strengths helped confirm the correctness of quantum mechanics over the Bohr model. The prevailing theory behind this difference lies in the shapes of the orbitals of the electrons, which vary according to the energy state of the electron.</p>
<p>The Bohr–Sommerfeld quantization conditions lead to questions in modern mathematics. Consistent semiclassical quantization condition requires a certain type of structure on the phase space, which places topological limitations on the types of symplectic manifolds which can be quantized. In particular, the symplectic form should be the <a href="/wiki/Curvature_form" title="Curvature form">curvature form</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Connection_(mathematics)" title="Connection (mathematics)">connection</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Charles_Hermite" title="Charles Hermite">Hermitian</a> <a href="/wiki/Line_bundle" title="Line bundle">line bundle</a>, which is called a <a href="/wiki/Geometric_quantization" title="Geometric quantization">prequantization</a>.</p>
