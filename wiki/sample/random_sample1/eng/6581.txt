<p>An <b>orphanage</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Residential_education" title="Residential education">residential institution</a> devoted to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Childcare" title="Childcare">care</a> of <a href="/wiki/Orphan" title="Orphan">orphans</a>—children whose <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological" title="Biological">biological</a> parents are deceased or otherwise unable or unwilling to care for them. Biological parents, and sometimes biological grandparents, are legally responsible for supporting children, but in the absence of these, no named <a href="/wiki/Godparent" title="Godparent">godparent</a>, or other relatives willing to care for the children, they become a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ward_of_the_state" title="Ward of the state">ward of the state</a>, and orphanages are one way of providing for their care, housing and education.</p>
<p>It is frequently used to describe institutions abroad, where it is a more accurate term, since the word orphan has a different definition in international adoption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orphanagestats_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-orphanagestats-1">[1]</a></sup> Most children who live in orphanages are not <a href="/wiki/Orphan" title="Orphan">orphans</a>; four out of five children in orphanages having at least one living parent and most having some extended family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Most orphanages have been closed in Europe and North America. There remain a large number of state funded orphanages in the former Soviet Bloc but they are slowly being phased out in favour of direct support to vulnerable families and the development of <a href="/wiki/Foster_care" title="Foster care">foster care</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adoption" title="Adoption">adoption</a> services where this is not possible.</p>
<p>Few large international charities continue to fund orphanages; however, they are still commonly founded by smaller charities and religious groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spectator1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-spectator1-3">[3]</a></sup> Especially in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a>, orphanages may prey on vulnerable families at risk of breakdown and actively recruit children to ensure continued funding. Orphanages in developing countries are rarely run by the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spectator1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-spectator1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other residential institutions for children can be called <i><a href="/wiki/Group_home" title="Group home">group homes</a></i>, <i>children's homes</i>, <i>refuges</i>, <i>rehabilitation centers</i>, <i>night shelters</i>, or <i>youth treatment centers</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jan_de_Bray_001.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2367" data-file-width="2758" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Jan_de_Bray_001.jpg/220px-Jan_de_Bray_001.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Jan_de_Bray_001.jpg/330px-Jan_de_Bray_001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Jan_de_Bray_001.jpg/440px-Jan_de_Bray_001.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jan_de_Bray_001.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Caring for orphans, by Dutch artist <a href="/wiki/Jan_de_Bray" title="Jan de Bray">Jan de Bray</a>, 1663</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Romans formed their first orphanages around 400 AD. <a href="/wiki/Halakha" title="Halakha">Jewish law</a> prescribed care for the widow and the orphan, and <a href="/wiki/History_of_Athens" title="History of Athens">Athenian law</a> supported all orphans of those killed in <a href="/wiki/Military_service" title="Military service">military service</a> until the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_majority" title="Age of majority">age of eighteen</a>. Plato (Laws, 927) says: "Orphans should be placed under the care of public guardians. Men should have a fear of the loneliness of orphans and of the souls of their departed parents. A man should love the unfortunate orphan of whom he is guardian as if he were his own child. He should be as careful and as diligent in the management of the orphan's property as of his own or even more careful still."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The care of orphans was referred to bishops and, during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monasteries</a>. As soon as they were old enough, children were often given as <a href="/wiki/Apprenticeship" title="Apprenticeship">apprentices</a> to households to ensure their support and to learn an occupation.</p>
<p>In medieval Europe, care for orphans tended to reside with the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Church" title="Christian Church">Church</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elizabethan_Poor_Law" title="Elizabethan Poor Law">Elizabethan Poor Laws</a> were enacted at the time of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reformation" title="Reformation">Reformation</a>, and placed public responsibility on individual parishes to care for the indigent poor.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundling_Hospitals">Foundling Hospitals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Foundling Hospitals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Foundling_Hospital.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="396" data-file-width="582" height="204" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Foundling_Hospital.jpg/300px-Foundling_Hospital.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Foundling_Hospital.jpg/450px-Foundling_Hospital.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/75/Foundling_Hospital.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Foundling_Hospital.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Foundling Hospital. The building has been demolished.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The growth of sentimental <a href="/wiki/Philanthropy" title="Philanthropy">philanthropy</a> in the 18th century, led to the establishment of the first charitable institutions catering for the orphan. The <a href="/wiki/Foundling_Hospital" title="Foundling Hospital">Foundling Hospital</a> was founded in 1741 by the <a href="/wiki/Philanthropy" title="Philanthropy">philanthropic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_(nautical)" title="Captain (nautical)">sea captain</a> <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Coram" title="Thomas Coram">Thomas Coram</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, as a children's home for the "education and maintenance of exposed and deserted young children." The first children were admitted into a temporary house located in <a href="/wiki/Hatton_Garden" title="Hatton Garden">Hatton Garden</a>. At first, no questions were asked about child or parent, but a distinguishing token was put on each child by the parent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>On reception, children were sent to <a href="/wiki/Wet_nurse" title="Wet nurse">wet nurses</a> in the countryside, where they stayed until they were about four or five years old. At sixteen, girls were generally <a href="/wiki/Apprenticeship" title="Apprenticeship">apprenticed</a> as <a href="/wiki/Maid" title="Maid">servants</a> for four years; at fourteen, boys were apprenticed into variety of occupations, typically for seven years. There was a small benevolent fund for adults.</p>
<p>In 1756, the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Great_Britain" title="House of Commons of Great Britain">House of Commons</a> resolved that all children offered should be received, that local receiving places should be appointed all over the country, and that the funds should be publicly guaranteed. A basket was accordingly hung outside the hospital; the maximum age for admission was raised from two months to twelve, and a flood of children poured in from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poor_Law" title="Poor Law">country workhouses</a>. Parliament soon came to the conclusion that the indiscriminate admission should be discontinued. The hospital adopted a system of receiving children only with considerable sums. This practice was finally stopped in 1801; and it henceforth became a fundamental rule that no money was to be received.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Children_at_crumpsall_workhouse_circa_1895.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="602" data-file-width="871" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Children_at_crumpsall_workhouse_circa_1895.jpg/220px-Children_at_crumpsall_workhouse_circa_1895.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Children_at_crumpsall_workhouse_circa_1895.jpg/330px-Children_at_crumpsall_workhouse_circa_1895.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Children_at_crumpsall_workhouse_circa_1895.jpg/440px-Children_at_crumpsall_workhouse_circa_1895.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Children_at_crumpsall_workhouse_circa_1895.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A group of orphans at Crumpsall Workhouse in the 19th century.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>By the early nineteenth century, the problem of abandoned children in urban areas, especially <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, began to reach alarming proportions. The <a href="/wiki/Workhouse" title="Workhouse">workhouse</a> system, instituted in <a href="/wiki/Poor_Law_Amendment_Act_1834" title="Poor Law Amendment Act 1834">1834</a>, although often brutal, was an attempt at the time to house orphans as well as other vulnerable people in society who could not support themselves in exchange for work. Conditions, especially for the women and children, were so bad as to cause an outcry among the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_reform" title="Social reform">social reform</a>-minded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle-class" title="Middle-class">middle-class</a>; some of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a>' most famous novels, including <i><a href="/wiki/Oliver_Twist" title="Oliver Twist">Oliver Twist</a></i>, highlighted the plight of the vulnerable and the often abusive conditions that were prevalent in the London orphanages.</p>
<p>Clamour for change led to the birth of the orphanage movement. In England, the movement really took off in the mid-19th century although orphanages such as the Orphan Working Home in 1758 and the Bristol Asylum for Poor Orphan Girls in 1795, had been set up earlier. Private orphanages were founded by private benefactors; these often received <a href="/wiki/Patronage" title="Patronage">royal patronage</a> and government oversight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ragged_school" title="Ragged school">Ragged schools</a>, founded by <a href="/wiki/John_Pounds" title="John Pounds">John Pounds</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Ashley-Cooper,_7th_Earl_of_Shaftesbury" title="Anthony Ashley-Cooper, 7th Earl of Shaftesbury">Lord Shaftesbury</a> were also set up to provide pauper children with basic education.</p>
<p>Orphanages were also set up in the United States from the early 19th century; for example, in 1806, the first private orphanage in New York (the Orphan Asylum Society, now <a href="/wiki/Graham_Windham" title="Graham Windham">Graham Windham</a>) was co-founded by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Schuyler_Hamilton" title="Elizabeth Schuyler Hamilton">Elizabeth Schuyler Hamilton</a>, widow of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Hamilton" title="Alexander Hamilton">Alexander Hamilton</a>, one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Founding_fathers_of_the_United_States" title="Founding fathers of the United States">founding fathers of the United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-segedin_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-segedin-9">[9]</a></sup> Under the influence of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Loring_Brace" title="Charles Loring Brace">Charles Loring Brace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Foster_care" title="Foster care">foster care</a> became a popular alternative from the mid-19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Later, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_Security_Act_of_1935#History" title="Social Security Act of 1935">Social Security Act of 1935</a> improved conditions by authorizing <a href="/wiki/Aid_to_Families_with_Dependent_Children" title="Aid to Families with Dependent Children">Aid to Families with Dependent Children</a> as a form of <a href="/wiki/Social_security" title="Social security">social security</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:121px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drbarnardo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="298" data-file-width="178" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Drbarnardo.jpg/119px-Drbarnardo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7d/Drbarnardo.jpg 1.5x" width="119"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drbarnardo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Thomas_John_Barnardo" title="Thomas John Barnardo">Thomas John Barnardo</a>, the founder of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barnardos" title="Barnardos">Barnardos Home</a> for orphaned children.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A very influential philanthropist of the era was <a href="/wiki/Thomas_John_Barnardo" title="Thomas John Barnardo">Thomas John Barnardo</a>, the founder of the charity <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barnardos" title="Barnardos">Barnardos</a>. Becoming aware of the great numbers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeless" title="Homeless">homeless</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Destitute" title="Destitute">destitute</a> children adrift in the cities of England and encouraged by the <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Ashley-Cooper,_7th_Earl_of_Shaftesbury" title="Anthony Ashley-Cooper, 7th Earl of Shaftesbury">7th Earl of Shaftesbury</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Cairns,_1st_Earl_Cairns" title="Hugh Cairns, 1st Earl Cairns">1st Earl Cairns</a>, he opened the first of the "Dr Barnardo’s Homes" in 1870. By his death in 1905, he had established 112 district homes, which searched for and received waifs and strays, to feed, clothe and educate them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-11">[11]</a></sup> The system under which the institution was carried on is broadly as follows: the infants and younger girls and boys were chiefly "boarded out" in rural districts; girls above fourteen years of age were sent to the industrial training homes, to be taught useful domestic occupations; boys above seventeen years of age were first tested in labour homes and then placed in employment at home, sent to sea, or emigrated; boys of between thirteen and seventeen years of age were trained for the various trades for which they might be mentally or physically fitted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deinstitutionalisation">Deinstitutionalisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Deinstitutionalisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deinstitutionalisation_of_orphanages_and_childrens_homes" title="Deinstitutionalisation of orphanages and childrens homes">Deinstitutionalisation of orphanages and childrens homes</a> programme began in the 1950s, after a series of scandals involving the coercion of birth parents and abuse of orphans (notably at <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Tann" title="Georgia Tann">Georgia Tann</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Children%27s_Home_Society" title="Tennessee Children's Home Society">Tennessee Children's Home Society</a>). Many countries accepted the need to de-institutionalize the care of vulnerable children—that is, close down orphanages in favor of foster care and accelerated adoption. Moreover, as it is no longer common for birth parents in Western countries to give up their children, and as far fewer people die of diseases or violence while their children are still young, the need to operate large orphanages has decreased.</p>
<p>Major charities are increasingly focusing their efforts on the re-integration of orphans in order to keep them with their parents or extended family and communities. Orphanages are no longer common in the European community, and Romania in particular has struggled to reduce the visibility of its <a href="/wiki/Romanian_orphans" title="Romanian orphans">children's institutions</a> to meet conditions of its entry into the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>.</p>
<p>It is important to understand the reasons for child abandonment, <i>then</i> set up targeted alternative services to support vulnerable families at risk of separation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> such as mother and baby units and day care centres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_to_alternatives">Comparison to alternatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Comparison to alternatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is an increasing body of evidence that orphanages, especially large orphanages, are the worst possible care option for children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Savethechildren.org.uk_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Savethechildren.org.uk-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In large institutions all children, but particularly babies may not receive enough eye contact, physical contact, and stimulation to promote proper physical, social or cognitive development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unicef1_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-unicef1-17">[17]</a></sup> In the worst cases, orphanages can be dangerous and unregulated places where children are subject to abuse and neglect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Savethechildren.org.uk_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Savethechildren.org.uk-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is only one significant study which disputes this. It was carried out by <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">Duke University</a>. Their researchers concluded that institutional care in America in the 20th century produced the same health, emotional, intellectual, mental, and physical outcomes as care by relatives, and better than care in the homes of strangers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKenzie_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-McKenzie-20">[20]</a></sup> One explanation for this is the prevalence of <i>permanent temporary foster care</i>. This is the name for a long string of short stays with different foster care families.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKenzie_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-McKenzie-20">[20]</a></sup> Permanent temporary foster care is highly disruptive to the child and prevents the child from developing a sense of security or belonging. Placement in the home of a relative maintains and usually improves the child's connection to family members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKenzie_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-McKenzie-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Whereas orphanages are intended to be reasonably permanent placements, group homes may be used for short-term placements. They may be <a href="/wiki/Residential_treatment_center" title="Residential treatment center">residential treatment centers</a>, and they frequently specialize in a particular population with psychiatric or behavioral problems, e.g., a group home for children and teens with <a href="/wiki/Autism" title="Autism">autism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eating_disorder" title="Eating disorder">eating disorders</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Substance_abuse" title="Substance abuse">substance abuse</a> problems or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Child_soldiers" title="Child soldiers">child soldiers</a> undergoing decommissioning.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most of the children living in institutions around the world have a surviving parent or close relative, and they most commonly entered orphanages because of poverty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-22">[22]</a></sup> It is speculated that, flush with money, orphanages are increasing and push for children to join even though demographic data show that even the poorest extended families usually take in children whose parents have died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-22">[22]</a></sup> Experts and child advocates maintain that orphanages are expensive and often harm children's <a href="/wiki/Child_development" title="Child development">development</a> by separating them from their families and that it would be more effective and cheaper to aid close relatives who want to take in the orphans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Children living in orphanages for prolonged periods get behind in development goals, have worse mental health. Orphanage children are not included in statistics making it easy to traffic them or abuse them in other ways. There are campaigns to include orphanage children and <a href="/wiki/Street_children" title="Street children">street children</a> in progress statistics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scams">Scams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Scams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Visitors to developing countries can be taken in by orphanage scams, which can include orphanages created for the day<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> or orphanages set up as a front to get foreigners to pay school fees of orphanage directors' extended families.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Alternatively the children whose upkeep is being funded by foreigners may be sent to work, not to school, the exact opposite of what the donor is expecting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The worst even sell children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Myrepublica.Com_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Myrepublica.Com-29">[29]</a></sup> In Cambodia some are bought from their parents for very little and passed on to westerners who pay a large fee to adopt them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> This also happens in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In Nepal, orphanages can be used as a way to remove a child from their parents before placing them for adoption overseas, which is equally lucrative to the owners who receive a number of official and unofficial payments and "donations".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In other countries, such as <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, orphanages are run as businesses, which will attract donations and make the owners rich; often the conditions orphans are kept in will deliberately be poor to attract more donations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Worldwide">Worldwide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Worldwide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The orphanages and institutions remaining in Europe tend to be in Eastern Europe and are generally state-funded.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Albania">Albania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Albania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are approximately 10 small orphanages in Albania; each one having only 12-40 children residing there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bosnia_and_Herzegovina">Bosnia and Herzegovina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Bosnia and Herzegovina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>SOS Children's Villages giving support to 240 orphaned children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Bulgaria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Bulgarian government has shown interest in strengthening children's rights.</p>
<p>In 2010, Bulgaria adopted a national strategic plan for the period 2010–2025 to improve the living standards of the country's children. Bulgaria is working hard to get all institutions closed within the next few years and find alternative ways to take care of the children.</p>
<p>Support is sporadically given to poor families and work during daytime; correspondingly, different kinds of day centers have started up, though the quality of care in these centers is poorly measured and difficult to monitor. A smaller number of children have also been able to be relocated into foster families".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-humanrights.gov.se_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-humanrights.gov.se-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are 7000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-humanrights.gov.se_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-humanrights.gov.se-38">[38]</a></sup> children living in Bulgarian orphanages wrongly classified as orphaned. Only 10 percent of these are orphans, with the rest of the children placed in orphanages for temporary periods when the family is in crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Estonia">Estonia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Estonia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As of 2009, there are 35 orphanages, which houses approximately 1300 orphaned children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hungary">Hungary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Hungary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A comprehensive national strategy for strengthening the rights of children was adopted by Parliament in 2007 and will run until 2032.</p>
<p>Child flow to orphanages has been stopped and children are now protected by social services. Violation of children's rights leads to litigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lithuania">Lithuania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Lithuania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a> there are 105 institutions. 41 percent of the institutions each have more than 60 children. Lithuania has the highest number of orphaned children in Northern Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Poland">Poland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Poland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Children's rights enjoys a relatively strong protection in <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>. Orphaned children are now protected by social services.</p>
<p>Social Workers' opportunities have increased by establishing more foster homes and aggressive family members can now be forced away from home, instead of re-placing the child/children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Moldova">Moldova</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Moldova">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>More than 8800 children are being raised in state institutions, but only three percent of them are orphans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Romania">Romania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Romania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Romanian child welfare system is in the process of being revised and has reduced the flow of infants into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orphanages" title="Orphanages">orphanages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Baroness Emma Nicholson, in some counties <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> now has "a completely new, world class, state of the art, child health development policy." Dickensian orphanages remain in Romania,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> but by 2020 Romanian institutions are to be replaced by family care services, as children in need will be protected by social services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hopeandhomes.org_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-hopeandhomes.org-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2011, there are 10,833 orphaned children in 256 large institutions in Romania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-50">[50]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Serbia">Serbia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Serbia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are many state orphanages "where several thousand children are kept and which are still part of an outdated child care system". The conditions for them are bad because the government does not pay enough attention in improving the living standards for disabled children in Serbia's orphanages and medical institutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Slovakia">Slovakia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Slovakia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Committee made recommendations, such as proposals for the adoption of a new "national 14" action plan for children for at least the next five years, and the creation of an independent institution for the protection of child rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">Sweden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Sweden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Sweden there are 5,000 children in the care of the state. None of them are currently living in an orphanage, because there is a social service law which requires that the children reside in a family home.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victorian_Era" title="Victorian Era">Victorian Era</a>, child abandonment was rampant, and orphanages were set up to reduce infant mortality. Such places were often so full of children that "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Killing_nurse&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Killing nurse (page does not exist)">killing nurses</a>" often administered <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Godfrey%27s_Cordial&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Godfrey's Cordial (page does not exist)">Godfrey's Cordial</a>, a special concoction of <a href="/wiki/Opium" title="Opium">opium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Treacle" title="Treacle">treacle</a>, to soothe <a href="/wiki/Baby_colic" title="Baby colic">baby colic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Orphaned children were placed in either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prisons" title="Prisons">prisons</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Poorhouse" title="Poorhouse">poorhouse</a>/<a href="/wiki/Workhouse" title="Workhouse">workhouse</a>, as there were so few places in orphanages, or else they were left to fend for themselves on the street. Such places as were available could only be obtained by procuring <a href="/wiki/Votes" title="Votes">votes</a> for admission, placing them out of reach of poor families.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sub-Saharan_Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Sub-Saharan Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Malawi_AIDS_Orphans.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Malawi_AIDS_Orphans.jpeg/180px-Malawi_AIDS_Orphans.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Malawi_AIDS_Orphans.jpeg/270px-Malawi_AIDS_Orphans.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Malawi_AIDS_Orphans.jpeg/360px-Malawi_AIDS_Orphans.jpeg 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Malawi_AIDS_Orphans.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
AIDS orphans in Malawi</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The majority of African orphanages (especially in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>) appear to be funded by donors, often from Western nations, rather than by domestic governments.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Ethiopia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>"For example, in the Jerusalem Association Children's Home (JACH), only 160 children remain of the 785 who were in JACH's three orphanages." / "Attitudes regarding the institutional care of children have shifted dramatically in recent years in Ethiopia. There appears to be general recognition by MOLSA and the NGOs with which Pact is working that such care is, at best, a last resort, and that serious problems arise with the social reintegration of children who grow up in institutions, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deinstitutionalisation_of_orphanages_and_childrens_homes" title="Deinstitutionalisation of orphanages and childrens homes">deinstitutionalization</a> through family reunification and independent living are being emphasized."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ghana">Ghana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Ghana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A 2007 survey sponsored by OAfrica (previously OrphanAid Africa) and carried out by the Department of Social Welfare came up with the figure of 4,800 children in institutional care in 148 orphanages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> The government is currently attempting to phase out the use of orphanages in favor of foster care placements and adoption. At least eighty-eight<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> homes have been closed since the passage of the National Plan of Action for Orphans and Vulnerable Children. The website www.ovcghana.org details these reforms.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Kenya">Kenya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Kenya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A 1999 survey of 36,000 orphans found the following number in institutional care: 64 in registered institutions and 164 in unregistered institutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Malawi">Malawi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Malawi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are about 101 orphanages in Malawi. There is a UNICEF/Government driven program on de-institutionalisation, but few orphanages are yet involved in the program.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rwanda">Rwanda</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Rwanda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Out of 400,000 orphans, 5,000 are living in orphanages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> The Government of Rwanda are working with Hope and Homes for Children to close the first institution and develop a model for community-based childcare which can be used across the country and ultimately Africa<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tanzania">Tanzania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Tanzania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>"Currently, there are 52 orphanages in Tanzania caring for about 3,000 orphans and vulnerable children."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> A world bank document on Tanzania showed it was six times more expensive to institutionalise a child there than to help the family become functional and support the child themselves.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nigeria">Nigeria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Nigeria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Nigeria, a rapid assessment of orphans and vulnerable children conducted in 2004 with UNICEF support revealed that there were about seven millions orphans in 2003 and that 800,000 more orphans were added during that same year. Out of this total number, about 1.8 million are <a href="/wiki/AIDS_orphan" title="AIDS orphan">orphaned by HIV/AIDS</a>. With the spread of HIV/AIDS, the number of orphans is expected to increase rapidly in the coming years to 8.2 million by 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Since 2000, South Africa does not license orphanages any more but they continue to be set up unregulated and potentially more harmful. Theoretically the policy supports community-based family homes but this is not always the case. One example is the homes operated by Thokomala.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Zambia">Zambia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Zambia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A 1996 national survey of orphans revealed no evidence of orphanage care. The breakdown of care was as follows: 38% grandparents 55% extended family 1% older orphan 6% non-relative Recently a group of students started a fundraising website for an orphanage in Zambia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_72-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Zimbabwe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are 39 privately run children's charity homes, or orphanages, in the country, and the government operates eight of its own. Privately run Orphanages can accommodate an average of 2000 children, though some are very small and located in very remote areas, hence can take in less than 150 children. Statistics on the total number of children in orphanages nationwide are unavailable, but caregivers say their facilities were becoming unmanageably overwhelmed almost on a daily basis. Between 1994 and 1998, the number of orphans in Zimbabwe more than doubled from 200,000 to 543,000, and in five years, the number is expected to reach 900,000. (Unfortunately, there is no room for these children.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Togo">Togo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Togo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Togo, there were an estimated 280,000 orphans under 18 years of age in 2005, 88,000 of them orphaned by AIDS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_80-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-80">[80]</a></sup> Ninety-six thousand orphans in Togo attend school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_80-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sierra_Leone">Sierra Leone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Sierra Leone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Senegal">Senegal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Senegal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>India has a very large number of orphans as well as destitute child population. Orphanages operated by the state are generally known as juvenile homes. In addition there is a vast number of privately run orphanages running into thousands spread across the country. These area run by various trusts, religious groups, individual citizens, citizens groups, NGO's etc.</p>
<p>While some of these places endeavour to place the children for adoption a vast majority just care and educate them till they are of legal majority age and help place them back on their feet. Prominent organizations in this field include BOYS TOWN, SOS children's villages etc.</p>
<p>There have been scandals especially with regard to adoption. Since government rules restrict funds unless there are a certain number of residents, some orphanages make sure the resident numbers remain high at the cost of adoption.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Thailand">Thailand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=45" title="Edit section: Thailand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are still a substantial number of NGO's and informal Orphanages in Thailand, particularly in Northern Thailand near the borders of Laos and Myanmar, e.g. around Chiang Rai. Very few of the children in these establishments are orphans, most have living parents. They attract funding from well meaning tourists. Often protecting the children from trafficking/abuse is cited but the names and photographs of the children are published in marketing material to attract more funding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> The reality is that the safest environment for these children is almost always with their parents or in their villages with familial connections where strangers are rarely seen and immediately recognised. A very few of these orphanages, go so far as to abduct or forcibly remove children from their homes, often across the border in Myanmar. The parents in local hill tribes may be encouraged to "buy a place" in the orphanage for vast sums, being told their child will have a better future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> Some childrens homes claim to always try to repatriate children with their families, but the local managers &amp; director of the homes know of no such procedures or processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cambodia">Cambodia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=47" title="Edit section: Cambodia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are numerous NGOs focusing their efforts on assisting Cambodia's orphans: one group, <a href="/wiki/World_Orphans" title="World Orphans">World Orphans</a>, constructed 47 orphanages housing over 1500 children in a three-year period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> The total number of orphans is much higher, but unknown: "There are no accurate figures available on how many orphans there are in Cambodia." One charity named "CHOICE Cambodia" is run by expats based in the capital city of Phnom Penh; it helps support extremely poor and homeless people and helps families stay together rather than have their children put into orphanages where they might get exploited.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=48" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>"Currently there are 50,000 children in Chinese orphanages, while the number of abandoned children shows no sign of slowing. Official figures show that fewer than 20,000 of China's orphans are now in any form of institutional care."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-96">[96]</a></sup> Chinese official records fail to account for most of the country's abandoned infants and children, only a small proportion of whom are in any form of acknowledged state care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_96-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-96">[96]</a></sup> The most recent figure provided seems implausibly low for a country with a total population of 1.2 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_96-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-96">[96]</a></sup> Even if it were accurate, however, the whereabouts of the great majority of China's orphans would still be a complete mystery, leaving crucial questions about the country's child welfare system unanswered and suggesting that the real scope of the catastrophe that has befallen China's unwanted children may be far larger than the evidence in this report documents.</p>

<p>The "Royal Charity Organization"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> is a Bahraini governmental charity organization founded in 2001 by King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hamad_ibn_Isa_Al_Khalifah" title="Hamad ibn Isa Al Khalifah">Hamad ibn Isa Al Khalifah</a> to sponsor all helpless Bahraini orphans and widows. <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia">Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=57" title="Edit section: Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Orphans_in_Russia" title="Orphans in Russia">Orphans in Russia</a></div>
<p>Over 700,000 orphans live in Russia, increasing at the rate of 113,000 per year. UNICEF estimates that 95% of these children are social orphans, meaning that they have at least one living parent who has given them up to the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> In 2011 Russian authorities registered 88,522 children who became orphans that year (down from 114,715 in 2009).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are few web pages for Russian orphanages in English, such as St Nicholas Orphanage<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alapaevsk" title="Alapaevsk">Alapaevsk</a> orphanage in the Urals. "Of a total of more than 600,000 children classified as being 'without parental care' (most of them live with other relatives and fosters), as many as one-third reside in institutions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, there were 1344 institutions for orphans in Russia<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fedstat1_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-fedstat1-107">[107]</a></sup> including 1094 orphanages ("Children homes")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> and 207 special ("corrective") orphanages for children with serious health issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Haiti">Haiti</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=69" title="Edit section: Haiti">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Haitians and expatriate childcare professionals are careful to make it clear that Haitian orphanages and children's homes are not orphanages in the North American sense, but instead shelters for vulnerable children, often housing children whose parent(s) are poor as well as those who are abandoned, neglected or abused by family guardians. Neither the number of children or the number of institutions is officially known, but Chambre de L'Enfance Necessiteusse Haitienne (CENH) indicated that it has received requests for assistance from nearly 200 orphanages from around the country for more than 200,000 children. Although not all are orphans, many are vulnerable or originate in vulnerable families that "hoped to increase their children's opportunities by sending them to orphanages." <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Relief_Services" title="Catholic Relief Services">Catholic Relief Services</a> provides assistance to 120 orphanages with 9,000 children in the <a href="/wiki/Ouest_(department)" title="Ouest (department)">Ouest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sud_(department)" title="Sud (department)">Sud</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sud-Est_(department)" title="Sud-Est (department)">Sud-Est</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grand%27Anse_(department)" title="Grand'Anse (department)">Grand'Anse</a>, but these include only orphanages that meet their criteria. They estimate receiving ten requests per week for assistance from additional orphanages and children's homes, but some of these are repeat requests."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, <a href="/wiki/UNICEF" title="UNICEF">UNICEF</a> estimated there were 380,000 orphans in Haiti, which has a population of just over 9 million, according to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CIA_World_Factbook" title="CIA World Factbook">CIA World Factbook</a>. However, since the <a href="/wiki/2010_Haiti_earthquake" title="2010 Haiti earthquake">January 2010 earthquake</a>, the number of orphans has skyrocketed, and the living conditions for orphans have seriously deteriorated. Official numbers are hard to find due to the general state of chaos in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup></p>

<p>Traditional orphanages are no longer a part of the American adoption process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orphanagegoodinfo_125-0"><a href="#cite_note-orphanagegoodinfo-125">[125]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup> Following World War II, most orphanages in the U.S. began closing or converting to boarding schools or group homes. Over the past few decades, orphanages in the U.S. have been replaced with smaller institutions that try to provide a group home or boarding school environment. Most children who would have been in orphanages are in these residential treatment centers (RTC), group homes, or with foster families. Adopting from RTCs, group homes, or foster families does not require working with an adoption agency, and in many areas, fostering to adopt is highly encouraged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orphanagestats_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-orphanagestats-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Significant_charities_that_help_orphans">Significant charities that help orphans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orphanage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=75" title="Edit section: Significant charities that help orphans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to the establishment of state care for orphans in First World countries, private charities existed to take care of destitute orphans, over time other charities have found other ways to care for children.</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.orphanedstarfish.org/" rel="nofollow">[12]</a><a href="/wiki/Orphaned_Starfish_Foundation" title="Orphaned Starfish Foundation">Orphaned Starfish Foundation</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Nonprofit_organization" title="Nonprofit organization">non-profit organisation</a> based in New York City that focuses on developing vocational schools for <a href="/wiki/Orphan" title="Orphan">orphans</a>, victims of abuse and at-risk youth. It runs fifty computer centres in twenty-five countries, serving over 10,000 children worldwide <a href="/wiki/Lumos_(charity)" title="Lumos (charity)">Lumos</a> works to replace institutions with community-based services that provide children with access to health, education and social care tailored to their individual needs.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hope_and_Homes_for_Children" title="Hope and Homes for Children">Hope and Homes for Children</a> are working with governments to deinstitutionalise their child care systems.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/SOS_Children%27s_Villages" title="SOS Children's Villages">SOS Children's Villages</a> is the world's largest non-governmental, non-denominational child welfare organization that provides loving family homes for orphaned and abandoned children.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dr_Barnardo" title="Dr Barnardo">Dr Barnardo's Homes</a> are now simply <a href="/wiki/Barnardo%27s" title="Barnardo's">Barnardo's</a> after closing their last orphanage in 1989.</li>
<li>OAfrica, previously OrphanAid Africa, has been working in Ghana since 2002, to get children out of orphanages and into families, in partnership with the government and as the only private implementing partner of the National Plan of Action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-129"><a href="#cite_note-129">[129]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Joint_Council_on_International_Children%27s_Services" title="Joint Council on International Children's Services">Joint Council on International Children's Services</a> is a nonprofit child advocacy organization based in Alexandria, Virginia. It is the largest association of international <a href="/wiki/Adoption" title="Adoption">adoption</a> agencies in America, and in addition to working in 51 different countries, advocates for ethical practices in American adoption agencies</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Orphan_Resources_International&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Orphan Resources International (page does not exist)">Orphan Resources International</a> is a non-profit that provides resources to orphanages in Guatemala Central America</li>
<li>www.careforchildren.com introduced family placement to China with a pilot project in Shanghai in 1998 working with the Shanghai Government. In 2004 Care for Children partnered with the MoCA and the China Social Work Association in Beijing to develop family placement on a nationwide basis. It is now thought that a generation of children who were abandoned are living in substitute families instea of institutions in China.</li>
<li>Abide Family Center (abidefamilycenter.org) is an organization working in Jinja, Uganda to reduce the number of children living in orphanages. They believe that poverty should never be a reason a child is separated from their family. For this reason the provide family preservation services including business and parenting classes, emergency/transitional housing, early childhood education and pastoral/counselling services.</li>
<li><a class="external text" href="https://t-o-i.org/transition-house/" rel="nofollow">Transition Home for Orphan Boys in Afghanistan</a>.The Transition Home was opened in 2014, and currently 6 boys live in the Transition Home. The goal of the Transition Home is that when these at risk youth leave the transition home they leave ready to live on their own. They have full-time jobs, they have furnishings for their own home, they have skills needed to live on their own, i.e. cooking, cleaning, and how to manage their finances. Prior to these youth leaving the Transition Home, the T-O-M mentors will take each youth out and help them make purchases in the markets for household items. This teaches them how to shop and what the value of a dollar is. Our mentors will then take them out to find a room/apartment to rent and help negotiate a contract and pay the upfront fees. All of this is done so that these youth now have a chance in life, they have options, and they have a choice to make something of themselves.</li>
</ul>
