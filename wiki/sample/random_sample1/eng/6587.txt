<p>The <b>Boeing X-32</b> was a concept demonstrator aircraft in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joint_Strike_Fighter_Program" title="Joint Strike Fighter Program">Joint Strike Fighter contest</a>. It lost to the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_X-35" title="Lockheed Martin X-35">Lockheed Martin X-35</a> demonstrator which was further developed into the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-35_Lightning_II" title="Lockheed Martin F-35 Lightning II">Lockheed Martin F-35 Lightning II</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_X-32&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1993, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defense_Advanced_Research_Projects_Agency" title="Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency">Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency</a> (DARPA) launched the Common Affordable Lightweight Fighter project (CALF). The project's purpose was to develop a stealth-enabled design to replace all of <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">United States Department of Defense</a> lighter weight fighter and attack aircraft, including the <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_F-16_Fighting_Falcon" title="General Dynamics F-16 Fighting Falcon">F-16 Fighting Falcon</a>, <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_F/A-18_Hornet" title="McDonnell Douglas F/A-18 Hornet">McDonnell Douglas F/A-18 Hornet</a>, and vertical/short takeoff / vertical landing (<a href="/wiki/V/STOL" title="V/STOL">V/STOL</a>) <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_AV-8B_Harrier_II" title="McDonnell Douglas AV-8B Harrier II">AV-8B Harrier II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Around the same time the Joint Advanced Strike Technology (JAST) project was started.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1994, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congress_of_the_United_States" title="Congress of the United States">U.S. Congress</a> ordered the two to be merged into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joint_Strike_Fighter_Program" title="Joint Strike Fighter Program">Joint Strike Fighter Program</a>.</p>
<p>Many companies took part in the first phase of this project, which involved drafting concept aircraft designs for submission to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a>. On 16 November 1996, <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a> were awarded contracts for them to produce two of their concept demonstrator aircraft (CDA) each. Under the contract, these fighters were required to demonstrate conventional take-off and landing (CTOL), carrier take-off and landing (CV version), and short take-off and vertical landing (STOVL). They were also expected to include ground demonstrations of a production representative aircraft's systems, such as the Preferred Weapon System Concept (PWSC).</p>
<p>One major departure from previous projects was the prohibition of the companies from using their own money to finance development. Each was awarded $750 million to produce their two aircraft – including avionics, software and hardware. This limitation promoted the adoption of low cost manufacturing and assembly techniques, and also prevented either Boeing or Lockheed Martin from <a href="/wiki/Bankruptcy" title="Bankruptcy">bankrupting</a> themselves in an effort to win such an important contest.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Designing_the_X-32">Designing the X-32</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_X-32&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Designing the X-32">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Boeing's strategy for a competitive advantage was to offer substantially lower manufacturing and life-cycle costs by minimizing variations between the different JSF versions. The X-32 therefore was designed around a large one piece <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber" title="Carbon fiber">carbon fiber</a> <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composite</a> <a href="/wiki/Delta_wing" title="Delta wing">delta wing</a>. The wing had a span of 9.15 meters, with a 55-degree leading edge sweep and could hold up to 20,000 pounds of fuel. The purpose of the high sweep angle was to allow for a thick wing section to be used while still providing limited <a href="/wiki/Transonic" title="Transonic">transonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_drag" title="Aerodynamic drag">aerodynamic drag</a>, and to provide a good angle for wing-installed <a href="/wiki/Conformal_antenna" title="Conformal antenna">conformal antenna</a> equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lockheed_Stealth_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lockheed_Stealth-3">[3]</a></sup> The wing would prove a challenge to fabricate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOVA_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOVA-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The compete-on-cost strategy also led Boeing to pick a direct-lift <a href="/wiki/Thrust_vectoring" title="Thrust vectoring">thrust vectoring</a> system, for the Marines' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Short_take-off_and_vertical_landing" title="Short take-off and vertical landing">short take-off and vertical landing</a> (STOVL) requirement, as this would only necessitate the addition of a thrust vectoring module around the main engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOVA_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-NOVA-4">[4]</a></sup> However, this choice required the engine to be mounted directly behind the cockpit, and moved the <a href="/wiki/Center_of_gravity_of_an_aircraft" title="Center of gravity of an aircraft">center of gravity of an aircraft</a> forward from its usual position in jet fighters (at the rear of the airplane) to enable a neutral-attitude hover. Boeing had proposed, in the 1960s, a similar supersonic fighter with a mid-center-of-gravity mounted engine with vectored thrust nozzles, but this never proceeded beyond pictures published in <a href="/wiki/Aviation_Week_%26_Space_Technology" title="Aviation Week &amp; Space Technology">Aviation Week</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> By comparison, the Lockheed entry looked like, if anything, a smaller version of the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-22_Raptor" title="Lockheed Martin F-22 Raptor">F-22 Raptor</a> stealth fighter. The Boeing in-house nickname of the X-32 was the "Monica".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Yet another effect of the selection of the direct-lift system was the large chin-mounted air intake, akin to the <a href="/wiki/Vought_F-8_Crusader" title="Vought F-8 Crusader">Vought F-8 Crusader</a> and <a href="/wiki/LTV_A-7_Corsair_II" title="LTV A-7 Corsair II">LTV A-7 Corsair II</a>. This was required to feed sufficient air to the main engine (to provide the thrust necessary to hover) during the zero horizontal velocity phase, when it could not exploit ram-air pressure. A knock on effect of this large intake, was the potential direct visibility of the compressor blades to radar (see <a href="/wiki/Radar_cross-section" title="Radar cross-section">radar cross-section</a>). Mitigation possibilities included variable baffles designed to block incoming <a href="/wiki/Radio_wave" title="Radio wave">radio waves</a> without adversely affecting airflow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lockheed_Stealth_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lockheed_Stealth-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_changes">Design changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_X-32&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Design changes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The two X-32 aircraft featured a delta wing design. However, eight months into construction of the concept demonstrator aircraft, the JSF's maneuverability and payload requirements were refined at the request of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">Navy</a> and Boeing's delta wing design fell short of the new targets. Engineers altered the aircraft's design with a conventional canted <a href="/wiki/Twin_tail" title="Twin tail">twin tail</a> (narrowly beating out a <a href="/wiki/Pelikan_tail" title="Pelikan tail">Pelikan tail</a>) that reduced weight and improved agility, but it was too late to change the aircraft. It was judged that they would be sufficient to demonstrate Boeing's technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOVA_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-NOVA-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 14 December 1999, Boeing unveiled both its concept demonstrators at its plant in Palmdale, California, in front of 5,500 attendants. While the X-32A was expected to make an appearance, the roll out of the X-32B was a surprise, as construction of the latter aircraft had started some three months after the former and was completed six weeks after the X-32A.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Boeing attributed the rapid construction of the STOVL version to the use of digital design and assembly methods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> After having the Pratt &amp; Whitney F119 engine installed in April 2000, the X-32A commenced low- and medium-speed taxi tests, which had been completed by late May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_testing">Flight testing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_X-32&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Flight testing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to the heavy delta wing design of the X-32, Boeing demonstrated STOVL and supersonic flight in separate configurations, with the STOVL configuration requiring that some parts be removed from the fighter. The company promised that their conventional tail design for production models would not require separate configurations. By contrast, the Lockheed Martin X-35 concept demonstrator aircraft were capable of transitioning between their STOVL and supersonic configurations in mid-flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOVA_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-NOVA-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first flight of the X-32A (designed for CTOL and carrier trials) took place on 18 September 2000, from Boeing's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palmdale" title="Palmdale">Palmdale</a> plant to <a href="/wiki/Edwards_Air_Force_Base" title="Edwards Air Force Base">Edwards Air Force Base</a>. The aircraft, piloted by Boeing test pilot Fred Knox, took 2,200 feet (670 m) of runway before becoming airborne at 150 knots at around 8:00 am.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-First_flight_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-First_flight-11">[11]</a></sup> Shortly after take off, a minor hydraulic leak was discovered and the flight was cut from the expected 30 or 40 minutes to 20 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> According to Knox, the F/A-18 chase plane required "a lot of afterburner" to keep up with the X-32 during the initial stages. During the flight, the aircraft reached 10,000 feet (3,000 m), attained a speed of 200 knots (370 km/h; 230 mph), and attained an angle of attack of 13°. Despite the shortened flight, about 80% of the planned test points were accomplished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-First_flight_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-First_flight-11">[11]</a></sup> It was powered by a conventional derivative of the F-22 reheated turbofan, designated F119-PW-614C.</p>
<p>On 29 March 2001, the X-32B STOVL version made its first flight. The flight lasted 50 minutes as the aircraft flew from Palmdale to Edwards AFB. The flight had originally been scheduled for the third quarter of 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A modified version of the -614C engine, known as the F119-PW-614S powered the STOVL aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In normal flight, the -614S was configured as a conventional reheated turbofan. However, in the STOVL mode a butterfly valve diverted the core stream exhaust gases to a pair of thrust vectoring nozzles located close to the aircraft's centre-of-gravity. Forward of these nozzles, a jet screen nozzle provided a sheet of cool bypass air to minimise hot gas recirculation. There was also a pair of ducts leading to roll nozzles near the wing tips. Two pairs of ducts fed the Aft-pitch yaw nozzles and the Forward pitch nozzles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The reheat was unlit, with no gas flow during Lift. The X-32B achieved STOVL flight in much the same way as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AV-8B_Harrier_II" title="AV-8B Harrier II">AV-8B Harrier II</a> with <a href="/wiki/Thrust_vectoring" title="Thrust vectoring">thrust vectoring</a> of the jet exhaust. A smooth Transition (between STOVL and Normal modes) was obtained by maintaining a constant engine match, facilitated by the control system algorithm maintaining a fixed total nozzle effective area. Thus the engine was unaware of various nozzles being opened up and closed off to complete the Transition.</p>
<p>Basically the F119-PW-614S was a Direct Lift engine, whereas the Lockheed Martin STOVL team used a more complex and riskier alternative, known as the F119-PW-611, which comprised a remote shaft-driven <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_LiftSystem" title="Rolls-Royce LiftSystem">lift fan</a> powered by the main engine. However, this generated more lift thrust than possible with only direct exhaust gases. A successful design would have greater payload, and thus longer range than a simple thrust vectored <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Flight testing of both companies' aircraft continued until July 2001.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="JSF_competition">JSF competition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_X-32&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: JSF competition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Boeing_JSF_X-32_on_tarmac.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="450" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/95/Boeing_JSF_X-32_on_tarmac.jpg/220px-Boeing_JSF_X-32_on_tarmac.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/95/Boeing_JSF_X-32_on_tarmac.jpg/330px-Boeing_JSF_X-32_on_tarmac.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/95/Boeing_JSF_X-32_on_tarmac.jpg/440px-Boeing_JSF_X-32_on_tarmac.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Boeing_JSF_X-32_on_tarmac.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Boeing's JSF production mockup. Note the separate wing and tailplanes.</div>
</div>

<p>On 26 October 2001, the Department of Defense announced that the Lockheed Martin X-35 won the JSF competition. The X-35 would be developed into the production <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-35_Lightning_II" title="Lockheed Martin F-35 Lightning II">Lockheed Martin F-35 Lightning II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOVA_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-NOVA-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The loss of the JSF contract to Lockheed Martin in 2001 was a major blow to Boeing, as it represented the most important international fighter aircraft project since the <a href="/wiki/Lightweight_Fighter_program" title="Lightweight Fighter program">Lightweight Fighter program</a> competition of the 1960s and 1970s, which had led to the <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_F-16_Fighting_Falcon" title="General Dynamics F-16 Fighting Falcon">F-16 Fighting Falcon</a> and <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_F/A-18_Hornet" title="McDonnell Douglas F/A-18 Hornet">F/A-18 Hornet</a>. At the time, the production run of the JSF was estimated at anywhere between 3,000 and 5,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Prior to the awarding of the contract, many lawmakers pushed the idea of retaining the losing competitor as a sub-contractor; however, the "winner takes all" principle was not changed. Nonetheless, Boeing views its work on the X-32 as a strategic investment, yielding important technologies which it has been able to adopt in the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_F/A-18E/F_Super_Hornet" title="Boeing F/A-18E/F Super Hornet">Boeing F/A-18E/F Super Hornet</a> and other studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

X-32B at <a href="/wiki/Patuxent_River_Naval_Air_Museum" title="Patuxent River Naval Air Museum">Patuxent River Naval Air Museum</a></div>

<p>In 2005, the Boeing X-32A was transferred to the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_the_United_States_Air_Force" title="National Museum of the United States Air Force">National Museum of the United States Air Force</a> near <a href="/wiki/Dayton,_Ohio" title="Dayton, Ohio">Dayton, Ohio</a>. Its condition deteriorated due to being outside for several years following the end of the JSF competition, but it is now indoors and planned to be restored.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The X-32B was transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Patuxent_River_Naval_Air_Museum" title="Patuxent River Naval Air Museum">Patuxent River Naval Air Museum</a> adjacent to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NAS_Patuxent_River" title="NAS Patuxent River">NAS Patuxent River</a> in <a href="/wiki/St._Mary%27s_County,_Maryland" title="St. Mary's County, Maryland">St. Mary's County, Maryland</a> in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> It underwent restoration at the museum's restoration facility in June 2009 and is now on display.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specifications">Specifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_X-32&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Specifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>General characteristics</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Crew:</b> 1</li>
<li><b>Length:</b> 45.01 ft (13.72 m)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">Wingspan</a>:</b> 36 ft (10.97 m)</li>
<li><b>Height:</b> (5.28 m)</li>
<li><b>Wing area:</b> 590 ft² (54.8 m²)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Maximum_takeoff_weight" title="Maximum takeoff weight">Max. takeoff weight</a>:</b> 38,000 <a href="/wiki/Pound_(mass)" title="Pound (mass)">lb</a> (17,200 kg)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">Powerplant</a>:</b> 1 × <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_F119" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney F119">Pratt &amp; Whitney F119</a> derivative afterburning <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofan</a>
<ul>
<li><b>Dry thrust:</b> 28,000 lbf (125 kN)</li>
<li><b>Thrust with <a href="/wiki/Afterburner" title="Afterburner">afterburner</a>:</b> 43,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pound-force" title="Pound-force">lbf</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> (191 kN)</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Performance</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/V_speeds#Regulatory_V-speeds" title="V speeds">Maximum speed</a>:</b> <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach</a> 1.6 (1,200 mph, 1,931 km/h) at altitude<br/></li>
<li><b>Range on <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">USAF</a> mission profile:</b> 850 nmi (1,574 km)</li>
<li><b>Range on <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">USN</a> mission profile:</b> 750 nmi (1,389 km)</li>
<li><b>Range on <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">USMC</a>/<a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">RN</a> mission profile:</b> 600 nmi (1,112 km)</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Armament</b></span><br/></p>
<ul>
<li>20 mm <a href="/wiki/M61_Vulcan" title="M61 Vulcan">M61A2</a> cannon, or 27 mm <a href="/wiki/Mauser_BK-27" title="Mauser BK-27">Mauser BK-27</a> cannon</li>
<li>Internal: 6 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AMRAAM" title="AMRAAM">AMRAAM</a> air-air missiles or 2 AMRAAM air-air missiles and 2 x 2,000 lb (900 kg) class guided bombs</li>
<li>External: Approx. 15,000 lb (6,800 kg) of full range of external stores including guided weapons, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SEAD" title="SEAD">anti-radiation missile</a>, air-to-surface weapons, auxiliary fuel tanks</li>
</ul>
