<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Extra (acting)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>A <b>background actor</b> or <b>extra</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Performer" title="Performer">performer</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">film</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_show" title="Television show">television show</a>, stage, musical, opera or ballet production, who appears in a nonspeaking or nonsinging (silent) capacity, usually in the background (for example, in an audience or busy street scene). <a href="/wiki/War_film" title="War film">War films</a> and <a href="/wiki/Epic_film" title="Epic film">epic films</a> often employ background actors in large numbers: some films have featured hundreds or even thousands of paid background actors as cast members (hence the term 'cast of thousands'). Likewise, <a href="/wiki/Grand_opera" title="Grand opera">grand opera</a> can involve many background actors appearing in spectacular productions.</p>
<p>On a film or TV set, background actors are usually referred to as "background talent", "background performers", "background artists", "background cast members" or simply "background" while the term "extra" is rarely used. In a stage production, background actors are commonly referred to as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supernumeraries" title="Supernumeraries">supernumeraries</a>". In opera and ballet, they are called either "extras" or "supers".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Casting">Casting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extra_(acting)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Casting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Casting criteria for background actors depend on the production. It has been said that background cast members often require little or no <a href="/wiki/Acting" title="Acting">acting</a> experience. This is not entirely true, as any type of unrealistic portrayal must include some form of imagination and acting. Punctuality, reliability and the ability to take direction also figure prominently for these cast members. Background actors are often selected on short notice, after all other preparations for the shoot have been finalized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several casting agencies specialize only in background work, whilst in the UK the directory <i>Contacts</i> published annually by <a href="/wiki/Spotlight_(Casting_Services_Company)" title="Spotlight (Casting Services Company)">Spotlight</a> list all accredited agencies and productions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMagpie_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-MMagpie-3">[3]</a></sup> Some agencies charge a registration fee, and some (mostly commercial background casting) will take between 10% and 15% commission from any booked worked. You will be required to provide a basic one-page A4 sized CV/resume, that states basic personal details and dimensions, any significant skills (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Stage_combat" title="Stage combat">stage combat</a>), and includes two 8x10 inch photographs on the rear: one head shot; one full body shot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMagpie_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-MMagpie-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>When hiring background actors, casting directors generally seek those with a specific "look," such as "high school students" or "affluent senior citizens," consistent with the context of the film. Casting directors may also look for background actors with a special skill for the scene, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rollerblading" title="Rollerblading">rollerblading</a>, or dancing. A background actor is often expected to bring his or her own wardrobe to the set, although there are also "fittings" for a specific scene or period. A casting director may favor the one who already has the required costume or prop, such as a police uniform, or a musical instrument. On other occasions, where a costume has already been prepared (for example, to fit another actor who is now unavailable), a background actor may be selected as a "costume double" simply because they are the right size to fit it. On smaller productions or student films, background actors may be hired <i>en masse</i> with little formality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The length of a background actor's employment on a production largely depends on the needs of the director and the scenes being filmed. Some background actors are needed on the set only for a day or two, while others may remain with the film for an extended period. For instance, on <a href="/wiki/James_Cameron" title="James Cameron">James Cameron</a>'s film <i><a href="/wiki/Titanic_(1997_film)" title="Titanic (1997 film)">Titanic</a></i>, a group of 150 "core background actors" was hired to play the ship's passengers, and employed throughout the filming.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extra_(acting)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the distinction between an actor and an extra is defined by agreements between the actors <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade unions</a> <a href="/wiki/Broadcasting,_Entertainment,_Cinematograph_and_Theatre_Union" title="Broadcasting, Entertainment, Cinematograph and Theatre Union">Broadcasting Entertainment Cinematograph and Theatre Union (BECTU)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Equity_(trade_union)" title="Equity (trade union)">Equity</a>, and the various commercial trade and production bodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> These state that once a performer says 13 or more words in any scene, they must become a contracted actor in that production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EquityRA_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-EquityRA-6">[6]</a></sup> Minimum pay rates are defined by UK Government minimum wage regulations, and both BECTU and Equity have agreed rates with each body. However, even on non-union productions an extra's pay is an agreed day-rate for ten hours of production time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMagpie_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-MMagpie-3">[3]</a></sup> All performers under agreement with BECTU/Equity are paid on-going royalties. Hence on many advertisements, which are often shown multiple times and distributed internationally, whilst the extra is paid a contracted day-rate, the largest payment is nominally due from ongoing royalties. Due to the resultant complex calculations from multiple international showings, performers under a union managed agreement are often bought-out of their advertisement royalties with a one-off payment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EquityRA_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-EquityRA-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extra_(acting)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United States, most major film and television productions used to fall under the jurisdiction of the <a href="/wiki/Screen_Actors_Guild" title="Screen Actors Guild">Screen Actors Guild</a> (SAG) or <a href="/wiki/American_Federation_of_Television_and_Radio_Artists" title="American Federation of Television and Radio Artists">AFTRA</a>. The two unions have merged into <a href="/wiki/SAG-AFTRA" title="SAG-AFTRA">SAG-AFTRA</a>.</p>
<p>SAG-signatory <a href="/wiki/Alliance_of_Motion_Picture_and_Television_Producers" title="Alliance of Motion Picture and Television Producers">producers</a> are allowed to hire non-union background actors after a certain number of SAG performers have been cast; non-union background actors are usually paid the minimum wage. On productions outside of union jurisdiction, payment for background actors is at the discretion of the producers, and ranges from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trade_Union" title="Trade Union">union</a>-scale rates to "copy and credit" (i.e., no pay). Those producers who do not pay their actors may be in violation of state and federal laws about minimum wage for a job.</p>
<p>Between 1946 and 1992, background actors in film and television were largely represented by the <b>Screen Extras Guild.</b> SEG was disbanded on 1 June 1992 and transferred its jurisdiction to SAG.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extra_(acting)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The silent film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Extra_Girl" title="The Extra Girl">The Extra Girl</a></i> (1923) portrays a small-town girl who comes to <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a> and becomes a background actor in her attempt to achieve stardom.</p>
<p>The television sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/Extras_(TV_series)" title="Extras (TV series)">Extras</a></i> follows the exploits of two professional background actors, Andy and Maggie. They spend most of their time on set looking for a speaking role and a boyfriend, respectively.</p>
<p>The Chinese-Hong Kong film <i><a href="/wiki/I_Am_Somebody" title="I Am Somebody">I Am Somebody</a></i> (2015) is about extras working at the <a href="/wiki/Hengdian_World_Studios" title="Hengdian World Studios">Hengdian World Studios</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
