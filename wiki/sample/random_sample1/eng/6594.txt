<p>The <b>Beaufort Sea</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>mer de Beaufort</i></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Marginal_sea" title="Marginal sea">marginal sea</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wright2001_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wright2001-4">[4]</a></sup> located north of the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territories" title="Northwest Territories">Northwest Territories</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Yukon" title="Yukon">Yukon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, west of <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Arctic_Archipelago" title="Canadian Arctic Archipelago">Canada's Arctic islands</a>. The sea is named after <a href="/wiki/Hydrography" title="Hydrography">hydrographer</a> Sir <a href="/wiki/Francis_Beaufort" title="Francis Beaufort">Francis Beaufort</a>. The major <a href="/wiki/Mackenzie_River" title="Mackenzie River">Mackenzie River</a> empties into the Canadian part of the sea, west of <a href="/wiki/Tuktoyaktuk" title="Tuktoyaktuk">Tuktoyaktuk</a>, which is one of the few permanent settlements on the sea shores.</p>
<p>The sea, characterized by severe climate, is frozen over most of the year. Historically, only a narrow pass up to 100 km (62 mi) opened in August–September near its shores, but recently due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_the_Arctic" title="Climate change in the Arctic">climate change in the Arctic</a> the ice-free area in late summer has greatly enlarged. Claims that the seacoast was populated about 30,000 years ago have been largely discredited (see below); present population density is very low. The sea contains significant resources of petroleum and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> under its shelf, such as the Amauligak field. They were discovered in the period between the 1950s and 1980s, and their exploration became the major human activity in the area since the 1980s. The traditional occupations of fishery and whale and seal hunting are practiced only locally, and have no commercial significance. As a result, the sea hosts one of the largest colonies of <a href="/wiki/Beluga_whale" title="Beluga whale">beluga whales</a>, and there is no sign of <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>. To prevent overfishing in its waters, the US adopted precautionary commercial fisheries management plan in August 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In April 2011 the Canadian government signed a memorandum of understanding with the Inuvialuit as a first step in developing a larger ocean management plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The Canadian government announced in October 2014 that no new commercial fisheries in the Beaufort Sea will be considered until research has shown sustainable stocks that would be made available to Inuvialuit first.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Canadian government has set a new block of the Beaufort Sea off the Parry Peninsula in the Amundsen as a <a href="/wiki/Marine_protected_area" title="Marine protected area">Marine Protected Area</a> (MPA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The protected area is set to protect species and habits for the <a href="/wiki/Inuvialuit_Settlement_Region" title="Inuvialuit Settlement Region">Inuvialuit</a> community.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extent">Extent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beaufort_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Extent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Hydrographic_Organization" title="International Hydrographic Organization">International Hydrographic Organization</a> defines the limits of the Beaufort Sea as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>On the North.</i> A line from <a href="/wiki/Point_Barrow" title="Point Barrow">Point Barrow</a>, Alaska, to Lands End, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Patrick_Island" title="Prince Patrick Island">Prince Patrick Island</a> (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Beaufort_Sea&amp;params=76_16_N_124_08_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">76°16′N</span> <span class="longitude">124°08′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">76.267°N 124.133°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">76.267; -124.133</span></span></span></a></span>).</p>
<p><i>On the East.</i> From Lands End through the Southwest coast of Prince Patrick Island to Griffiths Point, thence a line to Cape Prince Alfred, the Northwestern extreme of <a href="/wiki/Banks_Island" title="Banks Island">Banks Island</a>, through its West coast to Cape Kellet, the Southwestern point, and thence a line to <a href="/wiki/Cape_Bathurst" title="Cape Bathurst">Cape Bathurst</a> on the mainland (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Beaufort_Sea&amp;params=70_36_N_127_32_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">70°36′N</span> <span class="longitude">127°32′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">70.600°N 127.533°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">70.600; -127.533</span></span></span></a></span>).</p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Border_dispute">Border dispute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beaufort_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Border dispute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arctic_management_area_and_disputed_waters.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1312" data-file-width="2095" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Arctic_management_area_and_disputed_waters.jpg/350px-Arctic_management_area_and_disputed_waters.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Arctic_management_area_and_disputed_waters.jpg/525px-Arctic_management_area_and_disputed_waters.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Arctic_management_area_and_disputed_waters.jpg/700px-Arctic_management_area_and_disputed_waters.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arctic_management_area_and_disputed_waters.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The cross-hatched wedge-shaped region in the east is claimed by both Canada and the US</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Territorial_claims_in_the_Arctic#Beaufort_Sea" title="Territorial claims in the Arctic">Territorial claims in the Arctic § Beaufort Sea</a></div>
<p>There is an unresolved dispute involving a wedge-shaped slice on the <a href="/wiki/Canada%E2%80%93United_States_border" title="Canada–United States border">International Boundary</a> in the Beaufort Sea, between the Canadian <a href="/wiki/Provinces_and_territories_of_Canada" title="Provinces and territories of Canada">territory</a> of Yukon and the U.S. state of Alaska. Canada claims the <a href="/wiki/Maritime_boundary" title="Maritime boundary">maritime boundary</a> to be along the <a href="/wiki/141st_meridian_west" title="141st meridian west">141st meridian west</a> out to a distance of 200 <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nmi</a> (370 km; 230 mi), following the Alaska–Yukon land border.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rothwell1996_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rothwell1996-11">[11]</a></sup> The position of the United States is that the boundary line is perpendicular to the coast out to a distance of 200 nmi (370 km; 230 mi), following a line of equidistance from the coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rothwell1996_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rothwell1996-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This difference creates a wedge with an area of about 21,000 km<sup>2</sup> (8,100 sq mi) that is claimed by both nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Canada's position has its roots in the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Saint_Petersburg_(1825)" title="Treaty of Saint Petersburg (1825)">Treaty of Saint Petersburg (1825)</a> between the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a> that set the boundary between the two. Canada is the <a href="/wiki/Succession_of_states" title="Succession of states">successor state</a> to Great Britain in relation to this treaty, which stipulates:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rothwell1996_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rothwell1996-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>the line of demarcation shall follow the summit of the mountains situated parallel to the Coast, as far as the point of intersection of the 141st degree of West longitude […] and, finally, from the said point of intersection, the said Meridian Line of the 141st degree, in its prolongation as far as the Frozen Ocean</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Article 3, <i>Convention between Great Britain and Russia concerning the Limits of their Respective Possessions on the North-West Coast of America and the Navigation of the Pacific Ocean</i>, 1825-02-16, 75 CTS 95<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rothwell1996_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Rothwell1996-11">[11]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Canada maintains that this treaty is extensible from the land into the Beaufort Sea along the meridian. The United States rejects this extension and instead asserts a boundary line based upon equidistance, although its position is somewhat undermined by its acceptance in 1867 of similar treaty wording and a similar interpretation under the treaty whereby <a href="/wiki/Alaska_Purchase" title="Alaska Purchase">it acquired Alaska</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rothwell1996_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Rothwell1996-11">[11]</a></sup> Both the U.S. and Canada agree that they are bound by the 1958 <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Continental_Shelf" title="Convention on the Continental Shelf">Convention on the Continental Shelf</a>; and they both agree that the boundary should be "equitable", as determined by the <a href="/wiki/International_Court_of_Justice" title="International Court of Justice">International Court of Justice</a>. They differ on what should be deemed "equitable". The U.S. contends that "equidistance is an appropriate principle for determining a maritime boundary where there are no special circumstances in the area and when equidistance results in a boundary in accordance with equitable principles". Canada contends that an <a href="/wiki/Equidistance_principle" title="Equidistance principle">equidistance principle</a> does not result in an equitable boundary, because distortion would occur. The coast of Yukon is concave, whereas the coast of Alaska is convex; and thus an equidistance principle would result in a significant extension of the U.S. possession.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JohnstonSaunders1988_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-JohnstonSaunders1988-14">[14]</a></sup> This dispute has taken on increased significance due to the possible presence of natural reserves within the wedge,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rothwell1996_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-Rothwell1996-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> which according to Canada's <a href="/wiki/National_Energy_Board" title="National Energy Board">National Energy Board</a> may contain 1,700,000,000 m<sup>3</sup> (6.0<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>10</sup> cu ft) of gas, which would cover the national consumption for 20 years, and more than 1,000,000,000 m<sup>3</sup> (3.5<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>10</sup> cu ft) of oil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-13">[13]</a></sup> Because of this, Canada argues that "special circumstances" apply to this border, a position that the U.S. rejects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JohnstonSaunders1988_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-JohnstonSaunders1988-14">[14]</a></sup> This dispute is in this respect a mirror image of the dispute between the U.S. and Canada over the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Maine" title="Gulf of Maine">Gulf of Maine</a>, where the U.S. argued for "special circumstances" and Canada argued for the equidistance principle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JohnstonSaunders1988_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-JohnstonSaunders1988-14">[14]</a></sup> (It is to be noted that in that latter dispute, both of those arguments were rejected, and the border was drawn based upon geometric principles taking into account geographic factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JohnstonSaunders1988_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-JohnstonSaunders1988-14">[14]</a></sup>) Neither the U.S. nor Canada have pressed for a swift resolution for the matter, or arbitration at the International Court of Justice, however;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rothwell1996_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-Rothwell1996-11">[11]</a></sup> and the two have in the meantime cooperated in several measures aimed at preserving the maritime environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rothwell1996_11-7"><a href="#cite_note-Rothwell1996-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JohnstonSaunders1988_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-JohnstonSaunders1988-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before the end of 2004, the US leased eight <a href="/wiki/Scientific_visualization" title="Scientific visualization">plots</a> of land below the water for oil exploration and exploitation, provoking a diplomatic protest from Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ins_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ins-17">[17]</a></sup> On 20 August 2009, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Commerce" title="United States Secretary of Commerce">United States Secretary of Commerce</a> <a href="/wiki/Gary_Locke" title="Gary Locke">Gary Locke</a> announced a <a href="/wiki/Moratorium_on_commercial_fishing_of_the_Beaufort_Sea" title="Moratorium on commercial fishing of the Beaufort Sea">moratorium on commercial fishing of the Beaufort Sea</a> north of Alaska, including the disputed waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fish2009-08-20_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fish2009-08-20-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CanadaCom2009-09-14_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-CanadaCom2009-09-14-19">[19]</a></sup> In July 2010, US–Canada negotiations have started in Ottawa with the next meeting planned in 2011.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (April 2014)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup> A joint geological survey of the area has been initiated, and the issue was marked as settled by the <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">CIA</a> <a href="/wiki/The_World_Factbook" title="The World Factbook">World Factbook</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CIAfactIssues_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-CIAfactIssues-20">[20]</a></sup> though no official document has been released by September 2010.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beaufort_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BeaufortSeaBathymetry.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="369" data-file-width="359" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/BeaufortSeaBathymetry.jpg/220px-BeaufortSeaBathymetry.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/BeaufortSeaBathymetry.jpg/330px-BeaufortSeaBathymetry.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/27/BeaufortSeaBathymetry.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BeaufortSeaBathymetry.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Topography of the Beaufort Sea area.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Several rivers such as the <a href="/wiki/Kongakut_River" title="Kongakut River">Kongakut River</a> in Alaska and the Firth River in Yukon empty into the Beaufort. The major river to flow into the sea is the Mackenzie, Canada's longest, which empties into the Canadian part of the sea, west of Tuktoyaktuk. The <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">coastal shelf</a> area is rather narrow, especially near and east of Point Barrow in the Alaskan part of the sea, and contains numerous <a href="/wiki/Submarine_canyon" title="Submarine canyon">submarine valleys</a>. It becomes wider near the delta of the Mackenzie River but nowhere exceeds 145 km (90 mi). Near the coast, the depths are shallower than 60 m (200 ft) but they rapidly increase northwards up to a few kilometers, transforming into a massive platform which is geologically similar to that of the oceans. There are many small islands in the sea and in the delta of the Mackenzie River. A few larger ones lie west of the Mackenzie River, such as <a href="/wiki/Herschel_Island" title="Herschel Island">Herschel Island</a> (4 km (2.5 mi) off the shore, area 18 km<sup>2</sup> (6.9 sq mi)) and <a href="/wiki/Barter_Island" title="Barter Island">Barter Island</a> (0.3 km (0.19 mi) from the coast, area 13 km<sup>2</sup> (5.0 sq mi)). The coasts are low, with the maximum elevations between 250 and 750 m (820 and 2,460 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-brit-3">[3]</a></sup> The soil is frozen all year around at the depth below about 1 m (3 ft 3 in) or less, forming <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a>, and only top few tens centimeters thaws in summer. Consequently, buildings have to be elevated above ground on wooden piles that are immersed into the permafrost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britM_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-britM-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrology_and_climate">Hydrology and climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beaufort_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hydrology and climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sea_ice_terrain.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1160" data-file-width="1716" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Sea_ice_terrain.jpg/220px-Sea_ice_terrain.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Sea_ice_terrain.jpg/330px-Sea_ice_terrain.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Sea_ice_terrain.jpg/440px-Sea_ice_terrain.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sea_ice_terrain.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">Sea ice</a> pressure ridges in the Beaufort Sea off the northern coast of Alaska (spring 1949).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Beaufort Sea is frozen over through the year, except for August and September when the ice breaks near the coast and opens what was once a 50–100 km (31–62 mi) wide strip of open water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bse_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-bse-2">[2]</a></sup> During the 2000s, due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_the_Arctic" title="Climate change in the Arctic">climate change in the Arctic</a>, the ice-free area in late summer greatly enlarged. During the record minimum extent of Arctic sea ice in September, 2012, the sea ice boundary had retreated northward much farther than normal from the coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsidc_2012_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-nsidc_2012-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anderson_2012_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-anderson_2012-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_2012_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_2012-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The channels of the Mackenzie River thaw earlier, in late May–early June. This thawing increases the average water discharge from about 150,000 to 250,000 m<sup>3</sup>/s (5,300,000 to 8,800,000 cu ft/s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britM_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-britM-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hidden changes in the ice cover of the Beaufort Sea were discovered in 2009. Whereas the ice area remain stable, as detected by the observation satellites, so as the associated water temperature and <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a>, the ice structure has changed recently. The new ice, called <a href="/wiki/Rotten_ice" title="Rotten ice">rotten ice</a>, is thinner and much weaker structurally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sea water has a stable temperature and is separated into four distinct layers as follows. The top 100 m (330 ft) are <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">surface water</a> which has a temperature of −1.4 °C (29.5 °F) in summer and −1.8 °C (28.8 °F) in winter. The next layer is formed by the inflows from the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bering_Sea" title="Bering Sea">Bering Sea</a> coming through the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Strait" title="Bering Strait">Bering Strait</a>; it extends up to the <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">North Pole</a>. The warmest, deep <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> layer has the temperatures between 0 and 1 °C (32 to 34 °F), and water at the bottom is a bit colder at −0.4 to −0.8 °C (31.3 to 30.6 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-brit-3">[3]</a></sup> The average salinity varies between 28‰ and 32‰ (parts per thousand) from south to north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bse_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-bse-2">[2]</a></sup> Typical air temperatures (at Tuktoyaktuk) are −27 °C (−17 °F) in January and 11 °C (52 °F) in July.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Northstar_Offshore_Island_Beaufort_Sea.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Northstar_Offshore_Island_Beaufort_Sea.jpg/220px-Northstar_Offshore_Island_Beaufort_Sea.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Northstar_Offshore_Island_Beaufort_Sea.jpg/330px-Northstar_Offshore_Island_Beaufort_Sea.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Northstar_Offshore_Island_Beaufort_Sea.jpg/440px-Northstar_Offshore_Island_Beaufort_Sea.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Northstar_Offshore_Island_Beaufort_Sea.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Northstar_Island" title="Northstar Island">Northstar Island</a>, an artificial island northwest of Prudhoe Bay, is a site of oil and gas drilling</div>
</div>

<p>The water currents form the clockwise-directed <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_Gyre" title="Beaufort Gyre">Beaufort Gyre</a>, that results in south-westerly and westerly currents near the shores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The Mackenzie River partly affects this circulation inducing minor eastwards streams near its mouth. The river annually brings about 15 million tonnes of sediments which are rich in <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>. Those deposits are spread over the sea and mixed with mud and gravel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-brit-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora_and_fauna">Flora and fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beaufort_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Flora and fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The shoreline of the Beaufort Sea is covered with <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> and the northern limit of the terrestrial range<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> of the <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">polar bear</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. The Mackenzie River is an important habitat for whales and seabirds and is still relatively untouched by commercial traffic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The delta of Mackenzie River contains numerous lakes and ponds which are inhabited by <a href="/wiki/Muskrat" title="Muskrat">muskrat</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britM_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-britM-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sea hosts about 80 species of <a href="/wiki/Zooplankton" title="Zooplankton">zooplankton</a>, more than 70 species of <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a>, and nearly 700 species of <a href="/wiki/Polychaete" title="Polychaete">polychaetes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bryozoa" title="Bryozoa">bryozoans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mollusca" title="Mollusca">mollusks</a>, but their total volume is relatively small owing to the cold climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-brit-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Major fish species include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_cod" title="Polar cod">polar cod</a> (<i>Boreogadus saida</i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_cod" title="Arctic cod">Arctic cod</a> (<i>Arctogadus glacialis</i>), <a href="/wiki/Saffron_cod" title="Saffron cod">saffron cod</a> (<i>Eleginus gracilis</i>), <a href="/wiki/Arctic_char" title="Arctic char">Arctic char</a> (<i>Salvelinus alpinus</i>), chum salmon (<i>Oncorhynchus keta</i>), <a href="/wiki/Arctic_cisco" title="Arctic cisco">Arctic cisco</a> (<i>Coregonus autumnalis</i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Least_cisco" title="Least cisco">least cisco</a> (<i>Coregonus sardinella</i>), <a href="/wiki/Lake_whitefish" title="Lake whitefish">lake whitefish</a> (<i>Coregonus clupeaformis</i>), <a href="/wiki/Broad_whitefish" title="Broad whitefish">broad whitefish</a> (<i>Coregonus nasus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Pacific_herring" title="Pacific herring">Pacific herring</a> (<i>Clupea pallasii</i>), <a href="/wiki/Fourhorn_sculpin" title="Fourhorn sculpin">fourhorn sculpin</a> (<i>Myoxocephalus quadricornis</i>), <a href="/wiki/Stenodus_leucichthys" title="Stenodus leucichthys">inconnu</a> (<i>Stenodus leucichthys</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Flatfish" title="Flatfish">flatfish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EndicottIsland.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1040" data-file-width="1600" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/EndicottIsland.jpg/220px-EndicottIsland.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/EndicottIsland.jpg/330px-EndicottIsland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/EndicottIsland.jpg/440px-EndicottIsland.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EndicottIsland.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Endicott_Island" title="Endicott Island">Endicott Island</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The eastern part of the sea is a major habitat of beluga whales with an estimated population of 39,000.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This population is stable and might even be increasing; it is not affected by the offshore oil exploration in the area.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Belugas spend summer in the coastal area and Mackenzie River delta, which are free of ice then, and in winter migrate log distances to the polynyas of the deep sea.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Genetic analyses have confirmed that belugas of the Beaufort Sea are clearly distinct from those of other Canadian and Alaskan waters, despite they often share a common wintering habitat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The food chain of the Beaufort Sea is relatively simple: It starts with phytoplankton and epontic <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> (single-cell algae associated with the lower interface of sea ice<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>), which provide energy to zooplankton, and epontic and coastal amphipods. The latter serve as a food for seabirds and fish, primarily as polar cod and Arctic char. Polar cod is a major food of Arctic char, beluga, <a href="/wiki/Narwhal" title="Narwhal">narwhal</a>, seabirds and seals, which are dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Bearded_seal" title="Bearded seal">bearded seal</a> (<i>Erignatus barbatus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Ringed_seal" title="Ringed seal">ringed seal</a> (<i>Pusa hispida</i>). Bearded seal and <a href="/wiki/Walrus" title="Walrus">walrus</a> also feed on <a href="/wiki/Benthos" title="Benthos">benthic</a> invertebrates. On top of the food pyramid stands polar bear, which feeds primarily on seals, but also on any large marine mammals when it has a chance, such as carcasses and whales trapped in ice fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p215_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-p215-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_activities">Human activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beaufort_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Human activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:State-of-Alaska-Map.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="576" data-file-width="600" height="288" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/State-of-Alaska-Map.png/300px-State-of-Alaska-Map.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/State-of-Alaska-Map.png/450px-State-of-Alaska-Map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/30/State-of-Alaska-Map.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:State-of-Alaska-Map.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A map showing the Trans-Alaska Pipeline colored in red.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>"There is only one proposed Early-Entry site in eastern Beringia that still has proponents, Bluefish Caves in the Porcupine River Basin, Yukon Territory, Canada. Claims of great antiquity in the area [e.g., 30kya] have a convoluted history. A caribou tibia flesher, the most diagnostic human implement from the Old Crow basin, had been dated at near 28 kya. When redated using accelerator mass spectrometry (AMS) of small amounts of remnant collagen the bone produced a 1.8 kya date (Yesner 1996b:255)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> There is no evidence for anomalous occupation of Beaufort coasts in the context of Arctic cultures generally, including the arrival about 4,000 years ago by <a href="/wiki/Paleo-Eskimo" title="Paleo-Eskimo">Paleo-Eskimos</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Dorset_culture" title="Dorset culture">Dorset culture</a>, around 1,000 years ago by the <a href="/wiki/Thule_people" title="Thule people">Thule</a> and finally by the modern <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a>. From early ages, they practised fishing – bones of Arctic char were found at the 4,000 years old settlements. While originally they lived nomadic life, later, they started to form permanent settlements. Modern Inuit are mostly concentrated near the Mackenzie River delta. Their population is increasing, but the unemployment rate is relatively high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p215_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-p215-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bowhead whales were hunted in the sea between 1888 and 1914. This practice stopped, first because of the decline in whale population and then because of government regulations, but <a href="/wiki/International_Whaling_Commission" title="International Whaling Commission">resumed</a> in the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The major settlements along the Beaufort Sea are Tuktoyaktuk (population 930 in 2009<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-stats-37">[37]</a></sup>) in Canada and <a href="/wiki/Prudhoe_Bay,_Alaska" title="Prudhoe Bay, Alaska">Prudhoe Bay, Alaska</a>. Although Prudhoe Bay is permanently populated by only a few people, there are thousands of contract workers in the area employed on petroleum production at the <a href="/wiki/Prudhoe_Bay_Oil_Field" title="Prudhoe Bay Oil Field">Prudhoe Bay Oil Field</a>, which is on the coastal lowland known as the <a href="/wiki/Alaska_North_Slope" title="Alaska North Slope">North Slope</a>. <a href="/wiki/Artificial_island" title="Artificial island">Artificial islands</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Endicott_Island" title="Endicott Island">Endicott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northstar_Island" title="Northstar Island">Northstar</a>, have been raised near the shores in 1987 and 2001, respectively. The crude oil is transported through the <a href="/wiki/Trans-Alaska_Pipeline_System" title="Trans-Alaska Pipeline System">Trans-Alaska Pipeline System</a> to the southern port of <a href="/wiki/Valdez,_Alaska" title="Valdez, Alaska">Valdez</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-brit-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Beaufort Sea contains major gas and petroleum reserves beneath the seabed, a continuation of proven reserves in the nearby Mackenzie River and North Slope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ins_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-ins-17">[17]</a></sup> The Beaufort Sea was first explored for sub-shelf hydrocarbons in the 1950s and estimated to contain about 250 km<sup>3</sup> (60 cu mi) of oil and 300,000 km<sup>3</sup> (72,000 cu mi) of natural gas under its coastal shelf. Offshore drilling began in 1972; about 70 wells were set up by the 1980s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p233_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-p233-38">[38]</a></sup> and 200 wells by 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dow_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-dow-39">[39]</a></sup> These activities resulted in displacement of about 46.5 km<sup>3</sup> (11.2 cu mi) of the <a href="/wiki/Seabed" title="Seabed">sea bottom</a> soil which contained <a href="/wiki/Baryte" title="Baryte">barite</a>, clay, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">caustic soda</a>, as well as heavy metals zinc, copper, lead, chromium, cobalt, nickel, cadmium and mercury. About 50,400 m<sup>3</sup> (1,780,000 cu ft) of oil was produced in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p233_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-p233-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>A major gas field, named Taglu field, was discovered in the Mackenzie River delta in 1971, followed by the Parson Lake field and Niglintgak field. The estimated gas reserves of these fields are 58,600 km<sup>3</sup> (14,100 cu mi), 35,400 km<sup>3</sup> (8,500 cu mi) and 13,600 km<sup>3</sup> (3,300 cu mi), respectively. Moreover, further into the sea from the Mackenzie delta lies the Amauligak field. This, the largest known oil deposit of the Beaufort Sea, was discovered in 1984, and is estimated to contain 37.3 km<sup>3</sup> (8.9 cu mi) of oil and 38,500 km<sup>3</sup> (9,200 cu mi) of gas. The development of these fields is hindered by their remote location. This problem was alleviated for Prudhoe Bay by constructing the Trans-Alaska Pipeline, but is limiting regular commercial production at Mackenzie River deposits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dow_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-dow-39">[39]</a></sup> For example, the <b>Amauligak Project</b> was started soon after the discovery of the field. In September 1985, the tanker <i>Gulf Beaufort</i> has transported 50,300,000 l (316,377 bbl) of crude oil to Japan, which was the first shipment of oil from the Arctic deposits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> However, the project has stalled after that.</p>
<p>Fishing and sea hunting are practised by the local inhabitants and have no commercial value, especially after a US moratorium on commercial fishing of the Beaufort Sea, adopted in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-brit-3">[3]</a></sup> Trapping of muskrat at the Mackenzie River delta was the main source of income for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athabaskan" title="Athabaskan">Athabaskan</a> <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a> peoples and <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> during 1920–1960, but has since declined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britM_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-britM-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
