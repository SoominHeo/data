<p><b>Pedestrian zones</b> (also known as <b>auto-free zones</b> and <b>car-free zones</b>, and as <b>pedestrian precincts</b> in <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) are areas of a city or town reserved for pedestrian-only use and in which most or all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile_traffic" title="Automobile traffic">automobile traffic</a> may be prohibited. Converting a street or an area to pedestrian-only use is called <i>pedestrianisation</i>. Pedestrianisation usually aims to provide better accessibility and <a href="/wiki/Mobilities" title="Mobilities">mobility</a> for pedestrians, to enhance the volume of shopping and other business activity in the area and/or to improve the attractiveness of the local environment in terms of aesthetics, air pollution, noise and accidents involving pedestrians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chiquetto_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chiquetto-2">[2]</a></sup> However, pedestrianisation can sometimes lead to reductions in business activity, property devaluation, and displacement of economic activity to other areas. In some cases traffic in surrounding areas may increase, due to displacement rather than substitution of car traffic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chiquetto_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chiquetto-2">[2]</a></sup> Nonetheless, pedestrianisation schemes are often associated with significant drops in local air and noise pollution,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chiquetto_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chiquetto-2">[2]</a></sup> accidents, and frequently with increased retail turnover and increased property values locally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> A <i>car-free</i> development generally implies a large scale pedestrianised area that relies on modes of transport other than the car, while pedestrian zones may vary in size from a single square to entire districts, but with highly variable degrees of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car_dependence" title="Car dependence">dependence on cars</a> for their broader transport links.</p>
<p>Pedestrian zones have a great variety of approaches to human-powered vehicles such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bicycles" title="Bicycles">bicycles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inline_skates" title="Inline skates">inline skates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skateboard" title="Skateboard">skateboards</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kick_scooter" title="Kick scooter">kick scooters</a>. Some have a total ban on anything with wheels, others ban certain categories, others segregate the human-powered wheels from foot traffic, and others still have no rules at all. Many Middle Eastern <a href="/wiki/Kasbah" title="Kasbah">kasbahs</a> have no wheeled traffic, but use <a href="/wiki/Donkey" title="Donkey">donkey</a>-driven or hand-driven <a href="/wiki/Cart" title="Cart">carts</a> for freight transport.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The idea of separating pedestrians from wheeled traffic is an old one, dating back at least to the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hall_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-hall-4">[4]</a></sup> However, the earliest modern implementation of the idea in cities seems to date from about 1800, when the first covered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shopping_arcade" title="Shopping arcade">shopping arcade</a> was opened in Paris (see <a href="/wiki/Covered_passages_of_Paris" title="Covered passages of Paris">Covered passages of Paris</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hall_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-hall-4">[4]</a></sup> Separated shopping arcades were constructed throughout Europe in the 19th century, precursors of modern shopping malls. A number of architects and city planners, including <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Paxton" title="Joseph Paxton">Joseph Paxton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ebenezer_Howard" title="Ebenezer Howard">Ebenezer Howard</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Clarence_Stein" title="Clarence Stein">Clarence Stein</a>, in the 19th and early 20th centuries proposed plans to separate pedestrians from traffic in various new developments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hall2_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-hall2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first "pedestrianisation" of an existing street seems to have taken place "around 1929" in <a href="/wiki/Essen" title="Essen">Essen</a>, Germany. This was in a very narrow shopping street that could not accommodate both vehicular and pedestrian traffic. Two other German cities followed this model in the early 1930s, but the idea was not seen outside Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hall_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-hall-4">[4]</a></sup> Following the devastation of the Second World War a number of European cities implemented plans to pedestrianise city streets, although usually on a largely <a href="/wiki/Ad_hoc" title="Ad hoc">ad hoc</a> basis, through the early 1950s, with little landscaping or planning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hall_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-hall-4">[4]</a></sup> By 1955 twenty-one German cities had closed at least one street to traffic, although only four were "true" pedestrian streets, designed for the purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hall_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-hall-4">[4]</a></sup> At this time pedestrianisation was not seen as a <a href="/wiki/Traffic_calming" title="Traffic calming">traffic restraint</a> policy, but rather as a complement<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> to customers who would arrive by car in a city centre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hall_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-hall-4">[4]</a></sup> Pedestrianisation was also common in the United States during the 1950s and 60s as downtown businesses attempted to compete with new suburban shopping malls. However, most of these initiatives were not successful in the long term, and about 90% have been changed back to motorised areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions_and_types">Definitions and types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definitions and types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Car_free_towns.2C_cities_and_regions">Car free towns, cities and regions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Car free towns, cities and regions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>A car-free zone is different from a typical pedestrian zone, in that it implies a development largely predicated on modes of transport other than the car. A pedestrian zone may be much more limited in scope, for example a single square or street being for pedestrians, but largely serviced by cars.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A number of towns and cities in Europe have never allowed <a href="/wiki/Motor_vehicle" title="Motor vehicle">motor vehicles</a>. <a href="/wiki/Archetype" title="Archetype">archetypal</a> examples are:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Venice#Transport" title="Venice">Venice</a>, which occupies many islands in a lagoon, divided by and accessed from <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canals</a>. The city has been car-free for more than three decades. Motor traffic stops at the car park at the head of the viaduct from the mainland, and water transport or walking takes over from there. However, motor vehicles are allowed on the nearby <a href="/wiki/Lido_di_Venezia" title="Lido di Venezia">Lido</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Zermatt" title="Zermatt">Zermatt</a> in the Swiss Alps, which most visitors reach by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cog_railway" title="Cog railway">cog railway</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Other examples are:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cinque_Terre" title="Cinque Terre">Cinque Terre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ghent" title="Ghent">Ghent</a> in Belgium, one of the largest car-free areas in Europe</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhodes,_Greece" title="Rhodes, Greece">The Old Town of Rhodes</a>, where many, if not most, of the streets are too steep and/or narrow for car traffic.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mount_Athos" title="Mount Athos">Mount Athos</a>, an autonomous monastic state under the <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereignty</a> of <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, does not permit automobiles on its territory. Trucks and work-related vehicles only are in use there.</li>
<li>The medieval city of <a href="/wiki/Mdina" title="Mdina">Mdina</a> in <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a> does not allow automobiles past the city walls. It is known as the "Silent City" because of the absence of motor traffic in the city.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sark" title="Sark">Sark</a>, an island in the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>, is a car-free zone where only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bicycles" title="Bicycles">bicycles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carriages" title="Carriages">carriages</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tractors" title="Tractors">tractors</a> are used as transportation.</li>
</ul>
<p>To assist with transport from the car parks in at the edge of car-free cities, there are often bus stations, bicycle sharing stations, and the like.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Car-free_development">Car-free development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Car-free development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The term car-free <i>development</i> implies a physical change: either new building or changes to an existing built area.</p>
<p>Melia <i>et al.</i> (2010)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-melia_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-melia-7">[7]</a></sup> define car-free developments as residential or mixed use developments which:</p>
<ul>
<li>Normally provide a traffic-free immediate environment, and</li>
<li>Offer no parking or limited parking separated from the residence, and</li>
<li>Are designed to enable residents to live without owning a car.</li>
</ul>
<p>This definition (which they distinguish from the more common "low car development") is based mainly on experience in North West Europe, where the movement for car-free development began. Within this definition, three types are identified:</p>
<ul>
<li>Vauban model</li>
<li>Limited Access model</li>
<li>Pedestrianised centres with residential population</li>
</ul>

</div>
</div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Limited_Access_type">Limited Access type</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Limited Access type">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The more common form of carfree development involves some sort of physical barrier, which prevents motor vehicles from penetrating into a car-free interior. Melia <i>et al.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-melia_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-melia-7">[7]</a></sup> describe this as the "Limited Access" type. In some cases, such as Stellwerk 60 in <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>, there is a removable barrier, controlled by a residents' organisation. In others such as <a href="/wiki/Waterwijk_(Amsterdam)" title="Waterwijk (Amsterdam)">Waterwijk (Amsterdam)</a>, vehicular access is only available from the exterior.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pedestrianised_centres">Pedestrianised centres</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pedestrianised centres">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>Whereas the first two models apply to newly-built car-free developments, most pedestrianised city, town and district centres have been retro-fitted. Pedestrianised centres may be considered car-free developments where they include a significant number of residents, mostly without cars, due to new residential development within them, or because they already included dwellings when they were pedestrianised. The largest example in Europe is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Groningen_(city)" title="Groningen (city)">Groningen</a>, with a city centre population of 16,500.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics_and_benefits_of_carfree_developments">Characteristics and benefits of carfree developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Characteristics and benefits of carfree developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several studies have been carried out on European carfree developments. The most comprehensive was conducted in 2000 by Jan Scheurer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scheurer_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-scheurer-9">[9]</a></sup> Other more recent studies have been made of specific car-free areas such as Vienna's <a href="/wiki/Floridsdorf" title="Floridsdorf">Floridsdorf</a> car-free development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Characteristics of car-free developments:</p>
<ul>
<li>Very low levels of car use, resulting in much less traffic on surrounding roads</li>
<li>High rates of walking and cycling</li>
<li>More independent movement and active play for children</li>
<li>Less land taken by parking and roads, so more available for green or social space</li>
</ul>
<p>The main benefits found for car-free developments:</p>
<ul>
<li>Low atmospheric emissions</li>
<li>Low road accident rates</li>
<li>Better built environment conditions<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Encouragement of active modes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The main problems related to parking management. Where parking is not controlled in the surrounding area, this often results in complaints from neighbours about <a href="/wiki/Overspill_parking" title="Overspill parking">overspill parking</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Problems_caused_by_pedestrianisation">Problems caused by pedestrianisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Problems caused by pedestrianisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:National_Gallery_2004.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/National_Gallery_2004.jpg/220px-National_Gallery_2004.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/National_Gallery_2004.jpg/330px-National_Gallery_2004.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/National_Gallery_2004.jpg/440px-National_Gallery_2004.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:National_Gallery_2004.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_National_Gallery" title="The National Gallery">The National Gallery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trafalgar_Square" title="Trafalgar Square">Trafalgar Square</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There were calls for traffic to be reinstated in <a href="/wiki/Trafalgar_Square" title="Trafalgar Square">Trafalgar Square</a>, London, after pedestrianisation caused <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">noise nuisance</a> for visitors to the <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery" title="National Gallery">National Gallery</a>. The director of the gallery is reported to have blamed pedestrianisation for the "trashing of a civic space".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Local shopkeepers may be critical of the effect of pedestrianisation on their businesses. Reduced through traffic can lead to fewer customers using local businesses, depending on the environment and the area's dependence on the through traffic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bielsko-Bia%C5%82a,_11_Listopada_2.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1536" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Bielsko-Bia%C5%82a%2C_11_Listopada_2.JPG/220px-Bielsko-Bia%C5%82a%2C_11_Listopada_2.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Bielsko-Bia%C5%82a%2C_11_Listopada_2.JPG/330px-Bielsko-Bia%C5%82a%2C_11_Listopada_2.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Bielsko-Bia%C5%82a%2C_11_Listopada_2.JPG/440px-Bielsko-Bia%C5%82a%2C_11_Listopada_2.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bielsko-Bia%C5%82a,_11_Listopada_2.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Promenade in <a href="/wiki/Bielsko-Bia%C5%82a" title="Bielsko-Biała">Bielsko-Biała</a>, Poland</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The term "pedestrianised zone" is used in <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>, and most other European countries use a similar term (French: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>zone piétonne</i></span>, German: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Fußgängerzone</i></span>, Spanish: <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><i>zona peatonal</i></span>, Italian: <span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><i>area pedonale</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <span lang="nl" xml:lang="nl">'<b>voetgangerszone'</b></span>).</p>
<p>The first purpose-built pedestrian street in Europe is the <a href="/wiki/Lijnbaan" title="Lijnbaan">Lijnbaan</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rotterdam" title="Rotterdam">Rotterdam</a>, opened in 1953. The first pedestrianised shopping centre in the United Kingdom was in <a href="/wiki/Stevenage" title="Stevenage">Stevenage</a> in 1959.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/List_of_car-free_places" title="List of car-free places">large number of European towns and cities</a> have made part of their centres car-free since the early 1960s. These are often accompanied by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car_park" title="Car park">car parks</a> on the edge of the pedestrianised zone, and, in the larger cases, <a href="/wiki/Park_and_ride" title="Park and ride">park and ride</a> schemes. Central <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a> is one of the largest and oldest: it was converted from car traffic into a pedestrian zone in 1962 as an experiment, and is centered on <a href="/wiki/Str%C3%B8get" title="Strøget">Strøget</a>, which is not a single street but a series of interconnected avenues which create a very large pedestrian zone, although it is crossed in places by streets with vehicular traffic. Most of these zones allow delivery trucks to service the businesses there during the early morning, and street-cleaning vehicles will usually go through these streets after most shops have closed for the night.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SparksStreetatBank.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="414" data-file-width="565" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/SparksStreetatBank.jpg/220px-SparksStreetatBank.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/SparksStreetatBank.jpg/330px-SparksStreetatBank.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/SparksStreetatBank.jpg/440px-SparksStreetatBank.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SparksStreetatBank.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sparks_Street_Mall" title="Sparks Street Mall">Sparks Street Mall</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In North America, where a more commonly used term is pedestrian mall, such areas are still in their infancy. Few cities have pedestrian zones, but some have pedestrianized single streets. Some cities have <a href="/wiki/Transit_mall" title="Transit mall">transit malls</a>. Many pedestrian streets are surfaced with <a href="/wiki/Cobblestone" title="Cobblestone">cobblestones</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Sidewalk" title="Sidewalk">pavement</a> <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">bricks</a>, thus discouraging any kind of wheeled traffic, including wheelchairs. They are rarely completely free of motor vehicles. Often, all of the cross streets are open to motorized traffic, which thus intrudes on the pedestrian flow at every street corner. In a few pedestrian streets with no cross street cars or trucks, deliveries are made by trucks by night.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some Canadian examples are the <a href="/wiki/Sparks_Street" title="Sparks Street">Sparks Street</a> Mall area of <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Distillery_District" title="Distillery District">Distillery District</a> in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, Scarth Street Mall in <a href="/wiki/Regina,_Saskatchewan" title="Regina, Saskatchewan">Regina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Avenue" title="Stephen Avenue">Stephen Avenue</a> Mall in <a href="/wiki/Calgary" title="Calgary">Calgary</a> (with certain areas open to parking for permit holders) and part of Prince Arthur Street and the <a href="/wiki/Gay_Village,_Montreal" title="Gay Village, Montreal">Gay Village</a> in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>. Algonquin and Ward's Islands, parts of the <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Islands" title="Toronto Islands">Toronto Islands</a> group, are also car-free zones for all 700 residents. Since summer 2004, Toronto has also been experimenting with "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedestrian_Sunday" title="Pedestrian Sunday">Pedestrian Sundays</a>"<a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.pskensington.ca/" rel="nofollow">[3]</a> in its busy <a href="/wiki/Kensington_Market" title="Kensington Market">Kensington Market</a>. <a href="/wiki/Granville_Mall_(Halifax)" title="Granville Mall (Halifax)">Granville Mall</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halifax_Urban_Area" title="Halifax Urban Area">Halifax, Nova Scotia</a> was a run-down section of buildings on Granville Street built in the 1840s that was restored in the late 1970s. The area was then closed off to vehicles.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pedestrian_malls_in_the_United_States" title="Pedestrian malls in the United States">Pedestrian malls in the United States</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:M185mackinac.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="618" data-file-width="907" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/M185mackinac.jpg/220px-M185mackinac.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/M185mackinac.jpg/330px-M185mackinac.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/M185mackinac.jpg/440px-M185mackinac.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:M185mackinac.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Bicycle" title="Bicycle">Cyclists</a> enjoy the carfree highway (<a href="/wiki/M-185_(Michigan_highway)" title="M-185 (Michigan highway)">M-185</a>) on <a href="/wiki/Mackinac_Island" title="Mackinac Island">Mackinac Island</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, these zones are commonly called pedestrian malls or pedestrian streets. Pedestrian zones are rare in the United States, although some cities have created single pedestrian streets.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mackinac_Island" title="Mackinac Island">Mackinac Island</a>, between the upper and lower peninsulas of Michigan, banned <a href="/wiki/Horseless_carriage" title="Horseless carriage">horseless carriages</a> in 1896, making it auto-free. The original ban still stands, except for emergency vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Travel on the island is largely by foot, bicycle, or horse-drawn carriage. An 8-mile (13 km) road, <a href="/wiki/M-185_(Michigan_highway)" title="M-185 (Michigan highway)">M-185</a> rings the island, and numerous roads cover the interior. M-185 is the only highway in the United States without motorized vehicles. In 1959, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kalamazoo" title="Kalamazoo">Kalamazoo</a> was the first American city to implement a "pedestrian mall" in its downtown core.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robertson_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Robertson-14">[14]</a></sup> This became a popular method for downtowns to compete with the growing suburban shopping malls of the time. In the 1960s and 70s over 200 towns in the United States adopted this approach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robertson_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Robertson-14">[14]</a></sup> The idea of exclusive pedestrian zones lost popularity through the 1980s and into the 1990s.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Downtown_Crossing" title="Downtown Crossing">Downtown Crossing</a> in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> is a shopping district which prohibits automobiles during daytime hours. Both the main thoroughfare of <a href="/wiki/Golden_Gate_Park" title="Golden Gate Park">Golden Gate Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, and Memorial Drive, a busy road in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambridge,_MA" title="Cambridge, MA">Cambridge, MA</a> are closed to car traffic each Sunday during the summer to allow pedestrians, bikers, skateboarders and roller/inlineskaters an opportunity to use the road.</p>
<p>A number of streets and malls in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> are now pedestrian-only, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sixth_and_a_Half_Avenue" title="Sixth and a Half Avenue">6 1/2 Avenue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fulton_Street_(Brooklyn)" title="Fulton Street (Brooklyn)">Fulton Street</a>, parts of <a href="/wiki/Broadway_(Manhattan)" title="Broadway (Manhattan)">Broadway</a>, and a block of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/25th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="25th Street (Manhattan)">25th Street</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fire_Island" title="Fire Island">Fire Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/Suffolk_County,_New_York" title="Suffolk County, New York">Suffolk County, New York</a> is pedestrianised east of the <a href="/wiki/Fire_Island_Lighthouse" title="Fire Island Lighthouse">Fire Island Lighthouse</a> and west of <a href="/wiki/Smith_Point_County_Park" title="Smith Point County Park">Smith Point County Park</a> (with the exception of emergency vehicles).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Supai,_Arizona" title="Supai, Arizona">Supai, Arizona</a>, located within the <a href="/wiki/Havasupai_Indian_Reservation" title="Havasupai Indian Reservation">Havasupai Indian Reservation</a> is entirely car-free, the only community in the United States where mail is still carried out by mule. Supai is located eight miles from the nearest road, and is accessible only by foot, horse/mule, or helicopter.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/State_Street_(Madison)" title="State Street (Madison)">State Street</a> in <a href="/wiki/Madison,_Wisconsin" title="Madison, Wisconsin">Madison, Wisconsin</a> is the largest pedestrian mall in Wisconsin and connects the University of Wisconsin to Capitol Square in Downtown Madison. The street houses some of Madison's oldest buildings and stores, as well as theaters such as the Orpheum and the Overture Center for the Arts.</p>
<p>A portion of <a href="/wiki/Third_Street_Promenade" title="Third Street Promenade">Third Street</a> in Santa Monica, California was converted into a pedestrian mall in the 1960s to become what is now the Third Street Promenade, a very popular shopping district located just a few blocks from the beach and <a href="/wiki/Santa_Monica_Pier" title="Santa Monica Pier">Santa Monica Pier</a>.</p>
<p>Lincoln Road in Miami Beach, which had previously been a shopping street with traffic, was converted into a pedestrian only street in 1960. The designer was Morris Lapidus. Lincoln Road Mall is now one of the main attractions in Miami Beach.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexico">Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Playa del Carmen has a pedestrian mall the 5 ave. that stretches 4 km.and receives 4 million visitors annually with hundreds of shops and restaurants</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="South_America">South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Argentina">Argentina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Argentina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Buenos_Aires_-_Retiro_-_Calle_Florida.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Buenos_Aires_-_Retiro_-_Calle_Florida.jpg/220px-Buenos_Aires_-_Retiro_-_Calle_Florida.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Buenos_Aires_-_Retiro_-_Calle_Florida.jpg/330px-Buenos_Aires_-_Retiro_-_Calle_Florida.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Buenos_Aires_-_Retiro_-_Calle_Florida.jpg/440px-Buenos_Aires_-_Retiro_-_Calle_Florida.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<a href="/wiki/Florida_Street" title="Florida Street">Florida Street</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>, Argentina</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Argentina's big cities; <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba,_Argentina" title="Córdoba, Argentina">Córdoba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mendoza,_Argentina" title="Mendoza, Argentina">Mendoza</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rosario,_Argentina" title="Rosario, Argentina">Rosario</a> have lively pedestrianised street centers (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><i>peatonales</i></span>) combined with <a href="/wiki/Town_square" title="Town square">town squares</a> and parks which are crowded with people walking at every hour of the day and night. Most (if not all) of Argentina's cities are human-scale and pedestrian-friendly, although vehicle traffic may be hectic in some areas.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>, some stretches of <a href="/wiki/Florida_Street" title="Florida Street">Calle Florida</a> have been pedestrianised since 1913,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> which makes it one of the oldest car-free thoroughfares in the world today. Pedestrianised Florida, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lavalle_Street&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lavalle Street (page does not exist)">Lavalle</a> and other streets contribute to a vibrant shopping and restaurant scene where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Busking" title="Busking">street performers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tango_(dance)" title="Tango (dance)">tango</a> dancers abound, streets are crossed with vehicular traffic at <a href="/wiki/Chamfer" title="Chamfer">chamfered corners</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Paquet%C3%A1_Island" title="Paquetá Island">Paquetá Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> is auto-free. The only cars allowed on the island are police and ambulance vehicles. In Rio de Janeiro, the roads beside the beaches are auto-free on Sundays and holidays.</p>
<p>Downtown Rio de Janeiro, Ouvidor Street, over almost its entire length, has been continually a pedestrian space since the mid-nineteenth century when not even carts or carriages were allowed. And the Saara District, also downtown, consists of some dozen or more blocks of colonial streets, off limits to cars, and crowded with daytime shoppers. Likewise, many of the city's hillside favelas are effectively pedestrian zones as the streets are too narrow and/or steep for automobiles.</p>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eixo_Rodovi%C3%A1rio&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eixo Rodoviário (page does not exist)">Eixo Rodoviário</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Bras%C3%ADlia" title="Brasília">Brasília</a>, which is 13 kilometers long and 30 meters wide and is an <a href="/wiki/Arterial_road" title="Arterial road">arterial road</a> connecting the center of that city from both southward and northward wings of Brasília's <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Plano_Piloto&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Plano Piloto (page does not exist)">Plano Piloto</a>, perpendicular to the well known Eixo Monumental (<a href="/wiki/Monumental_Axis" title="Monumental Axis">Monumental Axis</a> in English), is auto-free on Sundays and holidays.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rua_XV_de_Novembro" title="Rua XV de Novembro">Rua XV de Novembro</a> (15 November Street) in <a href="/wiki/Curitiba" title="Curitiba">Curitiba</a> is one of the first major pedestrian streets in Brazil.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chile">Chile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Chile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Chile has many large pedestrian streets. An example is <a href="/wiki/Paseo_Ahumada" title="Paseo Ahumada">Paseo Ahumada</a> in Santiago and Calle Valparaíso in Viña del Mar.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colombia">Colombia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Colombia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During his 1998–2001 term, the former <a href="/wiki/Bogot%C3%A1" title="Bogotá">Bogotá</a> mayor, U.S.-born <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Pe%C3%B1alosa" title="Enrique Peñalosa">Enrique Peñalosa</a>, created several pedestrian streets, <a href="/wiki/Plaza" title="Plaza">plazas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bogot%C3%A1%27s_Bike_Paths_Network" title="Bogotá's Bike Paths Network">bike paths</a> integrated with a new <a href="/wiki/TransMilenio" title="TransMilenio">bus rapid transit system</a>.</p>
<p>The historic center of <a href="/wiki/Cartagena,_Colombia" title="Cartagena, Colombia">Cartagena</a> closes some streets to cars during certain hours.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Santa_Marta" title="Santa Marta">Santa Marta</a> also has permanent pedestrian zones in the historic center around the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cathedral_Bas%C3%ADlica_of_Santa_Marta&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cathedral Basílica of Santa Marta (page does not exist)">Cathedral Basílica of Santa Marta</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Peru">Peru</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Peru">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jirón de La Unión in Lima is a traditional pedestrian street located in the Historic Centre of Lima, part of the capital of Peru.</p>
<p>In the city of Arequipa, Mercaderes is also a considerably large pedestrian street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Also, recently three of the four streets surrounding the city`s main square or "Plaza de Armas" were also made pedestrian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aleppo-suq-Alp.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Aleppo-suq-Alp.JPG/220px-Aleppo-suq-Alp.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Aleppo-suq-Alp.JPG/330px-Aleppo-suq-Alp.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Aleppo-suq-Alp.JPG/440px-Aleppo-suq-Alp.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Vehicles have been banned in the town of <a href="/wiki/Matheran" title="Matheran">Matheran</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Maharashtra" title="Maharashtra">Maharashtra</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> since the time it was discovered in 1854.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMRDAmatheran_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-MMRDAmatheran-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, since 2000, the government has been implementing full-time or part-time pedestrian streets in a number of areas, including Causeway Bay, Central, Wan Chai, Mong Kok, and Tsim Sha Tsui.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The most popular pedestrian street is <a href="/wiki/Sai_Yeung_Choi_Street" title="Sai Yeung Choi Street">Sai Yeung Choi Street</a>. It was converted into a pedestrian street in 2003. From December 2008 to May 2009, there were three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mong_Kok_acid_attacks" title="Mong Kok acid attacks">acid attacks</a> during which corrosive liquids were placed in plastic bottles and thrown from the roof of apartments down onto the street.</p>

<a href="/wiki/Sai_Yeung_Choi_Street" title="Sai Yeung Choi Street">Sai Yeung Choi Street</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>.</div>
</div>

<p>Pedestrian zones in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> are called <i>hokōsha tengoku</i> (歩行者天国, literally "pedestrian heaven"). Clis Road, in <a href="/wiki/Sendai" title="Sendai">Sendai</a>, Japan, is a covered pedestrian mall, as is <a href="/wiki/Hond%C5%8Dri" title="Hondōri">Hondōri</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hiroshima" title="Hiroshima">Hiroshima</a>. Several major streets in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> are closed to vehicles during weekends. One particular temporary <i>hokōsha tengoku</i> in <a href="/wiki/Akihabara" title="Akihabara">Akihabara</a> was cancelled after the <a href="/wiki/Akihabara_massacre" title="Akihabara massacre">Akihabara massacre</a> in which a man rammed a truck into the pedestrian traffic and subsequently stabbed more than 12 people.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanjing_Road_(Shanghai)" title="Nanjing Road (Shanghai)">Nanjing Road</a> in Shanghai is perhaps the most well-known pedestrian zone in <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">mainland China</a>. <a href="/wiki/Wangfujing" title="Wangfujing">Wangfujing</a> is a famous tourist and retail oriented pedestrian zone in <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chunxilu" title="Chunxilu">Chunxilu</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chengdu" title="Chengdu">Chengdu</a> is the most well known in western China. Dongmen is the busiest business zone in <a href="/wiki/Shenzhen" title="Shenzhen">Shenzhen</a>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insadong" title="Insadong">Insadong</a> in <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> has a large pedestrian zone (<i>Insadong-gil</i>) during certain hours.</p>
<p>Also in South Korea, in 2013, in the Haenggun-dong neighbourhood of <a href="/wiki/Suwon" title="Suwon">Suwon</a>, streets were closed to cars as a month-long car-free experiment while the city hosted the <a href="/wiki/EcoMobility" title="EcoMobility">EcoMobility</a> World Festival. Instead of cars, residents used non-motorized vehicles provided by the festival organizers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The experiment was not unopposed; however, on balance it was considered a success. Following the festival, the city embarked on discussions about adopting the practice on a permanent basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ximending" title="Ximending">Ximending</a> in <a href="/wiki/Taipei" title="Taipei">Taipei</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> is a neighborhood and shopping district in the Wanhua District of Taipei,Taiwan. It was the first pedestrian zone in Taiwan. The district is very popular in Taiwan.</p>
<p>In India, a citizens’ initiative in Goa state, has made 18 June Road, Panjim’s main shopping boulevard a Non-Motorised Zone <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>(NoMoZo). The road is converted into a NoMoZo for half a day on one Sunday every month.</p>
<p>In Pune, Maharashtra, similar efforts have been made to convert M.G. Road (a.k.a. Main Street) into an open-air mall. The project in questioned aimed to create a so-called "Walking Plaza".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, some small streets (Soi) in <a href="/wiki/Bangkok" title="Bangkok">Bangkok</a> are designed to be all-time closed traffic, the city's famous shopping streets of Sampheng Lane in Chinatown or <a href="/wiki/Yaowarat_Road" title="Yaowarat Road">Yaowarat Road</a> and Wang Lang Market nearby to <a href="/wiki/Siriraj_Hospital" title="Siriraj Hospital">Siriraj Hospital</a>, are the most popular for both local and tourists shopping streets.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> contains some of the largest auto-free areas in the world. Fes-al-Bali, a <a href="/wiki/Medina_quarter" title="Medina quarter">medina</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fes" title="Fes">Fes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, with its population of 156,000, may be the world's largest contiguous completely carfree area, and the medinas of <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Casablanca" title="Casablanca">Casablanca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meknes" title="Meknes">Meknes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Essaouira" title="Essaouira">Essaouira</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tangier" title="Tangier">Tangier</a> are quite extensive.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, as in the US, these zones are commonly called pedestrian malls and in most cases comprise only one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedestrian_street" title="Pedestrian street">street</a>. Most pedestrian streets were created in the late 1970s and 1980s, the first being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_Walk" title="City Walk">City Walk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Garema_Place" title="Garema Place">Garema Place</a> in Canberra in 1971. Of 58 pedestrian streets created in Australia in the last quarter of the 20th century, 48 remain today, ten having re-introduced car access between 1990 and 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> All capital cities in Australia have at least one pedestrian street of which most central are: <a href="/wiki/Pitt_Street_Mall" title="Pitt Street Mall">Pitt Street Mall</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martin_Place,_Sydney" title="Martin Place, Sydney">Martin Place</a> in Sydney, <a href="/wiki/Bourke_Street,_Melbourne" title="Bourke Street, Melbourne">Bourke Street Mall</a> in Melbourne, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen_Street_Mall" title="Queen Street Mall">Queen Street Mall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brunswick_Street_Mall" title="Brunswick Street Mall">Brunswick Street Mall</a> in Brisbane, <a href="/wiki/Rundle_Mall" title="Rundle Mall">Rundle Mall</a> in Adelaide, <a href="/wiki/Hay_Street,_Perth" title="Hay Street, Perth">Hay Street</a> and <a href="/wiki/Murray_Street,_Perth" title="Murray Street, Perth">Murray Street</a> Malls in Perth, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Street_Mall" title="Elizabeth Street Mall">Elizabeth Street Mall</a> in Hobart, <a href="/wiki/City_Walk,_Canberra" title="City Walk, Canberra">City Walk</a> in Canberra, and <a href="/wiki/Smith_Street,_Darwin" title="Smith Street, Darwin">Smith Street</a> in Darwin. Many other mid-sized and regional Australian cities also feature pedestrian malls, examples include Langtree Avenue <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mildura,_Victoria" title="Mildura, Victoria">Mildura</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cavill_Avenue" title="Cavill Avenue">Cavill Avenue</a> <a href="/wiki/Gold_Coast,_Queensland" title="Gold Coast, Queensland">Gold Coast</a>, Bridge Street <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ballarat,_Victoria" title="Ballarat, Victoria">Ballarat</a>, Nicholas Street <a href="/wiki/Ipswich,_Queensland" title="Ipswich, Queensland">Ipswich</a>, Hargreaves Street <a href="/wiki/Bendigo" title="Bendigo">Bendigo</a>, Maude Street <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shepparton,_Victoria" title="Shepparton, Victoria">Shepparton</a> and Little Mallop Street <a href="/wiki/Geelong" title="Geelong">Geelong</a>.</p>

<p>Empircial studies by <a href="/wiki/Jan_Gehl" title="Jan Gehl">Jan Gehl</a> indicate an increase of pedestrian traffic as result of public domain improvements in the centres of Melbourne with 39% increase between 1994-2004<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gehl_Melbourne_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gehl_Melbourne-26">[26]</a></sup> and Perth with 13% increase between 1993-2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gehl_Perth_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gehl_Perth-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most intensive pedestrian traffic flows on a summer weekday have been recorded in Bourke Street Mall Melbourne with 81,000 pedestrians (2004),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gehl_Melbourne_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gehl_Melbourne-26">[26]</a></sup> Rundle Mall Adelaide with 61,360 pedestrians (2002), Pitt Street Mall Sydney with 58,140 (2007) and Murray Street Mall Perth with 48,350 pedestrians (2009).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gehl_Perth_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gehl_Perth-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The island of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rottnest" title="Rottnest">Rottnest</a> off Perth is a car free island, only allowing vehicles for essential services. The main form of transport on the island is bicycles, which can be hired or be taken on the ferry.</p>
<p>In Melbourne's north-eastern suburbs, there have been many proposals to make the <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_Hill" title="Doncaster Hill">Doncaster Hill</a> development area a pedestrian zone. If the proposals are passed, the zone could be one of the largest in the world, by area.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedestrian_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, It is becoming more common to use public transportation against cars, <a href="/wiki/Queenstown,_New_Zealand" title="Queenstown, New Zealand">Queenstown</a> - Being a prominent pedestrianised area, Including multiple lanes and streets inside the main blocks. Queenstown Lakes District Council and affiliated groups including Destination Queenstown and Downtown Queenstown propose adding another stretch into a car free zone. This continuing with large grassed settings along the full pedestrianised promenade. <a href="/wiki/Auckland" title="Auckland">Auckland</a> Is also coming to pedestrianised malls and streets, keeping its natural beauty for next generations to enjoy.</p>
