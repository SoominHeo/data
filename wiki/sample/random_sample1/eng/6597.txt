<p>The <b>Kingdom of Bohemia</b>, sometimes in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> literature referred to as the <b>Czech Kingdom</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brandshaw_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-brandshaw-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-castle_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-castle-3">[3]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a>: <span lang="cs" xml:lang="cs"><i>České království</i></span>; <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Königreich Böhmen</i></span>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Regnum Bohemiae</i></span>, sometimes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Regnum Czechorum</i></span>), was a <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Czech_lands_in_the_High_Middle_Ages" title="History of the Czech lands in the High Middle Ages">medieval</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Czech_lands" title="History of the Czech lands">early modern</a> monarchy in <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a>, the predecessor of the modern <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>. It was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_State" title="Imperial State">Imperial State</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Bohemian_monarchs" title="List of Bohemian monarchs">Bohemian king</a> was a <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">prince-elector</a> of the empire. The Czech king besides <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a> ruled also the <a href="/wiki/Lands_of_the_Bohemian_Crown" title="Lands of the Bohemian Crown">Lands of the Bohemian Crown</a>, which at various times included <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silesia" title="Silesia">Silesia</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg" title="Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>.</p>
<p>The kingdom was established by the <a href="/wiki/P%C5%99emyslid_dynasty" title="Přemyslid dynasty">Přemyslid dynasty</a> in the 12th century from <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Bohemia" title="Duchy of Bohemia">Duchy of Bohemia</a>, later ruled by the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Luxembourg" title="House of Luxembourg">House of Luxembourg</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Jagiellonian_dynasty" title="Jagiellonian dynasty">Jagiellonian dynasty</a>, and since 1526 by the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Habsburg" title="House of Habsburg">House of Habsburg</a> and its successor house <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habsburg-Lorraine" title="Habsburg-Lorraine">Habsburg-Lorraine</a>. Numerous kings of Bohemia were also elected <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperors</a> and the capital <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> was the imperial seat in the late 14th century, and at the end of the 16th and beginning of the 17th centuries.</p>
<p>After the dissolution of the Holy Roman Empire in 1806, the territory became part of the Habsburg <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Empire" title="Austrian Empire">Austrian Empire</a>, and subsequently the <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austro-Hungarian Empire</a> from 1867. Bohemia retained its name and formal status as a separate Kingdom of Bohemia until 1918, known as a crown land within the Austro-Hungarian Empire, and its capital Prague was one of the empire's leading cities. The Czech language (called the Bohemian language in English usage until the 19th century) was the main language of the Diet and the nobility until 1627 (after the <a href="/wiki/Bohemian_Revolt" title="Bohemian Revolt">Bohemian Revolt</a> was suppressed). German was then formally made equal with Czech and eventually prevailed as the language of the Diet until the Czech national revival in the 19th century. German was also widely used as the language of administration in many towns after Germans <a href="/wiki/Ostsiedlung" title="Ostsiedlung">immigrated and populated</a> <a href="/wiki/Sudetenland" title="Sudetenland">some areas of the country</a> in the 13th century. The royal court used the Czech, Latin, and German languages, depending on the ruler and period.</p>
<p>Following the defeat of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Powers" title="Central Powers">Central Powers</a> in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, both the Kingdom and Empire were dissolved. Bohemia became the core part of the newly formed <a href="/wiki/First_Czechoslovak_Republic" title="First Czechoslovak Republic">Czechoslovak Republic</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Bohemia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="13th_century_.28growth.29">13th century (growth)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Bohemia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 13th century (growth)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although some former rulers of Bohemia had enjoyed a non-hereditary royal title during the 11th and 12th centuries (<a href="/wiki/Vratislaus_II_of_Bohemia" title="Vratislaus II of Bohemia">Vratislaus II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vladislaus_II,_Duke_of_Bohemia" title="Vladislaus II, Duke of Bohemia">Vladislaus II</a>), the kingdom was formally established in 1198 by <a href="/wiki/Ottokar_I_of_Bohemia" title="Ottokar I of Bohemia">Přemysl Ottokar I</a>, who had his status acknowledged by <a href="/wiki/Philip_of_Swabia" title="Philip of Swabia">Philip of Swabia</a>, elected <a href="/wiki/King_of_the_Romans" title="King of the Romans">King of the Romans</a>, in return for his support against the rival Emperor <a href="/wiki/Otto_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Otto IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Otto IV</a>. In 1204 Ottokar's royal status was accepted by Otto IV as well as by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_III" title="Pope Innocent III">Pope Innocent III</a>. It was officially recognized in 1212 by the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Bull_of_Sicily" title="Golden Bull of Sicily">Golden Bull of Sicily</a> issued by Emperor <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick II</a>, elevating the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Bohemia" title="Duchy of Bohemia">Duchy of Bohemia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Realm" title="Realm">Kingdom</a> status.</p>
<p>Under these terms, the Czech king was to be exempt from all future obligations to the Holy Roman Empire except for participation in the imperial councils. The imperial prerogative to ratify each Bohemian ruler and to appoint the bishop of <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> was revoked. The king's successor was his son <a href="/wiki/Wenceslaus_I_of_Bohemia" title="Wenceslaus I of Bohemia">Wenceslaus I</a>, from his second marriage.</p>

<p>Wenceslaus I's sister <a href="/wiki/Agnes_of_Bohemia" title="Agnes of Bohemia">Agnes</a>, later canonized, was an extraordinarily courageous and energetic woman for her time; she refused to marry the Holy Roman Emperor and instead devoted her life to spiritual works. Corresponding with the Pope, she established the <a href="/wiki/Knights_of_the_Cross_with_the_Red_Star" title="Knights of the Cross with the Red Star">Knights of the Cross with the Red Star</a> in 1233, the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_order_(society)" title="Military order (society)">military order</a> in the Kingdom of Bohemia. Four other military orders were present in Bohemia: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Order_of_St._John_of_Jerusalem" title="Order of St. John of Jerusalem">Order of St. John of Jerusalem</a> from c. 1160; the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_Lazarus" title="Order of Saint Lazarus">Order of Saint Lazarus</a> from the late 12th century; the <a href="/wiki/Teutonic_Order" title="Teutonic Order">Teutonic Order</a> from c. 1200–1421; and the <a href="/wiki/Knights_Templar" title="Knights Templar">Knights Templar</a> from 1232–1312.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>The 13th century was the most dynamic period of the <a href="/wiki/P%C5%99emyslid_dynasty" title="Přemyslid dynasty">Přemyslid reign</a> over <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>. German Emperor <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick II</a>'s preoccupation with Mediterranean affairs and the dynastic struggles known as the Great Interregnum (1254–73) weakened imperial authority in Central Europe, thus providing opportunities for Přemyslid assertiveness. At the same time, the <a href="/wiki/Mongol_invasion_of_Europe" title="Mongol invasion of Europe">Mongol invasions (1220–42)</a> absorbed the attention of Bohemia's eastern neighbors, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Hungary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Poland_(1025%E2%80%931385)" title="Kingdom of Poland (1025–1385)">Poland</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ottokar_II_of_Bohemia" title="Ottokar II of Bohemia">Přemysl Ottokar II</a> (1253–78) married a German princess, <a href="/wiki/Margaret_of_Austria,_Queen_of_Bohemia" title="Margaret of Austria, Queen of Bohemia">Margaret of Babenberg</a>, and became duke of <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>. He thereby acquired <a href="/wiki/Upper_Austria" title="Upper Austria">Upper Austria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lower_Austria" title="Lower Austria">Lower Austria</a>, and part of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Styria" title="Duchy of Styria">Styria</a>. He conquered the rest of Styria, most of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Carinthia" title="Duchy of Carinthia">Carinthia</a>, and parts of <a href="/wiki/Carniola" title="Carniola">Carniola</a>. He was called "the king of iron and gold" (iron because of his conquests, gold because of his wealth). He campaigned as far as <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a>, where he defeated the pagan natives and in 1256, founded a city he named Královec in Czech, which later became Königsberg (now <a href="/wiki/Kaliningrad" title="Kaliningrad">Kaliningrad</a>).</p>
<p>In 1260, Ottokar defeated Hungary in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kressenbrunn" title="Battle of Kressenbrunn">Battle of Kressenbrunn</a>, where more than 200,000 men clashed. He ruled an area from <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Sea" title="Adriatic Sea">Adriatic Sea</a>. From 1273, however, <a href="/wiki/House_of_Habsburg" title="House of Habsburg">Habsburg</a> emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rudolph_I_of_Germany" title="Rudolph I of Germany">Rudolf</a> began to reassert imperial authority, checking Ottokar's power. He also had problems with rebellious nobility in Bohemia. All of Ottokar's German possessions were lost in 1276, and in 1278 he was abandoned by part of the Czech nobility and died in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_on_the_Marchfeld" title="Battle on the Marchfeld">Battle on the Marchfeld</a> against Rudolf.</p>
<p>Ottokar was succeeded by his son King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wenceslaus_II" title="Wenceslaus II">Wenceslaus II</a>, who was crowned King of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Poland_(1025%E2%80%931385)" title="Kingdom of Poland (1025–1385)">Poland</a> in 1300. Wenceslaus II's son <a href="/wiki/Wenceslaus_III_of_Bohemia" title="Wenceslaus III of Bohemia">Wenceslaus III</a> was crowned King of Hungary a year later. At this time, the Kings of Bohemia ruled from Hungary to the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>.</p>
<p>The 13th century was also a period of large-scale German immigration, during the <i><a href="/wiki/Ostsiedlung" title="Ostsiedlung">Ostsiedlung</a></i>, often encouraged by the Přemyslid kings. The Germans populated towns and mining districts on the Bohemian periphery and in some cases formed German colonies in the interior of the Czech lands. Stříbro, <a href="/wiki/Kutn%C3%A1_Hora" title="Kutná Hora">Kutná Hora</a>, Německý Brod (present-day <a href="/wiki/Havl%C3%AD%C4%8Dk%C5%AFv_Brod" title="Havlíčkův Brod">Havlíčkův Brod</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Jihlava" title="Jihlava">Jihlava</a> were important German settlements. The Germans brought their own code of law — the <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">ius teutonicum</span></i> — which formed the basis of the later commercial law of Bohemia and <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravia</a>. Marriages between Czech nobles and Germans soon became commonplace.</p>

<p>The 14th century — particularly the reign of <a href="/wiki/Charles_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles IV</a> (1342–78) — is considered the Golden Age of Czech history. In 1306, the Přemyslid line died out and, after a series of dynastic wars, <a href="/wiki/John_of_Bohemia" title="John of Bohemia">John, Count of Luxembourg</a>, was elected Bohemian king. He married <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elisabeth_of_Bohemia_(1292%E2%80%931330)" title="Elisabeth of Bohemia (1292–1330)">Elisabeth</a>, the daughter of Wenceslaus II. He was succeeded as king in 1346 by his son, Charles IV, the second king from the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Luxembourg" title="House of Luxembourg">House of Luxembourg</a>. Charles was raised at the French court and was cosmopolitan in attitude.</p>
<p>Charles IV strengthened the power and prestige of the Bohemian kingdom. In 1344 he elevated the bishopric of <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>, making it an archbishopric and freeing it from the jurisdiction of <a href="/wiki/Mainz" title="Mainz">Mainz</a>, and the archbishop was given the right to crown Bohemian kings. Charles curbed the Bohemian, Moravian, and Silesian nobility, and rationalized the provincial administration of Bohemia and Moravia. He created the <a href="/wiki/Lands_of_the_Bohemian_Crown" title="Lands of the Bohemian Crown">Crown of Bohemia</a>, incorporating Moravia, <a href="/wiki/Silesia" title="Silesia">Silesia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lusatia" title="Lusatia">Lusatia</a>.</p>

<p>In 1355 Charles was crowned Holy Roman Emperor. The next year he issued the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Bull_of_1356" title="Golden Bull of 1356">Golden Bull of 1356</a>, defining and codifying the process of <a href="/wiki/Imperial_election" title="Imperial election">election to the Imperial throne</a>, with the Bohemian king among the seven electors. Issuance of the Golden Bull together with the ensuing acquisition of the Brandenburg Electorate gave the Luxemburgs two votes in the <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">electoral college</a>. Charles also made <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> into an Imperial capital.</p>
<p>Extensive building projects undertaken by the king included the founding of the New Town southeast of the old city. The royal castle, <a href="/wiki/Hrad%C4%8Dany" title="Hradčany">Hradčany</a>, was rebuilt. Of particular significance was the founding of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_University_in_Prague" title="Charles University in Prague">Charles University in Prague</a> in 1348. Charles intended to make Prague into an international center of learning, and the university was divided into Czech, Polish, Saxon, and Bavarian "nations", each with one controlling vote. Charles University, however, would become the nucleus of intense Czech particularism.</p>
<p>Charles died in 1378, and the Bohemian crown went to his son, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wenceslaus,_King_of_the_Romans" title="Wenceslaus, King of the Romans">Wenceslas IV</a>. He had also been elected <a href="/wiki/King_of_the_Romans" title="King of the Romans">King of the Romans</a> in 1376, in the first election since his father's Golden Bull. He was deposed from the Imperial throne in 1400, however, having never been crowned Emperor. His brother, <a href="/wiki/Sigismund,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Sigismund, Holy Roman Emperor">Sigismund</a>, was eventually crowned Emperor in Rome in 1433, ruling until 1437, and he was the last male member of the House of Luxemburg.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="15th_century_.28Hussite_movement.29">15th century (Hussite movement)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Bohemia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 15th century (Hussite movement)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hussite" title="Hussite">Hussite</a> movement (1402–85) was primarily a religious, as well as national, manifestation. As a religious reform movement (the so-called <a href="/wiki/Bohemian_Reformation" title="Bohemian Reformation">Bohemian Reformation</a>), it represented a challenge to papal authority and an assertion of national autonomy in ecclesiastical affairs. The Hussites defeated four crusades from the Holy Roman Empire, and the movement is viewed by many as a part of the (worldwide) <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a>. Because many of warriors of the crusades were Germans, although many were also Hungarians and Catholic Czechs, the Hussite movement is seen as a Czech national movement. In modern times it acquired anti-imperial and anti-German associations and has sometimes been identified as a manifestation of a long-term ethnic Czech–German conflict.</p>
<p>Hussitism began during the long reign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wenceslaus,_King_of_the_Romans" title="Wenceslaus, King of the Romans">Wenceslas IV</a> (1378–1419), a period of papal schism and concomitant anarchy in the Holy Roman Empire. It was precipitated by a controversy at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_University_in_Prague" title="Charles University in Prague">Charles University in Prague</a>. In 1403 <a href="/wiki/Jan_Hus" title="Jan Hus">Jan Hus</a> became rector of the university. A reformist preacher, Hus espoused the anti-papal and anti-hierarchical teachings of <a href="/wiki/John_Wycliffe" title="John Wycliffe">John Wycliffe</a> of England, often referred to as the "Morning Star of the Reformation". Hus' teaching was distinguished by its rejection of what he saw as the wealth, corruption, and hierarchical tendencies of the Roman Catholic Church. He advocated the Wycliffe doctrine of clerical purity and poverty, and insisted on the laity receiving <a href="/wiki/Eucharist_in_the_Catholic_Church" title="Eucharist in the Catholic Church">communion</a> under both kinds, bread and wine. (The Roman Catholic Church in practice reserved the cup, or wine, for the clergy.) The more moderate followers of Hus, the <a href="/wiki/Utraquism" title="Utraquism">Utraquists</a>, took their name from the Latin <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">sub utraque specie</span></i>, meaning "under each kind". The <a href="/wiki/Taborite" title="Taborite">Taborites</a>, a more radical sect, soon formed, taking their name from the city of <a href="/wiki/T%C3%A1bor" title="Tábor">Tábor</a>, their stronghold in southern Bohemia. They rejected church doctrine and upheld the Bible as the sole authority in all matters of belief.</p>

<p>Soon after Hus assumed office, German professors of theology demanded the condemnation of Wycliffe's writings. Hus protested, receiving the support of the Czech element at the university. Having only one vote in policy decisions against three for the Germans, the Czechs were outvoted,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and the orthodox position was maintained. In subsequent years, the Czechs demanded a revision of the university charter, granting more adequate representation to the native Czech faculty. The university controversy was intensified by the vacillating position of the Bohemian king Wenceslas. His favoring of Germans in appointments to councillor and other administrative positions had aroused the nationalist sentiments of the Czech nobility and rallied them to Hus' defense. The German faculties had the support of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zbyn%C4%9Bk_Zaj%C3%ADc" title="Zbyněk Zajíc">Zbyněk Zajíc</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Prague" title="Archbishop of Prague">Archbishop of Prague</a>, and the German clergy. For political reasons, Wenceslas switched his support from the Germans to Hus and allied with the reformers. On January 18, 1409, Wenceslas issued the <a href="/wiki/Decree_of_Kutn%C3%A1_Hora" title="Decree of Kutná Hora">Decree of Kutná Hora</a>: (as was the case at other major universities in Europe) the Czechs would have three votes; the others, a single vote. In consequence, German faculty and students left Charles University en masse in the thousands, and many ended up founding the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Leipzig" title="University of Leipzig">University of Leipzig</a>.</p>
<p>Hus' victory was short lived. He preached against the sale of <a href="/wiki/Indulgence" title="Indulgence">indulgences</a>, which lost him the support of the king, who had received a percentage of such sales. In 1412 Hus and his followers were suspended from the university and expelled from Prague. For two years the reformers served as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Itinerant_minister" title="Itinerant minister">itinerant preachers</a> throughout Bohemia. In 1414 Hus was summoned to the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Constance" title="Council of Constance">Council of Constance</a> to defend his views. Imprisoned when he arrived, he was never given a chance to defend his ideas. The council condemned him as a heretic and <a href="/wiki/Death_by_burning" title="Death by burning">burned him at the stake</a> in 1415.</p>

<p>Hus's death sparked the <a href="/wiki/Hussite_Wars" title="Hussite Wars">Hussite Wars</a>, decades of religious warfare. <a href="/wiki/Sigismund,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Sigismund, Holy Roman Emperor">Sigismund</a>, the pro-papal king of <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> and successor to the Bohemian throne after the death of Wenceslas in 1419, failed repeatedly to gain control of the kingdom despite aid by Hungarian and German armies. Riots broke out in Prague. Led by a Czech yeoman, <a href="/wiki/Jan_%C5%BDi%C5%BEka" title="Jan Žižka">Jan Žižka</a>, the Taborites streamed into the capital. Religious strife pervaded the entire kingdom and was particularly intense in the German-dominated towns. Hussite Czechs and Catholic Germans turned on each other; many were massacred, and many German survivors fled or were exiled to the rest of the Holy Roman Empire. Emperor Sigismund led or instigated various crusades against Bohemia with the support of Hungarians and Bohemian Catholics.</p>
<p>The Hussite Wars followed a pattern. When a crusade was launched against Bohemia, moderate and radical Hussites would unite and defeat it. Once the threat was over, the Hussite armies would focus on ridding the land of Catholic sympathizers. Many historians have painted the Hussites as religious fanatics; they fought in part for a nationalist purpose: to protect their land from a King and a Pope who did not recognize the right of the Hussites to exist. Zizka led armies to storm castles, monasteries, churches, and villages, expelling the Catholic clergy, expropriating ecclesiastical lands, or accepting conversions.</p>
<p>During the struggle against Sigismund, Taborite armies penetrated into areas of modern-day <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a> as well. Czech refugees from the religious wars in Bohemia settled there, and from 1438 to 1453 a Czech noble, <a href="/wiki/John_Jiskra_of_Brand%C3%BDs" title="John Jiskra of Brandýs">John Jiskra of Brandýs</a>, controlled most of southern Slovakia from the centers of Zólyom (today <a href="/wiki/Zvolen" title="Zvolen">Zvolen</a>) and Kassa (today <a href="/wiki/Ko%C5%A1ice" title="Košice">Košice</a>). Thus Hussite doctrines and the Czech Bible were disseminated among the Slovaks, providing the basis for a future link between the Czechs and their Slovak neighbors.</p>

<p>When Sigismund died in 1437, the Bohemian estates elected <a href="/wiki/Albert_II_of_Germany" title="Albert II of Germany">Albert of Austria</a> as his successor. Albert died and his son, <a href="/wiki/Ladislaus_the_Posthumous" title="Ladislaus the Posthumous">Ladislaus the Posthumous</a> — so called because he was born after his father's death — was acknowledged as king. During Ladislaus' minority, Bohemia was ruled by a regency composed of moderate reform nobles who were Utraquists. Internal dissension among the Czechs provided the primary challenge to the regency. A part of the Czech nobility remained Catholic and loyal to the pope. A Utraquist delegation to the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Florence" title="Council of Florence">Council of Basel</a> in 1433 had negotiated a seeming reconciliation with the Catholic Church. The <a href="/wiki/Compacts_of_Basel" title="Compacts of Basel">Compacts of Basel</a> accepted the basic tenets of Hussitism expressed in the Four Articles of Prague: communion under both kinds; free preaching of the Gospels; expropriation of church land; and exposure and punishment of public sinners. The pope, however, rejected the compact, thus preventing the reconciliation of Czech Catholics with the Utraquists.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/George_of_Pod%C4%9Bbrady" title="George of Poděbrady">George of Poděbrady</a>, later to become the "national" king of Bohemia, emerged as leader of the Utraquist regency. George installed another Utraquist, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_of_Rokycan" title="John of Rokycan">John of Rokycan</a>, as archbishop of Prague and succeeded in uniting the more radical Taborites with the Czech Reformed Church. The Catholic party was driven out of Prague. After Ladislaus died of <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">leukemia</a> in 1457, the following year the Bohemian estates elected George of Poděbrady as king. Although George was noble-born, he was not a successor of royal dynasty; his election to the monarchy was not recognised by the Pope, or any other European monarchs.</p>
<p>George sought to establish a "Charter of a <a href="/wiki/Universal_Peace_Union" title="Universal Peace Union">Universal Peace Union</a>." He believed that all monarchs should work for a sustainable peace on the principle of national sovereignty of states, principles of non-interference, and solving problems and disputes before an International Tribunal. Also, Europe should unite together to fight the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Turks</a>. States would have one vote each, with a leading role for France. George did not see a specific role for Papal authority.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Czech Catholic nobles joined in the League of Zelena Hora in 1465, challenging the authority of George of Poděbrady; the next year, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_II" title="Pope Paul II">Pope Paul II</a> excommunicated George. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bohemian_War_(1468-1478)" title="Bohemian War (1468-1478)">Bohemian War (1468-1478)</a> pitted Bohemia against <a href="/wiki/Matthias_Corvinus" title="Matthias Corvinus">Matthias Corvinus</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frederick_III_of_Habsburg" title="Frederick III of Habsburg">Frederick III of Habsburg</a>, and the Hungarian forces occupied most of Moravia. George of Poděbrady died in 1471.</p>

<p>Upon the death of the Hussite king, the Bohemian estates elected a Polish prince <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vladislaus_II_of_Bohemia_and_Hungary" title="Vladislaus II of Bohemia and Hungary">Ladislaus Jagiellon</a> as king, who negotiated the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Olomouc" title="Peace of Olomouc">Peace of Olomouc</a> in 1479. In 1490 he also became king of Hungary, and the Polish Jagellonian line ruled both Bohemia and Hungary. The Jagellonians governed Bohemia as absentee monarchs; their influence in the kingdom was minimal, and effective government fell to the regional nobility. Czech Catholics accepted the Compact of Basel in 1485 and were reconciled with the Utraquists. The Bohemian estrangement from the Empire continued after Vladislav had succeeded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matthias_Corvinus_of_Hungary" title="Matthias Corvinus of Hungary">Matthias Corvinus of Hungary</a> in 1490 and both the Bohemian and the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Hungarian kingdom</a> were held in <a href="/wiki/Personal_union" title="Personal union">personal union</a>. Not considered an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_State" title="Imperial State">Imperial State</a>, the Lands of the Bohemian Crown were not part of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Circle" title="Imperial Circle">Imperial Circles</a> established by the 1500 <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Reform" title="Imperial Reform">Imperial Reform</a>.</p>
<p>In 1526 Vladislav's son, King <a href="/wiki/Louis_II_of_Hungary" title="Louis II of Hungary">Louis</a>, was decisively defeated by the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Turks</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Moh%C3%A1cs" title="Battle of Mohács">Battle of Mohács</a> and subsequently died. As a result, the Turks conquered part of the Kingdom of Hungary, and the rest (mainly present-day Slovakia territory) came under Habsburg rule under the terms of King Louis' marriage contract. The Bohemian estates elected <a href="/wiki/Archduchy_of_Austria" title="Archduchy of Austria">Austrian Archduke</a> <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Ferdinand I, Holy Roman Emperor">Ferdinand</a>, younger brother of Emperor <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a>, to succeed Louis as king of Bohemia. Thus began almost four centuries of Habsburg rule for both Bohemia and Slovakia.</p>
<p>The incorporation of Bohemia into the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Monarchy" title="Habsburg Monarchy">Habsburg Monarchy</a> against the resistance of the local <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> nobility sparked the 1618 <a href="/wiki/Defenestrations_of_Prague" title="Defenestrations of Prague">Defenestration of Prague</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>. Their defeat at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_White_Mountain" title="Battle of White Mountain">Battle of White Mountain</a> in 1620 put an end to the Bohemian autonomy movement.</p>

<p>In 1740 the <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Army" title="Prussian Army">Prussian Army</a> conquered Bohemian <a href="/wiki/Silesia" title="Silesia">Silesia</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Silesian_Wars" title="Silesian Wars">Silesian Wars</a> and forced <a href="/wiki/Maria_Theresa" title="Maria Theresa">Maria Theresa</a> in 1742 to cede the majority of Silesia, except the southernmost area with the duchies of Cieszyn, Krnov and Opava, to <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a>. In 1756 Prussian King <a href="/wiki/Frederick_the_Great" title="Frederick the Great">Frederick II</a> faced an enemy coalition led by Austria, when Maria Theresa was preparing for war with Prussia to reclaim Silesia. The Prussian army conquered <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a> and in 1757 invaded Bohemia. In the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Prague_(1757)" title="Battle of Prague (1757)">Battle of Prague (1757)</a> they defeated the Habsburgs and subsequently occupied Prague. More than one quarter of Prague was destroyed and the <a href="/wiki/St._Vitus_Cathedral" title="St. Vitus Cathedral">St. Vitus Cathedral</a> suffered heavy damage. In the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kol%C3%ADn" title="Battle of Kolín">Battle of Kolín</a>, however, Frederick lost and had to vacate Prague and retreat from Bohemia.</p>
<p>With the dissolution of the Holy Roman Empire in 1806, the Bohemian kingdom was incorporated into the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Empire" title="Austrian Empire">Austrian Empire</a> and the royal title retained alongside the title of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Austria" title="Emperor of Austria">Austrian Emperor</a>. In the course of the 1867 <a href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Compromise_of_1867" title="Austro-Hungarian Compromise of 1867">Austro-Hungarian Compromise</a> the provinces of Bohemia, Moravia and Austrian Silesia became <i><a href="/wiki/Imperial_and_Royal" title="Imperial and Royal">k. k.</a></i> crown lands of <a href="/wiki/Cisleithania" title="Cisleithania">Cisleithania</a>. The Bohemian Kingdom officially ceased to exist in 1918 by transformation into the <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovak Republic</a>.</p>
<p>The current Czech Republic consisting of Bohemia, Moravia and <a href="/wiki/Czech_Silesia" title="Czech Silesia">Czech Silesia</a> still uses most of the symbols of the Kingdom of Bohemia: a two-tailed lion in its coat-of-arms, red-white strips in the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_Czech_Republic" title="Flag of the Czech Republic">state flag</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Prague_Castle" title="Prague Castle">royal castle</a> as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/President_of_Czechia" title="President of Czechia">president's</a> office.</p>

<p>Bohemia was among the first countries in Europe to become industrialized. Mining of tin and silver began in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ore_mountains" title="Ore mountains">Ore mountains</a> in early 12th century.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lands_of_the_Bohemian_Crown">Lands of the Bohemian Crown</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Bohemia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lands of the Bohemian Crown">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Lands_of_the_Bohemian_Crown" title="Lands of the Bohemian Crown">Lands of the Bohemian Crown</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a> proper (<i>Čechy</i>) with the <a href="/wiki/County_of_Kladsko" title="County of Kladsko">County of Kladsko</a> (<i>Hrabství kladské</i>) was the main area of the Kingdom of Bohemia. The <a href="/wiki/Egerland" title="Egerland">Egerland</a> (<i>Chebsko</i>) was ultimatively obtained by King <a href="/wiki/Wenceslaus_II_of_Bohemia" title="Wenceslaus II of Bohemia">Wenceslaus II</a> between 1291–1305; given in pawn to Bohemia by King <a href="/wiki/Louis_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Louis IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Louis IV of Germany</a> in 1322 and subsequently joined in personal union with Bohemia proper. In 1348 <a href="/wiki/Charles_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles IV</a> created the <b>Crown of Bohemia</b> (<i>Koruna česká</i>), together with the incorporated provinces:</p>
<ul>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Margraviate_of_Moravia" title="Margraviate of Moravia">Margraviate of Moravia</a> (<i>Markrabství moravské</i>), acquired by <a href="/wiki/P%C5%99emyslid_dynasty" title="Přemyslid dynasty">Přemyslid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slavn%C3%ADk_dynasty" title="Slavník dynasty">Slavník</a> Bohemian rulers after the 955 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lechfeld" title="Battle of Lechfeld">Battle of Lechfeld</a>, lost in 999 to <a href="/wiki/History_of_Poland_during_the_Piast_dynasty" title="History of Poland during the Piast dynasty">Poland</a> and reconquered by Duke <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bretislaus_I,_Duke_of_Bohemia" title="Bretislaus I, Duke of Bohemia">Bretislaus I</a> in 1019/1029 (uncertain dating);</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Upper_Lusatia" title="Upper Lusatia">Upper Lusatia</a> (<i>Horní Lužice</i>), incorporated by Charles' father King <a href="/wiki/John_of_Bohemia" title="John of Bohemia">John of Bohemia</a> in 1319 (<a href="/wiki/Bautzen" title="Bautzen">Bautzen</a> Land) and 1329 (<a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6rlitz" title="Görlitz">Görlitz</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Lower_Lusatia" title="Lower Lusatia">Lower Lusatia</a> (<i>Dolní Lužice</i>, former <a href="/wiki/March_of_Lusatia" title="March of Lusatia">March of Lusatia</a>), acquired by Charles IV from the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Wittelsbach" title="House of Wittelsbach">Wittelsbach</a> duke <a href="/wiki/Otto_V,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="Otto V, Duke of Bavaria">Otto V of Bavaria</a> in 1367. The <a href="/wiki/House_of_Habsburg" title="House of Habsburg">Habsburg</a> emperor <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Ferdinand II, Holy Roman Emperor">Ferdinand II</a> ceded the Lusatias to the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Saxony" title="Electorate of Saxony">Electorate of Saxony</a> by the 1635 <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Prague_(1635)" title="Peace of Prague (1635)">Peace of Prague</a>;</li>
<li>the Duchies of <a href="/wiki/Silesia" title="Silesia">Silesia</a> (<i>Slezsko</i>), acquired by the 1335 <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Trentschin" title="Treaty of Trentschin">Treaty of Trentschin</a> between King John of Bohemia and King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casimir_III_of_Poland" title="Casimir III of Poland">Casimir III of Poland</a>. Queen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maria_Theresa_of_Austria" title="Maria Theresa of Austria">Maria Theresa</a> lost Silesia in 1742 to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussian</a> king <a href="/wiki/Frederick_the_Great" title="Frederick the Great">Frederick the Great</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Breslau" title="Treaty of Breslau">Treaty of Breslau</a>, with the exception of <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Silesia" title="Austrian Silesia">Austrian Silesia</a>.</li>
<li>the northern part of the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Palatinate" title="Upper Palatinate">Upper Palatinate</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Bohemian_Palatinate" title="Bohemian Palatinate">Bohemian Palatinate</a>") at <a href="/wiki/Sulzbach-Rosenberg" title="Sulzbach-Rosenberg">Sulzbach</a>, incorporated into the Bohemian crown by Charles IV in 1355. Charles exchanged parts of this territory for Brandenburg in 1373, while his son <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wenceslaus,_King_of_the_Romans" title="Wenceslaus, King of the Romans">Wenceslaus</a> lost the rest in 1400 to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electorate_of_the_Palatinate" title="Electorate of the Palatinate">Electorate of the Palatinate</a> under King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rupert_of_Germany" title="Rupert of Germany">Rupert of Germany</a>;</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Margraviate_of_Brandenburg" title="Margraviate of Brandenburg">Brandenburg Electorate</a>, acquired in 1373 by Charles IV from the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Wittelsbach" title="House of Wittelsbach">Wittelsbach</a> duke <a href="/wiki/Otto_V,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="Otto V, Duke of Bavaria">Otto V of Bavaria</a>. Charles' son Emperor <a href="/wiki/Sigismund,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Sigismund, Holy Roman Emperor">Sigismund</a> granted Brandenburg to <a href="/wiki/Frederick_I,_Elector_of_Brandenburg" title="Frederick I, Elector of Brandenburg">Frederick I of Hohenzollern</a> in 1415.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>at times were incorporated into the Kingdom of Bohemia these provinces:</p>
<ul>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Austria" title="Duchy of Austria">Duchy of Austria</a> in 1251, the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Styria" title="Duchy of Styria">Duchy of Styria</a> in 1261, the <a href="/wiki/Egerland" title="Egerland">Egerland</a> in 1266, the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Carinthia" title="Duchy of Carinthia">Duchy of Carinthia</a> with the <a href="/wiki/March_of_Carniola" title="March of Carniola">March of Carniola</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Windic_March" title="Windic March">Windic March</a> in 1269, and the March of <a href="/wiki/Friuli" title="Friuli">Friuli</a> in 1272, all acquired by the Přemyslid king <a href="/wiki/Ottokar_II_of_Bohemia" title="Ottokar II of Bohemia">Ottokar II of Bohemia</a> but lost to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rudolph_I_of_Germany" title="Rudolph I of Germany">Rudolph of Habsburg</a> in the 1278 <a href="/wiki/Battle_on_the_Marchfeld" title="Battle on the Marchfeld">Battle on the Marchfeld</a>;</li>
</ul>
<p>The modern Czech Republic is the legal successor of the Crown of Bohemia, as stated in the preamble to its Constitution.</p>
