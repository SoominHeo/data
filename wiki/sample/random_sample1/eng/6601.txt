<p>A <b>double-track</b> railway usually involves running one track in each direction, compared to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single_track_(rail)" title="Single track (rail)">single-track</a> railway where trains in both directions share the same track.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In the earliest days of railways in the United Kingdom, most lines were built as double-track because of the difficulty of co-ordinating operations before the invention of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telegraph" title="Telegraph">telegraph</a>. The lines also tended to be busy enough to be beyond the capacity of a single track. In the early days the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Trade" title="Board of Trade">Board of Trade</a> did not consider any single-track railway line to be complete.</p>

<p>In the earliest days of railways in the United States most lines were built as single-track for reasons of cost, and very inefficient timetable working systems were used to prevent head-on collisions on single lines. This improved with the development of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telegraph" title="Telegraph">telegraph</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Train_order" title="Train order">train order</a> system.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation">Operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Handedness">Handedness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Handedness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Right-_and_left-hand_traffic" title="Right- and left-hand traffic">Right- and left-hand traffic</a></div>
<p>In any given country, rail traffic generally runs to one side of a double-track line, not always the same side as road traffic. Thus in Belgium, China, France (apart from the former German Alsace and Lorraine), Sweden, Switzerland and Italy for example, the railways use left-hand running, while the roads use right-hand running. In countries such as <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, it is the reverse (right-hand running for railways and left-hand running for roads).</p>
<p>On the (pre-1918) French-German border, for example, <a href="/wiki/Overpass" title="Overpass">flyovers</a> were provided so that train moving on the left in France end up on the right in Germany and vice versa.</p>
<p>Handedness of traffic can affect locomotive design. For the driver, visibility is good from both sides of the driving cab so the choice on which side to site the driver less important. For example, the French <a href="/wiki/SNCF_Class_BB_7200" title="SNCF Class BB 7200">SNCF Class BB 7200</a> is designed for using the left-hand track and therefore uses LHD. When the design was modified for use in the Netherlands as <a href="/wiki/NS_Class_1600" title="NS Class 1600">NS Class 1600</a>, the driving cab was not completely redesigned, keeping the driver on the left despite the fact that trains use the right-hand track in the Netherlands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Generally, the left/right principle in a country is followed mostly on double track. On single track, when trains meet, the train that shall not stop often uses the straight path in the turnout, which can be left or right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bi-directional_running">Bi-directional running</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bi-directional running">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Single-line_working" title="Single-line working">Single-line working</a></div>
<p>Double-track railways, especially older ones, may use each track exclusively in one direction. This arrangement simplifies the <a href="/wiki/Railway_signalling" title="Railway signalling">signalling</a> systems, especially where the signalling is mechanical (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Railway_semaphore_signal" title="Railway semaphore signal">semaphore signals</a>).</p>
<p>Where the signals and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Points_(rail)" title="Points (rail)">points</a> (UK term) or rail switches (US) are power-operated, it can be worthwhile to signal each line in both directions, so that the double line becomes a pair of single lines. This allows trains to use one track where the other track is out of service due to track maintenance work, or a train failure, or for a fast train to overtake a slow train.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crossing_loops">Crossing loops</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Crossing loops">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most <a href="/wiki/Passing_loop" title="Passing loop">crossing loops</a> are not regarded as double-track even though they consist of multiple tracks. If the crossing loop is long enough to hold several trains, and to allow opposing trains to cross without slowing down or stopping, then that may be regarded as double-track. A more modern British term for such a layout is an extended loop.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Track_centres">Track centres</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Track centres">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The distance between the track centres makes a difference in cost and performance of a double-track line. The track centres can be as narrow and as cheap as possible, but maintenance must be done on the side. Signals for bi-directional working cannot be mounted between the tracks so must be mounted on the 'wrong' side of the line or on expensive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railroad_signal#Gantry_mounting" title="Railroad signal">signal bridges</a>. Track centres are usually wider on high speed lines, as pressure waves knock each other as high-speed trains pass.</p>
<p>Narrow track centres might be 4 metres (13 ft) or less. Narrow track centres may have to be widened on sharp curves to allow for long rail vehicles following the arc of the curve, and this increases a surveyor's workload. Widening a track centre to 5 metres (16 ft) or so suits high-speed trains passing each other, and eliminates the need to widen the centres on sharp curves. Increasing width of track centres of 6 metres (20 ft) or more makes it much easier to mount signals and overhead wiring structures.</p>
<p>In the United Kingdom the early lines of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Railway" title="Great Western Railway">Great Western Railway</a> were built to <a href="/wiki/Isambard_Kingdom_Brunel" title="Isambard Kingdom Brunel">Brunel</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Broad_gauge" title="Broad gauge">broad gauge</a>, which was also associated with a more generous <a href="/wiki/Loading_gauge" title="Loading gauge">loading gauge</a>. This is still apparent along those lines which remain in use today: structures such as bridges and tunnels are larger, and the distances between opposite platform faces are larger.</p>
<p>Very wide centres at major bridges can have military value. It also makes it harder for rogue ships and barges knocking out both bridges in the same accident.</p>
<p>Railway lines in desert areas affected by sand dunes are sometimes built on alternate routes so that if one is covered by sand, the other(s) are still serviceable.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents">Accidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Accidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On British lines, the space between the two running rails of a single railway track is called the "four foot" (owing to it being 'four foot something' in width), while the space between the different tracks is called the "six foot". It is not safe to stand in the gap between the tracks when trains pass by on both lines, as happened in the <a href="/wiki/Bere_Ferrers_rail_accident" title="Bere Ferrers rail accident">Bere Ferrers accident</a> of 1917.</p>
<ul>
<li>Narrow track centres on the <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_and_Manchester_Railway#Opening" title="Liverpool and Manchester Railway">Liverpool and Manchester Railway</a> contributed to a fatal accident on opening day.</li>
<li>A US naval scientist and submarine pioneer, Captain Jacques, was killed getting out of the wrong side of a train at <a href="/wiki/Hadley_Wood_railway_station" title="Hadley Wood railway station">Hadley Wood</a> in 1916.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Narrow track centres contribute to "Second Train Coming" accidents at <a href="/wiki/Level_crossing" title="Level crossing">level crossings</a> since it is harder to see the second train – for example, the accident at <a href="/wiki/Elsenham_railway_station#Elsenham_accidents" title="Elsenham railway station">Elsenham</a> level crossing in 2005.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temporary_single_track">Temporary single track</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Temporary single track">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When one track of a double-track railway is out of service for maintenance or a train breaks down, all trains may be concentrated on the one good track. There may be bi-directional signalling and suitable crossovers to enable trains to move on to the other track expeditiously (see, for example, the article on the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Tunnel#The_current_tunnel" title="Channel Tunnel">Channel Tunnel</a>), or there may be some kind of manual safeworking to control trains on what is now a section of single track. <i>See</i> <a href="/wiki/Single-line_working" title="Single-line working">single-line working</a>.</p>
<p>Accidents can occur if the temporary safeworking system is not implemented properly:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Br%C3%BChl_train_disaster" title="Brühl train disaster">Brühl train disaster</a>, Germany – 2000.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2006_Zoufftgen_rail_crash" title="2006 Zoufftgen rail crash">2006 Zoufftgen rail crash</a>, France</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Out-of-gauge_trains">Out-of-gauge trains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Out-of-gauge trains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From time to time, railways are asked to transport exceptional loads such as massive electrical transformers that are too tall, too wide or too heavy to operate normally. Special measures must be carefully taken to plan successful and safe operation of <a href="/wiki/Break-of-gauge" title="Break-of-gauge">Out-of-gauge trains</a>. For example, adjacent tracks of a double line might have to be shut down to avoid collisions with trains on those adjacent tracks.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Passing_lanes">Passing lanes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Passing lanes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To improve travel times and increase line capacity, the 300 kilometres (190 mi) of line between <a href="/wiki/Junee" title="Junee">Junee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> is to be partially duplicated in a configuration called <a href="/wiki/Passing_lane" title="Passing lane">Passing lanes</a>. Existing crossing loops are mostly 900 metres (3,000 ft) and 1,500 metres (4,900 ft) long, and these will be enhanced by loops 6 kilometres (3.7 mi) long which are long enough to be regarded as nearly double track.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Duplication">Duplication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Duplication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The process of expanding a single track to double track is called <b>duplication</b> or <b>doubling</b>, unless the expansion is to restore what was previously double track, in which case it is called <b>redoubling</b>.</p>
<p>The strongest evidence that a line was built as single-track and duplicated at a later date consists of major structures such as bridges and tunnels that are twinned. One example is the twin Slade tunnels on the <a href="/wiki/Ilfracombe_Branch_Line" title="Ilfracombe Branch Line">Ilfracombe Branch Line</a> in the UK. Twinned structures may be identical in appearance, or like some tunnels between <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belair,_South_Australia" title="Belair, South Australia">Belair</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a>, substantially different in appearance, being built to different <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Structure_gauges&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Structure gauges (page does not exist)">structure gauges</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tunnel_duplication">Tunnel duplication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Tunnel duplication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Tunnels are confined spaces and are difficult to duplicate while trains keep on running. Generally they are duplicated by building a second tunnel. An exception is the <a href="/wiki/Hoosac_Tunnel" title="Hoosac Tunnel">Hoosac Tunnel</a>, which was duplicated by enlarging the bore.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Provision_for_duplication">Provision for duplication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Provision for duplication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To reduce initial costs of a line that is certain to see heavy traffic in the future, a line may be built as single-track but with <a href="/wiki/Earthworks_(engineering)" title="Earthworks (engineering)">earthworks</a> and structures designed for ready duplication. An example is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strathfield" title="Strathfield">Strathfield</a> to <a href="/wiki/Hamilton_railway_station,_New_South_Wales" title="Hamilton railway station, New South Wales">Hamilton</a> line in <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, which was constructed as mainly single-track in the 1880s, with full duplication completed around 1910. All bridges, tunnels, stations, and earthworks were built for double track. Stations with platforms with 11' centres had to be widened later to 12' centres, except for <a href="/wiki/Gosford" title="Gosford">Gosford</a>.</p>
<p>The former <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_and_Ohio_Railroad" title="Baltimore and Ohio Railroad">Baltimore and Ohio Railroad</a> (B&amp;O) line between <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jersey_City" title="Jersey City">Jersey City</a>, now owned by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CSX" title="CSX">CSX</a> and <a href="/wiki/Conrail_Shared_Assets_Operations#North_Jersey_Shared_Assets_Area" title="Conrail Shared Assets Operations">Conrail Shared Assets Operations</a>, is an example of a duplication line that was reduced to single-track in most locations, but has since undergone re-duplication in many places between Baltimore and <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> when CSX increased freight schedules in the late 1990s.</p>
<p>Also:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Smardale#Railways" title="Smardale">Smardale</a> Gill viaduct.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Westerham_railway_station" title="Westerham railway station">Westerham line</a>.</li>
<li>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Menangle_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Menangle Bridge (page does not exist)">Menangle Bridge</a> was single track (1863) but built for double track (1890s). A second track was laid temporarily to allow testing both tracks at once.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Never-used_provision_for_duplication">Never-used provision for duplication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Never-used provision for duplication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some lines are built as single-track with provision for duplication, but the duplication is never carried out. Examples include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Swanage_Railway" title="Swanage Railway">Swanage Railway</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bluebell_Railway" title="Bluebell Railway">Bluebell Railway</a>: <a href="/wiki/Horsted_Keynes" title="Horsted Keynes">Horsted Keynes</a> to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Culver_Junction_(England)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Culver Junction (England) (page does not exist)">Culver Junction</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lewes" title="Lewes">Lewes</a>).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Keighley_and_Worth_Valley_Railway" title="Keighley and Worth Valley Railway">Keighley and Worth Valley Railway</a> – including bridge and tunnels and a deviation.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Westerham_Valley_Branch_Line" title="Westerham Valley Branch Line">Westerham</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mid-Wales_Railway" title="Mid-Wales Railway">Mid-Wales Railway</a> (parts).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Neath_and_Brecon_Railway" title="Neath and Brecon Railway">Neath and Brecon Railway</a></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Monkerei&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Monkerei (page does not exist)">Monkerei</a> Tunnel in <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> – double track size at summit reduces fume problem in tunnel.</li>
<li>Golgotha Tunnel near <a href="/wiki/Eythorne" title="Eythorne">Eythorne</a> on the <a href="/wiki/East_Kent_Light_Railway" title="East Kent Light Railway">East Kent Light Railway</a> was only partly excavated for the second track.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Skitube_Alpine_Railway" title="Skitube Alpine Railway">Skitube Alpine Railway</a> – A provision for a second 300-metre-long passing loop has been made inside the tunnel.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Linha_do_Leste&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Linha do Leste (page does not exist)">Linha do Leste</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Linha_do_Alentejo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Linha do Alentejo (page does not exist)">Linha do Alentejo</a> in Portugal. The embankments in all bridges were built to double track but the second one was never installed.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Singling">Singling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Singling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Charlbury_rail_track_singling.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Charlbury_rail_track_singling.jpg/220px-Charlbury_rail_track_singling.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Charlbury_rail_track_singling.jpg/330px-Charlbury_rail_track_singling.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Charlbury_rail_track_singling.jpg/440px-Charlbury_rail_track_singling.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Charlbury_rail_track_singling.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rail track after singling, seen at <a href="/wiki/Charlbury_railway_station" title="Charlbury railway station">Charlbury station</a>, Oxfordshire, UK (before the line there was redoubled in 2011)</div>

<p>When the capacity of a double-track railway is in excess of requirements, the two tracks may be reduced to one. In some countries this is called <b>singling</b>. Notable examples of this in the United Kingdom occurred on the Oxford-Worcester-Hereford, Princes Risborough-Banbury and Salisbury-Exeter main lines during the 1970s and 1980s. In all these cases, increases in traffic from the late 1990s have led to the partial reinstatement of double track. In New Zealand the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melling_Line" title="Melling Line">Melling Line</a> was <i>singled</i> to the <a href="/wiki/Western_Hutt_Railway_Station" title="Western Hutt Railway Station">Western Hutt Railway Station</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lower_Hutt" title="Lower Hutt">Lower Hutt</a> in 1958 after it became a branch line rather than part of the main <a href="/wiki/Hutt_Valley_Line" title="Hutt Valley Line">Hutt Valley Line</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tunnel_singling">Tunnel singling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Tunnel singling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A double-track tunnel with restricted clearances is sometimes singled to form a single track tunnel with more generous clearances, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Connaught_Tunnel" title="Connaught Tunnel">Connaught Tunnel</a> in Canada or the Tickhole Tunnel in <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, Australia. In the case of the Tickhole Tunnel a new single-track tunnel was built and the two tracks in the original tunnel were replaced by one track in the centreline of the tunnel. Another case where this was necessary was the <a href="/wiki/Hastings_Line" title="Hastings Line">Hastings Line</a> in the United Kingdom, where the tunnels were eventually singled to permit the passage of standard <a href="/wiki/Loading_gauge#Great_Britain" title="Loading gauge">British-gauge</a> rolling stock. Before the singling, narrow-bodied stock, specially constructed for the line, had to be used.</p>
<p>As part of the <a href="/wiki/Regional_Fast_Rail_project" title="Regional Fast Rail project">Regional Fast Rail project</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victoria,_Australia" title="Victoria, Australia">Victoria, Australia</a>, the rail line between <a href="/wiki/Kyneton" title="Kyneton">Kyneton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bendigo" title="Bendigo">Bendigo</a> was converted from double- to single-track to provide additional clearance through tunnels and under bridges for trains travelling at up to 160 km/h (99 mph).</p>
<p>A similar process can be followed on narrow bridges (like the bridge north of <a href="/wiki/Drogheda_railway_station" title="Drogheda railway station">Drogheda railway station</a> in <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>).</p>
<p>The bridge over the <a href="/wiki/Murray_River" title="Murray River">Murray River</a> between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albury,_New_South_Wales" title="Albury, New South Wales">Albury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wodonga" title="Wodonga">Wodonga</a> is double-track, but because of insufficient strength in the bridge only one train is allowed on it at a time.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wartime_doubling">Wartime doubling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Wartime doubling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Railways that become especially busy in wartime and are duplicated, especially in World War I, may revert to single track when peace arises and the extra capacity is no longer required. The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Passchendaele#British_plans_for_a_Flanders_campaign" title="Battle of Passchendaele">Flanders campaign</a> saw duplication of the <a href="/wiki/Hazebrouck" title="Hazebrouck">Hazebrouck</a>–<a href="/wiki/Ypres" title="Ypres">Ypres</a> line, amongst other works.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Triple_track">Triple track</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Triple track">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Severe gradients made the headway in the uphill direction much worse than the headway in the downhill direction. Between <a href="/wiki/Whittingham_railway_station" title="Whittingham railway station">Whittingham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maitland,_New_South_Wales" title="Maitland, New South Wales">Maitland, New South Wales</a>, a third track is being provided<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2016)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> to equalize the headway in both directions for heavy coal traffic. Triple track could be a compromise between double-track and <a href="/wiki/Quadruple_track" title="Quadruple track">quad-track</a>; such a system was proposed south of <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_Central_Station" title="Stockholm Central Station">Stockholm Central Station</a>, but was cancelled in favor of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citybanan" title="Citybanan">Citybanan</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> several double track lines have a third track signalled in both directions, so that two lines are available in the peak direction in the AM and PM peaks.</p>
<p>Triple track is used in some parts of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Subway" title="New York City Subway">New York City Subway</a> to supplement rush-hour service on some lines.</p>
<p>The Union Pacific Railroad mainline through Nebraska has a 108-mile stretch of triple track between North Platte NE and Gibbon Junction NE, due to a high traffic density of 150 trains per day.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dual_gauge">Dual gauge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Dual gauge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> to <a href="/wiki/Albury" title="Albury">Albury</a> railway originally consisted of separate <span class="nowrap">1,600 mm</span> (<span class="nowrap">5 ft 3 in</span>) gauge and <span class="nowrap">1,435 mm</span> (<span class="nowrap">4 ft <span class="frac">8<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> in</span>) gauge single track lines, but when traffic on the broad gauge declined, the lines were converted to <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bidirectional" title="wikt:bidirectional">bi-directional</a> double track <span class="nowrap">1,435 mm</span> (<span class="nowrap">4 ft <span class="frac">8<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> in</span>) gauge lines.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quadruple_track">Quadruple track</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Quadruple track">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Quadruple_track" title="Quadruple track">Quadruple track</a> consists of four parallel tracks. On a quad-track line, faster trains can overtake slower ones. Quadruple track is mostly used when there are "local" trains that stop often (or slow freight trains), and also faster inter-city or high-speed "express" trains. It can also be used in <a href="/wiki/Commuter_rail" title="Commuter rail">commuter rail</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rapid_transit" title="Rapid transit">rapid transit</a>, usually with the two outer tracks carrying the local trains that stop at every station, while the inner tracks carry the faster express trains that stop at fewer, busier stations; this can also be reversed, with express trains on the outside and locals on the inside. At the local train stations, the express trains pass through the station at full speed.</p>
<p>The most notable example of quadruple track in the United States, and perhaps the only four-track section of mainline therein, was the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a>'s main corridor through the heart of <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> around the famous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horseshoe_Curve" title="Horseshoe Curve">Horseshoe Curve</a>. This line is now owned by Norfolk Southern. Other examples include the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Line_(Metro-North)" title="Hudson Line (Metro-North)">Hudson</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Haven_Line_(Metro-North)" title="New Haven Line (Metro-North)">New Haven</a> Lines, both of which are shared between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metro-North" title="Metro-North">Metro-North</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a> in New York and Connecticut. The New Haven Line is quadruple track along its entire length, while the Hudson Line is only quadruple tracked along the shared portion from <a href="/wiki/Riverdale_(Metro-North_station)" title="Riverdale (Metro-North station)">Riverdale</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Croton%E2%80%93Harmon_(Metro-North_station)" title="Croton–Harmon (Metro-North station)">Croton–Harmon</a> and along the shared track from <a href="/wiki/Grand_Central_Terminal" title="Grand Central Terminal">Grand Central Terminal</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yankees_%E2%80%93_East_153rd_Street_(Metro-North_station)" title="Yankees – East 153rd Street (Metro-North station)">Yankees – East 153rd Street</a>.</p>
<p>Outside the United States the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chuo_Main_Line" title="Chuo Main Line">Chuo Main Line</a> is an example of a modern, heavily utilized urban quadruple track railway.</p>
<p>Quadruple track is used in rapid transit systems as well: throughout the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Subway" title="New York City Subway">New York City Subway</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicago_%27L%27" title="Chicago 'L'">Chicago 'L'</a>'s <a href="/wiki/North_Side_Main_Line_(CTA)" title="North Side Main Line (CTA)">North Side Main Line</a>, and <a href="/wiki/SEPTA" title="SEPTA">SEPTA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Broad_Street_Line" title="Broad Street Line">Broad Street Line</a> in the United States, and on the <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a> in the United Kingdom.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oddities">Oddities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Oddities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-parallel_double_track">Non-parallel double track</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Non-parallel double track">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The two tracks of a double-track railway do not have to follow the same alignment if the terrain is difficult. At <a href="/wiki/Frampton,_New_South_Wales" title="Frampton, New South Wales">Frampton, New South Wales</a>, Australia, the uphill track follows something of a horseshoe curve at 1 in 75 gradient, while the shorter downhill track follows the original single track at 1 in 40 grades.</p>
<p>A similar arrangement to Frampton could not be adopted between <a href="/wiki/Rydal,_New_South_Wales" title="Rydal, New South Wales">Rydal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sodwalls" title="Sodwalls">Sodwalls</a> because the 1 in 75 uphill track is on the wrong side of the 1 in 40 downhill track, so both tracks follow the 1 in 75 grade. Another example is at <a href="/wiki/Gunning,_New_South_Wales" title="Gunning, New South Wales">Gunning</a>.</p>
<p>Between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Junee,_New_South_Wales" title="Junee, New South Wales">Junee</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Marina,_New_South_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Marina, New South Wales (page does not exist)">Marina, New South Wales</a>, Australia the two tracks are at different levels, with the original southbound and downhill track following ground level with a steep gradient, while the newer northbound and uphill track has a gentler gradient at the cost of more <a href="/wiki/Cut_and_fill" title="Cut and fill">cut and fill</a>.</p>
<p>At <a href="/wiki/Bethungra" title="Bethungra">Bethungra</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, Australia, the downhill track follows the original short and steep alignment, while the uphill track follows a longer, more easily graded alignment including a <a href="/wiki/Spiral_(railway)" title="Spiral (railway)">spiral</a>.</p>
<p>At <a href="/wiki/Saunderton" title="Saunderton">Saunderton</a>, United Kingdom, what became the London-to-Birmingham main line of the Great Western Railway in 1909 was initially part of a single-track branch line from Maidenhead. Down trains follow the route of the old branch line, while up trains follow a more gently graded new construction through a tunnel. This scheme avoided the cost of a new double-track tunnel.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Directional_running">Directional running</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Directional running">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Directional running is when two separate lines are operationally combined to act as a double-track line by converting each line to unidirectional traffic. An example of directional running occurs in central <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Western_Pacific_Railroad" title="Western Pacific Railroad">Western Pacific</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Pacific_Railroad" title="Southern Pacific Railroad">Southern Pacific Railroads</a>, longtime rivals who each built and operated tracks between <a href="/wiki/Northern_California" title="Northern California">northern California</a> and <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a>, agreed to share their lines between meeting points near <a href="/wiki/Winnemucca,_Nevada" title="Winnemucca, Nevada">Winnemucca</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wells,_Nevada" title="Wells, Nevada">Wells</a>, a distance of approximately 180 miles (290 km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yuccamountain_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-yuccamountain-8">[8]</a></sup> Westbound trains from both companies used the Southern Pacific's <a href="/wiki/Overland_Route_(Union_Pacific_Railroad)" title="Overland Route (Union Pacific Railroad)">Overland Route</a> while eastbound trains used the Western Pacific's <a href="/wiki/Feather_River_Route" title="Feather River Route">Feather River Route</a> (now called the <a href="/wiki/Central_Corridor_(Union_Pacific_Railroad)" title="Central Corridor (Union Pacific Railroad)">Central Corridor</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-benchmark_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-benchmark-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Union_Pacific_Railroad" title="Union Pacific Railroad">Union Pacific Railroad</a> has since acquired both of these lines, but continues to operate them as separate lines in a directional running setup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Nevada example, crossovers were constructed where the lines ran in close proximity to allow reverse movements. This was necessary as at points the two tracks are several miles apart and some destinations can only be accessed from one of the lines. In other cases, where the shared lines already run in close proximity, the two companies may share facilities. In <a href="/wiki/Conshohocken,_Pennsylvania" title="Conshohocken, Pennsylvania">Conshohocken, Pennsylvania</a>, where the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reading_Railroad" title="Reading Railroad">Reading Railroad</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a> shared lines, the lines even shared overhead electrical wire supports, for a 2-mile stretch on the northern bank of the <a href="/wiki/Schuylkill_River" title="Schuylkill River">Schuylkill River</a>. Both lines eventually came under <a href="/wiki/Conrail" title="Conrail">Conrail</a> ownership in 1976, with the former PRR line being abandoned and now used as a hike/bike path.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>An unusual example used to exist on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a>, where until 1926 parallel tracks between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smallbrook_Junction" title="Smallbrook Junction">Smallbrook Junction</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ryde_St_Johns_Road_railway_station" title="Ryde St Johns Road railway station">St John's Road</a> existed. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Railway_(Great_Britain)" title="Southern Railway (Great Britain)">Southern Railway</a> installed the actual junction, but it was only used during heavily trafficked summer months. During the winter, the lines reverted to separate single-track routes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mixing_double_and_single_track">Mixing double and single track</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-track_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Mixing double and single track">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because double and single track may use different signalling systems, it may be awkward and confusing to mix double and single track too often. For example, intermediate mechanical signal boxes on a double-track line can be closed during periods of light traffic, but this cannot be done if there is a single-line section in between. This problem is less serious with electrical signalling such as <a href="/wiki/Centralized_traffic_control" title="Centralized traffic control">Centralized traffic control</a>.</p>
