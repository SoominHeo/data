<p><b>Evangelism</b> is the preaching of <a href="/wiki/The_gospel" title="The gospel">the gospel</a> or the practice of giving information about a particular <a href="/wiki/Doctrine" title="Doctrine">doctrine</a> or set of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beliefs" title="Beliefs">beliefs</a> to others with the intention of <a href="/wiki/Conversion_to_Christianity" title="Conversion to Christianity">converting others to the Christian faith</a>.</p>
<p>This term is not restricted to any particular Christian tradition, and should not be confused with <a href="/wiki/Evangelicalism" title="Evangelicalism">Evangelicalism</a>, a common term for a wide range of "Evangelical" <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> churches and groups.</p>
<p>Christians who specialize in evangelism are often known as evangelists, whether they are in their home communities or living as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Missionaries" title="Missionaries">missionaries</a> in the field, although some Christian traditions refer to such people as <i>missionaries</i> in either case. Some Christian traditions consider evangelists to be in a leadership position; they may be found preaching to large meetings or in governance roles.</p>
<p>Christian groups who actively encourage evangelism are sometimes known as evangelistic or <i>evangelist</i>. The scriptures do not use the word <i>evangelism</i>, but <i>evangelist</i> is used in (the translations of) <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Acts+21%3A8&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Acts 21:8</a></span>, <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Ephesians+4%3A11&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Ephesians 4:11</a></span>, and <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=2+Timothy+4%3A5&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">2 Timothy 4:5</a></span>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Good_news_(Christianity)" title="Good news (Christianity)">Good news (Christianity)</a></div>
<p>The word "evangelist" comes from the <a href="/wiki/Koine_Greek" title="Koine Greek">Koine Greek</a> word <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B5%E1%BD%90%CE%B1%CE%B3%CE%B3%CE%AD%CE%BB%CE%B9%CE%BF%CE%BD" title="wiktionary:εὐαγγέλιον">εὐαγγέλιον</a></span> (transliterated as <i>euangelion</i>) via Latinised <i>evangelium</i> as used in the canonical titles of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Four_Gospels" title="Four Gospels">Four Gospels</a>, authored by (or attributed to) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matthew_the_Evangelist" title="Matthew the Evangelist">Matthew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_the_Evangelist" title="Mark the Evangelist">Mark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luke_the_Evangelist" title="Luke the Evangelist">Luke</a>, and <a href="/wiki/John_the_Evangelist" title="John the Evangelist">John</a> (also known as the <a href="/wiki/Four_Evangelists" title="Four Evangelists">Four Evangelists</a>). The Greek word <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">εὐαγγέλιον</span> originally meant a reward given to the messenger for good news (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">εὔ</span> = "good", <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἀγγέλλω</span> = "I bring a message"; the word "<a href="/wiki/Angel" title="Angel">angel</a>" comes from the same root) and later "good news" itself.</p>
<p>The verb form of <i>euangelion</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (translated as "evangelism"), occurs rarely in older Greek literature outside the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, making its meaning more difficult to ascertain. Parallel texts of the Gospels of Luke and Mark reveal a synonymous relationship between the verb <i>euangelizo</i> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B5%E1%BD%91%CE%B1%CE%B3%CE%B3%CE%B5%CE%BB%CE%AF%CE%B6%CF%89" title="wiktionary:εὑαγγελίζω">εὑαγγελίζω</a></span>) and a Greek verb <i>kerusso</i> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BA%CE%B7%CF%81%CF%8D%CF%83%CF%83%CF%89" title="wiktionary:κηρύσσω">κηρύσσω</a></span>), which means "to proclaim".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proselytism">Proselytism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Proselytism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Proselytism" title="Proselytism">Proselytism</a></div>
<p>While <i>evangelism</i> is usually regarded as converting non-Christians to Christianity, this is not always the proper usage of the word. If converting to Christianity includes services or material benefits, evangelism is called <i>proselytism</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Different denominations follow different <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theological</a> interpretations which reflect upon the point of who is doing the actual conversion, whether the evangelist or the Holy Spirit or both. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calvinists" title="Calvinists">Calvinists</a>, for example, believe the soul is converted only if the Holy Spirit is effective in the act.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholic</a> missionary work in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> is commonly seen as evangelism, not proselytism. Archbishop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tadeusz_Kondrusiewicz_(archbishop)" title="Tadeusz Kondrusiewicz (archbishop)">Kondrusiewicz</a> openly stated, "that proselytism is absolutely unacceptable and cannot constitute a strategy for the development of our structures either in Russia or in any other country in the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Especially regarding claims by the Orthodox church that spreading the faith and receiving converts amounts to proselytism,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Congregation_for_the_Doctrine_of_the_Faith" title="Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith">Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith</a> issued a document called "Doctrinal Note on some Aspects of Evangelization"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> which states that evangelism is "an inalienable right and duty, an expression of religious liberty ...", and added, "The incorporation of new members into the Church is not the expansion of a power group, but rather entrance into the network of friendship with Christ which connects heaven and earth, different continents and age. It is entrance into the gift of communion with Christ...."</p>
<p><br/>
In recent history, certain <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> passages have been used to promote evangelism. <a href="/wiki/William_Carey_(missionary)" title="William Carey (missionary)">William Carey</a>, in a book entitled, 'An Enquiry into the Obligations of Christians to Use Means for the Conversion of the Heathens' popularised a quotation, where, according to the Bible, during his last days on earth <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> commanded his eleven disciples (the apostles) as follows:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Therefore go and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, and teaching them to obey everything I have commanded you. And surely I am with you always, to the very end of the age.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">Matthew</a> 28:19,20 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NIV" title="NIV">NIV</a></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>However, recent scholarship by Chris Wright and others has suggested that such activity is promoted by the entire Bible, or at least the wider term 'mission', although the meaning of the word 'mission' and its relationship to 'evangelism' is disputed amongst Christians.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_methods">Modern methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Approaches_to_evangelism" title="Approaches to evangelism">Approaches to evangelism</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Evangelism_in_Vienna.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Evangelism in Vienna - Westbahonhof</div>
</div>

<p>Breaking from tradition and going beyond television and radio a wide range of methods have been developed to reach people not inclined to attend traditional events in churches or revival meetings.</p>
<p>Dramas such as <i><a href="/wiki/Heaven%27s_Gates,_Hell%27s_Flames" title="Heaven's Gates, Hell's Flames">Heaven's Gates, Hell's Flames</a></i> have gained enormous popularity since the 1980s. These dramas typically depict fictional characters who die and learn whether they will go to heaven or hell.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Child_evangelism_movement" title="Child evangelism movement">child evangelism movement</a> is a Christian evangelism movement that originated in the 20th century. It focuses on the <b>4/14 Window</b> which centers on evangelizing children between the ages of 4 and 14 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4-14core_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-4-14core-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beginning in the 1970s, a group of Christian athletes known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Power_Team" title="The Power Team">The Power Team</a> spawned an entire genre of Christian entertainment based on strong-man exploits mixed with a Christian message and usually accompanied by an opportunity to respond with a prayer for salvation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other entertainment-based Christian evangelism events include comedy, live theater and music.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Christian_music_industry" title="Christian music industry">Christian music industry</a> has also played a significant role in modern evangelism. <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">Rock</a> (and other genres) concerts in which the artist(s) exhort non-believing attendees to pray a prayer for salvation have become common, and just as common are concerts that are focused on activity not necessarily on prayer and conversion, thus forming an environment that is not driven by conversion, but instead relaying of a message.</p>
<p>Evangelists such as <a href="/wiki/Reinhard_Bonnke" title="Reinhard Bonnke">Reinhard Bonnke</a> conduct mass evangelistic crusades around the world. Hundreds of church denominations and organizations participate in an evangelism movement known as the <a href="/wiki/Billion_Soul_Harvest" title="Billion Soul Harvest">Billion Soul Harvest</a>, which is a comprehensive initiative to convert a billion people to Christianity.</p>

<p>Sometimes, the regular minister of a church is called a <i>preacher</i> in a way that other groups would typically use the term <i><a href="/wiki/Pastor" title="Pastor">pastor</a></i>. The <i>evangelist</i> in some churches is one that travels from town to town and from church to church, spreading <a href="/wiki/The_gospel" title="The gospel">the gospel</a> of Jesus Christ. Many Christians of various <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theological</a> perspectives would call themselves "evangelists" because they are spreaders of the gospel. Many churches believe one of their major functions is to function as evangelists to spread the evangelist belief that Jesus is savior of humanity.</p>
<p>The title of evangelist is often associated with those who lead large meetings like those of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billy_Graham_(evangelist)" title="Billy Graham (evangelist)">Billy Graham</a>, Luis Palau<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and J.A. Pérez<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> possibly in tents or existing <a href="/wiki/Church_(building)" title="Church (building)">church buildings</a>, or those who address the public in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_air_preaching" title="Open air preaching">street corner preaching</a>, which targets listeners who happen to pass nearby. It can also be done in small groups or even on a one-to-one basis, but actually it is simply one who spreads the gospel. Increasingly, the internet enables anyone to become an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internet_evangelist" title="Internet evangelist">Internet evangelist</a>.</p>

<p>The New Testament urges believers to speak <a href="/wiki/The_gospel" title="The gospel">the gospel</a> clearly, fearlessly, graciously, and respectfully whenever an opportunity presents itself (see <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Colossians+4%3A2-6&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Colossians 4:2-6</a></span>, <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Ephesians+6%3A19-20&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Ephesians 6:19-20</a></span>, and <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=1+Peter+3%3A15&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">1 Peter 3:15</a></span>).</p>
<p>Throughout most of its history, Christianity has been spread evangelistically, though the extent of evangelism has varied significantly between Christian communities, and denominations. Evangelism, <a href="/wiki/Apologetics" title="Apologetics">apologetics</a> and apostolic ministry often go hand in hand. An <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%80%CF%80%CF%8C%CF%83%CF%84%CE%BF%CE%BB%CE%BF%CF%82" title="wiktionary:ἀπόστολος">ἀπόστολος</a> (<i>apostolos</i>) is literally "one who is ordered forth" and refers to the <a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">missionary</a> calling of being ordered forth into the world by the initiation of God. An example of an interplay between Evangelism and Apologetics can be seen in the USA when upon door to door Evangelism the prospect is an unbeliever and challenges the Evangelist wherein the Evangelist then follows into the role of the Apologist in defense of their faith with the hope that Evangelism may be restarted. Since missionaries often travel to areas or people groups where Jesus is not yet known, they frequently take on an evangelistic role. But the apostolic or missionary calling is not necessarily the same (and it is a misnomer and misinterpretation to equate them), as there are many who serve in missionary, church planting, and ministry development roles who have an apostolic calling or serve in an apostolic role but whose primary duty is not evangelism.</p>
