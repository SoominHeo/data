<p><b>The Von Bondies</b> was an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a> <a href="/wiki/Musical_ensemble" title="Musical ensemble">band</a> active from 1997 to 2011.</p>
<p>The original line-up formed at the 1997 <a href="/wiki/The_Cramps" title="The Cramps">Cramps</a>/<a href="/wiki/Guitar_Wolf" title="Guitar Wolf">Guitar Wolf</a> show by <a href="/wiki/Jason_Stollsteimer" title="Jason Stollsteimer">Jason Stollsteimer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marcie_Bolen" title="Marcie Bolen">Marcie Bolen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> They went through a variety of member changes and band names, including The Baby Killers, before settling on The Von Bondies in 2000. Don Blum joined the band around 1999 after attending numerous Baby Killers shows, while Leann Banks was picked via an audition. The Von Bondies got their break by playing a New Year's Eve show in <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a> in 2000. In attendance at the show was <a href="/wiki/Long_Gone_John" title="Long Gone John">Long Gone John</a>, owner of the <a href="/wiki/Sympathy_for_the_Record_Industry" title="Sympathy for the Record Industry">Sympathy for the Record Industry</a> label. This led to Sympathy releasing the band's debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Lack_of_Communication" title="Lack of Communication">Lack of Communication</a></i> in 2001.</p>
<p>In 2003, the band released the live <i>Raw and Rare</i> through <a href="/wiki/Dim_Mak_Records" title="Dim Mak Records">Dim Mak Records</a>, which was followed by their 2004 breakthrough release, <i><a href="/wiki/Pawn_Shoppe_Heart" title="Pawn Shoppe Heart">Pawn Shoppe Heart</a></i>, on <a href="/wiki/Sire_Records" title="Sire Records">Sire Records</a>. <i>Pawn Shoppe</i> was produced by <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Harrison" title="Jerry Harrison">Jerry Harrison</a> of <a href="/wiki/Talking_Heads" title="Talking Heads">Talking Heads</a> and co-produced by Stollsteimer.</p>
<p>The album reached a peak of No. 36 in the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-British_Hit_Singles_.26_Albums_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-British_Hit_Singles_.26_Albums-2">[2]</a></sup> and No. 8 on <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a>'</i>s Top Heatseekers chart and stayed in that chart for eight weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The hit single from this release was "C'mon C'mon", which reached No. 25 on the Billboard <a href="/wiki/Alternative_Songs" title="Alternative Songs">Alternative Songs</a> chart and garnered national radio play.</p>
<p>A shortened version of "C'mon C'mon" was the theme song to the TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Rescue_Me_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Rescue Me (U.S. TV series)">Rescue Me</a></i> and was performed live by the band in <a href="/wiki/Michael_Winterbottom" title="Michael Winterbottom">Michael Winterbottom</a>'s film <i><a href="/wiki/9_Songs" title="9 Songs">9 Songs</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/MLB_Network" title="MLB Network">MLB Network</a> also uses a brief clip of the song as the opening of their show <i>30 Clubs in 30 Days</i>.</p>
<p>In 2008, the band signed with indie label Majordomo Records,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> joining label mates <a href="/wiki/The_Airborne_Toxic_Event" title="The Airborne Toxic Event">The Airborne Toxic Event</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Earlimart_(band)" title="Earlimart (band)">Earlimart</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Their label debut, <i><a href="/wiki/Love,_Hate_and_Then_There%27s_You" title="Love, Hate and Then There's You">Love, Hate and Then There's You</a></i>, was released in February 2009 featuring the single "Pale Bride".</p>
<p>Known as a touring act, The Von Bondies have headlined tours of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>/<a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, taking along supporting bands like <a href="/wiki/The_Kills" title="The Kills">The Kills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kasabian" title="Kasabian">Kasabian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franz_Ferdinand_(band)" title="Franz Ferdinand (band)">Franz Ferdinand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Modey_Lemon" title="Modey Lemon">Modey Lemon</a>, <a href="/wiki/SSM_(band)" title="SSM (band)">SSM</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Subways" title="The Subways">The Subways</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Stills" title="The Stills">The Stills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hot_Panda" title="Hot Panda">Hot Panda</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Donnas" title="The Donnas">The Donnas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> They have also appeared on <i><a href="/wiki/Late_Show_with_David_Letterman" title="Late Show with David Letterman">Late Show with David Letterman</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Last_Call_with_Carson_Daly" title="Last Call with Carson Daly">Last Call with Carson Daly</a></i> and <i>CD:UK</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The group disbanded in July 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Its lineup at the time was <a href="/wiki/Jason_Stollsteimer" title="Jason Stollsteimer">Jason Stollsteimer</a> on vocals and lead guitar, Christy Hunt on rhythm guitar and Leann Banks on bass guitar.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Von_Bondies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_days">Early days</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Von_Bondies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early days">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2000, Stollsteimer and <a href="/wiki/Marcie_Bolen" title="Marcie Bolen">Marcie Bolen</a> attended a concert by Japanese <a href="/wiki/Garage_punk" title="Garage punk">garage punk</a> band <a href="/wiki/Guitar_Wolf" title="Guitar Wolf">Guitar Wolf</a>. At the time, Stollsteimer was working a job as a bowling alley bartender and Marcie as a hairdresser. The performance spurred Stollsteimer to create his own band, The Baby Killers, which toured with fellow Detroit bands <a href="/wiki/The_Detroit_Cobras" title="The Detroit Cobras">The Detroit Cobras</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Go" title="The Go">The Go</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_White_Stripes" title="The White Stripes">The White Stripes</a>. After recruiting Lauren Wilcox on bass and <a href="/wiki/Don_Blum" title="Don Blum">Don Blum</a> on drums the band changed their name to the Von Bondies.</p>
<p>While playing a handful of shows in the <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a> area, the quartet recorded singles "It Came from Japan", an ode to Guitar Wolf, and "Nite Train".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lack_of_Communication_.282001.E2.80.932002.29"><i>Lack of Communication</i> (2001–2002)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Von_Bondies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lack of Communication (2001–2002)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jack_White_(musician)" title="Jack White (musician)">Jack White</a> produced the Von Bondies' debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Lack_of_Communication" title="Lack of Communication">Lack of Communication</a></i>, in late 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It was recorded in three days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> It was released in 2001 by <a href="/wiki/Sympathy_for_the_Record_Industry" title="Sympathy for the Record Industry">Sympathy for the Record Industry</a>, and in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a> by Sweet Nothing Records. The hidden bonus track was a <a href="/wiki/Cover_version" title="Cover version">cover</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sam_Cooke" title="Sam Cooke">Sam Cooke</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Bring_It_On_Home_to_Me" title="Bring It On Home to Me">Bring It On Home to Me</a>", with Bolen on lead vocals. The band said this is the least expensive album they made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pawn_Shoppe_Heart_.282003.E2.80.932005.29"><i>Pawn Shoppe Heart</i> (2003–2005)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Von_Bondies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pawn Shoppe Heart (2003–2005)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The group relocated to a <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> recording studio in early 2002 with producer <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Harrison" title="Jerry Harrison">Jerry Harrison</a> to begin work on <i><a href="/wiki/Pawn_Shoppe_Heart" title="Pawn Shoppe Heart">Pawn Shoppe Heart</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Broken_ties">Broken ties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Von_Bondies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Broken ties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On the evening of December 13, 2003, an altercation occurred between Stollsteimer and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_Stripes" title="White Stripes">White Stripes</a> frontman <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jack_White_(musician)" title="Jack White (musician)">Jack White</a> during the record release party for the band <a href="/wiki/Blanche_(band)" title="Blanche (band)">Blanche</a> at The Magic Stick (a Detroit music club). Stollsteimer was treated for injuries at <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Receiving_Hospital" title="Detroit Receiving Hospital">Detroit Receiving Hospital</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Detroit police arrested White and the Wayne County prosecutor's office charged him with aggravated assault.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> White pleaded guilty to assault and a judge sentenced him to anger management classes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Love.2C_Hate_and_Then_There.27s_You_.282009.E2.80.932011.29"><i>Love, Hate and Then There's You</i> (2009–2011)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Von_Bondies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Love, Hate and Then There's You (2009–2011)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Von Bondies' third album is <i><a href="/wiki/Love,_Hate_and_Then_There%27s_You" title="Love, Hate and Then There's You">Love, Hate and Then There's You</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It was released on February 3, 2009. They released a limited-edition 7-inch single of "Pale Bride" from the album, backed with the non-album song "Falling in Love".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Von Bondies celebrated their ninth year together with this release. This was the first time that a Von Bondies release saw Don Blum co-write with Stollsteimer. <i>Love, Hate</i> was produced by Jason Stollsteimer, with three songs by Butch Walker and three songs by Rick Parker. All songs were written by Jason Stollsteimer, except "Blame Game" and "Earthquake", which were co-written by Stollsteimer and Blum.</p>
