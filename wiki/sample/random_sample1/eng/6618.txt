<p>A <b>bollard</b> is a sturdy, short, vertical post. Although it originally described a post on a <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ship</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wharf" title="Wharf">quay</a> used principally for <a href="/wiki/Mooring_(watercraft)" title="Mooring (watercraft)">mooring</a> boats, the word is now used to describe posts installed to control road traffic and posts designed to prevent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ram_raiding" title="Ram raiding">ram raiding</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car_ramming_attack" title="Car ramming attack">car ramming attacks</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bollard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term is probably related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bole_(botany)" title="Bole (botany)">bole</a>, meaning a tree trunk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-oed-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The earliest citation given by the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> (referring to a maritime bollard) dates from 1844,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-oed-1">[1]</a></sup> although a reference in the <i><a href="/wiki/Caledonian_Mercury" title="Caledonian Mercury">Caledonian Mercury</a></i> in 1817 describes bollards as huge posts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Previously, simpler terms such as "post" appear to have been used. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman-French" title="Norman-French">Norman-French</a> name "Boulard" (still often found in <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>) may be related.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bollard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>From the 17th and 18th centuries, old <a href="/wiki/Cannon" title="Cannon">cannon</a> were often used as bollards on quaysides to help moor ships alongside. The cannon would be buried in the ground muzzle-first to approximately half or two-thirds of their length, leaving the breech (rear end) projecting above ground for attaching ropes. Such cannon can still occasionally be found. Ninteenth-century bollards were purpose-made, but often inherited a very similar "cannon" shape.</p>
<p>Wooden posts were used for basic traffic management from at least the beginning of the 18th century. An early well-documented case is that of the "two oak-posts" set up next to the medieval <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_cross" title="Eleanor cross">Eleanor cross</a> at <a href="/wiki/Waltham_Cross" title="Waltham Cross">Waltham Cross</a>, Hertfordshire, in 1721, at the expense of the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Antiquaries_of_London" title="Society of Antiquaries of London">Society of Antiquaries of London</a>, "to secure Waltham Cross from injury by Carriages".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Similar posts can be seen in many historic paintings and engravings.</p>
<p>In the Netherlands, the <i><a href="/wiki/Amsterdammertje" title="Amsterdammertje">Amsterdammertjes</a></i> of Amsterdam were first erected in the 19th century. They became popular symbols of the city, but they are now gradually being removed and replaced with elevated sidewalks.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bollard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maritime_bollards">Maritime bollards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bollard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Maritime bollards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Mooring_(watercraft)" title="Mooring (watercraft)">Mooring (watercraft)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bolder-Merwedekanaal.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="2448" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Bolder-Merwedekanaal.jpg/170px-Bolder-Merwedekanaal.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Bolder-Merwedekanaal.jpg/255px-Bolder-Merwedekanaal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Bolder-Merwedekanaal.jpg/340px-Bolder-Merwedekanaal.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bolder-Merwedekanaal.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Merwede-Canal, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utrecht_(city)" title="Utrecht (city)">Utrecht</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> features bollards made from cannon</div>

<p>In the maritime contexts in which the term originates, a bollard is either a wooden or iron post found as a deck-fitting on a ship or boat, and used to secure ropes for towing, mooring and other purposes; or its counterpart on land, a short wooden, iron or stone post on a quayside to which craft can be moored. The <i>Sailor's Word-Book</i> of 1867 defines a bollard in a more specific context as "a thick piece of wood on the head of a whale-boat, round which the harpooner gives the line a turn, in order to veer it steadily, and check the animal's velocity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-oed-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-oed-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mooring bollards are seldom exactly cylindrical, but typically have a larger diameter near the top to discourage mooring warps (docklines) from coming loose. Single bollards sometimes include a cross rod to allow the mooring lines to be bent into a figure eight. Small mushroom-bollards are found on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lock_(water_transport)" title="Lock (water transport)">lock</a> approaches for advancing boats waiting for lock access.</p>
<p>A conventional measure of the pulling or towing power of a <a href="/wiki/Watercraft" title="Watercraft">watercraft</a> is known as <a href="/wiki/Bollard_pull" title="Bollard pull">bollard pull</a>, and is defined as the <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> exerted by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_vessel" title="Marine vessel">vessel</a> under full power on a shore-mounted bollard through a tow-line.</p>

</div>
</div>
<p>According to <i>Trafficcalming.org</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> bollards can be used, either to control traffic intake size by limiting movements, or to control traffic speed by narrowing the available space. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Technion" title="Technion">Israel's Transportation Research Institute</a> found that putting bollards at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Highway_exit" title="Highway exit">highway exits</a> to control traffic also reduced accidents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Permanent bollards intended for traffic-control purposes may be mounted near enough to each other that they block ordinary cars, for instance, but spaced widely enough to permit special-purpose vehicles and bicycles to pass through. Bollards may also be used to enclose <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car-free_zone" title="Car-free zone">car-free zones</a>. Bollards and other <a href="/wiki/Street_furniture" title="Street furniture">street furniture</a> are used to control <a href="/wiki/Overspill_parking" title="Overspill parking">overspill parking</a> onto <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sidewalks" title="Sidewalks">sidewalks</a> and verges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DfTpavement_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-DfTpavement-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BollardsOnWorksite.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="493" height="207" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/BollardsOnWorksite.jpg/170px-BollardsOnWorksite.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/BollardsOnWorksite.jpg/255px-BollardsOnWorksite.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/BollardsOnWorksite.jpg/340px-BollardsOnWorksite.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BollardsOnWorksite.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bollards can be temporary and portable, as this traffic control bollard separating the road from the worksite.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Tall (1.15 meter/4 foot) slim (10 cm/4 inch) fluorescent red or orange plastic bollards with reflective tape and removable heavy rubber bases are frequently used in <a href="/wiki/Road_traffic_control" title="Road traffic control">road traffic control</a> where <a href="/wiki/Traffic_cone" title="Traffic cone">traffic cones</a> would be inappropriate due to their width and ease of movement. Also referred to as "delineators", the bases are usually made from recycled rubber, and can be easily glued to the road surface to resist movement following minor impacts from passing traffic. The term "T-top bollards" refers to the T-bar moulded into the top for tying tape. Bollards are regarded as an economical and safe delineation system for motorways and busy arterial roads; and, in conjunction with plastic tape, for pedestrian control.</p>
<p>Traffic bollards used in the US are very similar to devices found throughout the UK, with the following exceptions:</p>
<ul>
<li>The traffic bollard shell displays the <a href="/wiki/Manual_on_Uniform_Traffic_Control_Devices" title="Manual on Uniform Traffic Control Devices">MUTCD</a> (Manual of Uniform <a href="/wiki/Road_traffic_control" title="Road traffic control">Traffic Control</a> Devices) "Keep Right" symbol (R4-7). In addition, the traffic bollard also has a yellow diamond below the "Keep Right" symbol instead of a yellow shield.</li>
<li>Unlike many existing traffic bollards found in the UK, most new modern traffic bollards installed along roadways today are made of materials that make them completely collapsible. When struck by a vehicle at low or high speed, the traffic bollard shell reverts to its original position with minimal to no damage to the unit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Illuminated_bollards">Illuminated bollards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bollard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Illuminated bollards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Internally illuminated traffic bollards have been in existence throughout the United Kingdom since the 1930s, although the term "bollard" only seems to have been in common use since the late 1940s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-oed-1">[1]</a></sup> An illuminated bollard has a recessed base light unit in the foundation which illuminates the traffic bollard from all angles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The main components are housed below the surface of the improvement (typically a concrete surface). Therefore, if a vehicle strikes the traffic bollard, the units below the surface are not damaged.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bollards2742Canthusus.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Bollards2742Canthusus.jpg/170px-Bollards2742Canthusus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Bollards2742Canthusus.jpg/255px-Bollards2742Canthusus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Bollards2742Canthusus.jpg/340px-Bollards2742Canthusus.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bollards2742Canthusus.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Internally illuminated traffic bollards direct vehicles to the appropriate side of an island in the United Kingdom.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>They are primarily used at <a href="/wiki/Roundabout" title="Roundabout">roundabout</a> <a href="/wiki/Intersection_(road)" title="Intersection (road)">intersections</a> within the splitter islands (a raised or painted area on the approach of a roundabout used to separate entering from exiting traffic, deflect and slow entering traffic, and provide a stopping place for pedestrians crossing the road in two stages)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and at the ends of <a href="/wiki/Refuge_island" title="Refuge island">pedestrian refuge</a> islands, typically located at mid-block pedestrian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crosswalk" title="Crosswalk">crosswalks</a>. Illuminated bollards are also used in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, a former British colony.</p>
<p>Illuminated bollards are also used to supplement <a href="/wiki/Traffic_sign" title="Traffic sign">street signs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Street_light" title="Street light">street lighting</a> to provide a visual cue to approaching drivers that an obstacle exists ahead during hours of darkness and during periods of low visibility:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> (fog, rain, snow, haze, etc.) and to indicate that braking may be required.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retroreflection" title="Retroreflection">Reflective</a> bollards may also be used; they need no power or maintenance, and can be built to recover to their normal position after being struck.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-surrey_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-surrey-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bell_bollards">Bell bollards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bollard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Bell bollards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bell_bollard.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="597" data-file-width="796" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Bell_bollard.png/220px-Bell_bollard.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Bell_bollard.png/330px-Bell_bollard.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Bell_bollard.png/440px-Bell_bollard.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bell_bollard.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A bell bollard is especially useful to deflect heavy vehicles.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A bell bollard is a style of bollard designed to deflect vehicle tires. The wheel mounts the lower part of the bollard and is deflected by its increasing slope. Such bollards are effective against heavy goods vehicles that may damage or destroy conventional bollards or other types of street furniture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> A variant on the bell bollard is the "Martello bollard", its sloped shape inspired by the profile of historic <a href="/wiki/Martello_tower" title="Martello tower">Martello towers</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Removable_bollards">Removable bollards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bollard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Removable bollards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Bollards may be hinged at ground level, allowing them to be folded flat to permit vehicles to drive over them. In such cases they are generally fitted with padlocks at the base, to prevent being lowered without proper authorization.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Retractible_Bollard.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2512" data-file-width="1668" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Retractible_Bollard.JPG/170px-Retractible_Bollard.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Retractible_Bollard.JPG/255px-Retractible_Bollard.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Retractible_Bollard.JPG/340px-Retractible_Bollard.JPG 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Retractible_Bollard.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rising bollards can be retracted into the roadway to allow traffic to pass, or deployed to stop it.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Removable bollards may be fitted into a permanent metal <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ground_socket&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ground socket (page does not exist)">ground socket</a>, from which they can be removed entirely to allow traffic to pass. A polypropylene ground socket is also available that protects the paving and foundations from damage when the bollard is struck. This design uses a self-locking taper to enable bollards to be easily removed and relocated.</p>
<p>Retractable or "rising" bollards can be lowered entirely below the road surface (generally using an electric or hydraulic mechanism) to enable traffic to pass, or raised to block traffic. Rising bollards are used to secure sensitive areas from attack, or to enforce traffic rules that are time related, or to restrict access to particular classes of traffic.</p>
<p>Manually retractable bollards (lowered by a key mechanism) are found useful in some cases since they do not require retrofitting into existing landscapes, or any electrical hookups or hydraulic systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term "robotic bollards" has been applied to traffic barricades capable of moving themselves into position on a roadway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Self-righting or self-recovering bollards can take a nudge from a vehicle and return to the upright position without causing damage to the bollard or vehicle. They are popular in car park buildings and other areas of high vehicle usage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reboundable_Bollards_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reboundable_Bollards-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Flexible_bollards">Flexible bollards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bollard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Flexible bollards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Flexible bollards are bollards designed to bend when struck by vehicles. They are typically made from synthetic plastic or rubber that is stiff on its own, but pliable under the weight of a car or truck. When struck, flexible bollards typically bend 90 degrees to ground—avoiding damage to vehicles and surrounding surfaces—and return to their original, upright position. While flexible bollards do not provide physical protection from vehicles, they offer clear visual guidance for drivers.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Racing_bollards">Racing bollards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bollard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Racing bollards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some forms of <a href="/wiki/Motorsport" title="Motorsport">motorsport</a> use removable, high-visibility bollards on <a href="/wiki/Road_racing" title="Road racing">road courses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Street_circuit" title="Street circuit">street courses</a> to mark the apex of certain corners. They are used to deter cutting a corner too tightly and violating track limits: in most racing series, drivers may incur a penalty for colliding with or driving inside these bollards.</p>
<p>Racing bollards are very lightweight and built to break off at the base when hit so as to not damage the vehicle; this feature of bollards makes them favored over "sausage kerbs"—also used to prevent cutting corners—which are elevated rigid structures that run along the inside of a turn. In contrast to bollards, sausage kerbs are much more unforgiving, and vehicles that hit them can be severely damaged or launched airborne upon contact.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protective_bollards">Protective bollards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bollard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Protective bollards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bollard_ramkraakbeveiliging.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1120" data-file-width="840" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Bollard_ramkraakbeveiliging.jpg/170px-Bollard_ramkraakbeveiliging.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Bollard_ramkraakbeveiliging.jpg/255px-Bollard_ramkraakbeveiliging.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Bollard_ramkraakbeveiliging.jpg/340px-Bollard_ramkraakbeveiliging.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Security_bollard-planter_in_Washington,_D.C.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="598" data-file-width="797" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Security_bollard-planter_in_Washington%2C_D.C.jpg/220px-Security_bollard-planter_in_Washington%2C_D.C.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Security_bollard-planter_in_Washington%2C_D.C.jpg/330px-Security_bollard-planter_in_Washington%2C_D.C.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Security_bollard-planter_in_Washington%2C_D.C.jpg/440px-Security_bollard-planter_in_Washington%2C_D.C.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Security_bollard-planter_in_Washington,_D.C.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A planter provides protection in Washington, D.C.</div>

</div>
<p>Bollards are used by government agencies and private businesses to protect public spaces, buildings, and the people in them from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car_ramming_attack" title="Car ramming attack">car ramming attacks</a>. They usually consist of a simple steel post either anchored to concrete, cored into a hard surface, buried in the ground or secured on a self-locking taper or impact recovery system to protect the surrounding foundations when a bollard is struck.</p>
<p>These bollards <a href="/wiki/Utility_location" title="Utility location">protect utilities</a>, electronics, machinery, buildings, or pedestrians from accidental collisions by vehicles, including passenger vehicles, transport trucking, and fork lifts. As collisions can cause damage to vehicles, operators, or the bollards themselves, new bollards have been developed that absorb some of the impact energy, lessening the violence of the collision. Some are made of forgiving plastics while others are made of steel but fitted with an <a href="/wiki/Elastomer" title="Elastomer">elastomer</a> to absorb the impact energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whole_Building_Design_Guide_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whole_Building_Design_Guide-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bollards are widely used to contribute to <a href="/wiki/Safety" title="Safety">safety</a> and <a href="/wiki/Security" title="Security">security</a>. The <a href="/wiki/American_Bar_Association" title="American Bar Association">American Bar Association</a> (ABA) states that bollards are used to contribute to <a href="/wiki/Homeland_security" title="Homeland security">homeland security</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The American <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Building_Sciences" title="National Institute of Building Sciences">National Institute of Building Sciences</a> site—the Whole Building Design Guide (WBDG)—recommends in its Design Guidance that open spaces surrounding and contiguous to buildings be included as integral parts of a <a href="/wiki/Crime_prevention_through_environmental_design" title="Crime prevention through environmental design">security design</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are two main kinds of security-related bollard:</p>
<ul>
<li>non crash-resistant bollards</li>
<li>crash- and attack-resistant bollards, a hardened barrier systems used to protect military, governmental and other buildings or compounds of higher security levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gov2010_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-gov2010-23">[23]</a></sup> They are often used by retailers to prevent <a href="/wiki/Ram-raiding" title="Ram-raiding">"ram-raiding" burglaries</a>. Large concrete <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flower_pot" title="Flower pot">planters</a> are a decorative alternative to traditional bollards.</li>
</ul>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Building_Sciences" title="National Institute of Building Sciences">National Institute of Building Sciences</a>, non crash-resistant bollards are "perceived impediments to access" and address the actions of two groups.</p>
<ul>
<li>Law-abiding persons who comply with civil prescriptions of behavior as defined by the manner in which bollards are put to use;</li>
<li>Potentially threatening and disruptive persons for whom bollard applications are proscriptive by announcing their behavior is anticipated, and that additional levels of security await them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oakes2010_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-oakes2010-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Rising bollards are increasingly common around the world to hinder vehicle-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrorist" title="Terrorist">terrorist</a> actions from achieving close proximity to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buildings" title="Buildings">buildings</a>, and are also used to prevent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vehicle_ramming_as_a_terrorism_tactic" title="Vehicle ramming as a terrorism tactic">terrorist vehicle ramming attacks</a> such as in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2007_Glasgow_International_Airport_attack" title="2007 Glasgow International Airport attack">2007 Glasgow International Airport attack</a>. They are also useful in mixed-use public spaces, which support both pedestrian use and emergency or service vehicle use. These bollards are usually priced between $11,000 to $100,000 depending on their ability to resist ramming vehicles, based on speed. The most expensive bollards can stop vehicles at speeds of about 50 mph (80 km/h).</p>
<p>Bollard sleeves in various alloys or finishes are designed to cover security bollards to enhance their visual attractiveness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bollard_Materials_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bollard_Materials-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dragon.27s_teeth">Dragon's teeth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bollard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Dragon's teeth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Large concrete structures called <a href="/wiki/Dragon%27s_teeth_(fortification)" title="Dragon's teeth (fortification)">dragon's teeth</a> were invented during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> to slow down, stop or channel enemy <a href="/wiki/Tank" title="Tank">tanks</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bollard_lights">Bollard lights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bollard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Bollard lights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bollard lights are a type of architectural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outdoor_lighting" title="Outdoor lighting">outdoor lighting</a> fixture comprising short, upright ground-mounted units, typically giving off light from the top or the sides, and used to illuminate walkways, steps or pathways.</p>
<p>Solar bollards store energy during daylight hours in order to illuminate during night. They use either a photocell or algorithms that determine whether the solar cells are receiving light to determine when to illuminate.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_fire_emergencies">In fire emergencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bollard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: In fire emergencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=International_Fire_Code&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="International Fire Code (page does not exist)">International Fire Code</a> (IFC-2009) and the American <a href="/wiki/National_Fire_Protection_Association" title="National Fire Protection Association">National Fire Protection Association</a> Fire Code 1 (NFPA-1) all new buildings or renovated buildings must have fire access roadways to accommodate fire apparatus and crews and other first responders. Thus the choice of bollard styles must apply to the NFPA’s Code 1710. Bollards are now designed in terms of how long it takes to remove or collapse them to allow first responders entry to the access roadway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trails_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-trails-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architectural_design_functions">Architectural design functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bollard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Architectural design functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Listed below are the building design objectives and requirements under fire or security related instances on campuses and their relationship to the use of bollards according to the Association of Higher Education Facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Accessible: fire lane bollards will accommodate persons using wheelchairs</li>
<li>Aesthetics: the fire lane and its bollard style complements surrounding landscape design</li>
<li>Cost-Effective: fire lane system life cycle costing</li>
<li>Functional/Operational: Building architect determines if entrances to building envelope can accommodate first responders or whether entrances require retrofitting, and authority having jurisdiction and first responders determine if the fire lane can readily be accessed through the bollards under emergency conditions</li>
<li>Historic Preservation: City planner address the special needs in historic districts and of historic buildings to accommodate fire lanes and bollards.</li>
<li>Productive: The HR officer comments on felt security of those working in the protected building, and all first responders pass on their ability to perform under fire mitigation conditions</li>
<li>Secure/Safe: Input by building safety or security officer and representative first responders to assure hallways and stairwells can accommodate first responders and that the fire lane is free of all obstructions 24/7</li>
<li>Sustainable: Landscape architect and surveyor comment on long term sustainability of area impacted by fire lane and use by first responders.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artwork">Artwork</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bollard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Artwork">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Geelong_Bollards.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1536" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Geelong_Bollards.jpg/170px-Geelong_Bollards.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Geelong_Bollards.jpg/255px-Geelong_Bollards.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Geelong_Bollards.jpg/340px-Geelong_Bollards.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<i>Ex libris</i> bollard outside <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University_Library" title="Cambridge University Library">Cambridge University Library</a>, by Harry Gray</div>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Geelong" title="Geelong">Geelong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, decorative bollards, sculpted and painted by <a href="/wiki/Jan_Mitchell" title="Jan Mitchell">Jan Mitchell</a>, are placed around the city to enhance the landscape as a form of outdoor public <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">sculpture</a>. Usually they are made of timber, minimally modified from the traditionally cylindrical, wooden, maritime bollard shape, but brightly painted to resemble human figures. Such figures – which may be historical or contemporary, particular or generic – are sited singly or in clusters along the waterfront and in other areas where people gather. Decorative bollards have become a well-known feature of the city of Geelong and reflect its history as a major Australian port.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antwerp,_Belgium" title="Antwerp, Belgium">Antwerp, Belgium</a>, artist Eddy Gabriel transformed a bollard to look like a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toadstool" title="Toadstool">toadstool</a> in 1993. This example was followed by other artists, turning the quayside of the river <a href="/wiki/Scheldt" title="Scheldt">Scheldt</a> into a street art gallery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Norwich" title="Norwich">Norwich</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, a set of 21 bollards was installed in 2008 in the Lanes area north of City Hall, designed by artist Oliver Creed and commissioned by the City Council as part of a regeneration programme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> They are coloured "<a href="/wiki/Rose_madder" title="Rose madder">madder</a> red", in reference to the red dye extracted from the <a href="/wiki/Rubia_tinctorum" title="Rubia tinctorum">madder</a> plant and used for <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">dying</a> cloth, one of the city's major industries during the 16th century; and they bear <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> <a href="/wiki/Finial" title="Finial">finials</a> also alluding to local history. 10 of these depict the madder plant, while the other 11 have unique designs, usually relevant to the specific location in which the bollard is placed, including a scene of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sheep-shearing" title="Sheep-shearing">sheep-shearing</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Green_Man" title="Green Man">Green Man</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Swan" title="Swan">swan</a>'s head in Swan Lane, and so on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the <a href="/wiki/Forecourt" title="Forecourt">forecourt</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University_Library" title="Cambridge University Library">Cambridge University Library</a>, England, a line of 14 bronze bollards made to resemble piles of books was installed in 2009. This work, <i>Ex Libris</i>, was created by sculptor <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Harry_Gray_(sculptor)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Harry Gray (sculptor) (page does not exist)">Harry Gray</a>. The ten outer bollards are static, but the "books" making up the four central bollards can be swiveled, so that the lettering on their spines aligns to form the Latin phrase <i>Ex Libris</i> ("from/out of the books"), commonly used on <a href="/wiki/Bookplate" title="Bookplate">bookplates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many other towns, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winchester,_England" title="Winchester, England">Winchester, England</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pristina,_Kosovo" title="Pristina, Kosovo">Pristina, Kosovo</a>, have painted ordinary bollards in unusual decorative patterns. See the "External links" section for pointers to Wikimedia Commons for examples.</p>
