<p><b>South Tyrol</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ladin_language" title="Ladin language">Ladin</a>: <span lang="lld"><i>Südtirol</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[ˈsyːtiˌroːl, ˈzyːttiˌʁoːl]</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <span lang="it"><i>Sudtirolo</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Italian" title="Help:IPA for Italian">[suttiˈrɔːlo]</a></span>), also known by its alternative Italian name <b>Alto Adige</b> (<small>pronounced </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Italian" title="Help:IPA for Italian">[ˈalto ˈaːdidʒe]</a></span>), is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_area" title="Autonomous area">autonomous</a> <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Italy" title="Provinces of Italy">province</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. It is one of the two autonomous provinces that make up the autonomous <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_Italy" title="Regions of Italy">region</a> of <a href="/wiki/Trentino-Alto_Adige/S%C3%BCdtirol" title="Trentino-Alto Adige/Südtirol">Trentino-Alto Adige/Südtirol</a>. The province has an area of 7,400 square kilometres (2,857 sq mi) and a total population of 511,750 inhabitants (31.12.2011). Its capital is the city of <a href="/wiki/Bolzano" title="Bolzano">Bolzano</a> (German: <i>Bozen</i>; Ladin: <i>Balsan</i> or <i>Bulsan</i>).</p>

<p>According to 2014 data based on the 2011 census, 62.3 percent of the population speaks <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> (<a href="/wiki/Standard_German" title="Standard German">Standard German</a> in the written norm and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austro-Bavarian_dialect" title="Austro-Bavarian dialect">Austro-Bavarian dialect</a> in the spoken form); 23.4 percent of the population speaks <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>, mainly in and around the two largest cities (Bolzano and <a href="/wiki/Merano" title="Merano">Merano</a>); 4.1 percent speaks <a href="/wiki/Ladin_language" title="Ladin language">Ladin</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Rhaeto-Romance_languages" title="Rhaeto-Romance languages">Rhaeto-Romance language</a>; 10.2% of the population (mainly recent immigrants) speaks another language as first language.</p>
<p>South Tyrol is granted a considerable level of self-government, consisting of a large range of exclusive legislative and executive powers and a fiscal regime that allows the province to retain a large part of most levied taxes, while nevertheless remaining a net contributor to the national budget. As of 2011, South Tyrol is among the wealthiest regions in Italy and the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>.</p>
<p>In the wider context of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, the province is one of the three members of the <a href="/wiki/Euroregion" title="Euroregion">Euroregion</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tyrol-South_Tyrol-Trentino" title="Tyrol-South Tyrol-Trentino">Tyrol-South Tyrol-Trentino</a>, which corresponds almost exactly to the historical region of <a href="/wiki/County_of_Tyrol" title="County of Tyrol">Tyrol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Tyrol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:(1874)_The_Valleys_of_TIROL.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2108" data-file-width="2734" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/%281874%29_The_Valleys_of_TIROL.jpg/220px-%281874%29_The_Valleys_of_TIROL.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/%281874%29_The_Valleys_of_TIROL.jpg/330px-%281874%29_The_Valleys_of_TIROL.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/%281874%29_The_Valleys_of_TIROL.jpg/440px-%281874%29_The_Valleys_of_TIROL.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:(1874)_The_Valleys_of_TIROL.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A map from 1874 showing <i>South Tirol</i> with approximately the borders of today's South and <a href="/wiki/East_Tyrol" title="East Tyrol">East Tyrol</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>South Tyrol</i> (occasionally <i>South Tirol</i>) is the term most commonly used in English for the province,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and its usage reflects that it was created from a portion of the southern part of the historic <a href="/wiki/County_of_Tyrol" title="County of Tyrol">County of Tyrol</a>. German and Ladin speakers usually refer to the area as <i>Südtirol</i>; the Italian equivalent <i>Sudtirolo</i> (sometimes spelled <i>Sud Tirolo</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>) is becoming increasingly common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Alto Adige</i> (literally translated in English: "Upper Adige"), one of the Italian names for the province, is also used in English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britishcouncil_article_using_.27Alto_Adige.27_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Britishcouncil_article_using_.27Alto_Adige.27-5">[5]</a></sup> The term had been the name of political subdivisions along the <a href="/wiki/Adige" title="Adige">Adige River</a> in the time of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">Napoleon Bonaparte</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> who created the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Alto_Adige" title="Department of Alto Adige">Department of Alto Adige</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(Napoleonic)" title="Kingdom of Italy (Napoleonic)">Napoleonic Kingdom of Italy</a>. It was reused as the Italian name of the current province after its post-World War I creation, and was a symbol of the subsequent forced <a href="/wiki/Italianization" title="Italianization">Italianization</a> of South Tyrol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The official name of the province today in German is <i>Autonome Provinz Bozen — Südtirol</i>. German speakers usually refer to it not as a <i>Provinz</i>, but as a <i>Land</i> (like the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">Länder</a> of Germany and Austria).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Provincial institutions are referred to using the prefix <i>Landes-</i>, such as <i>Landesregierung</i> (state government) and <i><a href="/wiki/Landeshauptmann" title="Landeshauptmann">Landeshauptmann</a></i> (governor).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The official name in Italian is <i>Provincia autonoma di Bolzano — Alto Adige</i>, in Ladin <i>Provinzia autonoma de Balsan/Bulsan — Südtirol</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Tyrol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_South_Tyrol" title="History of South Tyrol">History of South Tyrol</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Annexation_by_Italy">Annexation by Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Tyrol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Annexation by Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>South Tyrol as an administrative entity originated during the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_I" title="Allies of World War I">Allies</a> promised the area to Italy in the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_London_(1915)" title="Treaty of London (1915)">Treaty of London</a> of 1915 as an incentive to enter the war on their side. Until 1918 it was part of the Austro-Hungarian princely <a href="/wiki/County_of_Tyrol" title="County of Tyrol">County of Tyrol</a>, but this almost completely German-speaking territory was occupied by Italy at the end of the war in November 1918 and was annexed to the Kingdom of Italy in 1919. The province as it exists today was created in 1926 after an administrative reorganization of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(1861%E2%80%931946)" title="Kingdom of Italy (1861–1946)">Kingdom of Italy</a>, and was incorporated together with the province of Trento into the newly created region of <i>Venezia Tridentina</i> ("Trentine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venetia_(region)" title="Venetia (region)">Venetia</a>").</p>
<p>With the rise of <a href="/wiki/Italian_Fascism" title="Italian Fascism">Fascism</a>, the new regime made efforts to bring forward the <a href="/wiki/Italianization_of_South_Tyrol" title="Italianization of South Tyrol">Italianization of South Tyrol</a>. The German language was banished from public service, German teaching was officially forbidden, and German newspapers were censored (with the exception of the fascist <i>Alpenzeitung</i>). The regime also favored immigration from other Italian regions.</p>
<p>The subsequent alliance between <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a> declared that South Tyrol would not follow the destiny of Austria, which <a href="/wiki/Anschluss" title="Anschluss">had been annexed</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Reich" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a>. Instead the dictators agreed that the German-speaking population be transferred to German-ruled territory or dispersed around Italy, but the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a> prevented them from fully carrying out their intention.</p>
<p>In 1943, when the Italian government signed <a href="/wiki/Armistice_of_Cassibile" title="Armistice of Cassibile">an armistice</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a>, the region was occupied by <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, which reorganised it as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Zone_of_the_Alpine_Foothills" title="Operation Zone of the Alpine Foothills">Operation Zone of the Alpine Foothills</a> and put it under the administration of <a href="/wiki/Gauleiter" title="Gauleiter">Gauleiter</a> <a href="/wiki/Franz_Hofer" title="Franz Hofer">Franz Hofer</a>. The region was <i>de facto</i> annexed to the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">German Reich</a> (with the addition of the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Belluno" title="Province of Belluno">province of Belluno</a>) until the end of the war. This status ended along with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> regime, and Italian rule was restored in 1945.</p>

<p>After the war the Allies decided that the province would remain a part of Italy, under the condition that the German-speaking population be granted a significant level of self-government.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Italy and Austria negotiated an agreement in 1946, recognizing the rights of the German minority.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Alcide_De_Gasperi" title="Alcide De Gasperi">Alcide De Gasperi</a>, Italy's prime minister, a native of Trentino, wanted to extend the autonomy to his fellow citizens.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This led to the creation of the region called <i>Trentino-Alto Adige/Tiroler Etschland</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gruber-De_Gasperi_Agreement" title="Gruber-De Gasperi Agreement">Gruber-De Gasperi Agreement</a> of September 1946 was signed by the Italian and Austrian Foreign Ministers, creating the autonomous region of Trentino-South Tyrol, consisting of the autonomous provinces of Trentino and South Tyrol.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> German and Italian were both made official languages, and German-language education was permitted once more. Still Italians were the majority in the combined region.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>This, together with the arrival of new Italian-speaking immigrants, led to strong dissatisfaction among South Tyroleans, which culminated in terrorist acts perpetrated by the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Befreiungsausschuss_S%C3%BCdtirol" title="Befreiungsausschuss Südtirol">Befreiungsausschuss Südtirol</a></i> (BAS — Committee for the Liberation of South Tyrol).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In a first phase, only public edifices and fascist monuments were targeted.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The second phase was bloodier, costing 21 lives (15 members of Italian security forces, two civilians, and four terrorists).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="S.C3.BCdtirolfrage">Südtirolfrage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Tyrol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Südtirolfrage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The South Tyrolean question (<i>Südtirolfrage</i>) became an international issue. As the implementation of the post-war agreement was not seen as satisfactory by the Austrian government,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> it became a cause of significant friction with Italy and was taken up by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> in 1960.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A fresh round of negotiations took place in 1961 but proved unsuccessful, partly because of the campaign of <a href="/wiki/Terrorism" title="Terrorism">terrorism</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The issue was resolved in 1971, when a new Austro-Italian treaty was signed and ratified<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. It stipulated that disputes in South Tyrol would be submitted for settlement to the <a href="/wiki/International_Court_of_Justice" title="International Court of Justice">International Court of Justice</a> in <a href="/wiki/The_Hague" title="The Hague">The Hague</a>, that the province would receive greater autonomy within Italy, and that Austria would not interfere in South Tyrol's internal affairs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The new agreement proved broadly satisfactory to the parties involved, and the separatist tensions soon eased.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The new autonomous status, granted from 1972 onwards, has resulted in a considerable level of self-government,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Danspeckgruber_2002_193_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Danspeckgruber_2002_193-11">[11]</a></sup> also due to the large financial resources of South Tyrol, retaining almost 90% of all levied taxes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1992, Italy and Austria officially ended their dispute over the autonomy issue on the basis of the agreement of 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The extensive self-government<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Danspeckgruber_2002_193_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Danspeckgruber_2002_193-11">[11]</a></sup> provided by the current institutional framework has been advanced as a model for settling interethnic disputes and for the successful protection of linguistic minorities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> This is among the reasons why the Ladin municipalities of <a href="/wiki/Cortina_d%27Ampezzo" title="Cortina d'Ampezzo">Cortina d'Ampezzo</a>/Anpezo, <a href="/wiki/Livinallongo_del_Col_di_Lana" title="Livinallongo del Col di Lana">Livinallongo del Col di Lana</a>/Fodom and <a href="/wiki/Colle_Santa_Lucia" title="Colle Santa Lucia">Colle Santa Lucia</a>/Col have asked in a referendum to be detached from Veneto and reannexed to the province, from which they were separated under the fascist government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Euroregion">Euroregion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Tyrol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Euroregion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>In 1996, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euroregion_Tyrol-South_Tyrol-Trentino" title="Euroregion Tyrol-South Tyrol-Trentino">Euroregion Tyrol-South Tyrol-Trentino</a> was formed between the Austrian state of Tyrol and the Italian provinces of South Tyrol and Trentino. The boundaries of the association correspond to the old County of Tyrol. The aim is to promote regional peace, understanding and cooperation in many areas. The region's assemblies meet together as one on various occasions, and have set up a common liaison office with the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> in Brussels.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Tyrol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_South_Tyrol_(de).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="501" data-file-width="686" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Map_of_South_Tyrol_%28de%29.png/220px-Map_of_South_Tyrol_%28de%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Map_of_South_Tyrol_%28de%29.png/330px-Map_of_South_Tyrol_%28de%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Map_of_South_Tyrol_%28de%29.png/440px-Map_of_South_Tyrol_%28de%29.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_South_Tyrol_(de).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Detailed map of South Tyrol.</div>

<p>South Tyrol is located at the northernmost point in Italy. The province is bordered by Austria to the east and north, specifically by the Austrian federal-states <a href="/wiki/Tyrol_(state)" title="Tyrol (state)">Tyrol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salzburg_(state)" title="Salzburg (state)">Salzburg</a>, and by the <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Swiss</a> canton of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graub%C3%BCnden" title="Graubünden">Graubünden</a> to the west. The Italian provinces of <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Belluno" title="Province of Belluno">Belluno</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Province_of_Trentino" title="Province of Trentino">Trentino</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Sondrio" title="Province of Sondrio">Sondrio</a> border to the southeast, south, and southwest, respectively.</p>
<p>The landscape itself is mostly cultivated with different types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shrubs" title="Shrubs">shrubs</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forests" title="Forests">forests</a> and is highly mountainous.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drei_zinnen_gross.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Drei_zinnen_gross.jpg/220px-Drei_zinnen_gross.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Drei_zinnen_gross.jpg/330px-Drei_zinnen_gross.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Drei_zinnen_gross.jpg/440px-Drei_zinnen_gross.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Entirely located in the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>, the province's landscape is dominated by mountains. The highest peak is the <a href="/wiki/Ortler" title="Ortler">Ortler</a> (3,905 m) in the far west, which is also the highest peak in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Alps" title="Eastern Alps">Eastern Alps</a> outside the <a href="/wiki/Bernina_Range" title="Bernina Range">Bernina Range</a>. Even more famous are the craggy peaks of the <a href="/wiki/Dolomites" title="Dolomites">Dolomites</a> in the eastern part of the region.</p>
<p>The following mountain groups are (partially) in South Tyrol. All but the Sarntal Alps are on the border with Austria, Switzerland, or other Italian provinces. The ranges are clockwise from the west and for each the highest peak is given that is within the province or on its border.</p>

<p>Located in between the mountains are a large number of <a href="/wiki/Valleys_of_South_Tyrol" title="Valleys of South Tyrol">valleys</a>, which is where the majority of the population lives.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Administrative_divisions">Administrative divisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Tyrol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Administrative divisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_of_South_Tyrol" title="Municipalities of South Tyrol">Municipalities of South Tyrol</a></div>
<p>The province is divided into eight districts (German: <i>Bezirksgemeinschaften</i>, Italian: <i>comunità comprensoriali</i>), one of them being the chief city of Bolzano. Each district is headed by a president and two bodies called the district committee and the district council. The districts are responsible for resolving intermunicipal disputes and providing roads, schools, and social services such as retirement homes.</p>
<p>The province is further divided into 116 <i><a href="/wiki/Gemeinde" title="Gemeinde">Gemeinden</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Comune" title="Comune">comuni</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stinf-2010_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-stinf-2010-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>Climatically, South Tyrol may be divided into five distinct groups:</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Adige" title="Adige">Adige</a> valley area, with cold winters (24-h averages in January of about 0 °C) and warm summers (24-h averages in July of about 23 °C), usually classified as <a href="/wiki/Humid_subtropical_climate" title="Humid subtropical climate">Humid subtropical climate</a> — Cfa. It has the driest and sunniest climate of the province. The main city in this area is <a href="/wiki/Bolzano" title="Bolzano">Bolzano</a>.</p>
<p>The midlands, between 300 and 900 metres, with cold winters (24-h averages in January between -3 °C and 1 °C) and mild summers (24-h averages in July between 15 °C and 21 °C); This is a typical <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">Oceanic climate</a>, classified as Cfb. It is usually wetter than the subtropical climate, and very snowy during the winters. During the spring and autumn, there is a large foggy season, but fog may occur even on summer mornings. Main towns in this area are <a href="/wiki/Merano" title="Merano">Merano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruneck" title="Bruneck">Bruneck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sterzing" title="Sterzing">Sterzing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Brixen" title="Brixen">Brixen</a>. Near the lakes in higher lands (between 1000 and 1400 meters) the humidity may make the climate in these regions milder during winter, but also cooler in summer, then, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subpolar_oceanic_climate" title="Subpolar oceanic climate">Subpolar oceanic climate</a>, Cfc, may occur.</p>
<p>The alpine valleys between 900 and 1400 metres, with a typically <a href="/wiki/Humid_continental_climate" title="Humid continental climate">Humid continental climate</a> — Dfb, covering the largest part of the province. The winters are usually very cold (24-h averages in January between -8 °C and -3 °C), and the summers, mild with averages between 14 and 19 °C. It is a very snowy climate; snow may occur from early October to April or even May. Main municipalities in this area are <a href="/wiki/Urtij%C3%ABi" title="Urtijëi">Urtijëi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Badia,_South_Tyrol" title="Badia, South Tyrol">Badia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sexten" title="Sexten">Sexten</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toblach" title="Toblach">Toblach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stilfs" title="Stilfs">Stilfs</a>, <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6ran" title="Vöran">Vöran</a>, and <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BChlwald" title="Mühlwald">Mühlwald</a>.</p>
<p>The alpine valleys between 1400 and 1700 metres, with a <a href="/wiki/Subarctic_climate" title="Subarctic climate">Subarctic climate</a> — Dfc, with harsh winters (24-h averages in January between -9 °C and -5 °C) and cool, short, rainy and foggy summers (24-h averages in July of about 12 °C). These areas usually have five months below the freezing point, and snow sometimes occurs even during the summer, in September. This climate is the wettest of the province, with large rainfalls during the summer, heavy snowfalls during spring and fall. The winter is usually a little drier, marked by freezing and dry weeks, although not sufficiently dry to be classified as a Dwc climate. Main municipalities in this area are <a href="/wiki/Corvara,_South_Tyrol" title="Corvara, South Tyrol">Corvara</a>, <a href="/wiki/S%C3%ABlva" title="Sëlva">Sëlva</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cristina_Gherd%C3%ABina" title="Santa Cristina Gherdëina">Santa Cristina Gherdëina</a>.</p>
<p>The highlands above 1700 meters, with an <a href="/wiki/Alpine_climate" title="Alpine climate">alpine tundra climate</a>, ET, which becomes an Ice Cap Climate, EF, above 3000 meters. The winters are cold, but sometimes not as cold as the higher valleys' winters. In January, most of the areas at 2000 meters have an average temperature of about -5 °C, while in the valleys at about 1600 meters, the mean temperature may be as low as -8 or -9 °C. The higher lands, above 3000 meters are usually extremely cold, with averages of about -14 °C during the coldest month, January.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Tyrol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The local government system is based upon the provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Italy" title="Constitution of Italy">Italian Constitution</a> and the Autonomy Statute of the Region <a href="/wiki/Trentino-Alto_Adige/S%C3%BCdtirol" title="Trentino-Alto Adige/Südtirol">Trentino-Alto Adige/Südtirol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The 1972 second Statute of Autonomy for Trentino-Alto Adige/Südtirol <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolved</a> most legislative and executive competences from the regional level to the provincial level, creating <i><a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a></i> two separate regions.</p>
<p>The considerable <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislative power</a> of the province is vested in an assembly, the <a href="/wiki/Landtag_of_South_Tyrol" title="Landtag of South Tyrol">Landtag of South Tyrol</a> (German: <i>Südtiroler Landtag</i>; Italian: <i>Consiglio della Provincia Autonoma di Bolzano</i>; Ladin: <i>Cunsëi dla Provinzia Autonoma de Bulsan</i>). The legislative powers of the assembly are defined by the second Statute of Autonomy.</p>
<p>The executive powers are attributed to the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_South_Tyrol" title="Government of South Tyrol">government</a> (German: <i>Landesregierung</i>; Italian: <i>Giunta Provinciale</i>) headed by the <i><a href="/wiki/Landeshauptmann" title="Landeshauptmann">Landeshauptmann</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Arno_Kompatscher" title="Arno Kompatscher">Arno Kompatscher</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> He belongs to the <i><a href="/wiki/South_Tyrolean_People%27s_Party" title="South Tyrolean People's Party">South Tyrolean People's Party</a></i>, which has been governing with a parliamentary majority since 1948. South Tyrol is characterized by long sitting presidents, having only had two presidents between 1960 and 2014 (Silvius Magnago 1960-1989, Luis Durnwalder 1989-2014).</p>
<p>A fiscal regime allows the province to retain a large part of most levied taxes, in order to execute and administrate its competences. Nevertheless, South Tyrol remains a net contributor to the Italian national budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secessionist_movement">Secessionist movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Tyrol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Secessionist movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/South_Tyrolean_secessionist_movement" title="South Tyrolean secessionist movement">South Tyrolean secessionist movement</a></div>
<p>Given the region's historical and cultural association with neighboring Austria, calls for the secession of South Tyrol and its reunification with Austria are recurrent in the local and national political climate. A poll conducted in 2013 by an Austrian research institute suggested that a slight majority of German- and Ladin-speakers could favor secession from Italy, although falling short of the overall majority in the province when considering also the Italian-speaking population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nat_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-nat-20">[20]</a></sup> Among the political parties that support South Tyrol's reunification into Austria are <a href="/wiki/South_Tyrolean_Freedom" title="South Tyrolean Freedom">South Tyrolean Freedom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Die_Freiheitlichen" title="Die Freiheitlichen">Die Freiheitlichen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Citizens%27_Union_for_South_Tyrol" title="Citizens' Union for South Tyrol">Citizens' Union for South Tyrol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Tyrol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>In 2011 South Tyrol had a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GDP_per_capita" title="GDP per capita">GDP per capita</a> of €37,700, making it one of the richest provinces in Italy and the European Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Residents are employed in a variety of sectors, from <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> — the province is a large producer of apples, and its wines are also renowned — to <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industry</a> to <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a>. The unemployment level in 2007 was roughly 2.4% (2.0% for men and 3.0% for women).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Tyrol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FerroviaDelBrenneroE645aFleres.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="509" data-file-width="800" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/FerroviaDelBrenneroE645aFleres.jpg/220px-FerroviaDelBrenneroE645aFleres.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/FerroviaDelBrenneroE645aFleres.jpg/330px-FerroviaDelBrenneroE645aFleres.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/FerroviaDelBrenneroE645aFleres.jpg/440px-FerroviaDelBrenneroE645aFleres.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The region is, together with northern and eastern Tyrol, an important transit point between southern Germany and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Italy" title="Northern Italy">Northern Italy</a>. Freights by road and rail pass through here. One of the most important highways is the <a href="/wiki/Autostrade_of_Italy" title="Autostrade of Italy">A22</a>, also called the <i>Autostrada del Brennero</i>. It connects to the <a href="/wiki/Brenner_Autobahn" title="Brenner Autobahn">Brenner Autobahn</a> in Austria.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_registration_plates_of_Italy" title="Vehicle registration plates of Italy">vehicle registration plate</a> of South Tyrol is the two-letter provincial code <span style="font-variant:small-caps">Bz</span> for the capital city, Bolzano. Along with the autonomous Trentino (<span style="font-variant:small-caps">Tn</span>) and <a href="/wiki/Aosta_Valley" title="Aosta Valley">Aosta Valley</a> (<span style="font-variant:small-caps">Ao</span>), South Tyrol is allowed to surmount its license plates with its coat of arms.</p>
<p>Rail transport goes over the <a href="/wiki/Brenner_Pass" title="Brenner Pass">Brenner Pass</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Brenner_Railway" title="Brenner Railway">Brenner Railway</a> is a major line connecting the <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport_in_Austria" title="Rail transport in Austria">Austrian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport_in_Italy" title="Rail transport in Italy">Italian railways</a> from <a href="/wiki/Innsbruck_Hauptbahnhof" title="Innsbruck Hauptbahnhof">Innsbruck</a> and <a href="/wiki/Verona_Porta_Nuova_railway_station" title="Verona Porta Nuova railway station">Verona</a> climbing the <a href="/wiki/Wipptal" title="Wipptal">Wipptal</a>, passing over the Brenner Pass and descending down the <a href="/wiki/Eisacktal" title="Eisacktal">Eisack Valley</a> to Bolzano and then down the Adige Valley from Bolzano to Rovereto and to Verona. The line is part of the <a href="/wiki/Berlin%E2%80%93Palermo_railway_axis" title="Berlin–Palermo railway axis">Line 1</a> of <a href="/wiki/Trans-European_Transport_Networks" title="Trans-European Transport Networks">Trans-European Transport Networks</a> (TEN-T).</p>
<p>Other railways are the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pustertalbahn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pustertalbahn (page does not exist)">Pustertalbahn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rittnerbahn" title="Rittnerbahn">Ritten Railway</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vinschgaubahn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vinschgaubahn (page does not exist)">Vinschgaubahn</a>. Due to the steep slopes of the mountains, a number of <a href="/wiki/Funicular" title="Funicular">funiculars</a> exist, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gardena_Ronda_Express_funicular" title="Gardena Ronda Express funicular">Gardena Ronda Express funicular</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mendel_Funicular" title="Mendel Funicular">Mendel Funicular</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Brenner_Base_Tunnel" title="Brenner Base Tunnel">Brenner Base Tunnel</a> is under construction and scheduled to be completed by 2025. It will have a length of 64 km and will become the world's longest railway tunnel. This tunnel will increase freight train average speed to 120 km/h and reduce transit time by over an hour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Larger cities used to have their own tramway system, such as the <a href="/wiki/Meran_Tramway" title="Meran Tramway">Meran Tramway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bolzano_Tramway" title="Bolzano Tramway">Bolzano Tramway</a>. These were replaced after the Second World War with buses. Many other cities and municipalities have their own bus system or are connected with each other by it.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bolzano_Airport" title="Bolzano Airport">Bolzano Airport</a> is the only airport serving the region.</p>

<p>German and Italian are both official languages of South Tyrol. In some eastern municipalities Ladin is the third official language. A majority of the inhabitants of contemporary South Tyrol speak native <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austro-Bavarian" title="Austro-Bavarian">Austro-Bavarian</a> dialects of the German language. <a href="/wiki/Standard_German" title="Standard German">Standard German</a> plays a dominant role in education and media.</p>
<p>Every citizen has the right to use their own mother tongue, even at court. Schools are separated for each language group.</p>
<p>All traffic signs are officially bi- or trilingual. Most Italian toponyms are translations performed by Italian nationalist <a href="/wiki/Ettore_Tolomei" title="Ettore Tolomei">Ettore Tolomei</a>, the author of the <a href="/wiki/Prontuario_dei_nomi_locali_dell%27Alto_Adige" title="Prontuario dei nomi locali dell'Alto Adige">Prontuario dei nomi locali dell'Alto Adige</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In order to reach a fair allocation of jobs in public service a system called ethnic proportion (Ita. <i>proporzionale etnica</i>, Ger. <i>ethnischer Proporz</i>) has been established. Every ten years, when the general census of population takes place, each citizen has to declare to which linguistic group they belong or want to be aggregated to. According to the results they decide how many people of which group are going to be hired for public service.</p>
<p>At the time of the annexation of the southern part of <a href="/wiki/County_of_Tyrol" title="County of Tyrol">Tyrol</a> by Italy in 1919, the overwhelming majority of the population spoke German and identified with the Austrian or German nationality: In 1910, according to the last population census before <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the German-speaking population numbered 224,000, the Ladin 9,000 and the Italian 7,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Provincial_Statistics_Institute_of_the_Autonomous_Province_of_South_Tyrol_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Provincial_Statistics_Institute_of_the_Autonomous_Province_of_South_Tyrol-25">[25]</a></sup> As a result of the italianization of South Tyrol nowadays about 23% of the population are Italian-speakers (they were roughly 35% in the 1960s). According to the census of 2011, 103 out of 116 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comuni" title="Comuni">comuni</a> have a majority of German native speakers — with <a href="/wiki/Martell,_South_Tyrol" title="Martell, South Tyrol">Martell</a> reaching 100% — eight have a Ladin-speaking majority, and five a majority of Italian speakers. The Italian-speaking population is mainly based around the provincial capital <a href="/wiki/Bolzano" title="Bolzano">Bolzano</a>, where they are the majority (73.8% of the inhabitants), and is a direct result of <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a>'s policy of Italianisation after he took power in 1922, when he encouraged immigration from the rest of Italy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The other four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comuni" title="Comuni">comuni</a> where the Italian-speaking population is the majority are <a href="/wiki/Laives" title="Laives">Laives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salorno" title="Salorno">Salorno</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bronzolo" title="Bronzolo">Bronzolo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vadena" title="Vadena">Vadena</a>. The eight <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comuni" title="Comuni">comuni</a> with <a href="/wiki/Ladin_language" title="Ladin language">Ladin</a> majorities are: <a href="/wiki/La_Val" title="La Val">La Val</a>, <a href="/wiki/Badia,_South_Tyrol" title="Badia, South Tyrol">Badia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corvara,_South_Tyrol" title="Corvara, South Tyrol">Corvara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mareo" title="Mareo">Mareo</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Martin_de_Tor" title="San Martin de Tor">San Martin de Tor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cristina_Gherd%C3%ABina" title="Santa Cristina Gherdëina">Santa Cristina Gherdëina</a>, <a href="/wiki/S%C3%ABlva" title="Sëlva">Sëlva</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urtij%C3%ABi" title="Urtijëi">Urtijëi</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Tyrol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>South Tyrol is predominantly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a>: 96.1% of resident population in the area of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Bolzano-Brixen" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Bolzano-Brixen">Roman Catholic Diocese of Bolzano-Brixen</a> (its territory corresponds with that of the province of South Tyrol) in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> There is a <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_Evangelical_Church_in_Italy" title="Lutheran Evangelical Church in Italy">Lutheran</a> community in <a href="/wiki/Merano" title="Merano">Merano</a> (founded 1861) and another one in <a href="/wiki/Bolzano" title="Bolzano">Bolzano</a> (founded 1889). Since the Middle Ages the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> presence has been documented in South Tyrol. In 1901 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synagoge" title="Synagoge">Synagoge</a> of Merano was built. Today (2015) about 14,000 <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Muslims</a> live in South Tyrol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<p>The region features a large number of castles and churches. Many of the castles were built by the local nobility and the Habsburg rulers. See <a href="/wiki/List_of_castles_in_South_Tyrol" title="List of castles in South Tyrol">List of castles in South Tyrol</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Museums">Museums</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Tyrol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Museums">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Among the major museums of South Tyrol are:</p>
<ul>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/South_Tyrol_Museum_of_Archaeology" title="South Tyrol Museum of Archaeology">South Tyrol Museum of Archaeology</a>, which has the mummy of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%96tzi_the_Iceman" title="Ötzi the Iceman">Ötzi the Iceman</a></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Museion_(Bozen)" title="Museion (Bozen)">Museion</a>, Museum of modern and contemporary art of Bolzano</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Messner_Mountain_Museum" title="Messner Mountain Museum">Messner Mountain Museum</a> of <a href="/wiki/Reinhold_Messner" title="Reinhold Messner">Reinhold Messner</a></li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Tyrol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bozner_Bergsteigerlied" title="Bozner Bergsteigerlied">Bozner Bergsteigerlied</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Hofer" title="Andreas Hofer">Andreas-Hofer-Lied</a> are considered to be the unofficial anthems of South Tyrol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk musical</a> group <a href="/wiki/Kastelruther_Spatzen" title="Kastelruther Spatzen">Kastelruther Spatzen</a> from <a href="/wiki/Kastelruth" title="Kastelruth">Kastelruth</a> and the rock band <a href="/wiki/Frei.Wild" title="Frei.Wild">Frei.Wild</a> from <a href="/wiki/Brixen" title="Brixen">Brixen</a> have received high recognition in the German-speaking part of the world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Award-winning <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">electronic music</a> producer <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Moroder" title="Giorgio Moroder">Giorgio Moroder</a> was born and raised in South Tyrol with German as his primary language.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Tyrol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>South Tyrolean sportspeople have been successful at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winter_sports" title="Winter sports">winter sports</a>. They regularly are a large part of Italy's contingent at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winter_Olympics" title="Winter Olympics">Winter Olympics</a>. <a href="/wiki/Reinhold_Messner" title="Reinhold Messner">Reinhold Messner</a>, widely regarded as the greatest mountain climber of all time, is the first climber to conquer the 14 highest mountains in the world, many of them — including <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a>, the highest of all — without the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bottled_oxygen" title="Bottled oxygen">oxygen tanks</a>. <a href="/wiki/Armin_Z%C3%B6ggeler" title="Armin Zöggeler">Armin Zöggeler</a> is a famous Italian <a href="/wiki/Luge" title="Luge">luger</a> and double Olympic champion. <a href="/wiki/Carolina_Kostner" title="Carolina Kostner">Carolina Kostner</a> is a talented <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Figure_skater" title="Figure skater">figure skater</a>. HC Interspar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolzano-Bozen_Foxes" title="Bolzano-Bozen Foxes">Bolzano-Bozen Foxes</a> are one of Italy's most successful ice hockey teams.</p>
