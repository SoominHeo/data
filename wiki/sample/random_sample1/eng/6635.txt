<p>The <b>Bombardier CRJ100</b> and <b>CRJ200</b> are a family of <a href="/wiki/Regional_airliner" title="Regional airliner">regional airliners</a> manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Aerospace" title="Bombardier Aerospace">Bombardier</a>, and based on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bombardier_Challenger_600" title="Bombardier Challenger 600">Canadair Challenger</a> <a href="/wiki/Business_jet" title="Business jet">business jet</a>. These regional jet models were formerly known as the <b>Canadair CRJ100</b> and <b>CRJ200</b>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombardier_CRJ200&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CRJ_cockpit.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/CRJ_cockpit.jpg/220px-CRJ_cockpit.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/CRJ_cockpit.jpg/330px-CRJ_cockpit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/CRJ_cockpit.jpg/440px-CRJ_cockpit.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

CRJ cockpit</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The aircraft was based on the Canadair Challenger design, which was purchased by Canadair from <a href="/wiki/Bill_Lear" title="Bill Lear">Bill Lear</a> in 1976.</p>
<p>The wide <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a> of the Challenger which seats 2 passengers on each side of the aisle suggested early on to Canadair officials that it would be straightforward to stretch the <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> to accommodate more seats, and there was a plan for a <b>Challenger 610E</b>, which would have had seating for 24 passengers. That lengthening did not occur, the effort being canceled in 1981, but the idea did not disappear.</p>
<p>In 1987, studies began for a much more ambitious stretched configuration, leading to the formal launch of the <b>Canadair Regional Jet</b> program in the spring of 1989. The "Canadair" name was retained despite the fact that Bombardier had bought out the company. The first of three development machines for the initial <b>CRJ100</b> performed its first flight from <a href="/wiki/Montr%C3%A9al%E2%80%93Mirabel_International_Airport" title="Montréal–Mirabel International Airport">Montréal–Mirabel International Airport</a> on 10 May 1991, though the first prototype (C-FCRJ) was lost in a spin mishap on July 26, 1993 near the Bombardier test center in <a href="/wiki/Wichita,_Kansas" title="Wichita, Kansas">Wichita</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The type obtained certification in late 1992, with initial delivery to customers late in that year.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CRJ100">CRJ100</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombardier_CRJ200&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: CRJ100">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lufthansa.crj-100.d-aclp.arp.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1306" data-file-width="1800" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Lufthansa.crj-100.d-aclp.arp.jpg/220px-Lufthansa.crj-100.d-aclp.arp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Lufthansa.crj-100.d-aclp.arp.jpg/330px-Lufthansa.crj-100.d-aclp.arp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Lufthansa.crj-100.d-aclp.arp.jpg/440px-Lufthansa.crj-100.d-aclp.arp.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lufthansa.crj-100.d-aclp.arp.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Lufthansa" title="Lufthansa">Lufthansa</a> CRJ100 landing</div>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bombardier_Challenger_600#CL-600" title="Bombardier Challenger 600">CL-600</a> design was stretched 5.92 meters (19 feet 5 inches) to create the CRJ100, with fuselage plugs fore and aft of the wing, two more emergency exit doors, plus a reinforced and modified wing. Typical seating was 50 passengers, the maximum load being 52 passengers. The CRJ100 featured a Collins ProLine 4 avionics suite, Collins weather radar, GE CF34-3A1 turbofans with 41.0 kN (4,180 kgp / 9,220 lbf), new wings with extended span, more fuel capacity, and improved landing gear to handle the higher weights. It was followed by the <b>CRJ100 ER</b> subvariant with 20% more range, and the <b>CRJ100 LR</b> subvariant with 40% more range than the standard CRJ100. The <b>CRJ 100 SE</b> sub-variant was produced to more closely meet the needs of corporate and executive operators.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CRJ200">CRJ200</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombardier_CRJ200&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: CRJ200">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The CRJ200 is identical to the 100 model except for more efficient engines.</p>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinnacle_Airlines" title="Pinnacle Airlines">Pinnacle Airlines</a> had operated some with 44 seats, designated as <b>CRJ440</b>, with closets in the forward areas of the passenger cabin though these were converted to 50 seat airplanes. These modifications were designed to allow operations under their major airline contract "scope clause" which restricted major airlines' connection carriers from operating equipment carrying 50 or more passengers to guard against usurpation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Line_Pilots_Association" title="Air Line Pilots Association">Air Line Pilots Association</a> and <a href="/wiki/Allied_Pilots_Association" title="Allied Pilots Association">Allied Pilots Association</a> pilots' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_contract" title="Labor contract">union contract</a>; these scope clauses have been since relaxed when union contracts were re-written between unions and the three remaining U.S. legacy carriers. Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Comair" title="Comair">Comair</a>'s fleet of 40-seat CRJ200s were sold at a discounted price to discourage Comair from purchasing the less expensive and smaller <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embraer_145" title="Embraer 145">Embraer 135</a>.</p>
<p>There is also a CRJ200 freighter version which is designated <i>CRJ200 PF</i> (<i>Package Freighter</i>) which was developed in cooperation with <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Aerospace" title="Cascade Aerospace">Cascade Aerospace</a> on the request of <a href="/wiki/West_Air_Sweden" title="West Air Sweden">West Air Sweden</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombardier_CRJ200&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Several models of the CRJ have been produced, ranging in capacity from 40 to 50 passengers. The <b>Regional Jet</b> designations are marketing names and the official designation is <b>CL-600-2B19</b>.</p>
<dl>
<dt>CRJ100 </dt>
<dd>The CRJ100 is the original 50-seat version. It is equipped with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GE_Aircraft_Engines" title="GE Aircraft Engines">General Electric</a> <a href="/wiki/General_Electric_CF34" title="General Electric CF34">CF34-3A1</a> engines. Operators include <a href="/wiki/Air_Georgian" title="Air Georgian">Air Georgian</a> and <a href="/wiki/RwandAir" title="RwandAir">RwandAir</a>, among others.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>CRJ100SF </dt>
<dd>Passenger-to-freighter conversion of CRJ100.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>CRJ200 </dt>
<dd>The CRJ200 is identical to the CRJ100 except for its engines, which were upgraded to the CF34-3B1 model, offering improved efficiency.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>CRJ200PF </dt>
<dd>Package freighter version of CRJ200.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>CRJ200SF </dt>
<dd>Passenger-to-freighter conversion of CRJ200.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>CRJ440 </dt>
<dd>Certified up to 44-seat, this version was designed with fewer seats in order to meet the needs of some major United States airlines.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Challenger_850" title="Bombardier Challenger 850">Challenger 800/850</a> </dt>
<dd>A business jet variant of the CRJ200</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>CRJ500 </dt>
<dd>Proposed 50-seat version with wing and cabin improvements based on the CRJ700/900. Cancelled in 2001.</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Improvements_and_production">Improvements and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombardier_CRJ200&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Improvements and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the project was terminated and production stopped, no new CRJ-200/100s have been produced, but over the years maintenance and newer technologies have added to the planes. Some of the larger versions such as the CRJ700 have begun the processes of adding Wi-Fi access on board the aircraft, but no project has begun for the CRJ200/100 to implement Wi-Fi compatibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>U.S. airlines are accelerating retirement of these 50-seat regional jets because rising fuel prices were making them uneconomical to operate, and the number of flight cycles those aircraft go through. The retirements are also reducing the value of their parts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Airlines are slowly replacing the jet with more modern aircraft like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embraer_E-175" title="Embraer E-175">Embraer E-175</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CRJ-700" title="CRJ-700">CRJ-700</a>.</p>

<p>As of July 2015 a total of 563 CRJ100 and CRJ200 aircraft (all variants) are in airline service.</p>
<p>Major operators include <a href="/wiki/SkyWest_Airlines" title="SkyWest Airlines">SkyWest Airlines</a> (167), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expressjet" title="Expressjet">Expressjet</a> (78), <a href="/wiki/Endeavor_Air" title="Endeavor Air">Endeavor Air</a> (43), <a href="/wiki/Air_Wisconsin" title="Air Wisconsin">Air Wisconsin</a> (71), <a href="/wiki/PSA_Airlines" title="PSA Airlines">PSA Airlines</a> (35), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jazz_Aviation" title="Jazz Aviation">Jazz Aviation</a> (25), <a href="/wiki/RusLine" title="RusLine">RusLine</a> (17), <a href="/wiki/Air_Nostrum" title="Air Nostrum">Air Nostrum</a> (10), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SA_Express" title="SA Express">SA Express</a> (10), and other operators with fewer aircraft of the type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FI15_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FI15-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_incidents">Accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombardier_CRJ200&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Accidents and incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>On 16 December 1997, <a href="/wiki/Air_Canada" title="Air Canada">Air Canada</a> Flight 646, a Bombardier CRJ-100, crashed on a go-around at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greater_Fredericton_Airport" title="Greater Fredericton Airport">Greater Fredericton Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fredericton" title="Fredericton">Fredericton</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>. No fatalities were reported.</li>
<li>On 22 June 2003, <a href="/wiki/Brit_Air" title="Brit Air">Brit Air</a> Flight 5672 with registration F-GRJS from <a href="/wiki/Nantes" title="Nantes">Nantes</a> to <a href="/wiki/Brest,_France" title="Brest, France">Brest, France</a>, crashed 2.3 miles short and 0.3 miles to the left of the runway when attempting a landing at Brest's airport. The aircraft's captain was the sole fatality.</li>
<li>On 14 October 2004, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinnacle_Airlines" title="Pinnacle Airlines">Pinnacle Airlines</a> <a href="/wiki/Pinnacle_Airlines_Flight_3701" title="Pinnacle Airlines Flight 3701">Flight 3701</a>, a Bombardier CRJ-200, crashed on a non revenue, repositioning flight from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Little_Rock_National_Airport" title="Little Rock National Airport">Little Rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minneapolis-Saint_Paul_International_Airport" title="Minneapolis-Saint Paul International Airport">Minneapolis</a>. The pilots attempted to climb the aircraft to its published service ceiling of 41,000 feet, exceeding the aircraft's capabilities for the existing conditions. This resulted in the <a href="/wiki/Flameout" title="Flameout">flame out</a> and possible <a href="/wiki/Core_lock" title="Core lock">core lock</a> of both engines. The aircraft crashed about fifteen minutes later, in sight of the <a href="/wiki/Diversion_airport" title="Diversion airport">diversion airport</a>, killing both pilots.</li>
<li>On 21 November 2004, <a href="/wiki/China_Eastern_Airlines" title="China Eastern Airlines">China Eastern Airlines</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/China_Eastern_Flight_5210" title="China Eastern Flight 5210">Flight 5210</a>, a Bombardier CRJ-200LR, crashed shortly after takeoff, killing all 53 on board as well as two on the ground.</li>
<li>On 27 August 2006, <a href="/wiki/Comair" title="Comair">Comair</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comair_Flight_191" title="Comair Flight 191">Flight 5191</a>, marketed as <a href="/wiki/Delta_Connection" title="Delta Connection">Delta Connection</a> Flight 5191, a Bombardier CRJ-100ER, crashed during takeoff from the wrong runway at <a href="/wiki/Blue_Grass_Airport" title="Blue Grass Airport">Blue Grass Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lexington,_Kentucky" title="Lexington, Kentucky">Lexington, Kentucky</a>. There were 49 fatalities, with only the severely injured <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Officer_(civil_aviation)" title="First Officer (civil aviation)">first officer</a> surviving.</li>
<li>On 20 May 2007, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Canada_Jazz" title="Air Canada Jazz">Air Canada Jazz</a> Bombardier CRJ-100 which originated in <a href="/wiki/Moncton" title="Moncton">Moncton</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>, was substantially damaged when its landing gear collapsed after landing at Toronto-Pearson International Airport, ON (YYZ). There were no injuries to any crew or passengers. Flight AC8911 departed Moncton (YQM) on a domestic flight to Toronto. The aircraft landed on runway 6 right with a 90 degree crosswind from the left, gusting from 13 to 23 knots. The aircraft first contacted the runway in a left-wing-down sideslip. The left main landing gear struck the runway first and the aircraft sustained a sharp lateral side load before bouncing. Once airborne again, the flight and ground spoilers deployed and the aircraft landed hard. Both main landing gear trunnion fittings failed and the landing gear collapsed. The aircraft remained upright, supported by the landing gear struts and wheels. The aircraft slid down the runway and exited via the Delta 3 taxiway, where the passengers deplaned. There was no fire. There were no injuries to the crew; some passengers reported minor injuries as a result of the hard landing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 16 December 2007, <a href="/wiki/Air_Wisconsin" title="Air Wisconsin">Air Wisconsin</a> flight 758A, a Bombardier CRJ-200LR, overran the runway during landing at <a href="/wiki/T._F._Green_Airport" title="T. F. Green Airport">T. F. Green Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Providence,_Rhode_Island" title="Providence, Rhode Island">Providence, Rhode Island</a>. No injuries or fatalities were reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 13 February 2008, <a href="/wiki/Belavia" title="Belavia">Belavia</a> <a href="/wiki/Belavia_Flight_1834" title="Belavia Flight 1834">Flight 1834</a>, a Bombardier CRJ-100LR, crashed and flipped-over during takeoff at <a href="/wiki/Zvartnots_International_Airport" title="Zvartnots International Airport">Zvartnots International Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Yerevan" title="Yerevan">Yerevan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a>. Most passengers suffered some burns, and four were taken to the hospital. No fatalities were reported.</li>
<li>On 12 November 2009, <a href="/wiki/RwandAir" title="RwandAir">RwandAir</a> <a href="/wiki/RwandAir_Flight_205" title="RwandAir Flight 205">Flight 205</a>, a Bombardier CRJ-100, crashed into a VIP terminal shortly after an emergency landing at <a href="/wiki/Kigali_International_Airport" title="Kigali International Airport">Kigali International Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rwanda" title="Rwanda">Rwanda</a>; out of the ten passengers and five crew members, one passenger died.</li>
<li>On 19 January 2010, <a href="/wiki/PSA_Airlines" title="PSA Airlines">PSA Airlines</a> Canadair CRJ-200 N246PS overran the runway at <a href="/wiki/Yeager_Airport" title="Yeager Airport">Yeager Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_West_Virginia" title="Charleston, West Virginia">Charleston, West Virginia</a> following an aborted take-off. The aircraft was stopped by the <a href="/wiki/Engineered_materials_arrestor_system" title="Engineered materials arrestor system">EMAS</a> at the end of the runway, sustaining minor damage to its undercarriage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AH42607_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-AH42607-11">[11]</a></sup> The information in the referenced article regarding "substantial" damage was premature and inaccurate. The aircraft was flown away within days of the incident after removal of a damaged landing gear cover.</li>
<li>On 17 March 2011, Jetlink Express Bombardier CRJ 100, flight JO 752 from Nairobi Jomo Kenyatta International Airport veered off the runway at Kisumu Airport while attempting to land in light rain and misty weather. The aircraft stopped safely a few metres from the shores of Lake Victoria. There were no fatalities.</li>
<li>On 4 April 2011, a <a href="/wiki/Georgian_Airways" title="Georgian Airways">Georgian Airways</a> Canadair CRJ-100ER 4L-GAE operating under an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UN" title="UN">UN</a> mission as flight 834 from <a href="/wiki/Bangoka_International_Airport" title="Bangoka International Airport">Bangoka International Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kisangani" title="Kisangani">Kisangani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a> to <a href="/wiki/N%27djili_Airport" title="N'djili Airport">N'djili Airport</a> missed the runway on landing at Kinshasa. The aircraft subsequently broke into pieces and caught fire. Only one survivor is reported out of 29 passengers and 4 crew. The airport was experiencing torrential rain, thunderstorms and low visibility at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AVH-20110404_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-AVH-20110404-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 6 June 2011, a <a href="/wiki/SkyWest_Airlines" title="SkyWest Airlines">SkyWest Airlines</a> Canadair CRJ-200, flight OO 4443 (code share DL 443) from Cincinnati to Milwaukee couldn't extend right main landing gear; however, left main landing gear was extended and locked. After several failed attempts to extend the right main landing and running low on reserved fuel, airplane landed with right main gear up on runway 19R. Emergency service was on scene and no fire broke out. No injuries occurred. Runway was closed for two hours as the result.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 2 September 2011, <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Southeast_Airlines" title="Atlantic Southeast Airlines">Atlantic Southeast Airlines</a> Flight 5058, operated by Canadair CRJ-200 N875AS landed at <a href="/wiki/Baton_Rouge_Metropolitan_Airport" title="Baton Rouge Metropolitan Airport">Baton Rouge Metropolitan Airport</a> with the left main undercarriage retracted. There were no injuries amongst the 50 passengers and three crew on board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AH44248402_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-AH44248402-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 5 May 2012 an Akbars Aero CRJ-200, flying from St. Petersburg to Astrakhan, Russia, made a forced landing at Voronezh airport, due to cracking of the pilot's windshield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 17 July 2012, a suspended SkyWest employee attempted to steal a CRJ-200 from a Utah airport. Although his security access cards had been de-activated, the employee was able to enter the jet, start it, and attempt to taxi it toward a runway. The jet hit a jetway and a building, plowed into a parking lot and came to rest when its nose gear collapsed. After crashing the plane in the parking lot, the employee shot himself in the head and died at the scene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 29 January 2013, <a href="/wiki/SCAT_Airlines_Flight_760" title="SCAT Airlines Flight 760">SCAT Airlines Flight 760</a> crashed 5 km short of <a href="/wiki/Almaty" title="Almaty">Almaty</a> International airport in <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> near the village of Kyzyltu while attempting to land in bad weather conditions. 16 passengers and 5 crew died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 8 January 2016, <a href="/wiki/West_Air_Sweden_Flight_294" title="West Air Sweden Flight 294">West Air Sweden Flight 294</a> between Oslo (Gardermoen) and Tromsø (Langenes) crashed in northern Sweden near <a href="/wiki/Ritsem" title="Ritsem">Ritsem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Padjelanta_National_Park" title="Padjelanta National Park">Padjelanta National Park</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The plane was carrying 4.5 tonnes of mail on behalf of <a href="/wiki/Posten_Norge" title="Posten Norge">Norway Post</a>. The two crew members died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
