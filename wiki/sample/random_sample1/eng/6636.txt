<p>The <b>Bombardier Challenger 600 series</b> is a family of <a href="/wiki/Business_jet" title="Business jet">business jets</a>. It was first produced by <a href="/wiki/Canadair" title="Canadair">Canadair</a> as an independent company and then produced from 1986 by Canadair as a division of <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Aerospace" title="Bombardier Aerospace">Bombardier Aerospace</a>. Including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Challenger_300" title="Challenger 300">Challenger 300</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Challenger_850" title="Challenger 850">Challenger 850</a>, 1,600 <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Challenger" title="Bombardier Challenger">Bombardier Challengers</a> are in-service, the fleet has logged 7.3 million hours over 4.3 million flights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombardier_Challenger_600_series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bombardier_Challenger_600_2B16_-_2010_-_Photo_D_Ramey_Logan.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3200" data-file-width="4800" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Bombardier_Challenger_600_2B16_-_2010_-_Photo_D_Ramey_Logan.jpg/220px-Bombardier_Challenger_600_2B16_-_2010_-_Photo_D_Ramey_Logan.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Bombardier_Challenger_600_2B16_-_2010_-_Photo_D_Ramey_Logan.jpg/330px-Bombardier_Challenger_600_2B16_-_2010_-_Photo_D_Ramey_Logan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Bombardier_Challenger_600_2B16_-_2010_-_Photo_D_Ramey_Logan.jpg/440px-Bombardier_Challenger_600_2B16_-_2010_-_Photo_D_Ramey_Logan.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bombardier_Challenger_600_2B16_-_2010_-_Photo_D_Ramey_Logan.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
2010 Bombardier Challenger 600 2B16</div>

</div>
<p>The origin of the Challenger 600 lies in Canadair’s purchase of a concept for a business jet aircraft, the <i>LearStar 600</i> from the American inventor and aircraft developer <a href="/wiki/Bill_Lear" title="Bill Lear">Bill Lear</a>. However, Lear had practically no influence on the ensuing development and design of the aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pickler_and_Milberry_p._263_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pickler_and_Milberry_p._263-5">[5]</a></sup> Even the name LearStar was not new to this concept, since Lear had long before used the name for his conversion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lockheed_Lodestar" title="Lockheed Lodestar">Lockheed Lodestars</a> into business transports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Thus, Canadair quickly abandoned the name <i>LearStar</i> and adopted the name <i>Challenger</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Canadair's top management was of the opinion that Lear’s concept was sketchy at best.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pickler_and_Milberry_p._263_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pickler_and_Milberry_p._263-5">[5]</a></sup> Lear did not have an expert grasp of aeronautical engineering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He was also at a financial low point, with a skeleton staff. Thus, he had only been able to pay a California aeronautical consultant to do some very preliminary design explorations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, Canadair planned to use Lear’s name and skills at self-promotion to secure extensive financial guarantees for a business jet project from the Canadian Federal government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pickler_and_Milberry_p._263_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pickler_and_Milberry_p._263-5">[5]</a></sup> This proved an effective choice. In <i><a href="/wiki/Challenger:_An_Industrial_Romance" title="Challenger: An Industrial Romance">Challenger: An Industrial Romance</a></i> , the 1980 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_National_Film_Board_of_Canada" title="The National Film Board of Canada">The National Film Board of Canada</a> documentary on the development of the aircraft, future Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Jean_Chr%C3%A9tien" title="Jean Chrétien">Jean Chrétien</a> specifically refers to the effect of personal contact with Lear (on Chrétien’s decision to direct financial support to Canadair’s program).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the time of these events, Chrétien was successively President of the Treasury Board, Minister of Industry, Trade and Commerce, and Minister of Finance, in the Canadian Federal government. Due to the use of <a href="/wiki/Letter_of_comfort_(contract_law)" title="Letter of comfort (contract law)">letters of comfort</a>, the extent of the Ministry's financial commitments for Canadair could be kept from parliament and the public for several years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> These financial guarantees were later used as an academic example of insufficient monitoring and lax controls in government support of industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the Challenger would be similar in general configuration to other aircraft of its type already on the market, certain of its features would stand out, for example, the use of a widened fuselage that allowed a "walk-about cabin". The Challenger was also one of the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bizjet" title="Bizjet">bizjets</a> designed with a <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_airfoil" title="Supercritical airfoil">supercritical wing</a>.</p>
<p>On 8 November 1978, the prototype aircraft took off at <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal, Quebec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. The second and third prototypes flew in 1979. A test flight on 3 April 1980 in the Mojave Desert resulted in disaster, the aircraft crashing due to the failure of the release mechanism to detach the recovery chute after a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_stall" title="Deep stall">deep stall</a>, killing one of the test pilots (the other test pilot and the flight test engineer <a href="/wiki/Parachute" title="Parachute">parachuted</a> to safety).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the crash, both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Transport_(Canada)" title="Department of Transport (Canada)">Transport Canada</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a> in the United States certified the aircraft in 1980, albeit with restrictions to pilots including a limited maximum takeoff weight. A program to reduce the aircraft's weight was then implemented to improve the aircraft's range.</p>
<p>Challengers can be identified visually by their distinctive double slotted hinged flap design, where the fairings can be seen below the wings, a sight much more common on commercial airliners.</p>

<dl>
<dt>Canadair CC-144</dt>
<dd>12 aircraft purchased by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Air_Force" title="Royal Canadian Air Force">Royal Canadian Air Force</a>, including the CE-144 and CX-144</dd>
<dt>Canadair CE-144</dt>
<dd>Three Electronic warfare / EW trainers converted to/from basic CC-144.</dd>
<dt>Canadair CX-144</dt>
<dd>Second prototype, a CL-600-1A11, c/n 1002, allocated to the RCAF after finishing test programme. Used at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aerospace_Engineering_and_Test_Establishment&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aerospace Engineering and Test Establishment (page does not exist)">Aerospace Engineering and Test Establishment</a> (AETE), <a href="/wiki/CFB_Cold_Lake" title="CFB Cold Lake">CFB Cold Lake</a> until retirement in 1993, now preserved at the <a href="/wiki/CFB_Winnipeg" title="CFB Winnipeg">CFB Winnipeg</a>. Designated CC-144 in service.</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CL-601">CL-601</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombardier_Challenger_600_series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: CL-601">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt>CL-601-1A</dt>
<dd>A refined version including winglets to decrease drag and more powerful <a href="/wiki/General_Electric_CF34" title="General Electric CF34">General Electric CF34-1A</a> (66 built, including six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Forces" title="Canadian Forces">Canadian Forces</a> CC-144B)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>CL-601-1A/ER</dt>
<dd>601-1A retrofitted with an additional fuel tank in the tail</dd>
<dt>CL-601-3A</dt>
<dd>GE CF34-3A engines with a higher flat rating and a <a href="/wiki/Glass_cockpit" title="Glass cockpit">glass cockpit</a>. This was the first version marketed by Bombardier, indeed.</dd>
<dt>CL-601-3A/ER</dt>
<dd>601-3A with an additional, optional fuel tank in the tail</dd>
<dt>CL-601-3R</dt>
<dd>The tail tank was made standard, CF34-3A1 Engines were introduced.</dd>
<dt>CL-601s</dt>
<dd>Version powered by GE CF34-3A2 engines<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CL-604">CL-604</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombardier_Challenger_600_series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: CL-604">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt>CL-604</dt>
<dd>A major upgrade of the 601 design, incorporating more advanced GE CF34-3B engines; increased fuel capacity, including saddle tanks in the rear of the aircraft; new undercarriage for a higher takeoff and landing weight; structural improvements to wings and tail; and a new <a href="/wiki/Rockwell_Collins" title="Rockwell Collins">Rockwell Collins</a> ProLine 4 avionics system.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>CL-604 MMA</dt>
<dd>(Multi-Mission Aircraft), militarized version, developed by Field Aviation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CasrChallengerDane_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-CasrChallengerDane-16">[16]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a> service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CasrChallengerDane_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-CasrChallengerDane-16">[16]</a></sup> The aircraft are employed on maritime patrol and <a href="/wiki/Search_and_rescue" title="Search and rescue">search and rescue</a> missions.They are capable of landing on the short, rough, gravel airstrips common in the Arctic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CasrChallengerDane_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-CasrChallengerDane-16">[16]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>C-143A</dt>
<dd>A single Challenger 604 aircraft was acquired by the United States Coast Guard in December 2005 as its new Medium Range Command and Control Aircraft (MRC2A).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CL-605">CL-605</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombardier_Challenger_600_series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: CL-605">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Paris_Air_Show_2007-06-24_n21.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1548" data-file-width="3872" height="88" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Paris_Air_Show_2007-06-24_n21.jpg/220px-Paris_Air_Show_2007-06-24_n21.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Paris_Air_Show_2007-06-24_n21.jpg/330px-Paris_Air_Show_2007-06-24_n21.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Paris_Air_Show_2007-06-24_n21.jpg/440px-Paris_Air_Show_2007-06-24_n21.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<dl>
<dt>CL-605</dt>
<dd>Introduced in early 2006 as an avionics and structural upgrade of the 604 design. Structural improvements include larger cabin windows. Cockpit instrumentation updated with the Collins Proline 21 avionics and "<a href="/wiki/Electronic_flight_bag" title="Electronic flight bag">electronic flight bag</a>" capability. It can be visually identified by a new, rounded tailcone.</dd>
</dl>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MSA-N614BA-FAR14-3576.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3240" data-file-width="4320" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/MSA-N614BA-FAR14-3576.JPG/220px-MSA-N614BA-FAR14-3576.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/MSA-N614BA-FAR14-3576.JPG/330px-MSA-N614BA-FAR14-3576.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/MSA-N614BA-FAR14-3576.JPG/440px-MSA-N614BA-FAR14-3576.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MSA-N614BA-FAR14-3576.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Boeing Maritime Surveillance Aircraft (MSA) on display at 2014 Farnborough Air Show</div>
</div>
</div>
<dl>
<dt>CL-605 MSA</dt>
<dd>A <a href="/wiki/Maritime_patrol_aircraft" title="Maritime patrol aircraft">maritime patrol aircraft</a> design under development by Boeing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Boeing has proposed a repackaging of some of the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_P-8_Poseidon" title="Boeing P-8 Poseidon">Boeing P-8 Poseidon</a> sensors but not weapons into a less expensive airframe, the Bombardier Challenger 605 business jet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> This aircraft is named Maritime Surveillance Aircraft (MSA) and has been depicted with the AN/APY-10 radar, an electro-optical sensor in a retractable turret, and a magnetic anomaly detector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CASR201307_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-CASR201307-20">[20]</a></sup> On February 28, 2014, a MSA demonstrator which is a modified CL-604 made its first flight, but the final aircraft will use the CL-605 airframe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The demonstrator currently has the external shapes for the sensors and communications systems which will be added later. The final MSA is expected to cost $55 million to $60 million per aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CL-650">CL-650</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombardier_Challenger_600_series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: CL-650">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt>CL-650</dt>
<dd>The CL-650 is a major upgrade of the CL-605. It includes a completely redesigned interior cabin, the new "Vision" flight Deck which is a Rockwell Collins Proline 21 Enhanced, and a 5% increase of takeoff thrust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_incidents">Accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombardier_Challenger_600_series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Accidents and incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl>
<dt>Notable accidents involving Bombardier Challenger 600 aircraft</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>On October 10, 2000, at 1452 central daylight time, a Canadair Challenger CL-600-2B16 (CL-604) (Canadian registration C-FTBZ and operated by Bombardier Incorporated) was destroyed on impact with terrain and postimpact fire during initial climb from runway 19R at Wichita Mid-Continent Airport (ICT), Wichita, Kansas. The flight was operating under the provisions of 14 Code of Federal Regulations (CFR) Part 91 as an experimental test flight. The pilot and flight test engineer were killed. The copilot was seriously injured and died 36 days later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li>A Canadair Challenger CL-600-2B16 was destroyed on 5 January 2014 after crash-landing at Aspen-Pitkin County Airport in Colorado during a positioning flight from Tucson, Arizona to Salt Lake City, Utah, where a bank which was repossessing the aircraft was to take formal custody. The aircraft attempted a tailwind landing in low-level windshear and gust conditions, then declared a missed approach and executed a go-around. Upon the second landing attempt, the aircraft touched down, bounced airborne again and slammed into the runway, coming to rest upside-down near the departure end, in flames and its right wing separated from the airframe. One crewmember was killed; another crewmember and a passenger sustained serious injuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
