<p>The <b>bongo</b> (<i>Tragelaphus eurycerus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivorous</a>, mostly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a> forest <a href="/wiki/Ungulate" title="Ungulate">ungulate</a>. It is among the largest of the African forest <a href="/wiki/Antelope" title="Antelope">antelope</a> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>.</p>
<p>Bongos are characterised by a striking reddish-brown coat, black and white markings, white-yellow stripes and long slightly spiralled horns. Indeed, bongos are the only <a href="/wiki/Tragelaphus" title="Tragelaphus">tragelaphid</a> in which both sexes have horns. They have a complex social interaction and are found in African dense forest mosaics.</p>
<p>The <b>western</b> or <b>lowland bongo</b>, <i>T. e. eurycerus</i>, faces an ongoing population decline, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> Antelope Specialist Group considers it to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_Threatened" title="Near Threatened">Near Threatened</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Conservation_status" title="Conservation status">conservation status</a> scale.</p>
<p>The <b>eastern</b> or <b>mountain bongo</b>, <i>T. e. isaaci</i>, of <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, has a coat even more vibrant than that of <i>T. e. eurycerus</i>. The mountain bongo is only found in the wild in one remote region of central Kenya. This bongo is classified by the IUCN Antelope Specialist Group as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Critically_Endangered" title="Critically Endangered">Critically Endangered</a>, with more specimens in captivity than in the wild.</p>
<p>In 2000, the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Zoos_and_Aquariums" title="Association of Zoos and Aquariums">Association of Zoos and Aquariums</a> in the USA (AZA) upgraded the bongo to a Species Survival Plan participant and in 2006 added the Bongo Restoration to Mount Kenya Project to its list of the Top Ten Wildlife Conservation Success Stories of the year. However, in 2013, it seems, these successes have been negated with reports of possibly only 100 mountain bongos left in the wild due to <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poaching" title="Poaching">poaching</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bongo_(antelope)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_name" title="Scientific name">scientific name</a> of the bongo is <i>Tragelaphus eurycerus</i>, and it belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Tragelaphus" title="Tragelaphus">Tragelaphus</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Bovidae" title="Bovidae">Bovidae</a>. It was first described by Irish naturalist <a href="/wiki/William_Ogilby" title="William Ogilby">William Ogilby</a> in 1837.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSW3_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSW3-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generic_name_(biology)" title="Generic name (biology)">generic name</a> <i>Tragelaphus</i> is composed of two Greek words: <i>tragos</i>, meaning a male goat; and <i>elaphos</i>, meaning deer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">specific name</a> <i>eurycerus</i> originated from the fusion of <i>eurus</i> (broad, widespread) and <i>keras</i> (an animal's horn).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-huffman_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-huffman-5">[5]</a></sup> The common name "bongo" originated probably from the <a href="/wiki/Kele_language_(Gabon)" title="Kele language (Gabon)">Kele</a> language of <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">Gabon</a>. The first known use of the name "bongo" dates back to 1861.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bongos are further classified into two <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>: <i>T. e. eurycerus</i>, the lowland or western bongo, and the far rarer <i>T. e. isaaci</i>, the mountain or eastern bongo, restricted to the mountains of Kenya only. The eastern bongo is larger and heavier than the western bongo. Two other subspecies are described from West and Central Africa, but taxonomic clarification is required. They have been observed to live up to 19 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinage_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spinage-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bongo_(antelope)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bongos are found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a> <a href="/wiki/Jungle" title="Jungle">jungles</a> with dense undergrowth up to an altitude of 4,000 meters (13,000 feet) in Central Africa, with isolated populations in <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, and these West African countries: <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Republic" title="Central African Republic">Central African Republic</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Republic of the Congo">Republic of the Congo</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of Congo">Democratic Republic of Congo</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">Ivory Coast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea">Equatorial Guinea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">Gabon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guinea" title="Guinea">Guinea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone" title="Sierra Leone">Sierra Leone</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Sudan" title="South Sudan">South Sudan</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tragelaphus_eurycerus_isaaciPCCA20071227-8374B.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="385" data-file-width="600" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Tragelaphus_eurycerus_isaaciPCCA20071227-8374B.jpg/220px-Tragelaphus_eurycerus_isaaciPCCA20071227-8374B.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Tragelaphus_eurycerus_isaaciPCCA20071227-8374B.jpg/330px-Tragelaphus_eurycerus_isaaciPCCA20071227-8374B.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Tragelaphus_eurycerus_isaaciPCCA20071227-8374B.jpg/440px-Tragelaphus_eurycerus_isaaciPCCA20071227-8374B.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tragelaphus_eurycerus_isaaciPCCA20071227-8374B.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A bongo drinks from a <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamp</a>.</div>
</div>

<p>Historically, bongos are found in three disjunct parts of Africa: East, Central and West. Today, all three populationsâ ranges have shrunk in size due to habitat loss for agriculture and uncontrolled timber cutting, as well as <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunting</a> for <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a>.</p>
<p>Bongos favour disturbed forest mosaics that provide fresh, low-level green vegetation. Such habitats may be promoted by heavy browsing by <a href="/wiki/African_elephant" title="African elephant">elephants</a>, fires, flooding, tree-felling (natural or by logging), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fallowing" title="Fallowing">fallowing</a>. Mass <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a> die-off provides ideal habitat in East Africa. They can live in bamboo forests.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance">Appearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bongo_(antelope)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Appearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bongos are one of the largest of the forest antelopes. In addition to the deep <a href="/wiki/Chestnut" title="Chestnut">chestnut</a> colour of their coats, they have bright white stripes on their sides to help with <a href="/wiki/Camouflage" title="Camouflage">camouflage</a>.</p>
<p>Adults of both sexes are similar in size. Adult height is about 1.1 to 1.3Â m (3.6 to 4.3Â ft) at the shoulder and length is 2.15 to 3.15Â m (7.1 to 10.3Â ft), including a tail of 45â65Â cm (18â26Â in). Females weigh around 150â235Â kg (331â518Â lb), while males weigh about 220â405Â kg (485â893Â lb). Its large size puts it as the third-largest in the Bovidae tribe of Strepsicerotini, behind both the <a href="/wiki/Common_eland" title="Common eland">common</a> and <a href="/wiki/Giant_eland" title="Giant eland">greater elands</a> by about 300Â kg (660Â lb), and above the greater kudu by about 40Â kg (88Â lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both sexes have heavy spiral horns; those of the male are longer and more massive. All bongos in captivity are from the isolated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aberdare_Mountains" title="Aberdare Mountains">Aberdare Mountains</a> of central <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coat_and_body">Coat and body</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bongo_(antelope)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Coat and body">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BongoNEW.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/BongoNEW.jpg/220px-BongoNEW.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/BongoNEW.jpg/330px-BongoNEW.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/BongoNEW.jpg/440px-BongoNEW.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BongoNEW.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The side facial patination of an eastern bongo</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The bongo sports a bright <a href="/wiki/Auburn_hair" title="Auburn hair">auburn</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chestnut_(color)" title="Chestnut (color)">chestnut</a> coat, with the neck, chest, and legs generally darker than the rest of the body. Coats of male bongos become darker as they age until they reach a dark <a href="/wiki/Mahogany" title="Mahogany">mahogany</a>-brown colour. Coats of female bongos are usually more brightly coloured than those of males. The eastern bongo is darker in color than the western and this is especially pronounced in older makes which tend to be chestnut brown especially on the front part of their bodies.</p>
<p>The pigmentation in the coat rubs off quite easily; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anecdotal" title="Anecdotal">anecdotal</a> reports suggest rain running off a bongo may be tinted red with <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigment</a>. The smooth coat is marked with 10â15 vertical white-yellow stripes, spread along the back from the base of the neck to the rump. The number of stripes on each side is rarely the same. It also has a short, bristly, brown ridge of dorsal hair from the shoulder to the rump; the white stripes run into this ridge.</p>
<p>A white chevron appears between the eyes, and two large white spots grace each cheek. Another white chevron occurs where the neck meets the chest. The large ears are to sharpen hearing, and the distinctive coloration may help bongos identify one another in their dark forest habitats. Bongos have no special secretion glands, so rely less on scent to find one another than do other similar antelopes. The lips of a bongo are white, topped with a black muzzle.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Horns">Horns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bongo_(antelope)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Horns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bongo_Burger_Zoo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="896" data-file-width="640" height="238" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Bongo_Burger_Zoo.jpg/170px-Bongo_Burger_Zoo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Bongo_Burger_Zoo.jpg/255px-Bongo_Burger_Zoo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Bongo_Burger_Zoo.jpg/340px-Bongo_Burger_Zoo.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bongo_Burger_Zoo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A eastern bongo's horns</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Bongos have two heavy and slightly spiralled <a href="/wiki/Horn_(anatomy)" title="Horn (anatomy)">horns</a> that slope over their backs, and like many other <a href="/wiki/Antelope" title="Antelope">antelope</a> species, both male and female bongos have horns. Bongos are the only tragelaphids in which both sexes have horns. The horns of bongos are in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Lyre" title="Lyre">lyre</a> and bear a resemblance to those of the related antelope species of <a href="/wiki/Nyala" title="Nyala">nyalas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sitatunga" title="Sitatunga">sitatungas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bushbuck" title="Bushbuck">bushbucks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kudu" title="Kudu">kudus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taurotragus" title="Taurotragus">elands</a>.</p>
<p>Unlike <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a>, which have branched <a href="/wiki/Antler" title="Antler">antlers</a> shed annually, bongos and other antelopes have pointed horns they keep throughout their lives. Males have massive backswept horns, while females have smaller, thinner, and more parallel horns. The size of the horns range between 75 and 99Â cm (29.5 and 39Â in). The horns twist once.</p>
<p>Like all other horns of antelopes, the core of a bongo's horn is hollow and the outer layer of the horn is made of <a href="/wiki/Keratin" title="Keratin">keratin</a>, the same material that makes up human fingernails, toenails. and hair. The bongo runs gracefully and at full speed through even the thickest tangles of <a href="/wiki/Liana" title="Liana">lianas</a>, laying its heavy spiralled horns on its back so the brush cannot impede its flight. Bongos are hunted for their horns by humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_organization_and_behavior">Social organization and behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bongo_(antelope)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Social organization and behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mountain_bongo_mount_kenya.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2112" data-file-width="2816" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Mountain_bongo_mount_kenya.jpg/220px-Mountain_bongo_mount_kenya.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Mountain_bongo_mount_kenya.jpg/330px-Mountain_bongo_mount_kenya.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Mountain_bongo_mount_kenya.jpg/440px-Mountain_bongo_mount_kenya.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mountain_bongo_mount_kenya.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
This female eastern bongo presents her hindquarters while looking over her shoulder to check for threats at Mt. Kenya Wildlife Conservancy.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Like other forest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ungulates" title="Ungulates">ungulates</a>, bongos are seldom seen in large groups. Males, called bulls, tend to be solitary, while females with young live in groups of six to eight. Bongos have seldom been seen in herds of more than 20. <a href="/wiki/Gestation" title="Gestation">Gestation</a> is about 285Â days (9.5Â months), with one young per birth, and <a href="/wiki/Weaning" title="Weaning">weaning</a> occurs at six months. <a href="/wiki/Sexual_maturity" title="Sexual maturity">Sexual maturity</a> is reached at 24â27Â months. The preferred habitat of this species is so dense and difficult to operate in, that few Europeans or Americans observed this species until the 1960s. As young males mature and leave their maternal groups, they most often remain solitary, although rarely they join an older male. Adult males of similar size/age tend to avoid one another. Occasionally, they meet and spar with their horns in a ritualised manner and it is rare for serious fights to take place. However, such fights are usually discouraged by visual displays, in which the males bulge their necks, roll their eyes, and hold their horns in a vertical position while slowly pacing back and forth in front of the other male. They seek out females only during mating time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> When they are with a herd of females, males do not coerce them or try to restrict their movements as do some other antelopes.</p>
<p>Although mostly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a>, they are occasionally active during the day. However, like deer, bongos may exhibit <a href="/wiki/Crepuscular" title="Crepuscular">crepuscular</a> behaviour.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Bongos are both timid and easily frightened; after a scare, a bongo moves away at considerable speed, even through dense undergrowth. Once they find cover, they stay alert and face away from the disturbance, but peek every now and then to check the situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinage_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spinage-7">[7]</a></sup> The bongo's hindquarters are less conspicuous than the forequarters, and from this position the animal can quickly flee.</p>
<p>When in distress, the bongo emits a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bleat" title="Bleat">bleat</a>. It uses a limited number of vocalisations, mostly grunts and snorts; females have a weak mooing contact-call for their young. Females prefer to use traditional <a href="/wiki/Birth" title="Birth">calving</a> grounds restricted to certain areas, while newborn calves lie in hiding for a week or more, receiving short visits by the mother to <a href="/wiki/Breastfeeding" title="Breastfeeding">suckle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The calves grow rapidly and can soon accompany their mothers in the nursery herds. Their horns grow rapidly and begin to show in 3.5 months. They are weaned after six months and reach sexual maturity at about 20 months.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bongo_(antelope)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BongoP2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1390" data-file-width="895" height="264" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/BongoP2.jpg/170px-BongoP2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/BongoP2.jpg/255px-BongoP2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/BongoP2.jpg/340px-BongoP2.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BongoP2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A male bongo eating grass at <a href="/wiki/Louisville_Zoo" title="Louisville Zoo">Louisville Zoo</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Like many forest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ungulates" title="Ungulates">ungulates</a>, bongos are <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivorous</a> browsers and feed on tree/bush <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaves" title="Leaves">leaves</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bushes" title="Bushes">bushes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vine" title="Vine">vines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bark" title="Bark">bark</a> and pith of rotting trees, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grasses" title="Grasses">grasses</a>/herbs, <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">roots</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cereals" title="Cereals">cereals</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shrubs" title="Shrubs">shrubs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruits</a>.</p>
<p>Bongos require <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> in their <a href="/wiki/Diet_(nutrition)" title="Diet (nutrition)">diets</a>, and are known to regularly visit natural salt licks. Examination of bongo feces revealed that charcoal from trees burnt by lightning is consumed. This behavior is believed to be a means of getting salts and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minerals" title="Minerals">minerals</a> into their diets. This behavior has also been reported in the <a href="/wiki/Okapi" title="Okapi">okapi</a>. Another similarity to the okapi, though the bongo is unrelated, is that the bongo has a long <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prehensile" title="Prehensile">prehensile</a> tongue which it uses to grasp grasses and leaves.</p>
<p>Suitable habitats for bongos must have permanent water available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> As a large animal, the bongo requires an ample amount of food, and is restricted to areas with abundant year-round growth of herbs and low shrubs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_and_conservation">Population and conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bongo_(antelope)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Population and conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Few estimates of population density are available. Assuming average population densities of 0.25 animals per km<sup>2</sup> in regions where it is known to be common or abundant, and 0.02 per km<sup>2</sup> elsewhere, and with a total area of occupancy of 327,000Â km<sup>2</sup>, a total population estimate of around 28,000 is suggested. Only about 60% are in protected areas, suggesting the actual numbers of the lowland subspecies may only be in the low tens of thousands. In Kenya, their numbers have declined significantly and on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mt._Kenya" title="Mt. Kenya">Mt. Kenya</a>, they were <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/extirpate" title="wiktionary:extirpate">extirpated</a> within the last decade due to illegal hunting with dogs. Although information on their status in the wild is lacking, lowland bongos are not presently considered endangered.</p>
<p>Bongos are susceptible to diseases such as <a href="/wiki/Rinderpest" title="Rinderpest">rinderpest</a>, which almost exterminated the species during the 1890s. <i>Tragelaphus eurycerus</i> may suffer from <a href="/wiki/Goitre" title="Goitre">goitre</a>. Over the course of the disease, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thyroid_glands" title="Thyroid glands">thyroid glands</a> greatly enlarge (up to 10 x 20Â cm) and may become <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/polycystic" title="wikt:polycystic">polycystic</a>. Pathogenesis of goiter in the bongo may reflect a mixture of genetic predisposition coupled with environmental factors, including a period of exposure to a <a href="/wiki/Goitrogen" title="Goitrogen">goitrogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schiller_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-schiller-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Leopard" title="Leopard">Leopards</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spotted_hyena" title="Spotted hyena">spotted hyenas</a> are the primary natural predators (<a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lions</a> are seldom encountered due to differing habitat preferences); <a href="/wiki/Pythonidae" title="Pythonidae">pythons</a> sometimes eat bongo calves. Humans prey on them for their <a href="/wiki/Fur" title="Fur">pelts</a>, horns, and meat, with the species being a common local source for "bush meat".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSW3_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-MSW3-3">[3]</a></sup> Bongo populations have been greatly reduced by hunting, <a href="/wiki/Poaching" title="Poaching">poaching</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_trapping" title="Animal trapping">animal trapping</a>, although some bongo refuges exist.</p>
<p>Although bongos are quite easy for humans to catch using snares, many people native to the bongos' habitat believed that if they ate or touched bongo, they would have spasms similar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epileptic_seizures" title="Epileptic seizures">epileptic seizures</a>. Because of this <a href="/wiki/Superstition" title="Superstition">superstition</a>, bongos were less harmed in their native ranges than expected. However, these taboos are said no longer to exist, which may account for increased hunting by humans in recent times.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zoo_programmes">Zoo programmes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bongo_(antelope)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Zoo programmes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumbcaption">

Eastern bongo at <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_Zoo" title="Edinburgh Zoo">Edinburgh Zoo</a></div>

<p>An international studbook is maintained to help manage animals held in captivity. Because of its bright colour, it is very popular in zoos and private collections. In North America, over 400 individuals are thought to be held, a population that probably exceeds that of the mountain bongo in the wild.</p>
<p>In 2000, the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Zoos_and_Aquariums" title="Association of Zoos and Aquariums">Association of Zoos and Aquariums</a> (AZA) upgraded the bongo to a Species Survival Plan participant, which works to improve the <a href="/wiki/Genetic_diversity" title="Genetic diversity">genetic diversity</a> of managed animal populations. The target population for participating zoos and private collections in North America is 250 animals. Through the efforts of zoos in North America, a reintroduction to the population in Kenya is being developed.</p>
<p>At least one collaborative effort for reintroduction between North American wildlife facilities has already been carried out. In 2004, 18 eastern bongos born in North American zoos gathered at <a href="/wiki/White_Oak_Conservation" title="White Oak Conservation">White Oak Conservation</a> in <a href="/wiki/Yulee,_Florida" title="Yulee, Florida">Yulee, Florida</a> for release in Kenya. White Oak staff members traveled with the bongos to a Mt. Kenya holding facility, where they stayed until being reintroduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bongo_(antelope)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the last few decades, a rapid decline in the numbers of wild mountain bongo has occurred due to poaching and human pressure on their habitat, with local extinctions reported in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cherangani_Hills" title="Cherangani Hills">Cherangani</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chepalungu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chepalungu (page does not exist)">Chepalungu</a> hills, Kenya.</p>
<p>The Bongo Surveillance Programme, working alongside the <a href="/wiki/Kenya_Wildlife_Service" title="Kenya Wildlife Service">Kenya Wildlife Service</a>, have recorded photos of bongos at remote salt licks in the Aberdare Forests using camera traps, and, by analyzing <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> extracted from <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">dung</a>, have confirmed the presence of bongo in Mount Kenya, <a href="/wiki/Ol_Doinyo_Eburru" title="Ol Doinyo Eburru">Eburru</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mau_forest" title="Mau forest">Mau forests</a>. The programme estimate as few as 140 animals left in the wild â spread across four isolated populations. Whilst captive breeding programmes can be viewed as having been successful in ensuring survival of this species in Europe and North America, the situation in the wild has been less promising. Evidence exists of bongo surviving in Kenya. However, these populations are believed to be small, fragmented, and vulnerable to extinction.</p>
<p>Animal populations with impoverished genetic diversity are inherently less able to adapt to changes in their environments (such as climate change, disease outbreaks, habitat change, etc.). The isolation of the four remaining small bongo populations, which themselves would appear to be in decline, means a substantial amount of genetic material is lost each generation. Whilst the population remains small, the impact of transfers will be greater, so the establishment of a "metapopulation management plan" occurs concurrently with conservation initiatives to enhance <i>in situ</i> population growth, and this initiative is both urgent and fundamental to the future survival of mountain bongo in the wild.</p>
<p>The western/lowland bongo faces an ongoing population decline as habitat destruction and hunting pressures increase with the relentless expansion of human settlement. Its long-term survival will only be assured in areas which receive active protection and management. At present, such areas comprise about 30,000Â km<sup>2</sup>, and several are in countries where political stability is fragile. So, a realistic possibility exists whereby its status could decline to Threatened in the near future.</p>
<p>As the largest and most spectacular forest antelope, the western/lowland bongo is both an important flagship species for protected areas such as national parks, and a major trophy species which has been taken in increasing numbers in Central Africa by sport hunters during the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-j1_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-j1-16">[16]</a></sup> Both of these factors are strong incentives to provide effective protection and management of populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Trophy_hunting" title="Trophy hunting">Trophy hunting</a> has the potential to provide economic justification for the preservation of larger areas of bongo habitat than national parks, especially in remote regions of Central Africa, where possibilities for commercially successful tourism are very limited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-j1_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-j1-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The eastern/mountain bongoâs survival in the wild is dependent on more effective protection of the surviving remnant populations in Kenya. If this does not occur, it will eventually become extinct in the wild. The existence of a healthy captive population of this subspecies offers the potential for its reintroduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Groups_supporting_bongo_conservation_in_Kenya">Groups supporting bongo conservation in Kenya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bongo_(antelope)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Groups supporting bongo conservation in Kenya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Four_bongo_calves_with_nanny.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1808" data-file-width="2876" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/51/Four_bongo_calves_with_nanny.jpg/220px-Four_bongo_calves_with_nanny.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/51/Four_bongo_calves_with_nanny.jpg/330px-Four_bongo_calves_with_nanny.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/51/Four_bongo_calves_with_nanny.jpg/440px-Four_bongo_calves_with_nanny.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Four_bongo_calves_with_nanny.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Four bongo calves photographed here with a "nanny": All will be released into the wild near Mount Kenya.</div>

<p>In 2004, Dr. Jake Veasey, the head of the Department of Animal Management and Conservation at <a href="/wiki/Woburn_Safari_Park" title="Woburn Safari Park">Woburn Safari Park</a> and a member of the European Association of Zoos and Aquariums Population Management Advisory Group, with the assistance of Lindsay Banks, took over responsibility for the management and coordination of the European Endangered Species Programme for the eastern bongo. This includes some 250 animals across Europe and the Middle East.</p>
<p>Along with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rothschild_giraffe" title="Rothschild giraffe">Rothschild giraffe</a>, the eastern bongo is arguably one of the most threatened large mammals in Africa, with recent estimates numbering less than 140 animals, below a minimum sustainable viable population. The situation is exacerbated because these animals are spread across four isolated populations. Whilst the bongo endangered species program can be viewed as having been successful in ensuring survival of this species in Europe, it has not yet become actively involved in the conservation of this species in the wild in a coordinated fashion. The plan is to engage in conservation activities in Kenya to assist in reversing the decline of the eastern bongo populations and genetic diversity in Africa, and in particular, applying population management expertise to help ensure the persistence of genetic diversity in the free ranging wild populations.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Baby_Bongo_002.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1011" data-file-width="783" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Baby_Bongo_002.jpg/170px-Baby_Bongo_002.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Baby_Bongo_002.jpg/255px-Baby_Bongo_002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Baby_Bongo_002.jpg/340px-Baby_Bongo_002.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Baby_Bongo_002.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A baby eastern bongo at <a href="/wiki/Louisville_Zoo" title="Louisville Zoo">Louisville Zoo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>To illustrate significance of genetic diversity loss, assume the average metapopulation size is 35 animals based on 140 animals spread across four populations (140/4=35). Assuming stable populations, these populations will lose 8% of their genetic diversity every decade. By managing all four populations as one, through strategic transfers, gene loss is reduced from 8% to 2% per decade, without any increase in bongo numbers in Kenya. By managing the European and African populations as one â by strategic exports from Europe combined with <i>in situ</i> transfers, gene loss is reduced to 0.72% every 100 years, with both populations remaining stable. If populations in Kenya are allowed to grow through the implementation of effective conservation, including strategic transfers, gene loss can be effectively halted in this species and its future secured in the wild.</p>
<p>The initial aims of the project are:</p>
<ol>
<li>Through faecal <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> analysis, estimate the genetic diversity of the remaining wild bongos and calculate the relatedness of the isolated wild populations.</li>
<li>More accurately estimate the total population of wild bongos through faecal DNA analysis, camera trapping, and transect surveying.</li>
<li>Through direct sampling, estimate the genetic diversity of the captive bongo population and calculate its relatedness with the remaining isolated wild populations.</li>
<li>Collect DNA samples from western bongos to calculate the relatedness of the two subspecies.</li>
<li>Fund rangers to collect the above data in Kenya, enhance the degree of protection afforded to and level of understanding of the eastern bongos' ecological needs.</li>
<li>To realise such a metapopulation management plan, work with local communities is essential to reverse the decline and allow for the implementation of a transfer strategy. A substantial proportion of wild genetic diversity likely will have already been lost.</li>
</ol>
<p>If effective protection were implemented immediately and bongo populations allowed to expand without transfers, then this would create a bigger population of genetically impoverished bongos. These animals would be less able to adapt to a dynamic environment. Whilst the population remains small, the impact of transfers will be greater. For this reason, the 'metapopulation management plan' must occur concurrently with conservation strategies to enhance <i>in situ</i> population growth. This initiative is both urgent and fundamental to the future survival of the mountain bongo in the wild.</p>
<p>In 2013, SafariCom telecommunications donated money to the Bongo Surveillance Programme <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> to try to keep tabs on what are thought to be the last 100 eastern bongos left in the wild in the Mau Eburu Forest in central Kenya, whose numbers are still declining due to logging of their habitat and illegal poaching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bongo_(antelope)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2002 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> listed the western/lowland species as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_Threatened" title="Near Threatened">Near Threatened</a>. These bongos may be endangered due to human environmental interaction, as well as hunting and illegal actions towards wildlife.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/CITES" title="CITES">CITES</a> lists bongos as an Appendix III species, only regulating their exportation from a single country, Ghana. It is not protected by the US Endangered Species Act and is not listed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USFWS" title="USFWS">USFWS</a>.</p>
<p>The IUCN Antelope Specialist Group considers the western or lowland bongo, <i>T. e. eurycerus</i>, to be Lower Risk (Near Threatened), and the eastern or mountain bongo, <i>T. e. isaaci</i>, of Kenya, to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Critically_Endangered" title="Critically Endangered">Critically Endangered</a>. Other subspecific names have been used, but their validity has not been tested.</p>
