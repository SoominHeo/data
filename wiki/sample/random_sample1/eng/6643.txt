<p>A <b>charnel house</b> is a vault or building where human skeletal remains are stored. They are often built near <a href="/wiki/Church_(building)" title="Church (building)">churches</a> for depositing bones that are unearthed while digging <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grave_(burial)" title="Grave (burial)">graves</a>. The term can also be used more generally as a description of a place filled with <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">death</a> and destruction.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_World">Old World</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charnel_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Old World">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In countries where ground suitable for burial was scarce, corpses would be interred for approximately five years following death, thereby allowing decomposition to occur. After this, the remains would be exhumed and moved to an <a href="/wiki/Ossuary" title="Ossuary">ossuary</a> or charnel house, thereby allowing the original burial place to be reused. In modern times, the use of charnel houses is a characteristic of cultures living in rocky or arid places, such as the <a href="/wiki/Cyclades" title="Cyclades">Cyclades</a> archipelago and other Greek islands in the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">Aegean Sea</a>.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Saint_Catherine%27s_Monastery" title="Saint Catherine's Monastery">Saint Catherine's Monastery</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinai" title="Sinai">Sinai</a> is famous for having a working charnel house. Saint Catherine's was founded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Justinian" title="Justinian">Justinian</a> in the early 6th century, on the site of an older monastery founded about 313 AD and named for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helena_of_Constantinople" title="Helena of Constantinople">Helena of Constantinople</a>. The monastery comprises the whole <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church_organization" title="Eastern Orthodox Church organization">Autonomous Church</a> of Sinai, under the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Church_of_Jerusalem" title="Greek Orthodox Church of Jerusalem">Patriarchate of Jerusalem</a>. The site lies at the foot of Mount Sinai where Christians believe <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a> received the Ten Commandments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the Sinai is an inhospitable place, the brethren of St. Catherine's have struggled to eke out a subsistence-level existence. The difficulty in establishing a large cemetery in the rocky ground notwithstanding, relics are also gathered for temporal and spiritual reasons: a reminder to the monks of their impending death and fate in the hereafter. The Archbishop of Saint Catherine's is commonly the <a href="/wiki/Abbot" title="Abbot">Abbot</a> as well. After death, he is afforded the dignity of a special niche within the "Skull-House".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charnel_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A charnel house is also a structure commonly seen in some <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a> societies of the Eastern United States. Major examples are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hopewell_culture" title="Hopewell culture">Hopewell cultures</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mississippian_culture" title="Mississippian culture">Mississippian cultures</a>. These houses were used specifically for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mortuary" title="Mortuary">mortuary</a> services and, although they required many more resources to build and maintain than a <a href="/wiki/Crypt" title="Crypt">crypt</a>, they were widely used. They offered privacy and shelter as well as enough workspace for mortuary proceedings. These proceedings included <a href="/wiki/Cremation" title="Cremation">cremation</a> (in the included crematorium) as well as defleshing of the body before the cremation. Once the houses had served their purpose, they were burned to the ground and covered by earth, creating a sort of burial <a href="/wiki/Mound" title="Mound">mound</a>. Anthropologist William F. Romain in <i>Mysteries of the Hopewell</i> notes that these charnel houses were built in the form of a square, and their diagonals could be aligned to the direction of maximum and minimum moon-sets both north and south.</p>
