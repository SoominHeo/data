<p><b>Bösendorfer</b> (L. Bösendorfer Klavierfabrik <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GmbH" title="GmbH">GmbH</a>) is an Austrian piano manufacturer, and since 2008, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wholly_owned_subsidiary" title="Wholly owned subsidiary">wholly owned subsidiary</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yamaha" title="Yamaha">Yamaha</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yamaha_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-yamaha-1">[1]</a></sup> Bösendorfer is unusual in that it produces 97- and 92-<a href="/wiki/Key_(instrument)" title="Key (instrument)">key</a> models in addition to instruments with standard 88-key <a href="/wiki/Musical_keyboard" title="Musical keyboard">keyboards</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%B6sendorfer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Bösendorfer, one of the oldest piano manufacturers, was established in 1828 by <a href="/wiki/Ignaz_B%C3%B6sendorfer" title="Ignaz Bösendorfer">Ignaz Bösendorfer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oldest_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-oldest-2">[2]</a></sup> It has a history of producing highly respected instruments; in 1830, it was granted the status of official piano maker to the <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Austria" title="Emperor of Austria">Emperor of Austria</a>. Ignaz's son <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwig_B%C3%B6sendorfer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ludwig Bösendorfer (page does not exist)">Ludwig Bösendorfer</a> (15 April 1835 – 9 May 1919) assumed control in 1859, operating from new premises from 1860. Between 1872 and its closure in 1913, the associated Bösendorfer-Saal was one of the premier concert halls of Vienna. In 1909, Carl Hutterstrasser purchased the company and was succeeded by his sons Alexander and Wolfgang in 1931. In 1966, the Jasper Corporation (later renamed <a href="/wiki/Kimball_International" title="Kimball International">Kimball International</a>), parent company of Kimball Pianos, assumed control of Bösendorfer, where it remained before returning to Austrian hands, when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BAWAG_PSK_Gruppe" title="BAWAG PSK Gruppe">BAWAG PSK Gruppe</a> purchased it in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-History-3">[3]</a></sup> BAWAG signed an agreement to sell all stock in Bösendorfer to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yamaha" title="Yamaha">Yamaha</a> on 20 December 2007.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%B6sendorfer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Boesendorfer_002.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Boesendorfer_002.jpg/220px-Boesendorfer_002.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Boesendorfer_002.jpg/330px-Boesendorfer_002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Boesendorfer_002.jpg/440px-Boesendorfer_002.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Boesendorfer_002.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A Bösendorfer piano, model 214CS</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Bösendorfer pioneered the extension of the typical 88-key <a href="/wiki/Musical_keyboard" title="Musical keyboard">keyboard</a>, creating the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_B%C3%B6sendorfer_(piano)" title="Imperial Bösendorfer (piano)">Imperial Grand (Model 290)</a>, which has 97 keys (eight octaves). <a href="/wiki/Ferruccio_Busoni" title="Ferruccio Busoni">Ferruccio Busoni</a> initially ordered this innovation in 1909 as part of a custom piano as he wanted to transcribe an organ piece that extended to the C below the standard keyboard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imperial_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-imperial-4">[4]</a></sup> This innovation worked so well that this piano was added to regular product offerings and quickly became one of the world's most sought-after concert grands. Because of the 290's success, the extra strings were added to Bösendorfer's other line of instruments such as the 225 model, which has 92 keys. The extra keys, at the bass end of the keyboard, were originally hidden beneath a hinged panel mounted between the piano's conventional low A and the left-hand end-cheek to prevent their being struck accidentally during normal play; more recent models have omitted this device and simply have the upper surface of the extra natural keys finished in matte black instead of white to differentiate them from the standard 88.</p>
<p>The Bösendorfer sound is usually described as darker or richer than the purer but less full-bodied sound of other pianos, such as <a href="/wiki/Steinway_%26_Sons" title="Steinway &amp; Sons">Steinway &amp; Sons</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yamaha_(manufacturer)" title="Yamaha (manufacturer)">Yamaha</a>. On the Imperial Grand, this characteristic tonal quality in part derives from the inclusion of 9 additional bass notes below bottom A. These extra 9 keys were originally added so that pianists could play <a href="/wiki/Ferruccio_Busoni" title="Ferruccio Busoni">Busoni</a>'s transcriptions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.S._Bach" title="J.S. Bach">J.S. Bach</a>'s organ works, which required the 32' bass pipes (usually played on the pedal organ). As very little other music makes direct use of the extra strings, they usually contribute to the piano's sonic character not through being played directly but by resonating, when other strings in the piano are struck, contributing additional body to the tone. Moreover, the bass notes of the Bösendorfer, including the extra bass keys, are very powerful, adding volume in demanding literature.</p>
<p>The rim of a Bösendorfer grand piano is built quite differently from that of all other grands. Instead of veneers bent around a form, the rim is made in solid sections of spruce and jointed together. Spruce is better at transmitting sound than reflecting it. This is perhaps why Bösendorfers tend to have a more delicate treble and a bass that features the fundamental tone more than the higher harmonics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fine.2C_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fine.2C-5">[5]</a></sup> There are also two other features of Bösendorfers that are shared with only a few other piano brands: one is a removable <i>capo d'astro</i> bar in the treble, which facilitates rebuilding of the instrument and, Bösendorfer says, provides greater acoustic separation from the plate, decreasing tonal absorption; the other is single-stringing, providing each string its own individual hitch pin on the plate instead of connecting it to a neighboring string. This design may slightly improve tuning stability and is an advantage in case of string breakage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fine_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fine-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The latest development in the Bösendorfer range is the CEUS digital grand piano reproducing system, which incorporates a computer-controlled mechanism that records a performance and plays it back. The requisite equipment can be fitted to most Bösendorfer pianos to allow the direct recording of pieces while capturing all the <a href="/wiki/Keyboard_expression" title="Keyboard expression">keyboard velocity</a> data as a .boe file.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ceus_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ceus-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>Bösendorfer makes eight models of grand piano (from 5'1" to 9'6") and two vertical pianos (48" and 52" upright). The 9'6" Imperial Grand is one of the world's largest pianos.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservatory_Series">Conservatory Series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%B6sendorfer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservatory Series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To appeal to a wider market, Bösendorfer designed the Conservatory Series for colleges and universities that could not afford Bösendorfer's standard black-model pianos. The production of the two CS Series pianos spends less time in "non-critical areas", cutting down costs of production and purchase, making them more affordable than standard models. The cases and frames are of satin finish, rather than polished, and initially, the pianos were loop-strung<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buying_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-buying-9">[9]</a></sup> rather than single-strung, but that difference has since been abandoned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-accoustic_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-accoustic-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Special_and_Limited_editions">Special and Limited editions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%B6sendorfer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Special and Limited editions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bösendorfer has produced a number of specially designed pianos named after famous composers such as <a href="/wiki/Franz_Schubert" title="Franz Schubert">Franz Schubert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Chopin" title="Frédéric Chopin">Frédéric Chopin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Liszt" title="Franz Liszt">Franz Liszt</a>, as well as pianos designed for special occasions, such as Bösendorfer's 170th and 175th anniversaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-limited_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-limited-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="SE_reproducing_piano">SE reproducing piano</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%B6sendorfer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: SE reproducing piano">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Under the ownership of Kimball, Bösendorfer built and sold a small number of 290SE automatic reproducing pianos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palmieri_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-palmieri-12">[12]</a></sup> The 'SE' designation was for Stahnke Engineering, whose founder, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wayne_Stahnke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wayne Stahnke (page does not exist)">Wayne Stahnke</a>, invented the mechanism. The 290 was fitted with electronics and mechanics to record on magnetic tape and playback through electro-mechanical actuation of the piano. After the release of the <a href="/wiki/Windows_3.1x" title="Windows 3.1x">Microsoft Windows v3.1</a> operating system, the 290SE could be attached to a PC computer for recording, editing, and playback. The 290SE system was the first commercially available computer-controlled "<a href="/wiki/Player_piano" title="Player piano">player piano</a>" capable of accurately reproducing both the notes and intensity of a performer's playing. Unfortunately this system was not further developed or patented due to its high cost. Competitors soon introduced patented reproducing piano technologies such as the <a href="/wiki/Yamaha_Corporation" title="Yamaha Corporation">Yamaha</a> <a href="/wiki/Disklavier" title="Disklavier">Disklavier</a> in 1982.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disklavier_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-disklavier-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thirty seven SE models were produced between 1984 and 1986, including the 225SE, the 275SE, and the 290SE Imperial model pianos. In the 290 range, this included some 290 to 290SE conversions, while one third of the production were 290SEs that sold for $90,000.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The research that went into the 290SE later laid the foundation for the CEUS computerized reproducing piano system.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Designer_Models">Designer Models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%B6sendorfer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Designer Models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bösendorfer produces a limited number of Artisan Models annually, each available for order only during the calendar year in which it was developed. An example of a designer model is the Bösendorfer <a href="/wiki/Swarovski" title="Swarovski">Swarovski</a> Crystal Grand piano. Three of these special pianos were produced in 2003 in honor of Bösendorfer's 175th anniversary. Each piano's case is encrusted with 8000 crystals and layers of gold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-special_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-special-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Three notable architects who have designed Bösendorfer piano models are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theophil_Freiherr_von_Hansen" title="Theophil Freiherr von Hansen">Theophil Freiherr von Hansen</a> (1866), <a href="/wiki/Josef_Hoffmann" title="Josef Hoffmann">Josef Hoffmann</a> (1909) and <a href="/wiki/Hans_Hollein" title="Hans Hollein">Hans Hollein</a> (1990). There were only two Hans Hollein 225 models produced in 1990; one can be found in the lounge of the Grand Bohemian Hotel in Orlando, Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kunz_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kunz-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Noteworthy_events">Noteworthy events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%B6sendorfer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Noteworthy events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>On 10 April 2007, a second-hand Bösendorfer Model 275 concert grand worth £45,000, but insured for half that amount, was damaged when it was dropped 2.5 m (8.2 ft) from a delivery truck. The piano was to feature at the "Two Moors Festival" in <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>. Bösendorfer replaced the piano with one valued at £85,000, and delivered it personally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quinn_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-quinn-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kennedy_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-kennedy-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2006, a group of friends of local pianist Ward Virts commissioned a Model 214 as a gift for the fine arts department of the <a href="/wiki/College_of_Southern_Maryland" title="College of Southern Maryland">College of Southern Maryland</a> in his memory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-virts_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-virts-19">[19]</a></sup> The piano, now on campus in a humidity and temperature controlled room, is valued at over $85,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-accoustic_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-accoustic-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:223</sup></li>
<li>In 2008, a YouTube video went viral showing an elderly couple playing the Bösendorfer given as a gift in the atrium of Gonda building at the Mayo Clinic in Rochester, Minnesota.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mayo_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-mayo-20">[20]</a></sup> Fran and Marlo Cowan had been married 62 years in 2008 when the video was taken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-curmudgeon_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-curmudgeon-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="B.C3.B6sendorfer_artists">Bösendorfer artists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%B6sendorfer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Bösendorfer artists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Boesendorfer_Liszt_Franz_Joseph.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="382" data-file-width="600" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Boesendorfer_Liszt_Franz_Joseph.jpg/220px-Boesendorfer_Liszt_Franz_Joseph.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Boesendorfer_Liszt_Franz_Joseph.jpg/330px-Boesendorfer_Liszt_Franz_Joseph.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Boesendorfer_Liszt_Franz_Joseph.jpg/440px-Boesendorfer_Liszt_Franz_Joseph.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Among the earliest artists to be associated with Bösendorfer was <a href="/wiki/Franz_Liszt" title="Franz Liszt">Franz Liszt</a> who at least once opined that Bösendorfer and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C._Bechstein_Pianofortefabrik" title="C. Bechstein Pianofortefabrik">Bechstein</a> pianos were the only instruments capable of withstanding his tremendously powerful playing, although he purchased and officially endorsed Steinway &amp; Sons pianos. The renowned twentieth-century American composer-conductor <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Bernstein" title="Leonard Bernstein">Leonard Bernstein</a> has also performed on a Bösendorfer. Another great pianist who championed Bösendorfer pianos was <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Backhaus" title="Wilhelm Backhaus">Wilhelm Backhaus</a>.</p>
<p>In his memoirs, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Rubinstein" title="Arthur Rubinstein">Arthur Rubinstein</a> recounts having insisted on a Bechstein instead of the hall's Bösendorfer before a recital in Austria. After the performance, the then-head of the Bösendorfer company came backstage to meet this young artist who refused to play a piano highly cherished by his Russian namesake, <a href="/wiki/Anton_Rubinstein" title="Anton Rubinstein">Anton Rubinstein</a>; Rubinstein claims he thereafter always sought out Bösendorfers when in Austria. Both Rubinsteins were Steinway &amp; Sons artists and played these pianos when in the United States.</p>
<p>In the late 1970s, following a concert performed in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, jazz pianist <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Peterson" title="Oscar Peterson">Oscar Peterson</a> turned to his impresario, <a href="/wiki/Norman_Granz" title="Norman Granz">Norman Granz</a>, with the words: "Dammit, Norman, where does this box go? I also gotta have such a thing!" Such was his reaction to playing a Bösendorfer 290.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oscar_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-oscar-22">[22]</a></sup> Musician/comedian <a href="/wiki/Victor_Borge" title="Victor Borge">Victor Borge</a> also played Bösendorfer pianos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-borge_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-borge-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>More recent examples of notable artists who have played the Bösendorfer include Russian pianist <a href="/wiki/Sviatoslav_Richter" title="Sviatoslav Richter">Sviatoslav Richter</a> (who in later years chose to promote <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yamaha" title="Yamaha">Yamaha</a> claiming it had a preferable pianissimo sound and control, according to his own interview);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ciao_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-ciao-24">[24]</a></sup> Hungarian pianist <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A1s_Schiff" title="András Schiff">András Schiff</a>; Italian pianist <a href="/wiki/Arturo_Benedetti_Michelangeli" title="Arturo Benedetti Michelangeli">Arturo Benedetti Michelangeli</a>; American <a href="/wiki/Free_jazz" title="Free jazz">free jazz</a> pianist <a href="/wiki/Cecil_Taylor" title="Cecil Taylor">Cecil Taylor</a> and American singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Tori_Amos" title="Tori Amos">Tori Amos</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amos_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-amos-25">[25]</a></sup> German pianist <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_R%C3%BCbsam" title="Wolfgang Rübsam">Wolfgang Rübsam</a>; Austrian pianist <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Gulda" title="Friedrich Gulda">Friedrich Gulda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Klien" title="Walter Klien">Walter Klien</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Badura-Skoda" title="Paul Badura-Skoda">Paul Badura-Skoda</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-owners_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-owners-26">[26]</a></sup> British pianists <a href="/wiki/Leon_McCawley" title="Leon McCawley">Leon McCawley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mark_Gasser" title="Mark Gasser">Mark Gasser</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maestro_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-maestro-27">[27]</a></sup> as well as the Irish pianist <a href="/wiki/John_O%27Conor" title="John O'Conor">John O'Conor</a>. Ukrainian pianist <a href="/wiki/Valentina_Lisitsa" title="Valentina Lisitsa">Valentina Lisitsa</a> has recorded DVDs of Chopin and Schubert-Liszt on a 1925 model Bösendorfer, and has released a new video set of a recital using the 97-key Bösendorfer Imperial.</p>
<p>Minimalist composer <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne_Palestine" title="Charlemagne Palestine">Charlemagne Palestine</a> chose a nine-foot Bösendorfer as the vehicle on which to perform his 1974 composition <i>Strumming Music</i>. Released as his first compact disc in 1991, it features in excess of 45 minutes of Palestine forcefully playing two notes in rapid alternation, slowly expanding into clusters, with the sustain pedal depressed throughout.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-duguid_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-duguid-28">[28]</a></sup> As the music swells (and the piano gradually detunes), the harmonics build and the listener can hear a variety of timbres rarely produced by the piano.</p>
<p>Jazz pianist <a href="/wiki/Keith_Jarrett" title="Keith Jarrett">Keith Jarrett</a> performed the solo improvisations (his <a href="/wiki/The_K%C3%B6ln_Concert" title="The Köln Concert">Köln Concert</a>) at the Cologne Opera House in <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a> on 24 January 1975 on a Bösendorfer and became a Steinway &amp; Sons artist in 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The jazz singers/pianists <a href="/wiki/Nina_Simone" title="Nina Simone">Nina Simone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Horn" title="Shirley Horn">Shirley Horn</a> performed on Bösendorfers many times throughout their careers.</p>
<p>In a recent interview for Broadway.com, Academy Award winning composer <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Schwartz_(composer)" title="Stephen Schwartz (composer)">Stephen Schwartz</a> stated that he purchased a Bösendorfer after the initial success of his musical <a href="/wiki/Wicked_(musical)" title="Wicked (musical)">Wicked</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recordings">Recordings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%B6sendorfer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Recordings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bösendorfer pianos have appeared on numerous records. Some examples are:</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical">Classical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%B6sendorfer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Classical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<li><a href="/wiki/Sviatoslav_Richter" title="Sviatoslav Richter">Sviatoslav Richter</a> recorded Bach's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Well-Tempered_Clavier" title="Well-Tempered Clavier">Well-Tempered Clavier</a> on a Bösendorfer at Salzburg in two settings of 1972 and 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clavier_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-clavier-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carol_Rosenberger" title="Carol Rosenberger">Carol Rosenberger</a> recorded music of Liszt, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Griffes" title="Charles Griffes">Griffes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ravel" title="Ravel">Ravel</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Debussy" title="Debussy">Debussy</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenberger_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenberger-35">[35]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Beethoven</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Piano_Sonata_No._23_(Beethoven)" title="Piano Sonata No. 23 (Beethoven)"><i>Appassionata</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/Piano_Sonata_No._32_(Beethoven)" title="Piano Sonata No. 32 (Beethoven)">op. 111</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenberger_2_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenberger_2-36">[36]</a></sup> sonatas on an Imperial Concert Grand.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moritz_Rosenthal" title="Moritz Rosenthal">Moritz Rosenthal</a> played a Bösendorfer for his celebrated series of recordings for <a href="/wiki/HMV" title="HMV">HMV</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rosenthal_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-rosenthal-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Terry_Riley" title="Terry Riley">Terry Riley's</a> 1986 <a href="/wiki/Minimalism" title="Minimalism">minimalist</a> piano piece written in <a href="/wiki/Just_intonation" title="Just intonation">Just intonation</a>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Harp_of_New_Albion&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Harp of New Albion (page does not exist)">The Harp of New Albion</a></i>, was recorded on a Bösendorfer Imperial grand piano, specially tuned for Riley himself.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Silverman" title="Robert Silverman">Robert Silverman</a> committed a complete Beethoven sonata cycle to computer hard drive on a Bösendorfer 290SE reproducing piano. Bach, Gigue, French Suite No. 5 (G-major).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Valentina_Lisitsa" title="Valentina Lisitsa">Valentina Lisitsa</a> Chopin's 24 Études D.V.D. track. Op. 10 and Op. 25 Études.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Igor_Stravinsky" title="Igor Stravinsky">Igor Stravinsky</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le_Sacre_du_Printemps" title="Le Sacre du Printemps">Le Sacre du Printemps</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Firebird" title="The Firebird">The Firebird</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petrushka" title="Petrushka">Petrushka</a> played by <a href="/wiki/Dag_Achatz" title="Dag Achatz">Dag Achatz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roland_P%C3%B6ntinen" title="Roland Pöntinen">Roland Pöntinen</a> on <a href="/wiki/BIS_Records" title="BIS Records">BIS Records</a> was played on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bosendorfer" title="Bosendorfer">Bosendorfer</a> Model 275</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kimiko_Douglass-Ishizaka" title="Kimiko Douglass-Ishizaka">Kimiko Douglass-Ishizaka</a> has recorded <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">J.S. Bach</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Goldberg_Variations" title="Goldberg Variations">Goldberg Variations</a> for public domain release on the Internet by the <a href="/wiki/Open_Goldberg_Variations" title="Open Goldberg Variations">Open Goldberg Variations</a> project, an initiative sponsored in part by Bösendorfer. She played a C290 Imperial fitted with the CEUS system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Accompanying the recording—offered in <a href="/wiki/MP3" title="MP3">MP3</a>, <a href="/wiki/FLAC" title="FLAC">FLAC</a>, and 24 bit 44 K <a href="/wiki/WAV" title="WAV">WAV</a> formats—is a freshly made copy of the full score.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Ishizaka also recorded Bach's <a href="/wiki/The_Well-Tempered_Clavier" title="The Well-Tempered Clavier">The Well-Tempered Clavier</a> on a Bösendorfer 280.</li>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular">Popular</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%B6sendorfer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Popular">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Victor_Borge" title="Victor Borge">Victor Borge</a> was known for using Bösendorfer pianos during many of his shows.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Oscar_Peterson" title="Oscar Peterson">Oscar Peterson</a> used Bösendorfer almost exclusively from 1977 until 2006.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Brian_May" title="Brian May">Brian May</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freddie_Mercury" title="Freddie Mercury">Freddie Mercury</a> of the band <a href="/wiki/Queen_(band)" title="Queen (band)">Queen</a> employed the Bösendorfer in the song <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flash_(song)" title="Flash (song)">Flash</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Elvis_Costello" title="Elvis Costello">Elvis Costello</a> in his album <i><a href="/wiki/Punch_the_Clock" title="Punch the Clock">Punch the Clock</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Peter_Gabriel" title="Peter Gabriel">Peter Gabriel</a> in his album <i><a href="/wiki/Up_(Peter_Gabriel_album)" title="Up (Peter Gabriel album)">Up</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Richard_Wright_(musician)" title="Richard Wright (musician)">Rick Wright</a> played a Bösendorfer grand piano on "<a href="/wiki/Shine_On_You_Crazy_Diamond" title="Shine On You Crazy Diamond">Shine On You Crazy Diamond (Part 8)</a>" from the <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a> album, <i><a href="/wiki/Wish_You_Were_Here_(Pink_Floyd_album)" title="Wish You Were Here (Pink Floyd album)">Wish You Were Here</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Roger_Waters" title="Roger Waters">Roger Waters</a> chose a Bösendorfer grand piano "9-foot-six, please" as his luxury item on <i><a href="/wiki/Desert_Island_Discs" title="Desert Island Discs">Desert Island Discs</a></i>, though only after he was denied a satellite link to watch <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arsenal_Football_Club" title="Arsenal Football Club">Arsenal Football Club</a> matches.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rick_Astley" title="Rick Astley">Rick Astley</a> playing a Bösendorfer grand piano in <a href="/wiki/Cry_for_Help" title="Cry for Help">Cry for Help</a> videoclip.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bradley_Joseph" title="Bradley Joseph">Bradley Joseph</a> in his album <i><a href="/wiki/Rapture_(Bradley_Joseph_album)" title="Rapture (Bradley Joseph album)">Rapture</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indie_Journal_Interview_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Indie_Journal_Interview-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Allevi" title="Giovanni Allevi">Giovanni Allevi</a> in his album <i><a href="/wiki/13_Dita" title="13 Dita">13 Dita</a></i>.</li>
<li>According to composer <a href="/wiki/Jim_Steinman" title="Jim Steinman">Jim Steinman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roy_Bittan" title="Roy Bittan">Roy Bittan</a> played a Bösendorfer on the <a href="/wiki/Meat_Loaf" title="Meat Loaf">Meat Loaf</a> album <i><a href="/wiki/Bat_Out_of_Hell" title="Bat Out of Hell">Bat Out of Hell</a></i> in 1977, which was a deviation from Steinman's preference for Yamaha pianos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li>Dr. Evil plays a Bösendorfer in the <i><a href="/wiki/Austin_Powers_(film_series)" title="Austin Powers (film series)">Austin Powers</a></i> movies ('böse' means evil in German).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Greek composer <a href="/wiki/Vangelis" title="Vangelis">Vangelis</a> used a Bösendorfer grand piano on his albums <i><a href="/wiki/Heaven_and_Hell_(Vangelis_album)" title="Heaven and Hell (Vangelis album)">Heaven and Hell</a></i> (1975) and <i><a href="/wiki/China_(album)" title="China (album)">China</a></i> (1979).</li>
<li>Singer/songwriter <a href="/wiki/Tori_Amos" title="Tori Amos">Tori Amos</a> has recorded and toured exclusively with Bösendorfers since 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Keith_Jarrett" title="Keith Jarrett">Keith Jarrett</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ln_Concert" title="Köln Concert">Köln Concert</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matthew_Bellamy" title="Matthew Bellamy">Matthew Bellamy</a> of the rock band <a href="/wiki/Muse_(band)" title="Muse (band)">Muse</a> has recently started using a Bösendorfer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li>The electronic dance music band <a href="/wiki/Above_%26_Beyond_(band)" title="Above &amp; Beyond (band)">Above &amp; Beyond (band)</a> used <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Paavo_Siljam%C3%A4ki&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Paavo Siljamäki (page does not exist)">Paavo Siljamäki</a>'s Bösendorfer grand piano on their song "Small Moments" (2012).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pete_Townshend" title="Pete Townshend">Pete Townshend</a> of the rock band <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">The Who</a> is seen playing a large Bösendorfer on the song, "<a href="/wiki/Who_Are_You_(song)" title="Who Are You (song)">Who Are You</a>", filmed during a recording at <a href="/wiki/Ramport_Studios" title="Ramport Studios">Ramport Studios</a> and featured in the movie, <i>The Kids Are Alright</i>.</li>
</ul>
