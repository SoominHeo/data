<p><b>Bodh Gaya</b> is a religious site and place of pilgrimage associated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mahabodhi_Temple_Complex" title="Mahabodhi Temple Complex">Mahabodhi Temple Complex</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gaya_district" title="Gaya district">Gaya district</a> in the <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> <a href="/wiki/States_and_union_territories_of_India" title="States and union territories of India">state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bihar" title="Bihar">Bihar</a>. It is famous as it is the place where <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">Gautama Buddha</a> is said to have obtained <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enlightenment_(Buddhism)" title="Enlightenment (Buddhism)">Enlightenment</a> (Pali: bodhi) under what became known as the <a href="/wiki/Bodhi_Tree" title="Bodhi Tree">Bodhi Tree</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Madan_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Madan-3">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>For Buddhists, Bodh Gaya is the most important of the main four pilgrimage sites related to the life of Gautama Buddha, the other three being <a href="/wiki/Kushinagar" title="Kushinagar">Kushinagar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lumbini" title="Lumbini">Lumbini</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sarnath" title="Sarnath">Sarnath</a>. In 2002, Mahabodhi Temple, located in Bodh Gaya, became a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unesco_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-unesco-4">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Bodh Gaya is the most holy place for the followers of the Buddhist faith all over the world. Situated by the bank of <a href="/wiki/Lilajan_River" title="Lilajan River">river Neranjana</a> the place was then known as Uruwela. King Ashoka was the first to build a temple here.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Traditionally, Buddha was born in 563 BC in what is now Nepal <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> on the following auspicious Baisakhi purnima. As Siddhartha, he renounced his family at the age of 29 in 534 BC <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Banglapedia_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Banglapedia-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> and travelled and meditated in search of truth. After practicing self-mortification for six years at Urubela (Buddhagaya) in Gaya, he gave up that practice because it did not give him Vimukthi. Then he discovered Noble Eight-fold path without help from anyone and practiced it, then he attained Buddhatva or enlightenment. Enlightenment is a state of being completely free from lust (raga), hatred (dosa) and delusion (moha). By gaining enlightenment, you enter Nibbana, in which the final stage is Parinibbana.</p>
<p>At this place, the Buddha was abandoned by the five men who had been his companions of earlier austerities. All they saw was an ordinary man; they mocked his well-nourished appearance. "Here comes the mendicant Gautama," they said, "who has turned away from asceticism. He is certainly not worth our respect." When they reminded him of his former vows, the Buddha replied, "Austerities only confuse the mind. In the exhaustion and mental stupor to which they lead, one can no longer understand the ordinary things of life, still less the truth that lies beyond the senses. I have given up extremes of either luxury or asceticism. I have discovered the Middle Way". This is the path which is neither easy (a rich prince) nor hard (living in austere conditions practicing self-denial). Hearing this, the five ascetics became the Buddha's first disciples in Deer Park, <a href="/wiki/Sarnath" title="Sarnath">Sarnath</a>, 13 km n.e. of Benares.</p>
<p>The disciples of Gautama Siddhartha began to visit the place during the full moon in the month of <a href="/wiki/Vesak" title="Vesak">Vaisakh</a> (April–May), as per the <a href="/wiki/Hindu_calendar" title="Hindu calendar">Hindu calendar</a>. Over time, the place became known as Bodh Gaya, the day of enlightenment as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddha_Purnima" title="Buddha Purnima">Buddha Purnima</a>, and the tree as the Bodhi Tree.</p>
<p>The history of Bodh Gaya is documented by many inscriptions and pilgrimage accounts. Foremost among these are the accounts of the <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> pilgrims <a href="/wiki/Faxian" title="Faxian">Faxian</a> in the 5th century and <a href="/wiki/Xuanzang" title="Xuanzang">Xuanzang</a> in the 7th century. The area was at the heart of a Buddhist civilization for centuries, until it was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_invasion_of_India" title="Islamic invasion of India">conquered</a> by Turkic armies in the 13th century. The place-name, Bodh Gaya, did not come into use until the 18th century CE. Historically, it was known as Uruvela, Sambodhi, Vajrasana or Mahabodhi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> The main monastery of Bodh Gaya used to be called the Bodhimanda-vihāra (<a href="/wiki/Pali" title="Pali">Pali</a>). Now it is called the <a href="/wiki/Mahabodhi_Temple" title="Mahabodhi Temple">Mahabodhi Temple</a>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2013_Bodh_Gaya_blasts" title="2013 Bodh Gaya blasts">Five bombs</a> were detonated in Mahabodhi temple premises on 7 July 2013. 4 bombs were also detonated in town while three were defused.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mahabodhi_Temple">Mahabodhi Temple</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodh_Gaya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mahabodhi Temple">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mahabodhi_Temple" title="Mahabodhi Temple">Mahabodhi Temple</a></div>
<p>The complex, located about 110 kilometres from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patna,_India" title="Patna, India">Patna</a>, at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Bodh_Gaya&amp;params=24_41_43_N_84_59_38_E_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">24°41′43″N</span> <span class="longitude">84°59′38″E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">24.69528°N 84.99389°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">24.69528; 84.99389</span></span></span></a></span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nomination_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-nomination-11">[10]</a></sup> contains the Mahabodhi Temple with the diamond throne (called the <i>Vajrasana</i>) and the holy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bodhi_tree" title="Bodhi tree">Bodhi tree</a>. This tree was originally a sapling of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sri_Maha_Bodhi" title="Sri Maha Bodhi">Sri Maha Bodhi</a> tree in <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, itself grown from a sapling of the original Bodhi tree.</p>
<p>It is believed that about 280 years after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enlightenment_(Buddhism)" title="Enlightenment (Buddhism)">Enlightenment</a> of the Buddha, Emperor <a href="/wiki/Ashoka" title="Ashoka">Ashoka</a> visited Bodh Gaya. He is considered to be the founder of the original Mahabodhi temple. It consisted of an elongated spire crowned by a miniature stupa and a <i>chhatravali</i> on a platform. A double flight of steps led up to the platform and the upper sanctum. The mouldings on the spire contained <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">Buddha</a> images in niches. Some historians believe that the temple was constructed or renovated in the 1st century during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kushan" title="Kushan">Kushan</a> period. With the decline of Buddhism in India, the temple was abandoned and forgotten, buried under layers of soil and sand.</p>
<p>The temple was later restored by Sir <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Cunningham" title="Alexander Cunningham">Alexander Cunningham</a> in the late 19th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1883, Cunningham along with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J._D._Beglar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="J. D. Beglar (page does not exist)">J. D. Beglar</a> and Dr <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rajendralal_Miitra&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rajendralal Miitra (page does not exist)">Rajendralal Miitra</a> painstakingly excavated the site. Extensive renovation work was carried out to restore Bodh Gaya to its former glory. It has a height of 55 metres which can be viewed from a distance of 11 km.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_Buddhist_temples">Other Buddhist temples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodh_Gaya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other Buddhist temples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kittisirimegha&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kittisirimegha (page does not exist)">Kittisirimegha</a> of Sri Lanka, a contemporary of <a href="/wiki/Samudragupta" title="Samudragupta">Samudragupta</a>, erected with the permission of Samudragupta, a <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">Sanghārāma</a> near the Mahabodhi Temple, chiefly for the use of the Singhalese monks who went to worship the Bodhi tree. The circumstances in connection with the Sanghārāma are given by <a href="/wiki/Xuanzang" title="Xuanzang">Xuanzang</a> (<a href="/wiki/Samuel_Beal" title="Samuel Beal">Beal</a>, op. cit., 133ff) who gives a description of it as seen by himself. It was probably here that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhaghosa" title="Buddhaghosa">Buddhaghosa</a> met the Elder Revata who persuaded him to come to Ceylon.</p>
<p>Several Buddhist temples and monasteries have been built by the people of <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sikkim" title="Sikkim">Sikkim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> in a wide area around the Mahabodhi Temple. These buildings reflect the architectural style, exterior and interior decoration of their respective countries. The statue of Buddha in the Chinese temple is 200 years old and was brought from China. Japan's Nippon temple is shaped like a pagoda. The Myanmar (Burmese) temple is also <a href="/wiki/Pagoda" title="Pagoda">pagoda</a> shaped and is reminiscent of <a href="/wiki/Bagan" title="Bagan">Bagan</a>. The Thai temple has a typical sloping, curved roof covered with golden tiles. Inside, the temple holds a massive bronze statue of Buddha. Next to the Thai temple is 25-metre statue of Buddha <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> located within a garden which has existed there for over 100 years.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mahabodhi_Temple_Serial_Blasts">Mahabodhi Temple Serial Blasts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodh_Gaya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mahabodhi Temple Serial Blasts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2013_Bodh_Gaya_blasts" title="2013 Bodh Gaya blasts">2013 Bodh Gaya blasts</a></div>
<p>On 7 July 2013, at around 5:15 A.M., a low intensity <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2013_Bodh_Gaya_blasts" title="2013 Bodh Gaya blasts">bomb blast</a> took place in the 1500-year-old Mahabodhi Temple complex. This was followed by a series of nine low intensity blasts and two monks were injured, one Tibetan and the other a Burmese. These blasts were carried out by an Islamic terrorist organisation, to seek revenge for the killings of Muslims in Burma. The serial blasts did not cause any damage to the temple or the tree under which Buddha attained enlightenment. Two other bombs, one under the 80-foot statue of the Buddha and the other near Karmapa Temple were defused by the police.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blast_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-blast-14">[13]</a></sup> Investigation of the blast was given to NIA (National Investigating Agency). Three unexploded bombs were also found in the Temple complex.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodh_Gaya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As per the <a href="/wiki/2001_Census_of_India" title="2001 Census of India">2001 census</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> Bodh Gaya had a population of 30,883. Males constitute 54% of the population and females 46%. Bodh Gaya has an average literacy rate of 51%, lower than the national average of 59.5%; with male literacy of 63% and female literacy of 38%. 8% of the population is under 6 years of age.</p>

<ul>
<li>Bodhgaya is about 12 km from Gaya City. The NH83 Highway connects Gaya and Bodh Gaya.</li>
<li>The State Tourism Department provides travel facility from <a href="/wiki/Patna" title="Patna">Patna</a> to Bodh Gaya.</li>
<li>Train and bus service for Gaya are available from <a href="/wiki/Kolkata" title="Kolkata">Kolkata</a>, which is an overnight journey.</li>
<li>Buses have been introduced by BSTDC between <a href="/wiki/Patna" title="Patna">Patna</a> and Bodh Gaya via <a href="/wiki/Rajgir" title="Rajgir">Rajgir</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>A special caravan service called <b>Wonder on Wheel</b>, between Patna and Bodh Gaya, has been introduced by Bihar Tourism Deptt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup></li>
