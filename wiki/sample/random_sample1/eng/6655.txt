</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p><b>Buddhaghoṣa</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sanskrit-dictionary_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-sanskrit-dictionary-1">[1]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th">พระพุทธโฆษาจารย์</span>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh">覺音/佛音</span>) was a 5th-century Indian <a href="/wiki/Theravada" title="Theravada">Theravada Buddhist</a> commentator and scholar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStrong200475_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStrong200475-3">[3]</a></sup> His best-known work is the <i><a href="/wiki/Visuddhimagga" title="Visuddhimagga">Visuddhimagga</a></i> "Path of Purification", a comprehensive summary and analysis of the Theravada understanding of the Buddha's path to liberation. The interpretations provided by Buddhaghosa have generally constituted the orthodox understanding of Theravada scriptures since at least the 12th century CE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crosby837_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-crosby837-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGombrich201251_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGombrich201251-5">[5]</a></sup> He is generally recognized by both Western scholars and Theravadins as the most important commentator of the Theravada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStrong200475_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStrong200475-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-v.Hin.C3.BCber102_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-v.Hin.C3.BCber102-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Buddhagho%E1%B9%A3a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Limited reliable information is available about the life of Buddhaghosa. Three primary sources of information exist: short prologues and epilogues attached to Buddhaghosa's works; details of his life recorded in the <i><a href="/wiki/Mahavamsa" title="Mahavamsa">Mahavamsa</a></i>, a Sri Lankan chronicle; and a later biographical work called the <i>Buddhaghosuppatti</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxviii_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxviii-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGray1892_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGray1892-8">[8]</a></sup> A few other sources discuss the life of Buddhaghosa, but do not appear to add any reliable material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-v.Hin.C3.BCber102_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-v.Hin.C3.BCber102-6">[6]</a></sup> His name means "Voice of the Buddha" (<i>Buddha</i>+<i>ghosa</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Pali" title="Pali">Pali</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PaliDic_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-PaliDic-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The biographical excerpts attached to works attributed to Buddhaghosa reveal relatively few details of his life, but were presumably added at the time of his actual composition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-v.Hin.C3.BCber102_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-v.Hin.C3.BCber102-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Largely identical in form, these short excerpts describe Buddhaghosa as having come to Sri Lanka from India and settled in <a href="/wiki/Anuradhapura" title="Anuradhapura">Anuradhapura</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxix-xxx_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxix-xxx-11">[11]</a></sup> Besides this information, they provide only short lists of teachers, supporters, and associates of Buddhaghosa, whose names are not generally to be found elsewhere for comparison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxix-xxx_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxix-xxx-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Mahavamsa" title="Mahavamsa">Mahavamsa</a></i> records that Buddhaghosa was born into a <a href="/wiki/Brahmin" title="Brahmin">Brahmin</a> family in the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Magadha" title="Magadha">Magadha</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStrong200475_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStrong200475-3">[3]</a></sup> He is said to have been born near <a href="/wiki/Bodh_Gaya" title="Bodh Gaya">Bodh Gaya</a>, and to have been a master of the <a href="/wiki/Vedas" title="Vedas">Vedas</a>, traveling through India engaging in philosophical debates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxiv_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxiv-12">[12]</a></sup> Only upon encountering a Buddhist monk named Revata was Buddhaghosa bested in debate, first being defeated in a dispute over the meaning of a Vedic doctrine and then being confounded by the presentation of a teaching from the <a href="/wiki/Abhidharma" title="Abhidharma">Abhidhamma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxiv_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxiv-12">[12]</a></sup> Impressed, Buddhaghosa became a <a href="/wiki/Bhikkhu" title="Bhikkhu">bhikkhu</a> (Buddhism monk) and undertook the study of the <a href="/wiki/Tripi%E1%B9%ADaka" title="Tripiṭaka">Tipiṭaka</a> and its commentaries. On finding a text for which the commentary had been lost in India, Buddhaghosa determined to travel to Sri Lanka to study a Sinhalese commentary that was believed to have been preserved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxiv_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxiv-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Sri Lanka, Buddhaghosa began to study what was apparently a very large volume of commentarial texts that had been assembled and preserved by the monks of the <a href="/wiki/Anuradhapura_Maha_Viharaya" title="Anuradhapura Maha Viharaya">Anuradhapura Maha Viharaya</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxii_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxii-13">[13]</a></sup> Buddhaghosa sought permission to synthesize the assembled Sinhalese-language commentaries into a comprehensive single commentary composed in <a href="/wiki/Pali" title="Pali">Pali</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxv_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxv-14">[14]</a></sup> The elder monks sought to first test Buddhaghosa's knowledge by assigning him the task of elaborating the doctrine regarding two verses of the <a href="/wiki/Sutra" title="Sutra">suttas</a>; Buddhaghosa replied by composing the <i><a href="/wiki/Visuddhimagga" title="Visuddhimagga">Visuddhimagga</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStrong200476_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStrong200476-15">[15]</a></sup> His abilities were further tested when deities intervened and hid the text of his book, twice forcing him to recreate it from scratch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxc_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxc-16">[16]</a></sup> When the three texts were found to completely summarize all of the Tipiṭaka and match in every respect, the monks acceded to his request and provided Buddhaghosa with the full body of their commentaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxv_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxv-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Buddhaghosa went on to write commentaries on most of the other major books of the Pali Canon, with his works becoming the definitive Theravadin interpretation of the scriptures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStrong200475_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStrong200475-3">[3]</a></sup> Having synthesized or translated the whole of the Sinhalese commentary preserved at the Anuradhapura Maha Viharaya, Buddhaghosa reportedly returned to India, making a pilgrimage to <a href="/wiki/Bodh_Gaya" title="Bodh Gaya">Bodh Gaya</a> to pay his respects to the <a href="/wiki/Bodhi_Tree" title="Bodhi Tree">Bodhi Tree</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxv_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxv-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The details of the Mahavamsa account cannot readily be verified; while it is generally regarded by Western scholars as having been embellished with legendary events (such as the hiding of Buddhaghosa's text by the gods), in the absence of contradictory evidence it is assumed to be generally accurate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxv_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxv-14">[14]</a></sup> While the Mahavamsa claims that Buddhaghosa was born in northern India near Bodh Gaya, the epilogues to his commentaries make reference to only one location in India as being a place of at least temporary residence: <a href="/wiki/Kanchipuram" title="Kanchipuram">Kanci</a> in southern India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-v.Hin.C3.BCber102_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-v.Hin.C3.BCber102-6">[6]</a></sup> Some scholars thus conclude (among them Oskar von Hinüber and <a href="/wiki/Polwatte_Buddhadatta_Thera" title="Polwatte Buddhadatta Thera">Polwatte Buddhadatta Thera</a>) that Buddhaghosa was actually born in <a href="/wiki/South_India" title="South India">South India</a> and was relocated in later biographies to give him closer ties to the region of the Buddha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-v.Hin.C3.BCber102_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-v.Hin.C3.BCber102-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Buddhaghosuppatti</i>, a later biographical text, is generally regarded by Western scholars as being legend rather than history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxix_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxix-17">[17]</a></sup> It adds to the Mahavamsa tale certain details, such as the identity of Buddhaghosa's parents and his village, as well as several dramatic episodes, such as the conversion of Buddhaghosa's father and Buddhaghosa's role in deciding a legal case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxvii-xxxviii_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxvii-xxxviii-18">[18]</a></sup> It also explains the eventual loss of the Sinhalese originals that Buddhaghosa worked from in creating his Pali commentaries by claiming that Buddhaghosa collected and burnt the original manuscripts once his work was completed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxviii_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxviii-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writings_and_translations">Writings and translations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Buddhagho%E1%B9%A3a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Writings and translations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Buddhaghosa was reputedly responsible for an extensive project of synthesizing and translating a large body of <a href="/wiki/Sinhalese_language" title="Sinhalese language">Sinhala</a> commentaries on the Pāli Canon. His <i>Visuddhimagga</i> (Pāli: Path of Purification) is a comprehensive manual of Theravada Buddhism that is still read and studied today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Mahavamsa" title="Mahavamsa">Mahavamsa</a> ascribes a great many books to Buddhaghosa's composition, some of which are not believed to have been his work, but rather were composed later and attributed to him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<p>In the 12th century, the Sri Lankan monk Sariputta became the leading scholar of the Theravada following the reunification of the Sri Lankan monastic community by King <a href="/wiki/Parakramabahu_I" title="Parakramabahu I">Parakramabahu I</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crosby837_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-crosby837-4">[4]</a></sup> Sariputta incorporated many of the works of Buddhaghosa into his own interpretations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crosby837_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-crosby837-4">[4]</a></sup> In subsequent years, many monks from Theravada traditions in Southeast Asia sought ordination or re-ordination in Sri Lanka because of the reputation of the Sri Lankan Mahavihara lineage for doctrinal purity and scholarship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crosby837_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-crosby837-4">[4]</a></sup> The result was the spread of the teachings of the <a href="/wiki/Mahavihara" title="Mahavihara">Mahavihara</a> tradition — and thus Buddhaghosa — throughout the Theravada world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crosby837_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-crosby837-4">[4]</a></sup> Buddhaghosa's commentaries thereby became the standard method by which the Theravada scriptures were understood, establishing Buddhaghosa as the definitive interpreter of Theravada doctrine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStrong200476_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStrong200476-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In later years, Buddhaghosa's fame and influence inspired various accolades. His life story was recorded, in an expanded and likely exaggerated form, in a Pali chronicle known as the <i>Buddhaghosuppatti</i>, or "The Development of the Career of Buddhaghosa".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStrong200476_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStrong200476-15">[15]</a></sup> Despite the general belief that he was Indian by birth, he later may have been claimed by the <a href="/wiki/Mon_people" title="Mon people">Mon people</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhism_in_Burma" title="Buddhism in Burma">Burma</a> as an attempt to assert primacy over Sri Lanka in the development of Theravada tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Other scholars believe that the Mon records refer to another figure, but whose name and personal history are much in the mold of the Indian Buddhaghosa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxix_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxix-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Finally, Buddhaghosa's works likely played a significant role in the revival and preservation of the <a href="/wiki/Pali" title="Pali">Pali</a> language as the scriptural language of the Theravada, and as a <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a> in the exchange of ideas, texts, and scholars between Sri Lanka and the Theravada countries of mainland Southeast Asia. The development of new analyses of Theravada doctrine, both in Pali and Sinhalese, seems to have dried up prior to Buddhaghosa's emergence in Sri Lanka.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxvii_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxvii-27">[27]</a></sup> In India, new schools of Buddhist philosophy (such as the <a href="/wiki/Mahayana" title="Mahayana">Mahayana</a>) were emerging, many of them making use of classical <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> both as a scriptural language and as a language of philosophical discourse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxvii_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxvii-27">[27]</a></sup> The monks of the Mahavihara may have attempted to counter the growth of such schools by re-emphasizing the study and composition in Pali, along with the study of previously disused secondary sources that may have vanished in India, as evidenced by the Mahavamsa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxvii-xxviii_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxvii-xxviii-28">[28]</a></sup> Early indications of this resurgence in the use of Pali as a literary language may be visible in the composition of the <a href="/wiki/Dipavamsa" title="Dipavamsa">Dipavamsa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Vimuttimagga" title="Vimuttimagga">Vimuttimagga</a>, both dating to shortly before Buddhaghosa's arrival in Sri Lanka.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxviii_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxviii-7">[7]</a></sup> The addition of Buddhaghosa's works — which combined the pedigree of the oldest Sinhalese commentaries with the use of Pali, a language shared by all of the Theravada learning centers of the time — provided a significant boost to the revitalization of the Pali language and the Theravada intellectual tradition, possibly aiding the Theravada school in surviving the challenge to its position posed by emerging Buddhist schools of mainland India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxix-x_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhikkhu_.C3.91.C4.81.E1.B9.87amoli1999xxxix-x-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some scholars have argued that Buddhaghosa's writing evinces a strong but unacknowledged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yog%C4%81c%C4%81ra" title="Yogācāra">Yogācāra</a> Buddhist influence, which subsequently came to characterize Theravada thought in the wake of his profound influence on the Theravada tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critics">Critics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Buddhagho%E1%B9%A3a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Critics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Visuddhimagga reflects changes in interpretation which appeared during the centuries since the Buddha's time.</p>
<p>The Australian Buddhist monastic <a href="/wiki/Shravasti_Dhammika" title="Shravasti Dhammika">Shravasti Dhammika</a> is critical of contemporary practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brokenbuddha_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-brokenbuddha-31">[31]</a></sup> He concludes that Buddhaghosa did not believe that following the practice set forth in the <i>Visuddhimagga</i> will really lead him to Nirvana, basing himself on the postscript to the Visuddhimagga:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brokenbuddha_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-brokenbuddha-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Even Buddhaghosa did not really believe that Theravada practice could lead to Nirvana. His <i>Visuddhimagga</i> is supposed to be a detailed, step by step guide to enlightenment. And yet in the postscript [...] he says he hopes that the merit he has earned by writing the <i>Vishuddhimagga</i> will allow him to be reborn in heaven, abide there until Metteyya (<a href="/wiki/Maitreya" title="Maitreya">Maitreya</a>) appears, hear his teaching and then attain enlightenment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brokenbuddha_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-brokenbuddha-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[note 1]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Yet Ñāṇamoli notes that this postscript does not appear in the original Pali.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[note 2]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Kalupahana, Buddhaghosa was influenced by Mahayana-thought, which were subtly mixed with Theravada orthodoxy to introduce new ideas. Eventually this led to the flowering of metaphysical tendencies, in contrast to the original stress on <a href="/wiki/Anatta" title="Anatta">anattā</a> in early Buddhism<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKalupahana1994_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKalupahana1994-38">[36]</a></sup></p>
