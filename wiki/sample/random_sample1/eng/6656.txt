<p><i><b>Budae jjigae</b></i> (<small>Korean pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Korean" title="Help:IPA for Korean">[pudɛ tɕ͈iɡɛ]</a></span>; literally "army (military) base stew" or "troop stew") is a type of <i><a href="/wiki/Jjigae" title="Jjigae">jjigae</a></i> (a thick Korean soup similar to a Western stew). Soon after the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>, food was scarce in <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>. Some people made use of surplus foods from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Forces_Korea" title="US Forces Korea">U.S. military</a> bases around the <a href="/wiki/Uijeongbu" title="Uijeongbu">Uijeongbu</a> area, <a href="/wiki/Pyeongtaek" title="Pyeongtaek">Pyeongtaek</a> area (also called <a href="/wiki/Songtan" title="Songtan">Songtan</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Munsan" title="Munsan">Munsan</a> area, such as <a href="/wiki/Hot_dog" title="Hot dog">hot dogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spam_(food)" title="Spam (food)">Spam</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Ham" title="Ham">ham</a>, and incorporated them into a traditional spicy soup flavored with <i><a href="/wiki/Gochujang" title="Gochujang">gochujang</a></i> (red chili paste) and <a href="/wiki/Kimchi" title="Kimchi">kimchi</a>.</p>

<p><i>Budae jjigae</i> is still popular in South Korea. The dish often incorporates such modern ingredients as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Instant_noodles" title="Instant noodles">instant noodles</a> and sliced <a href="/wiki/American_cheese" title="American cheese">American cheese</a>. Other ingredients may include <a href="/wiki/Ground_beef" title="Ground beef">ground beef</a>, sliced <a href="/wiki/Sausage" title="Sausage">sausages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baked_beans" title="Baked beans">baked beans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oenanthe_javanica" title="Oenanthe javanica"><i>minari</i></a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Onions" title="Onions">onions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scallion" title="Scallion">green onions</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Tteok" title="Tteok">tteok</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Tofu" title="Tofu">tofu</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chili_peppers" title="Chili peppers">chili peppers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macaroni" title="Macaroni">macaroni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Garlic" title="Garlic">garlic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mushrooms" title="Mushrooms">mushrooms</a>, and other vegetables in season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Budaejjigae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The dish originated during the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>, and was popular for a time afterwards, when people had little to eat. People made this dish by combining leftover Spam and hot dogs from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army" title="U.S. Army">U.S. Army</a> facilities with whatever else was available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> All the leftovers were combined with water in a large pot and boiled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The dish is also referred to as Johnson Tang (존슨 탕), combining the surname <a href="/wiki/Johnson" title="Johnson">Johnson</a> for President <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tang_(food)" title="Tang (food)">tang</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">탕</a>, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%B9%AF" title="wikt:湯">湯</a>) a word meaning soup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city of <a href="/wiki/Uijeongbu" title="Uijeongbu">Uijeongbu</a>, which is bordered by Seoul to the south and has many army bases, is famous for its <i>budae jjigae</i>. In the late 20th century, the city of Uijeongbu stipulated that the dish be referred to as <i>Uijeongbu jjigae</i> to remove the military or war-time connotation in the name, though not many restaurants follow this guideline. Some restaurants have begun calling their product <i>Uijeongbu budae jjigae</i>. There is also what locals refer to as "Uijeongbu Budaejjigae Street" where there is a high concentration of buddaejjigae restaurants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
