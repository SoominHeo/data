<p><b>Burrata</b> is a fresh <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> cheese made from <a href="/wiki/Mozzarella" title="Mozzarella">mozzarella</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cream" title="Cream">cream</a>. The outer shell is solid mozzarella, while the inside contains <a href="/wiki/Stracciatella_di_bufala" title="Stracciatella di bufala">stracciatella</a> and cream, giving it an unusual, soft texture. It is also defined by some sources as an outer shell of mozzarella filled with butter or a mixture of butter and sugar. It is usually served fresh and at room temperature. The word <i>burrata</i> means "buttered" in <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burrata&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Burrata is a typical product of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Murgia" title="Murgia">Murgia</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_of_Italy" title="South of Italy">south of Italy</a>. It is produced from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cow" title="Cow">cow</a>'s milk, <a href="/wiki/Rennet" title="Rennet">rennet</a>, and cream. Burrata was probably first made around 1920, but may have origins dating back to about 1900<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> on the Bianchini farm in the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andria,_Italy" title="Andria, Italy">Andria</a> within Murgia, an area in the <a href="/wiki/Apulia" title="Apulia">Apulian</a> region. In the 1950s, it became more widely available after some local cheese factories began producing it and it can be a useful way of using up the <i>ritagli</i> ("scraps" or "rags") of mozzarella.</p>
<p>Established as an <a href="/wiki/Artisan_cheese" title="Artisan cheese">artisanal cheese</a>, burrata maintained its premium-product status even after it began to be made commercially in factories throughout Apulia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Burrata is also produced in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, particularly in artisanal cheese shops in cities on the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast</a> with historically large populations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_American" title="Italian American">Italian Americans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burrata&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:190px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Burrata_Cheese_Salad.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1578" height="244" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Burrata_Cheese_Salad.jpg/188px-Burrata_Cheese_Salad.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Burrata_Cheese_Salad.jpg/282px-Burrata_Cheese_Salad.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Burrata_Cheese_Salad.jpg/376px-Burrata_Cheese_Salad.jpg 2x" width="188"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Burrata_Cheese_Salad.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Burrata cheese on a salad</div>
</div>

<p>Burrata starts out much like mozzarella and many other cheeses, with <a href="/wiki/Rennet" title="Rennet">rennet</a> used to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curdle" title="Curdle">curdle</a> the warm milk. But then, unlike other cheeses, fresh mozzarella <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curds" title="Curds">curds</a> are plunged into hot <a href="/wiki/Whey" title="Whey">whey</a> or lightly salted water, kneaded, and pulled to develop the familiar stretchy strings (<a href="/wiki/Pasta_filata" title="Pasta filata">pasta filata</a>), then shaped in whatever form is desired.</p>
<p>When making burrata, the still-hot cheese is formed into a pouch, which is then filled with scraps of leftover mozzarella and topped off with fresh <a href="/wiki/Cream" title="Cream">cream</a> before closing. The finished burrata is traditionally wrapped in the leaves of <a href="/wiki/Asphodelus" title="Asphodelus">asphodel</a>, tied to form a little <a href="/wiki/Brioche" title="Brioche">brioche</a>-like topknot, and moistened with a little whey. The asphodel leaves should still be green when the cheese is served to indicate the cheese's freshness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> More recently, the cheese is often sold in a plastic bag or container.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Serving_indications">Serving indications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burrata&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Serving indications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the burrata is sliced open, a spurt of thickened cream flows out. The cheese has a rich, buttery flavor and retains its fresh milkiness. It is best when eaten within 24 hours and is considered past its prime after 48 hours. The flavor and different textures of the inside and outside make it go well with salad,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Prosciutto" title="Prosciutto">prosciutto crudo</a>, crusty bread, fresh tomatoes with olive oil, cracked black pepper, or <a href="/wiki/Pasta" title="Pasta">pasta</a>.</p>
