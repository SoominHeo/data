<p>The <b>Burrunan dolphin</b> (<i>Tursiops australis</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Bottlenose_dolphin" title="Bottlenose dolphin">bottlenose dolphin</a> found in parts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victoria,_Australia" title="Victoria, Australia">Victoria, Australia</a>. It was recognised as a species in 2011. By size, the Burrunan dolphin is between the other two species of bottlenose dolphin and only around 150 individuals have been found in two locations.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burrunan_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The species was formally named <i>Tursiops australis</i> by the researcher who described the species, <a href="/wiki/Kate_Charlton-Robb" title="Kate Charlton-Robb">Kate Charlton-Robb</a> of <a href="/wiki/Monash_University" title="Monash University">Monash University</a>, and colleagues. The dolphin's common name, Burrunan, is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Aborigines" title="Australian Aborigines">Aboriginal</a> name in the <a href="/wiki/Bunurong" title="Bunurong">Boonwurrung</a>, <a href="/wiki/Woiwurrung_language" title="Woiwurrung language">Woiwurrung</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taungurong" title="Taungurong">Taungurung</a> languages, meaning "large sea fish of the porpoise kind".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monash110915_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-monash110915-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plosone2011_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-plosone2011-2">[2]</a></sup> The species name <i>australis</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> adjective "southern", and refers to the Australian range of the dolphin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plosone2011_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-plosone2011-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Burrunan dolphin was thought to be one of the two recognized species of <a href="/wiki/Bottlenose_dolphin" title="Bottlenose dolphin">bottlenose dolphin</a>. Some differences had been noted, but for a long time there was not enough evidence to classify it as its own species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc110915_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc110915-3">[3]</a></sup> However, an examination of their skulls, external characteristics and DNA from old and current samples revealed unique characteristics which resulted in its classification as a separate species by researchers in a paper submitted on 27 January 2011 and published on 14 September 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monash110915_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-monash110915-1">[1]</a></sup> It is the third time since the late 19th century that a new dolphin species has been recognised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abc110915_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-abc110915-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burrunan_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Burrunan dolphin is dark bluish-gray at the top near to the <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_fin" title="Dorsal fin">dorsal fin</a> extending over the head and sides of the body. Along the midline it is a lighter gray which extends as a blaze over on the side near the dorsal fin. Ventrally it is off-white, which reaches over the eye and the flipper in some instances. It is smaller than the <a href="/wiki/Common_bottlenose_dolphin" title="Common bottlenose dolphin">common bottlenose dolphin</a> but larger than the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Pacific_bottlenose_dolphin" title="Indo-Pacific bottlenose dolphin">Indo-Pacific bottlenose dolphin</a>, measuring between 2.27â2.78 metres (7.4â9.1Â ft) in length.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plosone2011_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-plosone2011-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burrunan_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Only two resident populations of the Burrunan dolphin have been identified, one in <a href="/wiki/Port_Phillip" title="Port Phillip">Port Phillip</a> and the other in the <a href="/wiki/Gippsland_Lakes" title="Gippsland Lakes">Gippsland Lakes</a>. Their combined population has been estimated as about 150 dolphins (100 in Port Phillip and 50 in Gippsland).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monash110915_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-monash110915-1">[1]</a></sup> Additionally, <i>T. australis</i> <a href="/wiki/Haplotype" title="Haplotype">haplotypes</a> have been documented in dolphins located in waters off eastern Tasmania, and in coastal waters of South Australia in the <a href="/wiki/Spencer_Gulf" title="Spencer Gulf">Spencer Gulf</a> region and west to <a href="/wiki/St_Francis_Island" title="St Francis Island">St Francis Island</a>. The initial report on the Burrunan dolphin suggested that the low volume of individuals found might immediately qualify the species for protection under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_Protection_and_Biodiversity_Conservation_Act" title="Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act">Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plosone2011_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-plosone2011-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
