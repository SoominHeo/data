<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Wurtz–Fittig reaction</h1>

<p>The <b>Wurtz–Fittig reaction</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a> of <a href="/wiki/Aryl_halide" title="Aryl halide">aryl halides</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkyl_halide" title="Alkyl halide">alkyl halides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> metal to give substituted aromatic compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The reaction is named after <a href="/wiki/Charles-Adolphe_Wurtz" title="Charles-Adolphe Wurtz">Charles-Adolphe Wurtz</a>, who discovered in 1855 a similar reaction between two alkyl halides (<a href="/wiki/Wurtz_reaction" title="Wurtz reaction">Wurtz reaction</a>), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rudolph_Fittig" title="Rudolph Fittig">Rudolph Fittig</a>, who discovered that also aryl halides undergo this reaction.</p>
<dl>
<dd><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wurtz%E2%80%93Fittig_reaction_Picture.jpg" title="The Wurtz–Fittig reaction"><img alt="The Wurtz–Fittig reaction" data-file-height="100" data-file-width="1029" height="78" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Wurtz%E2%80%93Fittig_reaction_Picture.jpg/800px-Wurtz%E2%80%93Fittig_reaction_Picture.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e6/Wurtz%E2%80%93Fittig_reaction_Picture.jpg 1.5x" width="800"/></a></dd>
</dl>
<p>The reaction works best for forming asymmetrical products if the halide reactants are somehow separate in their relative <a href="/wiki/Reactivity_(chemistry)" title="Reactivity (chemistry)">chemical reactivities</a>. One way to accomplish this is to form the reactants with halogens of different <a href="/wiki/Period_(periodic_table)" title="Period (periodic table)">periods</a>. Typically the alkyl halide is made more reactive than the aryl halide, increasing the probability that the alkyl halide will form the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organosodium" title="Organosodium">organosodium</a> bond first and thus act more effectively as a <a href="/wiki/Nucleophile" title="Nucleophile">nucleophile</a> toward the aryl halide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Typically the reaction is used for the alkylation of aryl halides; however, with the use of ultrasound the reaction can also be made useful for the production of biphenyl compounds of type Ph–Ph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
