<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Burkini</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>burkini</b> (or <b>burqini</b>; <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of <a href="/wiki/Burqa" title="Burqa">burqa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bikini" title="Bikini">bikini</a>, though qualifying as neither of these garments) is a type of <a href="/wiki/Swimsuit" title="Swimsuit">swimsuit</a> for women, designed in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> by <a href="/wiki/Aheda_Zanetti" title="Aheda Zanetti">Aheda Zanetti</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SMH_19082016_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-SMH_19082016-1">[1]</a></sup> The suit covers the whole body except the face, the hands and the feet, while being light enough for swimming. The design is intended to accord with Islamic traditions of modest dress. Zanetti's company Ahiida owns the trademarks to the words <i>burkini</i> and <i>burqini</i>, but they have become generic terms for similar forms of Islamic swimwear. In 2016, a number of French municipalities banned the use of burkini, which sparked an international controversy.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burkini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Hijab" title="Hijab">Hijab</a></div>
<p>The design is intended to accord with Islamic traditions of modest dress. The suit covers the whole body except the face, the hands and the feet, whilst being light enough to enable swimming. It resembles a full-length <a href="/wiki/Wetsuit" title="Wetsuit">wetsuit</a> with a built-in <a href="/wiki/Hood_(headgear)" title="Hood (headgear)">hood</a>, but is somewhat looser and made of swimsuit material instead of <a href="/wiki/Neoprene" title="Neoprene">neoprene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Zanetti's company Ahiida owns the trademarks to the words <i>burqini</i> and <i>burkini</i>, but they have become generic terms for similar forms of Islamic swimwear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other styles of "Islamic" swimwear include the <a href="/wiki/Veilkini" title="Veilkini">veilkini</a> and MyCozzie brand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Zanetti criticized the mycozzie suit, claiming it used lycra and was unsafe. This was disputed by the designer of the mycozzie swimsuit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burkini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2014 some private pools in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>'s tourist hotspots prohibited the use of burkini citing "hygiene reasons", which sparked a political controversy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burkini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In August 2009, a woman in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> was prevented from swimming in a public pool wearing a burkini, amidst <a href="/wiki/Islamic_scarf_controversy_in_France" title="Islamic scarf controversy in France">ongoing controversy</a> about Islamic dress. The action was justified by reference to a law that forbids swimming in street clothes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2016, the mayor of <a href="/wiki/Cannes" title="Cannes">Cannes</a> banned the swimsuits, citing a possible link to Islamic extremism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> At least 20 other French towns, including <a href="/wiki/Nice" title="Nice">Nice</a> subsequently joined the ban.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubin_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-rubin-10">[10]</a></sup> Dozens of women were subsequently issued fines, with some tickets citing not wearing "an outfit respecting good morals and secularism", and some were verbally attacked by bystanders when they were confronted by the police.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubin_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-rubin-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cockburn_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cockburn-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quinn_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Quinn-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-row-escalates_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-row-escalates-13">[13]</a></sup> Enforcement of the ban also hit beachgoers wearing a wide range of modest attire besides the burkini.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubin_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-rubin-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-row-escalates_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-row-escalates-13">[13]</a></sup> Media reported that in one case armed police forced a woman to remove her clothing on a beach in Nice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cockburn_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cockburn-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quinn_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Quinn-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-row-escalates_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-row-escalates-13">[13]</a></sup> The Nice mayor's office denied that she was forced to do so and the mayor condemned what he called the "unacceptable provocation" of wearing such clothes in the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/2016_Nice_attack" title="2016 Nice attack">Nice terrorist attack</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubin_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-rubin-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-row-escalates_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-row-escalates-13">[13]</a></sup> The ban enacted by the commune of <a href="/wiki/Villeneuve-Loubet" title="Villeneuve-Loubet">Villeneuve-Loubet</a> has been suspended by <a href="/wiki/Conseil_d%27%C3%89tat_(France)" title="Conseil d'État (France)">France's highest administrative court</a>, setting a potential precedent for further legal challenges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burkini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The ban was supported by a number of French politicians including the socialist prime minister <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Valls" title="Manuel Valls">Manuel Valls</a> who said "The burkini is not a new range of swimwear, a fashion. It is the expression of a political project, a counter-society, based notably on the enslavement of women."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Some commentators in France criticized the bans and reports of Muslim women being stopped by police for wearing headscarves and long-sleeved clothes on beaches caused outrage among members of the French socialist party and rights groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-row-escalates_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-row-escalates-13">[13]</a></sup> A poll showed that 64% of the French public supported the bans while another 30% were indifferent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The bans and their enforcement prompted criticism and ridicule abroad, particularly in English-speaking countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubin_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-rubin-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-straights_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-straights-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bangkok_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-bangkok-18">[18]</a></sup> A <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> editorial called French politicians’ "paternalistic pronouncements on the republic’s duty to save Muslim women from enslavement" bigotry and hypocritical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-ed_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-ed-19">[19]</a></sup> Liberal British Muslim activist <a href="/wiki/Maajid_Nawaz" title="Maajid Nawaz">Maajid Nawaz</a> offered a critique of both the swimsuit and its ban: "Burkini is sad symbol of Islam today going backwards on gender issues. Banning it is sad symbol of liberalism today going backwards in reply."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Other Muslim commentators, particularly Muslim women, have argued that the burkini gives women who do not wish to expose their body for religious or other reasons the freedom to enjoy the beach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-straights_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-straights-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Watch" title="Human Rights Watch">Human Rights Watch</a> also criticized the ban, stating that it "actually amounts to banning women from the beach, in the middle of the summer, just because they wish to cover their bodies in public. It’s almost a form of collective punishment against Muslim women for the actions of others."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use">Use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burkini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Zanetti estimates that 40% of her customer base has been non-Muslim. She stated: "We've sold to Jews, Hindus, Christians, Mormons, women with various body issues. We've had men asking for them, too."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7-facts_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-7-facts-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Notable wearers have included <a href="/wiki/Nigella_Lawson" title="Nigella Lawson">Nigella Lawson</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> who wears a burkini not out of religious observance but to protect her skin.</p>
<p>The burkini has also found popularity in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, both among the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haredi" title="Haredi">Haredi</a> and among <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslims</a>, and is called either <i>burkini</i> or simply "modest swimwear".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Australia, the burkini has been worn by female beach <a href="/wiki/Lifeguard" title="Lifeguard">lifeguards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SMH_19082016_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-SMH_19082016-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> They wear a special yellow and red design created by Zanetti in 2007 when <a href="/wiki/Surf_Life_Saving_Australia" title="Surf Life Saving Australia">Surf Life Saving Australia</a> began to look for Muslim lifeguards in the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/2005_Cronulla_riots" title="2005 Cronulla riots">anti-Muslim riots</a> on Sydney's beaches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7-facts_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-7-facts-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
