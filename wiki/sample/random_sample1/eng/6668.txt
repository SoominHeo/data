<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Burleske</i></h1>

<p>The <i><b>Burleske in D minor</b></i> is a composition for piano and orchestra written by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Strauss" title="Richard Strauss">Richard Strauss</a> in 1885-86, when he was 21.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burleske&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Original_title_and_dedication">Original title and dedication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burleske&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Original title and dedication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The work's original title was <i>Scherzo in D minor</i>, and it was written for <a href="/wiki/Hans_von_B%C3%BClow" title="Hans von Bülow">Hans von Bülow</a>, who had appointed Strauss assistant conductor of the Meiningen Orchestra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSO_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSO-1">[1]</a></sup> However, von Bülow considered it a "complicated piece of nonsense" and refused to learn it. He said the piano part was "Lisztian" and "unplayable", particularly for a pianist with a small handspan (Strauss says that von Bülow could barely reach an octave).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFS_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-SFS-2">[2]</a></sup> Strauss rehearsed the work with the Meiningen Orchestra, conducting and playing the solo part himself, but then set it aside. He wrote to von Bülow: "[G]iven an outstanding (!) pianist, and a first-rate (!) conductor, perhaps the whole thing will not turn out to be the unalloyed nonsense I took it for after the first rehearsal. After the first run-through, I was totally discouraged."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSO_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-MSO-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaming.2C_rededication_and_premiere">Renaming, rededication and premiere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burleske&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Renaming, rededication and premiere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1889, Strauss became acquainted with <a href="/wiki/Eugen_d%27Albert" title="Eugen d'Albert">Eugen d'Albert</a>, who liked the work, although he suggested some cuts and changes to the piano part.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naxos_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-naxos-3">[3]</a></sup> Strauss rededicated the revised work to d'Albert, who premiered it under its new title <i>Burleske</i>, at a convention of the General German Music Association<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFS_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-SFS-2">[2]</a></sup> at <a href="/wiki/Eisenach" title="Eisenach">Eisenach</a> on 21 June 1890, in the same concert as the premiere of Strauss's <i><a href="/wiki/Death_and_Transfiguration" title="Death and Transfiguration">Death and Transfiguration</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFS_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-SFS-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naxos_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-naxos-3">[3]</a></sup> The word "Burleske" translates as "farce"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> or "mockery".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSO_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-MSO-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction">Reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burleske&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Von Bülow was still not impressed. In a letter to <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Brahms" title="Johannes Brahms">Johannes Brahms</a> in January 1891, he wrote: "Strauss's <i>Burleske</i> decidedly has some genius in it, but in other respects it is horrifying."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naxos_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-naxos-3">[3]</a></sup> Despite this, he conducted the work that month in Berlin, with Eugen d'Albert as soloist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFS_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-SFS-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Strauss was offered a handsome sum by a publisher for the rights to the work, but he was still not convinced of its merits, so he declined. He was no doubt influenced in his own opinion of the work by von Bülow's opinion of it. A quarter of a century later he wrote about von Bülow: "For anyone who ever heard him play <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Beethoven</a> or conduct <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Wagner</a>, who attended one of his piano lessons or observed him in orchestra rehearsal, he inevitably became the model of all the shining virtues of a performing artist, and his touching sympathy for me, his influence on the development of my artistic abilities, were the decisive factors in my career." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFS_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-SFS-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication">Publication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burleske&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Publication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>However, in 1894, Strauss agreed to publication of the <i>Burleske</i>, although it took him a long time to regard it with great favour. It eventually became one of his favourite works, and he programmed it in his last concert in London with the Philharmonia Orchestra in September 1947,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFS_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-SFS-2">[2]</a></sup> along with <i><a href="/wiki/Don_Juan_(Strauss)" title="Don Juan (Strauss)">Don Juan</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Symphonia_Domestica" title="Symphonia Domestica">Symphonia Domestica</a></i> and the waltzes from <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Rosenkavalier" title="Der Rosenkavalier">Der Rosenkavalier</a></i>.</p>
<p>Strauss never allocated an <a href="/wiki/Opus_number" title="Opus number">opus number</a> to the work,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSO_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-MSO-1">[1]</a></sup> but the opus number 11 has often come to be associated with it, although Strauss gave that number to his Horn Concerto No. 1 in E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">♭</span>. It appears as number 85 in the catalogue by Erich Herrmann Mueller von Asow, and number 145 in that by Franz Trenner (see <a href="/wiki/List_of_compositions_by_Richard_Strauss#Other_works" title="List of compositions by Richard Strauss">List of compositions by Richard Strauss#Other works</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_premieres">Other premieres</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burleske&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other premieres">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first performance of the <i>Burleske</i> in the United States was by <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Gebhard" title="Heinrich Gebhard">Heinrich Gebhard</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Symphony_Orchestra" title="Boston Symphony Orchestra">Boston Symphony Orchestra</a>, under <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Gericke" title="Wilhelm Gericke">Wilhelm Gericke</a>, in April 1903.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFS_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-SFS-2">[2]</a></sup> The first performance in Australia was by <a href="/wiki/Vera_Bradford" title="Vera Bradford">Vera Bradford</a> in 1937, with the <a href="/wiki/Melbourne_Symphony_Orchestra" title="Melbourne Symphony Orchestra">Melbourne Symphony Orchestra</a> conducted by <a href="/wiki/Georg_Schn%C3%A9evoigt" title="Georg Schnéevoigt">Georg Schnéevoigt</a>. Schnéevoigt considered the piano part manageable only by a male pianist, and demanded Vera Bradford be replaced; but she stood her ground and gave a celebrated performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_scoring">Structure and scoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burleske&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Structure and scoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The piece starts with a theme introduced on <a href="/wiki/Timpani" title="Timpani">timpani</a> and answered by the orchestra. The piano then enters in a state of high excitement. A second, more lyrical <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Brahms" title="Johannes Brahms">Brahmsian</a> theme emerges, followed by waltz-like measures not unlike the waltzes from <i>Der Rosenkavalier</i>. The work ends quietly, again on the timpani. It takes about 20 minutes to perform.</p>
<p>The piece is scored for <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a> solo and an orchestra of <a href="/wiki/Piccolo" title="Piccolo">piccolo</a>, 2 <a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">flutes</a>, 2 <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboes</a>, 2 <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">clarinets</a> in B-flat, 2 <a href="/wiki/Bassoon" title="Bassoon">bassoons</a>, 4 <a href="/wiki/French_horn" title="French horn">horns</a> (2 in F, 2 in D), 2 <a href="/wiki/Trumpet" title="Trumpet">trumpets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timpani" title="Timpani">timpani</a>, and <a href="/wiki/String_section" title="String section">strings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>Notable performers who have played or recorded the <i>Burleske</i> include <a href="/wiki/Martha_Argerich" title="Martha Argerich">Martha Argerich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Claudio_Arrau" title="Claudio Arrau">Claudio Arrau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emanuel_Ax" title="Emanuel Ax">Emanuel Ax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Backhaus" title="Wilhelm Backhaus">Wilhelm Backhaus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maurizio_Baglini" title="Maurizio Baglini">Maurizio Baglini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Buchbinder" title="Rudolf Buchbinder">Rudolf Buchbinder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michel_Dalberto" title="Michel Dalberto">Michel Dalberto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barry_Douglas_(musician)" title="Barry Douglas (musician)">Barry Douglas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Frager" title="Malcolm Frager">Malcolm Frager</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Freire" title="Nelson Freire">Nelson Freire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Gould" title="Glenn Gould">Glenn Gould</a>, <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A9l%C3%A8ne_Grimaud" title="Hélène Grimaud">Hélène Grimaud</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Gulda" title="Friedrich Gulda">Friedrich Gulda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marc-Andr%C3%A9_Hamelin" title="Marc-André Hamelin">Marc-André Hamelin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Byron_Janis" title="Byron Janis">Byron Janis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Kahane" title="Jeffrey Kahane">Jeffrey Kahane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Lonquich" title="Alexander Lonquich">Alexander Lonquich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elly_Ney" title="Elly Ney">Elly Ney</a> (1932), <a href="/wiki/Sviatoslav_Richter" title="Sviatoslav Richter">Sviatoslav Richter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Serkin" title="Rudolf Serkin">Rudolf Serkin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jascha_Spivakovsky" title="Jascha Spivakovsky">Jascha Spivakovsky</a> (with Strauss himself conducting) and <a href="/wiki/Jean-Yves_Thibaudet" title="Jean-Yves Thibaudet">Jean-Yves Thibaudet</a>. There is a recording of Strauss himself conducting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Philharmonia" title="The Philharmonia">the Philharmonia</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Albert_Hall" title="Royal Albert Hall">Royal Albert Hall</a> London, 19 October 1947, with Alfred Blumen on the piano (this was the last concert he ever conducted).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001, the music was used for the ballet <i>Burleske</i>, choreographed for the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Ballet" title="New York City Ballet">New York City Ballet</a> by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Martins" title="Peter Martins">Peter Martins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
