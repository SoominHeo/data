<p>The <b>coracoid <a href="/wiki/Process_(anatomy)" title="Process (anatomy)">process</a></b> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> κόραξ, raven<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) is a small hook-like structure on the lateral edge of the superior anterior portion of the <a href="/wiki/Scapula" title="Scapula">scapula</a> (hence: coracoid, or "like a raven's beak"). Pointing laterally forward, it, together with the <a href="/wiki/Acromion" title="Acromion">acromion</a>, serves to stabilize the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glenohumeral_joint" title="Glenohumeral joint">shoulder joint</a>. It is <a href="/wiki/Palpation" title="Palpation">palpable</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Deltopectoral_groove" title="Deltopectoral groove">deltopectoral groove</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Deltoid_muscle" title="Deltoid muscle">deltoid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pectoralis_major_muscle" title="Pectoralis major muscle">pectoralis major muscles</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coracoid_process&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The coracoid process is a thick curved process attached by a broad base to the upper part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neck_of_the_scapula" title="Neck of the scapula">neck of the scapula</a>; it runs at first upward and medialward; then, becoming smaller, it changes its direction, and projects forward and lateralward.</p>
<p>The <i>ascending portion</i>, flattened from before backward, presents in front a smooth concave surface, across which the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subscapularis" title="Subscapularis">Subscapularis</a> passes.</p>
<p>The <i>horizontal portion</i> is flattened from above downward; its upper surface is convex and irregular, and gives attachment to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pectoralis_minor" title="Pectoralis minor">Pectoralis minor</a>; its under surface is smooth; its medial and lateral borders are rough; the former gives attachment to the <a href="/wiki/Pectoralis_minor_muscle" title="Pectoralis minor muscle">Pectoralis minor</a> and the latter to the <a href="/wiki/Coracoacromial_ligament" title="Coracoacromial ligament">coracoacromial ligament</a>; the apex is embraced by the <a href="/wiki/Conjoint_tendon" title="Conjoint tendon">conjoined tendon</a> of origin of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coracobrachialis" title="Coracobrachialis">Coracobrachialis</a> and short head of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biceps_brachii" title="Biceps brachii">Biceps brachii</a> and gives attachment to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coracoclavicular_fascia" title="Coracoclavicular fascia">coracoclavicular fascia</a>.</p>
<p>On the medial part of the root of the coracoid process is a rough impression for the attachment of the <a href="/wiki/Conoid_ligament" title="Conoid ligament">conoid ligament</a>; and running from it obliquely forward and lateralward, on to the upper surface of the horizontal portion, is an elevated ridge for the attachment of the <a href="/wiki/Trapezoid_ligament" title="Trapezoid ligament">trapezoid ligament</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coracoid_process&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is the site of attachment for several structures:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Pectoralis_minor_muscle" title="Pectoralis minor muscle">pectoralis minor muscle</a> - to 3rd, 4th and 5th <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribs" title="Ribs">rib</a>.</li>
<li>The short head of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biceps_brachii_muscle" title="Biceps brachii muscle">biceps brachii muscle</a> - to <a href="/wiki/Radial_tuberosity" title="Radial tuberosity">Radial tuberosity</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Coracobrachialis_muscle" title="Coracobrachialis muscle">coracobrachialis muscle</a> - to medial <a href="/wiki/Humerus" title="Humerus">humerus</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Coracoclavicular_ligament" title="Coracoclavicular ligament">coracoclavicular ligament</a> - to the <a href="/wiki/Clavicle" title="Clavicle">clavicle</a>. (The ligament is formed by the <a href="/wiki/Conoid_ligament" title="Conoid ligament">conoid ligament</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trapezoid_ligament" title="Trapezoid ligament">trapezoid ligament</a>.)</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Coracoacromial_ligament" title="Coracoacromial ligament">coracoacromial ligament</a> - to the <a href="/wiki/Acromion" title="Acromion">acromion</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Coracohumeral_ligament" title="Coracohumeral ligament">coracohumeral ligament</a> - to the <a href="/wiki/Humerus" title="Humerus">humerus</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Superior_transverse_scapular_ligament" title="Superior transverse scapular ligament">superior transverse scapular ligament</a> - from the base of the <a href="/wiki/Coracoid" title="Coracoid">coracoid</a> to the medial portion of the <a href="/wiki/Suprascapular_notch" title="Suprascapular notch">suprascapular notch</a></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coracoid_process&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Clinical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The coracoid process is palpable just below the lateral end of the clavicle (collar bone). It is otherwise known as the "Surgeon's Lighthouse" because it serves as a landmark to avoid neurovascular damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Major neurovascular structures enter the upper limb medial to the coracoid process, so that surgical approaches to the shoulder region should always take place laterally to the coracoid process.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_animals">Other animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coracoid_process&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Monotreme" title="Monotreme">monotremes</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Coracoid" title="Coracoid">coracoid</a> is a separate bone. <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">Reptiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a> (but not <a href="/wiki/Salamander" title="Salamander">salamanders</a>) also possess a bone by this name, but is not <a href="/wiki/Homology_(biology)" title="Homology (biology)">homologous</a> with the coracoid process of mammals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VB_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-VB-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Analyses of the size and shape of the coracoid process in <i><a href="/wiki/Australopithecus_africanus" title="Australopithecus africanus">Australopithecus africanus</a></i> (STS 7) have shown that in this species it displayed a prominent dorsolateral tubercle placed more laterally than in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anatomically_modern_humans" title="Anatomically modern humans">modern humans</a>. This reflect, according to one interpretation, a scapula positioned high on a funnel-shaped thorax and a clavicle positioned obliquely as in extant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_apes" title="Great apes">great apes</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
