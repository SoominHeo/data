<p><b>Diastereomers</b> (sometimes called <b>diastereoisomers</b>) are a type of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereoisomer" title="Stereoisomer">stereoisomer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Diastereomerism occurs when two or more stereoisomers of a compound have different configurations at one or more (but not all) of the equivalent (related) <a href="/wiki/Stereocenter" title="Stereocenter">stereocenters</a> and are not mirror images of each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> When two diastereoisomers differ from each other at only one stereocenter they are <a href="/wiki/Epimer" title="Epimer">epimers</a>. Each stereocenter gives rise to two different configurations and thus increases the number of stereoisomers by a factor of two.</p>
<p>Diastereomers differ from <a href="/wiki/Enantiomer" title="Enantiomer">enantiomers</a> in that the latter are pairs of stereoisomers that differ in all stereocenters and are therefore mirror images of one another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Enantiomers of a compound with more than one stereocenter are also diastereomers of the other stereoisomers of that compound that are not their mirror image. Diastereomers have different physical properties (unlike enantiomers) and different chemical reactivity. <b>Diastereoselectivity</b> is the preference for the formation of one or more than one diastereomer over the other in an <a href="/wiki/Organic_reaction" title="Organic reaction">organic reaction</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Syn_.2F_anti">Syn / anti</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diastereomer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Syn / anti">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the single bond between the two centres is free to rotate, cis/trans descriptors are invalid. Two widely accepted prefixes used to distinguish diastereomers on sp³-hybridised bonds in an open-chain molecule are <b>syn</b> and <b>anti</b>. Masamune proposed the descriptors which work even if the groups are not on adjacent carbons. It also works regardless of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cahn-Ingold-Prelog_priority_rules" title="Cahn-Ingold-Prelog priority rules">CIP</a> priorities. Syn describes groups on the same face while anti describes groups on opposite faces. The concept applies only to the Zigzag projection. The descriptors only describe relative stereochemistry rather than absolute stereochemistry.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Erythro_.2F_threo">Erythro / threo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diastereomer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Erythro / threo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two older prefixes still commonly used to distinguish diastereomers are <b>threo</b> and <b>erythro</b>. In the case of carbohydrates, when drawn in the <a href="/wiki/Fischer_projection" title="Fischer projection">Fischer projection</a> the erythro isomer has two identical substituents on the same side and the threo isomer has them on opposite sides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> When drawn as a zig-zag chain, the erythro isomer has two identical substituents on different sides of the plane (anti). The names are derived from the diastereomeric aldoses <a href="/wiki/Erythrose" title="Erythrose">erythrose</a> (a syrup) and <a href="/wiki/Threose" title="Threose">threose</a> (melting point 126 °C). These prefixes are not recommended for use outside of the realm of carbohydrates because their definitions can lead to conflicting interpretations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another threo compound is <a href="/wiki/Threonine" title="Threonine">threonine</a>, one of the essential amino acids. The <i>erythro</i> diastereomer is called <i>allo</i>-threonine.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Multiple_stereocenters">Multiple stereocenters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diastereomer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Multiple stereocenters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>If a molecule contains two asymmetric carbons, there are up to 4 possible configurations, and they cannot all be non-superimposable mirror images of each other. The possibilities continue to multiply as there are more asymmetric centers in a molecule. In general, the number of configurational isomers of a molecule can be determined by calculating 2<sup><i>n</i></sup>, where <i>n</i> = the number of <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">chiral</a> centers in the molecule. This holds true except in cases where the molecule has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meso_compounds" title="Meso compounds">meso</a> forms.</p>
<p>For <i>n</i> = 3, there are eight stereoisomers. There are four pairs of enantiomers: R,R,R and S,S,S; R,R,S and S,S,R; R,S,S and S,R,R; and R,S,R and S,R,S. There are four diastereomers, because each of the pairs of enantiomers is a diastereomer with respect to the other three. For <i>n</i> = 4, there are sixteen stereoisomers, or eight pairs of enantiomers. The four <a href="/wiki/Pentose" title="Pentose">aldopentoses</a> and the eight <a href="/wiki/Hexose" title="Hexose">aldohexoses</a> (subsets of the five- and six-carbon sugars) are examples of sets of compounds that differ in this way.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Example">Example</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diastereomer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Example">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tartaric_acid" title="Tartaric acid">Tartaric acid</a> contains two asymmetric centers, but two of the "isomers" are equivalent and together are called a <a href="/wiki/Meso_compound" title="Meso compound">meso compound</a>. This configuration is not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_activity" title="Optical activity">optically active</a>, while the remaining two isomers are <small>D</small>- and <small>L</small>- mirror images, <i>i.e.</i>, enantiomers. The meso form is a diastereomer of the other forms.</p>

<p>The families of 4, 5 and 6 carbon <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a> contain many diastereomers because of the large numbers of asymmetric centres in these molecules.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diastereomer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As stated, two diastereomers will not have identical chemical properties. This knowledge is harnessed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiral_synthesis" title="Chiral synthesis">chiral synthesis</a> to separate a mixture of enantiomers. This is the principle behind <a href="/wiki/Chiral_resolution" title="Chiral resolution">chiral resolution</a>. After preparing the diastereomers, they are separated by <a href="/wiki/Chromatography" title="Chromatography">chromatography</a> or <a href="/wiki/Recrystallization_(chemistry)" title="Recrystallization (chemistry)">recrystallization</a>. Note also the example of the <a href="/wiki/Stereochemistry_of_ketonization_of_enols_and_enolates" title="Stereochemistry of ketonization of enols and enolates">stereochemistry of ketonization of enols and enolates</a>.</p>
