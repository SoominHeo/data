<p><b>Bouillon</b>, in <a href="/wiki/French_cuisine" title="French cuisine">French cuisine</a>, is a <a href="/wiki/Broth" title="Broth">broth</a> or soup prepared from broth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This name comes from the verb <i>bouillir</i>, meaning <i>to boil</i>. It is usually made by the simmering of <a href="/wiki/Mirepoix_(cuisine)" title="Mirepoix (cuisine)"><i>mirepoix</i></a> and aromatic herbs (usually a <i><a href="/wiki/Bouquet_garni" title="Bouquet garni">bouquet garni</a></i>) with either <a href="/wiki/Beef" title="Beef">beef</a>, <a href="/wiki/Veal" title="Veal">veal</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">poultry</a> bones and/or with <a href="/wiki/Shrimp" title="Shrimp">shrimp</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vegetables" title="Vegetables">vegetables</a> in boiling water.</p>
<p>Traditionally, the word <i>bouillon</i> was used to describe a soup based on beef broth, and <i>court-bouillon</i> if based on fish broth. Clear beef bouillon is called <a href="/wiki/Consomm%C3%A9" title="ConsommÃ©">consommÃ©</a>.</p>
<p>This is not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Bouillon_(soup)" title="Bouillon (soup)"><i>bouillon</i> soup</a>, a Haitian soup, or <a href="/wiki/Stock_(food)" title="Stock (food)">stock</a>, which is made in a (somewhat) similar manner, but serves an entirely different purpose in French cuisine.</p>
