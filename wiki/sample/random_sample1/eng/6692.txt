<p>The <b>Bouyei</b> (also spelled <i>Puyi</i>, <i>Buyei</i> and <i>Buyi</i>; self called: <b>Buxqyaix</b> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[puʔjai]</a></span>, or "Puzhong", "Burao", "Puman"; <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">布依族</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">Pinyin</a>: Bùyīzú; <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">Vietnamese</a>: <span lang="vi" xml:lang="vi"><i>người Bố Y</i></span>) are an <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnic group</a> living in southern mainland China. Numbering 2.5 million, they are the 11th largest of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Chinese_ethnic_groups" title="List of Chinese ethnic groups">56 ethnic groups</a> officially recognized by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>. Some Bouyei also live in <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, where they are one of that nation's <a href="/wiki/List_of_ethnic_groups_in_Vietnam" title="List of ethnic groups in Vietnam">54 officially recognized ethnic groups</a>. Despite the Chinese considering them a separate group, they consider themselves <a href="/wiki/Zhuang_people" title="Zhuang people">Zhuang</a> (<a href="/wiki/Tai_peoples" title="Tai peoples">Tai peoples</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Bouyei live in semi-tropical, high-altitude forests of <a href="/wiki/Guizhou" title="Guizhou">Guizhou</a> province, as well as in <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">Sichuan</a> provinces, and speak a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tai_language" title="Tai language">Tai language</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names">Names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bouyei_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Bouyei consist of various subgroups. Below are their autonyms written in the <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">International Phonetic Alphabet</a> with <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)#Asia" title="Tone (linguistics)">numerical Chao tones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>pu42 ʔjɐi42</i> 濮越</li>
<li><i>pu42 ʔji22</i> 濮夷</li>
<li><i>pu42 noŋ31</i> 布侬</li>
<li><i>pu42 loŋ31</i> 补笼</li>
<li><i>pu42 na31</i> 布那</li>
<li><i>pu42 tu42</i> 布土、布都</li>
<li><i>pu42 ʔjaŋ33</i> 布央</li>
<li><i>pu42 zoŋ31 xa35</i> 布笼哈</li>
</ul>
<p>Some clans within the Bouyei groups include:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>pu42 wu42</i> 布武</li>
<li><i>pu42 wei31</i> 布韦</li>
<li><i>pu42 lo24</i> 布鲁</li>
</ul>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Congjiang_County" title="Congjiang County">Congjiang County</a>, Guizhou, there is a group that refer to themselves as "Buyeyi 布也益", but are officially classified by the Chinese government as ethnic <a href="/wiki/Zhuang_people" title="Zhuang people">Zhuang</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bouyei_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bouyei_language" title="Bouyei language">Bouyei language</a></div>
<p>The Bouyei speak the <a href="/wiki/Bouyei_language" title="Bouyei language">Bouyei language</a>, which is very close to <a href="/wiki/Standard_Zhuang" title="Standard Zhuang">Standard Zhuang</a> language. There is a dialect continuum between these two. The Bouyei language has its own written form which was created by linguists in the 1950s based on the Latin alphabet and with spelling conventions similar for the <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">Pinyin</a> system that had been devised to romanise <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin Chinese</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bouyei_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Bouyei are the native Tai peoples of the plains of Guizhou. They are one of the oldest peoples of China, living in the area for more than 2,000 years. Prior to the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a>, the Bouyei and <a href="/wiki/Zhuang_people" title="Zhuang people">Zhuang</a> were linked together; the differences between both ethnic groups grew greater and from year 900 already they were two different groups. The <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing dynasty</a> abolished the system of local heads and commanded in its place to officials of the army which caused a change in the local economy; from then on, the land was in the hands of a few landowners, which caused the population to revolt. During the Nanlong Rebellion of 1797, the Bouyei underwent a strong repression that caused many of them to emigrate to faraway <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>.</p>
