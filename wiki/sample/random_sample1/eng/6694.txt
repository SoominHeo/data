<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Fu (tally)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_China" title="Ancient China">ancient China</a> a <i><b>fu</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">符</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin"><span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Zh-f%C3%BA.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a> </span><a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/12/Zh-f%C3%BA.ogg" title="Zh-fú.ogg">fú</a></span></span></span></i>) was a <a href="/wiki/Tally_stick" title="Tally stick">tally</a>, made of bamboo, wood, metal (gold, silver, bronze) or jade and used as a proof of authorization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CIO_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-CIO-1">[1]</a></sup> Most commonly, <i>fu</i> were made of wood or bamboo in sizes of wooden slips or bamboo <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chopstick" title="Chopstick">chopsticks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CIO_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-CIO-1">[1]</a></sup> Although <i>fu</i> have been made in various shapes (such as tiger, dragon, turtle, snake, fish or human), most of them come in the shape of a tiger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CIO_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-CIO-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Classical description of tallies is found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zhou_li" title="Zhou li">Zhou li</a>, which refers to the tallies of jade and horn, and mentions the shapes of tiger, human, dragon, seal, and banner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hu-fu">Hu-fu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fu_(tally)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Hu-fu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tiger_in_Chinese_culture" title="Tiger in Chinese culture">Tiger in Chinese culture</a></div>
<p>The two-piece tiger tallies (<i>hu-fu</i>, 虎符 in Chinese) were used by a king or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_emperor" title="Chinese emperor">emperor</a> to command and dispatch the army. The right piece was retained in the central government while the left piece was issued to the local officials or commanders. If someone wanted to dispatch the troops from a certain area, he had to show the right piece of the tiger tally in order to obtain authorization; both pieces of the tally had to match each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The word <i>hu-fu</i> first appears in the biography of Hsin-ling in the <i><a href="/wiki/Records_of_the_Grand_Historian" title="Records of the Grand Historian">Records of the Grand Historian</a></i>, which tells the story of the stealing of a tiger tally to relieve the <a href="/wiki/Zhao_(state)" title="Zhao (state)">state of Zhao</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Needham_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Needham-4">[4]</a></sup> During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warring_States" title="Warring States">Warring States</a> period this kind of <i>fu</i> was changed from jade to bronze.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Needham_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Needham-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The tiger tally from the tomb of King Zhao Mo 趙眜 (<a href="/wiki/Nanyue" title="Nanyue">Nanyue</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Han" title="Western Han">Western Han</a>), was cast as a single piece. It demonstrates that <i>hu-fu</i> were not uniform and might function not as tallies, but rather as tokens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The image penetrated popular culture: in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Qin_(animated_TV_series)" title="The Legend of Qin (animated TV series)">The Legend of Qin (animated TV series)</a> animated series tiger tallies are figuratively called "the spirit of Chu troops" (Season 4, episode 16).</p>
