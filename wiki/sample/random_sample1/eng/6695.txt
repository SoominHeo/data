<p><b>Denial</b>, in ordinary English usage, is asserting that a statement or allegation is not true.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OED_denial_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-OED_denial-1">[1]</a></sup> The same word, and also <b>abnegation</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Verneinung</i></span>), is used for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychological" title="Psychological">psychological</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defense_mechanism" title="Defense mechanism">defense mechanism</a> postulated by <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalysis" title="Psychoanalysis">psychoanalyst</a> <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">Sigmund Freud</a>, in which a person is faced with a fact that is too uncomfortable to accept and rejects it instead, insisting that it is not true despite what may be overwhelming <a href="/wiki/Evidence" title="Evidence">evidence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OED_denial_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-OED_denial-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> An individual that exhibits such behaviour is described as a denialist<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/True-believer_syndrome" title="True-believer syndrome">true believer</a>. Denial also could mean denying the happening of an event or the reliability of information, which can lead to a feeling of aloofness and to the ignoring of possibly beneficial information.</p>
<p>The subject may use:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>simple denial</b>: deny the <a href="/wiki/Reality" title="Reality">reality</a> of the unpleasant fact altogether</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Minimisation_(psychology)" title="Minimisation (psychology)">minimisation</a></b>: admit the fact but deny its seriousness (a combination of denial and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rationalization_(making_excuses)" title="Rationalization (making excuses)">rationalization</a>)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Psychological_projection" title="Psychological projection">projection</a></b>: admit both the fact and seriousness but deny <a href="/wiki/Moral_responsibility" title="Moral responsibility">responsibility</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blaming" title="Blaming">blaming</a> somebody or something else.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_psychoanalysis">In psychoanalysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In psychoanalysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of denial is particularly important to the study of <a href="/wiki/Substance_dependence" title="Substance dependence">addiction</a>.</p>
<p>The theory of denial was first researched seriously by <a href="/wiki/Anna_Freud" title="Anna Freud">Anna Freud</a>. She classified denial as a mechanism of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=(psychological)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="(psychological) (page does not exist)">immature</a> mind, because it conflicts with the ability to learn from and cope with reality. Where denial occurs in mature minds, it is most often associated with <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">death</a>, dying and <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">rape</a>. More recent research has significantly expanded the scope and utility of the concept. <a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_K%C3%BCbler-Ross" title="Elisabeth Kübler-Ross">Elisabeth Kübler-Ross</a> used denial as the first of <a href="/wiki/K%C3%BCbler-Ross_model" title="Kübler-Ross model">five stages in the psychology of a dying patient</a>, and the idea has been extended to include the reactions of survivors to news of a death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bonanno2009_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bonanno2009-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many contemporary psychoanalysts treat denial as the first stage of a <a href="/wiki/Coping_(psychology)" title="Coping (psychology)">coping</a> cycle. When an unwelcome change occurs, a trauma of some sort, the first impulse to disbelieve begins the process of coping. That denial, in a healthy mind, slowly rises to greater consciousness. Gradually becoming a subconscious pressure, just beneath the surface of overt awareness, the mechanism of coping then involves repression, while the person accumulates the emotional resources to fully face the trauma. Once faced, the person deals with the trauma in a stage alternately called acceptance or enlightenment, depending on the scope of the issue and the therapist's school of thought. After this stage, once sufficiently dealt with, or dealt with for the time being, the trauma must sink away from total conscious awareness again. Left metaphorically upon a back burner or put away in a cupboard, the process of <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(psychology)" title="Sublimation (psychology)">sublimation</a> involves a balance of neither quite forgetting nor quite remembering. This allows the trauma to re-emerge in consciousness if it involves an ongoing process such as a protracted illness. Or sublimation may begin the full resolution process, where the trauma finally sinks away into eventual forgetfulness. Occasionally this entire cycle has been referred to in modern parlance as denial, often confusing the full cycle with only one stage in the cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid6853047_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid6853047-6">[6]</a></sup> As well, to further muddy discourse, the term denial or cycle of denial sometimes gets used to refer to an unhealthy, dysfunctional cycle of unresolved coping, particularly with regard to addiction and compulsion.</p>
<p>Unlike some other defense mechanisms postulated by <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalysis" title="Psychoanalysis">psychoanalytic theory</a> (for instance, <a href="/wiki/Psychological_repression" title="Psychological repression">repression</a>), the general existence of denial is fairly easy to verify, even for non-specialists. On the other hand, denial is one of the most controversial defense mechanisms, since it can be easily used to create <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unfalsifiable" title="Unfalsifiable">unfalsifiable</a> theories: anything the subject says or does that appears to disprove the interpreter's theory is explained, not as evidence that the interpreter's theory is wrong, but as the subject's being "in denial". However, researchers note that in some cases of corroborated <a href="/wiki/Child_sexual_abuse" title="Child sexual abuse">child sexual abuse</a>, the victims sometimes make a series of partial confessions and recantations as they struggle with their own denial and the denial of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abusers" title="Abusers">abusers</a> or family members. Use of denial theory in a legal setting therefore must be carefully regulated and experts' credentials verified. "Formulaic <a href="/wiki/Guilt_(law)" title="Guilt (law)">guilt</a>" simply by "being a denier" has been castigated by English judges and academics. The main objection is that denial theory is founded on the premise that that which the supposed denier is denying is truth. This usurps the judge (and/or jury) as triers of fact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is important to note what makes denial denial and not just refusal to admit to or accept a truth or fact rests in the degree of individual's awareness of the existence of the truth or fact. In denial, an individual does not see or is mostly unconscious of existence of the truth or fact. The choice to refuse reality, then, is unconscious as well. Refusal to admit to or accept a truth or fact differs from denial in that the individual recognizes or is conscious of the existence of the truth or fact but consciously refuses to accept it as such.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Denial_and_disavowal">Denial and disavowal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Denial and disavowal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Freud employs the term <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Verleugnung</span></i> (usually translated either as "<b>disavowal</b>" or as "denial") as distinct from <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Verneinung</span></i> (usually translated as "denial" or as "abnegation"). In <i>Verleugnung</i>, the defense consists in denying something that affects the individual and is a way of affirming what he or she is apparently denying. For Freud, <i>Verleugnung</i> is related to <a href="/wiki/Psychosis" title="Psychosis">psychoses</a>, whereas <i><a href="/wiki/Psychological_repression" title="Psychological repression">Verdrängung</a></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Neurosis" title="Neurosis">neurotic</a> defense mechanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Freud broadened his clinical work on disavowal beyond the realm of psychosis. In "Fetishism" (1927), he reported a case of two young men each of whom denied the death of his father. Freud notes that neither of them developed a psychosis, even though "a piece of reality which was undoubtedly important has been disavowed [<i>verleugnet</i>], just as the unwelcome fact of women's castration is disavowed in <a href="/wiki/Sexual_fetishism" title="Sexual fetishism">fetishists</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of denial is important in <a href="/wiki/Twelve-step_program" title="Twelve-step program">twelve-step programs</a>, where the abandonment or reversal of denial forms the basis of the first, fourth, fifth, eighth and tenth steps. The ability to deny or minimize is an essential part of what enables an addict to continue his or her behavior despite evidence that—to an outsider—appears overwhelming. This is cited as one of the reasons that compulsion is seldom effective in treating addiction—the habit of denial remains.</p>
<p>Understanding and avoiding denial is also important in the treatment of various diseases. The <a href="/wiki/American_Heart_Association" title="American Heart Association">American Heart Association</a> cites denial as a principal reason that treatment of a <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">heart attack</a> is delayed. Because the symptoms are so varied, and often have other potential explanations, the opportunity exists for the patient to deny the emergency, often with fatal consequences. It is common for patients to delay mammograms or other tests because of a fear of cancer, even though this is clearly maladaptive. It is the responsibility of the care team, and of the nursing staff in particular, to train at-risk patients to avoid this behavior.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Denial_of_fact">Denial of fact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Denial of fact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In this form of denial, someone avoids a fact by utilizing deception. This lying can take the form of an outright falsehood (commission), leaving out certain details to tailor a story (omission), or by falsely agreeing to something (assent, also referred to as "yessing" behavior). Someone who is in denial of fact is typically using lies to avoid facts they think may be painful to themselves or others.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Denial_of_responsibility">Denial of responsibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Denial of responsibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This form of denial involves avoiding personal responsibility by:</p>
<ul>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blaming" title="Blaming">blaming</a></b>: a direct statement shifting culpability and may overlap with denial of fact</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Minimisation_(psychology)" title="Minimisation (psychology)">minimizing</a></b>: an attempt to make the effects or results of an action appear to be less harmful than they may actually be, or</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rationalization_(making_excuses)" title="Rationalization (making excuses)">justifying</a></b>: when someone takes a choice and attempts to make that choice look okay due to their perception of what is "right" in a situation.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Regression_(psychology)" title="Regression (psychology)">regression</a></b>: when someone acts in a way unbecoming of their age (e.g. whining, temper tantrum, etc.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SirriFava2013_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-SirriFava2013-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Someone using denial of responsibility is usually attempting to avoid potential harm or pain by shifting <a href="/wiki/Attention" title="Attention">attention</a> away from themselves.</p>
<p>For example:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Troy breaks up with his girlfriend because he is unable to control his anger, and then blames her for everything that ever happened.</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Denial_of_impact">Denial of impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Denial of impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Denial of impact involves a person's avoiding thinking about or understanding the harms of his or her behavior has caused to self or others, i.e. denial of the consequences. Doing this enables that person to avoid feeling a sense of <a href="/wiki/Guilt_(emotion)" title="Guilt (emotion)">guilt</a> and it can prevent him or her from developing remorse or empathy for others. Denial of impact reduces or eliminates a sense of pain or harm from poor decisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SirriFava2013_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-SirriFava2013-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Denial_of_cycle">Denial of cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Denial of cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many who use this type of denial will say things such as, "<a href="/wiki/Just-world_hypothesis" title="Just-world hypothesis">it just happened</a>". Denial of cycle is where a person avoids looking at their decisions leading up to an event or does not consider their pattern of decision making and how harmful behavior is repeated. The pain and harm being avoided by this type of denial is more of the effort needed to change the focus from a singular event to looking at preceding events. It can also serve as a way to blame or justify behavior (see above).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Denial_of_awareness">Denial of awareness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Denial of awareness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This form of denial attempts to divert pain by claiming that the level of <a href="/wiki/Awareness" title="Awareness">awareness</a> was inhibited by some mitigating variable. This is most typically seen in <a href="/wiki/Addiction" title="Addiction">addiction</a> situations where drug or alcohol abuse is a factor, though it also occasionally manifests itself in relation to <a href="/wiki/Mental_health" title="Mental health">mental health</a> issues or the pharmaceutical substances used to treat mental health issues. This form of denial may also overlap with denial of responsibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid11494588_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid11494588-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Denial_of_denial">Denial of denial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Denial of denial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This can be a difficult concept for many people to identify with in themselves, but is a major barrier to changing hurtful behaviors. Denial of denial involves thoughts, actions and behaviors which bolster confidence that nothing needs to be changed in one's personal behavior. This form of denial typically overlaps with all of the other forms of denial, but involves more self-delusion. Denial at this level can have significant consequences both personally and at a societal level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rayson2013_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rayson2013-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DARVO">DARVO</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: DARVO">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Harassment" title="Harassment">Harassment</a> covers a wide range of offensive behaviour. It is commonly understood as behaviour intended to disturb or upset. In the legal sense, it is behaviour which <i>is</i> found threatening or disturbing.</p>
<p><b>DARVO</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a> to describe a common strategy of abusers: <b>D</b>eny the abuse, then <b>A</b>ttack the victim for attempting to make them accountable for their offense, thereby <b>R</b>eversing <b>V</b>ictim and <b>O</b>ffender. This may involve <a href="/wiki/Gaslighting" title="Gaslighting">gaslighting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Victim_blaming" title="Victim blaming">victim blaming</a>.</p>
<p>Psychologist <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Freyd" title="Jennifer Freyd">Jennifer Freyd</a> writes:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>...I have observed that actual abusers threaten, bully and make a nightmare for anyone who holds them accountable or asks them to change their abusive behavior. This attack, intended to chill and terrify, typically includes threats of law suits, overt and covert attacks on the whistle-blower's credibility, and so on. The attack will often take the form of focusing on ridiculing the person who attempts to hold the offender accountable. [...] [T]he offender rapidly creates the impression that the abuser is the wronged one, while the victim or concerned observer is the offender. Figure and ground are completely reversed. [...] The offender is on the offense and the person attempting to hold the offender accountable is put on the defense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
