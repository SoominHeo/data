<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Nonwoven fabric</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Nonwoven fabric</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fabric" title="Fabric">fabric</a>-like material made from long fibers, bonded together by chemical, mechanical, heat or solvent treatment. The term is used in the <a href="/wiki/Textile_industry" title="Textile industry">textile manufacturing industry</a> to denote fabrics, such as <a href="/wiki/Felt" title="Felt">felt</a>, which are neither <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">woven</a> nor <a href="/wiki/Knitting" title="Knitting">knitted</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-geo-1">[1]</a></sup> Some nonwoven materials lack sufficient strength unless densified or reinforced by a backing. In recent years, nonwovens have become an alternative to <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">polyurethane</a> foam.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonwoven_fabric&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nonwoven fabrics are broadly defined as sheet or web structures bonded together by entangling fiber or filaments (and by perforating films) mechanically, thermally or chemically. They are flat or tufted porous sheets that are made directly from separate fibers, molten plastic or plastic film. They are not made by weaving or knitting and do not require converting the fibers to yarn. Typically, a certain percentage of recycled fabrics and oil-based materials are used in nonwoven fabrics. The percentage of recycled fabrics vary based upon the strength of material needed for the specific use. In addition, some nonwoven fabrics can be recycled after use, given the proper treatment and facilities. For this reason, some consider nonwovens a more ecological fabric for certain applications, especially in fields and industries where disposable or single use products are important, such as hospitals, schools, nursing homes and luxury accommodations.</p>
<p>Nonwoven fabrics are engineered fabrics that may have a limited life, single-use fabric or a very durable fabric. Nonwoven fabrics provide specific functions such as absorbency, liquid repellence, resilience, stretch, softness, strength, flame retardancy, washability, cushioning, thermal insulation, acoustic insulation, filtration, use as a bacterial barrier and sterility. These properties are often combined to create fabrics suited for specific jobs, while achieving a good balance between product use-life and cost. They can mimic the appearance, texture and strength of a woven fabric and can be as bulky as the thickest paddings. In combination with other materials they provide a spectrum of products with diverse properties, and are used alone or as components of apparel, home furnishings, health care, engineering, industrial and consumer goods.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing_processes">Manufacturing processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonwoven_fabric&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Manufacturing processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nonwovens are typically manufactured by putting small fibers together in the form of a sheet or web (similar to <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> on a <a href="/wiki/Paper_machine" title="Paper machine">paper machine</a>), and then binding them either mechanically (as in the case of felt, by interlocking them with serrated needles such that the inter-fiber friction results in a stronger fabric), with an <a href="/wiki/Adhesive" title="Adhesive">adhesive</a>, or thermally (by applying <a href="/wiki/Binder_(material)" title="Binder (material)">binder</a> (in the form of powder, paste, or <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a> melt) and melting the binder onto the web by increasing temperature).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Staple_nonwovens">Staple nonwovens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonwoven_fabric&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Staple nonwovens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Staple nonwovens are made in 4 steps. Fibers are first spun, cut to a few centimeters length, and put into <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bale#Etymology_3" title="wikt:bale">bales</a>. The staple fibers are then blended, "opened" in a multistep process, dispersed on a <a href="/wiki/Conveyor_belt" title="Conveyor belt">conveyor belt</a>, and spread in a uniform <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Web_handling" title="Web handling">web</a> by a wetlaid, airlaid, or carding/crosslapping process. Wetlaid operations typically use 0.25 to 0.75 in (0.64 to 1.91 cm) long fibers, but sometimes longer if the fiber is stiff or thick. Airlaid processing generally uses 0.5 to 4.0 in (1.3 to 10.2 cm) fibers. Carding operations typically use ~1.5" long fibers. <a href="/wiki/Rayon" title="Rayon">Rayon</a> used to be a common fiber in nonwovens, now greatly replaced by <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate" title="Polyethylene terephthalate">polyethylene terephthalate</a> (PET) and <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">polypropylene</a>. Fiberglass is wetlaid into mats for use in roofing and shingles. Synthetic fiber blends are wetlaid along with <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> for single-use fabrics. Staple nonwovens are bonded either thermally or by using resin. Bonding can be throughout the web by resin saturation or overall thermal bonding or in a distinct pattern via resin printing or thermal spot bonding. Conforming with staple fibers usually refers to a combination with melt blowing, often used in high-end textile insulations.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Melt-blown">Melt-blown</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonwoven_fabric&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Melt-blown">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Melt_blowing" title="Melt blowing">Melt-blown</a> nonwovens are produced by extruding melted polymer fibers through a spin net or die consisting of up to 40 holes per inch to form long thin fibers which are stretched and cooled by passing hot air over the fibers as they fall from the die.The resultant web is collected into rolls and subsequently converted to finished products. The extremely fine fibers (typically polypropylene) differ from other extrusions, particularly spun bond, in that they have low intrinsic strength but much smaller size offering key properties. Often melt blown is added to spun bond to form SM or SMS webs, which are strong and offer the intrinsic benefits of fine fibers such as fine filtration, low pressure drop as used in face masks or filters and physical benefits such as acoustic insulation as used in dishwashers. One of the largest users of SM and SMS materials is the disposable diaper and feminine care industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spunlaid_nonwovens">Spunlaid nonwovens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonwoven_fabric&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Spunlaid nonwovens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hydroentanglement" title="Hydroentanglement">Spunlaid</a>, also called spunbond, nonwovens are made in one continuous process. Fibers are spun and then directly dispersed into a web by deflectors or can be directed with air streams. This technique leads to faster belt speeds, and cheaper costs. Several variants of this concept are available, such as the REICOFIL machinery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> PP spunbonds run faster and at lower temperatures than PET spunbonds, mostly due to the difference in melting points</p>
<p>Spunbond has been combined with melt-blown nonwovens, conforming them into a layered product called SMS (spun-melt-spun). Melt-blown nonwovens have extremely fine fiber diameters but are not strong fabrics. SMS fabrics, made completely from PP are water-repellent and fine enough to serve as disposable fabrics. Melt-blown is often used as filter media, being able to capture very fine particles. Spunlaid is bonded by either resin or thermally. Regarding the bonding of Spunlaid, Rieter <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> has launched a new generation of nonwovens called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Spunjet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Spunjet (page does not exist)">Spunjet</a>. In fact, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Spunjet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Spunjet (page does not exist)">Spunjet</a> is the bonding of the Spunlaid filaments thanks to the <a href="/wiki/Hydroentanglement" title="Hydroentanglement">hydroentanglement</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flashspun">Flashspun</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonwoven_fabric&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Flashspun">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flashspun" title="Flashspun">Flashspun</a></div>
<p>Flashspun fabrics are created by spraying a dissolved resin into a chamber, where the solvent evaporates.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air-laid_paper">Air-laid paper</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonwoven_fabric&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Air-laid paper">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Air-laid_paper" title="Air-laid paper">air-laid paper</a></div>
<p>Air-laid <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> is a textile-like material categorized as a nonwoven fabric made from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wood_pulp" title="Wood pulp">wood pulp</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBG_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-PBG-7">[7]</a></sup> Unlike the normal <a href="/wiki/Paper_machine" title="Paper machine">papermaking process</a>, air-laid paper does not use water as the carrying medium for the fiber. Fibers are carried and formed to the structure of paper by air.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_2">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonwoven_fabric&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nonwovens can also start with films and fibrillate, serrate or vacuum-form them with patterned holes. Fiberglass nonwovens are of two basic types. Wet laid mat or "glass tissue" use wet-chopped, heavy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denier_(unit)" title="Denier (unit)">denier</a> fibers in the 6 to 20 micrometre diameter range. Flame attenuated mats or "batts" use discontinuous fine denier fibers in the 0.1 to 6 range. The latter is similar, though run at much higher temperatures, to melt-blown thermoplastic nonwovens. Wet laid mat is almost always wet resin bonded with a curtain coater, while batts are usually spray bonded with wet or dry resin. An unusual process produces polyethylene fibrils in a <a href="/wiki/Freon" title="Freon">Freon</a>-like fluid, forming them into a paper-like product and then calendering them to create <a href="/wiki/Tyvek" title="Tyvek">Tyvek</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bonding">Bonding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonwoven_fabric&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Bonding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Both staple and spunlaid nonwovens would have no mechanical resistance in and of themselves, without the bonding step. Several methods can be used:</p>
<ul>
<li>thermal bonding
<ul>
<li>Use of a <a href="/wiki/Heat_sealer" title="Heat sealer">heat sealer</a></li>
<li>using a large oven for <a href="/wiki/Curing_(chemistry)" title="Curing (chemistry)">curing</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Calendering" title="Calendering">calendering</a> through heated rollers (called spunbond when combined with spunlaid webs), calenders can be smooth faced for an overall bond or patterned for a softer, more tear resistant bond</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>hydro-entanglement: mechanical intertwining of fibers by water jets (called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Spunlace&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Spunlace (page does not exist)">spunlace</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>ultrasonic pattern bonding: used in high-loft or fabric insulation/quilts/bedding</li>
<li>needlepunching/needlefelting: mechanical intertwining of fibers by needles</li>
<li>chemical bonding (wetlaid process): use of binders (such as latex emulsion or solution polymers) to chemically join the fibers. A more expensive route uses binder fibers or powders that soften and melt to hold other non-melting fibers together
<ul>
<li>one type of cotton staple nonwoven is treated with sodium hydroxide to shrink bond the mat, the caustic causes the cellulose-based fibers to curl and shrink around one another as the bonding technique</li>
<li>one unusual polyamide(Cerex) is self-bonded with gas-phase acid</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>melt-blown: fiber is bonded as air attenuated fibers intertangle with themselves during simultaneous fiber and web formation.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disposability">Disposability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonwoven_fabric&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Disposability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The industry has attempted to define "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Flushability&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Flushability (page does not exist)">flushability</a>". They encourage voluntary testing of flushability by producers. They also encourage clear marking of non-flushable products as "No Flush" (rather than fine print on the bottom of products) including creating a "No Flush" logo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater industry</a> is encouraging a standard definition (rather than one which varies with each producer) of flushability, including <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dispersibility&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dispersibility (page does not exist)">dispersibility</a>, and third-party assessment or verification, such as by <a href="/wiki/NSF_International" title="NSF International">NSF International</a>. They believe that products should be both safe for both septic <i>and</i> sewer systems (flushable and dispersible, respectively).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Orange_County_Sanitation_District" title="Orange County Sanitation District">Orange County Sanitation District</a> has created a campaign, "What 2 Flush", which recommends flushing only the "three P’s—pee, poop and [toilet] paper".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
