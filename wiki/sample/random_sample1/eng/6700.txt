<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Hand fan</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><br/>
A <b>handheld fan</b> is an implement used to induce an airflow for the purpose of cooling or refreshing oneself. Any broad, flat surface waved back-and-forth will create a small airflow and therefore can be considered a rudimentary fan. Generally, purpose-made handheld fans are shaped like a <a href="/wiki/Circular_sector" title="Circular sector">sector</a> of a circle and made of a thin material (such as paper or feathers) mounted on slats which revolve around a pivot so that it can be closed when not in use.</p>
<p>The movement of a handheld fan provides cooling by increasing the airflow over the skin, which in turn increases the <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> rate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweating" title="Sweating">sweat</a> droplets on the skin, and increasing heat <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> by displacing the warmer air, produced by body heat, that surrounds the skin. This evaporation has a cooling effect due to the <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a> of evaporation of water. Fans are convenient to carry around, especially folding fans.</p>
<p>Next to the folding fans, the rigid hand screen fan, was also a highly decorative and desired object among the higher classes. Its purpose is different since they are more cumbersome to carry around. They were mostly used to shield the lady's face against the glare of the sun or the fire.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Hand_Fans_in_the_Eighteenth_Century" title="European Hand Fans in the Eighteenth Century">European Hand Fans in the Eighteenth Century</a></div>
<p>Archaeological ruins and ancient texts show that the hand fan was used in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a> at least since the 4th century BC and was known under the name <i>rhipis</i> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: ῥιπίς).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Christian Europe's earliest fan was the <a href="/wiki/Flabellum" title="Flabellum">flabellum</a> (or ceremonial fan), which dates to the 6th century. This was used during services to drive insects away from the consecrated bread and wine. Its use died out in western Europe, but continues in the Eastern Orthodox and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Church" title="Ethiopian Church">Ethiopian Churches</a>. Hand fans were absent in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> during the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High Middle Ages</a> until they were reintroduced in the 13th and 14th centuries Fans from the Middle East were brought back by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Crusades" title="The Crusades">Crusaders</a>. Portuguese traders brought them back from China and Japan in the 16th century, and fans became generally popular. The fan is especially popular in Spain, where flamenco dancers used the fan and extended its use to the nobility. European brands have introduced more modern designs and have enabled the hand fan to work with modern-day fashion.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="17th_century">17th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hand_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 17th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 17th century the folding fan, and its attendant semiotic culture, were introduced from Japan. Simpler formed fans were developed in China, Greek, and Egypt, Japanese fans (and Chinese imports) became popular in Europe. These fans are particularly well displayed in the portraits of the high-born women of the era. Queen Elizabeth I of England can be seen to carry both folding fans decorated with pom poms on their guardsticks as well as the older style rigid fan, usually decorated with feathers and jewels. These rigid style fans often hung from the skirts of ladies, but of the fans of this era it is only the more exotic folding ones which have survived. Those folding fans of the 15th century found in museums today have either leather leaves with cut out designs forming a lace-like design or a more rigid leaf with inlays of more exotic materials like mica. One of the characteristics of these fans is the rather crude bone or ivory sticks and the way the leather leaves are often slotted onto the sticks rather than glued as with later folding fans. Fans made entirely of decorated sticks without a fan 'leaf' were known as brisé fans. However, despite the relative crude methods of construction folding fans were at this era high status, exotic items on par with elaborate gloves as gifts to royalty.</p>
<p>In the 17th century the rigid fan which was seen in portraits of the previous century had fallen out of favour as folding fans gained dominance in Europe. Fans started to display well painted leaves, often with a religious or classical subject. The reverse side of these early fans also started to display elaborate flower designs. The sticks are often plain ivory or tortoiseshell, sometimes inlaid with gold or silver pique work. The way the sticks sit close to each other, often with little or no space between them is one of the distinguishing characteristics of fans of this era.</p>
<p>In 1685 the <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Nantes" title="Edict of Nantes">Edict of Nantes</a> was revoked in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. This caused large scale immigration from France to the surrounding Protestant countries (such as England) of many fan craftsman. This dispersion in skill is reflected in the growing quality of many fans from these non-French countries after this date.</p>
<p>In the 18th century, fans reached a high degree of artistry and were being made throughout Europe often by specialized craftsmen, either in leaves or sticks. Folded fans of <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Parchment" title="Parchment">parchment</a> were decorated and painted by artists. Fans were also imported from China by the East India Companies at this time. Around the middle 18th century, inventors started designing mechanical fans. Wind-up fans (similar to wind-up clocks) were popular in the 18th century. In the 19th century in the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">West</a>, European fashion caused fan decoration and size to vary.</p>
<p>It has been said that in the courts of England, Spain and elsewhere fans were used in a more or less secret, unspoken code of messages<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> These fan languages were a way to cope with the restricting social etiquette. However, modern research has proved that this was a marketing ploy developed in the 18th century (FANA Journal, spring 2004, Fact &amp; Fiction about the language of the fan by J.P. Ryan) - one that has kept its appeal remarkably over the succeeding centuries. This is now used for marketing by fan makers like <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cussons_%26_Sons_%26_Co._Ltd&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cussons &amp; Sons &amp; Co. Ltd (page does not exist)">Cussons &amp; Sons &amp; Co. Ltd</a> who produced a series of advertisements in 1954 showing "the language of the fan" with fans supplied by the well known French fan maker <a href="/wiki/Duvelleroy" title="Duvelleroy">Duvelleroy</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The rigid or screen fan (éventail a écran) became also fashionable during the 18th and 19th century. They never reached the same level of popularity as the easy to carry around, folding fans which became almost an integrated part of women's dress. The screen fan was mainly used inside the interior of the house. In 18th and 19th century paintings of interiors one sometimes sees one laying on a chimney mantle. They were mainly used to protect a woman's face against the glare and heat of the fire, to avoid getting 'coup rose' or ruddy cheeks from the heat. But probably not in the least it served to keep the heat from spoiling the carefully applied make-up which in those days was often wax-based. For a better understanding we have to realise that until the mid 20th century houses were heated by open fires in chimneys or by stoves, and the lack of insolation made many a house very draughty and cold during winter. Therefore, any social or family gathering would be in close proximity to the fireplace.</p>
<p>The design of the screen fan is a fixed handle, most often made out of exquisitely turned (painted or guided) wood, fixed to a flat screen. The screen could be made out of silk stretched on a frame or thin wood, leather or papier mache. The surface is often exquisitely painted with scenes ranging from flowers and birds of paradise to religious scenes. at the end of the 19th century they disappeared when the need for them ceased t exist During the 19th century names like the Birmingham-based firm of Jennens and Bettridge produced many papier-mâché fans.</p>

<p>The oldest existing Chinese fans are a pair of woven <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a> side-mounted fans from the 2nd century BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_character" title="Chinese character">Chinese character</a> for "fan" (扇) is etymologically derived from a picture of feathers under a roof. The Chinese fixed fan, <i>pien-mien</i>, means 'to agitate the air'. A particular status and gender would be associated with a specific type of fan. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" title="Song Dynasty">Song Dynasty</a>, famous artists were often commissioned to paint fans. The Chinese dancing fan was developed in the 7th century. The Chinese form of the hand fan was a row of feathers mounted in the end of a handle. In the later centuries, Chinese poems and four-word idioms were used to decorate the fans by using Chinese calligraphy pens. In ancient China, fans came in various shapes and forms (such as in a leaf, oval or a half-moon shape), and were made in different materials such as silk, bamboo, feathers, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese_foldable_fan">Japanese foldable fan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hand_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Japanese foldable fan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In ancient Japan, hand fans, such as oval and silk fans were influenced greatly by the Chinese fans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The earliest visual depiction of fans in Japan dates back to the 6th century CE, with burial tomb paintings showed drawings of fans. The folding fan was invented in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, with date ranging from the 6th to 9th centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halsey_.26_Friedman_1983_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Halsey_.26_Friedman_1983-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipinski_1999_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lipinski_1999-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qian_2000.2C_p.12_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Qian_2000.2C_p.12-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Verschuer_2006_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Verschuer_2006-9">[9]</a></sup> It was a court fan called the <i>Akomeogi</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">衵扇</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Akomeōgi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> after the court women's dress named <i>Akome</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halsey_.26_Friedman_1983_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Halsey_.26_Friedman_1983-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> According to the <i>Song Sui</i> (History of Song), a Japanese monk <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E3%81%A1%E3%82%87%E3%81%86%E7%84%B6" title="ja:ちょう然">Chonen</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">奝然</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Chōnen</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, 938－1016)</span> offered the folding fans (twenty wooden-bladed fans <i>hiogi</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">桧扇</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>hiōgi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> and two paper fans <i>kawahori-ogi</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">蝙蝠扇</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>kawahori-ōgi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>) to the emperor of China in 988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qian_2000.2C_p.12_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Qian_2000.2C_p.12-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Verschuer_2006_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Verschuer_2006-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Julia_.26_Alexander_1992_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Julia_.26_Alexander_1992-11">[11]</a></sup> Later in the 11th century, Korean envoys brought along Korean folding fans which were of Japanese origin as gifts to Chinese court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tsang_2002.2C_p.10_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tsang_2002.2C_p.10-12">[12]</a></sup> The popularity of folding fans was such that sumptuary laws were promulgated during <a href="/wiki/Heian_period" title="Heian period">Heian period</a> which restricted the decoration of both hiogi and paper folding fans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Julia_.26_Alexander_1992_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Julia_.26_Alexander_1992-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Medley_1976_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Medley_1976-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>They were made by tying thin stripes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hinoki_Cypress" title="Hinoki Cypress">hinoki</a> (or Japanese cypress) together with thread. The number of strips of wood differed according to the person's rank. Later in the 16th century, Portuguese traders introduced it to the west and soon both men and women throughout the continent adopted it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipinski_1999_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lipinski_1999-7">[7]</a></sup> They are used today by <a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Shinto</a> priests in formal costume and in the formal costume of the Japanese court (they can be seen used by the Emperor and Empress during <a href="/wiki/Enthronement_of_the_Japanese_Emperor" title="Enthronement of the Japanese Emperor">enthronement</a> and marriage) and are brightly painted with long tassels. Simple Japanese paper fans are sometimes known as <a href="/wiki/Harisen" title="Harisen">harisen</a>.</p>
<p>Printed fan leaves and painted fans are done on a paper ground. The paper was originally handmade and displayed the characteristic watermarks. Machine-made paper fans, introduced in the 19th century, are smoother, with an even texture. Even today, <a href="/wiki/Geisha" title="Geisha">Geisha</a> of all types (but maiko most often) use folding fans in their fan dances as well.</p>
<p>Japanese fans are made of paper on a bamboo frame, usually with a design painted on them. In addition to folding fans (<i>ōgi</i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> the non-bending fans (<i>uchiwa</i>) are popular and commonplace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The fan is primarily used for fanning oneself in hot weather. The <i>uchiwa</i> fan subsequently spread to other parts of Asia including Myanmar, Thailand, Cambodia and Sri Lanka and to this day are still used by Buddhist monks as "ceremonial fans".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was also used in the military as a way of sending signals on the field of battle, however fans were mainly used for social and court activities. In Japan, fans were variously used by warriors as a form of weapon, by actors and dancers for performances, and by children as a toy.</p>
<p>Traditionally, the rigid fan (also called fixed fan) was the most popular form in China,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> although the folding fan came into popularity during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a> between the years of 1368 and 1644, and there are many beautiful examples of these folding fans still remaining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Mai Ogi</i> (or Japanese dancing fan) has ten sticks and a thick paper mount showing the family crest, and Japanese painters made a large variety of designs and patterns. The slats, of <a href="/wiki/Ivory" title="Ivory">ivory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mica" title="Mica">mica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pearl" title="Pearl">mother of pearl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sandalwood" title="Sandalwood">sandalwood</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tortoiseshell_material" title="Tortoiseshell material">tortoise shell</a>, were carved and covered with <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> or <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">fabric</a>. Folding fans have "montures" which are the sticks and guards, and the leaves were usually painted by craftsmen. Social significance was attached to the fan in the Far East as well, and the management of the fan became a highly regarded feminine art. Fans were even used as a weapon - called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_fan" title="Iron fan">iron fan</a>, or <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tessen" title="Tessen">tessen</a></i> in Japanese.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Categories">Categories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hand_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Categories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hand fans have three general categories:</p>
<ol>
<li>Fixed (or rigid, flat) fans (Chinese: <span lang="zh-cn">平扇</span>, <i>píng shàn</i>; Japanese: <span lang="ja">団扇</span>, <i>uchiwa</i>, cannot be folded): circular fans, palm-leaf fans, straw fans, feather fans, etc.</li>
<li>Folding fans (Chinese: <span lang="zh-cn">折扇</span>, <i>zhé shàn</i>; Japanese: <span lang="ja">扇子</span>, <i>sensu</i>, can be freely opened): silk folding fans, paper folding fans, sandalwood fans, etc.</li>
<li>Modern powered mechanical hand fans: these are hand fans which, unlike most, appear as mini mechanical rotating fans with blades. These are usually axial fans, and often use blades made from a soft material for safety. These are usually battery operated, but can be hand cranked as well.</li>
</ol>
