<p><b>Ogcocephalidae</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lophiiformes" title="Lophiiformes">anglerfish</a> specifically adapted for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benthic" title="Benthic">benthic</a> lifestyle of crawling about on the seafloor. Ogcocephalid anglerfish are sometimes referred to as <b>batfishes</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fb_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-fb-2">[2]</a></sup> <b>deep-sea batfishes</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <b>handfishes</b>, and <b>seabats</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> They are found in tropical and subtropical oceans worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fb_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-fb-2">[2]</a></sup> They are mostly found at depths between 200 and 3,000 m (660 and 9,840 ft), but have been recorded as deep as 4,000 m (13,000 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fb_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-fb-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoF_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoF-5">[5]</a></sup> A few species live in much shallower coastal waters and exceptionally may enter river estuaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fb_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-fb-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>They are dorsoventrally compressed fishes similar in appearance to <a href="/wiki/Batoidea" title="Batoidea">rays</a>, with a large circular, triangular, or box-shaped (in <i>Coelophrys</i>) head and a small tail. The largest members of the family are about 50 cm (20 in) in <a href="/wiki/Fish_measurement" title="Fish measurement">standard length</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illicium_(fish_anatomy)" title="Illicium (fish anatomy)">illicium</a> (a modified <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_fin" title="Dorsal fin">dorsal fin</a> ray on the front of the head supporting the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esca_(fish_anatomy)" title="Esca (fish anatomy)">esca</a>, a bulbous lure) can be retracted into an illicial cavity above the mouth. The esca is not luminous as in most other groups of anglerfishes, but secretes a fluid thought to act as a chemical lure, attracting prey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Analysis of their stomach contents indicates that batfishes feed on fish, crustaceans, and <a href="/wiki/Polychaete" title="Polychaete">polychaete</a> worms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoF_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-EoF-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
