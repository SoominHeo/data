<p>The <b>69th Regiment Armory</b> is located at 68 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lexington_Avenue_(Manhattan)" title="Lexington Avenue (Manhattan)">Lexington Avenue</a> between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/25th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="25th Street (Manhattan)">East 25th</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/26th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="26th Street (Manhattan)">26th</a> Streets in the <a href="/wiki/Rose_Hill,_Manhattan" title="Rose Hill, Manhattan">Rose Hill</a> section of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. The historic building began construction in 1904 and was completed in 1906.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-armory_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-armory-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nycland_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nycland-4">[4]</a></sup> The building is still used to house the headquarters of the New York Army National Guard's <a href="/wiki/69th_Infantry_Regiment_(New_York)" title="69th Infantry Regiment (New York)">1st Battalion, 69th Infantry Regiment</a>, as well as for the presentation of special events. The armory was designed by the firm of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hunt_%26_Hunt" title="Hunt &amp; Hunt">Hunt &amp; Hunt</a>, and was the first <a href="/wiki/List_of_armories_and_arsenals_in_New_York_City_and_surrounding_counties" title="List of armories and arsenals in New York City and surrounding counties">armory built in New York City</a> to not be modeled on a medieval fortress; instead, it was designed in the <a href="/wiki/Beaux-Arts_architecture" title="Beaux-Arts architecture">Beaux-Arts</a> style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nycland_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-nycland-4">[4]</a></sup> The building was declared a <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark" title="National Historic Landmark">National Historic Landmark</a> in 1965,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhlsum_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-nhlsum-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrhpinv_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nrhpinv-5">[5]</a></sup> and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_New_York_City_Landmarks" title="List of New York City Landmarks">New York City landmark</a> in 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nycland_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-nycland-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Armory was the site of the controversial 1913 <a href="/wiki/Armory_Show" title="Armory Show">Armory Show</a>, in which <a href="/wiki/Modern_art" title="Modern art">modern art</a> was first publicly presented in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nycland_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-nycland-4">[4]</a></sup> It has a 5,000 seat arena that is used for sporting and entertainment events such as the <a href="/wiki/Victoria%27s_Secret_Fashion_Show" title="Victoria's Secret Fashion Show">Victoria's Secret Fashion Show</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_events">Notable events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=69th_Regiment_Armory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Notable events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>In 1913, the Armory Show exhibited art from many contemporary artists such as Vincent Van Gogh, Pablo Picasso, Henri Matisse and more. It was the first large-scale modern art show in the United States. It received mixed reactions from the public and media for its controversial new art forms, such as cubism, fauvism, and post-impressionism. It was a success and eventually moved on to Chicago, and Boston.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Thure_Johansson_(athlete)" title="Thure Johansson (athlete)">Thure Johansson</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> broke <a href="/wiki/Dorando_Pietri" title="Dorando Pietri">Dorando Pietri</a>'s indoor record for the <a href="/wiki/Marathon" title="Marathon">marathon</a> at the 69th Regiment Armory on March 1, 1910 (2:36:55.2).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Times1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Times1-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[nb 1]</a></sup> As of May 2010, the Association of Road Racing Statisticians notes that Johansson's mark still stands as the sixth fastest time on an indoor track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ARRS_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ARRS-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>In late 1948 and early 1949, the Armory hosted at least 17 <a href="/wiki/Roller_derby" title="Roller derby">Roller Derby</a> matches, including the first matches ever broadcast on <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Armory was the site of some <a href="/wiki/New_York_Knicks" title="New York Knicks">New York Knicks</a> home games from 1946 to 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> The New York Americans – now the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Nets" title="Brooklyn Nets">Brooklyn Nets</a> – of the new <a href="/wiki/American_Basketball_Association" title="American Basketball Association">American Basketball Association</a> wanted to play at the Armory in 1967, but pressure from the Knicks on the Armory management forced the new club to play in <a href="/wiki/Teaneck,_New_Jersey" title="Teaneck, New Jersey">Teaneck, New Jersey</a> instead.</li>
<li>In 1996, NBA Entertainment used the Armory to film <a href="/wiki/Denzel_Washington" title="Denzel Washington">Denzel Washington</a>'s portions of the documentary <i>NBA at 50</i>.</li>
<li>After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/September_11,_2001_attacks" title="September 11, 2001 attacks">September 11, 2001 attacks</a>, the Armory served as a counseling center for the victims and families.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2003, 2005, 2009, 2010, 2011, 2012, 2013 and 2015 the Armory was the venue used for the <a href="/wiki/Victoria%27s_Secret" title="Victoria's Secret">Victoria's Secret</a> Fashion Show.</li>
<li>The Armory has been the site of the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Comic_and_Cartoon_Art" title="Museum of Comic and Cartoon Art">Museum of Comic and Cartoon Art</a>'s MoCCA Art Festival since 2009.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Architectural_League_of_New_York" title="Architectural League of New York">Architectural League of New York</a> staged its annual "Beaux Arts Ball" at the Armory in 2013, to mark the centennial of the 1913 "Armory Show". For the event the ALNY commissioned giant illuminated cubist puppets designed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superior_Concept_Monsters" title="Superior Concept Monsters">Processional Art Workshop</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>In May 2014, The Armory hosted the inaugural edition of the Downtown Fair in which work from leading art galleries will be offered for sale.</li>
</ul>
