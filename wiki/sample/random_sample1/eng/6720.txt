<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Northern Group of Forces</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Northern Group of Forces</b> was the military formation of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Army" title="Soviet Army">Soviet Army</a> stationed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_Poland" title="People's Republic of Poland">Poland</a> from the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a> in 1945 until 1993 when they were withdrawn in the aftermath of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fall_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Fall of the Soviet Union">fall of the Soviet Union</a>. Although officially considered Polish allies under the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">Warsaw Pact</a> treaty, they were seen by most Poles as a Soviet occupation force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golon_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Golon-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Group_of_Forces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Group_of_Forces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Soviet forces entered <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> as they were advancing towards <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> in the course of the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Operation_Bagration" title="Operation Bagration">Operation Bagration</a> in the summer of 1944. Following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vistula-Oder_Offensive" title="Vistula-Oder Offensive">Vistula-Oder Offensive</a> in early 1945, all of Poland was cleared from Nazi occupation by Soviet forces. While formal Polish sovereignty was almost immediately restored, the territory of Poland fell under <i>de facto</i> Soviet control as the Soviet military and security forces acted to ensure that Poland would be ruled by the Soviet-installed <a href="/wiki/Polish_Committee_of_National_Liberation" title="Polish Committee of National Liberation">communist puppet government of Poland</a>.</p>
<p>As the war ended, the structure of the Soviet military was reorganized from a war-time to a peace-time mode. Directive No. 11097 of 10 June 1945 created several new formations, known as <i>Groups of Forces</i>, equivalent to <a href="/wiki/Military_district" title="Military district">military districts</a>, but used for command and administration of Soviet forces outside the Soviet Union itself. One of those new formations, at that time 300,000-400,000 strong, was to be stationed in Poland. It was mostly based on the <a href="/wiki/2nd_Belorussian_Front" title="2nd Belorussian Front">2nd Belorussian Front</a> of General <a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Rokossovsky" title="Konstantin Rokossovsky">Konstantin Rokossovsky</a> (formerly stationed around <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg" title="Mecklenburg">Mecklenburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg" title="Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polit_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-polit-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup> With the exception of <a href="/wiki/Szczecin" title="Szczecin">Szczecin</a> (Stettin), which fell under the operational territory of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Group_of_Forces" title="Western Group of Forces">Western Group of Forces</a>, the Northern Group of Forces was located entirely within the territory of Poland.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish_communist" title="Polish communist">Polish communist</a> government, which largely owed its existence to the Soviets (see <a href="/wiki/Polish_Committee_of_National_Liberation" title="Polish Committee of National Liberation">Polish Committee of National Liberation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polish_people%27s_referendum,_1946" title="Polish people's referendum, 1946">Polish people's referendum, 1946</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polish_legislative_election,_1947" title="Polish legislative election, 1947">Polish legislative election, 1947</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golon_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Golon-1">[1]</a></sup> signed several agreements with the Soviet Union regulating the status and purpose of the Soviet troops.</p>
<p>In the early years, the Soviet forces aided the Polish communists in establishing their government and combating anti-communist resistance, such as the Polish <i><a href="/wiki/Cursed_soldiers" title="Cursed soldiers">cursed soldiers</a></i> or the <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_Insurgent_Army" title="Ukrainian Insurgent Army">Ukrainian Insurgent Army</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Operation_Vistula" title="Operation Vistula">Operation Vistula</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golon_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Golon-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup> Another major task of the Northern Group was to organize and transport <a href="/wiki/War_reparations" title="War reparations">war reparations</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Former_eastern_territories_of_Germany" title="Former eastern territories of Germany">former eastern territories of Germany</a> attached to Poland after World War II (the so-called <a href="/wiki/Recovered_Territories" title="Recovered Territories">Recovered Territories</a>) to the Soviet Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golon_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Golon-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pol2_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-pol2-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPNMM_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPNMM-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPN-.C5.9A_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPN-.C5.9A-7">[7]</a></sup> These actions, often involving the complete stripping down of industrial facilities, sometimes also took place in traditionally Polish territories. This caused tensions between the Soviets and the Polish government, which intended to use the resources of those territories to rebuild Poland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golon_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Golon-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pol2_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-pol2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cold_War">Cold War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Group_of_Forces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cold War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By 1949 the Soviet Union had concluded twenty-year bilateral treaties of friendship, cooperation, and mutual assistance with Poland and several other countries, which usually granted the Soviet Union rights to a continued military presence on their territory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-loc-8">[8]</a></sup> The Polish government, however, had no operational control over the Soviet forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup> On December 17, 1956, as one of the agreements of the <a href="/wiki/Polish_October" title="Polish October">Polish October</a> period, the Polish and Soviet governments signed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Status_of_Forces_Agreement" title="Status of Forces Agreement">dedicated treaty</a> that would finally fully regulate the Soviet military presence in Poland, the "Agreement on Legal Status of Soviet Troops Temporarily Stationed in Poland".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jamgotch_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jamgotch-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polit_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-polit-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DBM_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-DBM-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golon_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Golon-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bor_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bor-11">[11]</a></sup> According to the 1956 treaty (which was further developed by later, more specific amendments), the Soviet military in Poland was limited to 66,000 troops, although the Soviets never fully disclosed the actual number of personnel of the Northern Group to the Polish government, and the Polish government had no right to inspect the Soviet bases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golon_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Golon-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup> The treaty also limited the number of Soviet bases in Poland to 39, while the actual number of bases reached 79.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polit_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-polit-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bor_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bor-11">[11]</a></sup> The Soviets also installed nuclear weapons in Poland, without informing the Polish government of that fact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup> The treaty's name declared the Soviet military presence to be temporary, while in fact the treaty did not contain any limitations to the duration of their stay, nor any provisions on their withdrawal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polit_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-polit-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bor_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bor-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Navr.C3.A1til_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Navr.C3.A1til-12">[12]</a></sup> Until the 1956 agreement, the Soviet troops stationed in Poland were seen by some Poles as occupying Polish territory;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golon_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Golon-1">[1]</a></sup> even afterwards they were <i>de facto</i> exempt from any Polish oversight<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polit_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-polit-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pol2_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-pol2-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bor_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bor-11">[11]</a></sup> and their role of an 'allied force' stationed in the territory was viewed by many as a mere <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a> claim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golon_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-Golon-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pol2_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-pol2-5">[5]</a></sup> The issue of Polish-Soviet military cooperation was further regulated the next year, and in the 1965 Polish-Soviet friendship treaty that reflected the Soviet domination of Polish military policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globsecorg_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-globsecorg-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Northern Group of Forces had several objectives. With the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, it was to act, together with other Groups of Forces, as a counterpart to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Allies" title="Western Allies">Western Allies</a> (particularly the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>) forces in Europe. Later, in that regard it represented part of the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">Warsaw Pact</a> forces, countering the <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> troops. Its second objective was much less stressed in public Soviet sources, but nonetheless crucial: it was to ensure the loyalty of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish_communist" title="Polish communist">Polish communist</a> government, and its <a href="/wiki/Polish_People%27s_Army" title="Polish People's Army">Polish People's Army</a>; a policy consistent with that of the <a href="/wiki/Brezhnev_Doctrine" title="Brezhnev Doctrine">Brezhnev Doctrine</a>, and enforced during events such as the <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_Revolution_of_1956" title="Hungarian Revolution of 1956">Hungarian Revolution of 1956</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Prague_Spring" title="Prague Spring">Prague Spring</a> of 1968.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golon_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-Golon-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pol2_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-pol2-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-loc-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globsecorg_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-globsecorg-13">[13]</a></sup> Soviet forces were mobilized and actually advanced on <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a> during <a href="/wiki/Polish_October" title="Polish October">Polish October</a> in 1956, and there were threats that they could be similarly used before the <a href="/wiki/Martial_law_in_Poland" title="Martial law in Poland">martial law in Poland</a> was introduced to stem the progress of the <a href="/wiki/Solidarity_(Polish_trade_union)" title="Solidarity (Polish trade union)">Solidarity</a> movement in 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup> All of the objectives of the Northern Group were shared with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_of_Soviet_Occupation_Forces_in_Germany" title="Group of Soviet Occupation Forces in Germany">Group of Soviet Occupation Forces in Germany</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Democratic_Republic" title="German Democratic Republic">German Democratic Republic</a> and to a lesser extent with the two Groups with a shorter history: the <a href="/wiki/Central_Group_of_Forces" title="Central Group of Forces">Central Group of Forces</a> in <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a> (from 1968 on) and the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Group_of_Forces" title="Southern Group of Forces">Southern Group of Forces</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> and (only until 1958) <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>.</p>
<p>The presence of Soviet forces on Polish territory caused several problems, in addition to the war reparations issue. Although not supported by the Soviet High Command, excesses by individual soldiers of the Red Army led to mounting tensions between Soviet forces and the Polish population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies2_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies2-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raack_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Raack-15">[15]</a></sup> Contemporary archives contained many reports of mugging, burglary, rape and murder attributed to Soviet soldiers in the immediate aftermath of the Second World War;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PadrK_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-PadrK-16">[16]</a></sup> even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish_Communists" title="Polish Communists">Polish Communists</a> were uneasy, as in 1945 the future Chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Polish_Council_of_State" title="Polish Council of State">Polish Council of State</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aleksander_Zawadzki" title="Aleksander Zawadzki">Aleksander Zawadzki</a>, worried that "raping and looting of the Soviet army would provoke a civil war".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-17">[17]</a></sup> In the early years, the Red Army appropriated any resources it needed from the Polish government with no thought of compensation, or treating Poles as their allies. For example, when the Northern Group commander, <a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Rokossovsky" title="Konstantin Rokossovsky">Konstantin Rokossovsky</a>, decided that it should be headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Legnica" title="Legnica">Legnica</a>, he ordered all Poles, including communist officials who were organizing the city and provincial government, to vacate one third of the city within 24 hours, later requisitioning any of their private belongings (like furniture) left behind. This incident was perceived by contemporaries as a particularly brutal action, and rumors circulated exaggerating its severity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Legnica_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Legnica-18">[18]</a></sup> Later, Polish settlements in which Soviet garrisons were placed were inconvenienced in other ways, for example by being removed from all official maps<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bor_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Bor-11">[11]</a></sup> or by the low flying Soviet jets, training at night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup> Approximately 600 Polish citizens died over the years 1945-1993 in crimes or accidents for which the Soviet soldiers were responsible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup> The Soviet army, which was subject to many financial privileges (reduced taxation, import tariffs, etc.),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup> often refused to pay for municipal resources it consumed, particularly water, gas or electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golon_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-Golon-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pol2_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-pol2-5">[5]</a></sup> On the other hand, Soviet units did also aid the locals with infrastructure projects, harvests, or during environmental disasters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fall_of_communism_in_Poland" title="Fall of communism in Poland">fall of communism in Poland</a> in 1989, and with the signs of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fall_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Fall of the Soviet Union">fall of the Soviet Union</a>, the new Polish government wanted the Soviet troops to leave Poland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-12"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup> By that time the Northern Group had already shrunk to 58,000 troops,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globsecorg_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-globsecorg-13">[13]</a></sup> but its military installations were still spread over about 700 square kilometres of Polish territory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bor_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-Bor-11">[11]</a></sup> After a new treaty in late 1991 and May 1992, and Poland's withdrawal from the Warsaw Pact, the Soviet agreed to withdraw military units by 1992 and support units by 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-13"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup> Soviet troops begun leaving Poland, with the first group exiting in 1991. All troops left Poland by the end of 1993, the last leaving on 18 September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polit_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-polit-2">[2]</a></sup> Symbolically, <a href="/wiki/President_of_Poland" title="President of Poland">Polish President</a> <a href="/wiki/Lech_Wa%C5%82%C4%99sa" title="Lech Wałęsa">Lech Wałęsa</a> saw them off on 17 September, the anniversary of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_invasion_of_Poland_(1939)" title="Soviet invasion of Poland (1939)">Soviet invasion of Poland in 1939</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polit_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-polit-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Group_of_Forces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_War">Post War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Group_of_Forces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Group was headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Legnica" title="Legnica">Legnica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lower_Silesia" title="Lower Silesia">Lower Silesia</a>, where Soviet military took over 1/3 of the city as their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extraterritorial" title="Extraterritorial">extraterritorial</a> enclave (although for six years the operational headquarters was in <a href="/wiki/%C5%9Awidnica" title="Świdnica">Świdnica</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polit_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-polit-2">[2]</a></sup> Other major Soviet military bases were located in <a href="/wiki/Bagicz" title="Bagicz">Bagicz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bia%C5%82ogard" title="Białogard">Białogard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brzeg" title="Brzeg">Brzeg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Borne_Sulinowo" title="Borne Sulinowo">Borne Sulinowo</a> (one of the two largest), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Burzykowo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Burzykowo (page does not exist)">Burzykowo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chojna" title="Chojna">Chojna</a>, <a href="/wiki/D%C4%99bice,_Lower_Silesian_Voivodeship" title="Dębice, Lower Silesian Voivodeship">Dębice</a>, <a href="/wiki/K%C4%99szyca_Le%C5%9Bna" title="Kęszyca Leśna">Kęszyca Leśna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kluczewo_Airfield" title="Kluczewo Airfield">Kluczewo</a>, <a href="/wiki/K%C5%82omino" title="Kłomino">Kłomino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nowa_S%C3%B3l" title="Nowa Sól">Nowa Sól</a>, <a href="/wiki/O%C5%82awa" title="Oława">Oława</a>, <a href="/wiki/Przemk%C3%B3w" title="Przemków">Przemków</a>-<a href="/wiki/Trzebie%C5%84,_Lower_Silesian_Voivodeship" title="Trzebień, Lower Silesian Voivodeship">Trzebień</a> (may refer to the same base as Strachów/Pstrąże), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pstr%C4%85%C5%BCe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pstrąże (page does not exist)">Strachów</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://pl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pstr%C4%85%C5%BCe" title="pl:Pstrąże">pl</a>)</span> (now - deserted town shown as Pstrąże on maps), <a href="/wiki/%C5%9Awidnica" title="Świdnica">Świdnica</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C5%9Awi%C4%99tosz%C3%B3w" title="Świętoszów">Świętoszów</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C5%9Awinouj%C5%9Bcie" title="Świnoujście">Świnoujście</a> (military harbor), <a href="/wiki/Szprotawa" title="Szprotawa">Szprotawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wschowa" title="Wschowa">Wschowa</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C5%BBaga%C5%84" title="Żagań">Żagań</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-14"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cela_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-cela-19">[19]</a></sup> Those bases included 15 airfields, 1 large and 11 smaller ports,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-15"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the late 1940s the NGF's forces included:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/43rd_Army_(Soviet_Union)" title="43rd Army (Soviet Union)">Soviet 43rd Army</a> was stationed in the region <a href="/wiki/Gda%C5%84sk" title="Gdańsk">Gdańsk</a> - <a href="/wiki/%C5%9Awinouj%C5%9Bcie" title="Świnoujście">Świnoujście</a> - <a href="/wiki/Szczecinek" title="Szczecinek">Szczecinek</a>; one of its corps was stationed on the <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a> island of <a href="/wiki/Bornholm" title="Bornholm">Bornholm</a>. However, it was disbanded in the summer of 1946.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/65th_Army_(Soviet_Union)" title="65th Army (Soviet Union)">Soviet 65th Army</a> was stationed in the region <a href="/wiki/%C5%81%C3%B3d%C5%BA" title="Łódź">Łódź</a> - <a href="/wiki/Pozna%C5%84" title="Poznań">Poznań</a> - <a href="/wiki/Wroc%C5%82aw" title="Wrocław">Wrocław</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/7th_Tank_Army" title="7th Tank Army">7th Red Banner Tank Army</a> was created in 1946 from HQ, 65th Army and in 1947 was transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Belorussian_Military_District" title="Belorussian Military District">Belorussian Military District</a>. After the creation of 7th Tank Army, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=18th_Rifle_Corps&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="18th Rifle Corps (page does not exist)">18th Rifle Corps</a> was transferred to the control of the Northern Group of Forces, where it remained from 12 June 1946 until it was disbanded in July 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/52nd_Army_(Soviet_Union)" title="52nd Army (Soviet Union)">Soviet 52nd Army</a> was stationed in the region <a href="/wiki/Kielce" title="Kielce">Kielce</a> - <a href="/wiki/Cz%C4%99stochowa" title="Częstochowa">Częstochowa</a> - <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w" title="Kraków">Kraków</a>.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=96th_Rifle_Corps&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="96th Rifle Corps (page does not exist)">96th Rifle Corps</a> was stationed in the region <a href="/wiki/%C5%81om%C5%BCa" title="Łomża">Łomża</a> - <a href="/wiki/M%C5%82awa" title="Mława">Mława</a> - <a href="/wiki/Pu%C5%82tusk" title="Pułtusk">Pułtusk</a>.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=3rd_Guards_Cavalry_Corps&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="3rd Guards Cavalry Corps (page does not exist)">3rd Guards Cavalry Corps</a> was stationed in <a href="/wiki/Lublin" title="Lublin">Lublin</a>.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=3rd_Guards_Tank_Corps_(Soviet_Union)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="3rd Guards Tank Corps (Soviet Union) (page does not exist)">3rd Guards Tank Corps</a> was stationed in <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w" title="Kraków">Kraków</a>.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=5th_Tank_Corps_(Soviet_Union)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="5th Tank Corps (Soviet Union) (page does not exist)">5th Tank Corps</a> was stationed in <a href="/wiki/Bia%C5%82ystok" title="Białystok">Białystok</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/10th_Tank_Corps" title="10th Tank Corps">10th Tank Corps</a> was stationed in <a href="/wiki/Krotoszyn" title="Krotoszyn">Krotoszyn</a>.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=20th_Tank_Corps_(Soviet)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="20th Tank Corps (Soviet) (page does not exist)">20th Tank Corps</a> was stationed in <a href="/wiki/Wroc%C5%82aw" title="Wrocław">Wrocław</a>. This formation became the 20th Tank Division later in 1945.</li>
<li>Elements of the Soviet <a href="/wiki/4th_Air_Army" title="4th Air Army">4th Air Army</a> were also stationed in Poland: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Soviet_8th_Fighter_Corps&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Soviet 8th Fighter Corps (page does not exist)">Soviet 8th Fighter Corps</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Soviet_4th_Air_Assault_Corps&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Soviet 4th Air Assault Corps (page does not exist)">Soviet 4th Air Assault Corps</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Soviet_5th_Bomber_Corps&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Soviet 5th Bomber Corps (page does not exist)">Soviet 5th Bomber Corps</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Altogether the Northern Group of Forces had three ground and one air Armies, 4 tank corps (from July 1945 reorganized into tank divisions), 30 rifle <a href="/wiki/Division_(military)" title="Division (military)">divisions</a>, 12 air divisions, 1 cavalry corps and 10 artillery divisions. The formation had a strength of around 300,000-400,000 soldiers stationed in Poland.</p>
<p>This large number of formations was quickly reduced as the post war demobilisations took place.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mid_1950s">Mid 1950s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Group_of_Forces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mid 1950s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By 1955 the force had been reduced to the <a href="/wiki/18th_Rifle_Division_(Soviet_Union)" title="18th Rifle Division (Soviet Union)">18th</a>, <a href="/wiki/26th_Rifle_Division_(Soviet_Union)" title="26th Rifle Division (Soviet Union)">26th</a>, and <a href="/wiki/27th_Rifle_Division_(Soviet_Union)" title="27th Rifle Division (Soviet Union)">27th Rifle Divisions</a>, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=20th_Tank_Division&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="20th Tank Division (page does not exist)">20th Tank Division</a>, and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=26th_Mechanised_Division&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="26th Mechanised Division (page does not exist)">26th Mechanised Division</a> - probably numbering no more than 100,000 troops.</p>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/83rd_Guards_Airborne_Brigade" title="83rd Guards Airborne Brigade">83rd Separate Air Assault Brigade</a> was formed at <a href="/wiki/Bia%C5%82ogard" title="Białogard">Białogard</a> in 1986.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/6th_Guards_Motor_Rifle_Division" title="6th Guards Motor Rifle Division">6th Guards Motor Rifle Division</a> (1982-) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=20th_Tank_Division&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="20th Tank Division (page does not exist)">20th Tank Division</a> were the principal Soviet formations stationed in the Group in the 1980s and early 1990s. Air support was provided by the <a href="/wiki/4th_Air_Army" title="4th Air Army">4th Air Army</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1990s, when the Group was preparing for leaving Poland, it had the strength of approximately 56,000 soldiers, with 600 tanks, 400 artillery pieces and 200 planes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-16"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Northern Group had its own newspaper, the <i>Znamia Pobiedy</i> (Flag of Victory).</p>
<p>The Northern Group had <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons" title="Nuclear weapons">nuclear weapons</a> deployed in at least three bases<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polit_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-polit-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-17"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup> in Poland with some 178 nuclear assets, growing to 250 in the late 1980s.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Group_of_Forces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Soviet Army was stationed in Poland for 48 years; it is estimated that its stay cost the Polish state 62.6 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1000000000_(number)" title="1000000000 (number)">billion</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Z%C5%82oty" title="Złoty">złoty</a> (in 1993 prices, approx. 3.5 billion <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_dollar" title="US dollar">US dollars</a>, not counting items removed from Polish territory after World War II); however, the Polish government decided to waive any claims to ensure a quicker evacuation of Soviet troops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-18"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pol2_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-pol2-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kprm_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-kprm-22">[22]</a></sup> The Soviets also claimed that any costs Poland incurred were balanced by the various aid (ex. infrastructure construction) provided over the years by the Soviet troops, as well as by the liberation from the Nazi occupation and later security.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-19"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1994 the Polish government passed legislation covering the use of the territories formerly used by the Soviet forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cela_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-cela-19">[19]</a></sup> Most of those territories have been put on sale by the Polish government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cela_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-cela-19">[19]</a></sup> Some of the Soviet administered areas were subject to ecological contamination and pollution (by oil products, heavy metals, <a href="/wiki/Unexploded_ordnance" title="Unexploded ordnance">unexploded ordnance</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-20"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cela_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-cela-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> They were also damaged by years of disrepair and poor maintenance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzecz_4-21"><a href="#cite_note-Rzecz-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
