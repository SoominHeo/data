<p>The <b>northern caenolestid</b> (<i>Caenolestes convelatus</i>), also known as the <b>blackish shrew opossum</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Shrew_opossum" title="Shrew opossum">shrew opossum</a> found in <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>. It is listed as <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">Vulnerable</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_etymology">Taxonomy and etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_caenolestid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The dusky caenolestid is one of the four members <i><a href="/wiki/Caenolestes" title="Caenolestes">Caenolestes</a></i>, and is placed in the family <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caenolestidae" title="Caenolestidae">Caenolestidae</a> (shrew opossums). It was first <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> by American zoologist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Harold_Elmer_Anthony&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Harold Elmer Anthony (page does not exist)">Harold Elmer Anthony</a> in 1924.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msw3_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-msw3-2">[2]</a></sup> In the latter part of 20th century, scientists believed that <i>Caenolestes</i> is closely related to <i>Lestoros</i> (the <a href="/wiki/Incan_caenolestid" title="Incan caenolestid">Incan caenolestid</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Over the years, it became clear that <i>Lestoros</i> is morphologically different from <i>Caenolestes</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gardner_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-gardner-5">[5]</a></sup> A 2013 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylogenetic" title="Phylogenetic">phylogenetic</a> study showed that the Incan caenolestid and the <a href="/wiki/Long-nosed_caenolestid" title="Long-nosed caenolestid">long-nosed caenolestid</a> (<i>Rhyncholestes raphanurus</i>) form a <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sister_taxon" title="Sister taxon">sister</a> to <i>Caenolestes</i>. The cladogram below is based on this study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>Caenolestid fossils date to as early as the early <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> (nearly 55 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mya_(unit)" title="Mya (unit)">mya</a>). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generic_name_(biology)" title="Generic name (biology)">generic name</a> <i>Caenolestes</i> derives from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> words <i>kainos</i> ("new") and <i>lestes</i> ("robber", "pirate").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_caenolestid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The northern caenolestid is similar to the <a href="/wiki/Gray-bellied_caenolestid" title="Gray-bellied caenolestid">gray-bellied caenolestid</a> in coat coloration but differs in cranial features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_caenolestid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The northern caenolestid occurs in and around alpine and <a href="/wiki/Secondary_forest" title="Secondary forest">secondary</a> forests. The populations appear to have been divided into two parts – the Andes of western Colombia and northcentral Ecuador. It occurs in an altitudinal range of 1,800 to 3,800 metres (5,900 to 12,500 ft) in Colombia, though in Ecuador it has been recorded at a height of 4,100 metres (13,500 ft). In 2008, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> classified the northern caenolestid as <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">Vulnerable</a> because it is known only from an area of 20,000 square kilometres (7,700 sq mi). Deforestation is a major threat, and more severe in Ecuador.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
