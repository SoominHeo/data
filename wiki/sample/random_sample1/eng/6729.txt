<p>The <b>Northern Wei</b> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">北魏</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Běi Wèi</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="Wade–Giles">Wade–Giles</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-wadegile" xml:lang="zh-Latn-wadegile">Pei<sup>3</sup> Wei<sup>4</sup></span></i>), also known as the <b>Tuoba Wei</b> (拓跋魏), <b>Later Wei</b> (後魏), or <b>Yuan Wei</b> (元魏), was a dynasty founded by the <a href="/wiki/Tuoba" title="Tuoba">Tuoba</a> clan of the <a href="/wiki/Xianbei" title="Xianbei">Xianbei</a>, which ruled northern China from 386 to 534<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rene_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rene-7">[7]</a></sup> (<i><a href="/wiki/De_jure" title="De jure">de jure</a></i> until 535), during the period of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_and_Northern_Dynasties" title="Southern and Northern Dynasties">Southern and Northern Dynasties</a>. Described as "part of an era of political turbulence and intense social and cultural change",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> the Northern Wei Dynasty is particularly noted for unifying northern China in 439: this was also a period of introduced foreign ideas; such as <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, which became firmly established.</p>
<p>During the Taihe period (477-499) of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Xiaowen_of_Northern_Wei" title="Emperor Xiaowen of Northern Wei">Emperor Xiaowen</a>, court advisers instituted sweeping reforms and introduced changes that eventually led to the dynasty moving its capital from <a href="/wiki/Datong" title="Datong">Datong</a> to <a href="/wiki/Luoyang" title="Luoyang">Luoyang</a>, in 494. The Tuoba renamed themselves the <a href="/wiki/Yuan_(surname)#Adoption_by_non-Han_peoples" title="Yuan (surname)">Yuan</a> as a part of systematic <a href="/wiki/Sinicization" title="Sinicization">Sinicization</a>. Towards the end of the dynasty there was significant internal dissension resulting in a split into <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Wei" title="Eastern Wei">Eastern Wei</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Wei" title="Western Wei">Western Wei</a>.</p>
<p>Many antiques and art works, both Daoist and Buddhist, from this period have survived. It was the time of the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Yungang_Grottoes" title="Yungang Grottoes">Yungang Grottoes</a> near <a href="/wiki/Datong" title="Datong">Datong</a> during the mid-to-late 5th century, and towards the latter part of the dynasty, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Longmen_Caves" title="Longmen Caves">Longmen Caves</a> outside the later capital city of Luoyang, in which more than 30,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> images from the time of this dynasty have been found.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Wei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_of_the_Tuoba_Xianbei">Rise of the Tuoba Xianbei</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Wei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rise of the Tuoba Xianbei">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jin_Dynasty_(265-420)" title="Jin Dynasty (265-420)">Jin Dynasty</a> had developed an alliance with the Tuoba against the <a href="/wiki/Xiongnu" title="Xiongnu">Xiongnu</a> state <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Zhao" title="Han Zhao">Han Zhao</a>. In 315 the Tuoba chief was granted the title of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_of_Dai" title="State of Dai">Prince of Dai</a>. After the death of its founding prince, <a href="/wiki/Tuoba_Yilu" title="Tuoba Yilu">Tuoba Yilu</a>, however, the Dai state stagnated and largely remained a partial ally and a partial tributary state to <a href="/wiki/Later_Zhao" title="Later Zhao">Later Zhao</a> and <a href="/wiki/Former_Yan" title="Former Yan">Former Yan</a>, finally falling to <a href="/wiki/Former_Qin" title="Former Qin">Former Qin</a> in 376.</p>
<p>After Former Qin's emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fu_Ji%C4%81n" title="Fu Jiān">Fu Jiān</a> was defeated by Jin forces at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fei_River" title="Battle of Fei River">Battle of Fei River</a> in his failed bid to unify China, the Former Qin state began to break apart. By 386, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Daowu_of_Northern_Wei" title="Emperor Daowu of Northern Wei">Tuoba Gui</a>, the son (or grandson) of <a href="/wiki/Tuoba_Shiyijian" title="Tuoba Shiyijian">Tuoba Shiyijian</a> (the last Prince of Dai), reasserted Tuoba independence initially as the Prince of Dai. Later he changed his title to the Prince of Wei, and his state was therefore known as Northern Wei. In 391, Tuoba Gui defeated the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rouran" title="Rouran">Rouran</a> tribes and killed their chief, Heduohan, forcing the Rouran to flee west.</p>
<p>Initially Northern Wei was a vassal of <a href="/wiki/Later_Yan" title="Later Yan">Later Yan</a>, but by 395 had rebelled and by 398 had conquered most of Later Yan territory north of the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_River" title="Yellow River">Yellow River</a>. In 399 Tuoba Gui he declared himself Emperor Daowu, and that title was used by Northern Wei's rulers for the rest of the state's history. That same year he defeated the <a href="/wiki/Tiele_people" title="Tiele people">Tiele</a> tribes near the Gobi desert</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Policies">Policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Wei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Early in Northern Wei history, the state inherited a number of traditions from its initial history as a Xianbei tribe, and some of the more unusual ones, from a traditional Chinese standpoint:</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nirun,_Tuyuhun,_Yueban,_Tuoba_Wei.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="519" data-file-width="943" height="121" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Nirun%2C_Tuyuhun%2C_Yueban%2C_Tuoba_Wei.jpg/220px-Nirun%2C_Tuyuhun%2C_Yueban%2C_Tuoba_Wei.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Nirun%2C_Tuyuhun%2C_Yueban%2C_Tuoba_Wei.jpg/330px-Nirun%2C_Tuyuhun%2C_Yueban%2C_Tuoba_Wei.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Nirun%2C_Tuyuhun%2C_Yueban%2C_Tuoba_Wei.jpg/440px-Nirun%2C_Tuyuhun%2C_Yueban%2C_Tuoba_Wei.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nirun,_Tuyuhun,_Yueban,_Tuoba_Wei.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Rouran_Khaganate" title="Rouran Khaganate">Rouran Khaganate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tuyuhun" title="Tuyuhun">Tuyuhun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yueban" title="Yueban">Yueban</a> and Northern Wei</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>The officials did not receive salaries, but were expected to requisition the necessities of their lives directly from the people they governed. As the empire's history progressed, this appeared to be a major contributing factor leading to corruption among officials. Not until the 2nd century of the empire's existence did the state begin to distribute salaries to its officials.</li>
<li>Empresses were not named according to imperial favors or nobility of birth, but required that the candidates submit themselves to a ceremony where they had to personally forge golden statues, as a way of discerning divine favor. Only an imperial consort who was successful in forging a golden statue could become the empress.</li>
<li>All men, regardless of ethnicity, were ordered to tie their hair into a single braid that would then be rolled and placed on top of the head, and then have a cap worn over the head.</li>
<li>When a <a href="/wiki/Crown_prince" title="Crown prince">crown prince</a> is named, his mother, if still alive, must be forced to commit suicide. (Some historians do not believe this to be a Tuoba traditional custom, but believed it to be a tradition instituted by the founding emperor <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Daowu_of_Northern_Wei" title="Emperor Daowu of Northern Wei">Emperor Daowu</a> based on <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wu_of_Han" title="Emperor Wu of Han">Emperor Wu of Han</a>'s execution of his favorite concubine Consort Zhao, the mother of his youngest son <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Zhao_of_Han" title="Emperor Zhao of Han">Liu Fuling</a> (the eventual Emperor Zhao), before naming Prince Fuling crown prince.)</li>
<li>As a result, because emperors would not have mothers, they often honored their <a href="/wiki/Wet_nurse" title="Wet nurse">wet nurses</a> with the honorific title, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nurse_Empress_Dowager" title="Nurse Empress Dowager">Nurse Empress Dowager</a>" (保太后, bǎo tài hòu).</li>
</ul>
<p>As sinicization of the Northern Wei state progressed, these customs and traditions were gradually abandoned.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization_of_the_Peasants">Organization of the Peasants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Wei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Organization of the Peasants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Five families formed a neighborhood (<i>lin</i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Five <i>lin</i> formed a village (<i>li</i>)</li>
<li>Five <i>li</i> formed a commune (<i>tang</i>)</li>
</ul>
<p>At each of these levels, leaders that were associated with the central government were appointed. In order for the state to reclaim dry, barren areas of land, the state further developed this system by dividing up the land according to the number of men of an age to cultivate it. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sui_Dynasty" title="Sui Dynasty">Sui</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tang_Dynasty" title="Tang Dynasty">Tang</a> Dynasties later <a href="/wiki/Equal-field_system" title="Equal-field system">resurrected this system</a> in the 7th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deportations">Deportations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Wei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Deportations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

Deportations typically took place once a new piece of territory had been conquered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sinicization">Sinicization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Wei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sinicization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the Northern Wei state grew, the emperors' desire for <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Han Chinese</a> institutions and advisors grew. <a href="/wiki/Cui_Hao" title="Cui Hao">Cui Hao</a> (381-450), an advisor at the courts in <a href="/wiki/Datong" title="Datong">Datong</a> played a great part in this process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup> He introduced Han Chinese administrative methods and penal codes in the Northern Wei state, as well as creating a <a href="/wiki/The_Northern_Celestial_Masters" title="The Northern Celestial Masters">Taoist theocracy</a> that lasted until 450. The attraction of Han Chinese products, the royal court's taste for luxury, the prestige of Chinese culture at the time, and <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">Taoism</a> were all factors in the growing Chinese influence in the Northern Wei state. Chinese influence accelerated during the capital's move to <a href="/wiki/Luoyang" title="Luoyang">Luoyang</a> in 494 and <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Xiaowen_of_Northern_Wei" title="Emperor Xiaowen of Northern Wei">Emperor Xiaowen</a> continued this by establishing a policy of systematic <a href="/wiki/Sinicization" title="Sinicization">sinicization</a> that was continued by his successors. Xianbei traditions were largely abandoned. The royal family took the <a href="/wiki/Sinicization" title="Sinicization">sinicization</a> a step further by changing their family name to Yuan. Marriages to Chinese families were encouraged. With this, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> temples started appearing everywhere, displacing Taoism as the state religion. The temples were often created to appear extremely lavish and extravagant on the outside of the temples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup> Also from 460 onwards the emperor's started erecting huge statues of the Buddha carved near their capital Pingcheng which declared the emperors as the representatives of the Buddha and the legitimate rulers of China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Northern Wei started to arrange for Han Chinese elites to marry daughters of the <a href="/wiki/Xianbei" title="Xianbei">Xianbei</a> <a href="/wiki/Tuoba" title="Tuoba">Tuoba</a> royal family in the 480s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watson1991_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Watson1991-11">[11]</a></sup> Some Han Chinese exiled royalty fled from southern China and defected to the Xianbei. Several daughters of the Xianbei <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Xiaowen_of_Northern_Wei" title="Emperor Xiaowen of Northern Wei">Emperor Xiaowen of Northern Wei</a> were married to Han Chinese elites, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liu_Song" title="Liu Song">Liu Song</a> royal Liu Hui (刘辉), married Princess Lanling (蘭陵公主) of the Northern Wei,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SwartzCampany2013_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-SwartzCampany2013-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Princess Huayang (華陽公主) to Sima Fei (司馬朏), a descendant of <a href="/wiki/Jin_dynasty_(265%E2%80%93420)" title="Jin dynasty (265–420)">Jin dynasty (265–420)</a> royalty, Princess Jinan (濟南公主) to Lu Daoqian (盧道虔), Princess Nanyang (南阳长公主) to <a href="/wiki/Xiao_Baoyin" title="Xiao Baoyin">Xiao Baoyin</a> (萧宝夤), a member of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Qi" title="Southern Qi">Southern Qi</a> royalty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Xiaozhuang_of_Northern_Wei" title="Emperor Xiaozhuang of Northern Wei">Emperor Xiaozhuang of Northern Wei</a>'s sister the Shouyang Princess was wedded to The <a href="/wiki/Liang_dynasty" title="Liang dynasty">Liang dynasty</a> ruler <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wu_of_Liang" title="Emperor Wu of Liang">Emperor Wu of Liang</a>'s son Xiao Zong <a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E8%95%AD%E7%B6%9C" title="zh:蕭綜">蕭綜</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>When the Eastern Jin dynasty ended Northern Wei received the Han Chinese Jin prince Sima Chuzhi (<a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%8F%B8%E9%A6%AC%E6%A5%9A%E4%B9%8B" title="zh:司馬楚之">司馬楚之</a>) as a refugee. A Northern Wei Princess married Sima Chuzhi, giving birth to Sima Jinlong (<a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%8F%B8%E9%A6%AC%E9%87%91%E9%BE%8D" title="zh:司馬金龍">司馬金龍</a>). <a href="/wiki/Northern_Liang" title="Northern Liang">Northern Liang</a> Xiongnu King <a href="/wiki/Juqu_Mujian" title="Juqu Mujian">Juqu Mujian</a>'s daughter married Sima Jinlong.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The Northern Wei's Eight Noble <a href="/wiki/Xianbei" title="Xianbei">Xianbei</a> surnames (<a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%85%AB%E5%A4%A7%E8%B4%B5%E6%97%8F" title="zh:八大贵族">八大贵族</a>) were the Buliugu (步六孤), Helai (賀賴), <a href="/wiki/Dugu" title="Dugu">Dugu</a> (<a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E7%8D%A8%E5%AD%A4" title="zh:獨孤">獨孤</a>), Helou (賀樓), Huniu (忽忸), Qiumu (丘穆), Gexi (紇奚), and Yuchi (<a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%B0%89%E9%81%B2" title="zh:尉遲">尉遲</a>). They adopted Chinese last names.</p>
<p>Kongzi was honoreed in sacrifices as was Earth and Heaven by the northern dynasties of non-Han origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009-17">[17]</a></sup> Kongzi was honored by the Murong Wei Former Yan Xianbei leader.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_2_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_2-18">[18]</a></sup> Kongzi was honored by the Di ruler <a href="/wiki/Fu_Jian_(337%E2%80%93385)" title="Fu Jian (337–385)">Fu Jian (337–385)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_3_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_3-19">[19]</a></sup> Kongzi was honored in sacrifices by the Northern Wei Xianbei dynasty. Kongzi was honored by Yuoba Si, the Mingyuan emperor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_4_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_4-20">[20]</a></sup> Han dynasty Emperors, Shang dynasty ruler Bigan, Emperor Yao and Emperor Shun were honored by Yuoba Si, the Mingyuan Emperor. Kongzi was honored extensively by Tuoba Hong, the Xiaowen Emperor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_5_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_5-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>A fief of 100 households and the rank of (崇聖侯) <a href="/wiki/Duke_Yansheng#Three_Kingdoms_period_.28220.E2.80.93280_CE.29_through_Northern_and_Southern_dynasties_era_.28420.E2.80.93589.29" title="Duke Yansheng"><i>Marquis who worships the sage</i></a> was bestowed upon a Confucius descendant, <a href="/wiki/Yan_Hui" title="Yan Hui">Yan Hui</a>'s lineage had 2 of its scions and Confucius's lineage had 4 of its scions who had ranks bestowed on them in Shandong in 495 and a fief of ten households and rank of (崇聖大夫) <i>Grandee who venerates the sage</i> was bestowed on Kong Sheng (孔乘) who was Confucius's scion in the 28th generation in 472 by Emperor Xiaowen of Northern Wei.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_6_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_6-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_7_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_7-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>An anti Buddhist plan was concocted by the Celestial Masters under <a href="/wiki/Kou_Qianzhi" title="Kou Qianzhi">Kou Qianzhi</a> along with <a href="/wiki/Cui_Hao" title="Cui Hao">Cui Hao</a> under the Taiwu Emperor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_27_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_27-24">[24]</a></sup> The Celestial Masters of the north urged the persecution of Buddhists under the Taiwu Emperor in the Northern Wei, attacking Buddhism and the Buddha as wicked and as anti stability and anti family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_28_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_28-25">[25]</a></sup> Anti Buddhism was the position of Kou Qianzhi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_29_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_29-26">[26]</a></sup> There was no ban on the Celestial Masters despite the nofullfilment of Cui Hao and Kou Qianzhi's agenda in their anti Buddhist campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_30_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-LagerweyL.C3.BC2009_30-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cui Zhen's wife Han Farong was buried in a Datong located grave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breakup_and_division">Breakup and division</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Wei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Breakup and division">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stone_Statue_in_front_of_tomb._Northern_Wei_Dynasty_(386-534_CE).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="633" height="404" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Stone_Statue_in_front_of_tomb._Northern_Wei_Dynasty_%28386-534_CE%29.jpg/250px-Stone_Statue_in_front_of_tomb._Northern_Wei_Dynasty_%28386-534_CE%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Stone_Statue_in_front_of_tomb._Northern_Wei_Dynasty_%28386-534_CE%29.jpg/375px-Stone_Statue_in_front_of_tomb._Northern_Wei_Dynasty_%28386-534_CE%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Stone_Statue_in_front_of_tomb._Northern_Wei_Dynasty_%28386-534_CE%29.jpg/500px-Stone_Statue_in_front_of_tomb._Northern_Wei_Dynasty_%28386-534_CE%29.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

<p>The heavy Chinese influence that had come into the Northern Wei state which went on throughout the 5th century had mainly affected the courts and the upper ranks of the Tuoba <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy" title="Aristocracy">aristocracy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup> Armies that guarded the Northern frontiers of the empire and the Xianbei people who were less sinicized began showing feelings of hostility towards the aristocratic court and the upper ranks of civil society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup> Early in Northern Wei history, defense on the northern border against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rouran" title="Rouran">Rouran</a> was heavily emphasized, and military duty on the northern border was considered honored service that was given high recognition. After all, throughout the founding and the early stages of the Northern Wei, it was the strength of the sword and bow that carved out the empire and kept it. But once Emperor Xiaowen's sinicization campaign began in earnest, military service, particularly on the northern border, was no longer considered an honorable status, and traditional Xianbei warrior families on the northern border were disrespected and disallowed many of their previous privileges; these warrior families who had originally being held as the upper-class now found themselves considered a lower-class on the social hierarchy.</p>
<p>In 523, rebellions broke out on <a href="/wiki/Six_Frontier_Towns" title="Six Frontier Towns">six major garrison-towns</a> on the northern border and spread like wildfire throughout the north. These rebellions lasted for a decade. Exacerbating the situation, <a href="/wiki/Empress_Dowager_Hu_(Northern_Wei)" title="Empress Dowager Hu (Northern Wei)">Empress Dowager Hu</a> poisoned her own son <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Xiaoming_of_Northern_Wei" title="Emperor Xiaoming of Northern Wei">Emperor Xiaoming</a> in 528 after Emperor Xiaoming showed disapproval of her handling of the affairs as he started coming of age and got ready to reclaim the power that had been held by the empress in his name when he inherited the throne as an infant, giving the Empress Dowager rule of the country for more than a decade. Upon hearing the news of the 18-year-old emperor's death, the general <a href="/wiki/Erzhu_Rong" title="Erzhu Rong">Erzhu Rong</a>, who had already mobilised on secret orders of the emperor to support him in his struggle with the Empress Dowager Hu, turned toward Luoyang. Announcing that he was installing a new emperor chosen by an ancient Xianbei method of casting bronze figures, <a href="/wiki/Erzhu_Rong" title="Erzhu Rong">Erzhu Rong</a> summoned the officials of the city to meet their new emperor. However, on their arrival, he told them they were to be punished for their misgovernment and butchered them, throwing the Empress Hu and her candidate (another puppet child emperor <a href="/wiki/Yuan_Zhao" title="Yuan Zhao">Yuan Zhao</a>) into the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_River" title="Yellow River">Yellow River</a>. Reports estimate 2,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Courtiers" title="Courtiers">courtiers</a> were killed in this Heyin (Ho-Yin) massacre on the 13th day of the second month of 528.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Two_Generals">The Two Generals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Wei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Two Generals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cernuschi_Museum_20060812_128.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1983" data-file-width="1752" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Cernuschi_Museum_20060812_128.jpg/200px-Cernuschi_Museum_20060812_128.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Cernuschi_Museum_20060812_128.jpg/300px-Cernuschi_Museum_20060812_128.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Cernuschi_Museum_20060812_128.jpg/400px-Cernuschi_Museum_20060812_128.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>

<p>Erzhu dominated the imperial court thereafter, the emperor held power in name only and most decisions actually went through the Erzhu. The emperor did stop most of the rebellions, largely reunifying the Northern Wei state. However, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Xiaozhuang_of_Northern_Wei" title="Emperor Xiaozhuang of Northern Wei">Emperor Xiaozhuang</a>, not wishing to remain a puppet emperor and highly wary of the Erzhu clan's widespread power and questionable loyalty and intentions towards the throne (after all, this man had ordered a massacre of the court and put to death a previous emperor and empress before), killed Erzhu Rong in 530 in an ambush at the palace, which led to a resumption of civil war, initially between Erzhu's clan and Emperor Xiaozhuang, and then, after their victory over Emperor Xiaozhuang in 531, between the Erzhu clan and those who resisted their rule. In the aftermath of these wars, two generals set in motion the actions that would result in the splitting of the Northern Wei into the Eastern and Western Wei.</p>

<p>General <a href="/wiki/Gao_Huan" title="Gao Huan">Gao Huan</a> was originally from the northern frontier, one of many soldiers who had surrendered to Erzhu, who eventually became one of the Erzhu clan's top lieutenants. But later, Gao Huan gathered his own men from both Han and non-Han troops, to turn against the Erzhu clan, entering and taking the capital Luoyang in 532. Confident in his success, he set up a nominee emperor on the Luoyang throne and continued his campaigns abroad. The emperor, however, together with the military head of Luoyang, <a href="/wiki/Husi_Chun" title="Husi Chun">Husi Chun</a>, began to plot against <a href="/wiki/Gao_Huan" title="Gao Huan">Gao Huan</a>. Gao Huan succeeded, however, in keeping control of Luoyang, and the unfaithful ruler and a handful of followers fled west, to the region ruled by the powerful warlord <a href="/wiki/Yuwen_Tai" title="Yuwen Tai">Yuwen Tai</a>. Gao Huan then announced his decision to move the Luoyang court to his capital city of <a href="/wiki/Ye_(ancient_China)" title="Ye (ancient China)">Ye</a>. "Within three days of the decree, 400,000 families--perhaps 2,000,000 people--had to leave their homes in and around the capital to move to Yeh as autumn turned to winter."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> There now existed two rival claimants to the Northern Wei throne, leading to the state's division in 534-535 into the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Wei" title="Eastern Wei">Eastern Wei</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Wei" title="Western Wei">Western Wei</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fall">Fall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Wei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Neither Eastern Wei nor Western Wei was long-lived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In 550, Gao Huan's son <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wenxuan_of_Northern_Qi" title="Emperor Wenxuan of Northern Qi">Gao Yang</a> forced <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Xiaojing_of_Eastern_Wei" title="Emperor Xiaojing of Eastern Wei">Emperor Xiaojing of Eastern Wei</a> to yield the throne to him, ending Eastern Wei and establishing the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Qi" title="Northern Qi">Northern Qi</a>. Similarly, in 557, Yuwen Tai's nephew <a href="/wiki/Yuwen_Hu" title="Yuwen Hu">Yuwen Hu</a> forced <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Gong_of_Western_Wei" title="Emperor Gong of Western Wei">Emperor Gong of Western Wei</a> to yield the throne to Yuwen Tai's son <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Xiaomin_of_Northern_Zhou" title="Emperor Xiaomin of Northern Zhou">Yuwen Jue</a>, ending the Western Wei and establishing the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Zhou" title="Northern Zhou">Northern Zhou</a>, finally extinguishing Northern Wei's imperial rule.</p>

Due to the briefness of Yuan Hao's claim on the throne and the limited geographic scope of his reign, he is usually not counted among the succession of Northern Wei emperors.</span></li>
