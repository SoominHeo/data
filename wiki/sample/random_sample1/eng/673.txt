<p><b>Sixty-nine</b> or <b>69</b>, also known by its <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> name <b>soixante-neuf</b> (69), is a group of <a href="/wiki/Sex_position" title="Sex position">sex positions</a> in which two people align themselves so that each person's mouth is near the other's genitals, simultaneously performing <a href="/wiki/Oral_sex" title="Oral sex">oral sex</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-little_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-little-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aggrawal_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-aggrawal-3">[3]</a></sup> The participants are thus mutually inverted like the numerals 6 and 9 in the <a href="/wiki/69_(number)" title="69 (number)">number 69</a>, hence the name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aggrawal_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-aggrawal-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This position can involve any combination of genders.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Method">Method</span></h2>
<p>Variations of the 69 positions include mutual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anal%E2%80%93oral_sex" title="Anal–oral sex">anilingus</a> or "double rimming", and <a href="/wiki/Fingering_(sexual_act)" title="Fingering (sexual act)">digital penetration</a> of either partner's <a href="/wiki/Anus" title="Anus">anus</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vagina" title="Vagina">vagina</a>.</p>

<p>In these positions, the partners are said to experience <a href="/wiki/Sexual_stimulation" title="Sexual stimulation">sexual stimulation</a> simultaneously, but this can also distract those who try to focus solely on pleasuring themselves. The position can also be awkward for partners who are not similar in height.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>"Mutual simultaneous oragenitalism is usually referred to in English under the euphemistic French numerical form, “s<i>oixante-neuf</i>.” … The ancient Chinese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yin_and_Yang" title="Yin and Yang">Yin and Yang</a> (female &amp; male) symbol is identical. … The term “<i>soixante-neuf</i>” has not been traced any earlier than certain <i>Whore’s Catechism</i>s published in the 1790s in France, usually attributed to the … early leader of the French Revolution, Mlle. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9roigne_de_M%C3%A9ricourt" title="Théroigne de Méricourt">Théroigne de Méricourt</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>"The earliest unequivocal representation of the sixty-nine appears to be that on an oil-lamp preserved in the <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> Museum (<a href="/wiki/Deutsches_Museum" title="Deutsches Museum">Deutsches Museum</a>), and first reproduced in Dr. Gaston Vorberg’s … portfolio, <i>Die Erotik der Antiken in Kleinkunst und Keramik</i> (Munich, 1921) plate 58, showing the woman lying on top of the man. Dr. Vorberg gives this … to be of the period of the Roman Caesars … . However, another oil-lamp of the same kind, showing the sixty-nine almost identically … is more recently reproduced as a full-color plate, in Prof. <a href="/wiki/Jean_Marcad%C3%A9" title="Jean Marcadé">Jean Marcadé</a>’s <i>Eros Kalos</i> (English-language edition, <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a> : Nagel, 1965), facing page 58, in … lamps preserved in the <a href="/wiki/Heraklion_Archaeological_Museum" title="Heraklion Archaeological Museum">Heracleion Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>"A <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> temple-sculpture from the sacred caverns of the island of <a href="/wiki/Elephanta_Island" title="Elephanta Island">Elephanta</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a> in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, showing this position with the man actually standing, and holding the woman hanging down in this from his shoulders, was … brought to England in the late eighteenth century … . … this sculptured fragment … is both discussed and illustrated in <a href="/wiki/Richard_Payne_Knight" title="Richard Payne Knight">Richard Payne Knight</a>’s <i>A Discourse on the Worship of <a href="/wiki/Priapus" title="Priapus">Priapus</a></i>, privately issued for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dilettanti_Society" title="Dilettanti Society">Dilettanti Society</a> of London in 1786 … . The illustration in question is a detail engraving given in Payne Knight’s plate XI; and the full form of this sculptured group is … given as plate XXIV."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Kama_Sutra" title="Kama Sutra">Kama Sutra</a></i> mentions this sex position, albeit by a different name: "When a man and woman lie down in an inverted order, i.e. with the head of the one towards the feet of the other and carry on [mouth] congress, it is called the 'congress of a crow'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
