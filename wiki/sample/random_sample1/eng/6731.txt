<b>49th parallel north</b></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <b>49th parallel north</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_latitude" title="Circle of latitude">circle of latitude</a> that is 49 <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">degrees</a> <a href="/wiki/True_north" title="True north">north</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth's</a> <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equatorial plane</a>. It crosses <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>.</p>
<p>The city of <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> is about 15 km (9 mi) south of the 49th parallel and is the largest city between the <a href="/wiki/48th_parallel_north" title="48th parallel north">48th</a> and 49th parallels. Its main airport, <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle_Airport" title="Charles de Gaulle Airport">Charles de Gaulle Airport</a>, lies on the parallel.</p>
<p>Roughly 3,500 kilometres (2,175 mi) of the <a href="/wiki/Canada%E2%80%93United_States_border" title="Canada–United States border">Canada–United States border</a> was designated to follow the 49th parallel from <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> side, and from <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a> to <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a> on the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> side, more specifically from the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Georgia" title="Strait of Georgia">Strait of Georgia</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Lake_of_the_Woods" title="Lake of the Woods">Lake of the Woods</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Border" title="Border">international border</a> was specified in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-American_Convention_of_1818" title="Anglo-American Convention of 1818">Anglo-American Convention of 1818</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Oregon_Treaty" title="Oregon Treaty">Oregon Treaty</a> of 1846, however the border as indicated by survey markers placed in the 19th century deviates from the 49th parallel by tens of meters.</p>
<p>From a point on the ground at this latitude, the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a> is above the horizon for 16 hours, 12 minutes during the <a href="/wiki/Summer_solstice" title="Summer solstice">summer solstice</a> and 8 hours, 14 minutes during the <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">winter solstice</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This latitude also roughly corresponds to the minimum latitude in which astronomical twilight can last all night near the summer solstice. Slightly less than 1/8 of the Earth's surface is north of the 49th parallel.</p>

<li>The <a href="/wiki/Peace_Arch" title="Peace Arch">Peace Arch</a> is a large monument between <a href="/wiki/Surrey,_British_Columbia" title="Surrey, British Columbia">Surrey</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Blaine,_Washington" title="Blaine, Washington">Blaine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>. It has as its focal point the <a href="/wiki/International_Peace_Garden" title="International Peace Garden">Peace Tower</a> (actually four identical monuments 120 feet (36.6 m) tall set parallel only a few feet apart, two on each side of the border, giving the appearance of a double tower from most vantage points in the Garden).</li>
<li>The Stadtgarten in <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe" title="Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe</a>, Germany, marks the 49th parallel with a stone and painted line.</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=49th_parallel_north&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Purchase" title="Louisiana Purchase">Louisiana Purchase</a> by the United States in 1803, it was generally agreed that the boundary between the new territory and <a href="/wiki/British_North_America" title="British North America">British North America</a> was along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_divide" title="Water divide">watershed</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_River" title="Missouri River">Missouri River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> basins on one side and the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Bay" title="Hudson Bay">Hudson Bay</a> basin on the other. However, it is often difficult to precisely determine the location of a watershed in a region of level plains, such as in central <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. The British and American committees that met after the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a> to resolve boundary disputes recognized there would be much animosity in surveying the watershed boundary, and agreed on a simpler border solution in 1818: the 49th parallel. Both sides gained and lost some territory by this convention, but the United States gained more than it lost, in particular securing title to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_River_Basin" title="Red River Basin">Red River Basin</a>. This convention established the boundary only between the <a href="/wiki/Lake_of_the_Woods" title="Lake of the Woods">Lake of the Woods</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a>; west of the Rockies, the convention established joint occupation of the <a href="/wiki/Oregon_Country" title="Oregon Country">Oregon Country</a> by both parties. A geographical oversight at the Lake of the Woods resulted in the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Angle" title="Northwest Angle">Northwest Angle</a>.</p>
<p>Although the Convention of 1818 settled the boundary, neither country was immediately able to control over the territories on its side of the line: effective control still rested with local Indian tribes, mainly the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis_people_(Canada)" title="Métis people (Canada)">Métis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assiniboine_people" title="Assiniboine people">Assiniboine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lakota_people" title="Lakota people">Lakota</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackfeet" title="Blackfeet">Blackfoot</a>. Their power was gradually ceded by conquest and treaty during the several decades that followed. Among these peoples, the 49th parallel was nicknamed the <b>Medicine Line</b> because of its seemingly magical ability to prevent U.S. soldiers from crossing it.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1844" title="United States presidential election, 1844">1844 U.S. presidential election</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Democratic_Party" title="United States Democratic Party">Democratic Party</a> asserted that the northern border of the <a href="/wiki/Oregon_Territory" title="Oregon Territory">Oregon Territory</a> should be 54°40′, later reflected in the 1846 slogan "<a href="/wiki/Oregon_boundary_dispute" title="Oregon boundary dispute">Fifty-Four Forty or Fight</a>!" However, the <a href="/wiki/Oregon_boundary_dispute" title="Oregon boundary dispute">Oregon boundary dispute</a> was settled diplomatically in the 1846 Oregon Treaty. This agreement divided the Oregon Country between <a href="/wiki/British_North_America" title="British North America">British North America</a> and the United States by extending the 49th parallel boundary to the west coast, ending in the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Georgia" title="Strait of Georgia">Strait of Georgia</a>; it then circumvents <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_Island" title="Vancouver Island">Vancouver Island</a> through <a href="/wiki/Boundary_Pass" title="Boundary Pass">Boundary Pass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haro_Strait" title="Haro Strait">Haro Strait</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Juan_de_Fuca" title="Strait of Juan de Fuca">Strait of Juan de Fuca</a>. This had the side effect of isolating <a href="/wiki/Point_Roberts,_Washington" title="Point Roberts, Washington">Point Roberts, Washington</a>.</p>

<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peacearch-boundarymarkers.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2272" data-file-width="1704" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Peacearch-boundarymarkers.jpg/200px-Peacearch-boundarymarkers.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Peacearch-boundarymarkers.jpg/300px-Peacearch-boundarymarkers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Peacearch-boundarymarkers.jpg/400px-Peacearch-boundarymarkers.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>

<p>Although parts of Vancouver Island and parts of Eastern Canada are south of the 49th parallel, and parts of the United States (<a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Angle" title="Northwest Angle">Northwest Angle</a>) are north of it, the term <i>49th parallel</i> is sometimes used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metonymic" title="Metonymic">metonymically</a> to refer to the entire Canada-U.S. border. Actually, many of Canada's most populated regions (and about 72% of the population) are south of the 49th parallel, including the two largest cities <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a> (43°42′ north) and <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a> (45°30′ north), the federal capital <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a> (45.25° north) and the capitals of all provinces except the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prairie_provinces" title="Prairie provinces">Prairie provinces</a> (<a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a>), these being the only provinces entirely north of the 49th parallel. The three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maritimes" title="Maritimes">Maritime provinces</a> are each entirely south of the parallel, but the vast majority of Canadian territory lies north of it.</p>
<p>Parts of the 49th parallel were originally surveyed using astronomical techniques that did not take into account slight departures of the Earth's shape from a simple <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">ellipsoid</a>, or the deflection of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plumb-bob" title="Plumb-bob">plumb-bob</a> by differences in terrestrial mass. The surveys were subject to the limitations of early to mid-19th century technology, but accurate results were obtained. However, in some places the surveyed 49th parallel is several hundred feet from the geographical 49th parallel for the currently adopted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Datum_(geodesy)" title="Datum (geodesy)">datum</a>, the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Datum" title="North American Datum">North American Datum</a> of 1983 (NAD 83). The <a href="/wiki/Digital_Chart_of_the_World" title="Digital Chart of the World">Digital Chart of the World</a> (DCW), which uses the Clarke 1866 ellipsoid, reports the border on average at latitude 48° 59′ 51″ north, roughly 270 metres (886 ft) south of the modern 49th parallel. It ranges between 48° 59′ 25″ and 49° 0′ 10″ north, 810 metres (2,657 ft) and 590 metres (1,936 ft) on either side of the average. In any case, the Earth's North Pole moves around slightly, notionally moving the 49th and other parallels with it; see <a href="/wiki/Polar_motion" title="Polar motion">polar motion</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Angle" title="Northwest Angle">Northwest Angle</a> is the only part of the contiguous 48 states that goes north of the 49th parallel. This was due to an erroneous map that showed the source of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> as being north of the 49th parallel.</p>
<p>At the time that the United States and Great Britain agreed on the 49th parallel as the boundary, much of the North American continent had not yet been mapped. After the boundary was established, British surveyors discovered that <a href="/wiki/Point_Roberts,_Washington" title="Point Roberts, Washington">Point Roberts</a> lay south of the 49th parallel. The British requested that the United States cede the territory to Great Britain, but no action was ever taken.</p>
<p>In 1909 the United States, United Kingdom and Canada signed and ratified a treaty confirming the original survey lines as the official and permanent international border. Nevertheless, in 2002 the difference of the survey from the geographical 49th parallel was argued in front of the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Supreme_Court" title="Washington Supreme Court">Washington Supreme Court</a> in the case of <i>State of Washington v. Norman</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> under the premise that Washington did not properly incorporate the portions of land north of the geographical 49th parallel, as laid out by detailed <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">GPS</a> surveying. The court decided against the premise, ruling that the internationally surveyed boundary also served as the state boundary, regardless of its actual position.</p>
