<p><b>Emperor Wencheng of Northern Wei</b> ((北)魏文成帝) (440–465), personal name <b>Tuoba Jun</b></p>
<p>(拓拔濬), was an <a href="/wiki/Emperor" title="Emperor">emperor</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Xianbei" title="Xianbei">Xianbei</a> dynasty <a href="/wiki/Northern_Wei" title="Northern Wei">Northern Wei</a>. He became emperor in the aftermaths of the <a href="/wiki/Eunuch" title="Eunuch">eunuch</a> <a href="/wiki/Zong_Ai" title="Zong Ai">Zong Ai</a>'s assassination of his grandfather <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Taiwu_of_Northern_Wei" title="Emperor Taiwu of Northern Wei">Emperor Taiwu</a> and uncle <a href="/wiki/Tuoba_Yu" title="Tuoba Yu">Tuoba Yu</a>, and he was generally described by historians as a ruler who sought foremost to allow his people to rest after his grandfather's expansionist policies and extensive campaigns, who also reformed the laws to become more lenient.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Wencheng_of_Northern_Wei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tuoba Jun was born in 440, when his father <a href="/wiki/Tuoba_Huang" title="Tuoba Huang">Tuoba Huang</a> was the crown prince of his grandfather, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Taiwu_of_Northern_Wei" title="Emperor Taiwu of Northern Wei">Emperor Taiwu</a>. (Some historical sources give his birthdate as 448, a date that appears rather unlikely because that would make him only six years older than his son, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Xianwen_of_Northern_Wei" title="Emperor Xianwen of Northern Wei">Emperor Xianwen</a>.) His mother, <a href="/wiki/Consort_Yujiul%C3%BC" title="Consort Yujiulü">Consort Yujiulü</a>, was the sister of the general Yujiulü Pi (郁久閭毗), who had been a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rouran" title="Rouran">Rouran</a> royal house but who had surrendered to Northern Wei. When he was little, he was much favored by his grandfather Emperor Taiwu, and often accompanied Emperor Taiwu, even on military campaigns. While he carried no official titles, he was known as the Prime Imperial Grandson (嫡皇孫). Around the new year 452, after his father Tuoba Huang had died in 451 after falling ill over his fear of false accusations by the <a href="/wiki/Eunuch" title="Eunuch">eunuch</a> <a href="/wiki/Zong_Ai" title="Zong Ai">Zong Ai</a>, Emperor Taiwu created him the Prince of Gaoyang, but then reconsidered, believing that a princely title was inappropriate for his oldest grandson, and therefore cancelled the title—signifying strongly that he intended for Tuoba Jun to succeed him.</p>
<p>However, Zong Ai, in fear that Emperor Taiwu would punish him over his false accusations against Crown Prince Huang, assassinated Emperor Taiwu in spring 452, and, skipping over Tuoba Jun, made Tuoba Huang's younger brother <a href="/wiki/Tuoba_Yu" title="Tuoba Yu">Tuoba Yu</a> the Prince of Nan'an emperor. Zong controlled all important matters of state, and when Tuoba Yu, displeased about Zong's arrogance, tried to strip him of power in fall 452, Zong assassinated him as well. The officials Dugu Ni (獨孤尼), <a href="/wiki/Yuan_He" title="Yuan He">Yuan He</a>, Baba Kehou (拔拔渴侯), and <a href="/wiki/Buliugu_Li" title="Buliugu Li">Buliugu Li</a>, however, then overthrew Zong and executed him, making Tuoba Jun emperor at the age of 12, as Emperor Wencheng.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_reign">Early reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Wencheng_of_Northern_Wei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Immediately following Emperor Wencheng's taking the throne, it appeared that the high-level officials began internecine struggles against each other, as for several years officials would be put into honored positions, only to be executed days or months later. These included senior officials held over from Emperor Taiwu's administration as well as those who had accomplished much in putting Emperor Wencheng on the throne, and included:</p>
<ul>
<li>Tuoba Shoule (拓拔壽樂), chief commander (winter 452)</li>
<li>Baba Kehou, a major participant in the coup bringing Emperor Wencheng to the throne (winter 452)</li>
<li>Tuxi Bi (吐奚弼), prime minister under Tuoba Yu (winter 452)</li>
<li>Zhang Li (張黎), chief commander under Tuoba Yu (winter 452)</li>
<li>Tuoba Zhouniu (拓拔周忸) the Prince of Leling, chief commander (winter 452)</li>
<li>Duguhun Yuanbao (獨孤渾元寶) the Prince of Jingzhao (spring 453)</li>
<li>Tuoba Chong (拓拔崇) the Prince of Jianning, Emperor Wencheng's granduncle, and his son Tuoba Li (拓拔麗) the Prince of Ji'nan (spring 453)</li>
<li>Yujiulü Ruowen (郁久閭若文) the Prince of Puyang (fall 453)</li>
<li>Tuoba Ren (拓拔仁) the Prince of Yongchang, Emperor Wencheng's distant uncle (fall 453)</li>
<li>Tuoba Ba (拓拔拔) the Prince of Leping, Emperor Wencheng's distant uncle (spring 455)</li>
</ul>
<p>It appeared that Yuan He and Buliugu Li were the key victors out of these struggles, for not only did they not suffer during the infighting, but became exceedingly honored late in Emperor Wencheng's reign. Both they and Dugu Ni were created princes. Whether Emperor Wencheng himself was involved in these internecine struggles is unclear, although the edicts were issued in his name. Yuchi Juan (尉遲眷), already a decorated general during Emperor Taiwu's reign, also became powerful.</p>
<p>Another characteristic of Emperor Wencheng's reign was that he often toured the provinces throughout his empire.</p>
<p>In winter 452, Emperor Wencheng's mother Consort Yujiulü died, and he subsequently posthumously honored his parents as emperor and empress.</p>
<p>Around the new year 453, Emperor Wencheng, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a>, officially ended the prohibitions against Buddhism that Emperor Taiwu had instituted in 445 (which became known as the first of <a href="/wiki/Three_Disasters_of_Wu" title="Three Disasters of Wu">Three Disasters of Wu</a>), and he personally performed <a href="/wiki/Tonsure" title="Tonsure">tonsure</a> on five monks. However, as per the traditions instituted by Emperor Taiwu, he also publicly received <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taoist" title="Taoist">Taoist</a> amulets in spring 454.</p>
<p>In spring 453, Emperor Wencheng honored his <a href="/wiki/Wet_nurse" title="Wet nurse">wet nurse</a> Lady Chang as <a href="/wiki/Empress_dowager" title="Empress dowager">empress dowager</a>, and treated her brothers as if they were his biological uncles.</p>
<p>In fall 454, Emperor Wencheng's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concubine" title="Concubine">concubine</a> Consort Li gave birth to his oldest son <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Xianwen_of_Northern_Wei" title="Emperor Xianwen of Northern Wei">Tuoba Hong</a>. In 456, he created another imperial consort, <a href="/wiki/Empress_Feng_(Wencheng)" title="Empress Feng (Wencheng)">Consort Feng</a>, empress and created Tuoba Hong crown prince—and then, pursuant to Northern Wei tradition, ordered Tuoba Hong's mother Consort Li to commit suicide.</p>
<p>In winter 458, Emperor Wencheng launched a major attack against Rouran, but considered abandoning it when his troops encountered a snow storm. At Yuchi Juan's urging (arguing that a withdrawal would unduly signal weakness to Rouran), however, Emperor Wencheng continued, and while he was not able to deal a major defeat to Rouran's Chuluo Khan <a href="/wiki/Yujiul%C3%BC_Tuhezhen" title="Yujiulü Tuhezhen">Yujiulü Tuhezhen</a>, a number of Rouran tribesmen surrendered. Meanwhile, in his absence, his generals Feng Chiwen (封敕文) and Pi Baozi (皮豹子) engaged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liu_Song" title="Liu Song">Liu Song</a> forces near the Ji River (濟水, the route of which had been overtaken by the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_River" title="Yellow River">Yellow River</a> in modern times), with inconclusive results.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_reign">Late reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Wencheng_of_Northern_Wei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Late reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In summer 460, Emperor Wencheng's wet nurse Empress Dowager Chang died.</p>
<p>Also in summer 460, based on advise given by Cao An (曹安) the Marquess of Dingyang, Emperor Wencheng launched a major attack on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuyuhun_Kingdom" title="Tuyuhun Kingdom">Tuyuhun</a>'s khan <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Murong_Shiyin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Murong Shiyin (page does not exist)">Murong Shiyin</a> (慕容拾寅), who had drawn Northern Wei officials' ire by accepting commissions by both Northern Wei and Liu Song and by showing off his wealth. The commanding generals were Emperor Wencheng's brother Tuoba Xincheng (拓拔新成) the Prince of Yangping and Li Hui (李惠) the Duke of Nan Commandery. However, while the armies were able to capture a large number of livestock, Murong Shiyin received advance warning and was able to flee into the mountains, and the armies also suffered from illnesses.</p>
<p>In 464, while on one of his tours to various parts of the empire, Emperor Wencheng personally attended a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaoche" title="Gaoche">Gaoche</a> tribal ceremony to sacrifice to the heavens, and the Gaoche people were very pleased.</p>
<p>In summer 465, Emperor Wencheng died and was succeeded by his son Tuoba Hong (as Emperor Xianwen). Power soon fell into the hands of the official <a href="/wiki/Yifu_Hun" title="Yifu Hun">Yifu Hun</a>, who tried to monopolize power and executed a number of other officials, including Buliugu Li, but in 466 was in turn ambushed by Emperor Wencheng's wife Empress Dowager Feng.</p>
