<p><b>BookCrossing</b> (also <b>BC</b>, <b>BCing</b> or <b>BXing</b>) is defined as "the practice of leaving a <a href="/wiki/Book" title="Book">book</a> in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_place" title="Public place">public place</a> to be picked up and read by others, who then do likewise." The term is derived from bookcrossing.com, a free online book club which was founded to encourage the practice, aiming to "make the whole world a library."</p>
<p>The 'crossing' or exchanging of books may take any of a number of forms, including wild-releasing books in public, direct swaps with other members of the websites, or "book rings" in which books travel in a set order to participants who want to read a certain book. The community aspect of BookCrossing.com has grown and expanded in ways that were not expected at the outset, in the form of blog or forum discussions, mailing lists and annual conventions throughout the world.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BookCrossing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Leaving reading materials in public places when no longer needed has long been a silent means of communication and sociability amongst bibliophiles. Ron Hornbaker conceived the idea for what is now known as BookCrossing in March 2001<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sitesay_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-sitesay-2">[2]</a></sup> and enlisted business partners and co-founders Bruce and Heather Pedersen<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bookcrossing-official_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-bookcrossing-official-3">[3]</a></sup> to launch BookCrossing.com on April 21, 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bookcrossing-init_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-bookcrossing-init-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>After two years the website had over 113,000 members and by 2004 it was prominent enough to be referenced in an episode of the <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> <a href="/wiki/Soap_opera" title="Soap opera">soap opera</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Neighbours" title="Neighbours">Neighbours</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neighbours_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-neighbours-5">[5]</a></sup> The same year it appeared as a new word in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concise_Oxford_Dictionary" title="Concise Oxford Dictionary">Concise Oxford Dictionary</a>,</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coed_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-coed-6">[6]</a></sup> although as of 2017 only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collins_Dictionary" title="Collins Dictionary">Collins</a> of the major online dictionaries retained it as a word.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Membership surpassed 1,000,000 by March 2012 and the registered book count exceeded 8,500,000. By 2017, there were over 1.7 million members and over 11.7 million books travelling through 132 countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2007 <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> became the first country to give the practice official status, designating 2,000 locations in the country as 'hotspots', similar to Official BookCrossing Zones, in an initiative launched with the <a href="/wiki/National_Library,_Singapore" title="National Library, Singapore">National Library of Singapore</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sing_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-sing-12">[12]</a></sup> The world's first official International BookCrossing Day took place on 21 April 2014.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BookCrossing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In May 2005 BookCrossing.com won two People's Voice awards in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Webby_Awards" title="Webby Awards">Webby Awards</a> for best community website and best social/networking website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webbc_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-webbc-13">[13]</a></sup> BookCrossing was also featured in a <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio" title="BBC Radio">BBC Radio</a> project broadcast as 84 Book Crossing Road, which involved releasing 84 copies of <a href="/wiki/Helene_Hanff" title="Helene Hanff">Helene Hanff</a>'s book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/84_Charing_Cross_Road" title="84 Charing Cross Road">84 Charing Cross Road</a></i> around the world. The programme was nominated for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sony_Radio_Academy_Awards" title="Sony Radio Academy Awards">Sony Radio Academy Award</a> in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sraa_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-sraa-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="About">About</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BookCrossing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: About">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Books are "set free" into public places...</div>

<p>Anyone who wishes to officially participate in "releasing" books, whether leaving it in a public place or passing it on to a friend, may register on the BookCrossing.com website,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freec_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-freec-15">[15]</a></sup> although there is the option to remain anonymous when "catching" or recording the find of a book. BookCrossing.com users can 'go hunting', where a member will go to the website to view a list of books that have recently been "released", then go to the location it was left to "catch" it. Books may also be left at Official BookCrossing Zones" (OBCZs), which are located in certain coffee shops, cafes, restaurants and other public places. The purpose of these locations is to get current members in the area to leave books to share with the public. This also advertises BookCrossing and creates more members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spi_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-spi-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conventions_and_unconventions">Conventions and unconventions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BookCrossing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conventions and unconventions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is a BookCrossing anniversary convention every April,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-con_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-con-17">[17]</a></sup> where BookCrossers go to enjoy organized literary-related events and release books together. The location of the convention changes each year: Melbourne, Australia (2014), Gothenburg, Sweden (2013), Dublin, Ireland (2012), Washington, D.C., USA (2011), Amsterdam, The Netherlands (2010), Christchurch, New Zealand (2009), London, UK (2008), Charleston, SC, USA (2007), Toronto, Canada (2006), Fort Worth, TX, USA (2005), St. Louis, MO, USA (2004). Many unofficial conventions or "unconventions" take place at other locations and times throughout the year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uncon_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-uncon-18">[18]</a></sup> making it easier for BookCrossers who cannot travel internationally for the convention to gather and share their love of books.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy_and_criticism">Controversy and criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BookCrossing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Controversy and criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2003, BookCrossing was criticized by novelist Jessica Adams, who claimed that books were being "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Devalued" title="Devalued">devalued</a>" by the website as BookCrossing could lead to lower sales of books and, therefore, the reduction in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royalties" title="Royalties">royalties</a> being paid to authors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Most BookCrossers dispute this argument, however. They claim that the website introduces readers to authors and genres that they have not read before, that the website encourages more people to take up or reclaim reading as a <a href="/wiki/Hobby" title="Hobby">hobby</a>, and that some members, having read a book that they have enjoyed, will buy extra copies to distribute through BookCrossing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2005, Caroline Martin, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Managing_director" title="Managing director">managing director</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Publishers" title="Publishers">publisher</a> Harper Press, said in a speech that "book publishing as a whole has its very own potential Napster crisis in the growing practice of bookcrossing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anov_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-anov-21">[21]</a></sup> BookCrossers rebut the link to Napster, saying that whilst music filesharing involves duplicating audio files countless times, BookCrossing doesn't involve duplicating books (and also does not involve violating copyright, as books can be sold or given away freely without permission of the publisher being needed). The founder of BookCrossing, Ron Hornbaker, originally wondered if people would make this comparison when BookCrossing was first launched.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
