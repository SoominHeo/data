<p><b>Nyctaginaceae</b>, the four o'clock family, is a family of around 33 genera and 290 species of <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plants</a>, widely distributed in tropical and subtropical regions, with a few representatives in temperate regions. The family has a unique fruit type, called an "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthocarp" title="Anthocarp">anthocarp</a>", and many genera have extremely large (&gt;100 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%9Cm" title="Μm">µm</a>) pollen grains.</p>
<p>The family has been almost universally recognized by plant taxonomists. The <a href="/wiki/APG_II_system" title="APG II system">APG II system</a> (2003; unchanged from the <a href="/wiki/APG_system" title="APG system">APG system</a> of 1998), assigns it to the order <a href="/wiki/Caryophyllales" title="Caryophyllales">Caryophyllales</a> in the clade <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Core_eudicots" title="Core eudicots">core eudicots</a>.</p>
<p>A phylogenetic study by Levin has justified the combination of <i>Selinocarpus</i> and <i>Ammocodon</i> into the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Acleisanthes" title="Acleisanthes">Acleisanthes</a></i>. The genus <i>Izabalea</i> is now considered a synonym of <i><a href="/wiki/Agonandra" title="Agonandra">Agonandra</a></i>, a genus in <a href="/wiki/Opiliaceae" title="Opiliaceae">Opiliaceae</a>. A more recent study by Douglas and Manos clarified the relationships among almost all of the genera in the family and demonstrated that a substantial diversification of herbaceous genera has occurred in arid North America. Many genera of Nyctaginaceae possess unusual characters. Notable examples include sticky bands on the stems between the nodes, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleistogamous" title="Cleistogamous">cleistogamous</a> flowers (which self-pollinate without opening), or <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gypsophily&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gypsophily (page does not exist)">gypsophily</a>, the ability to grow on soils with a high concentration of <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nyctaginaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The family contains one food crop, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mauka" title="Mauka">mauka</a> (<i>Mirabilis extensa</i>), a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Root_vegetable" title="Root vegetable">root vegetable</a> of minor local importance in the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a>. Garden Four-O'Clocks <i><a href="/wiki/Mirabilis_jalapa" title="Mirabilis jalapa">Mirabilis jalapa</a></i> species are grown as <a href="/wiki/Ornamental_plant" title="Ornamental plant">ornamental plants</a>, as are species of <i><a href="/wiki/Bougainvillea" title="Bougainvillea">Bougainvillea</a></i> (<i>Bougainvillea glabra, B. spectabilis</i>, and numerous hybrids), <i><a href="/wiki/Bougainvillea" title="Bougainvillea">Bougainvillea</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Abronia_(plant)" title="Abronia (plant)">Abronia</a></i> are commonly cultivated in warmer regions.</p>
