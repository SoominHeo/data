<p>This technique may be extended to any algebraic denominator, by multiplying the numerator and the denominator by all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algebraic_conjugate" title="Algebraic conjugate">algebraic conjugates</a> of the denominator, and expanding the new denominator into the <a href="/wiki/Field_norm" title="Field norm">norm</a> of the old denominator. However, except in special cases, the resulting fractions may have huge numerators and denominators, and, therefore, the technique is generally used only in the above elementary cases.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Generalizations">Generalizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rationalisation_(mathematics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Generalizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rationalisation can be extended to all <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_number" title="Algebraic number">algebraic numbers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_function" title="Algebraic function">algebraic functions</a> (as an application of <a href="/wiki/Norm_form" title="Norm form">norm forms</a>). For example, to rationalise a <a href="/wiki/Cube_root" title="Cube root">cube root</a>, two linear factors involving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cube_roots_of_unity" title="Cube roots of unity">cube roots of unity</a> should be used, or equivalently a quadratic factor.</p>
