<p>A <b>mill</b> is a device that breaks <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a> materials into smaller pieces by grinding, crushing, or cutting. Such <a href="/wiki/Comminution" title="Comminution">comminution</a> is an important <a href="/wiki/Unit_operation" title="Unit operation">unit operation</a> in many <a href="/wiki/Process_(engineering)" title="Process (engineering)">processes</a>. There are many different types of mills and many types of materials processed in them. Historically mills were powered by hand (e.g., via a <a href="/wiki/Crank_(mechanism)" title="Crank (mechanism)">hand crank</a>), <a href="/wiki/Working_animal" title="Working animal">working animal</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Horse_mill" title="Horse mill">horse mill</a>), wind (<a href="/wiki/Windmill" title="Windmill">windmill</a>) or water (<a href="/wiki/Watermill" title="Watermill">watermill</a>). Today they are usually powered by <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>.</p>
<p>The grinding of solid matters occurs under exposure of mechanical forces that trench the structure by overcoming of the interior bonding forces. After the grinding the state of the solid is changed: the grain size, the grain size disposition and the grain shape.</p>
<p>Milling also refers to the process of breaking down, separating, sizing, or classifying aggregate material. For instance rock crushing or grinding to produce uniform aggregate size for construction purposes, or separation of rock, soil or aggregate material for the purposes of structural fill or land reclamation activities. Aggregate milling processes are also used to remove or separate contamination or moisture from aggregate or soil and to produce "dry fills" prior to transport or structural filling.</p>
<p>Grinding may serve the following purposes in engineering:</p>
<ul>
<li>increase of the surface area of a solid</li>
<li>manufacturing of a solid with a desired grain size</li>
<li>pulping of resources</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grinding_laws">Grinding laws</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mill_(grinding)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Grinding laws">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grinding_machines">Grinding machines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mill_(grinding)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Grinding machines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In materials processing a <b>grinder</b> is a machine for producing fine particle size reduction through <a href="/wiki/Wear" title="Wear">attrition</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compression_(physical)" title="Compression (physical)">compressive</a> <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">forces</a> at the grain size level. See also <a href="/wiki/Crusher" title="Crusher">crusher</a> for mechanisms producing larger particles. In general, grinding processes require a relatively large amount of energy; for this reason, an experimental method to measure the energy used locally during milling with different machines was recently proposed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ball_mill">Ball mill</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mill_(grinding)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ball mill">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A typical type of fine grinder is the <a href="/wiki/Ball_mill" title="Ball mill">ball mill</a>. A slightly inclined or horizontal rotating cylinder is partially filled with <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">balls</a>, usually <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">stone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>, which grind material to the necessary fineness by <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a> and impact with the tumbling balls. Ball mills normally operate with an approximate ball charge of 30%. Ball mills are characterized by their smaller (comparatively) diameter and longer length, and often have a length 1.5 to 2.5 times the diameter. The feed is at one end of the cylinder and the discharge is at the other. Ball mills are commonly used in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Portland_cement" title="Portland cement">Portland cement</a> and finer grinding stages of mineral processing, one example being the <a href="/wiki/Sepro_Mineral_Systems#Sepro_Tyre_Drive_Grinding_Mills" title="Sepro Mineral Systems">Sepro tyre drive Grinding Mill</a>. Industrial ball mills can be as large as 8.5 m (28 ft) in diameter with a 22 MW motor,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> drawing approximately 0.0011% of the total world's power (see <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_electricity_consumption" title="List of countries by electricity consumption">List of countries by electricity consumption</a>). However, small versions of ball mills can be found in laboratories where they are used for grinding sample material for quality assurance.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rod_mill">Rod mill</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mill_(grinding)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rod mill">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A rotating drum causes friction and attrition between steel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)" title="Cylinder (geometry)">rods</a> and ore particles.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> But note that the term 'rod mill' is also used as a synonym for a <a href="/wiki/Slitting_mill" title="Slitting mill">slitting mill</a>, which makes rods of iron or other metal. Rod mills are less common than ball mills for grinding minerals.</p>
<p>The rods used in the mill, usually a high-carbon steel, can vary in both the length and the diameter. However, the smaller the rods, the larger is the total surface area and hence, the greater the grinding efficiency<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Autogenous_mill">Autogenous mill</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mill_(grinding)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Autogenous mill">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Autogenous or autogenic mills are so-called due to the self-grinding of the ore: a rotating drum throws larger rocks of ore in a cascading motion which causes impact breakage of larger rocks and compressive grinding of finer particles. It is similar in operation to a SAG mill as described below but does not use steel balls in the mill. Also known as ROM or "Run Of Mine" grinding.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SAG_mill">SAG mill</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mill_(grinding)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: SAG mill">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Principle_of_SAG_Mill_operation.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="464" data-file-width="566" height="287" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Principle_of_SAG_Mill_operation.jpg/350px-Principle_of_SAG_Mill_operation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Principle_of_SAG_Mill_operation.jpg/525px-Principle_of_SAG_Mill_operation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/08/Principle_of_SAG_Mill_operation.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Principle_of_SAG_Mill_operation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Principle of SAG Mill operation</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>SAG is an acronym for Semi-Autogenous Grinding. SAG mills are essentially autogenous mills, but utilize grinding balls to aid in grinding like in a ball mill. A SAG mill is generally used as a primary or first stage grinding solution. SAG mills use a ball charge of 8 to 21%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The largest SAG mill is 42' (12.8m) in diameter, powered by a 28 MW (38,000 HP) motor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A SAG mill with a 44' (13.4m) diameter and a power of 35 MW (47,000 HP) has been designed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Attrition between grinding balls and ore particles causes grinding of finer particles. SAG mills are characterized by their large diameter and short length as compared to ball mills. The inside of the mill is lined with lifting plates to lift the material inside the mill, where it then falls off the plates onto the rest of the ore charge. SAG mills are primarily used at gold, copper and platinum mines with applications also in the lead, zinc, silver, alumina and nickel industries.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pebble_mill">Pebble mill</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mill_(grinding)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pebble mill">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A rotating drum causes friction and attrition between rock <a href="/wiki/Pebble" title="Pebble">pebbles</a> and ore particles. May be used where product contamination by <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> from <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> balls must be avoided. Quartz or silica is commonly used because it is inexpensive to obtain.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High_pressure_grinding_rolls">High pressure grinding rolls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mill_(grinding)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: High pressure grinding rolls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A high pressure grinding roll, often referred to as <a class="external text" href="https://www.researchgate.net/publication/234128538_Energy_Consumption_in_a_Phosphate_Concentrator_Jhamarkotra_India" rel="nofollow">HPGRs or roller press</a>, consists out of two rollers with the same dimensions, which are rotating against each other with the same circumferential speed. The special feeding of bulk material through a hopper leads to a material bed between the two rollers. The bearing units of one roller can move linearly and are pressed against the material bed by springs or hydraulic cylinders. The pressures in the material bed are greater than 50 MPa (7,000 <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">PSI</a>). In general they achieve 100 to 300 MPa. By this the material bed is compacted to a solid volume portion of more than 80%.</p>
<p>The roller press has a certain similarity to roller crushers and roller presses for the compacting of powders, but purpose, construction and operation mode are different.</p>
<p>Extreme <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> causes the particles inside of the compacted material bed to <a href="/wiki/Fracture" title="Fracture">fracture</a> into finer particles and also causes microfracturing at the grain size level. Compared to ball mills HPGRs achieve a 30 to 50% lower specific energy consumption, although they are not as common as ball mills since they are a newer technology.</p>
<p>A similar type of intermediate crusher is the edge runner, which consists of a circular pan with two or more heavy <a href="/wiki/Wheel" title="Wheel">wheels</a> known as mullers rotating within it; material to be crushed is shoved underneath the wheels using attached <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plow" title="Plow">plow</a> blades.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vertical_shaft_impactor_mill_.28VSI_mill.29">Vertical shaft impactor mill (VSI mill)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mill_(grinding)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Vertical shaft impactor mill (VSI mill)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/VSI_mill" title="VSI mill">VSI mill</a></div>
<p>A VSI mill throws rock or ore particles against a wear plate by slinging them from a spinning center that rotates on a vertical shaft. This type of mill uses the same principle as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_crusher#Vertical_shaft_impactor_.28VSI.29" title="Rock crusher">VSI Crusher</a></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tower_mill">Tower mill</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mill_(grinding)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Tower mill">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tower mills, often called vertical mills, stirred mills or regrind mills, are a more efficient means of grinding material at smaller particle sizes, and can be used after ball mills in a grinding process. Like ball mills, grinding (steel) balls or pebbles are often added to stirred mills to help grind ore, however these mills contain a large screw mounted vertically to lift and grind material. In tower mills, there is no cascading action as in standard grinding mills. Stirred mills are also common for mixing quicklime (CaO) into a lime slurry. There are several advantages to the tower mill: low noise, efficient energy usage, and low operating costs.</p>
