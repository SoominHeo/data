<p>The <b>coat of arms of <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a></b> (<a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a>: <span lang="bg">Герб на България</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Bulgarian_and_Macedonian" title="Help:IPA for Bulgarian and Macedonian">[ɡɛrp nɐ bɐɫˈɡarijɐ]</a></span>) consists of a crowned golden <a href="/wiki/Lion_(heraldry)" title="Lion (heraldry)">lion rampant</a> over a dark red <a href="/wiki/Escutcheon_(heraldry)" title="Escutcheon (heraldry)">shield</a>; above the shield is the Bulgarian historical <a href="/wiki/Crown_(headgear)" title="Crown (headgear)">crown</a>. The shield is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supporters" title="Supporters">supported</a> by two crowned golden lions rampant; below the shield there is <a href="/wiki/Compartment_(heraldry)" title="Compartment (heraldry)">compartment</a> in the shape of <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> twigs and white bands with the national <a href="/wiki/Motto" title="Motto">motto</a> "Unity makes strength" inscribed on them.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coat_of_arms_of_Bulgaria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The current coat of arms of Bulgaria was adopted in 1997. The current arms are a slightly redesigned version of the coat of arms of Bulgaria from the period 1927–1946. Those arms were based on a similar earlier form, firstly used by <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I_of_Bulgaria" title="Ferdinand I of Bulgaria">Tsar Ferdinand I</a> (1887–1918) as his personal ruler's coat of arms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The previous emblem, which combined the traditional gold lion rampant with the pattern of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Coat of arms of the Soviet Union">coat of arms of the Soviet Union</a>, was abandoned since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Communist_Bulgaria" title="History of Communist Bulgaria">Communist rule</a> ended in the country in 1989. The new <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Bulgaria" title="Constitution of Bulgaria">Constitution of Bulgaria</a>, adopted in 1991, describes the Bulgarian coat of arms as follows:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">Art. 164. The coat of arms of the Republic of Bulgaria shall depict a gold lion rampant on a dark gules shield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>For many years, agreement on the design of the coat of arms was a source of great controversy in the Bulgarian government, as different parties argued over the design elements. The final design was legitimized in the Law for the coat of arms of the Republic of Bulgaria of 4 August 1998:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">Coat of arms of the Republic of Bulgaria
<p>Art. 1. The coat of arms of the Republic of Bulgaria shall be a state symbol expressing the independence and the sovereignty of the Bulgarian people and state.</p>
<p>Art. 2. (1) The coat of arms of the Republic of Bulgaria shall be a rampant golden crowned lion on a dark red field with the shape of a shield. Above the shield there shall be a big crown which originally were the crowns of Bulgarian Tsars (i.e. Emperors) of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Bulgarian_Empire" title="Second Bulgarian Empire">Second Bulgarian state</a> with five crosses and another cross over the crown. The shield shall be supported by two golden crowned rampant lions, turned towards the shield from the right and left heraldic sides. They shall stand above two crossed oak branches with fruits. Below the shield, over a white band put over the oak branches with a three-colored edge, shall be written with golden letters "Unity makes strength".</p>
<p>(2) The graphic and color image of the coat of arms according to the appendices shall be an inseparable part of this law.</p>
<p>Art. 3. (1) The coat of arms of the Republic of Bulgaria shall be depicted on the state seal in a way determined by a law for the state seal.</p>
<p>(2) The depiction of the coat of arms of the Republic of Bulgaria in other places as well as the reproduction of elements of the emblem on badges, commemoration medals etc. shall be admitted only by an act of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_of_Ministers_of_Bulgaria" title="Council of Ministers of Bulgaria">Council of Ministers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>

<p>The earliest example of a lion's image as the <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">heraldic symbol</a> of Bulgaria is documented in the <i>Lord Marshal's Roll</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> composed around 1294 AD and preserved in a copy from about 1640. In its first part under №15 is represented the coat of arms of <i>Le Rey de Bugrie</i> or the King of Bulgaria, most probably this of <a href="/wiki/Smilets_of_Bulgaria" title="Smilets of Bulgaria">Tsar Smilets</a> (1292–1298) or may be some of his recent predecessors. It consists of an argent lion rampant with golden crown over sable shield. In the end of 14th century an anonymous Arab traveller, who visited the capital of the Second Bulgarian Empire <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tarnovo" title="Tarnovo">Tarnovo</a>, saw and depicted three lions guardant passant gules painted on the round golden shields carried by the personal guards of <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Shishman_of_Bulgaria" title="Ivan Shishman of Bulgaria">Tsar Ivan Shishman</a> (1371–1395). His manuscript is now kept in the National Library of Morocco.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>After 1396, when all Bulgarian lands were subjugated by the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> but the Bulgarian crown was not given to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottoman_Dynasty" title="Ottoman Dynasty">Ottoman Dynasty</a> formally, the latter Bulgarian heraldic type as a sign of symbolically independent state was preserved in several European<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and Balkan<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> collections of coats of arms. Gradually, new and in some cases quite different versions appeared, but the lion remained the most widespread heraldic symbol of Bulgaria and its rulers. In the Illyrian collection of Korenich-Neorich from 1595 the three walking lions, usual for some earlier Western Europe's collections, were replaced by one red lion rampant over golden crowned shield. In the beginning of the 18th century the Illyrian heraldist <a href="/wiki/Pavao_Ritter_Vitezovi%C4%87" title="Pavao Ritter Vitezović">Pavao Ritter Vitezović</a> in two editions of his heraldic collection from 1701 and 1702 reversed the colours of this type and thus the lion became golden and the shield dark red.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>This variant was adopted by the famous painter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hristofor_Zhefarovich" title="Hristofor Zhefarovich">Hristofor Zhefarovich</a> in his <i>Stemmatographia</i>, printed in 1741. His version became the most influential among Bulgarian intellectuals and revolutionaries in the period of the <a href="/wiki/National_awakening_of_Bulgaria" title="National awakening of Bulgaria">national awakening of Bulgaria</a>, when the lion was considered and widely used as a major national symbol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> After the liberational <a href="/wiki/Russo-Turkish_War_(1877%E2%80%931878)" title="Russo-Turkish War (1877–1878)">Russo-Turkish War (1877–1878)</a> the coat of arms of Zhefarovich was laid in the base of the new state coat of arms, described in the <a href="/wiki/Tarnovo_Constitution" title="Tarnovo Constitution">Tarnovo Constitution</a> of 1879 as follows:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">Art. 21. The Bulgarian state coat of arms is a golden crowned lion on a dark red field. Above the field a princely coronet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>The type and the details regarding the state coat of arms were not described clearly and were not standardized by a special Act. Because of this, for several decades they took different forms: lesser form; lesser form without supporters, compartment and motto, but covered with mantle from 1879 to 1880; greater form with supporters, bearing two national flags, compartment, motto and mantle from 1881 to 1927; middle form with supporters, compartment and motto from 1915 until 1918/20. Besides these there were different variations within these types. This perplexed situation was resolved by a special parliamentary commission, which sat after 1923. In 1927 it legitimated the middle form of the coat of arms, similar to these used as personal coats of arms by Bulgarian monarchs Ferdinand I and his son <a href="/wiki/Boris_III_of_Bulgaria" title="Boris III of Bulgaria">Tsar Boris III</a> (1918–1943), but excluding all dynastic elements and preserving only the pure state symbolism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following 1944, new times began for the Bulgarian heraldry. In the communist era, the traditional type of coat of arms was replaced by the emblem, which preserved the golden lion rampant placed over a non-historically-justifiable oval azure field, but encircled by the ears of wheat, folded by banners, a gear-wheel, a five-pointed red star and some other elements. This composition was derived principally from the pattern of the emblem of the Soviet Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> After the breakdown of the totalitarian dictatorship in 1989 and several years of fierce partisan disputes, the traditional middle coat of arms from the period 1927–1946 was restored with some minor changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The crown on top of the shield, and thus upon the shielded lion, according to the Coat of Arms of the Republic of Bulgaria Act, is supposed to be not that of the last <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Independent_Bulgaria" title="History of Independent Bulgaria">Bulgarian monarchy</a> (1879–1946), but that of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Bulgarian_Empire" title="Second Bulgarian Empire">Second Bulgarian Empire</a> (1185–1396). This empire was established by the brothers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theodore_Peter_of_Bulgaria" title="Theodore Peter of Bulgaria">Peter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Asen_I_of_Bulgaria" title="Ivan Asen I of Bulgaria">Asen</a>, after it was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vlach-Bulgarian_Rebellion" title="Vlach-Bulgarian Rebellion">freed</a> from <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> control in the end of the 12th century, and it was subjugated by the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottomans</a> in the very end of the 14th century. In fact it significantly differs from the crowns known from medieval portraits such as these from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetraevangelia_of_Ivan_Alexander" title="Tetraevangelia of Ivan Alexander">Tetraevangelia</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Alexander_of_Bulgaria" title="Ivan Alexander of Bulgaria">Tsar Ivan Alexander</a> (1331–1371).</p>
<p>There is a very popular belief that the three lions represent the three major portions and historical regions of Bulgaria — <a href="/wiki/Moesia" title="Moesia">Moesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(region)" title="Macedonia (region)">Macedonia</a>, which has no connection with the native historical tradition and the principles of heraldry.</p>
