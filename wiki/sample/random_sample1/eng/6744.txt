<p><b>Buddhist feminism</b> is a movement that seeks to improve the religious, legal, and <a href="/wiki/Social_status" title="Social status">social status</a> of women within <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>. It is an aspect of <a href="/wiki/Feminist_theology" title="Feminist theology">feminist theology</a> which seeks to advance and understand the <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">equality</a> of <a href="/wiki/Man" title="Man">men</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woman" title="Woman">women</a> morally, socially, spiritually, and in leadership from a Buddhist perspective. The Buddhist feminist <a href="/wiki/Rita_Gross" title="Rita Gross">Rita Gross</a> describes Buddhist feminism as "the radical practice of the co-humanity of women and men."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Buddhist_Feminism_as_a_Recent_Understanding">Buddhist Feminism as a Recent Understanding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Buddhist_feminism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Buddhist Feminism as a Recent Understanding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Parallels between Buddhism and Feminist understanding of equality between race, gender, class, sexuality and nationality have only recently begun to be explored. Buddhism's belief of understanding the truth of reality through practicing spiritual development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is beneficial to feminist theory, especially in comparison to other religions. These parallels are undergoing evaluation as religious understandings of feminism become increasingly scrutinized in society and popular discourse.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ordination">Ordination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Buddhist_feminism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ordination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some Buddhist feminists advocate for the <a href="/wiki/Ordination_of_women_in_Buddhism" title="Ordination of women in Buddhism">ordination of women in Buddhism</a>. The ordination of women in Buddhism is and has always been practiced in some Buddhist regions, such as East Asia, is being revived in some countries such as Sri Lanka, and is newly beginning in some Western countries to which Buddhism has recently spread, such as the United States.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Buddhist_feminism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jean Byrne argues that within this beginning of Buddhist Feminist understanding an ignorance of the reality of female and male roles may exist. Within her paper "Why I Am Not a Buddhist Feminist" she outlines the similarities shared between the two, and the possible increase in equality of genders, but highlights that because of this some misogynist and discriminatory aspects of Buddhism may be overlooked. The belief that Buddhism is a completely egalitarian religion concerns Byrne that this will overshadow some of the realities of Feminism in Buddhism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
