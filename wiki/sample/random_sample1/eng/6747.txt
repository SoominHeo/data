<p><b>Bulat Shalvovich Okudzhava</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">ÐÑÐ»Ð°ÌÑ Ð¨Ð°ÌÐ»Ð²Ð¾Ð²Ð¸Ñ ÐÐºÑÐ´Ð¶Ð°ÌÐ²Ð°</span>; <a href="/wiki/Georgian_language" title="Georgian language">Georgian</a>: <span lang="ka">áá£ááá¢ ááá£á¯ááá</span>) (May 9, 1924 â June 12, 1997) was a Soviet poet, writer, musician, novelist, and singer-songwriter of Georgian-Armenian ancestry. He was one of the founders of the Soviet genre called "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Author_song" title="Author song">author song</a>" (<i>Ð°Ð²ÑÐ¾ÑÑÐºÐ°Ñ Ð¿ÐµÑÐ½Ñ</i>, <i>avtorskaya pesnya</i>), or "guitar song", and the author of about 200 songs, set to his own poetry. His songs are a mixture of Russian poetic and folksong traditions and the French <i><a href="/wiki/Chansonnier" title="Chansonnier">chansonnier</a></i> style represented by such contemporaries of Okudzhava as <a href="/wiki/Georges_Brassens" title="Georges Brassens">Georges Brassens</a>. Though his songs were never overtly political (in contrast to those of some of his fellow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_bard" title="Soviet bard">Soviet bards</a>), the freshness and independence of Okudzhava's artistic voice presented a subtle challenge to Soviet cultural authorities, who were thus hesitant for many years to give official recognition to Okudzhava.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bulat_Okudzhava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bulat Okudzhava was born in Moscow on May 9, 1924 into a family of communists who had come from <a href="/wiki/Tbilisi" title="Tbilisi">Tbilisi</a>, the capital of <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a>, to study and to work for the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Communist Party</a>. The son of a <a href="/wiki/Georgians" title="Georgians">Georgian</a> father, Shalva Okudzhava, and an <a href="/wiki/Armenians" title="Armenians">Armenian</a> mother, Ashkhen Nalbandyan, Bulat Okudzhava spoke and wrote only in Russian. Okudzava's mother was the niece of a well-known Armenian poet, <a href="/wiki/Vahan_Terian" title="Vahan Terian">Vahan Terian</a>. His father served as a <a href="/wiki/Political_commissar" title="Political commissar">political commissar</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Civil_War" title="Russian Civil War">Civil War</a> and as a high-ranking Communist Party member under the protection of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grigol_Ordzhonikidze" title="Grigol Ordzhonikidze">Sergo Ordzhonikidze</a> later on. His uncle Vladimir Okudzhava was an anarchist and a terrorist who left The Russian Empire after a failed attempt to assassinate the <a href="/wiki/Kutaisi" title="Kutaisi">Kutaisi</a> governor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He was listed among the passengers of the infamous <a href="/wiki/Sealed_train" title="Sealed train">sealed train</a> that delivered <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Vladimir Lenin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grigory_Zinoviev" title="Grigory Zinoviev">Grigory Zinoviev</a> and other revolutionary leaders from Switzerland to Russia in 1917.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shalva Okudzhava was arrested in 1937 during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Purge" title="Great Purge">Great Purge</a>, accused of <a href="/wiki/Trotskyism" title="Trotskyism">trotskyism</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wrecking_(Soviet_crime)" title="Wrecking (Soviet crime)">wrecking</a> and executed shortly after, along with his two brothers. His wife was also arrested in 1939 Â«for <a href="/wiki/Anti-Sovietism" title="Anti-Sovietism">anti-Soviet deeds</a>Â» and sent to a labor camp of <a href="/wiki/Gulag" title="Gulag">Gulag</a>. Bulat returned to Tbilisi to live with his relatives. His mother was released in 1946, but arrested for the second time in 1949, spending another 5 years in labor camps. She was fully released in 1954 and <a href="/wiki/Rehabilitation_(Soviet)" title="Rehabilitation (Soviet)">rehabilitated</a> in 1956, along with her husband.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bykov_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bykov-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1941, at the age of 17, one year before his scheduled school graduation, he volunteered for the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a> <a href="/wiki/Infantry" title="Infantry">infantry</a>, and from 1942 he participated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Patriotic_War" title="Great Patriotic War">war</a> with Nazi Germany. After his discharge from the service in 1944, he returned to Tbilisi where he passed his high school graduation exams and enrolled at <a href="/wiki/Tbilisi_State_University" title="Tbilisi State University">Tbilisi State University</a>, graduating in 1950. After graduating, he worked as a teacher, first in a rural school in the village of <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Shamordino" title="Shamordino">Shamordino</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kaluga_Oblast" title="Kaluga Oblast">Kaluga district</a>, and later in the city of <a href="/wiki/Kaluga" title="Kaluga">Kaluga</a> itself.</p>
<p>In 1956, three years after the death of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a>, Okudzhava returned to Moscow, where he worked first as an editor in the publishing house "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Young_Guard_(publisher)" title="Young Guard (publisher)">Young Guard</a>," and later as the head of the poetry division at the most prominent national literary weekly in the former USSR, <a href="/wiki/Literaturnaya_Gazeta" title="Literaturnaya Gazeta">Literaturnaya Gazeta</a> ("Literary Newspaper"). It was then, in the middle of the 1950s, that he began to compose songs and to perform them, accompanying himself on a <a href="/wiki/Russian_guitar" title="Russian guitar">Russian guitar</a>.</p>
<p>Soon he was giving concerts. He only employed a few <a href="/wiki/Chord_(music)" title="Chord (music)">chords</a> and had no formal training in music, but he possessed an exceptional melodic gift, and the intelligent lyrics of his songs blended perfectly with his music and his voice.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> His songs were praised by his friends, and amateur recordings were made. These unofficial recordings were widely copied as <a href="/wiki/Magnitizdat" title="Magnitizdat">magnitizdat</a>, and spread across the USSR and <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, where other young people picked up guitars and started singing the songs for themselves. In 1969, his lyrics appeared in the classic Soviet film <i><a href="/wiki/White_Sun_of_the_Desert" title="White Sun of the Desert">White Sun of the Desert</a></i>.</p>
<p>Though Okudzhava's songs were not published by any official media organization until the late 1970s, they quickly achieved enormous popularity, especially among the <a href="/wiki/Intelligentsia" title="Intelligentsia">intelligentsia</a> â mainly in the USSR at first, but soon among Russian-speakers in other countries as well.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Nabokov" title="Vladimir Nabokov">Vladimir Nabokov</a>, for example, cited his <i>Sentimental March</i> in the novel <i><a href="/wiki/Ada_or_Ardor:_A_Family_Chronicle" title="Ada or Ardor: A Family Chronicle">Ada or Ardor</a></i>.</p>
<p>Okudzhava, however, regarded himself primarily as a poet and claimed that his musical recordings were insignificant. During the 1980s, he also published a great deal of prose (his novel <i>The Show is Over</i> won him the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Booker-Open_Russia_Prize" title="Booker-Open Russia Prize">Russian Booker Prize</a> in 1994). By the 1980s, recordings of Okudzhava performing his songs finally began to be officially released in the Soviet Union, and many volumes of his poetry were also published. In 1991, he was awarded the <a href="/wiki/USSR_State_Prize" title="USSR State Prize">USSR State Prize</a>. He supported the reform movement in the USSR and in October 1993, signed the <a href="/wiki/Letter_of_Forty-Two" title="Letter of Forty-Two">Letter of Forty-Two</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-letter_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-letter-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Okudzhava died in Paris on June 12, 1997, and is buried in the <a href="/wiki/Vagankovo_Cemetery" title="Vagankovo Cemetery">Vagankovo Cemetery</a> in Moscow. A monument marks the building at 43 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arbat" title="Arbat">Arbat Street</a> where he lived. His <a href="/wiki/Dacha" title="Dacha">dacha</a> in <a href="/wiki/Peredelkino" title="Peredelkino">Peredelkino</a> is now a museum that is open to the public.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Minor_planet" title="Minor planet">minor planet</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3149_Okudzhava" title="3149 Okudzhava">3149 Okudzhava</a>, discovered by Czech astronomer <a href="/wiki/Zde%C5%88ka_V%C3%A1vrov%C3%A1" title="ZdeÅka VÃ¡vrovÃ¡">ZdeÅka VÃ¡vrovÃ¡</a> in 1981 is named after him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> His songs remain very popular and frequently performed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> They are also used for teaching Russian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:20%; padding: 6px; border: 2px solid #aaa; font-size: 85%; background-color: Cornsilk;">

<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">

<p>When, like a beast, the snow storm roars,<br/>
when, in a rage, it howls,<br/>
You do not have to lock the doors,<br/>

<br/>
When on a lasting trip you go<br/>

<br/>
As you leave home one quiet night,<br/>

mix up the burning pinewood light<br/>

<br/>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bulat_Okudzhava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Okudzhava, like most bards, did not come from a musical background. He learned basic guitar skills with the help of some friends. He also knew how to play basic chords on a piano.</p>
<p>Okudzhava tuned his <a href="/wiki/Russian_guitar" title="Russian guitar">Russian guitar</a> to the "Russian tuning" of D'-G'-C-D-g-b-d' (thickest to thinnest string), and often lowered it by one or two tones to better accommodate his voice. He played in a classical manner, usually finger picking the strings in an ascending/descending <a href="/wiki/Arpeggio" title="Arpeggio">arpeggio</a> or waltz pattern, with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alternating_bass" title="Alternating bass">alternating bass</a> line picked by the thumb.</p>
<p>Initially Okudzhava was taught three basic chords, and towards the end of his life he claimed to know a total of seven.</p>
<p>Many of Okudzhava's songs are in the key of C minor (with downtuning B flat or A minor), centering on the C minor chord (X00X011, thickest to thinnest string), then progressing to a D 7 (00X0433), then either an E-flat minor (X55X566) or C major (55X5555). In addition to the aforementioned chords, the E-flat major chord (X55X567) was often featured in songs in a major key, usually C major (with downtuning B-flat or A major).</p>
<p>By the nineties, Okudzhava adopted the increasingly popular six string guitar but retained the Russian tuning, subtracting the fourth string, which was convenient to his style of playing.</p>
