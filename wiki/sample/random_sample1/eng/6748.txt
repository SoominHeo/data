<p><b>Boulogne-sur-Mer</b> (<small>French pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[bu.lɔɲ.syʁ.mɛʁ]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata"> (<a href="/wiki/File:Fr-Paris--Boulogne-sur-Mer.ogg" title="File:Fr-Paris--Boulogne-sur-Mer.ogg"><img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a> <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2c/Fr-Boulogne-sur-Mer.ogg" title="Fr-Boulogne-sur-Mer.ogg">listen</a>)</small>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i>Gesoriacum</i> or <i>Bononia</i>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <span lang="nl" xml:lang="nl"><i>Bonen</i></span>), often called <b>Boulogne</b> (<span class="nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">UK</a></small> <span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/uː/ long 'oo' in 'food'">uː</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɔɪ/ 'oy' in 'boy'">ɔɪ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>), is a city in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_France" title="Northern France">Northern France</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Subprefectures_in_France" title="Subprefectures in France">sub-prefecture</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_France" title="Departments of France">department</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pas-de-Calais" title="Pas-de-Calais">Pas-de-Calais</a>. Boulogne lies on the <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te_d%27Opale" title="Côte d'Opale">Côte d'Opale</a>, a tourist coast on the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>, and is the most-visited location in its region after the <a href="/wiki/Lille" title="Lille">Lille</a> conurbation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Boulogne is its department's second-largest city after <a href="/wiki/Calais" title="Calais">Calais</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and the 60th largest in France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is also the country's largest fishing port, specialising in <a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herring</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Boulogne was the major <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> port for trade and communication with <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Britain</a>. After a period of Germanic presence following the collapse of the Empire, Boulogne was at the centre of an <a href="/wiki/County_of_Boulogne" title="County of Boulogne">eponymous county</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France" title="Kingdom of France">Kingdom of France</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, and was occupied by the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a> numerous times due to conflict between the two nations.</p>
<p>The city's 12th-century belfry is recognised by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> as a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nordmag.fr_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nordmag.fr-5">[5]</a></sup> while another popular attraction is the marine conservation centre <a href="/wiki/Nausica%C3%A4_Centre_National_de_la_Mer" title="Nausicaä Centre National de la Mer">Nausicaa</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boulogne-sur-Mer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> name <i>Boulogne</i> derives from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>Bononia</i>, which was also the Roman name for <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">Bologna</a> in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. Both places—and <a href="/wiki/Vindobona" title="Vindobona">Vindobona</a> (<a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>)—are thought to have derived from native <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celtic_placenames" title="Celtic placenames">Celtic placenames</a>, with <i>bona</i> possibly meaning "foundation", "citadel", or "granary".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The French epithet <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sur" title="wikt:sur">sur</a>-<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mer" title="wikt:mer">Mer</a></i> ("on-the-sea") distinguishes the city from <a href="/wiki/Boulogne-Billancourt" title="Boulogne-Billancourt">Boulogne-Billancourt</a> on the edge of <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>. In turn, the <i>Boulogne</i> in Boulogne-Billancourt originates from a church there dedicated to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Notre-Dame_de_Boulogne" title="Notre-Dame de Boulogne">Notre-Dame de Boulogne</a>, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Our_Lady_of" title="Our Lady of">Our Lady of</a> Boulogne[-sur-Mer]".</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boulogne-sur-Mer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Boulogne-sur-Mer is in Northern France, at the edge of the Channel and in the mouth of the river "<a href="/wiki/Liane_(river)" title="Liane (river)">Liane</a>". As the crow flies, Boulogne is approximately at 30 kilometres (19 miles) from <a href="/wiki/Calais" title="Calais">Calais</a>, 50 kilometres (31 miles) from <a href="/wiki/Folkestone" title="Folkestone">Folkestone</a>, 100 kilometres (62 miles) from <a href="/wiki/Lille" title="Lille">Lille</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amiens" title="Amiens">Amiens</a>, 150 kilometres (93 miles) from <a href="/wiki/Rouen" title="Rouen">Rouen</a> and 215 kilometres (134 miles) from <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>.</p>
<p>Boulogne is a relatively important city of the North, exercising an influence on the "<a href="/wiki/Boulonnais_(land_area)" title="Boulonnais (land area)">Boulonnais</a>" territory (74 towns and villages which surround Boulogne). The coast consists of important tourist natural sites, like the capes <a href="/wiki/Cap_Gris_Nez" title="Cap Gris Nez">Gris Nez</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cap_Blanc_Nez" title="Cap Blanc Nez">Blanc Nez</a> (which are the closest points of France to England), and attractive seaside resorts like <a href="/wiki/Wimereux" title="Wimereux">Wimereux</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wissant" title="Wissant">Wissant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neufch%C3%A2tel-Hardelot" title="Neufchâtel-Hardelot">Hardelot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Le_Touquet" title="Le Touquet">Le Touquet</a>. The hinterland is mainly rural and agricultural.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boulogne-sur-Mer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Boulogne is close to the <a href="/wiki/A16_autoroute" title="A16 autoroute">A16 motorway</a> (Paris-Amiens-Calais-Dunkerque). Metropolitan bus services are operated by "Marinéo". The company <a href="/wiki/Flixbus" title="Flixbus">Flixbus</a> propose a bus line connecting Paris to Boulogne. There are coach services to Calais and Dunkerque.</p>
<p>The city has railway stations, which the most important is <a href="/wiki/Gare_de_Boulogne-Ville" title="Gare de Boulogne-Ville">Boulogne-Ville station</a>, located in the south of the city. <a href="/wiki/Gare_de_Boulogne-Tintelleries" title="Gare de Boulogne-Tintelleries">Boulogne-Tintelleries station</a> is used for regional transit. It is located near the university and the city centre. The former <a href="/wiki/Gare_de_Boulogne-Maritime" title="Gare de Boulogne-Maritime">Boulogne-Maritime</a> and Boulogne-Aéroglisseurs stations served as a boat connection (to England) for the railway.</p>
<p>Boulogne currently has no cross channel ferry services since the closure of the route to <a href="/wiki/Dover" title="Dover">Dover</a> by <a href="/wiki/LD_Lines" title="LD Lines">LD Lines</a> in 2010.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Urbanization">Urbanization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boulogne-sur-Mer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Urbanization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The city is divided into several parts :</p>
<ul>
<li>City centre : groups historic and administrative buildings, and also accommodations, stores, banks, churches, pedestrian streets and places.</li>
<li>Fortified town : old-town where are a lot of historic monuments (the castle-museum, the basilica, the belfry, the imperial palace) and also the city hall and the courthouse. it is surrounded by 13th-century ramparts very appreciated today by walkers.</li>
<li>Gambetta-Sainte-Beuve : tourist area situated in the northwest of the city, on the edge of the beach and the recreational harbour.</li>
<li>Capécure : economic and industrial area, situated in the west of the city, around the harbour.</li>
<li>Saint-Pierre (<i>Saint Peter</i>) : former neighborhood of the fishermen, destroyed during the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and reconstructed after.</li>
<li>Chemin Vert (<i>Green path</i>) : zone borned in the 1950s, knowing today poverty and unemployment. it is the neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Franck_Rib%C3%A9ry" title="Franck Ribéry">Franck Ribéry</a>.</li>
<li>Dernier Sou (<i>Last penny</i>) : residential area situated in the east of the city.</li>
<li>Beaurepaire (<i>Beautiful hideout</i>) : residential area situated in the north of the city.</li>
<li>Bréquerecque : residential area situated in the south of the city.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boulogne-sur-Mer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_the_city">Origin of the city</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boulogne-sur-Mer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Origin of the city">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The foundation of the city known to the Romans as <b>Gesoriacum</b> is credited to the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Celtic_tribes" title="List of Celtic tribes">Celtic</a> <a href="/wiki/Boii" title="Boii">Boii</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the past, it was sometimes conflated with <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Caesar</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portus_Itius" title="Portus Itius">Portus Itius</a>, but that is now thought to have been a site near <a href="/wiki/Calais" title="Calais">Calais</a> which has since silted up. From the time of <a href="/wiki/Claudius" title="Claudius">Claudius</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Roman_conquest_of_Britain" title="Roman conquest of Britain">invasion</a> in <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span> 43, Gesoriacum formed the major port connecting the rest of the empire to <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Britain</a>. It was the chief base of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_navy" title="Roman navy">Roman navy</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Classis_Britannica" title="Classis Britannica">Britannic fleet</a> until the <a href="/wiki/Carausian_Revolt" title="Carausian Revolt">rebellion</a> of its admiral <a href="/wiki/Carausius" title="Carausius">Carausius</a> in 286. As part of the imperial response, the <a href="/wiki/Caesar_(title)" title="Caesar (title)">junior emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Constantius_Chlorus" title="Constantius Chlorus">Constantius Chlorus</a> successfully besieged it by land and sea in 293.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flagspot.net_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-flagspot.net-9">[9]</a></sup> The name of the settlement was changed to <b>Bononia</b> at some point between the sack of Gesoriacum and 310, possibly as a consequence of its refounding or possibly by the replacement of the sacked and lower-lying city by another nearby community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city was an important town of the <a href="/wiki/Morini" title="Morini">Morini</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Zosimus" title="Zosimus">Zosimus</a> called it <i>Germanorum</i> ("Germanic-speaking") at the end of the 4th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boulogne-sur-Mer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eustache_de_Boulogne-Bayeux.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="549" data-file-width="407" height="270" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Eustache_de_Boulogne-Bayeux.png/200px-Eustache_de_Boulogne-Bayeux.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Eustache_de_Boulogne-Bayeux.png/300px-Eustache_de_Boulogne-Bayeux.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Eustache_de_Boulogne-Bayeux.png/400px-Eustache_de_Boulogne-Bayeux.png 2x" width="200"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eustache_de_Boulogne-Bayeux.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Eustace_II,_Count_of_Boulogne" title="Eustace II, Count of Boulogne">Eustace II, Count of Boulogne</a>, as shown on the <a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry" title="Bayeux Tapestry">Bayeux Tapestry</a>.</div>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/County_of_Boulogne" title="County of Boulogne">County of Boulogne</a></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> Boulogne was the capital of an <a href="/wiki/Count_of_Boulogne" title="Count of Boulogne">eponymous county</a>, founded in the mid-9th century. An important Count, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eustace_II_of_Boulogne" title="Eustace II of Boulogne">Eustace II</a>, assisted <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a> in his conquest of England. His wife founded the city's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Notre-Dame_de_Boulogne" title="Notre-Dame de Boulogne">Notre Dame</a> cathedral, which became a site of pilgrimage from the 12th century onwards, attended by fourteen French kings and five of England. It was an important whaling center prior to 1121.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The city survived on herring fishing and received its municipal charter from Count <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renaud_of_Dammartin" title="Renaud of Dammartin">Renaud of Dammartin</a> in 1203.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flagspot.net_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-flagspot.net-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The area was fought over by the French and the English, including several English occupations during the course of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hundred_Years_War" title="Hundred Years War">Hundred Years War</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sieges_of_Boulogne_(1544%E2%80%931546)" title="Sieges of Boulogne (1544–1546)">Boulogne was again occupied by the English</a> from 1544 to 1550. In 1550, The <a href="/wiki/Rough_Wooing#Treaty_of_Boulogne" title="Rough Wooing">Peace of Boulogne</a> ended the war of England with <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and France. France bought back Boulogne for 400,000 crowns. A culture of smuggling was present in the city until 1659, when French gains in <a href="/wiki/County_of_Flanders" title="County of Flanders">Flanders</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_the_Pyrenees" title="Treaty of the Pyrenees">Treaty of the Pyrenees</a> moved the border northwards.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Napoleonic_period">The Napoleonic period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boulogne-sur-Mer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The Napoleonic period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Boulogne_Colonne_01.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="1728" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Boulogne_Colonne_01.JPG/200px-Boulogne_Colonne_01.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Boulogne_Colonne_01.JPG/300px-Boulogne_Colonne_01.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Boulogne_Colonne_01.JPG/400px-Boulogne_Colonne_01.JPG 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Boulogne_Colonne_01.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Column_of_the_Grande_Arm%C3%A9e" title="Column of the Grande Armée">Column of the Grande Armée</a> commemorates Napoleon's gathering of 200,000 soldiers near Boulogne for a proposed invasion of England</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Boulogne received its current status as a <a href="/wiki/Subprefectures_in_France" title="Subprefectures in France">subprefecture</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pas-de-Calais_(d%C3%A9partement)" title="Pas-de-Calais (département)">Pas-de-Calais</a> department in 1800 due to the territorial re-organisation in Revolutionary France. Three years later, it was given the title of an Imperial City <i>(Ville Impériale)</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flagspot.net_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-flagspot.net-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 19th century was a prosperous one for Boulogne, which became a bathing resort for wealthy Parisians after the completion of a railway line to the French capital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flagspot.net_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-flagspot.net-9">[9]</a></sup> In the 19th century, the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Notre-Dame_de_Boulogne" title="Basilica of Notre-Dame de Boulogne">Basilica of Notre-Dame de Boulogne</a> was reconstructed by the <a href="/wiki/Priest" title="Priest">priest</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benoit_Haffreingue" title="Benoit Haffreingue">Benoit Haffreingue</a>, who claimed to have received a call from God to reconstruct the town's ruined <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>, Napoleon amassed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Grande_Arm%C3%A9e" title="La Grande Armée">La Grande Armée</a> in Boulogne to invade the <a href="/wiki/Napoleon%27s_planned_invasion_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Napoleon's planned invasion of the United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> in 1805. However, his plans were halted by other European matters and the supremacy of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>.</p>
<p>A nephew of Bonaparte, Louis-Napoleon Bonaparte, later <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_III" title="Napoleon III">Napoleon III</a>, returned to France in secret from his exile in England, passing through Boulogne in August 1840. He was later jailed for trying to lead a revolt in <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_two_world_wars">The two world wars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boulogne-sur-Mer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The two world wars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Boulogne_(1940)" title="Battle of Boulogne (1940)">Battle of Boulogne (1940)</a></div>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a>, this was the debarkation port for the first unit of the <a href="/wiki/British_Expeditionary_Force_(World_War_I)" title="British Expeditionary Force (World War I)">British Expeditionary Force</a> to land in France, and for many others thereafter.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Memorabilia_relating_to_H.G._Bagster,_item_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="910" data-file-width="1600" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Memorabilia_relating_to_H.G._Bagster%2C_item_2.jpg/220px-Memorabilia_relating_to_H.G._Bagster%2C_item_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Memorabilia_relating_to_H.G._Bagster%2C_item_2.jpg/330px-Memorabilia_relating_to_H.G._Bagster%2C_item_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Memorabilia_relating_to_H.G._Bagster%2C_item_2.jpg/440px-Memorabilia_relating_to_H.G._Bagster%2C_item_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Memorabilia_relating_to_H.G._Bagster,_item_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laissez-Passer" title="Laissez-Passer">special pass</a>" issued for travel within Boulogne by the <a href="/wiki/British_Red_Cross" title="British Red Cross">British Red Cross</a> in May 1917, during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a></div>

<p>Boulogne, was one of the three base ports most extensively used by the Commonwealth armies on the Western Front throughout the First World War. It was closed and cleared on the 27 August 1914 when the Allies were forced to fall back ahead of the German advance, but was opened again in October and from that month to the end of the war, Boulogne and Wimereux formed one of the chief hospital areas.</p>
<p>Until June 1918, the dead from the hospitals at Boulogne itself were buried in the Cimetiere de L'Est, one of the town's cemeteries, the Commonwealth graves forming a long, narrow strip along the right hand edge of the cemetery. In the spring of 1918, it was found that space was running short in the Eastern Cemetery in spite of repeated extensions to the south, and the site of the new cemetery at Terlincthun was chosen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>It also was the site of an Allied (French and British) armaments production conference.</p>
<p>On 22 May 1940 during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_France" title="Battle of France">Battle of France</a>, two British <a href="/wiki/Brigade_of_Guards" title="Brigade of Guards">Guards</a> battalions and some <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_(military)" title="Pioneer (military)">pioneers</a> attempted to defend Boulogne against an attack by the German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2nd_Panzer_Division" title="2nd Panzer Division">2nd Panzer Division</a>. Despite fierce fighting, the British were overwhelmed and the survivors were evacuated by <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Destroyers" title="Destroyers">destroyers</a> while under direct German gunfire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> On 15 June 1944, 297 planes (155 <a href="/wiki/Avro_Lancaster" title="Avro Lancaster">Avro Lancasters</a>, 130 <a href="/wiki/Handley_Page_Halifax" title="Handley Page Halifax">Handley Page Halifaxes</a>, and 12 <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Mosquito" title="De Havilland Mosquito">De Havilland Mosquitos</a>) of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> bombed Boulogne harbour to suppress German naval activity following <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D-Day" title="D-Day">D-Day</a>. Some of the Lancasters carried <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tallboy_bomb" title="Tallboy bomb">Tallboy bombs</a>, and as a result, the harbour and the surrounding area were completely destroyed. In August, 1944 the town was declared a "fortress" by <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>, but it succumbed to <a href="/wiki/Operation_Wellhit" title="Operation Wellhit">assault</a> and liberation by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3rd_Canadian_Infantry_Division" title="3rd Canadian Infantry Division">3rd Canadian Infantry Division</a> in September. In one incident, a French civilian guided the Canadians to a "secret passage" leading into the walled old town and by-passing the German defenders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-offhist_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-offhist-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>To replace the destroyed urban infrastructure, affordable housing and public facility projects in functional, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brutalist" title="Brutalist">brutalist</a> building styles were carried out in the 1950s and 60s. The harbour, therefore, is not typical of a Northern French harbour.</p>

<p>Boulogne's 12th century belfry is one of 56 in northeastern France and <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> with shared <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNESCO_Heritage_Sites_List" title="UNESCO Heritage Sites List">World Heritage Site status</a>. It is the oldest building in the upper city, and currently serves as the home to a museum of Celtic remains from the Roman occupation. Founded as the Count's dungeon, the top floor was added in the 13th century. Damage by a fire in 1712 was built over by 1734.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nordmag.fr_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-nordmag.fr-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other than the belfry there are also the following sights:</p>
<ul>
<li>Medieval walls long 1,500 metres with 4 gates and 17 towers from 13th century</li>
<li>Medieval castle, whose foundations date to Roman times. It houses an <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egyptian</a> art collection</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> church of St. Nicholas, housing several 15th century statues</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Notre-Dame_de_Boulogne" title="Basilica of Notre-Dame de Boulogne">Cathedral basilica of Notre-Dame</a>, with a dome standing at over 100 m. The crypt is one of the largest in France, and has Roman, Romanesque and Gothic elements.</li>
<li>Opened in 1991, <a href="/wiki/Nausica%C3%A4_Centre_National_de_la_Mer" title="Nausicaä Centre National de la Mer">Nausicaä</a> - The French National Sea Centre is a science centre entirely dedicated to the relationship between mankind and the sea. It houses Aquaria, exhibitions on the marine fauna, and the exploitation and management of marine resources (fisheries, aquaculture, coastal planning, maritime transport, exploitation of energies and mineral, tourism).</li>
<li>The Boulogne Eastern Cemetery, created during the Great War</li>
<li><a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonne_de_la_Grande_Arm%C3%A9e" title="fr:Colonne de la Grande Armée">Colonne de la Grande Armée</a> - Statue of Napoleon I</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boulogne-sur-Mer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Boulogne-sur-Mer hosts one of the oldest <i>Universités de l'été</i> - summer courses in French language and culture.</p>
<p>The Saint-Louis building of the University of the Côte d'Opale's Boulogne campus opened its doors in 1991, on the site of the former St. Louis Hospital, the front entrance to which remains a predominant architectural feature. Its 6 major specialisms are Modern Languages, French Literature, Sport, Law, History and Economics. The university is situated in the town centre, about 5 minutes from the Boulogne Tintelleries railway station.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boulogne-sur-Mer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Boulogne-sur-Mer" title="Château de Boulogne-sur-Mer">Château de Boulogne-sur-Mer</a> (now a castle museum) of Boulogne, in the fortified town, houses the most important exhibition of masks from Alaska in the world, the second largest collection of Greek ceramics in France (after the Louvre), collections of Roman and medieval sculptures, paintings (15th-20th century), an Egyptian collection, African Arts etc. As these collections are exhibited in a medieval castle, one can also discover the Roman walls (in the underground) as well as rooms built in the 13th century (La Barbière, banqueting hall, chapel, covered parapet walk...)</li>
<li>La Casa San Martin is currently a museum where <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_de_San_Mart%C3%ADn" title="José de San Martín">José de San Martín</a> the leader of independence struggle in Argentina (also Chile and Peru) died in 1850, from 1930 to 1967 this house was the consulate of Argentina in France. There is a statue dedicated to his colleague <a href="/wiki/Sim%C3%B3n_Bol%C3%ADvar" title="Simón Bolívar">Simón Bolívar</a>, other liberator of South America in the revolutions against <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish</a> colonial rule in the 1810s. Bolivar planned to head in exile to this very part of France before his death in 1830. Historic emigration in the 19th century from the Nord-Pas de Calais region to <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> can explain some cultural ties with South America of the <i>Boulognais</i> and <a href="/wiki/Latino" title="Latino">Latino</a>/Ibero-American culture.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nausica%C3%A4_Centre_National_de_la_Mer" title="Nausicaä Centre National de la Mer">Nausicaä</a>, the French national sealife centre.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food">Food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boulogne-sur-Mer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As an international maritime port on the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> (<i>La Manche</i>), the town of Boulogne-sur-Mer has European and American influences in local cuisine. They include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Welsh_rarebit" title="Welsh rarebit">Welsh rarebit</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>)</li>
<li>Sandwich américain (an American sandwich introduced from the USA)</li>
<li>Kipper (<a href="/wiki/Flemish" title="Flemish">Flemish</a>: smoked <a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herring</a>)</li>
</ul>
