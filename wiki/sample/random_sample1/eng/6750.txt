<p>The <b>Bullmastiff</b> is a large-sized breed of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_dog" title="Domestic dog">domestic dog</a>, with a solid build and a short muzzle. The Bullmastiff shares the characteristics of <a href="/wiki/Molosser" title="Molosser">molosser</a> dogs, and was originally developed by 19th-century <a href="/wiki/Gamekeeper" title="Gamekeeper">gamekeepers</a> to guard estates. The breed's bloodlines are drawn from the <a href="/wiki/English_Mastiff" title="English Mastiff">English Mastiff</a> and the extinct <a href="/wiki/Old_English_Bulldog" title="Old English Bulldog">Old English Bulldog</a>. It was recognized as a purebred dog by the <a href="/wiki/The_Kennel_Club" title="The Kennel Club">English Kennel Club</a> in 1924. They are quiet dogs and very rarely bark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Size">Size</span></h3>
<p>Males should stand 25–27 inches (64–69 cm) tall (<a href="/wiki/American_Kennel_Club" title="American Kennel Club">American Kennel Club</a> (AKC) standard) at the <a href="/wiki/Withers" title="Withers">withers</a> and weigh110–130 pounds (50–59 kg). Females should be 24–26 inches (61–66 cm) at the withers, and 100–120 pounds (45–54 kg). Exceeding these dimensions is discouraged by breeders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Color">Color</span></h3>
<p>A Bullmastiff's coat may appear in fawn, red, or brindle. These are the only acceptable colors in the AKC standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Get_to_know_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Get_to_know-3">[3]</a></sup> The fawn can range from a very light brown to a reddish brown. Red can range from a light red-fawn to a dark, rich red. <a href="/wiki/Brindle" title="Brindle">Brindles</a> are a striped overlay of the fawn or red. A Bullmastiff should have no white markings, except for on the chest where a little white is allowed.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>Bullmastiffs are a large working breed originating in Britain in the mid 1800s as estate guardians to ward off poachers. They were bred by gamekeepers for strength, size and speed using a cross of the tough, heavy and aggressive <a href="/wiki/Bulldog" title="Bulldog">Bulldog</a> of the 19th century with the large, strong, less aggressive <a href="/wiki/Mastiff" title="Mastiff">Mastiff</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-4">[4]</a></sup> As a result, the Bullmastiff is known as the Gamekeeper's Night Dog. The preferred color, by gamekeepers, was brindle, as this color works as a more effective camouflage, especially at night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Bullmastiff breed was deemed pure by the English Kennel Club in 1924, with a genetic background of approximately 60% Mastiff and 40% Bulldog.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1934, the AKC recognized the Bullmastiff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Get_to_know_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Get_to_know-3">[3]</a></sup> The first standard for the breed was approved in 1935.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The standard has undergone several revisions since then; the most current version is available on the AKC website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trainability">Trainability</span></h2>
<p>Bullmastiffs are strong, powerful, but sensitive dogs. For a Bullmastiff to become a well-behaved family member, consistency is needed. Training and socialization is of high importance, as the breed can be independent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Get_to_know_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Get_to_know-3">[3]</a></sup> Dogs of this breed are natural guardians of their home and owners. No special guard training is needed for a Bullmastiff to react appropriately if its family is endangered. During training, a Bullmastiff requires a special approach, because these dogs do not like to repeat the same actions again and again. Activities Bullmastiffs enjoy include obedience, agility, tracking, and carting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span></h2>
<p>A UK survey puts the median lifespan of the Bullmastiff at 7 to 8 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A Bullmastiff does not stop growing until it is about three and a half years of age.</p>
<p>Health concerns within the breed include hip and elbow dysplasia, progressive retinal atrophy, bloat, and cancer, with a relatively high incidence of lymphoma and mast cell tumours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Bullmastiffs are prone to certain hereditary diseases, including:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hip_dysplasia_(canine)" title="Hip dysplasia (canine)">Hip dysplasia</a>, affecting 24.5% of individuals <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Elbow_dysplasia" title="Elbow dysplasia">Elbow dysplasia</a>, affecting 13.8% of individuals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Entropion" title="Entropion">Entropion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hypothyroidism" title="Hypothyroidism">hypothyroidism</a> affecting 2.8% of individuals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lymphoma_in_animals" title="Lymphoma in animals">Lymphoma</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Progressive_retinal_atrophy" title="Progressive retinal atrophy">Progressive retinal atrophy</a> is a particular problem, since the trait is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autosomal_dominant#Autosomal_dominant_gene" title="Autosomal dominant">autosomal dominant</a> one. (This has recently been called into question by another other medical team and has been proven that some Bullmastiffs have autosomal recessive PRA genes. In America, this is being investigated by the American Bullmastiff Health and Research Committee, and the DNA Optigen test only works for dominant genes, so it is considered inadequate at this time.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Arthritis" title="Arthritis">Arthritis</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bloat" title="Bloat">Bloat</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Cosmetic genetic problems include longhairs and "Dudleys". Both are recessives and not common. The Dudley, named after a notable Bulldog breeder of the 19th century, the Earl of Dudley, is a lack of pigment in the <a href="/wiki/Melanistic_mask" title="Melanistic mask">mask</a>. It can be liver colored or simply not present.</p>
