<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Faithless elector</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>In <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election" title="United States presidential election">United States presidential elections</a>, a <b>faithless elector</b> is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Electoral_College_(United_States)" title="Electoral College (United States)">United States Electoral College</a> who does not vote for the presidential or vice-presidential <a href="/wiki/Candidate" title="Candidate">candidate</a> for whom they had <a href="/wiki/Promise" title="Promise">pledged</a> to vote. That is, they break faith with the candidate they were pledged to and vote for another candidate, or fail to vote. A pledged elector is only considered a faithless elector by breaking their pledge; <a href="/wiki/Unpledged_elector" title="Unpledged elector">unpledged electors</a> have no pledge to break.</p>
<p>Electors are typically chosen and <a href="/wiki/Nomination" title="Nomination">nominated</a> by a political party or the party's <a href="/wiki/Presidential_nominee" title="Presidential nominee">presidential nominee</a>: they are usually party members with a reputation for high loyalty to the party and its chosen candidate. Thus, a faithless elector runs the risk of party censure and political retaliation from their party, as well as potential legal penalties in some states. Candidates for elector are nominated by state political parties in the months prior to <a href="/wiki/Election_Day_(United_States)" title="Election Day (United States)">Election Day</a>.</p>
<p>In some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2016)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> states such as Indiana, the electors are nominated in <a href="/wiki/Primary_election" title="Primary election">primaries</a>, the same way other candidates are nominated. In other states, such as <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, electors are nominated in party conventions. In <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, the campaign committee of each candidate names their candidates for elector (an attempt to discourage faithless electors). The parties have generally been successful in keeping their electors faithful, leaving out the cases in which a candidate died before the elector was able to cast a vote.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1836" title="United States presidential election, 1836">1836 election</a>, Virginia's entire 23-man electoral delegation faithlessly abstained<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SabatoErnst2014_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-SabatoErnst2014-2">[2]</a></sup> from voting for victorious <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic</a> vice presidential nominee <a href="/wiki/Richard_Mentor_Johnson#Marriage_and_family" title="Richard Mentor Johnson">Richard M. Johnson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fairvote_problems_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fairvote_problems-3">[3]</a></sup> The loss of Virginia's support caused Johnson to fall one electoral vote short of a majority, causing the vice presidential election to be thrown into the U.S. Senate for the only time in American history. However, Virginia's electors voted for Democratic presidential nominee <a href="/wiki/Martin_Van_Buren" title="Martin Van Buren">Martin Van Buren</a> as pledged, so the presidential election itself was not in dispute. The U.S. Senate ultimately elected Johnson after a party-line vote.</p>
<p>Despite over 150 other instances of faithlessness as of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Faithless_elector&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, faithless electors have not yet affected the results or ultimate outcome of any other election for president and vice president.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_position">Legal position</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Faithless_elector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Legal position">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Twenty-one states do not have laws compelling their electors to vote for a pledged candidate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Twenty-nine states plus the District of Columbia have laws to penalize faithless electors, although these have never been enforced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fairvote_problems_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fairvote_problems-3">[3]</a></sup> In lieu of penalizing a faithless elector, some states, such as <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>, specify the faithless elector's vote is void.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Minnesota invoked this law for the first time in 2016 when an elector pledged to <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton" title="Hillary Clinton">Hillary Clinton</a> attempted to vote for <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Sanders" title="Bernie Sanders">Bernie Sanders</a> instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Until 2008, Minnesota's electors cast secret ballots. Although the final count would reveal the occurrence of faithless votes (except in the unlikely case of two or more changes canceling out), it was impossible to determine which elector(s) were faithless. After an unknown elector was faithless in 2004, Minnesota amended its law to require public balloting of the electors' votes and invalidate any vote cast for someone other than the candidate to whom the elector was pledged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-7">[7]</a></sup> After an elector has voted, their vote can be changed only in states such as <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>, which invalidate votes other than those pledged. In the twenty-nine states with laws against faithless electors, a faithless elector may only be punished after they vote.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Supreme_Court">U.S. Supreme Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Faithless_elector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: U.S. Supreme Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The constitutionality of state <i>pledge</i> laws was confirmed by the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court</a> in 1952 in <i><a href="/wiki/Ray_v._Blair" title="Ray v. Blair">Ray v. Blair</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup> in a 5–2 vote. The court ruled states have the right to require electors to pledge to vote for the candidate whom their party supports, and the right to remove potential electors who refuse to pledge prior to the election. The court also wrote:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>However, even if such promises of candidates for the electoral college are legally unenforceable because violative of an <i>assumed constitutional freedom of the elector under the Constitution, Art. II, § 1, to vote as he may choose</i> in the electoral college, it would not follow that the requirement of a pledge in the primary is unconstitutional (emphasis added).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The ruling only held that requiring a pledge, not a vote, was constitutional and Justice Jackson, joined by Justice Douglas, wrote in his dissent, "no one faithful to our history can deny that the plan originally contemplated what is implicit in its text – that electors would be free agents, to exercise an independent and nonpartisan judgment as to the men best qualified for the Nation's highest offices."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> More recent legal scholars believe "a state law that would thwart a federal elector’s discretion at an extraordinary time when it reasonably must be exercised would clearly violate Article II and the Twelfth Amendment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Supreme Court has never ruled on the constitutionality of state laws punishing or replacing electors for actually casting a faithless vote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dcelector_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-dcelector-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Faithless_elector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Over 22 occasions, a total of 179 electors have not cast their votes for <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> as prescribed by the legislature of the state they represented. Of those, 71 electors changed their votes because the candidate to whom they were pledged died before the electoral ballot (1872, 1912). Two electors chose to abstain from voting for any candidate (1812, 2000).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fairvote_problems_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fairvote_problems-3">[3]</a></sup> The remaining 106 were changed by the elector's personal interest, or perhaps by accident. Usually, the faithless electors act alone. An exception was the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1836" title="United States presidential election, 1836">1836 election</a>, in which all 23 Virginia electors acted together.</p>
<p>The 1836 election was the only occasion when faithless electors altered the outcome of the electoral college vote. The Democratic ticket won states with 170 of the 294 electoral votes, but the 23 Virginia electors abstained in the vote for <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a>, so the Democratic nominee, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Mentor_Johnson" title="Richard Mentor Johnson">Richard M. Johnson</a>, got only 147 (exactly half), and was not elected. However, Johnson was elected Vice President by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">U.S. Senate</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_faithless_electors">List of faithless electors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Faithless_elector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: List of faithless electors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following is a list of all faithless electors (in reverse chronological order). The number preceding each entry is the number of faithless electors for the given year.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2016">2016</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Faithless_elector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2016">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Faithless_electors_in_the_United_States_presidential_election,_2016" title="Faithless electors in the United States presidential election, 2016">Faithless electors in the United States presidential election, 2016</a></div>
<p><b>7</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2016" title="United States presidential election, 2016">2016 election</a>: In <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>, Democratic party electors gave three presidential votes to <a href="/wiki/Colin_Powell" title="Colin Powell">Colin Powell</a> and one to <a href="/wiki/Faith_Spotted_Eagle" title="Faith Spotted Eagle">Faith Spotted Eagle</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and these electors cast vice-presidential votes for <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Warren" title="Elizabeth Warren">Elizabeth Warren</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maria_Cantwell" title="Maria Cantwell">Maria Cantwell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Susan_Collins" title="Susan Collins">Susan Collins</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Winona_LaDuke" title="Winona LaDuke">Winona LaDuke</a>. In Hawaii, <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Sanders" title="Bernie Sanders">Bernie Sanders</a> received one presidential vote and Elizabeth Warren received one vice-presidential vote. In Texas, <a href="/wiki/John_Kasich" title="John Kasich">John Kasich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ron_Paul" title="Ron Paul">Ron Paul</a> received one presidential vote each, and one of these electors gave <a href="/wiki/Carly_Fiorina" title="Carly Fiorina">Carly Fiorina</a> a vice-presidential vote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, three other electors attempted to vote against their pledge, but had their votes invalidated. In Colorado, Kasich received one vote for president, which was invalidated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Two additional electors, one in Maine and one in Minnesota, cast votes for Sanders for president but had their votes invalidated and their replacement electors cast for Clinton. The same Minnesota elector voted for <a href="/wiki/Tulsi_Gabbard" title="Tulsi Gabbard">Tulsi Gabbard</a> for vice president, but had that vote invalidated and given to <a href="/wiki/Tim_Kaine" title="Tim Kaine">Tim Kaine</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000_to_2004">2000 to 2004</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Faithless_elector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2000 to 2004">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>1</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2004" title="United States presidential election, 2004">2004 election</a>: An anonymous <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a> elector, pledged for Democrats <a href="/wiki/John_Kerry" title="John Kerry">John Kerry</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Edwards" title="John Edwards">John Edwards</a>, cast his or her presidential vote for "John Ewards" [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>],<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> rather than Kerry, presumably by accident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> (All of Minnesota's electors cast their vice presidential ballots for <a href="/wiki/John_Edwards" title="John Edwards">John Edwards</a>.) Minnesota's electors cast secret ballots, so the identity of the faithless elector is not known. As a result of this incident, Minnesota statutes were amended to provide for public balloting of the electors' votes and invalidation of a vote cast for someone other than the candidate to whom the elector is pledged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2000" title="United States presidential election, 2000">2000 election</a>: <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> elector <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Lett-Simmons" title="Barbara Lett-Simmons">Barbara Lett-Simmons</a>, pledged for Democrats <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joe_Lieberman" title="Joe Lieberman">Joe Lieberman</a>, cast no electoral votes as a protest of <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia_voting_rights" title="District of Columbia voting rights">Washington D.C.'s lack of voting congressional representation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dcelector_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-dcelector-11">[11]</a></sup> Lett-Simmons's electoral college abstention, the first since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_1864" title="U.S. presidential election, 1864">1864</a>, was intended to protest what Lett-Simmons referred to as the federal district's "colonial status".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dcelector_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-dcelector-11">[11]</a></sup> Lett-Simmons described her blank ballot as an act of <a href="/wiki/Civil_disobedience" title="Civil disobedience">civil disobedience</a>, not an act of a faithless elector; Lett-Simmons supported Gore and would have voted for Gore if she had thought he had a chance to win.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dcelector_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-dcelector-11">[11]</a></sup> This did not affect the outcome of the election.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1968_to_1996">1968 to 1996</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Faithless_elector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1968 to 1996">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>1</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1988" title="United States presidential election, 1988">1988 election</a>: <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a> Elector <a href="/wiki/Margarette_Leach" title="Margarette Leach">Margarette Leach</a>, pledged for Democrats <a href="/wiki/Michael_Dukakis" title="Michael Dukakis">Michael Dukakis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_Bentsen" title="Lloyd Bentsen">Lloyd Bentsen</a>, but as a form of protest against the winner-take-all custom of the Electoral College, instead cast her votes for the candidates in the reverse of their positions on the national ticket; her presidential vote went to Bentsen and her vice presidential vote to Dukakis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1976" title="United States presidential election, 1976">1976 election</a>: <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a> Elector <a href="/wiki/Mike_Padden" title="Mike Padden">Mike Padden</a>, pledged for Republicans <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">Gerald Ford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dole" title="Bob Dole">Bob Dole</a>, cast his presidential electoral vote for <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a>, who had challenged Ford for the Republican nomination. He cast his vice presidential vote, as pledged, for Dole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1972" title="United States presidential election, 1972">1972 election</a>: <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> Elector <a href="/wiki/Roger_MacBride" title="Roger MacBride">Roger MacBride</a>, pledged for Republicans <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spiro_Agnew" title="Spiro Agnew">Spiro Agnew</a>, cast his electoral votes for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Libertarian_Party" title="United States Libertarian Party">Libertarian</a> candidates <a href="/wiki/John_Hospers" title="John Hospers">John Hospers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tonie_Nathan" title="Tonie Nathan">Tonie Nathan</a>. MacBride's VP vote for Nathan was the first electoral vote cast for a woman in U.S. history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-first-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1968" title="United States presidential election, 1968">1968 election</a>: <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> Elector <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_W._Bailey" title="Lloyd W. Bailey">Lloyd W. Bailey</a>, pledged for Republicans <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spiro_Agnew" title="Spiro Agnew">Spiro Agnew</a>, cast his votes for <a href="/wiki/American_Independent_Party" title="American Independent Party">American Independent Party</a> candidates <a href="/wiki/George_Wallace" title="George Wallace">George Wallace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Curtis_LeMay" title="Curtis LeMay">Curtis LeMay</a>. Bailey later stated at a Senate hearing that he would have voted for Nixon if his vote would have altered the outcome of the election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bailey_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-bailey-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1912_to_1960">1912 to 1960</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Faithless_elector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1912 to 1960">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>1</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1960" title="United States presidential election, 1960">1960 election</a>: <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a> Elector <a href="/wiki/Henry_D._Irwin" title="Henry D. Irwin">Henry D. Irwin</a>, pledged for Republicans <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Cabot_Lodge,_Jr." title="Henry Cabot Lodge, Jr.">Henry Cabot Lodge, Jr.</a>, contacted the other 218 Republican electors to convince them to cast presidential electoral votes for Democratic non-candidate <a href="/wiki/Harry_F._Byrd" title="Harry F. Byrd">Harry F. Byrd</a> and vice presidential electoral votes for Republican <a href="/wiki/Barry_Goldwater" title="Barry Goldwater">Barry Goldwater</a>, though most replied they had a moral obligation to vote for Nixon; Irwin voted for Byrd and Goldwater and fourteen <a href="/wiki/Unpledged_elector" title="Unpledged elector">unpledged electors</a> (eight from Mississippi and six from Alabama) also voted for Byrd for president, but supported <a href="/wiki/Strom_Thurmond" title="Strom Thurmond">Strom Thurmond</a> for vice president; this action by the 14 was not faithless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edwards_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Edwards-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1956" title="United States presidential election, 1956">1956 election</a>: <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> Elector <a href="/wiki/W._F._Turner" title="W. F. Turner">W. F. Turner</a>, pledged for Democrats <a href="/wiki/Adlai_Stevenson_II" title="Adlai Stevenson II">Adlai Stevenson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Estes_Kefauver" title="Estes Kefauver">Estes Kefauver</a>, cast his votes for circuit judge <a href="/wiki/Walter_Burgwyn_Jones" title="Walter Burgwyn Jones">Walter Burgwyn Jones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herman_Talmadge" title="Herman Talmadge">Herman Talmadge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edwards_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Edwards-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1948" title="United States presidential election, 1948">1948 election</a>: <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a> elector <a href="/wiki/Preston_Parks" title="Preston Parks">Preston Parks</a> was on both the Democratic Party for <a href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman" title="Harry S. Truman">Harry S. Truman</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/States%27_Rights_Democratic_Party" title="States' Rights Democratic Party">States' Rights Democratic Party</a> for <a href="/wiki/Strom_Thurmond" title="Strom Thurmond">Strom Thurmond</a>. When the Democratic Party slate won, Parks voted for Thurmond and <a href="/wiki/Fielding_L._Wright" title="Fielding L. Wright">Fielding L. Wright</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edwards_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Edwards-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>8</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1912" title="United States presidential election, 1912">1912 election</a>: <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> vice presidential candidate <a href="/wiki/James_S._Sherman" title="James S. Sherman">James S. Sherman</a> died before the popular election. Eight Republican electors voted for <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Murray_Butler" title="Nicholas Murray Butler">Nicholas M. Butler</a>, who had been designated to receive the electoral votes that would have gone to Sherman.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1872_to_1896">1872 to 1896</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Faithless_elector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 1872 to 1896">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>27</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1896" title="United States presidential election, 1896">1896 election</a>: The Democratic Party and the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Party_(United_States)" title="People's Party (United States)">People's Party</a> both ran <a href="/wiki/William_Jennings_Bryan" title="William Jennings Bryan">William Jennings Bryan</a> as their presidential candidate, but ran different candidates for Vice President. The Democratic Party nominated <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Sewall" title="Arthur Sewall">Arthur Sewall</a> and the People’s Party nominated <a href="/wiki/Thomas_E._Watson" title="Thomas E. Watson">Thomas E. Watson</a>. Although the Populist ticket did not win the popular vote in any state, 27 Democratic electors for Bryan cast their vice-presidential vote for Watson instead of Sewall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1892" title="United States presidential election, 1892">1892 election</a>: In Oregon, the electors were pledged to vote for <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Harrison" title="Benjamin Harrison">Benjamin Harrison</a>; three electors voted for Harrison and one faithless elector voted for the third-party Populist candidate, <a href="/wiki/James_B._Weaver" title="James B. Weaver">James B. Weaver</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uselectionatlas_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-uselectionatlas-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>63</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1872" title="United States presidential election, 1872">1872 election</a>: <a href="/wiki/Horace_Greeley" title="Horace Greeley">Horace Greeley</a> was alive during the November election but died before the electoral vote. 63 out of 66 electors refused to vote for a deceased candidate, and out of those, 43 cast their presidential votes for four non-candidates, and 17 abstained. Greeley received three posthumous electoral votes, but these votes were disallowed by Congress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1812_to_1836">1812 to 1836</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Faithless_elector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 1812 to 1836">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>23</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1836" title="United States presidential election, 1836">1836 election</a>: The 23 electors from <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> were pledged to vote for <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic</a> candidates <a href="/wiki/Martin_Van_Buren" title="Martin Van Buren">Martin Van Buren</a> (for President) and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Mentor_Johnson" title="Richard Mentor Johnson">Richard M. Johnson</a> (for Vice President). However, they abstained from voting for Johnson, because of his <i>open</i> (and therefore scandalous) liaison with a slave mistress. This left Johnson with one fewer than a majority of electoral votes. Johnson was subsequently elected Vice President by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senate</a>.</p>
<p><b>32</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1832" title="United States presidential election, 1832">1832 election</a>: Two National Republican Party electors from the state of <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> refused to vote for presidential candidate <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clay" title="Henry Clay">Henry Clay</a> and did not cast a vote for him or for his running mate, <a href="/wiki/John_Sergeant_(politician)" title="John Sergeant (politician)">John Sergeant</a>. All 30 electors from <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> refused to support the Democratic vice presidential candidate <a href="/wiki/Martin_Van_Buren" title="Martin Van Buren">Martin Van Buren</a>, voting instead for <a href="/wiki/William_Wilkins_(U.S._politician)" title="William Wilkins (U.S. politician)">William Wilkins</a>.</p>
<p><b>7</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1828" title="United States presidential election, 1828">1828 election</a>: Seven of nine electors from <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> refused to vote for vice presidential candidate <a href="/wiki/John_C._Calhoun" title="John C. Calhoun">John C. Calhoun</a>. All seven cast their vice presidential votes for <a href="/wiki/William_Smith_(South_Carolina_senator)" title="William Smith (South Carolina senator)">William Smith</a> instead.</p>
<p><b>1</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1820" title="United States presidential election, 1820">1820 election</a>: <a href="/wiki/William_Plumer" title="William Plumer">William Plumer</a> was pledged to vote for <a href="/wiki/Democratic-Republican_Party" title="Democratic-Republican Party">Democratic-Republican</a> candidate <a href="/wiki/James_Monroe" title="James Monroe">James Monroe</a>, but he cast his vote for <a href="/wiki/John_Quincy_Adams" title="John Quincy Adams">John Quincy Adams</a>, who was not a candidate in the election. Some historians contend Plumer wanted George Washington to be the only unanimous selection and that he further wanted to draw attention to his friend Adams as a potential candidate. These claims are disputed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edwards_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-Edwards-22">[22]</a></sup> (Plumer cast his vice presidential vote for Richard Rush, not Daniel D. Tompkins.)</p>
<p><b>4</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1812" title="United States presidential election, 1812">1812 election</a>: Three electors pledged to vote for <a href="/wiki/Federalist_Party" title="Federalist Party">Federalist</a> vice presidential candidate <a href="/wiki/Jared_Ingersoll" title="Jared Ingersoll">Jared Ingersoll</a> voted for <a href="/wiki/Democratic-Republican_Party" title="Democratic-Republican Party">Democratic-Republican</a> <a href="/wiki/Elbridge_Gerry" title="Elbridge Gerry">Elbridge Gerry</a>. One <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> elector did not vote.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Before_1812">Before 1812</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Faithless_elector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Before 1812">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>6</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1808" title="United States presidential election, 1808">1808 election</a>: Six electors from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> were pledged to vote for <a href="/wiki/Democratic-Republican_Party" title="Democratic-Republican Party">Democratic-Republican</a> <a href="/wiki/James_Madison" title="James Madison">James Madison</a> for President and <a href="/wiki/George_Clinton_(vice_president)" title="George Clinton (vice president)">George Clinton</a> for Vice President. Instead, they voted for Clinton to be President, with three voting for Madison for Vice President and the other three voting for <a href="/wiki/James_Monroe" title="James Monroe">James Monroe</a> for Vice President.</p>
<p><b>19</b> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1796" title="United States presidential election, 1796">1796 election</a>: <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Miles" title="Samuel Miles">Samuel Miles</a>, an elector from <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, was pledged to vote for <a href="/wiki/Federalist_Party" title="Federalist Party">Federalist</a> presidential candidate <a href="/wiki/John_Adams" title="John Adams">John Adams</a>, but voted for <a href="/wiki/Democratic-Republican_Party" title="Democratic-Republican Party">Democratic-Republican</a> candidate Thomas Jefferson. He cast his other presidential vote as pledged for <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Pinckney" title="Thomas Pinckney">Thomas Pinckney</a>; there was no provision at the time for specifying president or vice president. An additional 18 electors voted for Adams as pledged, but refused to vote for Pinckney.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> This was an attempt to foil Alexander Hamilton's rumored plan to elect Pinckney as President, and this resulted in the unintended outcome that Adams's opponent, Jefferson, was elected Vice President instead of Adams's running mate Pinckney. This was the only time that the president and vice president have been from different parties. After the 1800 election also resulted in confusion, the <a href="/wiki/Twelfth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Twelfth Amendment to the United States Constitution">12th Amendment</a> was ratified in 1804. It changed the election procedure so that instead of casting two votes of the same type, electors would now make an explicit choice for president and vice president.</p>
