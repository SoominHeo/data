<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Fiat money</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">Yuan dynasty</a> banknotes are the earliest known fiat money.</div>

</div>
<p><b>Fiat money</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a> established as <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">money</a> by government regulation or law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mankiw_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-mankiw-1">[1]</a></sup> The term derives from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a> <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fiat#Latin" title="wikt:fiat">fiat</a></i> ("let it become", "it will become")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> used in the sense of an order or decree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mankiw_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-mankiw-1">[1]</a></sup> It differs from <a href="/wiki/Commodity_money" title="Commodity money">commodity money</a> and <a href="/wiki/Representative_money" title="Representative money">representative money</a>. Commodity money is created from a good, often a <a href="/wiki/Precious_metal" title="Precious metal">precious metal</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">gold</a> or <a href="/wiki/Silver_standard" title="Silver standard">silver</a>, which has uses other than as a <a href="/wiki/Medium_of_exchange" title="Medium of exchange">medium of exchange</a> (such a good is called a commodity), while representative money simply represents a claim on such a good.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mankiw_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-mankiw-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walsh_2003_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walsh_2003-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first use of fiat money was recorded in China around 1000 AD. Since then, it has been used by various countries, concurrently with commodity currencies.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiat_money&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fiat money has been defined variously as:</p>
<ul>
<li>Any money declared by a government to be <a href="/wiki/Legal_tender" title="Legal tender">legal tender</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>State-issued money which is neither convertible by law to any other thing, nor fixed in value in terms of any objective standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-keynesp7_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-keynesp7-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>Intrinsically valueless money used as money because of government decree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mankiw_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-mankiw-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>While gold- or silver-backed <a href="/wiki/Representative_money" title="Representative money">representative money</a> entails the legal requirement that the bank of issue redeem it in fixed weights of gold or silver, fiat money's value is unrelated to the value of any physical quantity. A coin is fiat currency to the extent that its <a href="/wiki/Face_value" title="Face value">face value</a>, value defined in law, is greater than its market value as metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walsh_2003_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Walsh_2003-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment_in_economics">Treatment in economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiat_money&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Treatment in economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Monetary_economics" title="Monetary economics">monetary economics</a>, fiat money is an intrinsically valueless object or record that is widely accepted as a means of payment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walsh_2003_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Walsh_2003-3">[3]</a></sup> In some micro-founded models of money, fiat money is <a href="/wiki/Endogeneity_(econometrics)" title="Endogeneity (econometrics)">created internally</a> in a community making feasible trades that would not otherwise be possible, either because producers and consumers may not anonymously write <a href="/wiki/IOU" title="IOU">IOUs</a>, or because of physical constraints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precious_metals">Precious metals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiat_money&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Precious metals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Circulating <a href="/wiki/Silver_coin" title="Silver coin">silver coins</a> in the 1960s ceased to be produced containing the precious metal when the <a href="/wiki/Face_value" title="Face value">face value</a> of the coin was below the cost of the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table#Metals.2C_metalloids_and_nonmetals" title="Periodic table">elemental metal</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Coinage_Act_of_1965" title="Coinage Act of 1965">Coinage Act of 1965</a> eliminated <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> from the circulating dimes and quarter dollars of the United States, and most other countries did the same with their coins.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Penny_(Canadian_coin)" title="Penny (Canadian coin)">The Canadian penny</a> was mostly <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> until 1996 and was removed from circulation in the fall of 2012 due to the cost of production relative to face value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007 the Royal Canadian Mint produced a million dollar <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> <a href="/wiki/Bullion_coin" title="Bullion coin">bullion coin</a> and sold five of them. In 2015, the gold in the coins was worth more than 3.5 times the face value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiat_money&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiat_money&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fiat money originated in 11th century <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and its use became widespread during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuan_Dynasty" title="Yuan Dynasty">Yuan</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming</a> dynasties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jiao_zi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="537" data-file-width="308" height="314" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Jiao_zi.jpg/180px-Jiao_zi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Jiao_zi.jpg/270px-Jiao_zi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d9/Jiao_zi.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jiao_zi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Song Dynasty <i>Jiaozi,</i> the world's earliest paper money.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" title="Song Dynasty">Song Dynasty</a> in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> was the first to issue paper money, <i><a href="/wiki/Jiaozi_(currency)" title="Jiaozi (currency)">jiaozi</a></i>, around the 10th century AD. Although the notes were valued at a certain exchange rate for gold, silver, or silk, conversion was never allowed in practice. The notes were initially to be redeemed after three years' service, to be replaced by new notes for a 3% service charge, but, as more of them were printed without notes being retired, inflation became evident. The government made several attempts to support the paper by demanding taxes partly in currency and making other laws, but the damage had been done, and the notes fell out of favor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The successive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuan_Dynasty" title="Yuan Dynasty">Yuan Dynasty</a> was the first dynasty in China to use paper currency as the predominant circulating medium. The founder of the Yuan Dynasty, <a href="/wiki/Kublai_Khan" title="Kublai Khan">Kublai Khan</a>, issued paper money known as <a href="/wiki/Chao_(currency)" title="Chao (currency)">Chao</a> in his reign. The original notes during the Yuan Dynasty were restricted in area and duration as in the Song Dynasty.</p>
<p>During the 13th century, <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">Marco Polo</a> described the fiat money of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuan_Dynasty" title="Yuan Dynasty">Yuan Dynasty</a> in his book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Travels_of_Marco_Polo" title="The Travels of Marco Polo">The Travels of Marco Polo</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MilesScott_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-MilesScott-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marco_Polo_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marco_Polo-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>All these pieces of paper are issued with as much solemnity and authority as if they were of pure gold or silver... and indeed everybody takes them readily, for wheresoever a person may go throughout the <a href="/wiki/Kublai_Khan" title="Kublai Khan">Great Kaan</a>'s dominions he shall find these pieces of paper current, and shall be able to transact all sales and purchases of goods by means of them just as well as if they were coins of pure gold.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">Marco Polo</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Travels_of_Marco_Polo" title="The Travels of Marco Polo">The Travels of Marco Polo</a></i></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiat_money&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tally_stick" title="Tally stick">Tally sticks</a> were employed as fiat currency in medieval England when, due to gold shortage, <a href="/wiki/Henry_I_of_England" title="Henry I of England">Henry I</a> initiated their use by the Exchequer in or around 1100; royal taxes had to be paid in tally sticks. <a href="/wiki/Washington_Irving" title="Washington Irving">Washington Irving</a> records an emergency use of paper money by the Spanish in a siege during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Granada" title="Conquest of Granada">Conquest of Granada</a> (1482-1492). In 1661, <a href="/wiki/Johan_Palmstruch" title="Johan Palmstruch">Johan Palmstruch</a> issued the first regular paper money in the West, under royal charter from the Kingdom of Sweden, through a new institution, the <a href="/wiki/Stockholms_Banco" title="Stockholms Banco">Bank of Stockholm</a>. While this private paper currency was largely a failure, the Swedish parliament eventually took over the issue of paper money in that country. By 1745, its paper money was inconvertible to specie, but acceptance was mandated by the government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> This fiat currency depreciated so rapidly that by 1776 it returned to a silver standard. Fiat money also has other roots in 17th-century Europe, having been introduced by the Bank of Amsterdam in 1683.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_France_1685.E2.80.931770">New France 1685–1770</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiat_money&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: New France 1685–1770">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Card_Money_in_New_France" title="Card Money in New France">Card Money in New France</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Canada_dollar" title="History of the Canada dollar">History of the Canada dollar</a></div>
<p>In 17th century <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a>, now part of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, the universally accepted <a href="/wiki/Medium_of_exchange" title="Medium of exchange">medium of exchange</a> was the <a href="/wiki/Beaver" title="Beaver">beaver</a> pelt. As the colony expanded, <a href="/wiki/Coin" title="Coin">coins</a> from <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> came to be widely used, but there was usually a shortage of French coins. In 1685, the colonial authorities in New France found themselves seriously short of <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">money</a>. A military expedition against the <a href="/wiki/Iroquois" title="Iroquois">Iroquois</a> had gone badly and tax revenues were down, reducing government money reserves. Typically, when short of funds, the government would simply delay paying merchants for purchases, but it was not safe to delay the payment of soldiers due to the risk of <a href="/wiki/Mutiny" title="Mutiny">mutiny</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jacques_de_Meulles" title="Jacques de Meulles">Jacques de Meulles</a>, the Intendant of Finance, came up with an ingenious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ad-hoc" title="Ad-hoc">ad-hoc</a> solution — the temporary issuance of paper money to pay the soldiers, in the form of <a href="/wiki/Playing_card" title="Playing card">playing cards</a>. He confiscated all the playing cards in the colony, cut them up into pieces, wrote denominations on the pieces, signed them, and issued them to the soldiers as pay in lieu of gold and silver. Because of the chronic shortages of money of all types in the colonies, these cards were readily accepted by merchants and the public and circulated freely at <a href="/wiki/Face_value" title="Face value">face value</a>. It was intended to be purely a temporary expedient, and it was not until years later that its role as a <a href="/wiki/Medium_of_exchange" title="Medium of exchange">medium of exchange</a> was recognized. The first issue of playing card money occurred in June 1685 and was redeemed three months later. However, the shortages of coinage reoccurred and more issues of card money were made in subsequent years. Because of their wide acceptance as money and the general shortage of money in the colony, many of the playing cards were not redeemed but continued to circulate, acting as a useful substitute for scarce gold and silver coins from France. Eventually, the <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_New_France" title="Governor of New France">Governor of New France</a> acknowledged their useful role as a circulating medium of exchange.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Candollar_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Candollar-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the finances of the French government deteriorated because of European wars, it reduced its financial support for its colonies, so the colonial authorities in Canada relied more and more on card money. By 1757, the government had discontinued all payments in coin and payments were made in paper instead. In an application of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gresham%E2%80%99s_Law" title="Gresham’s Law">Gresham’s Law</a> — bad money drives out good — people <a href="/wiki/Hoarding" title="Hoarding">hoarded</a> gold and silver, and used paper money instead. The costs of the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">war with the British</a> led to <a href="/wiki/Hyperinflation" title="Hyperinflation">rapid inflation</a> in New France. Following the <a href="/wiki/Canada_under_British_rule" title="Canada under British rule">British conquest</a> in 1760, the paper money became almost worthless, but business did not come to a halt because gold and silver that had been hoarded came back into circulation. Under the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1763)" title="Treaty of Paris (1763)">Treaty of Paris (1763)</a>, the French government agreed to convert the outstanding card money into <a href="/wiki/Debenture" title="Debenture">debentures</a>, but with the French government <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_default" title="Sovereign default">essentially bankrupt</a>, these bonds fell into default and by 1771 they were worthless.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Mint" title="Royal Canadian Mint">Royal Canadian Mint</a> still issues Playing Card Money in commemoration of its history, but now in 92.5% silver form with gold plate on the edge. It therefore has an <a href="/wiki/Intrinsic_value_(numismatics)" title="Intrinsic value (numismatics)">intrinsic value</a> which considerably exceeds its fiat value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The Bank of Canada and Canadian economists often use this early form of paper currency to illustrate the true nature of money for Canadians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Candollar_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Candollar-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="18th_and_19th_century">18th and 19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiat_money&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 18th and 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An early form of fiat currency in the <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies" title="Thirteen Colonies">American Colonies</a> were "<a href="/wiki/Bill_of_credit" title="Bill of credit">bills of credit</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michener2003_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Michener2003-20">[20]</a></sup> Provincial governments produced notes which were fiat currency, with the promise to allow holders to pay taxes in those notes. The notes were issued to pay current obligations and could be called by levying taxes at a later time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michener2003_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Michener2003-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>Since the notes were denominated in the local unit of account, they were circulated from person to person in non-tax transactions. These types of notes were issued particularly in <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>. Such money was sold at a discount of silver, which the government would then spend, and would expire at a fixed point in time later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michener2003_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Michener2003-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bills of credit have generated some controversy from their inception. Those who have wanted to highlight the dangers of inflation have focused on the colonies where the bills of credit depreciated most dramatically – New England and the Carolinas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michener2003_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-Michener2003-20">[20]</a></sup> Those who have wanted to defend the use of bills of credit in the colonies have focused on the middle colonies, where inflation was practically nonexistent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michener2003_20-4"><a href="#cite_note-Michener2003-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Colonial powers consciously introduced fiat currencies backed by taxes, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Hut_tax" title="Hut tax">hut taxes</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tax_per_head" title="Tax per head">poll taxes</a>, to mobilise economic resources in their new possessions, at least as a transitional arrangement. The purpose of such taxes was later served by <a href="/wiki/Property_tax" title="Property tax">property tax</a>. The repeated cycle of deflationary hard money, followed by inflationary paper money continued through much of the 18th and 19th centuries. Often nations would have dual currencies, with paper trading at some discount to specie backed money.</p>
<p>Examples include the “<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_(currency)" title="Continental (currency)">Continental</a>” issued by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Congress" title="U.S. Congress">U.S. Congress</a> before the <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">Constitution</a>; paper versus gold <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ducats" title="Ducats">ducats</a> in <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleonic</a> era <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, where paper often traded at 100:1 against gold; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Sea_Bubble" title="South Sea Bubble">South Sea Bubble</a>, which produced bank notes not backed by sufficient reserves; and the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Company" title="Mississippi Company">Mississippi Company</a> scheme of <a href="/wiki/John_Law_(economist)" title="John Law (economist)">John Law</a>.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, the Federal Government issued <a href="/wiki/United_States_Note" title="United States Note">United States Notes</a>, a form of paper fiat currency popularly known as 'greenbacks'. Their issue was limited by Congress just slightly over $340 million. During the 1870s, withdrawal of the notes from circulation was opposed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Greenback_Party" title="United States Greenback Party">United States Greenback Party</a>. The term 'fiat money' was used in the resolutions of an 1878 party convention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiat_money&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, governments and banks generally still promised to convert notes and coins into their underlying nominal commodity (redemption in specie) on demand. However, the costs of the war and the required repairs and economic growth based on government borrowing afterward made governments suspend redemption in specie. Some governments were careful of avoiding <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_default" title="Sovereign default">sovereign default</a> but not wary of the consequences of paying debts by consigning newly printed cash which had no metal-backed standard to their creditors, which led to <a href="/wiki/Hyperinflation" title="Hyperinflation">hyperinflation</a> – for example <a href="/wiki/Hyperinflation_in_the_Weimar_Republic" title="Hyperinflation in the Weimar Republic">the hyperinflation in the Weimar Republic</a>.</p>
<p>From 1944 to 1971, the <a href="/wiki/Bretton_Woods_system" title="Bretton Woods system">Bretton Woods agreement</a> fixed the value of 35 United States dollars to one <a href="/wiki/Troy_ounce" title="Troy ounce">troy ounce</a> of gold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Library_of_Congress_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Library_of_Congress-23">[23]</a></sup> Other currencies were pegged to the U.S. dollar at fixed rates. The U.S. promised to redeem dollars in gold to other central banks. Trade imbalances were corrected by gold reserve exchanges or by loans from the <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a>.</p>
<p>The Bretton Woods system collapsed in what became known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nixon_Shock" title="Nixon Shock">Nixon Shock</a>. This was a series of economic measures taken by United States President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> in 1971, including unilaterally canceling the direct <a href="/wiki/Convertibility" title="Convertibility">convertibility</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">United States dollar</a> to <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>. Since then, a system of national fiat monies has been used globally, with freely floating exchange rates between the major currencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Money_creation_and_regulation">Money creation and regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiat_money&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Money creation and regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Money_creation" title="Money creation">Money creation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monetary_policy" title="Monetary policy">Monetary policy</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Central_bank" title="Central bank">central bank</a> introduces new money into the economy by purchasing <a href="/wiki/Financial_asset" title="Financial asset">financial assets</a> or lending money to financial institutions. <a href="/wiki/Commercial_bank" title="Commercial bank">Commercial banks</a> then multiply this base money by credit creation through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fractional_reserve_banking" title="Fractional reserve banking">fractional reserve banking</a>, which expands the total supply of <a href="/wiki/Broad_money" title="Broad money">broad money</a> (cash plus <a href="/wiki/Demand_deposit" title="Demand deposit">demand deposits</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In modern economies, relatively little of the supply of broad money is in physical currency. For example, in December 2010 in the U.S., of the $8,853.4 billion in broad money supply (M2), only $915.7 billion (about 10%) consisted of physical coins and paper money.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The manufacturing of new physical money is usually the responsibility of the central bank, or sometimes, the government's <a href="/wiki/Treasury" title="Treasury">treasury</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inflation">Inflation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiat_money&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Inflation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">Inflation</a></div>
<p>The adoption of fiat currency by many countries, from the 18th century onwards, made much larger variations in the supply of money possible. Since then, huge increases in the supply of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paper_money" title="Paper money">paper money</a> have taken place in a number of countries, producing <a href="/wiki/Hyperinflation" title="Hyperinflation">hyperinflations</a> – episodes of extreme inflation rates much higher than those observed in earlier periods of <a href="/wiki/Commodity_money" title="Commodity money">commodity money</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Hyperinflation_in_the_Weimar_Republic" title="Hyperinflation in the Weimar Republic">hyperinflation in the Weimar Republic</a> of Germany is a notable example.</p>
<p>Economists generally believe that high rates of inflation and <a href="/wiki/Hyperinflation" title="Hyperinflation">hyperinflation</a> are caused by an excessive growth of the <a href="/wiki/Money_supply" title="Money supply">money supply</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Today, most economists favor a low and steady rate of inflation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-econjournalwatch.org_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-econjournalwatch.org-27">[27]</a></sup> Low (as opposed to zero or <a href="/wiki/Deflation" title="Deflation">negative</a>) inflation reduces the severity of economic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recessions" title="Recessions">recessions</a> by enabling the labor market to adjust more quickly in a downturn, and reduces the risk that a <a href="/wiki/Liquidity_trap" title="Liquidity trap">liquidity trap</a> prevents <a href="/wiki/Monetary_policy" title="Monetary policy">monetary policy</a> from stabilizing the economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> However, money supply growth does not always cause nominal price increases. Money supply growth may instead lead to stable prices at a time in which they would otherwise be falling. Some economists maintain that under the conditions of a <a href="/wiki/Liquidity_trap" title="Liquidity trap">liquidity trap</a>, large monetary injections are like "pushing on a string."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The task of keeping the rate of inflation low and stable is usually given to <a href="/wiki/Monetary_authority" title="Monetary authority">monetary authorities</a>. Generally, these monetary authorities are the <a href="/wiki/Central_bank" title="Central bank">central banks</a> that control monetary policy through the setting of <a href="/wiki/Interest_rate" title="Interest rate">interest rates</a>, through <a href="/wiki/Open_market_operation" title="Open market operation">open market operations</a>, and through the setting of banking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reserve_requirements" title="Reserve requirements">reserve requirements</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Loss_of_backing">Loss of backing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiat_money&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Loss of backing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A fiat-money currency greatly loses its value should the issuing <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a> or central <a href="/wiki/Bank" title="Bank">bank</a> either lose the ability to, or refuse to, further guarantee its value. The usual consequence is <a href="/wiki/Hyperinflation" title="Hyperinflation">hyperinflation</a>. Some examples where this has occurred are the <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwean_dollar" title="Zimbabwean dollar">Zimbabwean dollar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyperinflation#China" title="Hyperinflation">China in 1945</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hyperinflation_in_the_Weimar_Republic" title="Hyperinflation in the Weimar Republic">Mark in the Weimar Republic in 1923</a>.</p>
<p>But this need not necessarily occur; for example, the <a href="/wiki/Iraqi_Swiss_dinar" title="Iraqi Swiss dinar">so-called Swiss dinar</a> continued to retain value in <a href="/wiki/Kurdistan_Regional_Government" title="Kurdistan Regional Government">Kurdish Iraq</a> even after its legal tender status was withdrawn by the Iraqi central government which issued the notes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foote_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Foote-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
