<p>"<b>The Red-Headed League</b>" is one of the 56 <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a> short stories written by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Sir Arthur Conan Doyle">Sir Arthur Conan Doyle</a>. It first appeared in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strand_Magazine" title="Strand Magazine">The Strand Magazine</a></i> in August 1891, with illustrations by <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Paget" title="Sidney Paget">Sidney Paget</a>. Conan Doyle ranked "The Red-Headed League" second in his list of his twelve favourite Holmes stories. It is also the second of the twelve stories in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Sherlock_Holmes" title="The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes">The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes</a></i>, which was published in 1892.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">Plot summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Red-Headed_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Redh-02.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1507" data-file-width="1324" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Redh-02.jpg/220px-Redh-02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Redh-02.jpg/330px-Redh-02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Redh-02.jpg/440px-Redh-02.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Redh-02.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Watson reading the newspaper to Holmes and Wilson.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Jabez Wilson, a London <a href="/wiki/Pawnbroker" title="Pawnbroker">pawnbroker</a>, comes to consult Sherlock Holmes and Doctor Watson. While studying his client, both Holmes and Watson notice his <a href="/wiki/Red_hair" title="Red hair">red hair</a>, which has a distinct flame-like hue. Wilson tells them that some weeks before, his young assistant, Vincent Spaulding, urged him to respond to a newspaper want-ad offering highly-paid work to only red-headed male applicants. The next morning, Wilson had waited in a long line of fellow red-headed men, was interviewed and was the only applicant hired, because none of the other applicants qualified; their red hair was either too dark or too bright, and did not match Wilson's unique flame color.</p>
<p>Wilson tells Holmes that his business has been struggling. Since his pawn shop did most of its business in the evenings, he was able to vacate his shop for short periods in the afternoon, receiving £4 a week for several weeks (equal to £400/week today);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inflation-UK_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-UK-1">[1]</a></sup> the work was obviously useless clerical work in a bare office, only performed for nominal compliance with a <a href="/wiki/Will_and_testament" title="Will and testament">will</a>, whereupon he was made to copy the <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica" title="Encyclopædia Britannica">Encyclopædia Britannica</a></i>. Wilson learned much about the subjects starting with the "A" version and looked forward to getting into the "B" section. One morning, a sign on the locked office door inexplicably announced that "THE RED-HEADED LEAGUE IS DISSOLVED—Oct. 9, 1890."</p>
<p>Wilson went to the landlord, who said that he had never heard of Duncan Ross, the person who managed the league office. The landlord did remember the tenant with scarlet hair and gives him a card which directs Wilson to an artificial knee company. Wilson concludes by expressing his frustration at losing the £4 a week.</p>
<p>Holmes and Watson laugh at Wilson because of the ridiculous situation, but Holmes assures him that by Monday they will solve the case. Wilson leaves after having given the detective a description of Spaulding; Holmes decides to go and see Spaulding, who Holmes notices has dirty trouser knees. Holmes then taps on the pavement in front of the pawnbroker's shop. With the case solved, he calls <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Police_Inspector" title="Police Inspector">Police Inspector</a> Jones and Mr. Merryweather, a director of the bank located next door.</p>
<p>The four hide themselves in the bank vault and confront the thieves when they show up. They are John Clay, who has a long history of criminal activity already, and his helper Archie. Under the aliases of Spaulding and Ross, they had contrived the 'Red-Headed League' rigmarole to keep Wilson out of his shop while they dug in the basement, in order to break into the <a href="/wiki/Bank" title="Bank">bank</a> vault next door. Although paying Jabez Wilson four pounds a week was expensive, it was a pittance compared to the ill-gotten thousands they were looking to steal from the bank.</p>
<p>Back at Baker Street, Holmes explains to Watson how he solved the case.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Analysis">Analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Red-Headed_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The dates given in the story do not match the characters' descriptions of time passing. The date that Wilson sees the advertisement is 27 April 1890 and he has been at work for 8 weeks and says "Just two months ago."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Thus that happened by the end of June. However, the story begins by describing the Holmes's meeting with Wilson as being on "one day in the autumn of last year" and the date on the door telling of the League being dissolved is that of 9 October 1890, six months after the ad was placed.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dorothy_L._Sayers" title="Dorothy L. Sayers">Dorothy L. Sayers</a> analyzed this discrepancy and claims that the dates must have been 4 August and 4 October respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations">Adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Red-Headed_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The story was adapted for a TV episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes_(1951_TV_series)" title="Sherlock Holmes (1951 TV series)">Sherlock Holmes</a></i> starring <a href="/wiki/Alan_Wheatley" title="Alan Wheatley">Alan Wheatley</a> as Holmes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first American adaptation of the story was in the <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes_(1954_TV_series)" title="Sherlock Holmes (1954 TV series)">1954 TV series</a> starring <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Howard_(British_actor)" title="Ronald Howard (British actor)">Ronald Howard</a>.</p>
<p>An adaptation of "The Red-Headed League" was used for an episode of the 1965 television series <i><a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes_(1965_TV_series)" title="Sherlock Holmes (1965 TV series)">Sherlock Holmes</a></i> starring <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Wilmer" title="Douglas Wilmer">Douglas Wilmer</a> as Holmes and <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Stock_(actor)" title="Nigel Stock (actor)">Nigel Stock</a> as Watson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1985 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Red-Headed_League_(1985_Sherlock_Holmes_TV_Episode)" title="The Red-Headed League (1985 Sherlock Holmes TV Episode)">television adaptation</a> starring <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Brett" title="Jeremy Brett">Jeremy Brett</a>, the scheme was masterminded by <a href="/wiki/Professor_Moriarty" title="Professor Moriarty">Professor Moriarty</a> and Clay is Moriarty's star pupil of crime.</p>
<p>A radio adaptation aired on 26 April 1977, on the <i><a href="/wiki/CBS_Radio_Mystery_Theater" title="CBS Radio Mystery Theater">CBS Radio Mystery Theater</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Kevin_McCarthy_(actor)" title="Kevin McCarthy (actor)">Kevin McCarthy</a> was the voice of Holmes.</p>
<p>"The Red-Headed League" was dramatised for <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">BBC Radio 4</a> in 1990 as part of <a href="/wiki/Bert_Coules" title="Bert Coules">Bert Coules</a>' complete radio adaptation of the canon, starring <a href="/wiki/Clive_Merrison" title="Clive Merrison">Clive Merrison</a> as Holmes and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Williams_(actor)" title="Michael Williams (actor)">Michael Williams</a> as Watson. It also featured <a href="/wiki/James_Wilby" title="James Wilby">James Wilby</a> as Vincent Spaulding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the NHK puppetry <i><a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes_(puppetry)" title="Sherlock Holmes (puppetry)">Sherlock Holmes</a></i>, Jabez Wilson is a pupil of <a href="/wiki/Beeton_School" title="Beeton School">Beeton School</a> as well as Holmes and Watson and is invited to the Red-Headed Club by his senior Duncan Ross. But strangely enough, what he does in the club is painting balls, stones and bottles red. Holmes suspects that it is a means of Ross who wants to make Wilson stay away from a certain place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_other_media">In other media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Red-Headed_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In other media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the Sherlock Holmes <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parody</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Without_a_Clue" title="Without a Clue">Without a Clue</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Michael_Caine" title="Michael Caine">Michael Caine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ben_Kingsley" title="Ben Kingsley">Ben Kingsley</a>, Clay and Archie are shown breaking into the Royal Gallery.</p>
<p>The plot of the story was used by <a href="/wiki/The_deaf_man" title="The deaf man">The Deaf Man</a> as a means to confuse the detectives of the <a href="/wiki/87th_Precinct" title="87th Precinct">87th Precinct</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Ed_McBain" title="Ed McBain">Ed McBain</a> novel <i>The Heckler</i>.</p>
<p>A reference is made to "The Red-Headed League" in <i><a href="/wiki/Batman_and_Robin_(comic_book)" title="Batman and Robin (comic book)">Batman and Robin</a></i> #19 by villain Una Nemo. <a href="/wiki/Dick_Grayson" title="Dick Grayson">Batman</a> comments it is "A story where the whole point is--" and Nemo finishes "Not what it seems to be."</p>
<p>A reference to "The Red-Headed League" in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detective_Conan" title="Detective Conan">Detective Conan</a></i> episodes 225 and 464.</p>
<p>A reference to "The League of Red-Headed Gentleman" in <i><a href="/wiki/American_Dad!" title="American Dad!">American Dad!</a></i> "Escape From Pearl Balley" 2008.</p>
