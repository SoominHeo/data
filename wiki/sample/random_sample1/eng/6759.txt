<p>The <b>Red Fort</b> is a historical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort" title="Fort">fort</a> in the city of <a href="/wiki/Delhi" title="Delhi">Delhi</a> in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. It was the main residence of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Mughal_emperors" title="List of Mughal emperors">emperors</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mughal_dynasty" title="Mughal dynasty">Mughal dynasty</a> for nearly 200 years, until 1857. It is located in the center of Delhi and houses a number of museums. In addition to accommodating the emperors and their households, it was the ceremonial and political centre of the Mughal state and the setting for events critically impacting the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unesco_whl_entry_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-unesco_whl_entry-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Constructed in 1639 by the fifth Mughal Emperor <a href="/wiki/Shah_Jahan" title="Shah Jahan">Shah Jahan</a> as the palace of his fortified capital <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shahjahanabad" title="Shahjahanabad">Shahjahanabad</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Batra2008_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Batra2008-2">[2]</a></sup> the Red Fort is named for its massive enclosing walls of <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">red sandstone</a> and is adjacent to the older <a href="/wiki/Salimgarh_Fort" title="Salimgarh Fort">Salimgarh Fort</a>, built by <a href="/wiki/Islam_Shah_Suri" title="Islam Shah Suri">Islam Shah Suri</a> in 1546. The imperial apartments consist of a row of pavilions, connected by a water channel known as the Stream of Paradise (<i>Nahr-i-Bihisht</i>). The fort complex is considered to represent the zenith of Mughal creativity under Shah Jahan,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and although the palace was planned according to Islamic prototypes, each pavilion contains architectural elements typical of Mughal buildings that reflect a fusion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timurid_architecture" title="Timurid architecture">Timurid</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persian_architecture" title="Persian architecture">Persian</a> traditions. The Red Fort’s innovative architectural style, including its garden design, influenced later buildings and gardens in Delhi, <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">Rajasthan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Punjab" title="Punjab">Punjab</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kashmir" title="Kashmir">Kashmir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Braj" title="Braj">Braj</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rohilkhand" title="Rohilkhand">Rohilkhand</a> and elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unesco_whl_entry_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-unesco_whl_entry-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The fort was plundered of its artwork and jewels during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nadir_Shah" title="Nadir Shah">Nadir Shah</a>'s invasion of the Mughal Empire in 1747. Most of the fort's precious marble structures were subsequently destroyed by <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British colonialists</a> following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sepoy_Mutiny" title="Sepoy Mutiny">Sepoy Mutiny</a> of 1857.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The forts's defensive walls were largely spared, and the fortress was subsequently used as a <a href="/wiki/Garrison" title="Garrison">garrison</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The Red Fort was also the site where British colonialists put the <a href="/wiki/Bahadur_Shah_II" title="Bahadur Shah II">last Mughal Emperor</a> on trial before exiling him to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rangoon" title="Rangoon">Rangoon</a> in 1858.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Every year on the <a href="/wiki/Independence_Day_(India)" title="Independence Day (India)">Independence day of India</a> (15 August), the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_India" title="Prime Minister of India">Prime Minister</a> hoists the Indian <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_India" title="Flag of India">"tricolour flag"</a> at the main gate of the fort and delivers a nationally-broadcast speech from its <a href="/wiki/Defensive_wall" title="Defensive wall">ramparts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-business-standard.com_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-business-standard.com-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was designated a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNESCO_World_Heritage_Site" title="UNESCO World Heritage Site">UNESCO World Heritage Site</a> in 2007 as part of the Red Fort Complex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unesco_whl_entry_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-unesco_whl_entry-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Its English name, "Red Fort", is a translation of the <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_language" title="Hindustani language">Hindustani</a> <i>Lāl Qila</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urdu_language" title="Urdu language">Urdu</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ur" xml:lang="ur">لال قلعہ</span>‎, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindi_language" title="Hindi language">Hindi</a>: <span lang="hi" xml:lang="hi">लाल क़िला</span>) deriving from its red-sandstone walls. As the residence of the imperial family, the fort was originally known as the "Blessed Fort" (<i>Qila-i-Mubārak</i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urdu_language" title="Urdu language">Urdu</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ur" xml:lang="ur">قلعہ مبارک</span>‎, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindi_language" title="Hindi language">Hindi</a>: <span lang="hi" xml:lang="hi">क़िला मुबारक</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spellman2004_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spellman2004-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KiaOakes2002_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-KiaOakes2002-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Agra_Fort" title="Agra Fort">Agra Fort</a> is also called <i>Lāl Qila'.</i></p>

<p>Emperor <a href="/wiki/Shah_Jahan" title="Shah Jahan">Shah Jahan</a> commissioned construction of the Red Fort on 12 May 1639, when he decided to shift his capital from Agra to Delhi. Originally red and white, the Shah's favourite colours,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> its design is credited to architect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ustad_Ahmad_Lahauri" title="Ustad Ahmad Lahauri">Ustad Ahmad Lahauri</a>, who also constructed the <a href="/wiki/Taj_Mahal" title="Taj Mahal">Taj Mahal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The fort lies along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yamuna_River" title="Yamuna River">Yamuna River</a>, which fed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moats" title="Moats">moats</a> surrounding most of the walls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Construction began in the sacred month of <a href="/wiki/Muharram" title="Muharram">Muharram</a>, on 13 May 1638.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASI3_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ASI3-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:01</sup> Supervised by Shah Jahan, it was completed on 6 April 1648.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Unlike other Mughal forts, the Red Fort's boundary walls are asymmetrical to contain the older Salimgarh Fort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASI3_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-ASI3-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:04</sup> The fortress-palace was a focal point of the medieval city of Shahjahanabad, which is present-day <a href="/wiki/Old_Delhi" title="Old Delhi">Old Delhi</a>. Its planning and aesthetics represent the zenith of Mughal creativity prevailing during Shah Jahan's reign.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> His successor <a href="/wiki/Aurangzeb" title="Aurangzeb">Aurangzeb</a> added the Pearl Mosque to the emperor's private quarters, constructing <a href="/wiki/Barbican" title="Barbican">barbicans</a> in front of the two main gates to make the entrance to the palace more circuitous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASI3_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-ASI3-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:08</sup></p>
<p>The administrative and fiscal structure of the Mughal dynasty declined after Aurangzeb, and the 18th century saw a degeneration of the palace. When <a href="/wiki/Jahandar_Shah" title="Jahandar Shah">Jahandar Shah</a> took over the Red Fort in 1712, it had been without an emperor for 30 years. Within a year of beginning his rule, Shah was murdered and replaced by <a href="/wiki/Farrukhsiyar" title="Farrukhsiyar">Farrukhsiyar</a>. To raise money, the silver ceiling of the Rang Mahal was replaced by copper during this period. <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Shah" title="Muhammad Shah">Muhammad Shah</a>, known as 'Rangila' (the Colourful) for his interest in art, took over the Red Fort in 1719. In 1739, Persian emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nadir_Shah" title="Nadir Shah">Nadir Shah</a> easily defeated the Mughal army, plundering the Red Fort including the <a href="/wiki/Peacock_Throne" title="Peacock Throne">Peacock Throne</a>. Nadir Shah returned to Persia after three months, leaving a destroyed city and a weakened Mughal empire to Muhammad Shah.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASI3_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-ASI3-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:09</sup> The internal weakness of the Mughal empire made the Mughals titular heads of Delhi, and a 1752 treaty made the <a href="/wiki/Maratha_Empire" title="Maratha Empire">Marathas</a> protectors of the throne at Delhi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The 1758 Maratha conquest of <a href="/wiki/Lahore" title="Lahore">Lahore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peshawar" title="Peshawar">Peshawar</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> placed them in conflict with <a href="/wiki/Ahmad_Shah_Durrani" title="Ahmad Shah Durrani">Ahmad Shah Durrani</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In 1760, the <a href="/wiki/Maratha" title="Maratha">Marathas</a> removed and melted the silver ceiling of the <a href="/wiki/Diwan-i-Khas_(Red_Fort)" title="Diwan-i-Khas (Red Fort)">Diwan-i-Khas</a> to raise funds for the defence of Delhi from the armies of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ahmed_Shah_Durrani" title="Ahmed Shah Durrani">Ahmed Shah Durrani</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kulkarni345_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-kulkarni345-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google140_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-google140-23">[23]</a></sup> In 1761, after the Marathas lost the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Panipat_(1761)" title="Battle of Panipat (1761)">third battle of Panipat</a>, Delhi was raided by Ahmed Shah Durrani. Ten years later, <a href="/wiki/Shah_Alam_II" title="Shah Alam II">Shah Alam</a> ascended the throne in Delhi with Maratha support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASI3_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-ASI3-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:10</sup> In 1783 the Sikh <a href="/wiki/Misl" title="Misl">Misl</a> Karorisinghia, led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baghel_Singh_Dhaliwal" title="Baghel Singh Dhaliwal">Baghel Singh Dhaliwal</a>, conquered Delhi and the Red Fort. The Sikhs agreed to restore Shah Alam as emperor and retreat from the fort if the Mughals would build and protect seven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gurudwara" title="Gurudwara">Gurudwaras</a> in Delhi for the <a href="/wiki/Sikh_gurus" title="Sikh gurus">Sikh gurus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Second_Anglo-Maratha_War" title="Second Anglo-Maratha War">Second Anglo-Maratha War</a> in 1803, forces of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_East_India_Company" title="British East India Company">British East India Company</a> defeated Maratha forces in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Delhi,_1803" title="Battle of Delhi, 1803">Battle of Delhi</a>; this ended Maratha rule of the city and their control of the Red Fort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mayaram2003_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mayaram2003-25">[25]</a></sup> After the battle, the British took over the administration of Mughal territories and installed a <a href="/wiki/Resident_(title)#Residents_in_Asia" title="Resident (title)">Resident</a> at the Red Fort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASI3_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-ASI3-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:11</sup> The last Mughal emperor to occupy the fort, <a href="/wiki/Bahadur_Shah_II" title="Bahadur Shah II">Bahadur Shah II</a>, became a symbol of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Rebellion_of_1857" title="Indian Rebellion of 1857">1857 rebellion</a> against the British in which the residents of Shahjahanbad participated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASI3_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-ASI3-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:15</sup></p>
<p>Despite its position as the seat of Mughal power and its defensive capabilities, the Red Fort was not defended during the 1857 uprising against the British. After the rebellion failed, Bahadur Shah II left the fort on 17 September and was apprehended by British forces. He returned to Red Fort as a prisoner of the British, was tried in 1858 and exiled to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rangoon" title="Rangoon">Rangoon</a> on 7 October of that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> With the end of Mughal reign, the British sanctioned the systematic plunder of valuables from the fort's palaces. All furniture was removed or destroyed; the harem apartments, servants' quarters and gardens were destroyed, and a line of stone barracks built.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Only the marble buildings on the east side at the imperial enclosure escaped complete destruction, but were looted and damaged. While the defensive walls and towers were relatively unharmed, more than two-thirds of the inner structures were destroyed by the British. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Curzon" title="Lord Curzon">Lord Curzon</a>, Viceroy of India from 1899–1905, ordered repairs to the fort including reconstruction of the walls and the restoration of the gardens complete with a watering system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of the jewels and artworks of the Red Fort were looted and stolen during Nadir Shah's invasion of 1747 and again after the failed Indian Rebellion of 1857 against the British colonialists. They were eventually sold to private collectors or the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Library" title="British Library">British Library</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_and_Albert_Museum" title="Victoria and Albert Museum">Victoria and Albert Museum</a>. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Koh-i-Noor" title="Koh-i-Noor">Koh-i-Noor</a> diamond, the jade <a href="/wiki/Wine_cup_of_Shah_Jahan" title="Wine cup of Shah Jahan">wine cup of Shah Jahan</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Bahadur_Shah_II" title="Crown of Bahadur Shah II">crown of Bahadur Shah II</a> are all currently located in London. Various requests for <a href="/wiki/Restitution" title="Restitution">restitution</a> have so far been rejected by the British government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>1911 saw the visit of the British king and queen for the <a href="/wiki/Delhi_Durbar" title="Delhi Durbar">Delhi Durbar</a>. In preparation of the visit, some buildings were restored. The <a href="/wiki/Red_Fort_Archaeological_Museum" title="Red Fort Archaeological Museum">Red Fort Archaeological Museum</a> was also moved from the drum house to the <i><a href="/wiki/Mumtaz_Mahal" title="Mumtaz Mahal">Mumtaz Mahal</a></i>.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/INA_trials" title="INA trials">INA trials</a>, also known as the <i>Red Fort Trials</i>, refer to the courts-martial of a number of officers of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_National_Army" title="Indian National Army">Indian National Army</a>. The first was held between November and December 1945 at the Red Fort.</p>
<p>On 15 August 1947, the first <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_India" title="Prime Minister of India">Prime Minister of India</a> <a href="/wiki/Jawaharlal_Nehru" title="Jawaharlal Nehru">Jawaharlal Nehru</a> raised the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_India" title="Flag of India">Indian national flag</a> above the <a href="/wiki/Lahori_Gate,_Delhi" title="Lahori Gate, Delhi">Lahore Gate</a>. On each subsequent Independence Day, the prime minister has raised the flag and given a speech that is broadcast nationally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_India" title="Partition of India">Indian Independence</a>, the site experienced few changes, and the Red Fort continued to be used as a military <a href="/wiki/Cantonment" title="Cantonment">cantonment</a>. A significant part of the fort remained under Indian Army control until 22 December 2003, when it was given to the <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_Survey_of_India" title="Archaeological Survey of India">Archaeological Survey of India</a> for restoration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Defence2005_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Defence2005-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muslim_India_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Muslim_India-32">[32]</a></sup> In 2009 the Comprehensive Conservation and Management Plan (CCMP), prepared by the Archaeological Survey of India under <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_India" title="Supreme Court of India">Supreme Court</a> directions to revitalise the fort, was announced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_fort_today"><span id="Red_Fort_today"></span> The fort today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The fort today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Every year on <a href="/wiki/Independence_Day_(India)" title="Independence Day (India)">India's Independence Day</a> (15 August), the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_India" title="Prime Minister of India">Prime Minister of India</a> hoists the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_India" title="Flag of India">national flag</a> at the Red Fort and delivers a nationally-broadcast speech from its <a href="/wiki/Defensive_wall" title="Defensive wall">ramparts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-business-standard.com_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-business-standard.com-6">[6]</a></sup> The Red Fort, the largest monument in Delhi,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schreitm.C3.BCllerDhamotharan2012_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schreitm.C3.BCllerDhamotharan2012-36">[36]</a></sup> is one of its most popular tourist destinations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Devashish2011_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Devashish2011-37">[37]</a></sup> and attracts thousands of visitors every year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murthy_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Murthy-38">[38]</a></sup> A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_and_light_show" title="Sound and light show">sound and light show</a> describing Mughal history is a tourist attraction in the evenings. The major architectural features are in mixed condition; the extensive water features are dry. Some buildings are in fairly-good condition, with their decorative elements undisturbed; in others, the marble inlaid flowers have been removed by looters. The tea house, although not in its historical state, is a working restaurant. The mosque and <i>hamam</i> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turkish_Bath" title="Turkish Bath">Turkish Bath</a> are closed to the public, although visitors can peer through their glass windows or marble latticework. Walkways are crumbling, and public toilets are available at the entrance and inside the park.</p>
<p>The Lahore Gate entrance leads to a mall with jewellery and craft stores. There is also a museum of "blood paintings", depicting young 20th-century Indian martyrs and their stories, an archaeological museum and an Indian war-memorial museum.</p>
<p>The Red fort appears on the front of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_500-rupee_note" title="Indian 500-rupee note"><span style="white-space: nowrap">₹</span>500 note</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Mahatma_Gandhi_New_Series" title="Mahatma Gandhi New Series">Mahatma Gandhi New Series</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_rupee" title="Indian rupee">Indian rupee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-500RBI_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-500RBI-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Security"><span id="Security_threats"></span>Security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To prevent terrorist attacks, security is especially strict around the Red Fort on the eve of Indian Independence Day. <a href="/wiki/Delhi_Police" title="Delhi Police">Delhi Police</a> and paramilitary personnel keep watch on neighbourhoods around the fort, and <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Guard" title="National Security Guard">National Security Guard</a> sharpshooters are deployed on high-rises near the fort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNA1_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-DNA1-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The airspace around the fort is a designated <a href="/wiki/No-fly_zone" title="No-fly zone">no-fly zone</a> during the celebration to prevent air attacks,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Safe_house" title="Safe house">safe houses</a> exist in nearby areas to which the Prime Minister and other Indian leaders may retreat in the event of an attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNA1_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-DNA1-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The fort was the site of a <a href="/wiki/2000_terrorist_attack_on_Red_Fort" title="2000 terrorist attack on Red Fort">terrorist attack on 22 December 2000</a>, carried out by six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lashkar-e-Toiba" title="Lashkar-e-Toiba">Lashkar-e-Toiba</a> members. Two soldiers and a civilian were killed in what the news media described as an attempt to derail India-Pakistan peace talks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:White_Sawan_or_Bhadon_pavilion.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/White_Sawan_or_Bhadon_pavilion.jpg/220px-White_Sawan_or_Bhadon_pavilion.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/White_Sawan_or_Bhadon_pavilion.jpg/330px-White_Sawan_or_Bhadon_pavilion.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/White_Sawan_or_Bhadon_pavilion.jpg/440px-White_Sawan_or_Bhadon_pavilion.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:White_Sawan_or_Bhadon_pavilion.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
White Sawan or Bhadon pavilion</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Red Fort has an area of 254.67 acres (103.06 ha) enclosed by 2.41 kilometres (1.50 mi) of defensive walls,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Batra2008_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Batra2008-2">[2]</a></sup> punctuated by turrets and bastions and varying in height from 18 metres (59 ft) on the river side to 33 metres (108 ft) on the city side. The fort is octagonal, with the north-south axis longer than the east-west axis. The marble, floral decorations and double domes in the fort's buildings exemplify later Mughal architecture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langmead178_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Langmead178-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>It showcases a high level of ornamentation, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kohinoor" title="Kohinoor">Kohinoor</a> diamond was reportedly part of the furnishings. The fort's artwork synthesises Persian, European and Indian art, resulting in a unique Shahjahani style rich in form, expression and colour. Red Fort is one of the building complexes of India encapsulating a long period of history and its arts. Even before its 1913 commemoration as a monument of national importance, efforts were made to preserve it for posterity.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lahori_Gate_(Red_Fort)" title="Lahori Gate (Red Fort)">Lahori</a> and Delhi Gates were used by the public, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khizrabad" title="Khizrabad">Khizrabad</a> Gate was for the emperor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASI3_14-7"><a href="#cite_note-ASI3-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:04</sup> The Lahore Gate is the main entrance, leading to a domed shopping area known as the <i>Chatta Chowk</i> (covered bazaar).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_structures">Major structures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Major structures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Throne_of_king_Akbar_Red_fort_Delhi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Throne_of_king_Akbar_Red_fort_Delhi.jpg/220px-Throne_of_king_Akbar_Red_fort_Delhi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Throne_of_king_Akbar_Red_fort_Delhi.jpg/330px-Throne_of_king_Akbar_Red_fort_Delhi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Throne_of_king_Akbar_Red_fort_Delhi.jpg/440px-Throne_of_king_Akbar_Red_fort_Delhi.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Throne_of_king_Akbar_Red_fort_Delhi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Throne of The king <a href="/wiki/Akbar" title="Akbar">Akbar</a> at Red fort Delhi</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The most important surviving structures are the walls and ramparts, the main gates, the audience halls and the imperial apartments on the eastern riverbank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chawari_Bazar">Chawari Bazar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Chawari Bazar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Chawari Bazar is located in front of Red Fort.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lahori_Gate">Lahori Gate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lahori Gate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Red_Fort_in_Delhi_03-2016_img1.jpg"><img alt="Red sandstone gate of the fortress" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3387" data-file-width="5080" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Red_Fort_in_Delhi_03-2016_img1.jpg/220px-Red_Fort_in_Delhi_03-2016_img1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Red_Fort_in_Delhi_03-2016_img1.jpg/330px-Red_Fort_in_Delhi_03-2016_img1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Red_Fort_in_Delhi_03-2016_img1.jpg/440px-Red_Fort_in_Delhi_03-2016_img1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Red_Fort_in_Delhi_03-2016_img1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Delhi_Gate_(Red_Fort)" title="Delhi Gate (Red Fort)">Delhi Gate</a>, which is almost identical in appearance to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lahori_Gate_(Red_Fort)" title="Lahori Gate (Red Fort)">Lahori Gate</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lahori_Gate_(Red_Fort)" title="Lahori Gate (Red Fort)">Lahori Gate</a> is the main gate to the Red Fort, named for its orientation towards the city of <a href="/wiki/Lahore" title="Lahore">Lahore</a>. During <a href="/wiki/Aurangzeb" title="Aurangzeb">Aurangzeb</a>'s reign, the beauty of the gate was spoiled by the addition of bastions, which Shahjahan described as "a veil drawn across the face of a beautiful woman".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fanshawe_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-fanshawe-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Milli_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Milli-49">[49]</a></sup> Every <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_Independence_Day" title="Indian Independence Day">Indian Independence Day</a> since 1947, the national flag is unfurled and the Prime Minister makes a speech from its ramparts.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Delhi_Gate">Delhi Gate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Delhi Gate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Delhi_Gate_(Red_Fort)" title="Delhi Gate (Red Fort)">Delhi Gate</a> is the southern public entrance and in layout and appearance similar to the Lahori Gate. Two life-size stone elephants on either side of the gate face each other. These were renewed by Lord Curzon in 1903 after their earlier demolition by <a href="/wiki/Aurangzeb" title="Aurangzeb">Aurangzeb</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_Gate">Water Gate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Water Gate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A minor gate, the Water Gate is at the southeastern end of the walls. It was formerly on the riverbank; although the river has changed course since the fort's construction, the name has remained.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chhatta_Chowk">Chhatta Chowk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Chhatta Chowk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Adjacent to the Lahori Gate is the <i><a href="/wiki/Chhatta_Chowk" title="Chhatta Chowk">Chhatta Chowk</a></i>, where silk, jewellery and other items for the imperial household were sold during the Mughal period. The bazaar leads to an open outer court, where it crosses the large north-south street which originally divided the fort's military functions (to the west) from the palaces (to the east). The southern end of the street is the Delhi Gate.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Naubat_Khana">Naubat Khana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Naubat Khana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Naubat_Khana_Red_Fort_1857.jpg"><img alt="Photo of courtyard shortly after the 1857 uprising" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="538" data-file-width="712" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Naubat_Khana_Red_Fort_1857.jpg/220px-Naubat_Khana_Red_Fort_1857.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Naubat_Khana_Red_Fort_1857.jpg/330px-Naubat_Khana_Red_Fort_1857.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Naubat_Khana_Red_Fort_1857.jpg/440px-Naubat_Khana_Red_Fort_1857.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Naubat_Khana_Red_Fort_1857.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Naubat_Khana_(Red_Fort)" title="Naubat Khana (Red Fort)">Naubat Khana</a></i> and the courtyard before its destruction by the British, in an 1858 photograph</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The vaulted arcade of the <i>Chhatta Chowk</i> ends in the centre of the outer court, which measured 540 by 360 feet (160 m × 110 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archive1_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-archive1-51">[51]</a></sup> The side arcades and central tank were destroyed after the 1857 rebellion.</p>
<p>In the east wall of the court stands the now-isolated <i><a href="/wiki/Naubat_Khana_(Red_Fort)" title="Naubat Khana (Red Fort)">Naubat Khana</a></i> (also known as <i>Nakkar Khana</i>), the drum house. Music was played daily, at scheduled times and everyone, except royalty, was required to dismount.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diwan-i-Aam">Diwan-i-Aam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Diwan-i-Aam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Diwan-i-Aam,_Red_Fort,_Delhi_-_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="4640" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Diwan-i-Aam%2C_Red_Fort%2C_Delhi_-_2.jpg/220px-Diwan-i-Aam%2C_Red_Fort%2C_Delhi_-_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Diwan-i-Aam%2C_Red_Fort%2C_Delhi_-_2.jpg/330px-Diwan-i-Aam%2C_Red_Fort%2C_Delhi_-_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Diwan-i-Aam%2C_Red_Fort%2C_Delhi_-_2.jpg/440px-Diwan-i-Aam%2C_Red_Fort%2C_Delhi_-_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Diwan-i-Aam,_Red_Fort,_Delhi_-_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Diwan-i-Aam</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The inner main court to which the <i>Nakkar Khana</i> led was 540 feet (160 m) wide and 420 feet (130 m) deep, surrounded by guarded galleries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archive1_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-archive1-51">[51]</a></sup> On the far side is the <i><a href="/wiki/Diwan-i-Am_(Red_Fort)" title="Diwan-i-Am (Red Fort)">Diwan-i-Aam</a></i>, the Public Audience Hall.</p>
<p>The hall's columns and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Engrailed_(heraldry)" title="Engrailed (heraldry)">engrailed</a> arches exhibit fine craftsmanship, and the hall was originally decorated with white <i>chunam</i> stucco.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archive1_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-archive1-51">[51]</a></sup> In the back in the raised recess the emperor gave his audience in the marble balcony (<i><a href="/wiki/Jharokha" title="Jharokha">jharokha</a></i>).</p>
<p>The <i>Diwan-i-Aam</i> was also used for state functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langmead178_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Langmead178-45">[45]</a></sup> The courtyard (<i>mardana</i>) behind it leads to the imperial apartments.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nahr-i-Bihisht">Nahr-i-Bihisht</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Nahr-i-Bihisht">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The imperial apartments consist of a row of pavilions on a raised platform along the eastern edge of the fort, overlooking the <a href="/wiki/Yamuna" title="Yamuna">Yamuna</a>. The pavilions are connected by a canal, known as the <i>Nahr-i-Bihisht</i> ("Stream of Paradise"), running through the centre of each pavilion. Water is drawn from the Yamuna via a tower, the <i>Shahi Burj</i>, at the northeast corner of the fort. The palace is designed to emulate paradise as described in the Quran. In the riverbed below the imperial apartments and connected buildings was a space known as <i>zer-jharokha</i> ("beneath the <a href="/wiki/Latticework" title="Latticework">latticework</a>").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archive1_51-3"><a href="#cite_note-archive1-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mumtaz_Mahal">Mumtaz Mahal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Mumtaz Mahal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Red_Fort_8.JPG"><img alt="Simple white building, with persons on walkway" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Red_Fort_8.JPG/220px-Red_Fort_8.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Red_Fort_8.JPG/330px-Red_Fort_8.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Red_Fort_8.JPG/440px-Red_Fort_8.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Red_Fort_8.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Mumtaz_Mahal_(Red_Fort)" title="Mumtaz Mahal (Red Fort)">Mumtaz Mahal</a></i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The two southernmost pavilions of the palace are <i>zenana</i>s (women's quarters), consisting of the <i><a href="/wiki/Mumtaz_Mahal_(Red_Fort)" title="Mumtaz Mahal (Red Fort)">Mumtaz Mahal</a></i> and the larger <i>Rang Mahal</i>. The <i>Mumtaz Mahal</i> houses the <a href="/wiki/Red_Fort_Archaeological_Museum" title="Red Fort Archaeological Museum">Red Fort Archaeological Museum</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rang_Mahal">Rang Mahal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Rang Mahal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Rang_Mahal_(Red_Fort)" title="Rang Mahal (Red Fort)">Rang Mahal</a></i> housed the emperor's wives and mistresses. Its name means "Palace of Colours", since it was brightly painted and decorated with a mosaic of mirrors. The central marble pool is fed by the <i>Nahr-i-Bihisht</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Khas_Mahal">Khas Mahal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Khas Mahal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Khas_Mahal_(Red_Fort)" title="Khas Mahal (Red Fort)">Khas Mahal</a></i> was the emperor's apartment. Connected to it is the <i><a href="/wiki/Muthamman_Burj_(Red_Fort)" title="Muthamman Burj (Red Fort)">Muthamman Burj</a></i>, an octagonal tower where he appeared before the people waiting on the riverbank.This was done mostly by all the kings present at that time.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diwan-i-Khas">Diwan-i-Khas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Diwan-i-Khas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A gate on the north side of the <i><a href="/wiki/Diwan-i-Am_(Red_Fort)" title="Diwan-i-Am (Red Fort)">Diwan-i-Aam</a></i> leads to the innermost court of the palace (<i>Jalau Khana</i>) and the <i><a href="/wiki/Diwan-i-Khas_(Red_Fort)" title="Diwan-i-Khas (Red Fort)">Diwan-i-Khas</a></i> (Hall of Private Audience). It is constructed of white marble, inlaid with precious stones. The once-silver ceiling has been restored in wood. <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Bernier" title="François Bernier">François Bernier</a> described seeing the jewelled <a href="/wiki/Peacock_Throne" title="Peacock Throne">Peacock Throne</a> here during the 17th century. At either end of the hall, over the two outer arches, is an inscription by Persian poet <a href="/wiki/Amir_Khusrow" title="Amir Khusrow">Amir Khusrow</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>If heaven can be on the face of the earth,<br/></p>
<p>It is this, it is this, it is this.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hammam">Hammam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Hammam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Hammam_(Red_Fort)" title="Hammam (Red Fort)">hammam</a></i> were the imperial baths, consisting of three domed rooms floored with white marble.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Baoli">Baoli</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Baoli">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Red_Fort_Baoli.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2517" data-file-width="3776" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Red_Fort_Baoli.jpg/220px-Red_Fort_Baoli.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Red_Fort_Baoli.jpg/330px-Red_Fort_Baoli.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Red_Fort_Baoli.jpg/440px-Red_Fort_Baoli.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Red_Fort_Baoli.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The baoli (step-well) at the Red Fort, Delhi</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baoli" title="Baoli">baoli</a> or step-well, believed to pre-date Red Fort, is one of the few monuments that were not demolished by the British after the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Rebellion_of_1857" title="Indian Rebellion of 1857">Indian Rebellion of 1857</a>. The chambers within the baoli were converted into a prison. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_National_Army_Trials" title="Indian National Army Trials">Indian National Army Trials</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Fort_Trials" title="Red Fort Trials">Red Fort Trials</a>) in 1945-46, it housed Indian National Army officers Colonel Shah Nawaz Khan, Colonel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prem_Kumar_Sahgal" title="Prem Kumar Sahgal">Prem Kumar Sahgal</a>, and Colonel <a href="/wiki/Gurbaksh_Singh_Dhillon" title="Gurbaksh Singh Dhillon">Gurbaksh Singh Dhillon</a>. The Red Fort Baoli is uniquely designed with two sets of staircases leading down to the well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Red_Fort_Baoli_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Red_Fort_Baoli-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Moti_Masjid">Moti Masjid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Moti Masjid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>West of the hammam is the <i><a href="/wiki/Moti_Masjid_(Red_Fort)" title="Moti Masjid (Red Fort)">Moti Masjid</a></i>, the Pearl Mosque. A later addition, it was built in 1659 as a private mosque for Aurangzeb. It is a small, three-domed mosque carved in white marble, with a three-arched screen leading down to the courtyard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hira_Mahal">Hira Mahal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Hira Mahal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Hira_Mahal_(Red_Fort)" title="Hira Mahal (Red Fort)">Hira Mahal</a></i> is a pavilion on the southern edge of the fort, built under Bahadur Shah II and at the end of the <i>Hayat Baksh</i> garden. The <i>Moti Mahal</i> on the northern edge, a twin building, was destroyed during (or after) the 1857 rebellion.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fuerte_Rojo-Delhi-India62.JPG"><img alt="Low, white building with ornate pillars and arches" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3888" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Fuerte_Rojo-Delhi-India62.JPG/220px-Fuerte_Rojo-Delhi-India62.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Fuerte_Rojo-Delhi-India62.JPG/330px-Fuerte_Rojo-Delhi-India62.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Fuerte_Rojo-Delhi-India62.JPG/440px-Fuerte_Rojo-Delhi-India62.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fuerte_Rojo-Delhi-India62.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Shahi_Burj_(Red_Fort)" title="Shahi Burj (Red Fort)">Shahi Burj</a></i> and its pavilion</div>

<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Shahi_Burj_(Red_Fort)" title="Shahi Burj (Red Fort)">Shahi Burj</a></i> was the emperor's main study of the; its name means "Emperor's Tower", and it originally had a <i><a href="/wiki/Chhatri" title="Chhatri">chhatri</a></i> on top. Heavily damaged, the tower is undergoing reconstruction. In front of it is a marble pavilion added by Aurangzeb.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hayat_Bakhsh_Bagh">Hayat Bakhsh Bagh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Hayat Bakhsh Bagh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fuerte_Rojo-Delhi-India69.JPG"><img alt="Square red sandstone building in a dried-out water tank" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3888" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Fuerte_Rojo-Delhi-India69.JPG/220px-Fuerte_Rojo-Delhi-India69.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Fuerte_Rojo-Delhi-India69.JPG/330px-Fuerte_Rojo-Delhi-India69.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Fuerte_Rojo-Delhi-India69.JPG/440px-Fuerte_Rojo-Delhi-India69.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fuerte_Rojo-Delhi-India69.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Red <i>Zafar Mahal</i> and white <i>Sawan/Bhadon</i> pavilion behind it in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hayat_Bakhsh_Bagh_(Red_Fort)" title="Hayat Bakhsh Bagh (Red Fort)">Hayat Bakhsh Bagh</a></i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hayat_Bakhsh_Bagh_(Red_Fort)" title="Hayat Bakhsh Bagh (Red Fort)">Hayat Bakhsh Bagh</a></i> is the "Life-Bestowing Garden" in the northeast part of the complex. It features a <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoir</a>, which is now dry, and channels through which the Nahr-i-Bihisht flows. At each end is a white marble pavilion, called the Sawan and Bhadon Pavilions, named after the <a href="/wiki/Hindu_calendar" title="Hindu calendar">Hindu months</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shraavana" title="Shraavana">Sawan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bhadra_(Hindu_calendar)" title="Bhadra (Hindu calendar)">Bhadon</a>. In the centre of the reservoir is the red-sandstone <i>Zafar Mahal</i>, added in around 1842 by <a href="/wiki/Bahadur_Shah_II" title="Bahadur Shah II">Bahadur Shah Zafar</a>, and named after him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>Smaller gardens (such as the <i>Mehtab Bagh</i> or Moonlight Garden) existed west of it, but were destroyed when the British barracks were built.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASI3_14-8"><a href="#cite_note-ASI3-14">[14]</a></sup> There are plans to restore the gardens. Beyond these, the road to the north leads to an arched bridge and the <a href="/wiki/Salimgarh_Fort" title="Salimgarh Fort">Salimgarh Fort</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Princes.27_quarter">Princes' quarter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Princes' quarter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>North of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hayat_Bakhsh_Bagh_(Red_Fort)" title="Hayat Bakhsh Bagh (Red Fort)">Hayat Bakhsh Bagh</a></i> and the <i>Shahi Burj</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Princes%27_quarter_(Red_Fort)" title="Princes' quarter (Red Fort)">quarter of the imperial princes</a>. This was used by member of the Mughal royal family and was largely destroyed by the British forces after the rebellion. One of the palaces was converted into a tea house for the soldiers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Freedom-Struggle_Museum">Freedom-Struggle Museum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Freedom-Struggle Museum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Considering the role the Red Fort has played in the freedom struggle, <i>Swatantrata Sangram Sanghralaya</i> ("Freedom-Struggle Museum") was set up in one of the double storeyed army barracks in 1995. The museum provides a glimpse of major phases of India's struggle for freedom, from the <a href="/wiki/India%27s_First_War_of_Independence_(term)" title="India's First War of Independence (term)">First War of Independence of 1857</a> to India's Independence in 1947. In the museum, the history of the freedom struggle is depicted through photographs, documents, paintings, <a href="/wiki/Lithography" title="Lithography">lithographs</a> and objects like guns, pistols, swords, shields, badges, medals<a href="/wiki/Diorama" title="Diorama">, dioramas</a>, sculptures etc.</p>
