<p><i><b>Ceratophyllum demersum</b></i>, commonly known as <b>hornwort</b>, <b>rigid hornwort</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BSBI07_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-BSBI07-1">[1]</a></sup> <b>coontail</b>, or <b>coon's tail</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-issg-ecology_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-issg-ecology-2">[2]</a></sup> is a species of <i><a href="/wiki/Ceratophyllum" title="Ceratophyllum">Ceratophyllum</a></i>. It is a submerged, free-floating <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_plant" title="Aquatic plant">aquatic plant</a>, with a <a href="/wiki/Cosmopolitan_distribution" title="Cosmopolitan distribution">cosmopolitan distribution</a>, native to all continents except Antarctica. It is a harmful introduced weed in New Zealand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-issg-ecology_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-issg-ecology-2">[2]</a></sup> It is also a popular aquarium plant.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ceratophyllum_demersum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An aquatic plant, <i>Ceratophyllum demersum</i> has stems that reach lengths of 1–3 m (3–10 ft), with numerous side shoots making a single specimen appear as a large, bushy mass. The <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> are produced in whorls of six to twelve, each leaf 8–40 mm long, simple, or forked into two to eight thread-like segments edged with spiny teeth; they are stiff and brittle. It is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_sexuality" title="Plant sexuality">monoecious</a>, with separate male and female <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a> produced on the same plant. The flowers are small, 2 mm long, with eight or more greenish-brown petals; they are produced in the leaf axils. The <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> is a small nut 4–5 mm long, usually with three spines, two <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#Elongated_organisms_with_distinctive_ends" title="Anatomical terms of location">basal</a> and one <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#Elongated_organisms_with_distinctive_ends" title="Anatomical terms of location">apical</a>, 1–12 mm long. Plants with the two basal nut spines very short are sometimes distinguished as <i>Ceratophyllum demersum</i> var. <i>apiculatum</i> (Cham.) Asch., and those with no basal spines sometimes distinguished as <i>Ceratophyllum demersum</i> var. <i>inerme</i> Gay ex Radcl.-Sm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foc_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-foc-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fna_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-fna-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fnwe_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-fnwe-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blamey_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-blamey-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhs_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-rhs-7">[7]</a></sup> It can form <a href="/wiki/Turion_(botany)" title="Turion (botany)">turions</a>: buds that sink to the bottom of the water that stay there during the winter and form new plants in spring.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ceratophyllum_demersum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Ceratophyllum demersum</i> grows in lakes, ponds, and quiet streams with summer water temperatures of 15-30 °C<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and a rich nutrient status. In North America, it occurs in the entire US and Canada, except <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In Europe, it has been reported as far north as at a latitude of 66 degrees in Norway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Other reported occurrences include China, <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> (at 66 degrees North), <a href="/wiki/Burkina_Faso" title="Burkina Faso">Burkina Faso</a> (Africa), Vietnam, and New Zealand (introduced).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-issg-distrib_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-issg-distrib-10">[10]</a></sup> <i>Ceratophyllum demersum</i> grows in still or very slow-moving water.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Invasive_status">Invasive status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ceratophyllum_demersum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Invasive status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hornwort is a declared weed under the Tasmanian Weed Management Act 1999 in Tasmania, Australia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and is classed as an unwanted organism in New Zealand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ceratophyllum_demersum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>C. demersum</i> has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allelopathic" title="Allelopathic">allelopathic</a> qualities as it excretes substances that inhibit the growth of <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue-green_algae" title="Blue-green algae">blue-green algae</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-issg-ecology_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-issg-ecology-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Its dense growth can outcompete native underwater vegetation, leading to loss of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>. In New Zealand, it has caused problems with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_power" title="Hydroelectric power">hydroelectric power</a> plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-issg-ecology_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-issg-ecology-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation_and_uses">Cultivation and uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ceratophyllum_demersum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cultivation and uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species is often used as a floating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> plant in both coldwater and tropical <a href="/wiki/Aquarium" title="Aquarium">aquaria</a>. Though without roots, it may attach itself to the <a href="/wiki/Substrate_(aquarium)" title="Substrate (aquarium)">substrate</a> or objects in the aquarium. Its fluffy, filamentous, bright-green leaves provide excellent cover for newly hatched fish. It is propagated by cuttings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hiscock_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-hiscock-14">[14]</a></sup> It is frequently used as a model organism for studies of plant physiology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> One of the reasons is that it allows studies on shoot effects without influence of a root, which often makes interpretation of nutrition and toxicity experiments difficult in terrestrial plants. As it is free floating and therefore does not require a solid substrate, it has been used successfully in the biological life support systems "Aquarack/CEBAS" and "Omegahab" on space flights <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In aquaria this plant appears to drop all its leaves when exposed to products designed to kill snails. The stems can recover relatively quickly, growing new leaves within a few weeks.</p>
