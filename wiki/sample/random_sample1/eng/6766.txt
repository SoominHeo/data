<p>The <b>euglyphids</b> are a prominent group of filose <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amoeboid" title="Amoeboid">amoebae</a> that produce shells or tests from siliceous scales, plates, and sometimes spines. These elements are created within the cell and then assembled on its surface in a more or less regular arrangement, giving the test a textured appearance. There is a single opening for the long slender pseudopods, which capture food and pull the cell across the substrate.</p>
<p>Euglyphids are common in soils, marshes, and other organic-rich environments, feeding on tiny organisms such as <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>. The test is generally 30-100 μm in length, although the cell only occupies part of this space. During reproduction a second shell is formed opposite the opening, so both daughter cells remain protected. Different genera and species are distinguished primarily by the form of the test. <i>Euglypha</i> and <i>Trinema</i> are the most common.</p>
<p>The euglyphids are traditionally grouped with other amoebae. However, genetic studies instead place them with various amoeboid and <a href="/wiki/Flagellate" title="Flagellate">flagellate</a> groups, forming an assemblage called the <a href="/wiki/Cercozoa" title="Cercozoa">Cercozoa</a>. Their closest relatives are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thaumatomonad" title="Thaumatomonad">thaumatomonads</a>, flagellates that form similar siliceous tests.</p>
