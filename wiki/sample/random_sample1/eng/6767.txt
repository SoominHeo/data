<p><i><b>Bungeoppang</b></i> (lit. “<a href="/wiki/Crucian_carp" title="Crucian carp">crucian carp</a> cake/bread”) is the Korean name of a pastry similar to the Japanese fish-shaped pastry <i><a href="/wiki/Taiyaki" title="Taiyaki">taiyaki</a></i>.</p>
<p>Bungeoppangs are prepared using an appliance similar to a <a href="/wiki/Waffle_iron" title="Waffle iron">waffle iron</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Batter_(cooking)" title="Batter (cooking)">batter</a> is poured into a fish-shaped mold, <a href="/wiki/Red_bean_paste" title="Red bean paste">red bean paste</a> is added, then more batter to encase the red bean paste. The mold is then closed, and roasted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Korean, <i>bung'eo</i> (붕어) means <a href="/wiki/Carassius" title="Carassius">Carassius</a>, a kind of <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, and <i>ppang</i> (빵) means <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a>. This name simply comes from the fish-like shape and appearance of the pastry, and it does not contain any ingredients from its namesake fish or any other fish.</p>
<p><i>Bungeoppang</i> was first introduced into Korea by the Japanese during the <a href="/wiki/Korea_under_Japanese_rule" title="Korea under Japanese rule">Colonial Korea</a> in the 1930s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2016, one U.S. dollar could purchase three or four bungeoppangs, depending on the location.</p>
<p>The vendors sell them in a similar way to Korean <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eomuk" title="Eomuk">eomuk</a></i> (어묵) or Japanese <i><a href="/wiki/Kamaboko" title="Kamaboko">kamaboko</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Hotteok" title="Hotteok">Hotteoks</a> (호떡) are made and sold in a similar way to that of bungeoppang.</p>
<p>There are also <i>bungeoppang</i>-shaped waffles filled with <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream" title="Ice cream">ice cream</a> and <i>pat</i> (sweetened and boiled red beans or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Azuki_bean" title="Azuki bean">azuki beans</a>). These waffles are usually mass-produced and sold by retailers, not by open-air food vendors.</p>
<p>Similar variations also exist:</p>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Gukhwappang</b></i> (<span lang="ko-Hang"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%8F%8A%E8%8A%B1" title="wikt:菊花">국화</a>빵</span>, “<a href="/wiki/Chrysanthemum" title="Chrysanthemum">chrysanthemum</a> cake”) is essentially identical to <i>bungeoppang</i>, only it is a flower-shaped pastry.</li>
<li><i><b>gyeranppang</b></i> (<span lang="ko-Hang"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%B7%84" title="wikt:鷄">계</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%8D%B5" title="wikt:卵">란</a>빵</span>, lit. “chicken egg cake”) is filled with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(food)" title="Egg (food)">egg</a> and it has a shape of rounded rectangle.</li>
</ul>
<p>Because each pastry looks exactly the same, <i>Bungeoppang</i> in Korean can colloquially refer to things that look identical.</p>
