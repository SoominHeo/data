<p><i><b>A Beautiful Mind</b></i> is a 2001 American <a href="/wiki/Biographical_film" title="Biographical film">biographical</a> <a href="/wiki/Drama_(film_and_television)" title="Drama (film and television)">drama film</a> based on the life of <a href="/wiki/John_Forbes_Nash_Jr." title="John Forbes Nash Jr.">John Nash</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Memorial_Prize_in_Economic_Sciences" title="Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences">Nobel Laureate in Economics</a>. The film was directed by <a href="/wiki/Ron_Howard" title="Ron Howard">Ron Howard</a>, from a screenplay written by <a href="/wiki/Akiva_Goldsman" title="Akiva Goldsman">Akiva Goldsman</a>. It was inspired by a bestselling, <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a>-nominated 1998 <a href="/wiki/A_Beautiful_Mind_(book)" title="A Beautiful Mind (book)">book of the same name</a> by <a href="/wiki/Sylvia_Nasar" title="Sylvia Nasar">Sylvia Nasar</a>. The film stars <a href="/wiki/Russell_Crowe" title="Russell Crowe">Russell Crowe</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Ed_Harris" title="Ed Harris">Ed Harris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Connelly" title="Jennifer Connelly">Jennifer Connelly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Bettany" title="Paul Bettany">Paul Bettany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adam_Goldberg" title="Adam Goldberg">Adam Goldberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Judd_Hirsch" title="Judd Hirsch">Judd Hirsch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Josh_Lucas" title="Josh Lucas">Josh Lucas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Rapp" title="Anthony Rapp">Anthony Rapp</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Plummer" title="Christopher Plummer">Christopher Plummer</a> in supporting roles. The story begins in Nash's days as a graduate student at <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>. Early in the film, Nash begins to develop <a href="/wiki/Paranoid_schizophrenia" title="Paranoid schizophrenia">paranoid schizophrenia</a> and endures <a href="/wiki/Delusion" title="Delusion">delusional</a> episodes while painfully watching the loss and burden his condition brings on wife <a href="/wiki/Alicia_Nash" title="Alicia Nash">Alicia</a> and friends.</p>
<p>The film opened in the United States cinemas on December 21, 2001. It went on to gross over $313 million worldwide and won four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Awards</a>, for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Picture" title="Academy Award for Best Picture">Best Picture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Director" title="Academy Award for Best Director">Best Director</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Writing_(Adapted_Screenplay)" title="Academy Award for Best Writing (Adapted Screenplay)">Best Adapted Screenplay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress">Best Supporting Actress</a>. It was also nominated for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actor" title="Academy Award for Best Actor">Best Actor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Film_Editing" title="Academy Award for Best Film Editing">Best Film Editing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Makeup" title="Academy Award for Best Makeup">Best Makeup</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Original_Music_Score" title="Academy Award for Original Music Score">Best Original Score</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was well received by critics, but has been criticized for its inaccurate portrayal of some aspects of Nash's life, especially his other family and a son born out of wedlock. However, the filmmakers have stated that the film was not meant to be a literal representation.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Beautiful_Mind_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1947, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Forbes_Nash,_Jr." title="John Forbes Nash, Jr.">John Nash</a> (Crowe) arrives at <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>. He is co-recipient, with Martin Hansen (Lucas), of the prestigious Carnegie Scholarship for <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>. At a reception, he meets a group of other promising math and science graduate students, Richard Sol (Goldberg), Ainsley (<a href="/wiki/Jason_Gray-Stanford" title="Jason Gray-Stanford">Jason Gray-Stanford</a>), and Bender (Rapp). He also meets his roommate Charles Herman (Bettany), a literature student.</p>
<p>Nash is under extreme pressure to publish, but he wants to publish his own original idea. His inspiration comes when he and his fellow graduate students discuss how to approach a group of women at a bar. Hansen quotes <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a> and advocates "every man for himself", but Nash argues that a cooperative approach would lead to better chances of success. Nash develops a <a href="/wiki/Nash_equilibrium" title="Nash equilibrium">new concept of governing dynamics</a> and publishes an article on this. On the strength of this, he is offered an appointment at <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">MIT</a> where Sol and Bender join him.</p>
<p>Some years later, Nash is invited to <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">the Pentagon</a> to <a href="/wiki/Cryptanalysis" title="Cryptanalysis">crack</a> <a href="/wiki/Encryption" title="Encryption">encrypted</a> enemy telecommunication. Nash can decipher the code mentally, to the astonishment of other decrypters. He considers his regular duties at MIT uninteresting and beneath his talents, so he is pleased to be given a new assignment by his mysterious supervisor, William Parcher (Harris) of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">United States Department of Defense</a>. He is to look for patterns in magazines and newspapers in order to thwart a <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> plot. Nash becomes increasingly obsessive about searching for these hidden patterns and believes he is followed when he delivers his results to a secret mailbox.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, a student, <a href="/wiki/Alicia_Nash" title="Alicia Nash">Alicia Larde</a> (Connelly), asks him to dinner, and the two fall in love. On a return visit to Princeton, Nash runs into Charles and his niece, Marcee (Cardone). With Charles' encouragement, he proposes to Alicia and they marry.</p>
<p>Nash begins to fear for his life after witnessing a shootout between Parcher and Soviet agents, but Parcher blackmails him into staying on his assignment. While delivering a guest lecture at Harvard University, Nash tries to flee from people he thinks are foreign Russian agents, led by Dr. Rosen (Plummer). After punching Rosen in an attempt to flee, Nash is forcibly sedated and sent to a psychiatric facility he believes is run by the Soviets.</p>
<p>Dr. Rosen tells Alicia that Nash has <a href="/wiki/Paranoid_schizophrenia" title="Paranoid schizophrenia">paranoid schizophrenia</a> and that Charles, Marcee, and Parcher exist only in his imagination. Alicia investigates and finally confronts Nash with the unopened documents he had delivered to the secret mailbox. Nash is given a course of <a href="/wiki/Insulin_shock_therapy" title="Insulin shock therapy">insulin shock therapy</a> and eventually released. Frustrated with the <a href="/wiki/Adverse_drug_reaction" title="Adverse drug reaction">side-effects</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Antipsychotic" title="Antipsychotic">antipsychotic</a> medication he is taking, which make him lethargic and unresponsive, he secretly stops taking it. This causes a relapse and he meets Parcher again.</p>
<p>Shortly afterward, Alicia discovers Nash is once again working on his "assignment." Realizing he has relapsed, Alicia rushes into the house to find her baby submerged in the tub. Nash claims that Charles was watching the baby. Alicia calls Dr. Rosen, but Nash believes Parcher is trying to kill her. He rushes in to push Parcher away, and accidentally knocks Alicia and the baby to the ground. As Alicia flees the house with their baby, Nash jumps in front of Alicia's car and begs her to stay. Nash tells her that he realizes that he's never seen Marcee age, even though he's known her for three years. He finally accepts that Parcher and other figures are hallucinations. Against Dr. Rosen's advice, Nash decides not to restart his medication, believing that he can deal with his symptoms himself. Alicia decides to stay and support him in this.</p>
<p>Nash returns to Princeton and approaches his old rival, Hansen, now head of the mathematics department. He grants Nash permission to work out of the library and to audit classes. Over the next two decades, Nash learns to ignore his hallucinations. By the late 1970s, he is allowed to teach again.</p>
<p>In 1994, Nash wins the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bank_of_Sweden_Prize_in_Economic_Sciences_in_Memory_of_Alfred_Nobel" title="Bank of Sweden Prize in Economic Sciences in Memory of Alfred Nobel">Nobel Memorial Prize in Economics</a> for his revolutionary work on <a href="/wiki/Game_theory" title="Game theory">game theory</a>, and is honored by his fellow professors. The movie ends as Nash, Alicia, and their son leave the auditorium in Stockholm; Nash sees Charles, Marcee, and Parcher standing to one side and watching him.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Beautiful_Mind_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After producer <a href="/wiki/Brian_Grazer" title="Brian Grazer">Brian Grazer</a> first read an excerpt of <a href="/wiki/Sylvia_Nasar" title="Sylvia Nasar">Sylvia Nasar</a>'s book <i><a href="/wiki/A_Beautiful_Mind_(book)" title="A Beautiful Mind (book)">A Beautiful Mind</a></i> in <i><a href="/wiki/Vanity_Fair_(magazine)" title="Vanity Fair (magazine)">Vanity Fair</a></i> magazine, he immediately purchased the rights to the film. He eventually brought the project to director <a href="/wiki/Ron_Howard" title="Ron Howard">Ron Howard</a>, who had scheduling conflicts and was forced to pass. Grazer later said that many <a href="/wiki/A-list" title="A-list">A-list</a> directors were calling with their point of view on the project. He eventually focused on a particular director, who coincidentally was available only when Howard was also available. Grazer chose Howard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Partners_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Partners-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Grazer met with a number of screenwriters, mostly consisting of "serious dramatists", but he chose <a href="/wiki/Akiva_Goldsman" title="Akiva Goldsman">Akiva Goldsman</a> because of his strong passion and desire for the project. Goldsman's creative take on the project was to avoid having viewers understand they are viewing an alternate reality until a specific point in the film. This was done to rob the viewers of their understanding, to mimic how Nash comprehended his experiences. Howard agreed to direct the film based on the first draft. He asked Goldsman to emphasize the love story of Nash and his wife; she was critical to his being able to continue living at home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Screenplay_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Screenplay-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dave Bayer, a professor of Mathematics at Barnard College, Columbia University,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bayer_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bayer-5">[5]</a></sup> was consulted on the mathematical equations that appear in the film. Bayer later said that he approached his consulting role as an actor when preparing equations, such as when Nash is forced to teach a calculus class, and arbitrarily places a complicated problem on the blackboard. Bayer focused on a character who did not want to teach ordinary details and was more concerned with what was interesting. Bayer received a cameo role in the film as a professor who lays his pen down for Nash in the pen ceremony near the end of the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swarthmore_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Swarthmore-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Greg_Cannom" title="Greg Cannom">Greg Cannom</a> was chosen to create the makeup effects for <i>A Beautiful Mind</i>, specifically the age progression of the characters. Crowe had previously worked with Cannom on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Insider_(film)" title="The Insider (film)">The Insider</a></i>. Howard had also worked with Cannom on <i><a href="/wiki/Cocoon_(film)" title="Cocoon (film)">Cocoon</a></i>. Each character's stages of makeup were broken down by the number of years that would pass between levels. Cannom stressed subtlety between the stages, but worked toward the ultimate stage of "Older Nash". The production team originally decided that the makeup department would age Russell Crowe throughout the film; however, at Crowe's request, the makeup was used to push his look to resemble the facial features of John Nash. Cannom developed a new silicone-type makeup that could simulate skin and be used for overlapping applications; this shortened make-up application time from eight to four hours. Crowe was also fitted with a number of dentures to give him a slight <a href="/wiki/Malocclusion" title="Malocclusion">overbite</a> in the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Makeup_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Makeup-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Howard and Grazer chose frequent collaborator <a href="/wiki/James_Horner" title="James Horner">James Horner</a> to score the film because they knew of his ability to communicate. Howard said, regarding Horner, "It's like having a conversation with a writer or an actor or another director." A running discussion between the director and the composer was the concept of high-level mathematics being less about numbers and solutions, and more akin to a <a href="/wiki/Kaleidoscope" title="Kaleidoscope">kaleidoscope</a>, in that the ideas evolve and change. After the first screening of the film, Horner told Howard: "I see changes occurring like fast-moving weather systems." He chose it as another theme to connect to Nash's ever-changing character. Horner chose Welsh singer <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Church" title="Charlotte Church">Charlotte Church</a> to sing the soprano vocals after deciding that he needed a balance between a child and adult singing voice. He wanted a "purity, clarity and brightness of an instrument" but also a <a href="/wiki/Vibrato" title="Vibrato">vibrato</a> to maintain the humanity of the voice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Music_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Music-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film was shot 90% chronologically. Three separate trips were made to the <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a> campus. During filming, Howard decided that Nash's delusions should always be introduced first audibly and then visually. This provides a clue for the audience and establishes the delusions from Nash's point of view. The historic John Nash had only auditory delusions. The filmmakers developed a technique to represent Nash's mental epiphanies. Mathematicians described to them such moments as a sense of "the smoke clearing", "flashes of light" and "everything coming together", so the filmmakers used a flash of light appearing over an object or person to signify Nash's creativity at work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVD.com_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-DVD.com-9">[9]</a></sup> Two night shots were done at <a href="/wiki/Fairleigh_Dickinson_University" title="Fairleigh Dickinson University">Fairleigh Dickinson University</a>'s campus in <a href="/wiki/Florham_Park,_New_Jersey" title="Florham Park, New Jersey">Florham Park, New Jersey</a>, in the Vanderbilt Mansion ballroom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Portions of the film set at Harvard were filmed at <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_College" title="Manhattan College">Manhattan College</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many actors were considered for the role of John Nash, including <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Willis" title="Bruce Willis">Bruce Willis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Costner" title="Kevin Costner">Kevin Costner</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Travolta" title="John Travolta">John Travolta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Cruise" title="Tom Cruise">Tom Cruise</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Cusack" title="John Cusack">John Cusack</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Sheen" title="Charlie Sheen">Charlie Sheen</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Downey,_Jr." title="Robert Downey, Jr.">Robert Downey, Jr.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Cage" title="Nicolas Cage">Nicolas Cage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Depp" title="Johnny Depp">Johnny Depp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Fiennes" title="Ralph Fiennes">Ralph Fiennes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jared_Leto" title="Jared Leto">Jared Leto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brad_Pitt" title="Brad Pitt">Brad Pitt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alec_Baldwin" title="Alec Baldwin">Alec Baldwin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mel_Gibson" title="Mel Gibson">Mel Gibson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sean_Penn" title="Sean Penn">Sean Penn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guy_Pearce" title="Guy Pearce">Guy Pearce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Broderick" title="Matthew Broderick">Matthew Broderick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gary_Oldman" title="Gary Oldman">Gary Oldman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Keanu_Reeves" title="Keanu Reeves">Keanu Reeves</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Ron Howard ultimately cast <a href="/wiki/Russell_Crowe" title="Russell Crowe">Russell Crowe</a>.</p>
<p>The producers had not originally thought of <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Connelly" title="Jennifer Connelly">Jennifer Connelly</a> for the role of Alicia. <a href="/wiki/Portia_de_Rossi" title="Portia de Rossi">Portia de Rossi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catherine_McCormack" title="Catherine McCormack">Catherine McCormack</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meg_Ryan" title="Meg Ryan">Meg Ryan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Griffiths" title="Rachel Griffiths">Rachel Griffiths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amanda_Peet" title="Amanda Peet">Amanda Peet</a> were among the many actresses who lobbied for the role of Alicia.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>During production, Ron Howard and Russell Crowe appeared on a video-only webcam stationed at the set's craft services area and also communicated with <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a> and Tom Cruise on the set of <i><a href="/wiki/Minority_Report_(film)" title="Minority Report (film)">Minority Report</a></i> in Los Angeles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Divergence_from_history">Divergence from history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Beautiful_Mind_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Divergence from history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The narrative of the film differs considerably from the events of Nash's life, as filmmakers made choices for the sense of the story. The film has been criticized for this aspect, but the filmmakers said they never intended a literal representation of his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>One difficulty was the portrayal of his mental illness and trying to find a film visual language for this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inaccuracy_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Inaccuracy-16">[16]</a></sup> Sylvia Nasar said that the filmmakers "invented a narrative that, while far from a literal telling, is true to the spirit of Nash's story".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slate_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Slate-17">[17]</a></sup> Nash spent his years between Princeton and MIT as a consultant for the <a href="/wiki/RAND_Corporation" title="RAND Corporation">RAND Corporation</a> in California, but in the film he is portrayed as having worked for the Department of Defense at <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">the Pentagon</a> instead. His handlers, both from faculty and administration, had to introduce him to assistants and strangers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVD.com_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-DVD.com-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Broadcasting_Service" title="Public Broadcasting Service">PBS</a> documentary <i><a href="/wiki/A_Brilliant_Madness" title="A Brilliant Madness">A Brilliant Madness</a></i> tried to portray his life accurately.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Few of the characters in the film, besides John and Alicia Nash, correspond directly to actual people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The discussion of the <a href="/wiki/Nash_equilibrium" title="Nash equilibrium">Nash equilibrium</a> was criticized as over-simplified. In the film, Nash suffers schizophrenic hallucinations while he is in graduate school, but in his life he did not have this experience until some years later. No mention is made of Nash's homosexual experiences at RAND,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slate_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Slate-17">[17]</a></sup> which are noted in the biography;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NasarBook_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-NasarBook-20">[20]</a></sup> both Nash and his wife deny this occurred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBS_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBS-21">[21]</a></sup> Nash fathered a son, John David Stier (born June 19, 1953), by Eleanor Agnes Stier (1921–2005), a nurse whom he abandoned when she told him of her pregnancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Boston_Globe_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Boston_Globe-22">[22]</a></sup> The film did not include Alicia's divorce of John in 1963. It was not until after Nash won the Nobel Memorial Prize in 1994 that they renewed their relationship. Beginning in 1970, Alicia allowed him to live with her as a boarder. They remarried in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NasarBook_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-NasarBook-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nash is shown to join Wheeler Laboratory at MIT, but there is no such lab. Instead, he was appointed as C.L.E. Moore Instructor at MIT.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The so-called pen ceremony tradition at Princeton shown in the film is completely fictitious.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVD.com_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-DVD.com-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inaccuracies_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Inaccuracies-24">[24]</a></sup> The film has Nash saying in 1994: "I take the newer medications", but in fact, he did not take any medication from 1970 onwards, something highlighted in Nasar's biography. Howard later stated that they added the line of dialogue because they worried that the film would be criticized for suggesting that all people with schizophrenia can overcome their illness without medication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVD.com_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-DVD.com-9">[9]</a></sup> In addition, Nash never gave an acceptance speech for his Nobel prize.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Beautiful_Mind_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Beautiful_Mind_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>A Beautiful Mind</i> received a limited release on December 21, 2001, receiving positive reviews, with Crowe receiving wide acclaim for his performance. It was later released in the United States on January 4, 2002. <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> showed a 75% approval rating among critics, stating: "The well-acted <i>A Beautiful Mind</i> is both a moving love story and a revealing look at mental illness."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RT_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-RT-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Sun-Times" title="Chicago Sun-Times">Chicago Sun-Times</a></i> gave the film four out of four stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Mike Clark of <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> gave three-and-a-half out of four stars and also praised Crowe's performance, calling it a welcome follow-up to Howard's previous film, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Grinch_(film)" title="The Grinch (film)">The Grinch</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Desson Thomson of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Post" title="Washington Post">Washington Post</a></i> found the film to be "one of those formulaically rendered Important Subject movies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RT_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-RT-25">[25]</a></sup> The mathematics community praised the portrayal of mathematics in the film, including John Nash himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swarthmore_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Swarthmore-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>John Sutherland of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> noted the film's biopic distortions, but said:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Howard pulls off an extraordinary trick in <i>A Beautiful Mind</i> by seducing the audience into Nash's paranoid world. We may not leave the cinema with A level competence in game theory, but we do get a glimpse into what it feels like to be mad - and not know it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sutherland_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-sutherland-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office_performance">Box office performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Beautiful_Mind_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Box office performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the five-day weekend of the limited release, <i>A Beautiful Mind</i> opened at the #12 spot at the box office,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> peaking at the #2 spot following the wide release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The film went to gross $170,742,341 in the United States and Canada and $313,542,341 worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoxOfficeMojo_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-BoxOfficeMojo-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Beautiful_Mind_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>A Beautiful Mind</i> was released on <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">VHS</a> and <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> in the United States on June 25, 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVDRelease_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-DVDRelease-31">[31]</a></sup> The DVD set includes audio commentaries, deleted scenes and documentaries. The film was also released on <a href="/wiki/Blu-ray" title="Blu-ray">Blu-ray</a> in North America on January 25, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Beautiful_Mind_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2002, the film was awarded four awards at the <a href="/wiki/74th_Academy_Awards" title="74th Academy Awards">74th Academy Awards</a>, for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Writing_(Adapted_Screenplay)" title="Academy Award for Writing (Adapted Screenplay)">Best Adapted Screenplay</a> (<a href="/wiki/Akiva_Goldsman" title="Akiva Goldsman">Akiva Goldsman</a>), <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Picture" title="Academy Award for Best Picture">Best Picture</a> (<a href="/wiki/Brian_Grazer" title="Brian Grazer">Brian Grazer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ron_Howard" title="Ron Howard">Ron Howard</a>), <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Director" title="Academy Award for Best Director">Best Director</a> (Ron Howard) and <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress">Best Supporting Actress</a> (<a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Connelly" title="Jennifer Connelly">Jennifer Connelly</a>). It also received four other nominations for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actor" title="Academy Award for Best Actor">Best Actor</a> (<a href="/wiki/Russell_Crowe" title="Russell Crowe">Russell Crowe</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Film_Editing" title="Academy Award for Film Editing">Best Film Editing</a> (<a href="/wiki/Mike_Hill_(film_editor)" title="Mike Hill (film editor)">Mike Hill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daniel_P._Hanley" title="Daniel P. Hanley">Daniel P. Hanley</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Makeup" title="Academy Award for Best Makeup">Best Makeup</a> (<a href="/wiki/Greg_Cannom" title="Greg Cannom">Greg Cannom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Colleen_Callaghan" title="Colleen Callaghan">Colleen Callaghan</a>), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Original_Music_Score" title="Academy Award for Original Music Score">Best Original Score</a> (<a href="/wiki/James_Horner" title="James Horner">James Horner</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/55th_British_Academy_Film_Awards" title="55th British Academy Film Awards">55th British Academy Film Awards</a> the film won Best Actor in a Leading Role and Best Actress in a Supporting Role to Russell Crowe and Jennifer Connelly, respectively. It also nominated the film for Best Film, Best Screenplay and the David Lean Award for Direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the 2002 <a href="/wiki/American_Film_Institute" title="American Film Institute">AFI Awards</a>, Jennifer Connelly won for Best Featured Female Actor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> In 2006, it was named No. 93 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years..._100_Cheers" title="AFI's 100 Years... 100 Cheers">AFI's 100 Years... 100 Cheers</a>. In the following year, it was nominated for <a href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years...100_Movies_(10th_Anniversary_Edition)" title="AFI's 100 Years...100 Movies (10th Anniversary Edition)">AFI's 100 Years...100 Movies (10th Anniversary Edition)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The film was also nominated for Movie of the Year, Actor of the Year (Russell Crowe), and Screenwriter of the Year (Akiva Goldsman).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
