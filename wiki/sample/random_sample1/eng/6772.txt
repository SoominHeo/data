<p><b>Brahmo Samaj</b> (<a href="/wiki/Bengali_language" title="Bengali language">Bengali</a> ব্রাহ্ম সমাজ <i>Bramho Shômaj</i>) is the societal component of <a href="/wiki/Brahmoism" title="Brahmoism">Brahmoism</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monotheist" title="Monotheist">monotheistic</a> reformist and <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">renaissance</a> movement of <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hindu religion</a>. It is practised today mainly as the <a href="/wiki/Adi_Dharm" title="Adi Dharm">Adi Dharm</a> after its eclipse in <a href="/wiki/Bengal" title="Bengal">Bengal</a> consequent to the exit of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tattwabodini_Sabha" title="Tattwabodini Sabha">Tattwabodini Sabha</a> from its ranks in 1859. After the publication of <a href="/wiki/Hemendranath_Tagore" title="Hemendranath Tagore">Hemendranath Tagore</a>'s Brahmo <i>Anusthan</i> (code of practice) in 1860 which formally divorced Brahmoism from <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, the first Brahmo Samaj was founded in 1861 at <a href="/wiki/Lahore" title="Lahore">Lahore</a> by Pandit Nobin Chandra Roy.</p>
<p>It was one of the most influential religious movements in India <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and made a significant contribution to the making of modern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It was started at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calcutta" title="Calcutta">Calcutta</a> on 20 August 1828 by Raja <a href="/wiki/Ram_Mohan_Roy" title="Ram Mohan Roy">Ram Mohan Roy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Debendranath_Tagore" title="Debendranath Tagore">Debendranath Tagore</a> as reformation of the prevailing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahminical_Hinduism" title="Brahminical Hinduism">Brahmanism</a> of the time (specifically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kulin_Brahmin" title="Kulin Brahmin">Kulin</a> practices) and began the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bengal_Renaissance" title="Bengal Renaissance">Bengal Renaissance</a> of the 19th century pioneering all religious, social and educational advance of the Hindu community in the 19th century. Its Trust Deed was made in 1830 formalising its inception and it was duly and publicly inaugurated in January 1830 by the consecration of the first house of prayer, now known as the Adi Brahmo Samaj.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> From the <i>Brahmo Samaj</i> springs Brahmoism, the most recent of legally recognised religions in India and Bangladesh, reflecting its foundation on reformed spiritual Hinduism with vital elements of Judeo-Islamic faith and practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meaning_of_the_name">Meaning of the name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brahmo_Samaj&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meaning of the name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Brahmo Samaj literally denotes community (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanskrit_language" title="Sanskrit language">Sanskrit</a>: <span lang="sa" xml:lang="sa">samaj</span>) of men who worship <a href="/wiki/Brahman" title="Brahman">Brahman</a> the highest reality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In reality Brahmo Samaj does not discriminate between caste, creed or religion and is an assembly of all sorts and descriptions of people without distinction, meeting publicly for the sober, orderly, religious and devout adoration of "<i>the (nameless) unsearchable Eternal, Immutable Being who is the Author and Preserver of the Universe</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brahmo_Sabha">Brahmo Sabha</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brahmo_Samaj&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Brahmo Sabha">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 20 August 1828 the first assembly of the <i>Brahmo Sabha</i> (progenitor of the Brahmo Samaj) was held at the North Calcutta house of <a href="/wiki/Basu#Notables" title="Basu"><i>Feringhee</i> Kamal Bose</a>. This day was celebrated by <a href="/wiki/Brahmo" title="Brahmo">Brahmos</a> as <i>Bhadrotsab</i> (ভাদ্রোৎসব <i>Bhadrotshôb</i> "<a href="/wiki/Bhadro" title="Bhadro">Bhadro</a> celebration"). These meetings were open to all Brahmins and there was no formal organisation or theology as such.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 8 January 1830 influential progressive members of the closely related <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kulin_Brahmin" title="Kulin Brahmin">Kulin Brahmin</a> clan<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> scurrilously<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> described as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pirali_Brahmin" title="Pirali Brahmin">Pirali Brahmin</a> <i>ie.</i> ostracised for service in the Mughal <i>Nizaamat</i> of Bengal) of Tagore (<i>Thakur</i>) and Roy (<i>Vandopādhyāya</i>) <i>zumeendar</i> family mutually executed the <a href="/wiki/Trust_deed_of_Brahmo_Sabha" title="Trust deed of Brahmo Sabha">Trust deed of Brahmo Sabha</a> for the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adi_Brahmo_Samaj" title="Adi Brahmo Samaj">Adi Brahmo Samaj</a> (place of worship) on Chitpore Road (now Rabindra Sarani), <a href="/wiki/Kolkata" title="Kolkata">Kolkata</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> with <a href="/wiki/Ram_Chandra_Vidyabagish" title="Ram Chandra Vidyabagish">Ram Chandra Vidyabagish</a> as first resident superintendent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 23 January 1830 or 11th <i>Magh</i>, the <i>Adi Brahmo</i> premises were publicly inaugurated (with about 500 Brahmins and 1 Englishman present). This day is celebrated by Brahmos as <i>Maghotsab</i> (মাঘোৎসব <i>Maghotshôb</i> "<a href="/wiki/Magh_(Bengali_calendar)" title="Magh (Bengali calendar)">Magh</a> celebration").</p>
<p>In November 1830 Rammohun Roy left for England. <a href="/wiki/Akbar_II" title="Akbar II">Akbar II</a> had conferred the title of 'Raja' to Rammohun Roy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brief_Eclipse_of_Brahmo_Sabha">Brief Eclipse of Brahmo Sabha</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brahmo_Samaj&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Brief Eclipse of Brahmo Sabha">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With Rammohun's departure for England in 1830, the affairs of Brahmo Sabha were effectively managed by Trustees <a href="/wiki/Dwarkanath_Tagore" title="Dwarkanath Tagore">Dwarkanath Tagore</a> and Pandit Ram Chandra Vidyabagish, with Dwarkanath instructing his <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diwan_(title)" title="Diwan (title)">diwan</a></i> to manage affairs.</p>
<p>By the time of Rammohun's death in 1833 near Bristol (UK), attendance at the <i>Sabha</i> dwindled and the Telugu Brahmins revived idolatry. The <i>zameendars</i>, being preoccupied in business, had little time for affairs of <i>Sabha</i>, and flame of <i>Sabha</i> was almost extinguished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tattwabodhini_period">Tattwabodhini period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brahmo_Samaj&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tattwabodhini period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 6 October 1839 <a href="/wiki/Debendranath_Tagore" title="Debendranath Tagore">Debendranath Tagore</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/Dwarkanath_Tagore" title="Dwarkanath Tagore">(Prince) Dwarkanath Tagore</a>, established <i>Tattvaranjini Sabha</i> which was shortly thereafter renamed the Tattwabodhini (<i>Truth-seekers</i>) Sabha. Initially confined to immediate members of the Tagore family, in 2 years it mustered over 500 members. In 1840 Debendranath published a Bangla translation of <i>Katha Upanishad</i>. A modern researcher describes the Sabha's philosophy as <i>modern middle-class (bourgeois) Vedanta.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Among its first members were the "two giants of Hindu reformation and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bengal_Renaissance" title="Bengal Renaissance">Bengal Renaissance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Akshay_Kumar_Datta" title="Akshay Kumar Datta">Akshay Kumar Datta</a> "who in 1839 emerged from the life of an anonymous squalor-beset individual" and <a href="/wiki/Ishwar_Chandra_Vidyasagar" title="Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar">Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar</a> the "indigenous modernizer".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></i></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Covenant_and_merger_with_the_Tattwabodhini_Sabha">First Covenant and merger with the Tattwabodhini Sabha</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brahmo_Samaj&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: First Covenant and merger with the Tattwabodhini Sabha">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 7th Pous 1765 Shaka (1843) Debendranath Tagore and twenty other Tattwabodhini stalwarts were formally invited by Pt. Vidyabagish into the Trust of Brahmo Sabha. The Pous Mela at Santiniketan starts on this day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> From this day forth, the Tattwabodhini Sabha dedicated itself to promoting Ram Mohan Roy's creed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The other Brahmins who swore the First Covenant of Brahmoism are:-</p>
<ul>
<li>Shridhar Bhattacharya</li>
<li>Shyamacharan Bhattacharya</li>
<li>Brajendranath Tagore</li>
<li>Girindranath Tagore, brother of <a href="/wiki/Debendranath_Tagore" title="Debendranath Tagore">Debendranath Tagore</a> &amp; father of <a href="/wiki/Ganendranath_Tagore" title="Ganendranath Tagore">Ganendranath Tagore</a></li>
<li>Anandachandra Bhattacharya</li>
<li>Taraknath Bhattacharya</li>
<li>Haradev Chattopadhyaya, the future father-in-law to MahaAcharya <a href="/wiki/Hemendranath_Tagore" title="Hemendranath Tagore">Hemendranath Tagore</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>Shyamacharan Mukhopadhyaya</li>
<li>Ramnarayan Chattopadhyaya</li>
<li>Sashibhushan Mukhopadhyaya</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disagreement_with_the_Tattwabodhini">Disagreement with the Tattwabodhini</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brahmo_Samaj&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Disagreement with the Tattwabodhini">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Nov 1855 the Rev. Charles Dall (a Unitarian minister of Boston) arrived in Calcutta to start his mission and immediately established contact with Debendranath and other Brahmos. Debendranath's suspicion of foreigners alienated Dall and in 1857, Debendranath Tagore barred the entry of the Reverend from the Sabha premises for preaching <i>the name of Christ who some people worship as God</i> within.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Debendranath then proceeded on spiritual retreat to <a href="/wiki/Shimla" title="Shimla">Simla</a>. Dall, immediately formed a counter group "The friends of Rammmohun Roy Society" and then got admitted a protégé to Sabha. The presence of Dall's protégé <a href="/wiki/Keshub_Chandra_Sen" title="Keshub Chandra Sen">Keshub Chandra Sen</a> (a non-Brahmin) into the Calcutta Brahmo Sabha in 1857 while Debendranath was away in Simla caused considerable stress in the movement, with many long time Tattvabodhini Brahmin members publicly leaving the Brahmo Sabha and institutions due to his high-handed ways. In September 1858, Debendranath returned to Calcutta to resolve the simmering disputes. but his conservative mien did not allow him to take decisive steps. He proceeded on a sea voyage to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceylon" title="Ceylon">Ceylon</a> accompanied by Sen and his 2nd son Satyendranath (a firm admirer of Mr Sen) but no concord was achieved. In 1859, the venerable and beloved Secretary of the Tattwabodhini Sabha <a href="/wiki/Ishwar_Chandra_Vidyasagar" title="Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar">Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar</a> resigned from the Brahmo Sabha in the face of Debendranath's vacillation. A meeting of the Tatwabodhini was promptly summoned with Debendranath resigning from the group he had founded. His third son <a href="/wiki/Hemendranath_Tagore" title="Hemendranath Tagore">Hemendranath Tagore</a> then a boy barely 15 years in age, and the favorite pupil of Vidyasgar, was commonly acclaimed as Debendranath's successor to head the Tattwabodhini. In the course of time he would become known as the <i>MahaAcharya</i> (or <i>Great Teacher</i>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion_of_the_Tattwabodhini_Sabha">Expansion of the Tattwabodhini Sabha</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brahmo_Samaj&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Expansion of the Tattwabodhini Sabha">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Disgusted by politics within the <a href="/wiki/Tagore_family" title="Tagore family">Tagore family</a> and the support to K. C. Sen's faction by his own brother <a href="/wiki/Satyendranath_Tagore" title="Satyendranath Tagore">Satyendranath Tagore</a>, Hemendranath took the bold decisions to expand his Sabha out from Calcutta. His close associate Pandit <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nobin_Chandra_Roy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nobin Chandra Roy (page does not exist)">Nobin Chandra Roy</a> who had joined the new institution of "Railways" in 1860 as its "Paymaster" for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_India" title="Upper India">Upper India</a> was tasked to spread Brahmoism there. With a predominantly monotheistic populace following <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sikhism" title="Sikhism">Sikhism</a> it was perceived as fertile soil for Rammohun's message. The Tattwabodhini decreed that the uncorrupted faith of the original 1830 Trust Deed would be known there as the <a href="/wiki/Adi_Dharm" title="Adi Dharm">Adi Dharm</a> to distinguish it from the distorted versions of the squabbling factions of Calcutta. The steps taken by Hemendranath Tagore, with the blessing of his father, was to institute in 1860 a suit before the Supreme Court to restore the title "Brahmo Samaj" to his faction. After losing in this suit in 1861, Keshub Sen's faction altered the name of their Samaj from "The Brahmo Samaj of India" to "Navabidhan (or the New Dispensation)". With victory in this suit and the promulgation of his <i>Brahmo Anusthan</i> (Code of Brahmaic doctrine and practice) in 1861, Hemendranath's Samaj-ists are henceforth known as the "Anusthanic" Brahmos (or Brahmos who follow the Code). The other factions were designated as "<i>An</i>anusthanic" Brahmos (or those who do not follow the Code) (this distinction was again to be legally examined before the Privy Council of Great Britain in 1901 and in 1902 the Privy Council upheld the 1897 finding of the Chief Court of the Punjab that the Adi Dharm (<i>anusthanic</i> Brahmos) were definitely not Hindus whereas the <i>Ananusthanics</i> Brahmos of Calcutta fall within Hinduism).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundation_of_the_Brahmo_Samaj">Foundation of the Brahmo Samaj</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brahmo_Samaj&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Foundation of the Brahmo Samaj">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1861 the Brahmo <i>Somaj</i> (as it was spelled then) was founded at <a href="/wiki/Lahore" title="Lahore">Lahore</a> by Nobin Roy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> It included many Bengalis from the Lahore Bar Association. Many branches were opened in the Punjab, at <a href="/wiki/Quetta" title="Quetta">Quetta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rawalpindi" title="Rawalpindi">Rawalpindi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amritsar" title="Amritsar">Amritsar</a> etc.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Secession">First Secession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brahmo_Samaj&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: First Secession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Disagreement with the Tattvabodhini came to a head publicly between the period of 1 August 1865 till November 1866 with many tiny splinter groups styling themselves as <i>Brahmo</i>. The most notable of these groups styled itself "Brahmo Samaj of India". This period is also referred to in the histories of the secessionists as the "First Schism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_status_and_number_of_adherents">Current status and number of adherents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brahmo_Samaj&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Current status and number of adherents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While the various Calcutta sponsored movements declined after 1920 and faded into obscurity after the <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_India" title="Partition of India">Partition of India</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Adi_Dharm" title="Adi Dharm">Adi Dharm</a> creed has expanded and is now the 9th largest of India's enumerated religions with 7.83 million adherents, heavily concentrated between the states of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Punjab_(India)" title="Punjab (India)">Punjab</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh" title="Uttar Pradesh">Uttar Pradesh</a>. In the Indian census of 2001 only 177 persons declared themselves a "Brahmo", but the number of subscriber members to Brahmo Samaj is somewhat larger at around 20,000 members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_and_religious_reform">Social and religious reform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brahmo_Samaj&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Social and religious reform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In all fields of social reform, including abolition of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caste_system" title="Caste system">caste system</a> and of the <a href="/wiki/Dowry#In_India" title="Dowry">dowry system</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emancipation_of_women" title="Emancipation of women">emancipation of women</a>, and improving the educational system, the <i>Brahmo Samaj</i> reflected the ideologies of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bengal_Renaissance" title="Bengal Renaissance">Bengal Renaissance</a>. Brahmoism, as a means of discussing the dowry system, was a central theme of <a href="/wiki/Sarat_Chandra_Chattopadhyay" title="Sarat Chandra Chattopadhyay">Sarat Chandra Chattopadhyay</a>'s noted 1914 <a href="/wiki/Bengali_language" title="Bengali language">Bengali language</a> <a href="/wiki/Novella" title="Novella">novella</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Parineeta" title="Parineeta">Parineeta</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1866, <a href="/wiki/Keshub_Chandra_Sen" title="Keshub Chandra Sen">Keshub Chandra Sen</a> organised the more radical "Brahmo Samaj of India" with overtones of Christianity. He campaigned for the education of women and against child marriages. But he nonetheless arranged a marriage for his own underage daughter. The <i>Brahmo Samaj of India</i> split after this act of underage marriage generated a controversy and his pro-British utterances and leaning towards Christian rites generated more controversies. A third group, "Sadharan (ordinary) Brahmo Samaj", was formed in 1878. It gradually reverted to the teaching of the Upanishads but continued the work of social reform. The movement, always an elite group without significant popular following, lost force in the 20th century.</p>
<p>After the controversy of underage marriage of Keshub Chunder Sen's daughter, the <i>Special Marriages Act of 1872</i> was enacted to set the minimum age of 14 years for marriage of girls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> All Brahmo marriages were thereafter solemnised under this law. Many Indians resented the requirement of the affirmation "I am not Hindu, nor a Mussalman, nor a Christian" for solemnising a marriage under this Act. The requirement of this declaration was imposed by <a href="/wiki/Henry_James_Sumner_Maine" title="Henry James Sumner Maine">Henry James Sumner Maine</a>, legal member of Governor General's Council appointed by Britain. The 1872 Act was repealed by the <a href="/wiki/Special_Marriage_Act,_1954" title="Special Marriage Act, 1954">Special Marriage Act, 1954</a> under which any person of any religion could marry. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindu_Marriage_Act" title="Hindu Marriage Act">Hindu Marriage Act</a>, 1955 applies to all Hindus including followers of the Brahmo Samaj. In India the statutory minimum age of marriage for followers of Brahmo Samaj is the same as for all Indians, viz., 21 years for males and 18 years for females. It is also the age marriage in Bangladesh.</p>
<p>It also supported social reform movements of people not directly attached to the <i>Samaj</i>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iswar_Chandra_Vidyasagar" title="Iswar Chandra Vidyasagar">Pandit Iswar Chandra Vidyasagar</a>’s movement which promoted widow remarriage.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Doctrine">Doctrine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brahmo_Samaj&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Doctrine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following doctrines, as noted in Renaissance of Hinduism, are common to all varieties and offshoots of the <i>Brahmo Samaj</i>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Brahmo Samajists have no faith in any scripture as an authority.</li>
<li>Brahmo Samajists have no faith in Avatars.</li>
<li>Brahmo Samajists denounce polytheism and idol-worship.</li>
<li>Brahmo Samajists are against caste restrictions.</li>
<li>Brahmo Samajists make faith in the doctrines of Karma and Rebirth optional.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anusthanic_versus_Ananusthanic_Brahmos">Anusthanic versus Ananusthanic Brahmos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brahmo_Samaj&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Anusthanic versus Ananusthanic Brahmos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To understand the differences between the 2 streams of Brahmo Samaj it is essential to understand that these implicit distinctions are based on <a href="/wiki/Caste" title="Caste">caste</a>. The Anusthanic Brahmos are exclusively either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahmins" title="Brahmins">Brahmins</a> or casteless, and exclusively adhere to Brahmoism and have no other faith. The Ananusthanic Brahmo Samajists, however, are from the remaining main caste divisions of Hinduism like <a href="/wiki/Kayastha" title="Kayastha">Kayastha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baidya" title="Baidya">Baidya</a> etc. and hence within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karmic" title="Karmic">Karmic</a> / <a href="/wiki/Reincarnation" title="Reincarnation">Rebirth</a> wheel to eternally progress (i.e. <i>Sanatan Dharm</i>) to God by moving up caste hierarchies, unlike anusthanic Brahmos for whom the next step after death is reintegration and renewal with 'God'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
