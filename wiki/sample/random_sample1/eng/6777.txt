<p><b>Brian K. Vaughan</b> (born July 17, 1976) is an American comic book and television writer, best known for the comic book series <i><a href="/wiki/Y:_The_Last_Man" title="Y: The Last Man">Y: The Last Man</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ex_Machina_(comics)" title="Ex Machina (comics)">Ex Machina</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Runaways_(comics)" title="Runaways (comics)">Runaways</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pride_of_Baghdad" title="Pride of Baghdad">Pride of Baghdad</a></i>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saga_(comics)" title="Saga (comics)">Saga</a></i>.</p>
<p>Vaughan was a writer, story editor and producer of the television series <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lost_(2004_TV_series)" title="Lost (2004 TV series)">Lost</a></i> during seasons three through five. He was nominated for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Writers_Guild_of_America_Awards" title="Writers Guild of America Awards">Writers Guild of America Award</a> for Best Dramatic Series at the <a href="/wiki/Writers_Guild_of_America_Awards_2008" title="Writers Guild of America Awards 2008">February 2009 ceremony</a> for his work on the fourth season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The writing staff was nominated for the award again at the <a href="/wiki/Writers_Guild_of_America_Awards_2009" title="Writers Guild of America Awards 2009">February 2010</a> ceremony for their work on the fifth season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WGA_10_Official_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-WGA_10_Official-2">[2]</a></sup> He was formerly the <a href="/wiki/Showrunner" title="Showrunner">showrunner</a> and executive producer of the TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Under_the_Dome_(TV_series)" title="Under the Dome (TV series)">Under the Dome</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Time-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Wired_(magazine)" title="Wired (magazine)">Wired</a></i> describes Vaughan's comics work as "quirky, acclaimed stories that don't pander and still pound pulses". His creator-owned comics work is also characterized by "finite, meticulous, years-long story arcs", on which Vaughan comments, "That's storytelling, with a beginning, a middle, and an end. Something like Spider-Man, a book that never has a third act, that seems crazy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wired4.24.07_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wired4.24.07-4">[4]</a></sup> Erik Malinowski, also of <i>Wired</i>, has called Vaughan "the greatest comic book visionary of the last five years", comparing him to <a href="/wiki/Frank_Miller_(comics)" title="Frank Miller (comics)">Frank Miller</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Moore" title="Alan Moore">Alan Moore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Pope" title="Paul Pope">Paul Pope</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Niles" title="Steve Niles">Steve Niles</a>, and praised his addition to the TV series <i>Lost</i> as redeeming that series' third season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wired4.19.07_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wired4.19.07-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2015, Vaughan was entered into the Comic Book Hall of Fame by the <a href="/wiki/Eisner_Award" title="Eisner Award">Eisner Awards</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_K._Vaughan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Brian K. Vaughan was born July 17, 1976<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ContempAuthors_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ContempAuthors-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WritersDictionary_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-WritersDictionary-7">[7]</a></sup> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleveland,_Ohio" title="Cleveland, Ohio">Cleveland, Ohio</a>, to Geoffrey and Catherine Vaughan. He grew up in <a href="/wiki/Rocky_River,_Ohio" title="Rocky River, Ohio">Rocky River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Westlake,_Ohio" title="Westlake, Ohio">Westlake</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cleveland.com_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cleveland.com-8">[8]</a></sup> Vaughan and his older brother are both fans of writer <a href="/wiki/Peter_David" title="Peter David">Peter David</a>, and according to Vaughan, their adolescent comics reading was largely defined by a shared love of David's 12-year run on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Incredible_Hulk" title="The Incredible Hulk">The Incredible Hulk</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saga.2310_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saga.2310-9">[9]</a></sup> Vaughan also cites <a href="/wiki/Joss_Whedon" title="Joss Whedon">Joss Whedon</a> as the reason he wanted to become a writer,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorkTimes_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorkTimes-10">[10]</a></sup> a decision he made while attending <a href="/wiki/Saint_Ignatius_High_School_(Cleveland)" title="Saint Ignatius High School (Cleveland)">St. Ignatius High School</a>, from which he graduated in 1994. He then attended <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a> to study film. While a student there, Vaughan took part in <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Stan-hattan_Project" title="Stan-hattan Project">Stan-hattan Project</a>, a class for fledgling comic book writers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cleveland.com_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cleveland.com-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_K._Vaughan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vaughan's first credit was for <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Age_of_Apocalypse" title="Age of Apocalypse">Tales From the Age of Apocalypse</a></i> #2 (December 1996). He would eventually write for some of the highest-profile characters at Marvel, including <a href="/wiki/X-Men" title="X-Men">X-Men</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spider-Man" title="Spider-Man">Spider-Man</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Captain_America" title="Captain America">Captain America</a>. He would also write <a href="/wiki/Batman" title="Batman">Batman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green_Lantern" title="Green Lantern">Green Lantern</a> for <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Buffy_the_Vampire_Slayer_Season_Eight" title="Buffy the Vampire Slayer Season Eight">Buffy the Vampire Slayer Season Eight</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/Dark_Horse_Comics" title="Dark Horse Comics">Dark Horse Comics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cleveland.com_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cleveland.com-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 2002 to 2008, Vaughan, who came to prefer writing his own characters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WizardWorld_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-WizardWorld-11">[11]</a></sup> wrote the creator-owned monthly series <i><a href="/wiki/Y:_The_Last_Man" title="Y: The Last Man">Y: The Last Man</a></i>, a post-apocalyptic science fiction series about the only man to survive the apparent simultaneous death of every male mammal on Earth. The series was published in sixty issues by <a href="/wiki/Vertigo_(DC_Comics)" title="Vertigo (DC Comics)">Vertigo</a> and collected in a series of ten paperback volumes (and later a series of five hardcover "Deluxe" volumes). The series received <a href="/wiki/Eisner_Award" title="Eisner Award">Eisner Awards</a> in 2005 and 2008, and numerous other nominations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisners05_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eisners05-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SDCC08_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-SDCC08-13">[13]</a></sup> The film rights to the series were acquired by <a href="/wiki/New_Line_Cinema" title="New Line Cinema">New Line Cinema</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Vaughan wrote his own screenplay for the project,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wired4.24.07_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wired4.24.07-4">[4]</a></sup> though it was reported in March 2012 that Matthew Federman and Stephen Scaia were in final negotiations to write their own version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, Vaughan published the graphic novel <i><a href="/wiki/Pride_of_Baghdad" title="Pride of Baghdad">Pride of Baghdad</a></i>, which centers on a group of lions who escape from an Iraqi zoo after the start of the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wired4.24.07_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wired4.24.07-4">[4]</a></sup> The book was praised by <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> who named it the Best Original Graphic Novel of 2006, calling it a "modern classic", lauding it for combining a tale of survival and family with a powerful analogy of war, and praising Vaughan for representing various viewpoints through the different lion characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGNPride_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGNPride-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 2004 to 2010 Vaughan wrote another creator-owned series, <i><a href="/wiki/Ex_Machina_(comics)" title="Ex Machina (comics)">Ex Machina</a></i>, a political thriller that depicts the life of Mitchell Hundred, a former superhero known as the Great Machine who, in the wake of his heroism during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/September_11,_2001_attacks" title="September 11, 2001 attacks">September 11, 2001 attacks</a>, is elected <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_New_York_City" title="Mayor of New York City">Mayor of New York City</a>. The story is set during Hundred's term in office, and interwoven with flashbacks to his past as the Great Machine. Through this, the series explores both the political situations Hundred finds himself in, and the mysteries surrounding his superpowers. <a href="/wiki/New_Line_Cinema" title="New Line Cinema">New Line Cinema</a> purchased the film rights to the series in July 2005, and commissioned Vaughan to write one of the two commissioned scripts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> which he was reported to be working on in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wired4.24.07_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wired4.24.07-4">[4]</a></sup> Following the conclusion of <i>Ex Machina</i> in 2010, Vaughan reiterated his previous statement that he would concentrate on creator-owned work, saying, "I realized when I turned in this final Ex Machina script that it would be the first time I wasn't under some kind of deadline at Marvel or DC since 1996. That's a huge chunk of my life to spend with those characters. I love them, and I still read Marvel and DC's superhero books. I just think I'm better when I'm working on my own creations. When there are so many talented creators out there who are better at that stuff than me, I should leave those characters to them. I should do what I'm fortunate enough to be in the position to do, which is to create more new stuff."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>Vaughan was a writer, executive story editor and producer for seasons 3 to 5 on the <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a> TV series <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lost_(2004_TV_series)" title="Lost (2004 TV series)">Lost</a></i>, a job he earned on the basis of his work on <i>Y: The Last Man</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wired4.24.07_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Wired4.24.07-4">[4]</a></sup> of which <i>Lost</i> co-creator and executive producer <a href="/wiki/Damon_Lindelof" title="Damon Lindelof">Damon Lindelof</a> was an ardent fan. Lindelof showed that book to series <a href="/wiki/Showrunner" title="Showrunner">showrunner</a> and executive producer <a href="/wiki/Carlton_Cuse" title="Carlton Cuse">Carlton Cuse</a>. Lindelof relates, "And I told him, 'We need a guy like this on the show, but I don't think he'd ever do it. I don't think he even works in L.A.' And the next thing we knew, he was on the show." He began his stint on the series as executive story editor with the episode "<a href="/wiki/The_Man_from_Tallahassee" title="The Man from Tallahassee">The Man from Tallahassee</a>", which premiered in March 2007. Vaughan continued as story editor on several episodes until he began writing episodes, beginning with the episode "<a href="/wiki/Catch-22_(Lost)" title="Catch-22 (Lost)">Catch-22</a>", which Vaughan co-wrote with Jeff Pinker, and premiered in April that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cleveland.com_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cleveland.com-8">[8]</a></sup> That episode was praised by <i><a href="/wiki/Wired_(magazine)" title="Wired (magazine)">Wired</a></i> writer Erik Malinowski, who stated that the themes that Vaughan's carried over to <i>Lost</i> from his comics work, including intricately weaved storylines typified by pathos and hope, as well as pop culture references, redeemed that series' third season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wired4.19.07_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wired4.19.07-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Vaughan would write a total of 7 episodes, the last of which was the April 2009 episode "<a href="/wiki/Dead_Is_Dead" title="Dead Is Dead">Dead Is Dead</a>". He was first credited as a producer with the fourth season premiere "<a href="/wiki/The_Beginning_of_the_End_(Lost)" title="The Beginning of the End (Lost)">The Beginning of the End</a>", eventually acting as producer on a total of 29 episodes. He was also a co-producer on <i><a href="/wiki/Lost:_Missing_Pieces" title="Lost: Missing Pieces">Lost: Missing Pieces</a></i>, a spinoff Internet short film series produced during the hiatus between the show's third and fourth seasons.</p>
<p>In November 2011 <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a> selected Vaughan to adapt the <a href="/wiki/Stephen_King" title="Stephen King">Stephen King</a> novel <i><a href="/wiki/Under_the_Dome_(novel)" title="Under the Dome (novel)">Under the Dome</a></i> into a television series for <a href="/wiki/Showtime_(TV_network)" title="Showtime (TV network)">Showtime</a>, which is Vaughan's first television work since <i>Lost</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Vaughan was the showrunner and executive producer of the series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Time-3">[3]</a></sup> He exited the show before the second season premiered in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vaughan_exits_Under_The_Dome_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vaughan_exits_Under_The_Dome-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>On March 14, 2012, <a href="/wiki/Image_Comics" title="Image Comics">Image Comics</a> published the first issue of Vaughan and <a href="/wiki/Fiona_Staples" title="Fiona Staples">Fiona Staples</a>' epic <a href="/wiki/Space_opera" title="Space opera">space opera</a>/fantasy series, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saga_(comics)" title="Saga (comics)">Saga</a></i>, which he conceived to be a concept strictly relegated to comics, and not adapted to other media. Although Vaughan was a child<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ComicsAlliance_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-ComicsAlliance-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollywoodReporterSaga_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-HollywoodReporterSaga-23">[23]</a></sup> when he first conceived of the ideas for the book - which owes its inspiration to <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">Star Wars</a></i> - it was not until his wife became pregnant with his second child that he began to write the series, which harbors parenthood as an underlying theme. The series depicts two aliens from warring races trying to survive with their newborn daughter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollywoodReporterSaga_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-HollywoodReporterSaga-23">[23]</a></sup> The book is Vaughan's first publication for Image Comics,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and represents the first time he has employed <a href="/wiki/First-person_narrative" title="First-person narrative">first-person narration</a> in his comics writing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ComicsAlliance_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-ComicsAlliance-22">[22]</a></sup> The first issue sold out of its first printing ahead of its March 14 release date, with a second printing ordered for April 11, the same release date for issue #2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ComicsBulletin_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-ComicsBulletin-25">[25]</a></sup> The series has received positive reviews<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> from <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ain%27t_it_Cool_News" title="Ain't it Cool News">Ain't it Cool News</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Comic_Book_Resources" title="Comic Book Resources">Comic Book Resources</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Publishers_Weekly" title="Publishers Weekly">Publishers Weekly</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Time-3">[3]</a></sup> It has also appeared on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times_Bestseller_List" title="New York Times Bestseller List"><i>New York Times</i> Graphic Books Best Seller List</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> won three 2013 <a href="/wiki/Eisner_Award" title="Eisner Award">Eisner Awards</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013Eisners_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-2013Eisners-33">[33]</a></sup> won a <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Award_for_Best_Graphic_Story" title="Hugo Award for Best Graphic Story">Hugo Award</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and was nominated for seven <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Award" title="Harvey Award">Harvey Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBR_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBR-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2013, Vaughan published the first issue of <a href="/wiki/The_Private_Eye" title="The Private Eye">The Private Eye</a> with artist <a href="/wiki/Marcos_Mart%C3%ADn" title="Marcos Martín">Marcos Martín</a> on <a href="/wiki/Panel_Syndicate" title="Panel Syndicate">Panel Syndicate</a>, a pay-what-you-want host for their creative efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Panel Syndicate offers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DRM-Free" title="DRM-Free">DRM-Free</a> comics available for purchase/download for whatever price readers wish to pay. Through Panel Syndicate, Vaughan and Martin published 10 issues of Private Eye and released the first issue of Barrier in late 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the Image Expo in January 2015, it was announced that Vaughan would release two new books through Image Comics in 2015: <i>Paper Girls</i> with <a href="/wiki/Cliff_Chiang" title="Cliff Chiang">Cliff Chiang</a> and Matthew Wilson, and <i><a href="/wiki/We_Stand_On_Guard" title="We Stand On Guard">We Stand On Guard</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Steve_Skroce" title="Steve Skroce">Steve Skroce</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_K._Vaughan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vaughan and his wife, a native of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottawa,_Canada" title="Ottawa, Canada">Ottawa, Canada</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and playwright, live in Los Angeles<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WizardWorld_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-WizardWorld-11">[11]</a></sup> and have two children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollywoodReporterSaga_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-HollywoodReporterSaga-23">[23]</a></sup> They also have a pet <a href="/wiki/Dachshund" title="Dachshund">Dachshund</a> named Hamburger that has been repeatedly referenced as Vaughan's aide in selecting letters for the <i>Saga</i> letter column,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> including appearing in an illustration of Vaughan and Staples that was included in a 2013 <i>Time</i> magazine story on <i>Saga</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Time-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_media">Social media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_K._Vaughan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Social media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite a long absence from social media in the earlier part of his career, Vaughan created a Twitter account in November, 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> He had avoided an online presence deliberately, crediting this to a preference for privacy and being a lazy writer who struggled with the idea of writing for free.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Subsequently, he would turn his Twitter account on and off again every so often.</p>
