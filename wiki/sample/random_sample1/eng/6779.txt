<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_d%27Arcy_James&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The actor's middle name by birth is <b>d'Arcy</b>. As there was another actor named Brian James in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Actors_Equity" title="Actors Equity">Equity</a>, he uses his full name as his professional name. He graduated from <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_University" title="Northwestern University">Northwestern University</a>'s School of Communication. He received a nomination for the <a href="/wiki/Tony_Award_for_Best_Featured_Actor_in_a_Musical" title="Tony Award for Best Featured Actor in a Musical">Tony Award for Best Featured Actor in a Musical</a> in 2002 for his portrayal of Sidney Falco in <i><a href="/wiki/Sweet_Smell_of_Success_(musical)" title="Sweet Smell of Success (musical)">Sweet Smell of Success</a></i>, co-starring <a href="/wiki/John_Lithgow" title="John Lithgow">John Lithgow</a>. He received an <a href="/wiki/Obie_Award" title="Obie Award">Obie Award</a> for his performance in the one-man play <i>The Good Thief</i> by <a href="/wiki/Conor_McPherson" title="Conor McPherson">Conor McPherson</a>.</p>
<p>Additional Broadway credits include playing Frederick Barrett in <i><a href="/wiki/Titanic_(musical)" title="Titanic (musical)">Titanic</a></i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lincoln_Center" title="Lincoln Center">Lincoln Center</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Carousel_(musical)" title="Carousel (musical)">Carousel</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Blood_Brothers_(musical)" title="Blood Brothers (musical)">Blood Brothers</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Off-Broadway" title="Off-Broadway">Off-Broadway</a> credits include <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Lippa" title="Andrew Lippa">Andrew Lippa</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wild_Party_(Lippa_musical)" title="The Wild Party (Lippa musical)">The Wild Party</a></i> in 2000 opposite <a href="/wiki/Julia_Murney" title="Julia Murney">Julia Murney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Idina_Menzel" title="Idina Menzel">Idina Menzel</a> for which he received a <a href="/wiki/Drama_Desk_Award" title="Drama Desk Award">Drama Desk Award</a> nomination, as well as <a href="/wiki/Adam_Guettel" title="Adam Guettel">Adam Guettel</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Floyd_Collins_(musical)" title="Floyd Collins (musical)">Floyd Collins</a></i> and the Gershwins' <i><a href="/wiki/Pardon_My_English" title="Pardon My English">Pardon My English</a></i>. He appeared in <a href="/wiki/Martin_McDonagh" title="Martin McDonagh">Martin McDonagh</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lieutenant_of_Inishmore" title="The Lieutenant of Inishmore">The Lieutenant of Inishmore</a></i> on Broadway, replaced <a href="/wiki/Norbert_Leo_Butz" title="Norbert Leo Butz">Norbert Leo Butz</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Dirty_Rotten_Scoundrels_(musical)" title="Dirty Rotten Scoundrels (musical)">Dirty Rotten Scoundrels</a></i>, and starred in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Apple_Tree" title="The Apple Tree">The Apple Tree</a></i> opposite <a href="/wiki/Kristin_Chenoweth" title="Kristin Chenoweth">Kristin Chenoweth</a>. In 2004, he released a Christmas album titled <i>From Christmas Eve to Christmas Morn</i>. He was the Broadway version of Bob Wallace, whose character was originated by <a href="/wiki/Bing_Crosby" title="Bing Crosby">Bing Crosby</a>, in <i><a href="/wiki/White_Christmas_(musical)" title="White Christmas (musical)">White Christmas</a></i> in 2004. He played the role of Dan Goodman in the new musical <i><a href="/wiki/Next_to_Normal" title="Next to Normal">Next to Normal</a></i> Off-Broadway at <a href="/wiki/Second_Stage_Theatre" title="Second Stage Theatre">Second Stage Theatre</a> in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He then starred opposite <a href="/wiki/Sutton_Foster" title="Sutton Foster">Sutton Foster</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Sieber" title="Christopher Sieber">Christopher Sieber</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Shrek_(character)" title="Shrek (character)">title character</a> in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shrek_the_Musical" title="Shrek the Musical">Shrek the Musical</a></i>. The show began previews on Broadway November 8, 2008 and opened on December 14 at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Broadway_Theatre" title="The Broadway Theatre">The Broadway Theatre</a> after a tryout in <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> For this role he won the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Critics_Circle_Award" title="Outer Critics Circle Award">Outer Critics Circle Award</a> for Outstanding Actor in a Musical and the <a href="/wiki/Drama_Desk_Award_for_Outstanding_Actor_in_a_Musical" title="Drama Desk Award for Outstanding Actor in a Musical">Drama Desk Award for Outstanding Actor in a Musical</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He was also nominated for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tony_Award_for_Best_Leading_Actor_in_a_Musical" title="Tony Award for Best Leading Actor in a Musical">Tony Award for Best Leading Actor in a Musical</a> for his portrayal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He departed the cast after one year in the role and was replaced by Ben Crawford.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>James starred in the Broadway play, <i><a href="/wiki/Time_Stands_Still_(play)" title="Time Stands Still (play)">Time Stands Still</a></i> which began preview performances on January 5, 2010 and officially opened on January 25 at the <a href="/wiki/Samuel_J._Friedman_Theatre" title="Samuel J. Friedman Theatre">Samuel J. Friedman Theatre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The show ended its limited run on March 27, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> For this role he won the broadway.com Audience Award for Favorite Featured Actor in a Play.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He reprised the role of Dan Goodman in the Broadway company of <i>Next to Normal</i> at the <a href="/wiki/Booth_Theatre" title="Booth Theatre">Booth Theatre</a>. He replaced <a href="/wiki/J._Robert_Spencer" title="J. Robert Spencer">J. Robert Spencer</a> beginning May 17, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> James ended his limited engagement on July 18, 2010 and was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Jason_Danieley" title="Jason Danieley">Jason Danieley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 19, 2010 James performed in front of President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> and First Lady <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Obama" title="Michelle Obama">Michelle Obama</a> at <i>A Broadway Celebration: In Performance at the White House</i>, which also aired on <a href="/wiki/PBS" title="PBS">PBS</a> October 20, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> He returned to <i><a href="/wiki/Time_Stands_Still_(play)" title="Time Stands Still (play)">Time Stands Still</a></i> when the show returned to Broadway. It closed on January 30, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>James was part of the cast of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Broadcasting_Company" title="National Broadcasting Company">NBC</a> musical series <i><a href="/wiki/Smash_(TV_series)" title="Smash (TV series)">Smash</a></i>, playing Frank, who was the husband of <a href="/wiki/Debra_Messing" title="Debra Messing">Debra Messing</a>'s character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> NBC officially picked up <i>Smash</i> as a series on May 11, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The program made its series premiere on February 6, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> He did not return to the show as a series regular for its second and final season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> James starred in <a href="/wiki/Torstein_Blixfjord" title="Torstein Blixfjord">Torstein Blixfjord</a>'s 2012 short film <i>Bird In A Box</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>He co-hosted the 57th <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drama_Desk_Awards" title="Drama Desk Awards">Drama Desk Awards</a> with <a href="/wiki/Brooke_Shields" title="Brooke Shields">Brooke Shields</a> on June 3, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>James starred as Bick in the new musical, <i><a href="/wiki/Giant_(musical)" title="Giant (musical)">Giant</a></i>, which ran at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Theater" title="Public Theater">Public Theater</a> from October 26 - December 16, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> For this role James received a nomination for Best Actor in a Musical at the Drama Desk Awards and <a href="/wiki/Drama_League_Award" title="Drama League Award">Drama League Award</a> for Distinguished Performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>He also performed at the 29th birthday celebration of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Pops" title="New York Pops">New York Pops</a>, which was a celebration called "Journey On", celebrating the work of <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Flaherty" title="Stephen Flaherty">Stephen Flaherty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lynn_Ahrens" title="Lynn Ahrens">Lynn Ahrens</a>. He performed "Wheels of a Dream" from <i><a href="/wiki/Ragtime_(musical)" title="Ragtime (musical)">Ragtime</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Rannells" title="Andrew Rannells">Andrew Rannells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rebecca_Luker" title="Rebecca Luker">Rebecca Luker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liz_Callaway" title="Liz Callaway">Liz Callaway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Chamberlin" title="Kevin Chamberlin">Kevin Chamberlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jason_Danieley" title="Jason Danieley">Jason Danieley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boyd_Gaines" title="Boyd Gaines">Boyd Gaines</a>, Lewis Grosso, <a href="/wiki/Norm_Lewis" title="Norm Lewis">Norm Lewis</a>, Kecia Lewis-Evans, <a href="/wiki/Darlene_Love" title="Darlene Love">Darlene Love</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marin_Mazzie" title="Marin Mazzie">Marin Mazzie</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tiler_Peck" title="Tiler Peck">Tiler Peck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nikki_M._James" title="Nikki M. James">Nikki M. James</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rachel_York" title="Rachel York">Rachel York</a>, and <a href="/wiki/LaChanze" title="LaChanze">LaChanze</a>, along with Flaherty and Ahrens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<p>He played Banquo in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lincoln_Center_Theater" title="Lincoln Center Theater">Lincoln Center Theater</a> production of <i><a href="/wiki/Macbeth" title="Macbeth">Macbeth</a></i>, which began previews in October 2013 and officially opened at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vivian_Beaumont_Theatre" title="Vivian Beaumont Theatre">Vivian Beaumont Theatre</a> in November 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> He starred as the original <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_George_III" title="King George III">King George III</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Off-Broadway" title="Off-Broadway">off-Broadway</a> production of the acclaimed musical <i><a href="/wiki/Hamilton_(musical)" title="Hamilton (musical)">Hamilton</a></i>, with previews starting January 20, 2015 and officially opening on February 17. Actor <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Groff" title="Jonathan Groff">Jonathan Groff</a> replaced James in the off-Broadway run on March 3, 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>He stars in a new musical on Broadway, <i><a href="/wiki/Something_Rotten!" title="Something Rotten!">Something Rotten!</a></i>, which opened in previews at the <a href="/wiki/St._James_Theatre" title="St. James Theatre">St. James Theatre</a> on March 23, 2015 and officially on April 22,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> for which he received his third Tony nomination. James played Matt Carroll in the 2015 film <i><a href="/wiki/Spotlight_(film)" title="Spotlight (film)">Spotlight</a></i>. In March 2016, it was announced that James was cast in the CBS pilot, <i>Superior Donuts</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_d%27Arcy_James&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>James was born in <a href="/wiki/Saginaw,_Michigan" title="Saginaw, Michigan">Saginaw, Michigan</a>, the son of Mary (nÃ©e Kelly), a seller of children's books, and Thomas F. James (deceased), a lawyer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> His maternal grandfather was <a href="/wiki/Harry_Kelly_(politician)" title="Harry Kelly (politician)">Harry Kelly</a>, a former Governor of <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> His uncle, <a href="/wiki/Brian_Kelly_(actor)" title="Brian Kelly (actor)">Brian Kelly</a>, was an actor featured in the series <i><a href="/wiki/Flipper_(1964_TV_series)" title="Flipper (1964 TV series)">Flipper</a></i> and a producer of the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a></i>. He has three siblings: brother Andrew, a portfolio manager; sister Kate, an actress and writer; and sister Anne (Noonan), an actress and teacher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Northwestern_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Northwestern-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brian is married to Jennifer Prescott, whom he met when both performed in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lincoln_Center" title="Lincoln Center">Lincoln Center</a> production, and together they have a daughter, Grace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Northwestern_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Northwestern-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
