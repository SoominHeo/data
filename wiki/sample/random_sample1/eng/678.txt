<p><b>70 mm film</b> (or <b>65 mm film</b>) is a wide high-resolution <a href="/wiki/Film_gauge" title="Film gauge">film gauge</a> for still and motion picture photography, with higher resolution than the standard <a href="/wiki/35_mm_film" title="35 mm film">35 mm</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_film_formats" title="List of film formats">motion picture film format</a>. As used in cameras, the film is 65 mm (2.6 in) wide. For projection, the original 65 mm film is printed on 70 mm (2.8 in) film. The additional 5 mm are for 4 magnetic strips holding six tracks of sound. Although later 70 mm prints use digital sound encoding, the vast majority of existing and surviving 70 mm prints predate this technology. Each frame is five <a href="/wiki/Film_perforations" title="Film perforations">perforations</a> tall, with an <a href="/wiki/Aspect_ratio_(image)" title="Aspect ratio (image)">aspect ratio</a> of 2.20:1. The vast majority of cinemas are unable to handle 70 mm film, and so original 70 mm films are shown using either 35 mm prints in the regular <a href="/wiki/CinemaScope" title="CinemaScope">CinemaScope</a>/​<a href="/wiki/Panavision" title="Panavision">Panavision</a> aspect ratio of 2.35:1, or, in later years, by means of <a href="/wiki/Digital_cinema" title="Digital cinema">digital projectors</a> at these venues.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=70_mm_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Films formatted with a width of 70 mm have existed since the early days of the motion picture industry. The first 70 mm format film was most likely footage of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henley_Regatta" title="Henley Regatta">Henley Regatta</a>, which was projected in 1896 and 1897, but may have been filmed as early as 1894. It required a specially built projector built by <a href="/wiki/Herman_Casler" title="Herman Casler">Herman Casler</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canastota,_New_York" title="Canastota, New York">Canastota, New York</a> and had a ratio similar to full frame, with an aperture of 2.75 inches (70 mm) by 2 inches (51 mm). There were also several film formats of various sizes from 50 to 68 mm which were developed from 1884 onwards, including <a href="/wiki/Cin%C3%A9orama" title="Cinéorama">Cinéorama</a> (not to be confused with the entirely distinct "<a href="/wiki/Cinerama" title="Cinerama">Cinerama</a>" format), started in 1900 by <a href="/wiki/Raoul_Grimoin-Sanson" title="Raoul Grimoin-Sanson">Raoul Grimoin-Sanson</a>. In 1914 the Italian <a href="/wiki/Filoteo_Alberini" title="Filoteo Alberini">Filoteo Alberini</a> invented a panoramic film system utilising a 70 mm wide film called Panoramica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fox_Grandeur">Fox Grandeur</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=70_mm_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fox Grandeur">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/70_mm_Grandeur_film" title="70 mm Grandeur film">70 mm Grandeur film</a></div>
<p>In 1928, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox_Film_Corporation" title="Fox Film Corporation">Fox Film Corporation</a> started working on a wide film format using 70 mm film which they named <a href="/wiki/70_mm_Grandeur_film" title="70 mm Grandeur film">Grandeur</a>. This was one of a number of wide-film processes developed by some of the major film studios at about that time. However, due to strong resistance from movie theater owners, who were in the process of equipping their theaters for sound, none of these systems became commercially successful. Fox dropped Grandeur in 1930.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Todd-AO">Todd-AO</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=70_mm_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Todd-AO">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Todd-AO_process" title="Todd-AO process">Todd-AO process</a></div>
<p>Producer <a href="/wiki/Mike_Todd" title="Mike Todd">Mike Todd</a> had been one of the founders of <a href="/wiki/Cinerama" title="Cinerama">Cinerama</a>, a wide-screen movie process that was launched in 1952. Cinerama employed three 35 mm film projectors running in synchronism to project a wide (2.6:1) image onto a deeply curved screen. Although the results were impressive, the system was expensive, cumbersome and had some serious shortcomings due to the need to match up three separate projected images. Todd left the company to develop a system of his own which, he hoped, would be as impressive as Cinerama, yet be simpler and cheaper and avoid the problems associated with three-strip projection; in his own words, he wanted "Cinerama out of one hole".</p>
<p>In collaboration with the American Optical company, Todd developed a system which was to be called "<a href="/wiki/Todd-AO" title="Todd-AO">Todd-AO</a>". This uses a single 70 mm wide film and was introduced with the film <i><a href="/wiki/Oklahoma!_(1955_film)" title="Oklahoma! (1955 film)">Oklahoma!</a></i> in October 1955. The 70 mm film is perforated at the same pitch (0.187 inch, 4.75mm) as standard 35 mm film. With a five-perforation pull-down, the Todd-AO system provides a frame dimension of 1.912 inch (48.56mm) by 0.816 inch (20.73mm) giving an aspect ratio of 2.2:1.</p>
<p>The original version of Todd-AO used a frame rate of 30 per second, 25% faster than the 24 frames per second that was (and is) the standard; this was changed after the second film – <i><a href="/wiki/Around_the_World_in_80_Days_(1956_film)" title="Around the World in 80 Days (1956 film)">Around the World in 80 Days</a></i> - because of the need to produce (24 frame/sec) 35 mm reduction prints from the Todd-AO 65mm negative. The Todd-AO format was originally intended to use a deeply curved Cinerama-type screen but this failed to survive beyond the first few films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, in the 1960s and 70s, such films as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sound_of_Music_(film)" title="The Sound of Music (film)">The Sound of Music</a></i> (which had been filmed in Todd-AO) and <i><a href="/wiki/Patton_(film)" title="Patton (film)">Patton</a></i> (which had been filmed in a copycat process known as Dimension 150) were shown in some Cinerama cinemas, which allowed for deeply curved screens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Todd-AO adopted a similar multi-channel magnetic sound system to the one developed for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cinemascope" title="Cinemascope">Cinemascope</a> two years earlier, recorded on "stripes" of magnetic oxide deposited on the film. However Todd-AO has six channels instead of the four of Cinemascope and due to the wider stripes and faster film speed provides superior audio quality. Five of these six channels are fed to five speakers spaced behind the screen, and the sixth is fed to surround speakers around the walls of the auditorium.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Panavision_and_the_65.2F70mm_format">Panavision and the 65/70mm format</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=70_mm_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Panavision and the 65/70mm format">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Super_Panavision_70" title="Super Panavision 70">Super Panavision 70</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ultra_Panavision_70" title="Ultra Panavision 70">Ultra Panavision 70</a></div>
<p>Panavision developed their own 65/70mm system that was technically compatible and virtually identical to Todd-AO. Monikered as <a href="/wiki/Super_Panavision_70" title="Super Panavision 70">Super Panavision 70</a>, it used spherical lenses and the same 2.20:1 aspect ratio at 24 frames per second. Panavision also had another 65mm system, (<a href="/wiki/Ultra_Panavision_70" title="Ultra Panavision 70">Ultra Panavision 70</a>), which sprang from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MGM_Camera_65" title="MGM Camera 65">MGM Camera 65</a> system they helped develop for MGM that was used to film <i><a href="/wiki/Raintree_County_(film)" title="Raintree County (film)">Raintree County</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Ben-Hur_(1959_film)" title="Ben-Hur (1959 film)">Ben-Hur</a></i>. Both Ultra Panavision 70 and MGM Camera 65 employed an anamorphic lens with a 1.25x squeeze on a 65mm negative (as opposed to 35mm <a href="/wiki/CinemaScope" title="CinemaScope">CinemaScope</a> which used a 2x compression, or 8-perf, horizontally filmed 35mm <a href="/wiki/Technirama" title="Technirama">Technirama</a> which used a 1.5x compression). When projected on a 70mm print, a 1.25x anamorphic projection lens was used to decompress the image to an aspect ratio of 2.76:1, one of the widest ever used in commercial cinema.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline">Decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=70_mm_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to the high cost of 70 mm film and the expensive projection system and screen required to use the stock, distribution for films using the stock was limited, although this did not always hurt profits. Most 70 mm films were also re-released on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/35mm_film" title="35mm film">35mm film</a> for a wider distribution after the initial debut of the film. <i><a href="/wiki/South_Pacific_(1958_film)" title="South Pacific (1958 film)">South Pacific</a></i> (1958), <i><a href="/wiki/Lawrence_of_Arabia_(film)" title="Lawrence of Arabia (film)">Lawrence of Arabia</a></i> (1962), <i><a href="/wiki/My_Fair_Lady_(film)" title="My Fair Lady (film)">My Fair Lady</a></i> (1964), and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sound_of_Music_(film)" title="The Sound of Music (film)">The Sound of Music</a></i> (1965) are well-known films widely shown in 70 mm format with a general release in 35 mm format.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blow-ups">Blow-ups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=70_mm_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Blow-ups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the 1970s, use of 65 mm stock for original photography declined markedly. However 70 mm "blow-ups" of films made in 35 mm were sometimes made for prestige showings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> These included such films as <i><a href="/wiki/Camelot_(film)" title="Camelot (film)">Camelot</a></i> (1967), <i><a href="/wiki/Oliver!_(film)" title="Oliver! (film)">Oliver!</a></i> (1968), <i><a href="/wiki/Cromwell_(film)" title="Cromwell (film)">Cromwell</a></i> (1970), and <i><a href="/wiki/Fiddler_on_the_Roof_(film)" title="Fiddler on the Roof (film)">Fiddler on the Roof</a></i> (1971). These enlargements did not have the sharpness and smoothness of 70 mm origination, but these larger prints allowed for a brighter image on very big screens and were more stable when projected. In addition 70 mm prints also had better sound quality than was possible from 35 mm. However these "blow-ups" rarely used the full six channels of the Todd-AO system and instead used the four-track mixes made for 35 mm prints, the additional half-left and half-right speakers of the Todd-AO layout being fed with a simple mix of the signals intended for the adjacent speakers (known as a "spread") or simply left blank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-6">[6]</a></sup> However, if a 70mm film was shown in a Cinerama theatre, the Cinerama sound system was used. From 1976 onwards many 70 mm prints used Dolby noise reduction on the magnetic tracks but Dolby disapproved of the "spread" and instead re-allocated the 6 available tracks to provide for left, center and right screen channels, left and right surround channels plus a "low-frequency enhancement" channel to give more body to low-frequency bass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This layout came to be known as "<a href="/wiki/5.1_surround_sound" title="5.1 surround sound">5.1</a>" (the "point one" is the low-frequency enhancement channel) and was subsequently adopted for digital sound systems used with 35 mm.</p>
<p>In the 1980s the use of these "blow-ups" increased with large numbers of 70 mm prints being made of some blockbusters of the period such as the 125 70 mm prints made of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Empire_Strikes_Back" title="The Empire Strikes Back">The Empire Strikes Back</a></i> (1980).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-6">[6]</a></sup> However the early 1990s saw the advent of digital sound systems (<a href="/wiki/Dolby_Digital" title="Dolby Digital">Dolby Digital</a>, <a href="/wiki/DTS_(sound_system)" title="DTS (sound system)">DTS</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sony_Dynamic_Digital_Sound" title="Sony Dynamic Digital Sound">SDDS</a>) for 35 mm prints which meant that 35 mm could finally match 70 mm for sound quality but at a far lower cost. Coupled with the rise of the multiplex cinema, which meant that audiences were increasingly seeing films on relatively small screens rather than the giant screens of the old "Picture Palaces", this meant that the expensive 70 mm format went out of favour again. The <a href="/wiki/DTS_(sound_system)#DTS_70_mm" title="DTS (sound system)">DTS</a> digital sound-on-disc system was adapted for use with 70 mm film, thus saving the significant costs of magnetic striping, but this has not been enough to stop the decline, and 70 mm prints were rarely made.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_use">Current use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=70_mm_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Current use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the late 20th century, the usage of 65 mm negative film drastically reduced, in part due to the high cost of 65 mm raw stock and processing. Some of the few films since 1990 shot entirely on 65 mm stock are <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Branagh" title="Kenneth Branagh">Kenneth Branagh</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Hamlet_(1996_film)" title="Hamlet (1996 film)">Hamlet</a></i> (1996), <a href="/wiki/Ron_Fricke" title="Ron Fricke">Ron Fricke</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Baraka_(film)" title="Baraka (film)">Baraka</a></i> (1992), and its sequel <i><a href="/wiki/Samsara_(2011_film)" title="Samsara (2011 film)">Samsara</a></i> (2011), <a href="/wiki/Paul_Thomas_Anderson" title="Paul Thomas Anderson">Paul Thomas Anderson</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Master_(2012_film)" title="The Master (2012 film)">The Master</a></i> (2012) and <a href="/wiki/Quentin_Tarantino" title="Quentin Tarantino">Quentin Tarantino</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hateful_Eight" title="The Hateful Eight">The Hateful Eight</a></i> (2015). Other films used 65 mm cameras sparingly, for selected scenes or special effects. Films with limited 65 mm footage include <a href="/wiki/Terrence_Malick" title="Terrence Malick">Terrence Malick</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_World_(2005_film)" title="The New World (2005 film)">The New World</a></i> (2005) and <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Nolan" title="Christopher Nolan">Christopher Nolan</a>'s latest four movies, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dark_Knight_(film)" title="The Dark Knight (film)">The Dark Knight</a></i> (featured 28 minutes of IMAX footage), <i><a href="/wiki/Inception" title="Inception">Inception</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dark_Knight_Rises" title="The Dark Knight Rises">The Dark Knight Rises</a></i> (over an hour in IMAX) and <i><a href="/wiki/Interstellar_(film)" title="Interstellar (film)">Interstellar</a></i>.</p>
<p>Since the 2010s most of the movie theaters across the world have converted to digital projection systems, largely eliminating 70mm film projectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> 70mm has retained a niche market of amateurs and enthusiasts.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Digital_70_mm_cameras">Digital 70 mm cameras</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=70_mm_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Digital 70 mm cameras">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are three types of digital cinema cameras with a 65 mm sensor, the <a href="/wiki/Digital_cinematography#Vision_Research_Phantom" title="Digital cinematography">Phantom 65</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Arri_Alexa" title="Arri Alexa">Arri Alexa 65</a> and the forthcoming <a href="/wiki/IMAX" title="IMAX">IMAX 2D Digital Camera</a>. Otti International's Phil Kroll developed the world's first 65/70 mm <a href="/wiki/Telecine" title="Telecine">telecine</a> transfer system. This camera has been used in Hollywood to digitally master 70 and 65 mm films.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=70_mm_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For home theater, VHS and DVD did not offer enough resolution to carry the full image quality captured by 70 mm film, and VHS and DVD video transfers were usually prepared from 35 mm reduction elements. The high-definition <a href="/wiki/Blu-ray" title="Blu-ray">Blu-ray</a> format, in contrast, can potentially reveal the quality advantage of 70 mm productions. Although <a href="/wiki/Telecine" title="Telecine">telecine</a> machines for 70 mm scanning are uncommon, high-resolution transfers from high-quality full-gauge elements can reveal impressive technical quality.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses_of_70.C2.A0mm">Uses of 70 mm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=70_mm_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Uses of 70 mm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ultra_Panavision">Ultra Panavision</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=70_mm_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ultra Panavision">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anamorphic" title="Anamorphic">anamorphic</a> squeeze combined with 65 mm film allowed for extremely wide aspect ratios to be used while still preserving quality. This was used in the 1957 film <i><a href="/wiki/Raintree_County_(film)" title="Raintree County (film)">Raintree County</a></i> and to incredible success in the 1959 film <i><a href="/wiki/Ben-Hur_(1959_film)" title="Ben-Hur (1959 film)">Ben-Hur</a></i> and the 2015 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hateful_Eight" title="The Hateful Eight">The Hateful Eight</a></i>, which was filmed with the <a href="/wiki/Ultra_Panavision_70" title="Ultra Panavision 70">MGM Camera 65</a> process at an aspect ratio of 2.76:1. It required the use of a 1.25x anamorphic lens to horizontally compress the image, and a corresponding lens on the projector to uncompress it.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Special_effects">Special effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=70_mm_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Special effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Limited use of 65 mm film was revived in the late 1970s for some of the visual effects sequences in films like <i><a href="/wiki/Close_Encounters_of_the_Third_Kind" title="Close Encounters of the Third Kind">Close Encounters of the Third Kind</a></i>, mainly because the larger negative did a better job than 35 mm negative of minimizing visible film grain during optical <a href="/wiki/Compositing" title="Compositing">compositing</a>. Since the 1990s, a handful of films (such as <i><a href="/wiki/Spider-Man_2" title="Spider-Man 2">Spider-Man 2</a></i>) have used it for this purpose, but the usage of <a href="/wiki/Digital_intermediate" title="Digital intermediate">digital intermediate</a> for compositing has largely negated these issues. Digital intermediate offers other benefits such as lower cost and a greater range of available lenses and accessories to ensure a consistent look to the footage.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IMAX">IMAX</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=70_mm_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: IMAX">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/IMAX" title="IMAX">IMAX</a></div>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horizontal_pulldown" title="Horizontal pulldown">horizontal</a> variant of 70 mm, with an even bigger picture area, is used for the high-performance IMAX format which uses a frame that is 15 perforations wide on 70 mm film. The Dynavision and Astrovision systems each use slightly less film per frame and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertical_pulldown" title="Vertical pulldown">vertical pulldown</a> to save print costs while being able to project onto an IMAX screen. Both were rare, with Astrovision largely used in Japanese <a href="/wiki/Planetarium" title="Planetarium">planetariums</a>. In the 2014 movie <i><a href="/wiki/Interstellar_(film)" title="Interstellar (film)">Interstellar</a></i>, a significant amount was shot in the IMAX format. Other scenes were shot in either 35 mm or in the standard 'vertical' 5-perf 65 mm format. IMAX introduced a digital projection system in the late 2000s and most IMAX venues have migrated to digital setup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="70.C2.A0mm_3D_early_use">70 mm 3D early use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=70_mm_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: 70 mm 3D early use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first commercial introduction of 70 mm single projector 3D was the 1967 release of <i>Con la muerte a la espalda</i>, a Spanish/French/Italian co-production which used a process called Hi-Fi Stereo 70, itself based on a simplified, earlier developed soviet process called Stereo-70. This process captured two anamorphic images, one for each eye, side by side on 65 mm film. A special lens on a 70 mm projector added polarization and merged the two images on the screen. The 1971 re-release of <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/House_of_Wax_(1953_film)" title="House of Wax (1953 film)">House of Wax</a></i> used the side-by-side StereoVision format and was distributed in both anamorphically squeezed 35 mm and deluxe non-anamorphic 70 mm form. The system was developed by Allan Silliphant and <a href="/wiki/Chris_Condon" title="Chris Condon">Chris Condon</a> of StereoVision International Inc., which handled all technical and marketing aspects on a five-year special-royalty basis with Warner Bros. The big screen 3D image was both bright and clear, with all the former sync and brightness problems of traditional dual 35 mm 3D eliminated. Still, it took many years more before IMAX began to test the water for big-screen 3D, and sold the concept to Hollywood executives.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IMAX_3D">IMAX 3D</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=70_mm_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: IMAX 3D">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hollywood has released films shot on 35 mm as IMAX blow-up versions. Many 3D films were shown in the 70 mm IMAX format. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Polar_Express_(film)" title="The Polar Express (film)">The Polar Express</a></i> in IMAX 3D 70 mm earned 14 times as much, per screen, as the simultaneous 2D 35 mm release of that film in the fall of 2004.</p>
<p>In 2011 IMAX introduced a 3D Digital camera based on two <a href="/wiki/Digital_cinematography#Vision_Research_Phantom" title="Digital cinematography">Phantom 65</a> cores. The camera has been used for documentaries as well as Hollywood films, the first being the 2014 release of <i><a href="/wiki/Transformers:_Age_of_Extinction" title="Transformers: Age of Extinction">Transformers: Age of Extinction</a></i>.</p>

<ul>
<li><i>projection aperture</i>: 48.59 by 22.05 mm (1.913 by 0.868 in)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-awsm_dimensions_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-awsm_dimensions-11">[11]</a></sup></li>

These new lenses were far superior to the prism anamorphics—they were lighter, transmitted more light and suffered from less spherical and chromatic aberration.</li>
<li>1.25x squeeze factor, projected aspect ratio 2.76:1</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IMAX_Dome_.2F_OMNIMAX"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IMAX_Dome" title="IMAX Dome">IMAX Dome</a> / OMNIMAX</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=70_mm_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: IMAX Dome / OMNIMAX">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Omnivision_Cinema_180">Omnivision Cinema 180</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=70_mm_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Omnivision Cinema 180">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>same as standard 65/70 except:</i></p>
<ul>
<li>photographed and projected with special fisheye lenses matched to large 180 degree dome screen</li>
<li>Theatres upgraded from 70 mm 6track analog sound to DTS digital sound in 1995.</li>
</ul>
<p>Omnivision started in <a href="/wiki/Sarasota,_Florida" title="Sarasota, Florida">Sarasota, Florida</a>. Theatres were designed to compete with Omnimax but with much lower startup and operating costs. Most theatres were built in fabric domed structures designed by <a href="/wiki/Siemens" title="Siemens">Siemens</a> Corporation. The last known OmniVision theatres to exist in USA are The Alaska Experience Theatre in <a href="/wiki/Anchorage,_Alaska" title="Anchorage, Alaska">Anchorage, Alaska</a>, built in 1981 (closed in 2007, reopened in 2008), and the Hawaii Experience Theatre in <a href="/wiki/Lahaina,_Hawaii" title="Lahaina, Hawaii">Lahaina, Hawaii</a> (closed in 2004). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainbow%27s_End_(Theme_Park)" title="Rainbow's End (Theme Park)">Rainbow's End (Theme Park)</a> in NZ had the only remaining permanent Cinema 180 attraction until May 2015 when it was demolished.</p>
<p>One of the few producers of 70 mm films for Cinema 180 was the German company Cinevision (today AKPservices GmbH, Paderborn).</p>
