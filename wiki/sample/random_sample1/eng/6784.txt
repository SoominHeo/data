<p><b>Brian Kent Kobilka</b> (born May 30, 1955)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is an American physiologist and a recipient of the 2012 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Lefkowitz" title="Robert Lefkowitz">Robert Lefkowitz</a> for discoveries that reveal the inner workings of an important family <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G_protein-coupled_receptors" title="G protein-coupled receptors">G protein-coupled receptors</a>. He is currently a professor in the department of Molecular and Cellular Physiology at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University_School_of_Medicine" title="Stanford University School of Medicine">Stanford University School of Medicine</a>. He is also the co-founder of ConfometRx, a <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a> company focusing on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G_protein-coupled_receptors" title="G protein-coupled receptors">G protein-coupled receptors</a>. He was named a member of the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> in 2011.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_Kobilka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kobilka, a Roman Catholic, attended St. Mary's Grade School in <a href="/wiki/Little_Falls,_Minnesota" title="Little Falls, Minnesota">Little Falls, Minnesota</a>, a part of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Saint_Cloud" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Saint Cloud">Roman Catholic Diocese of Saint Cloud</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He then Graduated from Little Falls High School. He received a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bachelor%E2%80%99s_Degree" title="Bachelorâs Degree">Bachelorâs Degree</a> in <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">Biology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">Chemistry</a> from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_Duluth" title="University of Minnesota Duluth">University of Minnesota Duluth</a>, and earned his <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Medicine" title="Doctor of Medicine">M.D.</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cum_laude" title="Cum laude">cum laude</a></i>, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yale_University_School_of_Medicine" title="Yale University School of Medicine">Yale University School of Medicine</a>. Following the completion of his residency in <a href="/wiki/Internal_medicine" title="Internal medicine">internal medicine</a> at <a href="/wiki/Barnes-Jewish_Hospital" title="Barnes-Jewish Hospital">Barnes-Jewish Hospital</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis, Missouri</a>, Kobilka worked in research as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postdoctoral_fellow" title="Postdoctoral fellow">postdoctoral fellow</a> under <a href="/wiki/Robert_Lefkowitz" title="Robert Lefkowitz">Robert Lefkowitz</a> at <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">Duke University</a>, where he started work on cloning the <a href="/wiki/Beta-2_adrenergic_receptor" title="Beta-2 adrenergic receptor">Î²<sub>2</sub>-adrenergic receptor</a>. Kobilka moved to Stanford in 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He was a <a href="/wiki/Howard_Hughes_Medical_Institute" title="Howard Hughes Medical Institute">Howard Hughes Medical Institute</a> (HHMI) investigator from 1987-2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_Kobilka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kobilka is best known for his research on the structure and activity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G_protein-coupled_receptors" title="G protein-coupled receptors">G protein-coupled receptors</a> (GPCRs); in particular, work from Kobilka's laboratory determined the molecular structure of the <a href="/wiki/Beta-2_adrenergic_receptor" title="Beta-2 adrenergic receptor">Î²<sub>2</sub>-adrenergic receptor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forbes_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rasmussen_2007_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rasmussen_2007-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cherezov_2007_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cherezov_2007-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenbaum_2007_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenbaum_2007-8">[8]</a></sup> This work has been highly cited by other scientists because GPCRs are important targets for pharmaceutical therapeutics, but notoriously difficult to work with in <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">X-ray crystallography</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interview_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-interview-9">[9]</a></sup> Before, <a href="/wiki/Rhodopsin" title="Rhodopsin">rhodopsin</a> was the only G-protein coupled receptor where the structure had been determined at high resolution. The Î²<sub>2</sub>-adrenergic receptor structure was soon followed by the determination of the molecular structure of several other G-protein coupled receptors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kobilka is the 1994 recipient of the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_for_Pharmacology_and_Experimental_Therapeutics" title="American Society for Pharmacology and Experimental Therapeutics">American Society for Pharmacology and Experimental Therapeutics</a> John J. Abel Award in Pharmacology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> His GPCR structure work was named "runner-up" for the 2007 "Breakthrough of the Year" award from <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The work was, in part, supported by Kobilka's 2004 Javits Neuroscience Investigator Award<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> from the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Neurological_Disorders_and_Stroke" title="National Institute of Neurological Disorders and Stroke">National Institute of Neurological Disorders and Stroke</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> He received the 2012 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Lefkowitz" title="Robert Lefkowitz">Robert Lefkowitz</a> for his work on G protein-coupled receptors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnews_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-dnews-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_Kobilka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kobilka is from <a href="/wiki/Little_Falls,_Minnesota" title="Little Falls, Minnesota">Little Falls</a> in <a href="/wiki/Central_Minnesota" title="Central Minnesota">central Minnesota</a>. Both his grandfather Felix J. Kobilka (1893-1991) and his father Franklyn A. Kobilka (1921-2004) were bakers and natives of Little Falls, Minnesota.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franklyn_A._Kobilka.2C_83_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Franklyn_A._Kobilka.2C_83-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Kobilka's grandmother, Isabelle Susan Kobilka (nÃ©e Medved, 1891-1980), belonged to the Medved and Kiewel families of <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussian</a> immigrants, who from 1888 owned the historical Kiewel brewery in Little Falls. His mother is Betty L. Kobilka (nÃ©e Faust, b. 1930).</p>
<p>Kobilka met his wife Tong Sun Thian, a Malaysian-Chinese woman,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> at the University of Minnesota Duluth. They have two children, Jason and Megan Kobilka.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franklyn_A._Kobilka.2C_83_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Franklyn_A._Kobilka.2C_83-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
