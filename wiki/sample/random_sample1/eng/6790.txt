<p><b>Brazzaville</b> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capital_(political)" title="Capital (political)">capital</a> and largest city of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Republic of the Congo">Republic of the Congo</a> and is located on the <a href="/wiki/Congo_River" title="Congo River">Congo River</a>. As of the 2007 census, it had a population of 1.37 million. The projection of the CNSEE (national statistics centre) shows an increase to 1.7 million by 2015,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-population_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-population-1">[1]</a></sup> but the projection was made before 2007 and based on a lower estimate of the population (1.26 million) than recorded in the census. The United Nations Population Division estimate for 2014 is 1.83 million. The populous city of <a href="/wiki/Kinshasa" title="Kinshasa">Kinshasa</a> (more than 10 million inhabitants in 2014),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kinshasa_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kinshasa-2">[2]</a></sup> capital of the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a>, lies across the Congo River from Brazzaville. Together with Kinshasa, the combined <a href="/wiki/Conurbation" title="Conurbation">conurbation</a> of Kinshasa-Brazzaville has thus about 12 million inhabitants (although significant political and infrastructure challenges prevent the two cities from functioning with any meaningful connection). Over a third of the population of the Republic of Congo lives in the capital, and it is home to 40% of non-agricultural employment. It is also a financial and administrative capital.</p>

<p>In order to distinguish between the two African countries with "Congo" in their names, the Republic of the Congo is sometimes called Congo-Brazzaville, as opposed to Congo-Kinshasa (the Democratic Republic of the Congo, known from 1971 to 1997 as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Za%C3%AFre" title="Zaïre">Zaïre</a>, the capital of which is <a href="/wiki/Kinshasa" title="Kinshasa">Kinshasa</a>). Kinshasa lies on the southern bank of the Congo, across from Brazzaville. This is the only place in the world where two national <a href="/wiki/Capital_city" title="Capital city">capital cities</a> are situated on opposite banks of a river, within sight of each other. There have been proposals to connect the two capitals by a <a href="/wiki/Brazzaville%E2%80%93Kinshasa_Bridge" title="Brazzaville–Kinshasa Bridge">Brazzaville–Kinshasa Bridge</a>.</p>
<p>While Kinshasa lies to the south, Brazzaville lies to the north of the Congo River. The city is 506 kilometres (314 miles) inland from the Atlantic Ocean and approximately 474 kilometres (295 miles) south of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>. The city is a commune that is separated from the other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regions_of_the_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Regions of the Republic of the Congo">regions</a> of the republic; it is surrounded by the Pool Region. Around the city are large plains. The town is relatively flat, and situated at an altitude of 317 metres (1,040 feet).</p>

<p>The city was founded on 10 September 1880 on the site of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bateke" title="Bateke">Bateke</a> village named <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nkuna&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nkuna (page does not exist)">Nkuna</a> by an Italo-French explorer, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Savorgnan_de_Brazza" title="Pierre Savorgnan de Brazza">Pierre Savorgnan de Brazza</a>, after whom the city was named. The local leader, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Makoko_of_the_T%C3%A9k%C3%A9&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Makoko of the Téké (page does not exist)">Makoko of the Téké</a>, signed a treaty of protection with de Brazza which subjugated his lands to the <a href="/wiki/French_colonial_empire" title="French colonial empire">French Empire</a>. The city was built four years later in order to become a competitor with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%C3%A9opoldville" title="Léopoldville">Léopoldville</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Kinshasa" title="Kinshasa">Kinshasa</a>) which was built by the Belgians on the other side of the river. The site was occupied from October 1880 until May 1882 by a small squad of troops led by Senegalese Sergeant <a href="/wiki/Malamine_Camara" title="Malamine Camara">Malamine Camara</a>, who prevented the land from falling into Belgian hands.</p>
<p>French control over the area was made official by the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Conference" title="Berlin Conference">Berlin Conference</a> of 1884. The city became the capital first of the <a href="/wiki/French_Congo" title="French Congo">French Congo</a>, and then of <a href="/wiki/French_Equatorial_Africa" title="French Equatorial Africa">French Equatorial Africa</a>, a federation of states which encompassed <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">Gabon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Republic" title="Central African Republic">Central African Republic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>. In 1924, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congo-Oc%C3%A9an_railway" title="Congo-Océan railway">Congo-Océan railway</a> was brought into service which linked Brazzaville with the port of <a href="/wiki/Pointe-Noire" title="Pointe-Noire">Pointe-Noire</a>. Construction of the railway resulted in the death of over 17,000 Africans which led to a revolt in 1928.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>During World War II Brazzaville, and the rest of <a href="/wiki/French_Equatorial_Africa" title="French Equatorial Africa">French Equatorial Africa</a>, remained beyond the control of <a href="/wiki/Vichy_France" title="Vichy France">Vichy France</a>. In 1944, Brazzaville hosted a meeting of the <a href="/wiki/Free_France" title="Free France">Free French</a> forces and representatives of France's African colonies. The resulting <a href="/wiki/Brazzaville_Conference_of_1944" title="Brazzaville Conference of 1944">Brazzaville Declaration</a> was intended to redefine the relationship between France and its African colonies after the war.</p>
<p>Until the 1960s, the city was divided into European (the center of the city) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnic_groups_of_Africa" title="Ethnic groups of Africa">African</a> sections (<a href="/wiki/Poto-Poto" title="Poto-Poto">Poto-Poto</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacongo" title="Bacongo">Bacongo</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mak%C3%A9l%C3%A9k%C3%A9l%C3%A9&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Makélékélé (page does not exist)">Makélékélé</a>). In 1980 it became a "commune" separated from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pool_Region" title="Pool Region">Pool Region</a> and divided into nine "arrondissements": Makélékélé (1), Bacongo (2), Poto-Poto (3), Moungali (4), <a href="/wiki/Ouenz%C3%A9" title="Ouenzé">Ouenzé</a> (5), Talangaï (6), Mfilou (7), Madibou (8), and Djiri (9).</p>
<p>The city has frequently been a staging ground for wars, including conflicts between rebel and government forces and between forces of the Republic of Congo, the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a>. It was also the scene of bloody civil wars throughout the 1990s which resulted in thousands of civilian deaths and which forced hundreds of thousands of refugees to flee the city.</p>

<p>Industries present in Brazzaville include <a href="/wiki/Machining" title="Machining">machine shops</a>, <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tanning_(leather)" title="Tanning (leather)">tanning</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a>. As a key port on the <a href="/wiki/Congo_River" title="Congo River">Congo River</a>, the city takes deliveries of <a href="/wiki/Raw_material" title="Raw material">raw materials</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Natural_rubber" title="Natural rubber">rubber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural products</a>. From here they are generally sent onward to <a href="/wiki/Pointe-Noire" title="Pointe-Noire">Pointe-Noire</a> for export.</p>
<p>As the capital city of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Congo" title="Republic of Congo">Republic of Congo</a>, many companies, government organizations and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NGO" title="NGO">NGOs</a> have regional offices in the city. The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> has its regional office for Africa located in Brazzaville.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Buildings_and_institutions">Buildings and institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brazzaville&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Buildings and institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Notable buildings in the city include <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=St_Anne%27s_Basilica_(Brazzaville)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="St Anne's Basilica (Brazzaville) (page does not exist)">St Anne's Basilica</a>, built in 1949 by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Erell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Roger Erell (page does not exist)">Roger Erell</a>, and known for its green <a href="/wiki/Tile" title="Tile">tiled</a> roof; Erell also designed a house in the city for <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle" title="Charles de Gaulle">Charles de Gaulle</a>. Other buildings include the <a href="/wiki/Nabemba_Tower" title="Nabemba Tower">Nabemba Tower</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Congressional_Palace_(Brazzaville)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Congressional Palace (Brazzaville) (page does not exist)">Congressional Palace</a>. Other features include the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Marien_Ngouabi_Mausoleum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Marien Ngouabi Mausoleum (page does not exist)">Marien Ngouabi Mausoleum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazzaville_Zoo" title="Brazzaville Zoo">Brazzaville Zoo</a> and the Poto-Poto School of Painting. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Embassy_for_the_Republic_of_the_Congo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="United States Embassy for the Republic of the Congo (page does not exist)">United States Embassy for the Republic of the Congo</a> is also located in Brazzaville, as is the Dutch Honorary Consulate.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> Regional Office for Africa is based in Brazzaville on a vast campus at the Cité du Djoué.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brazzaville&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Brazzaville, much like neighboring <a href="/wiki/Kinshasa" title="Kinshasa">Kinshasa</a>, features a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_wet_and_dry_climate" title="Tropical wet and dry climate">tropical wet and dry climate</a>. Its wet season, which runs from October–May, is longer than its dry season, which covers the remaining months. Brazzaville's driest months, July and August, see on average no significant precipitation. Since Brazzaville is south of the equator, its dry season begins at around its "winter" solstice, which is the month of June. The city experiences relatively consistent temperatures throughout the course of the year.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brazzaville&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Taxibrazza.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="660" data-file-width="950" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Taxibrazza.png/200px-Taxibrazza.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Taxibrazza.png/300px-Taxibrazza.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Taxibrazza.png/400px-Taxibrazza.png 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Taxibrazza.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Taxis in Brazzaville</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The city is home to <a href="/wiki/Maya-Maya_Airport" title="Maya-Maya Airport">Maya-Maya Airport</a> which has regular flights to <a href="/wiki/Pointe-Noire" title="Pointe-Noire">Pointe-Noire</a> as well as international destinations in Africa, Europe and the Middle East.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congo-Ocean_Railway" title="Congo-Ocean Railway">Congo-Ocean Railway</a> has a station in the city and in 2014 was operating the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Gazelle_train" title="La Gazelle train"><i>La Gazelle</i></a> train service every other day to <a href="/wiki/Pointe-Noire" title="Pointe-Noire">Pointe-Noire</a> and intermediate destinations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city is also an important <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_port" title="River port">river port</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Ferry" title="Ferry">ferries</a> sailing to <a href="/wiki/Kinshasa" title="Kinshasa">Kinshasa</a> and to <a href="/wiki/Bangui" title="Bangui">Bangui</a> via <a href="/wiki/Impfondo" title="Impfondo">Impfondo</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Livingstone_Falls" title="Livingstone Falls">Livingstone Falls</a> lie on the outskirts of the city, where the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Djou%C3%A9_River&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Djoué River (page does not exist)">Djoué River</a> meets the Congo, rendering river transport to the coast impossible, qualifying the railway as a <a href="/wiki/Portage_railway" title="Portage railway">portage railway</a>.</p>
<p>Although there is no organised public transport system privately owned buses are available in the capital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Taxis are available on every street and are easily recognized, being painted with a green body and white top, and the fare for a short trip is CF700. About twenty percent of the vehicles in Brazzaville are taxis. There are also collective taxis that drive certain routes and charge CF150.</p>
