<p><b>De Brazza's monkey</b> (<i>Cercopithecus neglectus</i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_monkey" title="Old World monkey">Old World monkey</a> endemic to the wetlands of central Africa. It is one of the most widespread African primates that live in forests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_Brazza%27s_monkey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Locally known as swamp monkeys,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eng1998_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eng1998-3">[3]</a></sup> these primates are named after the Italian-French explorer <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Savorgnan_de_Brazza" title="Pierre Savorgnan de Brazza">Pierre Savorgnan de Brazza</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_Brazza%27s_monkey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Guenon" title="Guenon">guenon</a> has grey <a href="/wiki/Agouti_(coloration)" title="Agouti (coloration)">agouti</a> fur with a reddish-brown back, black limbs and tail and a white rump. A white stripe runs down its thigh, and an orange crescent-shaped marking appears on its forehead. Its white eyelids match its muzzle and beard. Due to this distinctive appearance, the monkey is sometimes referred to as the "Ayatollah Monkey" after the similarly-bearded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Ayatollah" title="Grand Ayatollah">Grand Ayatollah</a> <a href="/wiki/Ruhollah_Khomeini" title="Ruhollah Khomeini">Ruhollah Khomeini</a>. Both sexes have cheek pouches in which to carry food while they forage, and males have a blue scrotum.</p>
<p>De Brazza's monkey is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexually_dimorphic" title="Sexually dimorphic">sexually dimorphic</a> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>; males weigh around 7 kilograms, while females weigh around 4 kilograms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stein2002_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stein2002-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_Brazza%27s_monkey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>De Brazza's monkey ranges across the swamps, bamboo and dry mountain forests of <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Republic" title="Central African Republic">Central African Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Republic of the Congo">Congo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="The Democratic Republic of the Congo">The Democratic Republic of the Congo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea">Equatorial Guinea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">Gabon</a>, Saiwa Swamp and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cherangani_Hills" title="Cherangani Hills">Cherangani Hills</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Sudan" title="South Sudan">South Sudan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>. They are mainly arboreal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_Brazza%27s_monkey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brazzameerkatze_1790-2-2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2394" data-file-width="3874" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Brazzameerkatze_1790-2-2.jpg/220px-Brazzameerkatze_1790-2-2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Brazzameerkatze_1790-2-2.jpg/330px-Brazzameerkatze_1790-2-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Brazzameerkatze_1790-2-2.jpg/440px-Brazzameerkatze_1790-2-2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brazzameerkatze_1790-2-2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Mother with young</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>lifespan of De Brazza's monkey in the wild is about 22 years, but it is thought to correspond similarly to other members of <i>Cercopithecus</i> which live up to 30 years in captivity. It is a shy, territorial monkey that lives in small social groups. De Brazza Monkeys live in groups varying from 5 to 30. At the head of each social group is the strongest male, whose job is to protect his fellow group members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stein2002_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stein2002-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>De Brazza's monkey communicates with booming sounds, shaking tree branches, and a variety of facial expressions and movements (e.g. shaking its head when stressed out, or nodding with approval).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stein2002_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stein2002-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Predators of the De Brazza's monkey include the <a href="/wiki/Leopard" title="Leopard">leopard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Common_chimpanzee" title="Common chimpanzee">common chimpanzees</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stein2002_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Stein2002-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
