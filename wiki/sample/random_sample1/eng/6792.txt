<p>The <b>real</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese_language" title="Brazilian Portuguese language">Brazilian Portuguese</a>: <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Portuguese" title="Help:IPA for Portuguese">[ʁeˈaw]</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Plural" title="Plural">pl.</a> <b>reais</b>) is the present-day <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a> of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>. Its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Currency_sign" title="Currency sign">sign</a> is <b>R$</b> and its <a href="/wiki/ISO_4217" title="ISO 4217">ISO code</a> is <b>BRL</b>. It is subdivided into 100 <i><b>centavos</b></i> ("Cents").</p>
<p>The modern real was introduced in 1994, when it replaced the old currency, the <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_cruzeiro_real" title="Brazilian cruzeiro real">cruzeiro real</a>, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Plano_Real" title="Plano Real">Plano Real</a>, a substantial <a href="/wiki/Monetary_reform" title="Monetary reform">monetary reform</a> package that aimed to put an end to three decades of rampant <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">inflation</a>. At the time it was meant to have approximately fixed 1:1 <a href="/wiki/Exchange_rate" title="Exchange rate">exchange rate</a> with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">United States dollar</a>. It suffered a sudden <a href="/wiki/Devaluation" title="Devaluation">devaluation</a> to a rate of about 2:1 in 1999, reached almost 4:1 in 2002 and then partially recovered until the domestic economic crisis of 2015. The exchange rate as of September 2015 was BRL 4.05 to <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a> 1.00. The currency has since been in a gradual recovery period, reaching 3.1 BRL per USD by October 2016.</p>
<p>The dollar-like sign (<a href="/wiki/Cifr%C3%A3o" title="Cifrão">cifrão</a>) is the currency's symbol (both historic and modern), and in all the other past Brazilian currencies, is officially written with two vertical strokes (<a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg"><img alt="Cifrão symbol.svg" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="15" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg/15px-Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg/23px-Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg/30px-Cifr%C3%A3o_symbol.svg.png 2x" width="15"/></a>) rather than one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> However <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> considers the difference to be only a matter of <a href="/wiki/Font" title="Font">font</a> design, and does not have a separate code for the two-stroked version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>The modern real (plural <i>reais</i>) was introduced on 1 July 1994, during the presidency of <a href="/wiki/Itamar_Franco" title="Itamar Franco">Itamar Franco</a>, when <a href="/wiki/Rubens_Ricupero" title="Rubens Ricupero">Rubens Ricupero</a> was the Minister of Finance, as part of a broader plan to stabilize the Brazilian <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economy</a>, known as the <a href="/wiki/Plano_Real" title="Plano Real">Plano Real</a>. The new currency replaced the short-lived <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_cruzeiro_real" title="Brazilian cruzeiro real">cruzeiro real</a> (CR$). The reform included the <a href="/wiki/Legal_tender#demonetisation" title="Legal tender">demonetisation</a> of the cruzeiro real and required a massive banknote replacement.</p>
<p>At its introduction, the real was defined to be equal to 1 <i><a href="/wiki/Unidade_real_de_valor" title="Unidade real de valor">unidade real de valor</a></i> (URV, "real value unit") a non-circulating currency unit. At the same time the URV was defined to be worth 2750 cruzeiros reais, which was the average exchange rate of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._dollar" title="U.S. dollar">U.S. dollar</a> to the cruzeiro real on that day. As a consequence, the real was worth exactly one U.S. dollar as it was introduced. Combined with all previous currency changes in the country's history, this reform made the new real equal to 2.75 × 10<sup>18</sup> (2.75 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quintillion" title="Quintillion">quintillions</a>) of Brazil's original "réis".</p>
<p>Soon after its introduction, the real unexpectedly gained value against the U.S. dollar, due to large capital inflows in late 1994 and 1995. During that period it attained its maximum dollar value ever, about US$1.20. Between 1996 and 1998 the exchange rate was tightly controlled by the Central Bank, so that the real depreciated slowly and smoothly in relation to the dollar, dropping from near 1:1 to about 1.2:1 by the end of 1998. In January 1999 the deterioration of the international markets, disrupted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_default" title="Russian default">Russian default</a>, forced the Central Bank, under its new president <a href="/wiki/Arminio_Fraga" title="Arminio Fraga">Arminio Fraga</a>, to float the exchange rate. This decision produced a major devaluation, to a rate of almost R$2 : US$1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the following years, the currency's value against the dollar followed an erratic but mostly downwards path from 1999 until late 2002, when the prospect of the election of leftist candidate <a href="/wiki/Luiz_In%C3%A1cio_Lula_da_Silva" title="Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva">Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva</a>, considered a radical populist by sectors of the financial markets, prompted another currency crisis and a spike in inflation. Many Brazilians feared another default on government debts or a resumption of heterodox economic policies, and rushed to exchange their reais into tangible assets or foreign currencies. In October 2002 the exchange rate reached its historic low of almost R$4 per US$1.</p>
<p>The crisis subsided once Lula took office, after he, his finance minister <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Palocci" title="Antonio Palocci">Antonio Palocci</a>, and Arminio Fraga reaffirmed their intention to continue the orthodox macroeconomic policies of his predecessor (including inflation-targeting, primary fiscal surplus and <a href="/wiki/Floating_exchange_rate" title="Floating exchange rate">floating exchange rate</a>, as well as continued payments of the public debt). The value of the real in dollars continued to fluctuate but generally upwards, so that by 2005 the exchange was a little over R$2 : US$1. In May 2007, for the first time since 1999, the real became worth more than US$0.50 — even though the Central Bank, concerned about its effect on the Brazilian economy, had tried to keep it below that symbolic threshold.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Users">Users</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brazilian_real&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Users">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Brazil, and sometimes used in bordering countries, especially Brazil/Uruguay and Brazil/Paraguay borders in which every year millions of Brazilians go shopping at the duty-free stores etc. It is also widely accepted in Argentina, not only in shops and duty-free stores in border cities like <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Iguaz%C3%BA" title="Puerto Iguazú">Puerto Iguazú</a> but also in shops and restaurants in the capital city, <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coins">Coins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brazilian_real&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Coins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_series_.281994.E2.80.931997.29">First series (1994–1997)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brazilian_real&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: First series (1994–1997)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Along with the first series of currency, coins were introduced in denominations of 1, 5, 10 and 50 centavos and 1 real; the 25 centavos piece soon followed. All were struck in <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a>. The original 1-real coins dated 1994-1997 have been <a href="/wiki/Legal_tender#Withdrawal_from_circulation" title="Legal tender">withdrawn from circulation</a> since 31 December 1997; all other coins remain <a href="/wiki/Legal_tender" title="Legal tender">legal tender</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_series_.281998.E2.80.93present.29">Second series (1998–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brazilian_real&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Second series (1998–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1998, a second series of coins was introduced. It featured copper-plated steel coins of 1 and 5 centavos, <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brass</a>-plated steel coins of 10 and 25 centavos, a <a href="/wiki/Cupronickel" title="Cupronickel">cupronickel</a> 50 centavos coin, and a bi-coloured brass and cupronickel coin of 1 real. However, from 2002 onwards, steel was used for the 50 centavos coin and the central part of the 1 real coin.</p>
<p>In November 2005, the Central Bank <a href="/wiki/Withdrawal_of_low-denomination_coins" title="Withdrawal of low-denomination coins">discontinued the production</a> of 1 centavo coins, but the existing ones continue to be legal tender. Retailers now generally round their prices to the next 5 or 10 centavos.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polymer_10_real_note">Polymer 10 real note</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brazilian_real&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Polymer 10 real note">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brazil-2000-Bill-10-Obverse.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="138" data-file-width="300" height="92" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Brazil-2000-Bill-10-Obverse.jpg/200px-Brazil-2000-Bill-10-Obverse.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5b/Brazil-2000-Bill-10-Obverse.jpg 1.5x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brazil-2000-Bill-10-Obverse.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The polymer banknote, with Cabral's images.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In April 2000, in commemoration of the 500th anniversary of the Portuguese arrival on Brazilian shores, the Brazilian Central Bank released a <a href="/wiki/Polymer_banknote" title="Polymer banknote">polymer</a> 10 real banknote that circulates along with the other banknotes above. The <a href="/wiki/Casa_da_Moeda_do_Brasil" title="Casa da Moeda do Brasil">Brazilian Mint</a> printed 250 million of these notes, which at the time accounted for about half of the 10 real banknotes in circulation.</p>
<p>This note contains a more complex design, as follows:</p>
<p>Obverse:</p>
<ul>
<li>Image of <a href="/wiki/Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral" title="Pedro Álvares Cabral">Pedro Álvares Cabral</a>, the Portuguese sea captain;</li>
<li>A representation of the map "Terra Brasilis", one of the earliest drawings of the land;</li>
<li>A passage from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pero_Vaz_de_Caminha" title="Pero Vaz de Caminha">Pero Vaz de Caminha</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carta_de_Pero_Vaz_de_Caminha" title="Carta de Pero Vaz de Caminha">letter</a> to King <a href="/wiki/Manuel_I_of_Portugal" title="Manuel I of Portugal">Manuel I of Portugal</a>, the first known description of Brazil;</li>
<li>A 16th-century Portuguese <a href="/wiki/Compass_rose" title="Compass rose">Rose of Winds</a>;</li>
<li>To the right of the map, five ships from Cabral's expedition appear;</li>
<li>In the background, decorative elements from Portuguese tiles can be seen;</li>
<li>The white area around the red dot is actually transparent (the red dot is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Translucent" title="Translucent">translucent</a>);</li>
<li>Finally, also in the background, the Cross from the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Christ_(Brazil)" title="Order of Christ (Brazil)">Order of Christ</a>, which was present in all Portuguese ships of the time, appears.</li>
</ul>
<p>Reverse:</p>
<p>A stylized map of Brazil with photographs depicting the ethnic variety of the Brazilian people (white, black, and amerindian).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_series">New series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brazilian_real&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: New series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 3 February 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Central_Bank_of_Brazil" title="Central Bank of Brazil">Central Bank of Brazil</a> announced a new series of the real banknotes which would begin to be released in April 2010. The new design added security enhancements in an attempt to reduce <a href="/wiki/Counterfeit_money" title="Counterfeit money">counterfeiting</a>. The notes have different sizes according to their values to help <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blindness" title="Blindness">vision-impaired</a> people. The changes were made reflecting the growth of the <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Brazil" title="Economy of Brazil">Brazilian economy</a> and the need for a stronger and safer currency. The new banknotes began to enter circulation in December 2010, coexisting with the older ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id=".22God_Be_Praised.22_controversy">"God Be Praised" controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brazilian_real&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title='Edit section: "God Be Praised" controversy'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 1986, Brazilian bank notes contain the words “<a href="/wiki/Deus" title="Deus">Deus</a> Seja Louvado” (God Be Praised). In 2012, a federal prosecutor from São Paulo sought a court order to force the central bank to replace the nation's entire supply of paper currency with bills that do not display these words, arguing that Brazil is a secular state and that this phrase violates the rights of non-Christians and nonbelievers. The Bank responded by stating that the preamble to the Brazilian constitution explicitly states that the democracy was formed “under the protection of God”, and that the state, “not being atheist, anticlerical or antireligious, can legitimately make a reference to the existence of a higher being, a divinity, as long as, in doing so, it does not make an allusion to a specific religious doctrine.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
