<p>The <b>tapeti</b> (<i><b>Sylvilagus brasiliensis</b></i>), also known as the <b>Brazilian cottontail</b> or <b>forest cottontail</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Cottontail_rabbit" title="Cottontail rabbit">cottontail rabbit</a> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>. Its range extends from southern <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> to northern <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>. It is small to medium-sized with a small, dark tail, short hind feet, and short ears. It is classified as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>" by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tapeti&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The species was first <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> scientifically by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of Systema Naturae</a>, published in 1753.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Linnaeus_1753_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Linnaeus_1753-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_locality_(biology)" title="Type locality (biology)">type locality</a> was in <a href="/wiki/Pernambuco" title="Pernambuco">Pernambuco</a>, Brazil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSW3_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSW3-4">[4]</a></sup> In addition to its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vernacular_name" title="Vernacular name">vernacular name</a> "tapeti", it commonly known as the "forest cottontail"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schubert_2003_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schubert_2003-5">[5]</a></sup> or the "Brazilian cottontail".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisenberg_2000_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eisenberg_2000-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tapeti&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tapeti is a small- to medium-sized rabbit. It has a head-body length of 320 mm (13 in), a tail that is 21 mm (0.83 in), hind feet measuring 71 mm (2.8 in), ears that are 54 mm (2.1 in) (measured from notch to tip), and it weighs an average of 934 grams (32.9 oz). The color of its back is brown with a speckled appearance (resulting from the black hairs tips), and it has a <a href="/wiki/Rufous" title="Rufous">rufous</a> spot on its neck. Its belly and tail underside are also rufous. It has six <a href="/wiki/Mammary_gland" title="Mammary gland">mammae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisenberg_2000_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eisenberg_2000-6">[6]</a></sup> Two different <a href="/wiki/Karyotype" title="Karyotype">karyotypes</a> have been reported for this species: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diploid" title="Diploid">2n</a>=36, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_number" title="Fundamental number">FN</a>=68; and 2n=40, FN=76.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSW3_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-MSW3-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is a solitary, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a> animal, usually seen after nightfall or before dawn, feeding on grass and browse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emmons_1997_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emmons_1997-7">[7]</a></sup> It has also been recorded eating <i><a href="/wiki/Harrya_chromapes" title="Harrya chromapes">Harrya chromapes</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Bolete" title="Bolete">bolete</a> mushroom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wainwright_2007_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wainwright_2007-8">[8]</a></sup> It is found in forested habitats, close to swamps and along river edges, and in disturbed areas, such as gardens and plantations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emmons_1997_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Emmons_1997-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat.2C_distribution.2C_and_ecology">Habitat, distribution, and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tapeti&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habitat, distribution, and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sylvilagus_brasiliensis1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Sylvilagus_brasiliensis1.jpg/220px-Sylvilagus_brasiliensis1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Sylvilagus_brasiliensis1.jpg/330px-Sylvilagus_brasiliensis1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Sylvilagus_brasiliensis1.jpg/440px-Sylvilagus_brasiliensis1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sylvilagus_brasiliensis1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
In Brazil</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The tapeti occurs in tropical rain forests, deciduous forests, and second growth forests in Mexico and Central America, as well as pastures surrounding forest habitat. Its range extends from southern <a href="/wiki/Tamaulipas" title="Tamaulipas">Tamaulipas</a> in Mexico, south along the eastern coast of Mexico, through <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>, possibly <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>, eastern <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>, eastern <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a>. It occurs through the northern half of South America (except at high altitudes), including <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, and much of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup> The southern tip of its known distribution occurs in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tucuman_province" title="Tucuman province">Tucuman province</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisenberg_2000_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Eisenberg_2000-6">[6]</a></sup> It occurs at elevations from sea level to 4,800 m (15,700 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup> It is the only <a href="/wiki/Leporidae" title="Leporidae">leporid</a> species found in most of its range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emmons_1997_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Emmons_1997-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rabbits build nests built of dry grasses above the ground to rear their young. They have a central chamber and three or four smaller chambers at the end of a corridor. The <a href="/wiki/Gestation" title="Gestation">gestation</a> period varies with the geographical location. Rabbits in <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a>, Mexico gestate for about 28 days, and have three to eight offspring, while rabbits in the <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A1ramo" title="Páramo">Páramos</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> gestate for 44 days, and have an average litter size of 1.2. Both of these populations breed year-round.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapman_1990_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chapman_1990-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Like its California relative, the <a href="/wiki/Brush_rabbit" title="Brush rabbit">brush rabbit</a> (<i>Sylvilagus bachmani</i>), the tapeti is a <a href="/wiki/Natural_reservoir" title="Natural reservoir">natural reservoir</a> for the <i><a href="/wiki/Myxoma_virus" title="Myxoma virus">myxoma</a></i> virus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williams_2008_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williams_2008-10">[10]</a></sup> This relationships was discovered by Brazilian physician <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Henrique_de_Beaurepaire_Rohan_Arag%C3%A3o&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Henrique de Beaurepaire Rohan Aragão (page does not exist)">Henrique de Beaurepaire Rohan Aragão</a> in the 1940s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williamson_1996_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williamson_1996-11">[11]</a></sup> The virus causes a benign <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cutaneous" title="Cutaneous">cutaneous</a> <a href="/wiki/Fibroma" title="Fibroma">fibroma</a> in its hosts, but it causes the lethal disease <a href="/wiki/Myxomatosis" title="Myxomatosis">myxomatosis</a>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_rabbits" title="European rabbits">European rabbits</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kerr_2012_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kerr_2012-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
