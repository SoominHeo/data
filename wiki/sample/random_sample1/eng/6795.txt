<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Bracteate</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>bracteate</b> (from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>bractea</i>, a thin piece of metal) is a flat, thin, single-sided gold <a href="/wiki/Medal" title="Medal">medal</a> worn as jewelry that was produced in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern Europe</a> predominantly during the <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">Migration Period</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_Iron_Age" title="Germanic Iron Age">Germanic Iron Age</a> (including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vendel_era" title="Vendel era">Vendel era</a> in Sweden). The term is also used for thin discs, especially in gold, to be sewn onto clothing in the ancient world, as found for example in the ancient Persian <a href="/wiki/Oxus_Treasure" title="Oxus Treasure">Oxus Treasure</a>, and also later <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> coins produced in <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">central Europe</a> during the early <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gold_bracteates_from_the_Migration_Period">Gold bracteates from the Migration Period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracteate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gold bracteates from the Migration Period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vadstena-brakteaten,_Nordisk_familjebok.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="871" data-file-width="782" height="245" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Vadstena-brakteaten%2C_Nordisk_familjebok.png/220px-Vadstena-brakteaten%2C_Nordisk_familjebok.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Vadstena-brakteaten%2C_Nordisk_familjebok.png/330px-Vadstena-brakteaten%2C_Nordisk_familjebok.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Vadstena-brakteaten%2C_Nordisk_familjebok.png/440px-Vadstena-brakteaten%2C_Nordisk_familjebok.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vadstena-brakteaten,_Nordisk_familjebok.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Vadstena_bracteate" title="Vadstena bracteate">Vadstena bracteate</a>, a typical C-bracteate.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Gold bracteates commonly denote a certain type of jewelry, made mainly in the 5th to 7th century AD, represented by numerous gold specimens. Bead-rimmed and fitted with a loop, most were intended to be worn suspended by a string around the neck, supposedly as an <a href="/wiki/Amulet" title="Amulet">amulet</a>. The gold for the bracteates came from coins paid as peace money by the Roman Empire to their Northern Germanic neighbors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OLDTIDENS_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-OLDTIDENS-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motifs">Motifs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracteate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Motifs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Welschingen-B.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="490" data-file-width="476" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Welschingen-B.jpg/220px-Welschingen-B.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Welschingen-B.jpg/330px-Welschingen-B.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Welschingen-B.jpg/440px-Welschingen-B.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Welschingen-B.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
B-bracteate of the B7 or "Fürstenberg" type, found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welschingen" title="Welschingen">Welschingen</a> (IK 389), interpreted as depicting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frija-Frigg" title="Frija-Frigg">Frija-Frigg</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Many of the bracteates feature ruler portraits of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_monarchy" title="Germanic monarchy">Germanic kings</a> with characteristic hair that is plaited back and depictions of figures from <a href="/wiki/Germanic_mythology" title="Germanic mythology">Germanic mythology</a> influenced to varying extents by Roman coinage while others feature entirely new motifs. The motifs are commonly those of Germanic mythology and some are believed to be <a href="/wiki/Germanic_paganism" title="Germanic paganism">Germanic pagan</a> <a href="/wiki/Icon" title="Icon">icons</a> giving protection or for divination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OLDTIDENS_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-OLDTIDENS-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Often depicted is a figure with a <a href="/wiki/Sleipnir" title="Sleipnir">horse</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hugin_and_Munin" title="Hugin and Munin">birds</a> and sometimes a <a href="/wiki/Gungnir" title="Gungnir">spear</a> – that some scholars interpret as a representation of the Germanic god <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wodan" title="Wodan">Wodan</a> – and aspects of the figure that would later appear in 13th century depictions as <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Poetic_Edda" title="Poetic Edda">Poetic Edda</a>. For this reason the bracteates are a target of iconographic studies by scholars interested in Germanic religion. Several bracteates also feature <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Runic_alphabet" title="Runic alphabet">runic alphabet</a> inscriptions (a total of 133 inscriptions on bracteates are known, amounting to more than a third of the entire <a href="/wiki/Elder_Futhark" title="Elder Futhark">Elder Futhark</a> corpus). Numerous Bracteates feature <a href="/wiki/Swastika" title="Swastika">swastikas</a> as a common motif.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OLDTIDENS_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-OLDTIDENS-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Typology">Typology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracteate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Typology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The typology for bracteates divides them into several letter-named categories, a system introduced in an 1855 treatise by the Danish numismatist <a href="/wiki/Christian_J%C3%BCrgensen_Thomsen" title="Christian Jürgensen Thomsen">Christian Jürgensen Thomsen</a> named <i>Om Guldbracteaterne og Bracteaternes tidligste Brug som Mynt</i> and finally defined formally by the Swedish numismatist <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Montelius" title="Oscar Montelius">Oscar Montelius</a> in his 1869 treatise <i>Från jernåldern</i>:</p>
<ul>
<li>A-bracteates (approximately 92 specimens): showing the face of a human, modelled after antique imperial coins</li>
<li>B-bracteates (appr. 91 specimens): one to three human figures in standing, sitting or kneeling positions, often accompanied by animals</li>
<li>C-bracteates (best represented, by appr. 426 specimens): showing a male's head above a quadruped, often interpreted as the Germanic god <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woden" title="Woden">Woden</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OLDTIDENS_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-OLDTIDENS-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>D-bracteates (appr. 359 specimens): showing one or more highly stylized animals</li>
<li>E-bracteates (appr. 280 specimens): showing an animal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triskele" title="Triskele">triskele</a> under a circular feature</li>
<li>F-bracteates (appr. 17 specimens): as a subgroup of the D-bracteates, showing an imaginary animal</li>
<li>M-'bracteates' (appr. 17 specimens): two-sided imitations of Roman imperial medallions</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Corpus">Corpus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracteate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Corpus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>More than 1,000 Migration Period bracteates of type A-, B-, C-, D-, and F are known in total (Heizmann &amp; Axboe 2011). Of these, 135 (ca. 11%) bear <a href="/wiki/Elder_Futhark" title="Elder Futhark">Elder Futhark</a> inscriptions which are often very short; the most notable inscriptions are found on the <a href="/wiki/Seeland-II-C" title="Seeland-II-C">Seeland-II-C</a> (offering traveling protection to the one who wears it), <a href="/wiki/Vadstena_bracteate" title="Vadstena bracteate">Vadstena</a> (giving a listing of the Elder Futhark combined with a potential magical inscription) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tjurk%C3%B6_bracteate" title="Tjurkö bracteate">Tjurkö</a> (featuring an inscription in scaldic verse) bracteates.</p>
<p>To these can be added the ca. 270 E-bracteates (Gaimster 1998), which belong to the Vendel Period and thus are slightly later than the other types. They were produced only on Gotland, and while the earlier bracteates (apart from a few English pieces) all were made from gold, many E-bracteates were made from silver or bronze.</p>
<p>The German historian <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Karl_Hauck&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Karl Hauck (page does not exist)">Karl Hauck</a>, Danish archaeologist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Morten_Axboe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Morten Axboe (page does not exist)">Morten Axboe</a> and German runologist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Klaus_D%C3%BCwel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Klaus Düwel (page does not exist)">Klaus Düwel</a> have worked since the 1960s to create a complete corpus of the early Germanic bracteates from the migration period, complete with large scale photographs and drawings. This has been published in three volumes in German named <i>Die Goldbrakteaten der Völkerwanderungszeit. Ikonographischer Katalog</i>. A catalogue supplement is included in Heizmann &amp; Axboe 2011.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_medieval_bracteates">Early medieval bracteates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracteate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early medieval bracteates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brakteat_bischof_ulrich_von_halberstadt_und_albrecht_der_baer.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="253" data-file-width="251" height="222" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/31/Brakteat_bischof_ulrich_von_halberstadt_und_albrecht_der_baer.jpg/220px-Brakteat_bischof_ulrich_von_halberstadt_und_albrecht_der_baer.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/3/31/Brakteat_bischof_ulrich_von_halberstadt_und_albrecht_der_baer.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brakteat_bischof_ulrich_von_halberstadt_und_albrecht_der_baer.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Medieval silver bracteate portraying bishop <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ulrichs_von_Halberstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ulrichs von Halberstadt (page does not exist)">Ulrichs von Halberstadt</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albert_I_of_Brandenburg" title="Albert I of Brandenburg">Albert I of Brandenburg</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Silver bracteates are different from the migration period bracteates and were the main type of coin minted in German-speaking areas, with the exception of the <a href="/wiki/Rhineland" title="Rhineland">Rhineland</a>, beginning at around 1130 in <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a> and were taken out of circulation at about 1520. In some <a href="/wiki/Cantons_of_Switzerland" title="Cantons of Switzerland">cantons of Switzerland</a>, bracteate-like <a href="/wiki/Rappen" title="Rappen">rappen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heller_(money)" title="Heller (money)">heller</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Angster&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Angster (page does not exist)">angster</a> were produced during the 18th century.</p>
<p>The term <i>bracteate</i> for these coins was not used contemporarily. It was first used in the 17th century.</p>
<p>The bracteates were usually called back regularly, about once or twice a year, and could be exchanged for new coins with a deduction (<i>Renovatio Monetae</i>). This system worked like a <a href="/wiki/Demurrage_(currency)" title="Demurrage (currency)">demurrage</a>: People wouldn't hoard their coins, because they lost their value. So this money was used more as a medium of exchange than for storing value. This increased the <a href="/wiki/Velocity_of_money" title="Velocity of money">velocity of money</a> and stimulated the economy.</p>
<p>Medieval silver bracteates may be large, but most are about 15 millimeters across and weigh about 1 gram. Sometimes the coins could be divided to pay smaller amounts.</p>
<p>The town leagues were not interested in such a system and introduced in 1413 the <i>Ewiger Pfennig</i> (perpetual penny) without this decay.</p>
