<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">1321 leper scare</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>The <b>1321 leper scare</b> (also known as the <b>lepers' plot</b>) was an alleged conspiracy of French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lepers" title="Lepers">lepers</a> to spread their disease by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Well_water_contamination" title="Well water contamination">contaminating well water</a> with their powders and poisons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McVaugh_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-McVaugh-1">[1]</a></sup> Lepers were the most abused people during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> according to the American Jewish historian <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Grayzel" title="Solomon Grayzel">Solomon Grayzel</a>; they were thrown out of settlements and treated as wild animals due to the widespread belief that the disease was highly contagious.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayzel_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grayzel-2">[2]</a></sup> Jews and Muslims were said to be implicated in the plot, providing an excuse for local authorities to attack both Jewish and leper communities. The hysteria quickly spread to the neighbouring realms, most notably to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Aragon" title="Kingdom of Aragon">Kingdom of Aragon</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1321_leper_scare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The rumours of the conspiracy broke out in the spring of 1321 and terrified the people of <a href="/wiki/Southern_France" title="Southern France">southern France</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayzel_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grayzel-2">[2]</a></sup> Torture of lepers ensued, and eventually a confession was forced out, stating that the lepers were acting on the orders of Jews, who in turn had been bribed by the <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Muslims of Spain</a>, in an attempt to "poison the Christian population of Europe".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grayzel_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Grayzel-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jordan_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jordan-3">[3]</a></sup> The scare flourished in the wake of the previous year's <a href="/wiki/Shepherds%27_Crusade_(1320)" title="Shepherds' Crusade (1320)">Shepherds' Crusade</a>, which saw young rural men and women form a mob and attack Jews in France and the neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Aragon" title="Kingdom of Aragon">Kingdom of Aragon</a>, despite being ordered to stop by <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XXII" title="Pope John XXII">Pope John XXII</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philip_V_of_France" title="Philip V of France">King Philip V of France</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_II_of_Aragon" title="James II of Aragon">King James II of Aragon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jordan_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jordan-3">[3]</a></sup> Already in 1320, some of the apprehended and later hanged "crusaders" claimed to have found barrels filled with rotten bread while pillaging a <a href="/wiki/Leper_colony" title="Leper colony">leper colony</a> (possibly near <a href="/wiki/Le_Mas-d%27Agenais" title="Le Mas-d'Agenais">Le Mas-d'Agenais</a>), and made a strikingly uncommon accusation, alleging that the lepers had intended to use the bread to prepare poisons for contaminating well water. The rumours that sparked the violence in 1321 may have started here. While the Shepherds' Crusade was led by rioters, the persecution of lepers was orchestrated by municipal authorities, making it judicial though extralegal (judicial power being royal prerogative).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nirenberg_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nirenberg-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>King Philip was on a tour of the region when the stories started circulating. He found himself in a difficult position; he could openly neither condone nor condemn the unstoppable persecution, as the former would have led to more violence, while the latter would have undermined his authority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nirenberg_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nirenberg-4">[4]</a></sup> The Dominican inquisitor <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Gui" title="Bernard Gui">Bernard Gui</a> was instructed to conduct extensive investigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franklin_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Franklin-5">[5]</a></sup> On 21 June, Philip ordered by edict that all lepers be imprisoned and examined under torture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McVaugh_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-McVaugh-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nirenberg_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nirenberg-4">[4]</a></sup> Those found guilty were to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burnt_at_the_stake" title="Burnt at the stake">burnt at the stake</a>. Since their crimes were declared <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A8se-majest%C3%A9" title="Lèse-majesté">lèse-majesté</a>, the lepers' property was to be confiscated by the Crown, but this decision was successfully defied by his vassals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nirenberg_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nirenberg-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aragon">Aragon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1321_leper_scare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Aragon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table cellspacing="5" class="toccolours" style="float: right; margin-right: 0em; margin-right: 1em; font-size: 85%; background:#c6dbf7; color:black; width:24em; max-width:40%">
<tr>
<td align="left">"Know also, lord, that lepers have been captured in <a href="/wiki/Avignon" title="Avignon">Avignon</a> and subjected to torture, and it is said that they have confessed that they were to poison all the waters of wells and fountains that were outside of houses, and that this is to be presumed. It has already been decreed in Avignon that no one should use water from outside fountains. And it is said that Jews consented to all this. We are notifying Your Serenity of this so that you may take precautions that from this or similar deeds no harm come to your people."</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td align="left">Excerpt from Sancho's letter to James<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nirenberg_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nirenberg-4">[4]</a></sup></td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>News of the uncovered plot spread rapidly to the neighbouring countries. King James learned of the supposed conspiracy and associated violence almost immediately.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McVaugh_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-McVaugh-1">[1]</a></sup> James's cousin, <a href="/wiki/Sancho_of_Majorca" title="Sancho of Majorca">King Sancho of Majorca</a>, informed him of the situation in France by a letter dated 2 June, but James deliberated for nearly a week. The French lepers fleeing the "lash of justice", as James put it in a letter to his officials, were already seeking shelter in his realm. He cautiously commanded the arrest and expulsion of all leprous foreigners, while Jews were not mentioned. By 27 June, James had changed his mind in favour of a harsher approach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nirenberg_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Nirenberg-4">[4]</a></sup> He ordered not only the seizure of the diseased, destruction of their powders, and questioning under torture,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McVaugh_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-McVaugh-1">[1]</a></sup> but also the arrest and expulsion of non-leprous foreigners, "since it is difficult, truly even impossible, to recognize such and identify them".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nirenberg_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Nirenberg-4">[4]</a></sup> Local inquisitions were set up in <a href="/wiki/Manresa" title="Manresa">Manresa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ejea_de_los_Caballeros" title="Ejea de los Caballeros">Ejea de los Caballeros</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huesca" title="Huesca">Huesca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montblanc,_Tarragona" title="Montblanc, Tarragona">Montblanc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tarazona" title="Tarazona">Tarazona</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>. Those who confessed were burnt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McVaugh_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-McVaugh-1">[1]</a></sup> Leper colonies were attacked and their goods seized, including the ancient leprosarium attached to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Church_of_Santa_Maria_de_Cervera&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Church of Santa Maria de Cervera (page does not exist)">Church of Santa Maria de Cervera</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nirenberg_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-Nirenberg-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>A suspected leper was most likely examined and diagnosed with the disease by frightened lay people rather than experienced physicians. A year after the scare, a physician called Amonant decided to move from <a href="/wiki/Gascony" title="Gascony">Gascony</a> to Aragon, only to be apprehended in Huesca and accused of being a leper intending to poison the water. The physician appealed to King James's son <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_II_of_Aragon" title="Alfonso II of Aragon">Alfons</a> and was granted examination by local physicians, who confirmed that he was not infected. Frightened, he chose to nevertheless leave Aragon. The incident was probably one of many that helped promote medical diagnosis of leprosy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McVaugh_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-McVaugh-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
