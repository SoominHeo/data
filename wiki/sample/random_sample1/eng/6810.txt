<p><b>Bram Cohen</b> (born October 12, 1975)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is an American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_programmer" title="Computer programmer">computer programmer</a>, best known as the author of the <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer" title="Peer-to-peer">peer-to-peer</a> (P2P) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BitTorrent_(protocol)" title="BitTorrent (protocol)">BitTorrent protocol</a>, as well as the first <a href="/wiki/File_sharing" title="File sharing">file sharing</a> program to use the protocol, also known as <a href="/wiki/BitTorrent_(software)" title="BitTorrent (software)">BitTorrent</a>. He is also the co-founder of <a href="/wiki/CodeCon" title="CodeCon">CodeCon</a> and organizer of the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">San Francisco Bay Area</a> P2P-hackers meeting, and was the co-author of <a href="/wiki/Codeville" title="Codeville">Codeville</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_career">Early life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bram_Cohen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cohen grew up in the <a href="/wiki/Upper_West_Side" title="Upper West Side">Upper West Side</a> of Manhattan, New York City as the son of a teacher and computer scientist. He claims he learned the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BASIC_programming_language" title="BASIC programming language">BASIC programming language</a> at the age of 5 on his family's <a href="/wiki/Timex_Sinclair" title="Timex Sinclair">Timex Sinclair</a> computer. Cohen passed the <a href="/wiki/American_Invitational_Mathematics_Examination" title="American Invitational Mathematics Examination">American Invitational Mathematics Examination</a> to qualify for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_of_America_Mathematical_Olympiad" title="United States of America Mathematical Olympiad">United States of America Mathematical Olympiad</a> (USAMO) while he attended <a href="/wiki/Stuyvesant_High_School" title="Stuyvesant High School">Stuyvesant High School</a> in New York City. He graduated from Stuyvesant in 1993<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and attended <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_at_Buffalo,_The_State_University_of_New_York" title="University at Buffalo, The State University of New York">SUNY Buffalo</a>. He later dropped out of college to work for several <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_company" title="Dot-com company">dot com companies</a> throughout the mid to late 1990s, the last being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MojoNation" title="MojoNation">MojoNation</a>, an ambitious but ill-fated project he worked on with Jim McCoy.</p>
<p>MojoNation allowed people to break up confidential files into encrypted chunks and distribute those pieces on computers also running the software. If someone wanted to download a copy of this encrypted file, he would have to download it simultaneously from many computers. This concept, Cohen thought, was perfect for a <a href="/wiki/File_sharing" title="File sharing">file sharing</a> program, since programs like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KaZaA" title="KaZaA">KaZaA</a> take a long time to download a large file because the file is (usually) coming from one source (or "peer"). Cohen designed BitTorrent to be able to download files from many different sources, thus speeding up the download time, especially for users with faster <a href="/wiki/Download" title="Download">download</a> than <a href="/wiki/Upload" title="Upload">upload</a> speeds. Thus, the more popular a file is, the faster a user will be able to download it, since many people will be downloading it at the same time, and these people will also be uploading the data to other users.</p>
<p>Cohen claims he has <a href="/wiki/Asperger_syndrome" title="Asperger syndrome">Asperger syndrome</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-wired-3">[3]</a></sup> based on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self_diagnosis" title="Self diagnosis">self diagnosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="BitTorrent">BitTorrent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bram_Cohen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: BitTorrent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In April 2001, Cohen quit MojoNation and began work on BitTorrent. Cohen unveiled his novel ideas at the first <a href="/wiki/CodeCon" title="CodeCon">CodeCon</a> conference, which he and his roommate <a href="/wiki/Len_Sassaman" title="Len Sassaman">Len Sassaman</a> created as a showcase event for novel technology projects after becoming disillusioned with the state of technology conferences. It remains an event for those seeking information about new directions in software, though BitTorrent continues to lay claim to the title of "most famous presentation".</p>
<p>Cohen wrote the first BitTorrent client implementation in <a href="/wiki/Python_(programming_language)" title="Python (programming language)">Python</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BitTorrent_client" title="BitTorrent client">many other programs</a> have since implemented the protocol.</p>
<p>In the summer of 2002, Cohen collected free pornography to lure <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_test" title="Beta test">beta testers</a> to use the program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-wired-3">[3]</a></sup> BitTorrent gained its fame for its ability to quickly share large music and movie files online. Cohen himself has claimed he has never violated copyright law using his software.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-wired-3">[3]</a></sup> Regardless, he is outspoken in his belief that the current media business was doomed to being outmoded despite the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RIAA" title="RIAA">RIAA</a> and MPAA's legal or technical tactics, such as <a href="/wiki/Digital_rights_management" title="Digital rights management">digital rights management</a>. In May 2005, Cohen released a <a href="/wiki/BitTorrent_tracker" title="BitTorrent tracker">trackerless</a> beta version of BitTorrent.</p>
<p>In late 2003, Cohen served a short career at <a href="/wiki/Valve_Corporation" title="Valve Corporation">Valve Corporation</a> to work on <a href="/wiki/Steam_(software)" title="Steam (software)">Steam</a>, their <a href="/wiki/Digital_distribution" title="Digital distribution">digital distribution</a> system introduced for <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life_2" title="Half-Life 2">Half-Life 2</a></i>.</p>
<p>By 2004, he had left Valve and formed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BitTorrent,_Inc." title="BitTorrent, Inc.">BitTorrent, Inc.</a> with his brother <a href="/wiki/Ross_Cohen" title="Ross Cohen">Ross Cohen</a> and business partner <a href="/wiki/Ashwin_Navin" title="Ashwin Navin">Ashwin Navin</a>. In 2012 he announced a beta-version of BitTorrent Live for TV broadcasting through the Internet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-btlive_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-btlive-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="BitTorrent_and_the_MPAA">BitTorrent and the MPAA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bram_Cohen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: BitTorrent and the MPAA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By mid-2005, BitTorrent, Inc. was funded by venture capitalist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=David_Chao&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="David Chao (page does not exist)">David Chao</a> from <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Doll_Capital_Management&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Doll Capital Management (page does not exist)">Doll Capital Management</a>, and in late 2005 Cohen and Navin made a deal with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MPAA" title="MPAA">MPAA</a> to remove links to illegal content on the official BitTorrent website. The deal was with the seven largest studios in America. The agreement means the site will comply with procedures outlined in the <a href="/wiki/Digital_Millennium_Copyright_Act" title="Digital Millennium Copyright Act">Digital Millennium Copyright Act</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bram_Cohen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 2008, Cohen lives in the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">San Francisco Bay Area</a>, United States, with his wife Jenna and their three children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cohen's hobbies include original <a href="/wiki/Origami" title="Origami">origami</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juggling" title="Juggling">juggling</a> up to five balls, but his main interest is in <a href="/wiki/Recreational_mathematics" title="Recreational mathematics">recreational mathematics</a>. Cohen maintains a <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blog</a> where he frequently discusses <a href="/wiki/Trust_metric" title="Trust metric">trust metrics</a> with <a href="/wiki/Raph_Levien" title="Raph Levien">Raph Levien</a>, as well as money systems, games of skill, and other math-related topics. He is also an <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_puzzle" title="Mechanical puzzle">assembly puzzle</a> enthusiast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He has designed several puzzles including some in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/Oskar_van_Deventer" title="Oskar van Deventer">Oskar van Deventer</a> including several gear-based puzzles such as Gear Shift<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and a multiple <a href="/wiki/Rubik%27s_Cube" title="Rubik's Cube">Rubik's Cube</a> variant called Bram's Fortress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Some of Bram's puzzle designs are available for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3-D_printing" title="3-D printing">3-D printing</a> via <a href="/wiki/Shapeways" title="Shapeways">Shapeways</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
