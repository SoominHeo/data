<p>A <b>Brodmann area</b> is a region of the <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex" title="Cerebral cortex">cerebral cortex</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Human_brain" title="Human brain">human</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">primate</a> <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a>, defined by its <a href="/wiki/Cytoarchitecture" title="Cytoarchitecture">cytoarchitecture</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Histological" title="Histological">histological</a> structure and organization of <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brodmann_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:482px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1307_Brodmann_Areas.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1394" data-file-width="2353" height="284" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/1307_Brodmann_Areas.jpg/480px-1307_Brodmann_Areas.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/1307_Brodmann_Areas.jpg/720px-1307_Brodmann_Areas.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/1307_Brodmann_Areas.jpg/960px-1307_Brodmann_Areas.jpg 2x" width="480"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1307_Brodmann_Areas.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A number of important Brodmann areas have been marked out on this brain.</div>

<p>Brodmann areas were originally defined and numbered by the <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anatomist" title="Anatomist">anatomist</a> <a href="/wiki/Korbinian_Brodmann" title="Korbinian Brodmann">Korbinian Brodmann</a> based on the <a href="/wiki/Cytoarchitecture" title="Cytoarchitecture">cytoarchitectural</a> organization of <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neurons</a> he observed in the cerebral cortex using the <a href="/wiki/Franz_Nissl#Named_histology_concepts" title="Franz Nissl">Nissl method</a> of cell staining. Brodmann published his maps of cortical areas in humans, monkeys, and other species in 1909,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> along with many other findings and observations regarding the general cell types and <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex#Laminar_pattern" title="Cerebral cortex">laminar organization</a> of the mammalian cortex. The same Brodmann area number in different species does not necessarily indicate homologous areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A similar, but more detailed cortical map was published by <a href="/wiki/Constantin_von_Economo" title="Constantin von Economo">Constantin von Economo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Georg_N._Koskinas" title="Georg N. Koskinas">Georg N. Koskinas</a> in 1925.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economo_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Economo-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Present_importance">Present importance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brodmann_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Present importance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Brodmann areas have been discussed, debated, refined, and renamed exhaustively for nearly a century and remain the most widely known and frequently cited cytoarchitectural organization of the human cortex.</p>
<p>Many of the areas Brodmann defined based solely on their neuronal organization have since been correlated closely to diverse cortical functions. For example, Brodmann areas 1, 2 and 3 are the <a href="/wiki/Postcentral_gyrus" title="Postcentral gyrus">primary somatosensory cortex</a>; area 4 is the <a href="/wiki/Primary_motor_cortex" title="Primary motor cortex">primary motor cortex</a>; area 17 is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_visual_cortex" title="Primary visual cortex">primary visual cortex</a>; and areas 41 and 42 correspond closely to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_auditory_cortex" title="Primary auditory cortex">primary auditory cortex</a>. <a href="/wiki/Higher-order_thinking" title="Higher-order thinking">Higher order functions</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex#Association_areas" title="Cerebral cortex">association cortical areas</a> are also consistently localized to the same Brodmann areas by <a href="/wiki/Neurophysiology" title="Neurophysiology">neurophysiological</a>, <a href="/wiki/Functional_magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Functional magnetic resonance imaging">functional imaging</a>, and other methods (e.g., the consistent localization of <a href="/wiki/Broca%27s_area" title="Broca's area">Broca's</a> speech and language area to the left Brodmann areas 44 and 45). However, functional imaging can only identify the approximate localization of brain activations in terms of Brodmann areas since their actual boundaries in any individual brain requires its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Histological" title="Histological">histological</a> examination.</p>

</ul>
<p>(*) Area only found in non-human <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">primates</a>.</p>
<p>Some of the original Brodmann areas have been subdivided further, e.g., "23a" and "23b".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brodmann_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When von Bonin and Bailey constructed a brain map for the <a href="/wiki/Macaque" title="Macaque">macaque</a> monkey they found the description of Brodmann inadequate and wrote: "Brodmann (1907), it is true, prepared a map of the human brain which has been widely reproduced, but, unfortunately, the data on which it was based was never published"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> They instead used the cytoarchitechtonic scheme of <a href="/wiki/Constantin_von_Economo" title="Constantin von Economo">Constantin von Economo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Georg_N._Koskinas" title="Georg N. Koskinas">Georg N. Koskinas</a> published in 1925<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economo_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Economo-3">[3]</a></sup> which had the "only acceptable detailed description of the human cortex".</p>
