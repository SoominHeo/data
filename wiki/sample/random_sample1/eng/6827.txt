<p>A <b>bromance</b> is a close, emotionally intense, non-sexual bond between two men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeAngelis_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-DeAngelis-1">[1]</a></sup> It is an exceptionally tight <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affectional_orientation" title="Affectional orientation">affectional</a>, <a href="/wiki/Homosociality" title="Homosociality">homosocial</a> <a href="/wiki/Male_bonding" title="Male bonding">male bonding</a> relationship exceeding that of usual friendship,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elder_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Elder-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strudwick_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strudwick-3">[3]</a></sup> and is distinguished by a particularly high level of emotional intimacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeAngelis_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-DeAngelis-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SanFilippo_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-SanFilippo-4">[4]</a></sup> The emergence of the concept since the beginning of the 21st century has been seen as reflecting a change in societal perception and interest in the theme,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeAngelis_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-DeAngelis-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elder_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Elder-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strudwick_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Strudwick-3">[3]</a></sup> with an increasing openness of society in the twenty-first century to reconsider gender, sexuality, and exclusivity constraints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chen_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chen-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bromance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Bromance</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Bro_(subculture)" title="Bro (subculture)">bro</a></i> or <i>brother</i> and <i>romance</i>. <a href="/wiki/Dave_Carnie" title="Dave Carnie">Dave Carnie</a> is credited with coining the term as editor of the skateboard magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Big_Brother_(magazine)" title="Big Brother (magazine)">Big Brother</a></i> in the 1990s to refer specifically to the sort of relationships that develop between skaters who spent a great deal of time together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeAngelis_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-DeAngelis-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elliott_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Elliott-6">[6]</a></sup> The term did not attain broad currency until approximately 2005<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeAngelis_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-DeAngelis-1">[1]</a></sup> when the theme became more prominent in the motion picture industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeAngelis_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-DeAngelis-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SanFilippo_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-SanFilippo-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical">Historical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bromance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Romantic_friendship" title="Romantic friendship">Romantic friendship</a></div>
<p>Numerous examples exist of intense <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homosocial" title="Homosocial">homosocial</a> friendships throughout history, between men and also between women; such relationships were common. <a href="/wiki/Romantic_friendship" title="Romantic friendship">Romantic friendship</a>, a historical construct with a different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homoerotic" title="Homoerotic">homoerotic</a> <a href="/wiki/Connotation" title="Connotation">connotation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_context" title="Social context">social construction</a>, is discussed within that article.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bromance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bromance has been examined from viewpoints such as <a href="/wiki/Historiography" title="Historiography">historiography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Discourse_analysis" title="Discourse analysis">discourse analysis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_research" title="Social research">social research</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Queer_theory" title="Queer theory">queer theory</a> in book-length reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeAngelis_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-DeAngelis-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yalof_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yalof-7">[7]</a></sup> The emergence of bromance over the past decade has been seen as reflecting how society has collectively changed its perception and interest in the theme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeAngelis_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-DeAngelis-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elder_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Elder-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strudwick_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Strudwick-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SanFilippo_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-SanFilippo-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yalof_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Yalof-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several characteristics of bromance have been cited. Bromance conveys a male <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homosocial" title="Homosocial">homosocial</a> relationship that goes much further than traditional homosocial practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeAngelis_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-DeAngelis-1">[1]</a></sup> The increased closeness goes beyond being mere friends, to a deep bond that has been characterized as capturing the conceptual edge of "is <a href="/wiki/Gay" title="Gay">gay</a>/is not gay".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeAngelis_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-DeAngelis-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SanFilippo_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-SanFilippo-4">[4]</a></sup> Its emergence as a distinctive conceptual genre and theme in the movie and television industry is seen as reflective of a "broader acceptance of nonheteronormative cultural expressions as well as the prospect of a same-sex intimacy that transcends matters of sexual orientation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeAngelis_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-DeAngelis-1">[1]</a></sup> Contemporary cultural circumstances, including the struggle for and attainment of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gay_marriage" title="Gay marriage">gay marriage</a> equality, and specific elements of the depiction of bromance in movies and television separate it from <a href="/wiki/Buddy_film" title="Buddy film">buddy films</a>, as well as historic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romantic_friendships" title="Romantic friendships">romantic friendships</a>, which reflect a different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_construction" title="Social construction">social construction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeAngelis_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-DeAngelis-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strudwick_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Strudwick-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SanFilippo_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-SanFilippo-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yalof_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Yalof-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deresiewicz_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deresiewicz-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tenden_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tenden-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knight_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Knight-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Chen, society has taken a collective interest in reexamination of some of the traditional constraints on male friendship, and in potentially reshaping the constructs of gender, sexuality, and intimacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chen_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chen-5">[5]</a></sup> Bromance provides "a case study of gender, sexuality, and exclusivity constraints in twenty-first century America as they operate in law and beyond. Those constraints in turn speak to the privilege and subordination imbued in this type of relationship, with implications for other types as well."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chen_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chen-5">[5]</a></sup> This is in distinction to the connotations of <a href="/wiki/Romantic_friendship" title="Romantic friendship">romantic friendship</a>, a terminology of 20th century historical scholarship that retrospectively described close <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homosocial" title="Homosocial">homosocial</a> relationships, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_marriages" title="Boston marriages">Boston marriages</a>, which had become less common after potential physical intimacy between non-sexual partners came to be regarded with anxiety in the second half of the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Faderman_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Faderman-11">[11]</a></sup> On the one hand, social interest in the theme has been seen as driving the film industry, which has then fed back to society at large, exploring peoples' mindsets and addressing acceptance of “other types of relationships" between people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlbertiQR_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-AlbertiQR-12">[12]</a></sup> On the other, some have seen the emphasis on <a href="/wiki/Platonic_love" title="Platonic love">platonic love</a> as a rejection of <a href="/wiki/Homoeroticism" title="Homoeroticism">homoeroticism</a>, or as a deliberate confusion of homosocial with homoerotic relationships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bromance has also been seen as a reflection of greater "discursive expressivity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeAngelis_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-DeAngelis-1">[1]</a></sup> The experiences of friendship and masculinity, perhaps due to more open parenting styles from the 1970s, reflect a trend toward more openness emotionally, with increased expressivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elder_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Elder-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yalof_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Yalof-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alberti_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alberti-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacey_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jacey-15">[15]</a></sup> According to sociologist Peter Nardi, "men are less afraid of being perceived as gay. It has become more acceptable for them to show some emotion."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bindley_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bindley-16">[16]</a></sup> Men are marrying later, if at all, which impacts <a href="/wiki/Male_bonding" title="Male bonding">male bonding</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bindley_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bindley-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Badoo_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Badoo-17">[17]</a></sup> According to the 2010 US Census, the average age of a man's first marriage is 28, up from 23 in 1960; men with more education are waiting until their 30s before getting married.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gay-straight">Gay-straight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bromance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gay-straight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the term has generally been applied to straight relationships, mixed gay-straight relationships with no form of sexual intimacy have also been dubbed bromances. Examples of well-known gay-straight bromances include <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Kroell" title="Ronnie Kroell">Ronnie Kroell</a> and Ben DiChiara from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bravo_(US_TV_channel)" title="Bravo (US TV channel)">Bravo</a> reality series <i><a href="/wiki/Make_Me_a_Supermodel_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Make Me a Supermodel (U.S. TV series)">Make Me a Supermodel</a></i>, in which the pair was nicknamed "Bronnie",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aterovis_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aterovis-18">[18]</a></sup> the relationship on <i><a href="/wiki/Survivor:_Gabon" title="Survivor: Gabon">Survivor: Gabon</a></i> between Charlie Herschel and Marcus Lehman,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/American_Idol_(season_8)" title="American Idol (season 8)">American Idol</a></i>s <a href="/wiki/Kris_Allen" title="Kris Allen">Kris Allen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adam_Lambert" title="Adam Lambert">Adam Lambert</a>, which was given the name "Kradam".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Celebrity">Celebrity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bromance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Celebrity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of celebrity relationships have been popularly characterised as bromances.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ben_Affleck" title="Ben Affleck">Ben Affleck</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matt_Damon" title="Matt Damon">Matt Damon</a> were described as "perhaps <i>the</i> pioneering bromance in showbiz history,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yaskua_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yaskua-21">[21]</a></sup> which led to a hit <a href="/wiki/Off-Broadway" title="Off-Broadway">off-Broadway</a> play called <i>Matt and Ben</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Casablanca_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Casablanca-22">[22]</a></sup> The relationship between <a href="/wiki/Zachary_Quinto" title="Zachary Quinto">Zachary Quinto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chris_Pine" title="Chris Pine">Chris Pine</a>, stars of the 2009 <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek_(film)" title="Star Trek (film)">Star Trek</a></i> film, has been described similarly, in common with their on-screen characters' relationship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burr_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burr-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The close friendship between <a href="/wiki/George_Clooney" title="George Clooney">George Clooney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brad_Pitt" title="Brad Pitt">Brad Pitt</a> was once suggested to be "George's longest lasting affair"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Synnot_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Synnot-24">[24]</a></sup> and Clooney's bromantic tendencies served as the basis for an episode of the animated series <i><a href="/wiki/American_Dad!" title="American Dad!">American Dad!</a></i> entitled "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tears_of_a_Clooney" title="Tears of a Clooney">Tears of a Clooney</a>", in which lead character <a href="/wiki/Stan_Smith_(American_Dad!)" title="Stan Smith (American Dad!)">Stan Smith</a> becomes bromantically involved with Clooney as part of an elaborate revenge plot.</p>
<p>The tight relationship (both on- and off-stage) between <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Springsteen" title="Bruce Springsteen">Bruce Springsteen</a> and the late <a href="/wiki/E_Street_Band" title="E Street Band">E Street Band</a> saxophonist <a href="/wiki/Clarence_Clemons" title="Clarence Clemons">Clarence Clemons</a> has often been described<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> as one of the most fitting examples of bromance in Western modern music. This relationship is most notably depicted in Springsteen's song "<a href="/wiki/Tenth_Avenue_Freeze-Out" title="Tenth Avenue Freeze-Out">Tenth Avenue Freeze-Out</a>", from <i><a href="/wiki/Born_to_Run" title="Born to Run">Born to Run</a></i> (in which Springsteen and Clemons appear respectively under their pseudonyms Bad Scooter and Big Man), as well as in Clemons' autobiography <i>Big Man: Real Life &amp; Tall Tales</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Professional <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">footballers</a> <a href="/wiki/Eric_Dier" title="Eric Dier">Eric Dier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dele_Alli" title="Dele Alli">Dele Alli</a>, who play together for <a href="/wiki/Tottenham_Hotspur_F.C." title="Tottenham Hotspur F.C.">Tottenham Hotspur</a> and <a href="/wiki/England_national_football_team" title="England national football team">England</a>, have a close relationship that has been described as a bromance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Japanese and <a href="/wiki/K-pop" title="K-pop">Korean music industry</a> actively encourages bromance among male celebrities (particularly members of <a href="/wiki/Boy_band" title="Boy band">boy bands</a>) as part of the <a href="/wiki/Fan_service" title="Fan service">fan service</a> to please the audience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bromance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Buddy_film" title="Buddy film">Buddy films</a> have to a degree been rebranded as bromance films, although critics draw a distinction between the two, noting that a buddy film tends to be more explicitly violent and less open about its latent homosexual content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeAngelis_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-DeAngelis-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carbone_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carbone-33">[33]</a></sup> The intersection between buddy films and what would come to be called the bromance film was noted comedically at least as early as 1978, when <i><a href="/wiki/National_Lampoon_(magazine)" title="National Lampoon (magazine)">National Lampoon</a></i> ran a parody ad for the football-themed buddy film <i><a href="/wiki/Semi-Tough" title="Semi-Tough">Semi-Tough</a></i>, renamed "Semi-Sweet" and featuring an illustration of stars <a href="/wiki/Burt_Reynolds" title="Burt Reynolds">Burt Reynolds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kris_Kristofferson" title="Kris Kristofferson">Kris Kristofferson</a> holding hands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russo_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Russo-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prominent examples of <a href="/wiki/Bromantic_comedy" title="Bromantic comedy">bromantic comedy</a> include Judd Apatow's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_40_Year_Old_Virgin" title="The 40 Year Old Virgin">The 40 Year Old Virgin</a></i> (2005) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knocked_Up_(film)" title="Knocked Up (film)"><i>Knocked Up</i></a> (2007), which targeted non-sexual homosocial behavior and masculinity in inventive ways,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Setoodeh_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Setoodeh-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/David_Dobkin_(director)" title="David Dobkin (director)">David Dobkin</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Wedding_Crashers" title="Wedding Crashers">Wedding Crashers</a></i> (2005),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SanFilippo_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-SanFilippo-4">[4]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Zoolander" title="Zoolander">Zoolander</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Funny_People" title="Funny People">Funny People</a></i> (2009),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harbidge_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harbidge-37">[37]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Hamburg" title="John Hamburg">John Hamburg</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_Love_You_Man" title="I Love You Man">I Love You Man</a></i> (2009),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlbertiQR_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-AlbertiQR-12">[12]</a></sup> Todd Phillips' <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hangover" title="The Hangover">The Hangover</a></i> (2009),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harbidge_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harbidge-37">[37]</a></sup> and Gordon's <i><a href="/wiki/Horrible_Bosses" title="Horrible Bosses">Horrible Bosses</a></i> (2011).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harbidge_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-Harbidge-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a>'s novels predate what could formally be called a "bromance," the portrayal of the relationships between <a href="/wiki/Frodo_Baggins" title="Frodo Baggins">Frodo Baggins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Samwise_Gamgee" title="Samwise Gamgee">Samwise Gamgee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meriadoc_Brandybuck" title="Meriadoc Brandybuck">Meriadoc Brandybuck</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peregrin_Took" title="Peregrin Took">Peregrin Took</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gimli_(Middle-earth)" title="Gimli (Middle-earth)">Gimli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Legolas" title="Legolas">Legolas</a> in the novels and the films have been characterized as bromance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Time-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>The theme remains popular, with different genres looking at the concept in various ways, such as <i>Best of Enemies</i>, a documentary about the 1960s feud between intellectuals <a href="/wiki/Gore_Vidal" title="Gore Vidal">Gore Vidal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_F._Buckley" title="William F. Buckley">William F. Buckley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bromance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bromance on television has also become more commonplace. It appeared early on in the partnership of two CIA/KGB spies in the 1960s' <i><a href="/wiki/The_Man_from_U.N.C.L.E." title="The Man from U.N.C.L.E.">Man from U.N.C.L.E.</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>, and in the 1970s' buddy-cop show <a href="/wiki/Starsky_%26_Hutch" title="Starsky &amp; Hutch">Starsky &amp; Hutch</a>, which producer Aaron Spelling called TV’s first heterosexual love affair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Some critics also point to the 1970s' <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Odd_Couple_(TV_series)" title="The Odd Couple (TV series)">Odd Couple</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gilbert_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gilbert-42">[42]</a></sup>, about which executive producer <a href="/wiki/Garry_Marshall" title="Garry Marshall">Garry Marshall</a> has said, "The network was concerned that we were being too gay."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2008, <i><a href="/wiki/TV_Guide" title="TV Guide">TV Guide</a></i> placed <a href="/wiki/Gregory_House" title="Gregory House">Gregory House</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hugh_Laurie" title="Hugh Laurie">Hugh Laurie</a>) and <a href="/wiki/James_Wilson_(House)" title="James Wilson (House)">James Wilson</a> (<a href="/wiki/Robert_Sean_Leonard" title="Robert Sean Leonard">Robert Sean Leonard</a>) on the cover, under the headline "Isn't It Bromantic?". <a href="/wiki/Brody_Jenner" title="Brody Jenner">Brody Jenner</a>, featured on MTV's reality show <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hills_(TV_series)" title="The Hills (TV series)">The Hills</a></i> and the subject of bromance discussions for his relationships with castmates <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Justin_Bobby" title="Justin Bobby">Justin Bobby</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spencer_Pratt" title="Spencer Pratt">Spencer Pratt</a>, debuted his own series on the network, called <i><a href="/wiki/Bromance_(TV_series)" title="Bromance (TV series)">Bromance</a></i>, on December 29, 2008. The six-episode series features Jenner selecting from amongst competitors to become part of Jenner's "entourage".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yagedaran_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yagedaran-44">[44]</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/Scrubs_(TV_series)" title="Scrubs (TV series)">Scrubs</a></i>, J.D. is a sensitive doctor "completely in touch with his feelings. He's not afraid of showing his best pal Turk how much he loves him."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burbach_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burbach-45">[45]</a></sup> In one episode, they sing, "Guy love. That’s all it is."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burbach_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Burbach-45">[45]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Good_Guys_(2010_TV_series)" title="The Good Guys (2010 TV series)">The Good Guys</a></i> "promotes male bonding while self-consciously acknowledging its homoerotic overtones."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> <i>The Independent</i> analyzed the BBC's <i><a href="/wiki/Sherlock_(TV_series)" title="Sherlock (TV series)">Sherlock</a></i> as a bromance, and looked at bromance thematically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strudwick_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Strudwick-3">[3]</a></sup> The relationship between Sherlock Holmes and Dr. Watson as a bromance has been visited elsewhere also.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pelling_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pelling-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bromance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The cultural concept that bromance connotes particular closeness has been taken up thematically. The concept has been visited in biology,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zielinski_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zielinski-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cheney_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cheney-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lavelle_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lavelle-51">[51]</a></sup> as well as an experimental acrobatic video dance piece, <i>Bromance</i>, which explores “... the intimacy of physical interaction between guys; of their 'bromance.'”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Instinct_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Instinct-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>The relationship between <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> and former press secretary <a href="/wiki/Scott_McClellan" title="Scott McClellan">Scott McClellan</a> as told in McClellan's book <i><a href="/wiki/What_Happened" title="What Happened">What Happened</a></i> was called by one reviewer "the tale of one long, failed bromance".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The former premiers of <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dalton_McGuinty" title="Dalton McGuinty">Dalton McGuinty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jean_Charest" title="Jean Charest">Jean Charest</a>, were described as a "burgeoning bromance".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Harper" title="Stephen Harper">Stephen Harper</a> of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tony_Abbott" title="Tony Abbott">Tony Abbott</a> of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and their respective countries, were characterized as a "conservative bromance".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knight_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Knight-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horgan20131219_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Horgan20131219-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horgan20140610_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Horgan20140610-57">[57]</a></sup> The term has been used to describe <a href="/wiki/Narendra_Modi" title="Narendra Modi">Narendra Modi</a> from <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> from the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> during the January 2015 visit,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parsons_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Parsons-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campbell_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Agrawal_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Agrawal-60">[60]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Putin" title="Vladimir Putin">Vladimir Putin</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federation" title="Russian Federation">Russian Federation</a> with <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Schr%C3%B6der" title="Gerhard Schröder">Gerhard Schröder</a> from <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newcomb_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Newcomb-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2017 Obama's relationship with Vice President <a href="/wiki/Joe_Biden" title="Joe Biden">Joe Biden</a> was described as a "bromance".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
