<p>The <b>Brønsted–Lowry theory</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction" title="Acid–base reaction">acid–base reaction</a> theory which was proposed independently by <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Nicolaus_Br%C3%B8nsted" title="Johannes Nicolaus Brønsted">Johannes Nicolaus Brønsted</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Martin_Lowry" title="Thomas Martin Lowry">Thomas Martin Lowry</a> in 1923.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The fundamental concept of this theory is that when an acid and a base react with each other, the acid forms its <a href="/wiki/Conjugate_acid" title="Conjugate acid">conjugate base</a>, and the base forms its conjugate acid by exchange of a <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> (the hydrogen cation, or H<sup>+</sup>). This theory is a generalization of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arrhenius_theory" title="Arrhenius theory">Arrhenius theory</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions_of_acids_and_bases">Definitions of acids and bases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_acid%E2%80%93base_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions of acids and bases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tmulti tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:251px;max-width:251px">
<div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:122px;max-width:122px">
<div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Johannes_Br%C3%B8nsted.jpg"><img alt="" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="343" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Johannes_Br%C3%B8nsted.jpg/120px-Johannes_Br%C3%B8nsted.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Johannes_Br%C3%B8nsted.jpg/180px-Johannes_Br%C3%B8nsted.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Johannes_Br%C3%B8nsted.jpg/240px-Johannes_Br%C3%B8nsted.jpg 2x" width="120"/></a></div>
</div>
<div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:125px;max-width:125px">
<div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thomas_Martin_Lowry2.jpg"><img alt="" data-file-height="1593" data-file-width="1248" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Thomas_Martin_Lowry2.jpg/123px-Thomas_Martin_Lowry2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Thomas_Martin_Lowry2.jpg/185px-Thomas_Martin_Lowry2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Thomas_Martin_Lowry2.jpg/246px-Thomas_Martin_Lowry2.jpg 2x" width="123"/></a></div>
</div>
<div style="clear:left"></div>
<div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left;text-align:left;background-color:transparent"><a href="/wiki/Johannes_Nicolaus_Br%C3%B8nsted" title="Johannes Nicolaus Brønsted">Johannes Nicolaus Brønsted</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Lowry" title="Martin Lowry">Thomas Martin Lowry</a>, independently, formulated the idea that <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a> are proton (H<sup>+</sup>) donors while bases are proton acceptors.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction#Arrhenius_definition" title="Acid–base reaction">Arrhenius theory</a> acids are defined as substances which dissociate in aqueous solution to give H<sup>+</sup> (hydrogen ions). Bases are defined as substances which dissociate in aqueous solution to give OH<sup>−</sup> (hydroxide ions).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1923 <a href="/wiki/Physical_chemistry" title="Physical chemistry">physical chemists</a> Johannes Nicolaus Brønsted in Denmark and Thomas Martin Lowry in England independently proposed the theory that carries their names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In the Brønsted–Lowry theory acids and bases are defined by the way they react with each other, which allows for greater generality. The definition is expressed in terms of an equilibrium expression</p>
<dl>
<dd>acid + base ⇌ conjugate base + conjugate acid.</dd>
</dl>
<p>With an acid, HA, the equation can be written symbolically as:</p>
<dl>
<dd>HA + B ⇌ A<sup>−</sup> + HB<sup>+</sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>The equilibrium sign, ⇌, is used because the reaction can occur in both forward and backward directions. The acid, HA, can lose a proton to become its conjugate base, A<sup>−</sup>. The base, B, can accept a proton to become its conjugate acid, HB<sup>+</sup>. Most acid-base reactions are fast so that the components of the reaction are usually in <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_equilibrium" title="Dynamic equilibrium">dynamic equilibrium</a> with each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aqueous_solutions">Aqueous solutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_acid%E2%80%93base_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Aqueous solutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Acetic-acid-dissociation-3D-balls.png"><img alt="Acetic acid, CH3COOH, is composed of a methyl group, CH3, bound chemically to a carboxylate group, COOH. The carboxylate group can lose a proton and donate it to a water molecule, H2O, leaving behind an acetate anion CH3COO− and creating a hydronium cation H3O+. This is an equilibrium reaction, so the reverse process can also take place." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="513" data-file-width="2150" height="84" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Acetic-acid-dissociation-3D-balls.png/350px-Acetic-acid-dissociation-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Acetic-acid-dissociation-3D-balls.png/525px-Acetic-acid-dissociation-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Acetic-acid-dissociation-3D-balls.png/700px-Acetic-acid-dissociation-3D-balls.png 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Acetic-acid-dissociation-3D-balls.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">Acetic acid</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weak_acid" title="Weak acid">weak acid</a>, donates a proton (hydrogen ion, highlighted in green) to water in an equilibrium reaction to give the <a href="/wiki/Acetate" title="Acetate">acetate</a> ion and the <a href="/wiki/Hydronium" title="Hydronium">hydronium</a> ion. Red: oxygen, black: carbon, white: hydrogen.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Consider the following acid–base reaction:</p>
<dl>
<dd>CH<sub>3</sub>COOH + H<sub>2</sub>O ⇌ CH<sub>3</sub>COO<sup>−</sup> + H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup></dd>
</dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">Acetic acid</a>, CH<sub>3</sub>COOH, is an acid because it donates a proton to water (H<sub>2</sub>O) and becomes its conjugate base, the <a href="/wiki/Acetate" title="Acetate">acetate</a> ion (CH<sub>3</sub>COO<sup>−</sup>). H<sub>2</sub>O is a base because it accepts a proton from CH<sub>3</sub>COOH and becomes its conjugate acid, the <a href="/wiki/Hydronium" title="Hydronium">hydronium</a> ion, (H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The reverse of an acid-base reaction is also an acid-base reaction, between the conjugate acid of the base in the first reaction and the conjugate base of the acid. In the above example, acetate is the base of the reverse reaction and hydronium ion is the acid.</p>
<dl>
<dd>H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup> + CH<sub>3</sub>COO<sup>−</sup> ⇌ CH<sub>3</sub>COOH + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
</dl>
<p>The power of the Brønsted–Lowry theory is that, in contrast to Arrhenius theory, it does not require an acid to dissociate.</p>

The amphoteric nature of water</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The essence of Brønsted–Lowry theory is that an acid only exists as such in relation to a base, and <i>vice versa</i>. Water is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoteric" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a> as it can act as an acid or as a base. In the image shown at the right one molecule of H<sub>2</sub>O acts as a base and gains H<sup>+</sup> to become H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup>while the other acts as an acid and loses H<sup>+</sup> to become OH<sup>−</sup>.</p>
<p>Another example is furnished by substances like <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_hydroxide" title="Aluminium hydroxide">aluminium hydroxide</a>, Al(OH)<sub>3</sub>.</p>
<dl>
<dd>Al(OH)<sub>3</sub> + OH<sup>−</sup> ⇌ <span class="chemf nowrap">Al(OH)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/>
4</span></span>, acting as an acid</dd>
<dd>3H<sup>+</sup> + Al(OH)<sub>3</sub> ⇌ 3H<sub>2</sub>O + Al<sup>3+</sup>(aq), acting as a base</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-aqueous_solutions">Non-aqueous solutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_acid%E2%80%93base_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Non-aqueous solutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The hydrogen ion, or hydronium ion, is a Brønsted–Lowry acid in aqueous solutions, and the hydroxide ion is a base, by virtue of the self-dissociation reaction</p>
<dl>
<dd>H<sub>2</sub>O + H<sub>2</sub>O ⇌ H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup> + OH<sup>−</sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>An analogous reaction occurs in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_ammonia" title="Liquid ammonia">liquid ammonia</a></p>
<dl>
<dd>NH<sub>3</sub> + NH<sub>3</sub> ⇌ <span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/>
4</span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/>
2</span></span></dd>
</dl>
<p>Thus, the ammonium ion, <span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/>
4</span></span>, plays the same role in liquid ammonia as does the hydronium ion in water and the amide ion, <span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/>
2</span></span>, is analogous to the hydroxide ion. Ammonium salts behave as acids, and amides behave as bases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some non-aqueous solvents can behave as bases, that is, proton acceptors, in relation to Brønsted–Lowry acids.</p>
<dl>
<dd>HA + S ⇌ A<sup>−</sup> + SH<sup>+</sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>where S stands for a solvent molecule. The most important such solvents are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimethylsulfoxide" title="Dimethylsulfoxide">dimethylsulfoxide</a>, DMSO, and <a href="/wiki/Acetonitrile" title="Acetonitrile">acetonitrile</a>, CH<sub>3</sub>CN, as these solvents has been widely used to measure the <a href="/wiki/Acid_dissociation_constant#Acidity_in_nonaqueous_solutions" title="Acid dissociation constant">acid dissociation constants</a> of organic molecules. Because DMSO is a stronger proton acceptor than H<sub>2</sub>O the acid becomes a stronger acid in this solvent than in water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Indeed, many molecules behave as acids in non-aqueous solution that do not do so in aqueous solution. An extreme case occurs with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_acid" title="Carbon acid">carbon acids</a>, where a proton is extracted from a C-H bond.</p>
<p>Some non-aqueous solvents can behave as acids. An acidic solvent will increase basicity of substances dissolved in it. For example, the compound CH<sub>3</sub>COOH is known as <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a> because of its acidic behaviour in water. However it behaves as a base in liquid <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride" title="Hydrogen chloride">hydrogen chloride</a>, a much more acidic solvent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>HCl + CH<sub>3</sub>COOH ⇌ Cl<sup>−</sup> + <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
3</span>C(OH)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/>
2</span></span></dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_Lewis_acid.E2.80.93base_theory">Comparison with Lewis acid–base theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_acid%E2%80%93base_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Comparison with Lewis acid–base theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the same year that Brønsted and Lowry published their theory, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G._N._Lewis" title="G. N. Lewis">G. N. Lewis</a> proposed an alternative theory of acid–base reactions. The Lewis theory is based on <a href="/wiki/Electronic_structure" title="Electronic structure">electronic structure</a>. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_base" title="Lewis base">Lewis base</a> is defined as a compound that can donate an <a href="/wiki/Electron_pair" title="Electron pair">electron pair</a> to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acid</a>, a compound that can accept an electron pair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-review1940_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-review1940-13">[13]</a></sup> Lewis's proposal gives an explanation to the Brønsted–Lowry classification in terms of electronic structure.</p>
<dl>
<dd>HA + B<b>:</b> ⇌ A<b>:</b><sup>−</sup> + BH<sup>+</sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>In this representation both the base, B, and the conjugate base, A<sup>−</sup>, are shown carrying a lone pair of electrons and the proton, which is a Lewis acid, is transferred between them.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NH3-BF3-adduct-bond-lengthening-2D.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="516" data-file-width="1100" height="103" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/NH3-BF3-adduct-bond-lengthening-2D.png/220px-NH3-BF3-adduct-bond-lengthening-2D.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/NH3-BF3-adduct-bond-lengthening-2D.png/330px-NH3-BF3-adduct-bond-lengthening-2D.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/NH3-BF3-adduct-bond-lengthening-2D.png/440px-NH3-BF3-adduct-bond-lengthening-2D.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NH3-BF3-adduct-bond-lengthening-2D.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Adduct of ammonia and boron trifluoride</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Lewis later wrote in "To restrict the group of acids to those substances that contain hydrogen interferes as seriously with the systematic understanding of chemistry as would the restriction of the term <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizing agent</a> to substances containing <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-review1940_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-review1940-13">[13]</a></sup> In Lewis theory an acid, A, and a base, B<b>:</b>, form an adduct, AB, in which the electron pair is used to form a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dative_covalent_bond" title="Dative covalent bond">dative covalent bond</a> between A and B. This is illustrated with the formation of the adduct H<sub>3</sub>N−BF<sub>3</sub> from <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boron_trifluoride" title="Boron trifluoride">boron trifluoride</a>, a reaction that cannot occur in aqueous solution because boron trifluoride reacts violently with water in a hydrolysis reaction.</p>
<dl>
<dd>BF<sub>3</sub> + 3H<sub>2</sub>O → B(OH)<sub>3</sub> + 3HF</dd>
<dd>HF ⇌ H<sup>+</sup> + F<sup>−</sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>These reactions illustrate that BF<sub>3</sub> is an acid in both Lewis and Brønsted–Lowry classifications and emphasizes the consistency between both theories.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Boric_acid" title="Boric acid">Boric acid</a> is recognized as a Lewis acid by virtue of the reaction</p>
<dl>
<dd>B(OH)<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O ⇌ <span class="chemf nowrap">B(OH)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/>
4</span></span> + H<sup>+</sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>In this case the acid does not dissociate, it is the base, H<sub>2</sub>O that dissociates. A solution of B(OH)<sub>3</sub> is acidic because hydrogen ions are liberated in this reaction.</p>
<p>There is strong evidence that dilute aqueous solutions of <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> contain negligible amounts of the ammonium ion</p>
<dl>
<dd>H<sub>2</sub>O + NH<sub>3</sub> ⥇ OH<sup>−</sup> + <span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/>
4</span></span></dd>
</dl>
<p>and that, when dissolved in water, ammonia functions as a Lewis base.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_the_Lux-Flood_theory">Comparison with the Lux-Flood theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_acid%E2%80%93base_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Comparison with the Lux-Flood theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The reactions between certain oxides in non-aqueous media cannot be explained on the basis of Brønsted–Lowry theory. For example, the reaction</p>
<dl>
<dd>2MgO + SiO<sub>2</sub> → Mg<sub>2</sub>SiO<sub>4</sub></dd>
</dl>
<p>does not fall within the scope of the Brønsted–Lowry definition of acids and bases. On the other hand, MgO is basic and SiO<sub>2</sub> is acidic in the Brønsted–Lowry sense, referring to mixtures in water.</p>
<dl>
<dd>2H<sup>+</sup> + MgO(s) → Mg<sup>2+</sup>(aq) + 2H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
<dd>SiO<sub>2</sub>(s) + 2H<sub>2</sub>O → <span class="chemf nowrap">SiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">4−<br/>
4</span></span> + 4H<sup>+</sup> (≡ Si(OH)<sub>4</sub>(aq))</dd>
</dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction#Other_acid.E2.80.93base_theories" title="Acid–base reaction">Lux-Flood</a> theory also classifies <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_oxide" title="Magnesium oxide">magnesium oxide</a> as a base in non-aqueous circumstances. This classification is important in <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">geochemistry</a>. Minerals such as <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a>, (Mg,Fe)SiO<sub>4</sub> are classed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultramafic" title="Ultramafic">ultramafic</a>; olivine is a compound of a very basic oxide, MgO, with an acidic oxide, silica, SiO<sub>2</sub>.</p>
