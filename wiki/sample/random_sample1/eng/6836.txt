<p><b>Bruce Alan Beutler</b> (born December 29, 1957) is an American immunologist and geneticist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Together with <a href="/wiki/Jules_A._Hoffmann" title="Jules A. Hoffmann">Jules A. Hoffmann</a>, he received one-half of the 2011 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Physiology or Medicine</a>, for "their discoveries concerning the activation of innate immunity" (the other half went to <a href="/wiki/Ralph_M._Steinman" title="Ralph M. Steinman">Ralph M. Steinman</a> for "his discovery of the dendritic cell and its role in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptive_immunity" title="Adaptive immunity">adaptive immunity</a>").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nobel_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nobel-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beutler is currently Director of the Center for the Genetics of Host Defense at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_Southwestern_Medical_Center" title="University of Texas Southwestern Medical Center">University of Texas Southwestern Medical Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-profile_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-profile-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bruce_Beutler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Between 1959 and 1977, Beutler lived in Southern California. He received his secondary school education at <a href="/wiki/Polytechnic_School" title="Polytechnic School">Polytechnic School</a> in Pasadena, California. He attended college at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_San_Diego" title="University of California, San Diego">University of California, San Diego</a>, graduating at the age of 18 in 1976. He enrolled in medical school at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a> in 1977 and received his M.D. degree in 1981 at the age of 23<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>During his childhood and early adolescent years, Beutler developed a lasting interest in biological science. Some of his formative experiences in biology included studies in the laboratory of his father, and later, in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Hope_National_Medical_Center" title="City of Hope National Medical Center">City of Hope</a> laboratory of <a href="/wiki/Susumu_Ohno" title="Susumu Ohno">Susumu Ohno</a>, a mammalian geneticist known for his work on evolution, genome structure, and sex differentiation. In addition, he worked in the laboratories of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Abraham_Braude&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Abraham Braude (page does not exist)">Abraham Braude</a>, an expert in the biology of <a href="/wiki/Lipopolysaccharide" title="Lipopolysaccharide">lipopolysaccharide</a> (LPS), also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endotoxin" title="Endotoxin">endotoxin</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Patricia_Spear&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Patricia Spear (page does not exist)">Patricia Spear</a>, an authority on <a href="/wiki/Herpes_simplex_virus" title="Herpes simplex virus">Herpes simplex virus</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Later, Beutler was to perform extensive research on both LPS and herpesviruses, aimed principally at understanding inborn host resistance to infectious diseases, often referred to as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Innate_immunity" title="Innate immunity">innate immunity</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_positions">Academic positions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bruce_Beutler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Academic positions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Beutler majored in biology as an undergraduate at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_San_Diego" title="University of California, San Diego">University of California, San Diego</a>, where he graduated in 1976 at the age of 18. He attended medical school at the University of Chicago. From 1981 to 1983 Beutler continued his medical training at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_Southwestern_Medical_Center" title="University of Texas Southwestern Medical Center">University of Texas Southwestern Medical Center</a> in Dallas, Texas, as an intern in the Department of Internal Medicine, and as a resident in the Department of Neurology. Between 1983 and 1985 he was a postdoctoral fellow at <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_University" title="Rockefeller University">Rockefeller University</a> in the laboratory of <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Cerami" title="Anthony Cerami">Anthony Cerami</a>. He became an Assistant Professor at Rockefeller University in 1985. He was also an Associate Physician at the Rockefeller University Hospital between 1984 and 1986.</p>
<p>Beutler returned to Dallas in 1986 as an assistant professor in the Department of Internal Medicine of the University of Texas Southwestern Medical Center at Dallas, and an Assistant Investigator at the <a href="/wiki/Howard_Hughes_Medical_Institute" title="Howard Hughes Medical Institute">Howard Hughes Medical Institute</a>, where he retained a position for the next 14 years. He became an associate professor and an associate investigator with HHMI in 1990, and a professor in 1996.</p>
<p>In 2000, Beutler moved to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Scripps_Research_Institute" title="The Scripps Research Institute">The Scripps Research Institute</a> in La Jolla, California, as a professor in the Department of Immunology. In 2007, he became chairman of the newly created Department of Genetics at Scripps Research. In 2011, Beutler returned to UT Southwestern Medical Center to become director of the Center for the Genetics of Host Defense.</p>
<p>On October 4, 2011, Beutler was named regental professor of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_System" title="University of Texas System">University of Texas System</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He also sits on the Selection Committee for Life Science and Medicine which chooses winners of the <a href="/wiki/Shaw_Prize" title="Shaw Prize">Shaw Prize</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_contributions">Scientific contributions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bruce_Beutler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Scientific contributions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Beutler is best known for his pioneering molecular and genetic studies of inflammation and innate immunity. He was the first to isolate mouse <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tumor_necrosis_factor-alpha" title="Tumor necrosis factor-alpha">tumor necrosis factor-alpha</a> (TNF),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and to demonstrate the inflammatory potential of this cytokine, proving its important role in endotoxin-induced shock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Subsequently, he invented recombinant molecules expressly designed to neutralize TNF, fusing the binding portion of TNF receptor proteins to the heavy chain of an immunoglobulin molecule to force receptor dimerization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> These molecules were later used extensively as the drug <a href="/wiki/Etanercept" title="Etanercept">Etanercept</a> in the treatment of <a href="/wiki/Rheumatoid_arthritis" title="Rheumatoid arthritis">rheumatoid arthritis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crohn%27s_disease" title="Crohn's disease">Crohn's disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psoriasis" title="Psoriasis">psoriasis</a>, and other forms of inflammation. Interested in the mechanism by which LPS activates mammalian immune cells, Beutler used TNF production as a phenotypic endpoint to identify the LPS receptor. Identification of the receptor hinged on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Positional_cloning" title="Positional cloning">positional cloning</a> of the mammalian <i>Lps</i> locus, which had been known since the 1960s as a key genetic determinant of all biological responses to LPS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Beutler thus discovered the key sensors of microbial infection in mammals, demonstrating that one of the mammalian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toll-like_receptors" title="Toll-like receptors">Toll-like receptors</a>, TLR4, acts as the membrane-spanning component of the mammalian LPS receptor complex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The TLRs (of which ten are now known to exist in humans) are now widely known to function in the perception of microbes, each detecting signature molecules that herald infection. These receptors also mediate severe illness, including shock and systemic inflammation as it occurs in the course of an infection. They are central to the pathogenesis of sterile inflammatory and autoimmune diseases such as <a href="/wiki/Systemic_lupus_erythematosus" title="Systemic lupus erythematosus">systemic lupus erythematosus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The research on TLRs won him the Nobel Prize in 2011.</p>

<p>The positional cloning of <i>Lps</i> was completed in 1998. Beutler thereafter continued to apply a forward genetic approach to the analysis of immunity in mammals. In this process, germline mutations that alter immune function are created through a random process using the alkylating agent <a href="/wiki/ENU" title="ENU">ENU</a>, detected by their phenotypic effects, and then isolated by positional cloning. His work disclosed numerous essential signaling molecules required for the innate immune response,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and helped to delineate the biochemistry of innate immunity.</p>
<p>ENU mutagenesis was also used by Beutler and colleagues to study the global response to a defined infectious agent. By screening mutant mice for susceptibility to mouse <a href="/wiki/Cytomegalovirus" title="Cytomegalovirus">cytomegalovirus</a> (MCMV), they identified a large number of genes that make a life-or-death difference during infection, and termed this set of genes the MCMV "resistome".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> These genes fall into "sensing," "signaling," "effector," "homeostatic," and "developmental" categories, and some of them were wholly unexpected. For example, <a href="/wiki/KCNJ8" title="KCNJ8">K<sub>ir</sub>6.1</a> <a href="/wiki/ATP-sensitive_potassium_channel" title="ATP-sensitive potassium channel">ATP-sensitive potassium channels</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smooth_muscle" title="Smooth muscle">smooth muscle</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Coronary_circulation" title="Coronary circulation">coronary arteries</a> serve an essential homeostatic role during infection by this microbe, and mutations that affect them cause sudden death during infection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the course of their work, Beutler and his colleagues identified genes required for other important biological processes, including the regulation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_metabolism#Absorbing_iron_from_the_diet" title="Iron metabolism">iron absorption</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearing_(sense)" title="Hearing (sense)">hearing</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Embryogenesis" title="Embryogenesis">embryonic development</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> since their disruption by ENU created strikingly abnormal visible phenotypes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_recognition">Awards and recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bruce_Beutler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Awards and recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Beutler has been elected to numerous honorary academic societies. These include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">United States National Academy of Sciences</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Institute_of_Medicine" title="Institute of Medicine">Institute of Medicine</a>. Beutler is also an Associate Member (foreign associate) of the <a href="/wiki/European_Molecular_Biology_Organization" title="European Molecular Biology Organization">European Molecular Biology Organization</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EMBO" title="EMBO">EMBO</a>), and a member of the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_American_Physicians" title="Association of American Physicians">Association of American Physicians</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_for_Clinical_Investigation" title="American Society for Clinical Investigation">American Society for Clinical Investigation</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bruce_Beutler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Beutler is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ashkenazi_Jewish" title="Ashkenazi Jewish">Ashkenazi Jewish</a>, the son of <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Beutler" title="Ernest Beutler">Ernest Beutler</a> (geneticist) and Brondelle May Fleisher (journalist). He married Barbara Beutler (nÃ©e Lanzl) in 1980 and divorced in 1988, Beutler has three children: Daniel (born 1983), Elliot (born 1984), and Jonathan (born 1987).</p>
<p>His father, <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Beutler" title="Ernest Beutler">Ernest Beutler</a>, a hematologist and medical geneticist, was also a professor and department chairman at Scripps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bruce's grandmother, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kathe_Beutler&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kathe Beutler (page does not exist)">Kathe Beutler</a>, was the first cousin of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kurt_Rosenthal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kurt Rosenthal (page does not exist)">Kurt Rosenthal</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> grandfather of <a href="/wiki/Pamela_Ronald" title="Pamela Ronald">Pamela Ronald</a>, who discovered the first plant pattern recognition receptor, XA21. The Beutler and Rosenthal families fled Berlin after Hitler came to power and reunited in California after the war.</p>
