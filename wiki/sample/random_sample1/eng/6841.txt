<p><b>Brooke Megan Greenberg</b> (January 8, 1993 – October 24, 2013)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spiegel1029_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-spiegel1029-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sollev_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-sollev-2">[2]</a></sup> was an American who remained physically and cognitively similar to a <a href="/wiki/Toddler" title="Toddler">toddler</a>, despite her increasing age. She was about 30 in (76 cm) tall, weighed about 16 lb (7.3 kg) and had an estimated <a href="/wiki/Mental_age" title="Mental age">mental age</a> of nine months to one year. Brooke's doctors termed her condition <i>Syndrome X</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth_and_early_life">Birth and early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brooke_Greenberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Birth and early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Brooke was born on January 8, 1993, to <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spiegel1029_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-spiegel1029-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> parents Howard and Melanie Greenberg<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FFvideo_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FFvideo-4">[4]</a></sup> at <a href="/wiki/Sinai_Hospital" title="Sinai Hospital">Sinai Hospital</a> in <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a>, Maryland. She was delivered by <a href="/wiki/Caesarean_section" title="Caesarean section">Caesarean section</a>, one month before her due date due to "intermittent growth",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TLCdoc_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-TLCdoc-5">[5]</a></sup> weighing just four pounds (1.8 kg). She was born with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anterior_hip_dislocation" title="Anterior hip dislocation">anterior hip dislocation</a>, a condition that caused her legs to be swiveled upward toward her shoulders; it was corrected surgically. Otherwise, Brooke appeared to be a normal infant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frozen_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-frozen-6">[6]</a></sup> She was the third of four girls born to her parents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TLCdoc_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-TLCdoc-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In her first six years, she went through a series of unexplained <a href="/wiki/Medical_emergency" title="Medical emergency">medical emergencies</a> from which she recovered. She had seven perforated <a href="/wiki/Peptic_ulcer" title="Peptic ulcer">stomach ulcers</a>. She also suffered a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seizure" title="Seizure">seizure</a>. This was followed by what was later diagnosed as a <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>; weeks later, no damage was detected. At age five, Brooke had a mass in her brain that caused her to go into a deep sleep (after confirmation that it was not a <a href="/wiki/Coma" title="Coma">coma</a>) for 14 days. The doctors diagnosed the mass as a <a href="/wiki/Brain_tumor" title="Brain tumor">brain tumor</a>. She later awoke, and physicians found no tumor present. Brooke's pediatrician, Dr. Lawrence Pakula, states that the source of her sudden illness remains a mystery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abc1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-abc1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>During an interview on the talk show <i><a href="/wiki/Katie_(talk_show)" title="Katie (talk show)">Katie</a></i>, her father stated that between the ages of four and five, she stopped growing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <i>Child Frozen In Time</i>, a documentary about Brooke,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TLCdoc_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-TLCdoc-5">[5]</a></sup> was first broadcast on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TLC_(network)" title="TLC (network)">TLC</a> on August 9, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unexplained_condition">Unexplained condition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brooke_Greenberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Unexplained condition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Over several years, the Greenbergs visited many specialists, looking for an explanation for their daughter's strange condition, yet there was no diagnosis of any known genetic syndrome or chromosomal abnormality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walker_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-walker-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2001, when <i><a href="/wiki/Dateline_NBC" title="Dateline NBC">Dateline</a></i> documented Brooke, she was still the size of a six-month-old infant, weighing just 13 lb (5.9 kg) at 30 inches (76 cm) tall. The family still had no explanation. Brooke's mother Melanie said, "They [the specialists] just said she’ll catch up. Then we went to the <a href="/wiki/Nutritionist" title="Nutritionist">nutritionist</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endocrinologist" title="Endocrinologist">endocrinologist</a>. We tried the <a href="/wiki/Growth_hormone" title="Growth hormone">growth hormone</a>…". The growth hormone treatment had no effect. Howard, Brooke's father, said, "I mean, she did not put on an ounce, or she did not grow an inch... That’s when I knew there was a problem." After the growth hormone administration failed, the doctors, unable to <a href="/wiki/Medical_diagnosis" title="Medical diagnosis">diagnose</a> a known condition, named her condition <i>Syndrome X</i>.</p>
<p>The Greenbergs made many visits to nearby <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_Children%27s_Center" title="Johns Hopkins Children's Center">Johns Hopkins Children's Center</a> and even took Brooke to New York’s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Sinai_Hospital,_New_York" title="Mount Sinai Hospital, New York">Mount Sinai Hospital</a>, searching for information about their daughter’s condition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frozen_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-frozen-6">[6]</a></sup> When <a href="/wiki/Geneticist" title="Geneticist">geneticists</a> sequenced Greenberg's <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>, they found that the <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a> associated with the <a href="/wiki/DNA_repair-deficiency_disorder" title="DNA repair-deficiency disorder">premature aging diseases</a> were normal, unlike the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genetic_mutation" title="Genetic mutation">mutated</a> versions in patients with <a href="/wiki/Werner_syndrome" title="Werner syndrome">Werner syndrome</a> and <a href="/wiki/Progeria" title="Progeria">progeria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newscientist_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-newscientist-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_studies">Medical studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brooke_Greenberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Medical studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2006, Richard Walker, an endocrine physiologist at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_South_Florida_College_of_Medicine" title="University of South Florida College of Medicine">University of South Florida College of Medicine</a>, said that Brooke's body was not developing as a coordinated unit but as independent parts that were out of synchronization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (December 2015)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> She was never diagnosed with any known <a href="/wiki/Genetic_disorder" title="Genetic disorder">genetic disorder</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromosomal_abnormality" title="Chromosomal abnormality">chromosomal abnormality</a> that would help explain why.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abc1_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-abc1-7">[7]</a></sup> Her <a href="/wiki/Telomere" title="Telomere">telomeres</a> seemed to be shortening at the normal rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walker_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-walker-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009, Walker said, "There've been very minimal changes in Brooke's brain … Various parts of her body, rather than all being at the same stage, seem to be disconnected."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abc1_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-abc1-7">[7]</a></sup> Walker noted that Brooke's brain, for example, was not much more mature than that of a newborn infant. He estimated her mental age at around nine months to a year old. Brooke could make gestures and recognize sounds but could not speak. Her bones were like those of a ten-year-old, and she still had her <a href="/wiki/Deciduous_teeth" title="Deciduous teeth">baby teeth</a>, which had an estimated developmental age of about eight years. Said Walker, "We think that Brooke's condition presents us with a unique opportunity to understand the process of aging."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> "Different parts of her body are developing at different rates, as if they were not a unit but parts of separate organisms," Walker explained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spiegel1029_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-spiegel1029-1">[1]</a></sup> Dr. Walker did not say whether the genes responsible for development are the same ones responsible for aging.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brooke_Greenberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Brooke Greenberg died on October 24, 2013, at the Herman and Walter Samuelson Children's Hospital at Sinai Medical Center in Baltimore, the same hospital where she was born. Her funeral service took place on October 27, 2013, and that same morning, she was buried at Baltimore Hebrew Cemetery - Berrymans Lane, in <a href="/wiki/Reisterstown,_Maryland" title="Reisterstown, Maryland">Reisterstown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sollev_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-sollev-2">[2]</a></sup> The cause of her death was <a href="/wiki/Bronchomalacia" title="Bronchomalacia">bronchomalacia</a>, a medical condition usually occurring in children, which results in difficulty breathing due to weak cartilage in the walls of the <a href="/wiki/Bronchus" title="Bronchus">bronchial tubes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparable_cases">Comparable cases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brooke_Greenberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Comparable cases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dr. Walker believed that Brooke Greenberg's condition had resulted from a failure of central control genes. He identified two more people with similar developmental issues: Gabrielle Williams<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a> (born in 2004) and Nicky Freeman<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> (born in 1970), a 46-year-old man who looks like a preteen boy.</p>
<p>Gabrielle Williams's condition has since been determined to have been the result of a genetic mutation. Nicky Freeman has been diagnosed with deformity of the pituitary gland.</p>
<p>María Audenete do Nascimento, a Brazilian woman who lives in the town of <a href="/wiki/Caucaia" title="Caucaia">Caucaia</a> near <a href="/wiki/Fortaleza" title="Fortaleza">Fortaleza</a> in the state of <a href="/wiki/Cear%C3%A1" title="Ceará">Ceará</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, was once thought to have also been suffering from a condition similar to Greenberg's. She was reportedly born in May 1981 but stopped growing after nine months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> She has been diagnosed with severe <a href="/wiki/Hypothyroidism" title="Hypothyroidism">hypothyroidism</a>.</p>
<p>In May 2015, it was reported that Layla Qualls of Oklahoma looks like she is 9 or 10 months old, but is 3 years old. She is among seven children worldwide with Syndrome X that are being studied by researchers at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Los_Angeles" title="University of California, Los Angeles">University of California, Los Angeles</a>. In their ongoing research they found that the blood of Syndrome X children appears to age normally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Layla has also been featured in a TLC documentary titled "The Girls Who Don't Age". This documentary aired July 18, 2016 on TLC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TLCdocumentary_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-TLCdocumentary-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2016, it was reported that Alyssa Pennington of New Mexico looks like she is five years old, but is 12 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TLCdocumentary_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-TLCdocumentary-19">[19]</a></sup> She is another Syndrome X case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another case that has been reported in July 2016 is Jenifer Sandoval of Colorado. Although she looks like she is four years old, she is 22 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TLCdocumentary_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-TLCdocumentary-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Blood tissue from five other female Syndrome X cases (whose average age was 6.3 years) turned out to be age appropriate<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walkerEpigenetics_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-walkerEpigenetics-22">[22]</a></sup> according to a biomarker of aging known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_clock_(aging)" title="Biological clock (aging)">epigenetic clock</a>. The mean epigenetic age of the five pure Syndrome X subjects was 6.7 years (standard error=1.0) which is not significantly different from the mean chronological age of 6.3 years (standard error=1.8). Notably, the oldest pure Syndrome X case had an epigenetic age of 14.5 years which was 3.2 years older than her true chronological age. It is not yet known whether the epigenetic age of other tissues is also age appropriate in these cases.</p>
