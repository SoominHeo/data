<p><b>Brigham Young</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/əm/ 'm' in 'rhythm'">əm</span></span>/</a></span></span>; June 1, 1801 – August 29, 1877) was an American leader in the <a href="/wiki/Latter_Day_Saint_movement" title="Latter Day Saint movement">Latter Day Saint movement</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Settler" title="Settler">settler</a> of the Western United States. He was the second <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Church_(LDS_Church)" title="President of the Church (LDS Church)">President</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a> (LDS Church) from 1847 until his death in 1877. He founded <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">Salt Lake City</a> and he served as the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governor_of_Utah" title="Governor of Utah">governor</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Utah_Territory" title="Utah Territory">Utah Territory</a>. Young also led the foundings of the precursors to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Utah" title="University of Utah">University of Utah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brigham_Young_University" title="Brigham Young University">Brigham Young University</a>.</p>
<p>Young had many nicknames, among the most popular being "American <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (alternatively, the "Modern Moses" or "Mormon Moses"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> because, like the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">biblical</a> figure, Young led his followers, the <a href="/wiki/Mormon_pioneers" title="Mormon pioneers">Mormon pioneers</a>, in an <a href="/wiki/Emigration" title="Emigration">exodus</a> through a desert, to what they saw as a promised land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Young was dubbed by his followers the "Lion of the Lord" for his bold personality and was also commonly called "Brother Brigham" by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latter-day_Saint" title="Latter-day Saint">Latter-day Saints</a>. Young was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polygamy_and_the_Latter_Day_Saint_movement" title="Polygamy and the Latter Day Saint movement">polygamist</a> and was involved in controversies regarding <a href="/wiki/Black_people_and_early_Mormonism" title="Black people and early Mormonism">black people and the Priesthood</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Utah_War" title="Utah War">Utah War</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Mountain_Meadows_massacre" title="Mountain Meadows massacre">Mountain Meadows massacre</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_succession_to_Latter_Day_Saint_leadership">Early life and succession to Latter Day Saint leadership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigham_Young&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and succession to Latter Day Saint leadership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:227px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brigham_Young_and_his_brothers.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="723" data-file-width="1038" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Brigham_Young_and_his_brothers.jpg/225px-Brigham_Young_and_his_brothers.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Brigham_Young_and_his_brothers.jpg/338px-Brigham_Young_and_his_brothers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Brigham_Young_and_his_brothers.jpg/450px-Brigham_Young_and_his_brothers.jpg 2x" width="225"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brigham_Young_and_his_brothers.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The five sons of John and Nabby Young<br/>
From left to right: Lorenzo Dow, Brigham, <a href="/wiki/Phineas_Young" title="Phineas Young">Phineas H.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Young" title="Joseph Young">Joseph</a>, and John.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Young was born to John Young and Abigail "Nabby" Howe, a farming family in <a href="/wiki/Whitingham,_Vermont" title="Whitingham, Vermont">Whitingham, Vermont</a>, and worked as a travelling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carpenter" title="Carpenter">carpenter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blacksmith" title="Blacksmith">blacksmith</a>, among other trades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Young was first married in 1824 to Miriam Angeline Works. Though he had converted to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methodist" title="Methodist">Methodist</a> faith in 1823, Young was drawn to <a href="/wiki/Mormonism" title="Mormonism">Mormonism</a> after reading the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Mormon" title="Book of Mormon">Book of Mormon</a> shortly after its publication in 1830. He officially joined <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Christ_(Latter_Day_Saints)" title="Church of Christ (Latter Day Saints)">the new church</a> in 1832 and traveled to <a href="/wiki/Upper_Canada" title="Upper Canada">Upper Canada</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mormon_missionary" title="Mormon missionary">missionary</a>. After his wife died in 1832, Young joined many Mormons in establishing a community in <a href="/wiki/Kirtland,_Ohio" title="Kirtland, Ohio">Kirtland, Ohio</a>. Young was ordained a member of the original <a href="/wiki/Quorum_of_the_Twelve" title="Quorum of the Twelve">Quorum of the Twelve Apostles</a> in 1835, and he assumed a leadership role within that organization in taking Mormonism to the United Kingdom and organizing the exodus of Latter Day Saints from Missouri in 1838.</p>
<p>In 1844, while in jail awaiting trial for treason charges, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Smith" title="Joseph Smith">Joseph Smith</a>, <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Church" title="President of the Church">president of the church</a>, was <a href="/wiki/Death_of_Joseph_Smith" title="Death of Joseph Smith">killed by an armed mob</a>. Several claimants to the role of church president emerged during the <a href="/wiki/Succession_crisis_(Latter_Day_Saints)" title="Succession crisis (Latter Day Saints)">succession crisis</a> that ensued. Before a large meeting convened to discuss the succession in <a href="/wiki/Nauvoo,_Illinois" title="Nauvoo, Illinois">Nauvoo, Illinois</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Rigdon" title="Sidney Rigdon">Sidney Rigdon</a>, the senior surviving member of the church's <a href="/wiki/First_Presidency" title="First Presidency">First Presidency</a>, argued there could be no successor to the deceased prophet and that he should be made the "Protector" of the church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Young opposed this reasoning and motion. Smith had earlier recorded a <a href="/wiki/Revelation_(Latter_Day_Saints)" title="Revelation (Latter Day Saints)">revelation</a> which stated the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles was "equal in authority and power" to the First Presidency,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> so Young claimed that the leadership of the church fell to the Twelve Apostles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The majority in attendance were persuaded that the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles was to lead the church with Young as the Quorum's president. Many of Young's followers would later reminisce that while Young spoke to the congregation, he looked or sounded exactly like Smith, which they attributed to the power of God.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Young was ordained President of the Church in December 1847, three and a half years after Smith's death. Rigdon became the president of a separate <a href="/wiki/Rigdonite" title="Rigdonite">church organization</a> based in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Pennsylvania" title="Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania">Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania</a>, and other potential successors emerged to lead what became other denominations of the movement.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Migration_west">Migration west</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigham_Young&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Migration west">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Mormon_pioneers" title="Mormon pioneers">Mormon pioneers</a></div>
<p>Repeated conflict led Young to relocate his group of Latter-day Saints to the <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_Valley" title="Salt Lake Valley">Salt Lake Valley</a>, which was then part of <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. Young organized the journey that would take the <a href="/wiki/Mormon_pioneers" title="Mormon pioneers">Mormon pioneers</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winter_Quarters,_Nebraska" title="Winter Quarters, Nebraska">Winter Quarters, Nebraska</a>, in 1846, then to the Salt Lake Valley. By the time Young arrived at the final destination, it had come under American control as a result of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican-American_War" title="Mexican-American War">war with Mexico</a>, although U.S. sovereignty would not be confirmed <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Guadalupe_Hidalgo" title="Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo">until 1848</a>. Young arrived in the Salt Lake Valley on July 24, 1847, a date now recognized as <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_Day_(Utah)" title="Pioneer Day (Utah)">Pioneer Day</a> in Utah. Young's expedition was one the largest and one of the best organized westward treks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> On August 22, 29 days after arriving in the Salt Lake Valley, Young organized the <a href="/wiki/Mormon_Tabernacle_Choir" title="Mormon Tabernacle Choir">Mormon Tabernacle Choir</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>After three years of leading the church as the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Quorum_of_the_Twelve_Apostles_(LDS_Church)" title="President of the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles (LDS Church)">President of the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles</a>, Young reorganized a new <a href="/wiki/First_Presidency_(LDS_Church)" title="First Presidency (LDS Church)">First Presidency</a> and was declared president of the church on December 27, 1847.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governor_of_Utah_Territory">Governor of Utah Territory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigham_Young&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Governor of Utah Territory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brigham_Young_by_Charles_Roscoe_Savage,_1855.jpg"><img alt="Brigham Young by Charles Roscoe Savage, 1855.jpg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1205" data-file-width="803" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Brigham_Young_by_Charles_Roscoe_Savage%2C_1855.jpg/220px-Brigham_Young_by_Charles_Roscoe_Savage%2C_1855.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Brigham_Young_by_Charles_Roscoe_Savage%2C_1855.jpg/330px-Brigham_Young_by_Charles_Roscoe_Savage%2C_1855.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Brigham_Young_by_Charles_Roscoe_Savage%2C_1855.jpg/440px-Brigham_Young_by_Charles_Roscoe_Savage%2C_1855.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brigham_Young_by_Charles_Roscoe_Savage,_1855.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As colonizer and founder of <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">Salt Lake City</a>, Young was appointed the territory's first governor and superintendent of <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">American Indian</a> affairs by <a href="/wiki/Millard_Fillmore" title="Millard Fillmore">President Millard Fillmore</a> on February 3, 1851.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> During his time as governor, Young directed the establishment of settlements throughout present-day Utah, Idaho, Arizona, Nevada, California and parts of southern Colorado and northern Mexico. Under his direction, the Mormons built roads and bridges, forts, irrigation projects; established public welfare; organized a militia; <a href="/wiki/Battle_at_Fort_Utah" title="Battle at Fort Utah">issued an extermination order against the Timpanogos</a> and after a series of wars eventually made peace with the Native Americans. Young was also one of the first to subscribe to <a href="/wiki/Union_Pacific_Railroad" title="Union Pacific Railroad">Union Pacific</a> stock, for the construction of the <a href="/wiki/First_Transcontinental_Railroad" title="First Transcontinental Railroad">First Transcontinental Railroad</a>. Young organized the first legislature and established <a href="/wiki/Fillmore,_Utah" title="Fillmore, Utah">Fillmore</a> as the territory's first capital.</p>
<p>Young organized a Board of Regents to establish a university in the Salt Lake Valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> It was established on February 28, 1850, as the University of Deseret; its name was eventually changed to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Utah" title="University of Utah">University of Utah</a>.</p>
<p>In 1851, Young and several federal officials, including territorial Secretary <a href="/wiki/Broughton_Harris" title="Broughton Harris">Broughton Harris</a>, became unable to work cooperatively. Harris and the others departed Utah without replacements being named, and these individuals later became known as the <a href="/wiki/Runaway_Officials_of_1851" title="Runaway Officials of 1851">Runaway Officials of 1851</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1852, Young led the efforts to <a href="/wiki/History_of_slavery_in_Utah" title="History of slavery in Utah">legalize slavery in Utah</a>, based on <a href="/wiki/Mormonism_and_slavery" title="Mormonism and slavery">Mormon beliefs on slavery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young_Collier_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Young_Collier-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1856, Young organized an efficient mail service. In 1858, following the events of the <a href="/wiki/Utah_War" title="Utah War">Utah War</a>, he stepped down to his successor <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Cumming_(governor)" title="Alfred Cumming (governor)">Alfred Cumming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Church_Presidency">Church Presidency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigham_Young&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Church Presidency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Young was the longest serving President of the LDS Church in history, having served for 29 years.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Educational_endeavors">Educational endeavors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigham_Young&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Educational endeavors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Having previously established the University of Deseret during his tenure as governor, on October 16, 1875, Young personally purchased land in <a href="/wiki/Provo,_Utah" title="Provo, Utah">Provo, Utah</a>, to extend the reach of the University of Deseret.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Young said, "I hope to see an Academy established in Provo ... at which the children of the Latter-day Saints can receive a good education unmixed with the pernicious atheistic influences that are found in so many of the higher schools of the country."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dusenberry_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-dusenberry-25">[25]</a></sup> The school broke off from the University of Deseret and became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brigham_Young_Academy" title="Brigham Young Academy">Brigham Young Academy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dusenberry_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-dusenberry-25">[25]</a></sup> the precursor to <a href="/wiki/Brigham_Young_University" title="Brigham Young University">Brigham Young University</a>.</p>
<p>Within the church, Young reorganized the <a href="/wiki/Relief_Society" title="Relief Society">Relief Society</a> for women (1867), and he created organizations for <a href="/wiki/Young_Women_(organization)" title="Young Women (organization)">young women</a> (1869) and <a href="/wiki/Young_Men_(organization)" title="Young Men (organization)">young men</a> (1875).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temple_building">Temple building</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigham_Young&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Temple building">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Young was involved in <a href="/wiki/Temple_(LDS_Church)" title="Temple (LDS Church)">temple</a> building throughout his membership in the LDS Church and made temple building a priority of his church presidency. Under Smith's leadership, Young participated in the building of the <a href="/wiki/Kirtland_Temple" title="Kirtland Temple">Kirtland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nauvoo_Temple" title="Nauvoo Temple">Nauvoo</a> temples. Just four days after arriving in the Salt Lake Valley, Young designated the location for the <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_Temple" title="Salt Lake Temple">Salt Lake Temple</a>; he presided over its groundbreaking on April 6, 1853.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoM_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoM-26">[26]</a></sup> During his tenure, Young oversaw construction of the <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_Tabernacle" title="Salt Lake Tabernacle">Salt Lake Tabernacle</a> and he announced plans to build the <a href="/wiki/St._George_Utah_Temple" title="St. George Utah Temple">St. George</a> (1871), <a href="/wiki/Manti_Utah_Temple" title="Manti Utah Temple">Manti</a> (1875), and <a href="/wiki/Logan_Utah_Temple" title="Logan Utah Temple">Logan</a> (1877) temples. He also provisioned the building of the <a href="/wiki/Endowment_House" title="Endowment House">Endowment House</a>, a "temporary temple" which began to be used in 1855 to provide temple <a href="/wiki/Ordinance_(Latter_Day_Saints)" title="Ordinance (Latter Day Saints)">ordinances</a> to church members while the Salt Lake Temple was under construction.</p>

<p>Though <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plural_marriage" title="Plural marriage">polygamy</a> was practiced by Young's predecessor <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Smith" title="Joseph Smith">Joseph Smith</a>, the practice is often associated with Young. Some Latter Day Saint denominations, such as the <a href="/wiki/Community_of_Christ" title="Community of Christ">Community of Christ</a>, consider Young the "Father of Mormon Polygamy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In 1853, Young made the church's first official statement on the subject since the church had arrived in Utah. He spoke about the issue nine years after the purported <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_Latter_Day_Saint_polygamy" title="Origin of Latter Day Saint polygamy">original revelation</a> of Smith, and five years after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mormon_Exodus" title="Mormon Exodus">Mormon Exodus</a> to Utah.</p>
<p>One of the more controversial teachings of Young was the <a href="/wiki/Adam%E2%80%93God_doctrine" title="Adam–God doctrine">Adam–God doctrine</a>. According to Young, he was taught by Smith that <a href="/wiki/Adam" title="Adam">Adam</a> is "our Father and our God, and the only God with whom we have to do". According to the doctrine, Adam was once a mortal man who became resurrected and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exaltation_(Latter_Day_Saints)" title="Exaltation (Latter Day Saints)">exalted</a>. From another planet, Adam brought <a href="/wiki/Eve" title="Eve">Eve</a>, one of his wives, with him to the earth, where they became mortal by eating the fruit of the <a href="/wiki/Garden_of_Eden" title="Garden of Eden">Garden of Eden</a>. After bearing mortal children and establishing the human race, Adam and Eve returned to their heavenly thrones where Adam acts as the god of this world. Later, as Young is generally understood to have taught, Adam returned to the earth to become the biological father of Jesus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The LDS Church has since repudiated the Adam–God doctrine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Young is generally considered to have instituted <a href="/wiki/Black_people_and_Mormonism" title="Black people and Mormonism">a church ban</a> against conferring the priesthood on men of black African descent, who had been treated equally in this respect under Joseph Smith's presidency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bush_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bush-32">[32]</a></sup> After settling in Utah in 1848, Young announced the ban,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bush_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bush-32">[32]</a></sup> which also forbade blacks from participating in Mormon temple rites such as the <a href="/wiki/Endowment_(Latter_Day_Saints)" title="Endowment (Latter Day Saints)">endowment</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sealing_(Latter_Day_Saints)" title="Sealing (Latter Day Saints)">sealings</a>. On many occasions, Young taught that blacks were denied the priesthood because they were "the seed of Cain",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> but also stated that they would eventually receive the priesthood after "all the other children of Adam have the privilege of receiving the Priesthood, and of coming into the kingdom of God, and of being redeemed from the four quarters of the earth, and have received their resurrection from the dead, then it will be time enough to remove the curse from Cain and his posterity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In 1863, Young stated "Shall I tell you the law of God in regard to the African race? If the white man who belongs to the chosen seed mixes his blood with the seed of Cain, the penalty, under the law of God, is death on the spot. This will always be so."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> These racial restrictions remained in place until 1978, when the policy was rescinded by LDS Church president <a href="/wiki/Spencer_W._Kimball" title="Spencer W. Kimball">Spencer W. Kimball</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and the LDS Church subsequently "disavow[ed] theories advanced in the past" to explain this ban,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gospel_Topics-Race_and_the_Priesthood_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gospel_Topics-Race_and_the_Priesthood-37">[37]</a></sup> thereby "plac[ing] the origins of black priesthood denial blame squarely on Brigham Young."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conflicts">Conflicts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigham_Young&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Conflicts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shortly after the arrival of Young's pioneers, the new Mormon colonies were incorporated into the United States through the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Cession" title="Mexican Cession">Mexican Cession</a>. Young petitioned the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Congress" title="U.S. Congress">U.S. Congress</a> to create the <a href="/wiki/State_of_Deseret" title="State of Deseret">State of Deseret</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Compromise_of_1850" title="Compromise of 1850">Compromise of 1850</a> instead carved out <a href="/wiki/Utah_Territory" title="Utah Territory">Utah Territory</a> and Young was installed as governor. As governor and church president, Young directed both religious and economic matters. He encouraged independence and self-sufficiency. Many cities and towns in Utah, and some in neighboring states, were founded under Young's direction. Young's leadership style has been viewed as autocratic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> When federal officials received reports of widespread and systematic obstruction of federal officials in Utah (most notably judges), U.S. President <a href="/wiki/James_Buchanan" title="James Buchanan">James Buchanan</a> decided to install a non-Mormon governor. Buchanan accepted the reports of the judges without any further investigation, and the new non-sectarian governor was accompanied by troops sent to garrison forts in the new territory. The troops passed by the <a href="/wiki/Bleeding_Kansas" title="Bleeding Kansas">bloody Kansas–Missouri war</a> without intervening in it, as it was not open warfare and only isolated sporadic incidents. When Young received word that federal troops were headed to Utah with his replacement, he called out his militia to ambush the federal force. During the defense of Utah, now called the <a href="/wiki/Utah_War" title="Utah War">Utah War</a>, Young held the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army" title="U.S. Army">U.S. Army</a> at bay for a winter by taking their cattle and burning supply wagons. The Mormon forces were largely successful thanks to <a href="/wiki/Lot_Smith" title="Lot Smith">Lot Smith</a>. Young made plans to burn Salt Lake City and move his followers to Mexico, but at the last minute he relented and agreed to step down as governor<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Reliable source needed showing this is historically accurate statement (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. He later received a <a href="/wiki/Pardon" title="Pardon">pardon</a> from Buchanan. Relations between Young and future governors and U.S. Presidents were mixed.</p>

<p>The degree of Young's involvement in the <a href="/wiki/Mountain_Meadows_massacre" title="Mountain Meadows massacre">Mountain Meadows massacre</a>, which took place in <a href="/wiki/Washington_County,_Utah" title="Washington County, Utah">Washington County</a> in 1857, is disputed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Leonard_J._Arrington" title="Leonard J. Arrington">Leonard J. Arrington</a> reports that Young received a rider at his office on the day of the massacre, and that when he learned of the contemplated attack by the members of the LDS Church in Parowan and Cedar City, he sent back a letter directing that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fancher_party" title="Fancher party">Fancher party</a> be allowed to pass through the territory unmolested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Young's letter reportedly arrived on September 13, 1857, two days after the massacre. As governor, Young had promised the federal government he would protect immigrants passing through Utah Territory, but over 120 men, women and children were killed in this incident. There is no debate concerning the involvement of individual Mormons from the surrounding communities by scholars. Only children under the age of seven, who were cared for by local Mormon families, survived, and the murdered members of the wagon train were left unburied. The remains of about forty people were later found and buried, and <a href="/wiki/Union_Army" title="Union Army">Union Army</a> officer <a href="/wiki/James_Henry_Carleton" title="James Henry Carleton">James Henry Carleton</a> had a large cross made from local trees, the transverse beam bearing the engraving, "Vengeance Is Mine, Saith The Lord: I Will Repay" and erected a cairn of rocks at the site. A large slab of granite was put up on which he had the following words engraved: "Here 120 men, women and children were massacred in cold blood early in September, 1857. They were from Arkansas." For two years the monument stood as a memorial to those travelling the <a href="/wiki/Old_Spanish_Trail_(trade_route)" title="Old Spanish Trail (trade route)">Spanish Trail</a> through Mountain Meadow. Some claim that, in 1861, Young brought an entourage to Mountain Meadows and had the cairn and cross destroyed, while exclaiming, "Vengeance is mine and I have taken a little".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigham_Young&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Before his death in Salt Lake City on August 29, 1877,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Young was suffering from cholera morbus and inflammation of the bowels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> It is believed that he died of <a href="/wiki/Peritonitis" title="Peritonitis">peritonitis</a> from a ruptured <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vermiform_appendix" title="Vermiform appendix">appendix</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-byubio_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-byubio-2">[2]</a></sup> His last words were "Joseph! Joseph! Joseph!", invoking the name of the late Joseph Smith, founder of the Mormon faith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> On September 2, 1877, Young's funeral was held in the <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_Tabernacle" title="Salt Lake Tabernacle">Tabernacle</a> with an estimated 12,000 to 15,000 people in attendance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> He is buried on the grounds of the <a href="/wiki/Mormon_Pioneer_Memorial_Monument" title="Mormon Pioneer Memorial Monument">Mormon Pioneer Memorial Monument</a> in the heart of Salt Lake City. A bronze marker was placed at the grave site June 10, 1938, by members of the <a href="/wiki/Young_Men_(organization)" title="Young Men (organization)">Young Men</a> and <a href="/wiki/Young_Women_(organization)" title="Young Women (organization)">Young Women</a> organizations, which he founded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigham_Young&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigham_Young&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A century after his death, one writer stated that</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>[Joseph Smith] was succeeded by one of the outstanding organizers of the 19th century, Brigham Young. If the circumstances of his life had worked out differently [he] might have become a <a href="/wiki/Captain_of_industry" title="Captain of industry">captain of industry</a>—an <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Carnegie" title="Andrew Carnegie">Andrew Carnegie</a> or <a href="/wiki/John_D._Rockefeller" title="John D. Rockefeller">John D. Rockefeller</a> or a railroad builder. Instead, this able, energetic, earthy man became the absolute ruler and the revered, genuinely loved father figure of all Mormons everywhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paul197412_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-paul197412-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>He credited Young's leadership with helping to settle much of the American West: During the 30 years between the Mormons' arrival in Utah in 1847 and [his death in] 1877, Young directed the founding of 350 towns in the Southwest. Thereby the Mormons became the most important single agency in colonizing that <a href="/wiki/Intermountain_West" title="Intermountain West">vast arid West between the Rockies and the Sierra Nevada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paul197412_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-paul197412-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Memorials to Young include a bronze statue in front of the Abraham O. Smoot Administration Building, <a href="/wiki/Brigham_Young_University" title="Brigham Young University">Brigham Young University</a>; a marble statue in the <a href="/wiki/National_Statuary_Hall_Collection" title="National Statuary Hall Collection">National Statuary Hall Collection</a> at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">United States Capitol</a>, donated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_of_Utah" title="State of Utah">State of Utah</a> in 1950;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> and a statue atop the <i><a href="/wiki/This_is_the_Place_Monument" title="This is the Place Monument">This is the Place Monument</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">Salt Lake City</a>.</p>

<p>Young's <a href="/wiki/Teachings_of_Presidents_of_the_Church" title="Teachings of Presidents of the Church">teachings</a> were the 1998–99 course of study in the LDS Church's Sunday <a href="/wiki/Relief_Society" title="Relief Society">Relief Society</a> and <a href="/wiki/Melchizedek_priesthood_(Latter_Day_Saints)" title="Melchizedek priesthood (Latter Day Saints)">Melchizedek priesthood</a> classes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_and_descendants">Family and descendants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigham_Young&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Family and descendants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Brigham_Young%27s_wives" title="List of Brigham Young's wives">List of Brigham Young's wives</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:In_memoriam_brigham_young_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="430" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/In_memoriam_brigham_young_2.jpg/220px-In_memoriam_brigham_young_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/In_memoriam_brigham_young_2.jpg/330px-In_memoriam_brigham_young_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b3/In_memoriam_brigham_young_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:In_memoriam_brigham_young_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Caricature of Young's wives, after his death</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Young was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plural_marriage" title="Plural marriage">polygamist</a>, marrying a total of 55 wives, 54 of them after he converted to Mormonism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-joj_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-joj-50">[50]</a></sup> The policy was difficult for many in the church. Young stated that upon being taught about plural marriage, "It was the first time in my life that I desired the grave."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> By the time of his death, Young had 56 children by 16 of his wives; 46 of his children reached adulthood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-byubio_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-byubio-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sources have varied on the number of Young's wives, due to differences in what scholars have considered to be a "wife".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-joj_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-joj-50">[50]</a></sup> There were 55 women that Young was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sealing_(Latter_Day_Saints)" title="Sealing (Latter Day Saints)">sealed</a> to during his lifetime. While the majority of the sealings were <a href="/wiki/Celestial_marriage" title="Celestial marriage">"for eternity"</a>, some were "for time only". Researchers believe that not all of the 55 marriages were <a href="/wiki/Marriage#Rights_and_obligations" title="Marriage">conjugal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-joj_50-2"><a href="#cite_note-joj-50">[50]</a></sup> Young did not live with a number of his wives or publicly hold them out as wives, which has led to confusion on the number and identities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-joj_50-3"><a href="#cite_note-joj-50">[50]</a></sup> This is in part due to the complexity of how wives were identified in the Mormon society at the time.</p>
<table class="rquote floatright" role="presentation" style="border-collapse:collapse; border-style:none; float:right; margin:0.5em 0.75em; width:33%;">
<tr style="text-align:left; vertical-align:top;">
<td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">“</td>
<td style="padding:0 10px;">Young's ability to keep [dozens of] wives from quarreling and so many children from overwhelming him would in itself prove that [he] must have been a remarkable, not to say a master, diplomat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paul197412_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-paul197412-48">[48]</a></sup></td>
<td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; text-align:right; vertical-align:bottom; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">”</td>

</tr>

<p>Of Young's 55 wives, 21 had never been married before; 16 were widows; six were divorced; six had living husbands; and the marital status of six others are unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-joj_50-4"><a href="#cite_note-joj-50">[50]</a></sup> In 1856, Young built the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lion_House_(Salt_Lake_City,_Utah)" title="Lion House (Salt Lake City, Utah)">Lion House</a> to accommodate his sizable family. This building remains a Salt Lake City landmark, together with the <a href="/wiki/Beehive_House" title="Beehive House">Beehive House</a>, another Young family home. A contemporary of Young wrote: "It was amusing to walk by Brigham Young's big house, a long rambling building with innumerable doors. Each wife has an establishment of her own, consisting of parlor, bedroom, and a front door, the key of which she keeps in her pocket."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> At the time of Young's death, 19 of his wives had predeceased him, he was divorced from ten, and 23 survived him. The status of four was unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-joj_50-5"><a href="#cite_note-joj-50">[50]</a></sup> One of his wives, Zina Huntington Young, served as the third president of the Relief Society. In his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Will_(law)" title="Will (law)">will</a>, Young shared his estate with the 16 surviving wives who had lived with him; the six surviving non-conjugal wives were not mentioned in the will.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-joj_50-6"><a href="#cite_note-joj-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_descendants">Notable descendants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigham_Young&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Notable descendants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Descendants_of_Brigham_Young" title="Descendants of Brigham Young">Descendants of Brigham Young</a></div>
<p>In 1902, 25 years after his death, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The</a></i> <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times"><i>New York Times</i></a> established that Young's direct descendants numbered more than 1,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Some of Young's descendants have become leaders in the LDS Church, while other have become notable for events outside of LDS Church service.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fictional_references">Fictional references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigham_Young&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Fictional references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_literature">In literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigham_Young&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: In literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Florence_Claxton" title="Florence Claxton">Florence Claxton</a>'s graphic novel, <i>The Adventures of a Woman in Search of her Rights</i> (1872), satirizes a would-be emancipated woman whose failure to establish an independent career results in her marriage to Young before she wakes to discover she's been dreaming.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Arthur Conan Doyle">Arthur Conan Doyle</a> based his first <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a> novel, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Study_in_Scarlet" title="A Study in Scarlet">A Study in Scarlet</a></i>, on Mormon history, mentioning Young by name. When asked to comment on the story, which had "provoked the animosity of the Mormon faithful", Doyle noted, "all I said about the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danite_Band" title="Danite Band">Danite Band</a> and the murders is historical so I cannot withdraw that though it is likely that in a work of fiction it is stated more luridly than in a work of history." Doyle's daughter stated: "You know father would be the first to admit that his first Sherlock Holmes novel was full of errors about the Mormons."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">Mark Twain</a> devoted a chapter and much of an appendix to Young in <i><a href="/wiki/Roughing_It" title="Roughing It">Roughing It</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_television">In television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigham_Young&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: In television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Byron_Morrow" title="Byron Morrow">Byron Morrow</a> played Young in a cameo appearance in the <i><a href="/wiki/Death_Valley_Days" title="Death Valley Days">Death Valley Days</a></i> episode, "An Organ for Brother Brigham" (1966). In the storyline, the organ built and guided west to Salt Lake City by Joseph Harris Ridges (1827-1914) of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> becomes mired in the <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>. Wagonmaster Luke Winner (portrayed <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Woodward" title="Morgan Woodward">Morgan Woodward</a>) feels compelled to leave the instrument behind until Ridges finds solid rock under the sand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gregg_Henry" title="Gregg Henry">Gregg Henry</a> depicts Young in the fourth (2014) and fifth (2015) seasons of the TV series <a href="/wiki/Hell_on_Wheels_(TV_series)" title="Hell on Wheels (TV series)"><i>Hell on Wheels</i></a>, a fictional story about the construction of the <a href="/wiki/First_Transcontinental_Railroad" title="First Transcontinental Railroad">First Transcontinental Railroad</a>. As the competing rail lines approach Utah from the east and west coasts, Young supplies Mormon laborers to both railroad companies and negotiates with the railways to have them make Salt Lake City their meeting point. In the Season 5 mid-season finale, "False Prophets," Young's son, Phineas, attempts to murder his father. Persuaded by The Swede, Phineas believed he was the chosen one to go forward to lead the Mormons, instead of his father.</p>
