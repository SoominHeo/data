<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Brig</h1>

<p>A <b>brig</b> is a sailing vessel with two <a href="/wiki/Square_rig" title="Square rig">square-rigged</a> masts. During the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Sail" title="Age of Sail">Age of Sail</a>, brigs were seen as fast and maneuverable and were used as both naval <a href="/wiki/Warship" title="Warship">warships</a> and merchant vessels. They were especially popular in the 18th and early 19th centuries. Brigs fell out of use with the arrival of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam_ship" title="Steam ship">steam ship</a> because they required a relatively large crew for their small size and were difficult to sail into the wind. They are not to be confused with a <a href="/wiki/Brigantine" title="Brigantine">brigantine</a>, which has different rigging (a brigantine has a gaff-rigged mainsail, while a brig has a square mainsail with an additional gaff-rigged spanker behind the mainsail). A brig is distinct from a three-masted <a href="/wiki/Full-rigged_ship" title="Full-rigged ship">ship</a> by virtue of only having two masts.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rigging">Rigging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brig&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Rigging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brig3.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="528" data-file-width="800" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Brig3.png/220px-Brig3.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Brig3.png/330px-Brig3.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Brig3.png/440px-Brig3.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brig3.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A typical brig <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sail_plan" title="Sail plan">sail plan</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Sailing" title="Sailing">sailing</a>, a full-rigged <b>brig</b> is a vessel with two <a href="/wiki/Square_rig" title="Square rig">square rigged</a> <a href="/wiki/Mast_(sailing)" title="Mast (sailing)">masts</a> (fore and main).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sand_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sand-2">[2]</a></sup> The main mast of a brig is the aft one. To improve maneuverability, the mainmast carries a small (<a href="/wiki/Gaff_rig" title="Gaff rig">gaff rigged</a>) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fore-and-aft" title="Fore-and-aft">fore-and-aft</a> sail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NJ_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NJ-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brig sails are named after the masts to which they are attached: the <a href="/wiki/Mainsail" title="Mainsail">mainsail</a>; above that the main <a href="/wiki/Topsail" title="Topsail">topsail</a>; above that the main <a href="/wiki/Topgallant_sail" title="Topgallant sail">topgallant sail</a>; and occasionally a very small sail, called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_sail" title="Royal sail">royal</a>, is above that. Behind the main sail there is a small fore-and-aft sail called the <a href="/wiki/Spanker_(sail)" title="Spanker (sail)">spanker</a> or <a href="/wiki/Boom_(sailing)" title="Boom (sailing)">boom</a> mainsail (it is somewhat similar to the main sail of a <a href="/wiki/Schooner" title="Schooner">schooner</a>). On the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foremast" title="Foremast">foremast</a> is a similar sail, called the <a href="/wiki/Trysail" title="Trysail">trysail</a>. Attached to the respective <a href="/wiki/Yard_(sailing)" title="Yard (sailing)">yards</a> of square-rigged ships are smaller spars, which can be extended, thus lengthening the yard, thus receiving an additional sailing wing on each side. These are called <a href="/wiki/Studding_sail" title="Studding sail">studding sails</a>, and are used with fair and light wind only. The wings are named after the sails to which they are fastened, i.e. the main studding sails, main top studding sails, and the main top gallant studding sails, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Man_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Man-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The brig’s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foremast" title="Foremast">foremast</a> is smaller than the main mast. The fore mast holds a fore sail, fore top sail, fore top gallant sail, and fore royal. Between the fore mast and the <a href="/wiki/Bowsprit" title="Bowsprit">bowsprit</a> are the fore <a href="/wiki/Staysail" title="Staysail">staysail</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jib" title="Jib">jib</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flying_jib" title="Flying jib">flying jib</a>. All the yards are manipulated by a complicated arrangement of cordage named the <a href="/wiki/Running_rigging" title="Running rigging">running rigging</a>. This is opposed to the <a href="/wiki/Standing_rigging" title="Standing rigging">standing rigging</a> which is fixed, and keeps mast and other objects rigid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Man_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Man-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hull_material">Hull material</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brig&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hull material">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A brig is "generally built on a larger scale than a <a href="/wiki/Schooner" title="Schooner">schooner</a>, and may approach the magnitude of a full-sized, three-masted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full_rigged_ship" title="Full rigged ship">ship</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Man_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Man-4">[4]</a></sup> Brigs vary in length between 75 and 165 ft (23–50 m) with tonnages up to 480.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Texas_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Texas-5">[5]</a></sup> Historically, most brigs were made of wood, although some later brigs were built with hulls and masts of steel or iron (such as the brig <i>Bob Allen</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bob_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bob-6">[6]</a></sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NJ_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-NJ-3">[3]</a></sup> A brig made of pine in the 19th century was designed to last for about twenty years (many lasted longer).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NJ_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-NJ-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_the_brig">Development of the brig</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brig&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Development of the brig">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word "brig" has been used in the past as an abbreviation of <a href="/wiki/Brigantine" title="Brigantine">brigantine</a> (which is the name for a two-masted vessel with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foremast" title="Foremast">foremast</a> fully square rigged and her mainmast rigged with both a <a href="/wiki/Fore-and-aft_rig" title="Fore-and-aft rig">fore-and-aft</a> mainsail, square topsails and possibly topgallant sails). The brig actually developed as a variant of the brigantine. Re-rigging a brigantine with two square-rigged masts instead of one gave it greater sailing power. The square-rigged brig's advantage over the fore-and-aft rigged brigantine was "that the sails, being smaller and more numerous, are more easily managed, and require fewer men or 'hands' to work them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Man_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Man-4">[4]</a></sup> The variant was so popular that the term "brig" came to exclusively signify a ship with this type of rigging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pirate_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pirate-7">[7]</a></sup> By the 17th century the British Royal Navy defined "brig" as having two square rigged masts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_usage">Historic usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brig&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Historic usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Brigs were used as small warships carrying about 10 to 18 guns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Texas_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Texas-5">[5]</a></sup> Due to their speed and maneuverability they were popular among pirates (though they were rare among American and Caribbean pirates).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Man_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Man-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pirate_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pirate-7">[7]</a></sup> While their use stretches back before the 17th century, one of the most famous periods for the brig was during the 19th century when they were involved in famous naval battles such as the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lake_Erie" title="Battle of Lake Erie">Battle of Lake Erie</a>. In the early 19th century the brig was a standard cargo ship. It was seen as "fast and well sailing", but required a large crew to handle its rigging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tre_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tre-9">[9]</a></sup> While brigs could not sail into the wind as easily as fore-and-aft–rigged vessels such as schooners, a trait that is common to all square-rigged ships, a skilled brig captain could "manoeuvre it with ease and elegance; a brig could for instance turn around almost on the spot".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> A brig's square-rig also had the advantage over a fore-and-aft–rigged vessel when travelling offshore, in the trade winds, where vessels sailed down wind for extended distances and where "the danger of a sudden <a href="/wiki/Jibe" title="Jibe">jibe</a> was the large schooner-captain's nightmare".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This trait later led to the evolution of the <a href="/wiki/Barquentine" title="Barquentine">barquentine</a>. The need for large crews in relation to their relatively small size led to the decline of the production of brigs. They were replaced in commercial traffic by gaffsail schooners (which needed fewer personnel) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam_boat" title="Steam boat">steam boats</a> (which did not have the windward performance problems of square rigged ships).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tre_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tre-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Telos</i>, built in <a href="/wiki/Bangor,_Maine" title="Bangor, Maine">Bangor, Maine</a> in 1883, was reportedly the last brig to join the American merchant marine, and was "considered to be the finest vessel of her class ever constructed in Maine". She was wrecked on Aves Island, off <a href="/wiki/Bonaire" title="Bonaire">Bonaire</a> in the Caribbean, in 1900.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
