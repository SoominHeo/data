<p><b>Brigitta Christina Scherzenfeldt</b>, as married <b>Bernow</b>, <b>Lindström</b>, <b>Ziems</b>, and <b>Renat</b> (1684 – 4 April 1736), was a <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> <a href="/wiki/Memoir" title="Memoir">memoirist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">weaving</a> <a href="/wiki/Teacher" title="Teacher">teacher</a> who was captured during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Northern_War" title="Great Northern War">Great Northern War</a> and lived as a slave in the <a href="/wiki/Dzungar_Khanate" title="Dzungar Khanate">Dzungar Khanate</a> in <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>. She dictated her memoirs, describing her life as a slave, after her release. Her story is regarded as a unique source of information about life among the Dzungars.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigitta_Scherzenfeldt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A4ckaskog" title="Bäckaskog">Bäckaskog</a> Manor in <a href="/wiki/Scania" title="Scania">Scania</a> in Sweden as the child of the noble <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant" title="Lieutenant">Lieutenant</a> Knut Scherzenfeldt and Brigitta Tranander, she married the military Mats Bernow at the <a href="/wiki/Svea_Life_Guards" title="Svea Life Guards">Life Guards</a> in 1699 and followed him to war in 1700. She mainly lived in <a href="/wiki/Riga" title="Riga">Riga</a>, and when her spouse died in <a href="/wiki/Toru%C5%84" title="Toruń">Thorn</a> in 1703, she married the military officer Johan Lindström. After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Narva_(1704)" title="Battle of Narva (1704)">Battle of Narva</a>, they were both taken to <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a> as prisoners, where she became a widow in 1711. She remarried again in 1712, this time to a lieutenant, Michael Ziems, a <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> who had been taken prisoner of war in by the Russians during his service in the Swedish army; they were subsequently both deported to <a href="/wiki/Tobolsk" title="Tobolsk">Tobolsk</a> in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>.</p>
<p>Ziems, who was clearly not a Swedish subject, joined the service of the <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> army in 1715 to gain their freedom. In 1716, Ziems was a part of the reinforcements sent to the <a href="/wiki/Garrison" title="Garrison">garrison</a> of Ivan Bucholtz at Jarmyn Lake, above the <a href="/wiki/Irtysh_River" title="Irtysh River">Irtysh River</a>, by Governor Matvei Petrovich Gagarin. Scherzenfeldt, as well as several other Swedish and German people in Russian service, was a part of that convoy. At the same time, the garrison was attacked and captured by the Dzungar, who also met and defeated the convoy, killing Michael Ziems during the conflict.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Slavery">Slavery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigitta_Scherzenfeldt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Slavery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scherzenfeldt was captured, abused with iron and ropes, stripped and almost raped, but she defended herself so forcefully that she tore a piece of flesh from the leg of her attacker. The attacker then wished to kill her, but was stopped by a comrade, and she was then taken naked to the Khanate in <a href="/wiki/Ili_Kazakh_Autonomous_Prefecture" title="Ili Kazakh Autonomous Prefecture">Ili</a> with the other survivors and presented to the <a href="/wiki/Khan_(title)" title="Khan (title)">Khan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tsewang_Rabtan" title="Tsewang Rabtan">Tsewang Rabtan</a>, himself; he curiously asked her why she had resisted the rape so forcefully, and when she told him about the customs of her country, he ordered that she should never be sexually attacked in the future. The Khan then gave her as a gift to one of his wives, a Princess from <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>, who gave Scherzenfeldt clothes to wear. The story about the rape attempt is not in her official story, but was told to an English woman, Mrs. Vigor, several years later in Moscow.</p>
<p>Scherzenfeldt was made teacher in <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">weaving</a> and <a href="/wiki/Knitting" title="Knitting">knitting</a>, and soon became appreciated for her knowledge in these crafts and her good manners; she was appointed knitting instructor to the Khan's favorite daughter, Princess Seson, and was soon regarded as more of a lady-in-waiting than a slave; during a period of two years, she was the official representative for the purchases from the Princess' dowry in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yarkent_County" title="Yarkent County">Yarkent County</a> in <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a> in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, where she would have been the first Swedish person until the 1890s. She was also active in making a better life for other slaves belonging to the Dzungars.</p>
<p>Among them was a Swedish man named <a href="/wiki/Johan_Gustaf_Renat" title="Johan Gustaf Renat">Johan Gustaf Renat</a> (born 1682 in Stockholm), the son of <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_the_Netherlands" title="History of the Jews in the Netherlands">Dutch Jewish</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immigrant" title="Immigrant">immigrants</a> who converted nationalities in 1681, and he was then captured during his service in the Swedish army; he became an instructor in the making of cannons and the printing of books, and led an attack during a battle against the <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a>; he also made some of the weaving-looms for Scherzenfeldt's <a href="/wiki/Workshop" title="Workshop">workshops</a>.</p>
<p>Princess Seson wanted Scherzenfeldt to come with her when she was to marry the Khan of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volga" title="Volga">Volga</a> <a href="/wiki/Kalmyks" title="Kalmyks">Kalmyks</a>, but she refused, as she feared she would never again see Sweden if she did so, and instead, she "married" Renat (the marriage never actually took place), and left the court of the Princess.</p>
<p>Shortly after this, in 1727, the Khan, <a href="/wiki/Tsewang_Rabtan" title="Tsewang Rabtan">Tsewang Rabtan</a> died, and the Princess, her mother, and everyone belonging to her court were accused of having poisoned him in order to place Seson's brother on the throne; they confessed, and were tortured and executed.</p>
<p>Scherzenfeldt was the target of many suspicions and intrigues, but survived due to her good sense and great care, and even managed to get the new Khan to agree on her demand that the eighteen Swedish and one hundred and thirty four Russian slaves be released.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_release">The release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brigitta_Scherzenfeldt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1733, Scherzenfeldt and Renat left <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> in the company of a Russian ambassador and twenty Kalmyk or Dzungar slaves, which were given to them upon their departure; six of them were subsequently kept by the Russians, but several others died before arriving in Moscow. They had been allowed to leave only to visit their home country; they were expected to return. In Moscow, she told an English woman, Mrs Vigor, some about her experiences, which were published by Vigor in a book about Russia. When the survivors entered Stockholm in 1734, the three remaining Kalmyks (Altan, Iamakiss and Zara) were baptized to become Anna Catharina, Maria Stina and Sara Greta; they then became maids in the Renat household.</p>
<p>Scherzenfeldt died in Stockholm in 1736. Her Dzungar costume of red silk is now on display in the <a href="/wiki/Livrustkammaren" title="Livrustkammaren">Livrustkammaren</a> in Stockholm.</p>
