<p><b>Bridgestone Corporation</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">æ ªå¼ä¼ç¤¾ããªãã¹ãã³</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Kabushiki-gaisha Burijisuton</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> (<a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Stock_Exchange" title="Tokyo Stock Exchange">TYO</a>: <a class="external text" href="http://www2.tse.or.jp/tseHpFront/StockSearch.do?callJorEFlg=1&amp;method=&amp;topSearchStr=5108" rel="nofollow">5108</a>, <a href="/wiki/OTC_Markets_Group" title="OTC Markets Group">OTC Pink</a>: <a class="external text" href="http://www.otcmarkets.com/stock/BRDCY/quote" rel="nofollow">BRDCY</a>) is a multinational auto and truck parts manufacturer founded in 1931 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shojiro_Ishibashi" title="Shojiro Ishibashi">Shojiro Ishibashi</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">ç³æ©æ­£äºé</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Ishibashi ShÅjirÅ</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> in the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kurume" title="Kurume">Kurume</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fukuoka_Prefecture" title="Fukuoka Prefecture">Fukuoka</a>, Japan. The name <b>Bridgestone</b> comes from a <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">calque</a> translation and transposition of <i>ishibashi</i>, meaning "stone bridge" in Japanese.</p>
<p>As of 2015, the company is the largest manufacturer of tires in the world, slightly ahead of <a href="/wiki/Michelin" title="Michelin">Michelin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bridgestone Group had 141 production facilities in 24 countries as of 2005.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridgestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The very first Bridgestone tire was produced on 9 April 1930, by the Japanese "<a href="/wiki/Tabi" title="Tabi">Tabi</a>" Socks Tyre Division (actually made <a href="/wiki/Jika-tabi" title="Jika-tabi">jika-tabi</a>). One year later on 1 March 1931, the founder, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shojiro_Ishibashi" title="Shojiro Ishibashi">Shojiro Ishibashi</a>, made the "Tabi" Socks Tyre Division independent and established the Bridgestone Tire Co., Ltd. in the city of Kurume, Fukuoka Prefecture. "Bridgestone" was named after the name of the founder, Shojiro Ishibashi (<i>Ishi</i> = Stone, <i>Bashi</i> = Bridge).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asiatimes_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-asiatimes-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Foregoing dependence on European and North American technology, the Bridgestone Tire Co., Ltd. set its eyes on manufacturing tires based largely on Japanese technology. The fledgling company experienced many difficulties in the areas of technology, production, and sales in the early days. Eventually, improvements were achieved in quality and manufacturing processes which led to the business rapidly expanding in domestic and overseas markets.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges_during_and_after_World_War_II">Challenges during and after World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridgestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Challenges during and after World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wartime regulations were in effect throughout Japan, and tires also came under the jurisdiction of these regulations. This resulted in nearly all of the company's output being used to satisfy military demand. 1945 saw the end of armed conflict, but the company was devastated by the war. The Tokyo headquarters was destroyed during an aerial bombing raid, and all overseas assets were lost. Fortunately, the plants in Kurume and <a href="/wiki/Yokohama" title="Yokohama">Yokohama</a> escaped unscathed, and production was able to resume immediately after the war ended. Brushing aside the problems caused by a labour union strike that lasted for forty-six days, the foundations of the company were further reinforced after this.</p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> Bridgestone started manufacturing motorcycles, but its main income was from supplying tires to its rival motorcycle makers such as <a href="/wiki/Honda" title="Honda">Honda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suzuki" title="Suzuki">Suzuki</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yamaha_Motor_Corporation" title="Yamaha Motor Corporation">Yamaha</a> and it was later decided to cease motorcycle manufacturing.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technological_innovation">Technological innovation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridgestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Technological innovation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 1951, Bridgestone was the first company in Japan to begin selling <a href="/wiki/Rayon" title="Rayon">rayon</a> cord tires, and a five-year project to modernize production facilities was started. This year also saw another Bridgestone building opened in <a href="/wiki/Ky%C5%8Dbashi" title="KyÅbashi">KyÅbashi</a>, Tokyo, which contained the Bridgestone Museum. Sales surpassed ten billion yen in 1953, placing Bridgestone at the top of the tire industry in Japan, and celebrations were held to commemorate the 25th anniversary of the company's foundation in Kurume.</p>
<p>The sale of nylon tires was started in 1959, and work forged ahead with the construction of the new Tokyo plant, which was opened in 1960, in order to cope with the fast-expanding market for motorization.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radial_tires_and_overseas_expansion">Radial tires and overseas expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridgestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Radial tires and overseas expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The company issued stock shares and was listed on the stock exchange in 1961. A new system of administration was ushered in by Shojiro Ishibashi as the chairman, and Kanichiro Ishibashi as the president. As part of the transition across to administrative reform, the Deming Plan in honour of <a href="/wiki/W._Edwards_Deming" title="W. Edwards Deming">W. Edwards Deming</a>, which involves overall quality control activities, was adopted, and the company was awarded the prestigious <a href="/wiki/Deming_Prize" title="Deming Prize">Deming Prize</a> in 1968.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Also, additions were built onto the Tokyo plant in 1962 to house the new Technical Centre, and a progressive system of research and development was established. On the product front, 1967 saw the sale of the company's first ever <a href="/wiki/Radial_tire" title="Radial tire">radial tire</a>, the RD10.</p>
<p>Bridgestone's first overseas plant since the end of the war was opened in Singapore in 1965, and production was also commenced in Thailand in 1969. The 1960s for Bridgestone was an era of overseas expansion that also included the establishment of Bridgestone America in the United States in 1967 to act as Bridgestone's USA representative sales branch.</p>
<p>At the start of the period of Japan's economic stagnation, brought about by the first oil shock, the company was placing even more emphasis on establishing its own technology for the manufacture of radial tires, and it was also at this time that further domestic plants were constructed and fitted out. Its Super Filler Radial was placed on the market in 1978, and in 1979 the company introduced the high-performance POTENZA radial tire, from an Italian word for power.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The company was actively engaged in overseas expansion activities at this time. In addition to starting up production in Indonesia and Iran in 1976, it also invested in a Taiwan tire manufacturer and purchased a tire plant and a plant for diversified products in Australia in 1980. The founder, Shojiro Ishibashi, died on 11 September 1976.</p>
<p>On 1 March 1981, the company celebrated its 50th anniversary. At the same time, the company initiated activities to strengthen its home base that supported overseas expansion strategy with the aim of being ranked as one of the world's top three manufacturers of rubber products. New production facilities were also established in Thailand, India, Poland, China, the United States and other countries. The company changed the name from Bridgestone Tyre Co., Ltd. to Bridgestone Corporation in 1984.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Firestone_purchase">Firestone purchase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridgestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Firestone purchase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1988, Bridgestone purchased the <a href="/wiki/Firestone_Tire_and_Rubber_Company" title="Firestone Tire and Rubber Company">Firestone Tire and Rubber Company</a> of Akron, Ohio. Placing considerable financial and personnel resources into rebuilding Firestone after the purchase, Bridgestone achieved surplus annual profits for the year 1992 with BFE (<i>Bridgestone Firestone Europe</i>) and again in 1993 with BFS (<i>Bridgestone Firestone USA</i>). The Firestone Tire and Rubber Company and Bridgestone Tire Company Ltd USA were amalgamated in 1990 and became <i>"Bridgestone Firestone North American Holdings Ltd"</i>. The North American subsidiary of Bridgestone Corporation is now named Bridgestone Americas, Inc. The tire division is Bridgestone Americas Tire Operations, LLC. Bridgestone Americas opened a $100Â million technical center in Akron in 2012.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridgestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bridgestone Australia began as the SA Rubber Mills in 1939. In 1980 Bridgestone took over the Australian plants which were at that time operated by the Uniroyal Tire Company. Bridgestone Australia<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> had a major manufacturing tyre factory in Australia: located in <a href="/wiki/Salisbury,_South_Australia" title="Salisbury, South Australia">Salisbury, South Australia</a> (this plant was eventually decommissioned in April 2011). Bridgestone has State Offices in all states of Australia, and has a large number of retail outlets across the country.</p>
<p>In 2000 Bridgestone Australia Ltd purchased the BANDAG Retreading plant and its operations in Australia. Bandag Manufacturing Pty Limited<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> has 35 franchised dealers across Australia, New Zealand, Fiji, Papua New Guinea and NoumÃ©a. Bandag Manufacturing Pty Limited is a wholly owned subsidiary of Bridgestone Australia Limited, and operates under license to Bandag Incorporated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 2006 Bridgestone purchased Bandag Incorporated, which is now a subsidiary of Bridgestone Corporation.</p>
<p>From the purchase in 1981, The Australian operations of Bridgestone have been run as a publicly listed company on the Australian Stock exchange. Bridgestone Corporation has maintained a majority share holding. As of mid-2007 the Australian operation was delisted from the Australian Stock exchange and became a solely owned Division of Bridgestone Corporation (pending minority shareholder approval as per Australian Corporate Law).</p>
<p>Following the cancellation on Friday, 11 May 2007, of all shares held by minority shareholders, Bridgestone Australia Ltd. became a wholly owned subsidiary of Bridgestone Corporation of Japan. The selective capital reduction and subsequent privatisation which cost $49 million was approved and completed. Bridgestone Australia Ltd was delisted from the ASX on 30 May 2007.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridgestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bridgestone EU has its head office in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>, Belgium, and was set up in 1972. There are 7 production plants in the region and a 32 hectare <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a> centre near Rome, Italy. The company distributes more than 25 million tyres a year through 17 national sales subsidiaries and 2 distributors. They directly employ over 12,000 people with Mr. Tsuda as the CEO.</p>
<p>At present there are national headquarters in the following locations: <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, Austria; <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>, Czech Republic; <a href="/wiki/Hinnerup" title="Hinnerup">Hinnerup</a>, Denmark; <a href="/wiki/Vantaa" title="Vantaa">Vantaa</a>, Finland; <a href="/wiki/Fresnes,_Val-de-Marne" title="Fresnes, Val-de-Marne">Fresnes</a>, France; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bad_Homburg" title="Bad Homburg">Bad Homburg</a>, Germany; <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>, Greece; <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>, Hungary; <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, Ireland; <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, Italy; <a href="/wiki/Moerdijk" title="Moerdijk">Moerdijk</a>, Netherlands; <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>, Norway; <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a>, Poland; <a href="/wiki/Alcochete" title="Alcochete">Alcochete</a>, Portugal; <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>, Spain; <a href="/wiki/Sundsvall" title="Sundsvall">Sundsvall</a>, Sweden; <a href="/wiki/Spreitenbach" title="Spreitenbach">Spreitenbach</a>, Switzerland; and <a href="/wiki/Warwick" title="Warwick">Warwick</a>, UK.</p>
<p>Bridgestone EU runs a continent-wide scheme called Truckpoint wherein fleets can take their vehicles to any Bridgestone approved garage throughout Europe and get Bridgestone specialist work carried out on their tyres.</p>
<p>There are no Bridgestone factories in the UK but there is a technical bay at which tyres returned by dissatisfied customers are inspected in <a href="/wiki/Coventry" title="Coventry">Coventry</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridgestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As part of reinforcement plans, the company purchased a plant in Tennessee from the Firestone Tire and Rubber Company, its first manufacturing plant in North America, and started the production of radial tires for trucks and buses in 1983. Bridgestone also has a steel cord plant in Clarksville, Tenn., named Bridgestone Metalpha. Metalpha is currently ranked as the top provider in the global steel cord market.</p>
<p>In May 1988, a takeover bid of America's No. 2 tire manufacturer, Akron, Ohio-based Firestone, was successful, and Firestone was placed under the Bridgestone umbrella as a subsidiary company. This purchase brought a large number of Firestone global production sites into the Bridgestone organization. These sites included North America, Central and South America, Europe, New Zealand and other locations. Bridgestone also commenced production in Turkey.</p>
<p>Nashville-based Bridgestone Americas, Inc. (BSA) is the U.S. subsidiary of the Bridgestone Corporation. BSA and its subsidiaries develop, manufacture and market Bridgestone, Firestone, and associate brand tires for consumers, automotive and commercial vehicle original equipment manufacturers, and those in the agricultural, forestry and mining industries. The companies also produce air springs, roofing materials, synthetic rubber and industrial fibres and textiles and operate the world's largest chain of automotive tire and service centers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 2015, Bridgestone Americas Inc. signed a deal giving its dealers the option to install digital air calibration machines from Excel Tire Gauge Inc. in their stores. The digital air calibration machines streamline the tire inflation process by automatically inflating or deflating tires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports_sponsorships">Sports sponsorships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridgestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sports sponsorships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formula_racing">Formula racing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridgestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Formula racing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Formula_One_tyres" title="Formula One tyres">Formula One tyres</a></div>
<p>Bridgestone started to invest in motorsport in the 1980s by developing race tires for feeder series like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formula_2" title="Formula 2">Formula 2</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formula_3" title="Formula 3">Formula 3</a>, <a href="/wiki/Formula_Ford" title="Formula Ford">Formula Ford</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Formula_Opel_Lotus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Formula Opel Lotus (page does not exist)">Formula Opel Lotus</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karting" title="Karting">karting</a>.</p>
<p>In order to increase the Firestone subsidiary's brand awareness, <i>Bridgestone Firestone NAH Ltd</i>, re-entered the Firestone brand into <a href="/wiki/Champ_Car" title="Champ Car">CART</a> IndyCar open-wheel racing in 1995 to challenge <a href="/wiki/Goodyear_Tire_and_Rubber_Company" title="Goodyear Tire and Rubber Company">Goodyear</a>. The tires proved better and Goodyear retired for 2000. Since then, Firestone has been the single tire provider for the renamed and disappeared <a href="/wiki/Champ_Car" title="Champ Car">Champ Car</a>, the <a href="/wiki/IndyCar_Series" title="IndyCar Series">IndyCar Series</a> and its feeder series <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firestone_Indy_Lights" title="Firestone Indy Lights">Firestone Indy Lights</a>.</p>

<p>The Japanese company decided to supply tires for Formula One in 1995, backed-up by the CEO Yoichiro Kaizaki, aiming to improve Bridgestone's name value in the European market which was greatly inferior compared with their archrivals, <a href="/wiki/Michelin" title="Michelin">Michelin</a>. Though it was scheduled to enter the championship in the <a href="/wiki/1998_Formula_One_season" title="1998 Formula One season">1998 season</a> at first, this was brought forward to <a href="/wiki/1997_Formula_One_season" title="1997 Formula One season">1997</a> because the engineering section led by <a href="/wiki/Hirohide_Hamashima" title="Hirohide Hamashima">Hirohide Hamashima</a> had quickly advanced development. Thus, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hiroshi_Yasukawa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hiroshi Yasukawa (page does not exist)">Hiroshi Yasukawa</a>, the general manager of Motorsport Department, also made the best use of the experience and networks in Europe since the Bridgestone's European <a href="/wiki/Formula_Two" title="Formula Two">F2</a> era (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1981_European_Formula_Two_season" title="1981 European Formula Two season">1981</a>â<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1984_European_Formula_Two_season" title="1984 European Formula Two season">1984</a>) and constructed logistics for Formula One at once.</p>
<p>The first title was acquired right away in the second year, 1998 by <a href="/wiki/Mika_H%C3%A4kkinen" title="Mika HÃ¤kkinen">Mika HÃ¤kkinen</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McLaren_(racing)" title="McLaren (racing)">McLaren</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_HighPerformanceEngines" title="Mercedes-Benz HighPerformanceEngines">Mercedes</a>. And Bridgestone users took five <a href="/wiki/List_of_Formula_One_World_Drivers%27_Champions" title="List of Formula One World Drivers' Champions">Drivers' championship</a> titles and five <a href="/wiki/List_of_Formula_One_World_Constructors%27_Champions" title="List of Formula One World Constructors' Champions">Constructors' championship</a> titles (<a href="/wiki/1998_Formula_One_season" title="1998 Formula One season">1998</a>, <a href="/wiki/2001_Formula_One_season" title="2001 Formula One season">2001</a>â<a href="/wiki/2004_Formula_One_season" title="2004 Formula One season">2004</a>) for the period that competed with <a href="/wiki/Goodyear_Tire_and_Rubber_Company" title="Goodyear Tire and Rubber Company">Goodyear</a> (<a href="/wiki/1997_Formula_One_season" title="1997 Formula One season">1997</a>â<a href="/wiki/1998_Formula_One_season" title="1998 Formula One season">1998</a>) and Michelin (<a href="/wiki/2001_Formula_One_season" title="2001 Formula One season">2001</a>â<a href="/wiki/2006_Formula_One_season" title="2006 Formula One season">2006</a>). Especially, cooperation with <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Ferrari" title="Scuderia Ferrari">Scuderia Ferrari</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Schumacher" title="Michael Schumacher">Michael Schumacher</a> functioned well in this period.</p>
<p>From 2008 to 2010 Bridgestone was due to be the sole tire supplier to the FIA Formula One World Championship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> However, because Michelin chose to conclude its Formula One tire programme at the end of the <a href="/wiki/2006_Formula_One_season" title="2006 Formula One season">2006 season</a>, all teams used Bridgestone tires from the <a href="/wiki/2007_Formula_One_season" title="2007 Formula One season">2007 season</a> to the <a href="/wiki/2010_Formula_One_season" title="2010 Formula One season">2010 Formula One season</a>.</p>
<p>On 2 November 2009 Bridgestone announced that they will not be renewing their contract to supply tires to Formula One teams after 2010. The company said it was "addressing the impact of the continuing evolution of the business environment".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pirelli" title="Pirelli">Pirelli</a> announced in June 2010 that it would serve as sole supplier for tires in the <a href="/wiki/2011_Formula_One_season" title="2011 Formula One season">2011 season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports_car_and_touring_car_racing">Sports car and touring car racing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridgestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sports car and touring car racing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1980s and 1990s, Bridgestone provided tires to the <a href="/wiki/24_Hours_of_Le_Mans" title="24 Hours of Le Mans">Le Mans</a> sport prototypes of teams <a href="/wiki/Nismo" title="Nismo">Nismo</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TOM%27s" title="TOM's">TOM's</a>, backed by Japanese automobile manufacturers <a href="/wiki/Nissan" title="Nissan">Nissan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toyota" title="Toyota">Toyota</a> respectively. In the early 1990s, Bridgestone expanded to <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-AMG" title="Mercedes-AMG">Mercedes-AMG</a>, which entered the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Tourenwagen_Meisterschaft" title="Deutsche Tourenwagen Meisterschaft">DTM</a> and later Le Mans and the <a href="/wiki/FIA_GT_Championship" title="FIA GT Championship">FIA GT Championship</a>. The brand left international sports car racing in 2000, but remains as one of the main suppliers in the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Super_GT" title="Super GT">Super GT</a> championship.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motorcycling">Motorcycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridgestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Motorcycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2002, Bridgestone entered the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Prix_motorcycle_racing" title="Grand Prix motorcycle racing">Grand Prix motorcycle racing</a>'s main class <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MotoGP" title="MotoGP">MotoGP</a>. From 2009 to 2015, it was the exclusive tire supplier of the championship and reached the milestone of 100 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MotoGP" title="MotoGP">MotoGP</a> victories in 2012. Nine-time World Champion <a href="/wiki/Valentino_Rossi" title="Valentino Rossi">Valentino Rossi</a> was 'Bridgestone Tyre Adviser'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> having won two MotoGP titles on Bridgestone tyres in 2008 and 2009.</p>
<p>In May 2014, Bridgestone announced they would leave Moto GP at the end of the 2015 season. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diversified_products">Diversified products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridgestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Diversified products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The predecessors of Bridgestone began making diversified products<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> in the 1930s, soon after they started making tires. Today, Bridgestone diversified operations encompass automotive components, industrial products, polyurethane foam products, construction materials, parts and materials for electronic equipment, bicycles and sporting goods. Diversified business generates about one-fourth of total sales in the Bridgestone Group.</p>
<p>Automotive parts are an especially large line of business for Bridgestone in diversified operations. Bridgestone supplies automakers with vibration-isolating components, such as engine mounts. Bridgestone also supplies air springs for trucks, automobiles and train carriages. Bridgestone market aluminium wheels and other automotive accessories, too.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_products">Industrial products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridgestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Industrial products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bridgestone's industrial products span a vast range, including polyurethane foam for automotive seats and interiors, for bedding and furniture and for insulation and sealing in appliances and buildings; water hoses; marine hoses for loading and unloading oil tankers; specialty precision hose products, such as wire-blade hose for construction equipment and machine tools; conveyor belts; and rubber tracks for crawler tractors. Additionally, they make <a href="/wiki/Retread" title="Retread">retreaded</a> tires for aircraft. Their corporate headquarters for the Bridgestone Aircraft tire division is located in Miami in the United States.</p>
<p>The Bridgestone product line in construction and civil engineering materials is similarly broad. Bridgestone supply Multi-Rubber Bearings for installing in foundations to protect buildings from earthquakes, inflatable rubber dams for managing waterways (no longer manufactured â 2008), marine fenders for protecting wharves, additives for pavement, waterproofing sheet, rubberized ceiling and roofing materials, panel tanks for storing water, bath fixtures and residential flooring, air and water systems.</p>
<p>Bridgestone's line of products for electronic equipment includes precision rollers for office machines and functional films for solar cells and plasma displays. In sporting goods, Bridgestone market golf balls and clubs and tennis balls and rackets. Bridgestone's line of bicycles is Japan's most extensive â see above information re. Anchor Cycles.</p>

<p>The Bridgestone Cycle Co Ltd<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> originated in 1949. It offers bicycles under its own brand and under the Anchor brand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>At one time, Bridgestone marketed bicycles under the name Kabuki.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The U.S. marketing director of the Bicycle division, <a href="/wiki/Grant_Petersen" title="Grant Petersen">Grant Petersen</a>, developed a reputation for resisting popular trends in the bicycle industry and instead followed his own personal philosophy of building dependable and comfortable bicycles. Peterson applied this approach to <a href="/wiki/Rivendell_Bicycle_Works" title="Rivendell Bicycle Works">Rivendell Bicycle Works</a> after Bridgestone stopped marketing bicycles in the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The company was an innovator in the nascent mountain bike scene, designing mountain bikes with shorter chain-stays and steeper frame angles than then popular. This made it more nimble and a better climber.</p>
<p>Until 1986, Bridgestone models were numbered in multiples of 100, with the higher numbers indicating a higher end bike. The entry level road bike in 1986, for example, may have been the Bridgestone 100, while the Bridgestone 700 would have been a competition level race bike. Beginning in 1987 there was a change, with model designations consisting of two letters followed by a numeral. The letters indicated the type of bike, and the number indicated the position in the product lineup. The numbering was also reversed, with the smaller numbers indicating a higher position in the product line. For example, the RB-1 would have been the top of the line road bike, followed by the RB-2, RB-3, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<p>In Japan, it is known as a manufacturer of utility, mountain and (under the brand name Anchor) road racing bikes.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bridgestone_Aircraft_Tire">Bridgestone Aircraft Tire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridgestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Bridgestone Aircraft Tire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bridgestone Aircraft Tire (USA) is a manufacturer of aircraft tires as well as retread servicing. Bridgestone America's Holdings announced in May 2006 that it would be moving its Miami, Florida aviation operation to its new 160,000-square-foot (15,000Â m<sup>2</sup>) facility located in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mayodan" title="Mayodan">Mayodan</a>, North Carolina, this move is expected to be completed by mid-2007 and will create approximately 95 new jobs. CEO and President of Bridgestone Aircraft Tire (USA) Joe Rayna is quoted as saying; "<i>This move allows us to continue to prepare for a changing market, both in demand and in product mix, New modern aircraft, such as the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_787_Dreamliner" title="Boeing 787 Dreamliner">Boeing 787 Dreamliner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A380" title="Airbus A380">Airbus A380</a>, arrive fitted with new Bridgestone technology aircraft tires, and this new facility will complement that technology</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Bridgestone Aircraft Tire (USA) has been a major supplier of aircraft tires and retreads to the aircraft industry for over 70 years and has sister aviation facilities in Tokyo, Hong Kong and Belgium.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Golf_Products">Golf Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridgestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Golf Products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bridgestone_Golf" title="Bridgestone Golf">Bridgestone Golf</a></div>
<p>Bridgestone Golf is a division of Bridgestone,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and is the brand name under which Bridgestone's Golfing Products are marketed. Bridgestone has been producing golf-related products since 1935. The Golf division of Bridgestone currently produces both clubs, balls and accessories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Along with technical developments in tire technology came breakthroughs in golf ball technology<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> that have led Bridgestone to be the number one <a href="/wiki/Golf_ball" title="Golf ball">golf ball</a> producer in Japan.</p>
<p>Design and production of golf clubs followed in 1972.</p>
<p>Bridgestone has global coverage of its golfing products with major divisions in USA (Bridgestone Golf USA),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Australia (Bridgestone Golf Australia)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and Korea (Sokio Corporation).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bridgestone_e-Reporter">Bridgestone e-Reporter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridgestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Bridgestone e-Reporter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bridgestone e-reporter is a pan-European competition for aspiring young sports journalists, open to all students aged 18â30, who are in full-time education.</p>
<p>Now in its fifth year, Bridgestone e-reporter<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> continues to provide up-and-coming writers with first hand experience, interviewing <a href="/wiki/GP2_Series" title="GP2 Series">GP2</a> drivers and issuing race reports from a European GP2 race weekend.</p>
<p>The 2008 competition was officially launched on 1 February 2008 with the deadline for entries at 12.00 GMT on 31 March 2008. As of 2009, Bridgestone has not held further editions of the eReporter competition. However it may return in a different form in the future.</p>
<p>Bridgestone was the sole tire supplier for the GP2 series, an open-wheeled racing championship that is widely regarded as the feeder series for Formula One.</p>
