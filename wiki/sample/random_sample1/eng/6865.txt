<p>The <b>British Isles</b> are a <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">group of islands</a> off the north-western coast of <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">continental Europe</a> that consist of the islands of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and over six thousand smaller isles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-6">[6]</a></sup> Situated in the North <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a>, the islands have a total area of approximately 315,159 km<sup>2</sup>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-area_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-area-5">[5]</a></sup> and a combined population of just under 70 million. Two <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign states</a> are located on the islands: <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a> (which covers roughly five-sixths of the island with the same name)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland</a>. The British Isles also include three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crown_Dependencies" title="Crown Dependencies">Crown Dependencies</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> and, by tradition, the <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Bailiwick of Jersey</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Bailiwick of Guernsey</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a>, although the latter are not physically a part of the <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The oldest rocks in the group are in the north west of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, Ireland and North Wales and are 2,700 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mya_(unit)" title="Mya (unit)">million years old</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Silurian" title="Silurian">Silurian</a> period the north-western regions <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">collided</a> with the south-east, which had been part of a separate continental landmass. The topography of the islands is modest in scale by global standards. <a href="/wiki/Ben_Nevis" title="Ben Nevis">Ben Nevis</a> rises to an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Summit_(topography)" title="Summit (topography)">elevation</a> of only 1,344 metres (4,409 ft), and <a href="/wiki/Lough_Neagh" title="Lough Neagh">Lough Neagh</a>, which is notably larger than other lakes on the isles, covers 390 square kilometres (151 sq mi). The climate is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperateness" title="Temperateness">temperate</a> <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">marine</a>, with mild winters and warm summers. The <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Current" title="North Atlantic Current">North Atlantic Drift</a> brings significant moisture and raises temperatures 11 °C (20 °F) above the global average for the latitude. This led to a landscape which was long dominated by <a href="/wiki/Temperate_rainforest" title="Temperate rainforest">temperate rainforest</a>, although human activity has since cleared the vast majority of forest cover. The region was re-inhabited after the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a> of <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">Quaternary glaciation</a>, by 12,000 BC when Great Britain was still a <a href="/wiki/Doggerland" title="Doggerland">peninsula of the European continent</a>. Ireland, which became an island by 12,000 BC, was not inhabited until after 8000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Great Britain became an island by 5600 BC.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Hiberni</a> (Ireland), <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Pictish</a> (northern Britain) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Britons_(historical)" title="Britons (historical)">Britons</a> (southern Britain) tribes, all speaking <a href="/wiki/Insular_Celtic_languages" title="Insular Celtic languages">Insular Celtic</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> inhabited the islands at the beginning of the 1st millennium AD. Much of Brittonic-controlled Britain was <a href="/wiki/Roman_conquest_of_Britain" title="Roman conquest of Britain">conquered by the Roman Empire</a> from AD 43. The first <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxons</a> arrived as Roman power waned in the 5th century and eventually dominated the bulk of what is now England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking" title="Viking">Viking</a> invasions began in the 9th century, followed by more permanent settlements and political change—particularly in England. The subsequent <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Norman</a> conquest of England in 1066 and the later <a href="/wiki/Angevin_Empire" title="Angevin Empire">Angevin</a> partial conquest of Ireland from 1169 led to the imposition of a new Norman ruling elite across much of Britain and parts of Ireland. By the <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">Late Middle Ages</a>, Great Britain was separated into the Kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Scotland" title="Kingdom of Scotland">Scotland</a>, while control in Ireland fluxed between <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_Ireland" title="Gaelic Ireland">Gaelic kingdoms</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hiberno-Norman" title="Hiberno-Norman">Hiberno-Norman lords</a> and the English-dominated <a href="/wiki/Lordship_of_Ireland" title="Lordship of Ireland">Lordship of Ireland</a>, soon restricted only to <a href="/wiki/The_Pale" title="The Pale">The Pale</a>. The 1603 <a href="/wiki/Union_of_the_Crowns" title="Union of the Crowns">Union of the Crowns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Acts of Union 1707</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1800" title="Acts of Union 1800">Acts of Union 1800</a> attempted to consolidate Britain and Ireland into a single political unit, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland</a>, with the Isle of Man and the Channel Islands remaining as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crown_Dependencies" title="Crown Dependencies">Crown Dependencies</a>. The expansion of the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> and migrations following the <a href="/wiki/Great_Famine_(Ireland)" title="Great Famine (Ireland)">Irish Famine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Highland_Clearances" title="Highland Clearances">Highland Clearances</a> resulted in the distribution of the islands' population and culture throughout the world and a rapid de-population of Ireland in the second half of the 19th century. Most of Ireland seceded from the United Kingdom after the <a href="/wiki/Irish_War_of_Independence" title="Irish War of Independence">Irish War of Independence</a> and the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Anglo-Irish Treaty</a> (1919–1922), with six counties remaining in the UK as <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.</p>
<p>The term <i>British Isles</i> is <a href="/wiki/British_Isles_naming_dispute" title="British Isles naming dispute">controversial</a> in Ireland,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> where there are objections to its usage due to the association of the word <i>British</i> with Ireland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-postwarculture_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-postwarculture-15">[15]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland" title="Government of Ireland">Government of Ireland</a> does not recognise or use the term<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Written_Answers_-_Official_Terms_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Written_Answers_-_Official_Terms-16">[16]</a></sup> and its embassy in London discourages its use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> As a result, <b>Britain and Ireland</b> is used as an alternative description,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-postwarculture_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-postwarculture-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reformationBI_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-reformationBI-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and <b>Atlantic Archipelago</b> has had limited use among a minority in academia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Acrossthemargins_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Acrossthemargins-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Archipelagicidentities_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Archipelagicidentities-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EnglishIdentity_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-EnglishIdentity-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BritishAtlantic_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-BritishAtlantic-23">[23]</a></sup> while <i>British Isles</i> is still commonly employed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reformationBI_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-reformationBI-18">[18]</a></sup> Within them, they are also sometimes referred to as <i>these islands</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Britain_(place_name)" title="Britain (place name)">Britain (place name)</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Isles_naming_dispute" title="British Isles naming dispute">British Isles naming dispute</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Terminology_of_the_British_Isles" title="Terminology of the British Isles">Terminology of the British Isles</a></div>
<p>The earliest known references to the islands as a group appeared in the writings of sea-farers from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greek</a> colony of <a href="/wiki/Marseille#Prehistory_and_classical_antiquity" title="Marseille">Massalia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-25">[25]</a></sup> The original records have been lost; however, later writings, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Avienus" title="Avienus">Avienus</a>'s <i>Ora maritima</i>, that quoted from the <a href="/wiki/Massaliote_Periplus" title="Massaliote Periplus">Massaliote Periplus</a> (6th century BC) and from <a href="/wiki/Pytheas" title="Pytheas">Pytheas</a>'s <i>On the Ocean</i> (<a href="/wiki/Circa" title="Circa">circa</a> 325–320 BC)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> have survived. In the 1st century BC, <a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">Diodorus Siculus</a> has <i>Prettanikē nēsos</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diodorus.285.21.1.29_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Diodorus.285.21.1.29-27">[27]</a></sup> "the British Island", and <i>Prettanoi</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diodorus.285.21.2.29_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Diodorus.285.21.2.29-28">[28]</a></sup> "the Britons".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> used Βρεττανική (<i>Brettanike</i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strabo.281.4.2.29_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strabo.281.4.2.29-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strabo.284.2.1.29_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strabo.284.2.1.29-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strabo.284.4.1.29_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strabo.284.4.1.29-31">[31]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Marcian_of_Heraclea" title="Marcian of Heraclea">Marcian of Heraclea</a>, in his <i>Periplus maris exteri</i>, used αἱ Πρεττανικαί νῆσοι (<i>the Prettanic Isles</i>) to refer to the islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MarcianPeriplus_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-MarcianPeriplus-32">[32]</a></sup> Historians today, though not in absolute agreement, largely agree that these Greek and Latin names were probably drawn from native <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic-language</a> names for the archipelago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Along these lines, the inhabitants of the islands were called the Πρεττανοί (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Priteni" title="Priteni">Priteni</a></i> or <i>Pretani</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The shift from the "P" of <i>Pretannia</i> to the "B" of <i><a href="/wiki/Britannia" title="Britannia">Britannia</a></i> by the Romans occurred during the time of <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snyder_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-snyder-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Greeks_in_Egypt" title="Greeks in Egypt">Greco-Egyptian</a> scientist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemy" title="Claudius Ptolemy">Claudius Ptolemy</a> referred to the larger island as <i>great Britain</i> (<i>μεγάλης Βρεττανίας - megális Brettanias</i>) and to Ireland as <i>little Britain</i> (<i>μικρής Βρεττανίας - mikris Brettanias</i>) in his work <i><a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">Almagest</a></i> (147–148 AD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In his later work, <i><a href="/wiki/Geography_(Ptolemy)" title="Geography (Ptolemy)">Geography</a></i> (c. 150 AD), he gave these islands the names <i><a href="/wiki/Albion" title="Albion">Alwion</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hibernia" title="Hibernia">Iwernia</a></i>, and <i>Mona</i> (the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> suggesting these may have been names of the individual islands not known to him at the time of writing <i>Almagest</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The name <i>Albion</i> appears to have fallen out of use sometime after the <a href="/wiki/Roman_conquest_of_Britain" title="Roman conquest of Britain">Roman conquest of Great Britain</a>, after which <i>Britain</i> became the more commonplace name for the island called Great Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snyder_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-snyder-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The earliest known use of the phrase <i>Brytish Iles</i> in the English language is dated 1577 in a work by <a href="/wiki/John_Dee" title="John Dee">John Dee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-John_Dee_1577._p._65_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-John_Dee_1577._p._65-39">[39]</a></sup> Today, this name is seen by some as carrying imperialist overtones<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reformationBI_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-reformationBI-18">[18]</a></sup> although it is still commonly used. Other names used to describe the islands include the <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Celtic" title="Anglo-Celtic">Anglo-Celtic</a> Isles</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> <i>Atlantic archipelago</i>, <i>British-Irish Isles</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <i>Britain and Ireland</i>, <i>UK and Ireland</i>, and <i>British Isles and Ireland</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Owing to political and national associations with the word <i>British</i>, the Government of Ireland does not use the term <i>British Isles</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Written_Answers_-_Official_Terms_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Written_Answers_-_Official_Terms-16">[16]</a></sup> and in documents drawn up jointly between the British and Irish governments, the archipelago is referred to simply as "these islands".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cavendish_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-cavendish-44">[44]</a></sup> Nonetheless, British Isles is still the most widely accepted term for the archipelago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cavendish_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-cavendish-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_England" title="Geography of England">Geography of England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Wales" title="Geography of Wales">Geography of Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Scotland" title="Geography of Scotland">Geography of Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Ireland" title="Geography of Ireland">Geography of Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Geography of the United Kingdom">Geography of the United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_the_Isle_of_Man" title="Geography of the Isle of Man">Geography of the Isle of Man</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geography_of_the_Channel_Islands" title="Geography of the Channel Islands">Geography of the Channel Islands</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

The British Isles in relation to the north-west <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Europe" title="Geography of Europe">European</a> <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a>.</div>

</div>
<p>The British Isles lie at the juncture of several regions with past episodes of <a href="/wiki/Tectonics" title="Tectonics">tectonic</a> mountain building. These <a href="/wiki/Orogeny" title="Orogeny">orogenic belts</a> form a complex geology that records a huge and varied span of Earth's history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Of particular note was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caledonian_Orogeny" title="Caledonian Orogeny">Caledonian Orogeny</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> Period, c. 488–444 <a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">Ma</a> and early <a href="/wiki/Silurian" title="Silurian">Silurian</a> period, when the <a href="/wiki/Craton" title="Craton">craton</a> <a href="/wiki/Baltica" title="Baltica">Baltica</a> collided with the <a href="/wiki/Terrane" title="Terrane">terrane</a> <a href="/wiki/Avalonia" title="Avalonia">Avalonia</a> to form the mountains and hills in northern Britain and Ireland. Baltica formed roughly the northwestern half of Ireland and Scotland. Further collisions caused the <a href="/wiki/Variscan_orogeny" title="Variscan orogeny">Variscan orogeny</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Devonian" title="Devonian">Devonian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> periods, forming the hills of <a href="/wiki/Munster" title="Munster">Munster</a>, southwest England, and southern Wales. Over the last 500 million years the land that forms the islands has drifted northwest from around 30°S, crossing the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> around 370 million years ago to reach its present northern latitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>The islands have been shaped by numerous glaciations during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quaternary_Period" title="Quaternary Period">Quaternary Period</a>, the most recent being the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Devensian" title="Devensian">Devensian</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As this ended, the central <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a> was deglaciated and the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> flooded, with sea levels rising to current levels some 4,000 to 5,000 years ago, leaving the British Isles in their current form. Whether or not there was a land bridge between Great Britain and Ireland at this time is somewhat disputed, though there was certainly a single ice sheet covering the entire sea.</p>
<p>The west coasts of Ireland and Scotland that directly face the Atlantic Ocean are generally characterised by long <a href="/wiki/Peninsula" title="Peninsula">peninsulas</a>, and headlands and bays; the internal and eastern coasts are "smoother".</p>
<p>There are about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_the_British_Isles_by_area" title="List of the British Isles by area">136 permanently inhabited islands</a> in the group, the largest two being Great Britain and Ireland. Great Britain is to the east and covers 83,700 sq mi (217,000 km<sup>2</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-islandsarea_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-islandsarea-47">[47]</a></sup> Ireland is to the west and covers 32,590 sq mi (84,400 km<sup>2</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-islandsarea_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-islandsarea-47">[47]</a></sup> The largest of the other islands are to be found in the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a> to the north, <a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">Anglesey</a> and the Isle of Man between Great Britain and Ireland, and the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a> near the coast of France.</p>
<p>The islands are at relatively low altitudes, with central Ireland and southern Great Britain particularly low lying: the lowest point in the islands is <a href="/wiki/Holme,_Cambridgeshire" title="Holme, Cambridgeshire">Holme, Cambridgeshire</a> at −2.75 m (−9.02 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Scottish Highlands</a> in the northern part of Great Britain are mountainous, with <a href="/wiki/Ben_Nevis" title="Ben Nevis">Ben Nevis</a> being the highest point on the islands at 1,343 m (4,406 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Other mountainous areas include Wales and parts of Ireland, however only seven peaks in these areas reach above 1,000 m (3,281 ft). Lakes on the islands are generally not large, although <a href="/wiki/Lough_Neagh" title="Lough Neagh">Lough Neagh</a> in Northern Ireland is an exception, covering 150 square miles (390 km<sup>2</sup>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The largest freshwater body in Great Britain (by area) is <a href="/wiki/Loch_Lomond" title="Loch Lomond">Loch Lomond</a> at 27.5 square miles (71 km<sup>2</sup>), and <a href="/wiki/Loch_Ness" title="Loch Ness">Loch Ness</a>, by volume whilst <a href="/wiki/Loch_Morar" title="Loch Morar">Loch Morar</a> is the deepest freshwater body in the British Isles, with a maximum depth of 310 m (1,017 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gazetteer_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gazetteer-50">[50]</a></sup> There are a number of major rivers within the British Isles. The longest is the <a href="/wiki/River_Shannon" title="River Shannon">Shannon</a> in Ireland at 224 mi (360 km).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The <a href="/wiki/River_Severn" title="River Severn">river Severn</a> at 220 mi (354 km)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> is the longest in Great Britain. The isles have a <a href="/wiki/Temperate_climate" title="Temperate climate">temperate</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_climate" title="Marine climate">marine climate</a>. The <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Current" title="North Atlantic Current">North Atlantic Drift</a> ("Gulf Stream") which flows from the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> brings with it significant moisture and raises temperatures 11 °C (20 °F) above the global average for the islands' latitudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Winters are cool and wet, with summers mild and also wet. Most Atlantic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">depressions</a> pass to the north of the islands, combined with the general <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerly circulation</a> and interactions with the landmass, this imposes an east-west variation in climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora_and_fauna">Flora and fauna</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Fauna_of_Great_Britain" title="Fauna of Great Britain">Fauna of Great Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fauna_of_Ireland" title="Fauna of Ireland">Fauna of Ireland</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trees_of_Britain_and_Ireland" title="Trees of Britain and Ireland">Trees of Britain and Ireland</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Irl-female_red_deer_Killarney.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="728" data-file-width="934" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Irl-female_red_deer_Killarney.jpg/220px-Irl-female_red_deer_Killarney.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Irl-female_red_deer_Killarney.jpg/330px-Irl-female_red_deer_Killarney.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Irl-female_red_deer_Killarney.jpg/440px-Irl-female_red_deer_Killarney.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Irl-female_red_deer_Killarney.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Some female red deer in <a href="/wiki/Killarney_National_Park" title="Killarney National Park">Killarney National Park</a>, Ireland.</div>
</div>

<p>The islands enjoy a mild climate and varied <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soils" title="Soils">soils</a>, giving rise to a diverse pattern of <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a>. Animal and plant life is similar to that of the northwestern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_continent" title="European continent">European continent</a>. There are however, fewer numbers of <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, with Ireland having even less. All native <a href="/wiki/Flora" title="Flora">flora</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">fauna</a> in Ireland is made up of species that migrated from elsewhere in Europe, and Great Britain in particular. The only window when this could have occurred was between the end of <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">the last Ice Age</a> (about 12,000 years ago) and when the land bridge connecting the two islands was flooded by sea (about 8,000 years ago).</p>
<p>As with most of Europe, prehistoric Britain and Ireland were covered with forest and swamp. Clearing began around 6000 BC and accelerated in medieval times. Despite this, Britain retained its primeval forests longer than most of Europe due to a small population and later development of trade and industry, and wood shortages were not a problem until the 17th century. By the 18th century, most of Britain's forests were consumed for shipbuilding or manufacturing charcoal and the nation was forced to import lumber from Scandinavia, North America, and the Baltic. Most forest land in Ireland is maintained by state <a href="/wiki/Afforestation" title="Afforestation">forestation programmes</a>. Almost all land outside urban areas is <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">farmland</a>. However, relatively large areas of forest remain in east and north Scotland and in southeast England. <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">Oak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elm" title="Elm">elm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fraxinus" title="Fraxinus">ash</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beech" title="Beech">beech</a> are amongst the most common trees in England. In Scotland, <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a> are most common. Natural forests in Ireland are mainly oak, ash, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wych_elm" title="Wych elm">wych elm</a>, birch and pine. Beech and <a href="/wiki/Tilia" title="Tilia">lime</a>, though not native to Ireland, are also common there. Farmland hosts a variety of semi-natural vegetation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grasses" title="Grasses">grasses</a> and flowering plants. Woods, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hedgerows" title="Hedgerows">hedgerows</a>, mountain slopes and <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a> host <a href="/wiki/Ericaceae" title="Ericaceae">heather</a>, wild grasses, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gorse" title="Gorse">gorse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bracken" title="Bracken">bracken</a>.</p>
<p>Many larger animals, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf">wolf</a>, bear and the European <a href="/wiki/Moose" title="Moose">elk</a> are today extinct. However, some species such as <a href="/wiki/Red_deer" title="Red deer">red deer</a> are protected. Other small mammals, such as <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">foxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Badger" title="Badger">badgers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hare" title="Hare">hares</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hedgehog" title="Hedgehog">hedgehogs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stoat" title="Stoat">stoats</a>, are very common and the European beaver has been reintroduced in parts of Scotland. <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">Wild boar</a> have also been reintroduced to parts of southern England, following escapes from boar farms and illegal releases. Many rivers contain <a href="/wiki/Otter" title="Otter">otters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">seals</a> are common on coasts. Over 200 species of bird reside permanently and another 200 migrate. Common types are the <a href="/wiki/Common_chaffinch" title="Common chaffinch">common chaffinch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_blackbird" title="Common blackbird">common blackbird</a>, <a href="/wiki/House_sparrow" title="House sparrow">house sparrow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_starling" title="Common starling">common starling</a>; all small birds. Large birds are declining in number, except for those kept for game such as <a href="/wiki/Pheasant" title="Pheasant">pheasant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Partridge" title="Partridge">partridge</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Red_grouse" title="Red grouse">red grouse</a>. Fish are abundant in the rivers and lakes, in particular <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trout" title="Trout">trout</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perch" title="Perch">perch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Esox" title="Esox">pike</a>. Sea fish include <a href="/wiki/Squalidae" title="Squalidae">dogfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sole_(fish)" title="Sole (fish)">sole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pollock" title="Pollock">pollock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bass_(fish)" title="Bass (fish)">bass</a>, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mussels" title="Mussels">mussels</a>, crab and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oysters" title="Oysters">oysters</a> along the coast. There are more than 21,000 species of insects.</p>
<p>Few species of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reptiles" title="Reptiles">reptiles</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphibians" title="Amphibians">amphibians</a> are found in Great Britain or Ireland. Only three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snakes" title="Snakes">snakes</a> are native to Great Britain: the <a href="/wiki/Vipera_berus" title="Vipera berus">common European adder</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natrix_natrix" title="Natrix natrix">grass snake</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Coronella_austriaca" title="Coronella austriaca">smooth snake</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> none are native to Ireland. In general, Great Britain has slightly more variation and native wild life, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weasels" title="Weasels">weasels</a>, <a href="/wiki/European_polecat" title="European polecat">polecats</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wildcats" title="Wildcats">wildcats</a>, most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shrews" title="Shrews">shrews</a>, <a href="/wiki/European_mole" title="European mole">moles</a>, <a href="/wiki/European_water_vole" title="European water vole">water voles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roe_deer" title="Roe deer">roe deer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_toad" title="Common toad">common toads</a> also being absent from Ireland. This pattern is also true for birds and insects. Notable exceptions include the <a href="/wiki/Kerry_slug" title="Kerry slug">Kerry slug</a> and certain species of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wood_lice" title="Wood lice">wood lice</a> native to Ireland but not Great Britain.</p>
<p>Domestic animals include the <a href="/wiki/Connemara_pony" title="Connemara pony">Connemara pony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shetland_pony" title="Shetland pony">Shetland pony</a>, <a href="/wiki/English_Mastiff" title="English Mastiff">English Mastiff</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_wolfhound" title="Irish wolfhound">Irish wolfhound</a> and many varieties of <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span></h2>

<p>The demographics of the British Isles today are characterised by a generally high density of population in England, which accounts for almost 80% of the total population of the islands. In elsewhere on Great Britain and on Ireland, high density of population is limited to areas around, or close to, a few large cities. The largest urban area by far is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greater_London_Urban_Area" title="Greater London Urban Area">Greater London Urban Area</a> with 9 million inhabitants. Other major populations centres include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester_Urban_Area" title="Greater Manchester Urban Area">Greater Manchester Urban Area</a> (2.4 million), <a href="/wiki/West_Midlands_conurbation" title="West Midlands conurbation">West Midlands conurbation</a> (2.4 million), <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire_Urban_Area" title="West Yorkshire Urban Area">West Yorkshire Urban Area</a> (1.6 million) in England,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Greater_Glasgow" title="Greater Glasgow">Greater Glasgow</a> (1.2 million) in Scotland<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Greater_Dublin_Area" title="Greater Dublin Area">Greater Dublin Area</a> (1.1 million) in Ireland.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The population of England rose rapidly during the 19th and 20th centuries whereas the populations of Scotland and Wales have shown little increase during the 20th century, with the population of Scotland remaining unchanged since 1951. Ireland for most of its history comprised a population proportionate to its land area (about one third of the total population). However, since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Irish_Famine" title="Great Irish Famine">Great Irish Famine</a>, the population of Ireland has fallen to less than one tenth of the population of the British Isles. The famine, which caused a century-long population decline, drastically reduced the Irish population and permanently altered the demographic make-up of the British Isles. On a global scale, this disaster led to the creation of an <a href="/wiki/Irish_diaspora" title="Irish diaspora">Irish diaspora</a> that numbers fifteen times the current population of the island.</p>
<p>The linguistic heritage of the British Isles is rich,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> with twelve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Languages" title="Languages">languages</a> from six groups across four branches of the <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European</a> <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">family</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Insular_Celtic_languages" title="Insular Celtic languages">Insular Celtic languages</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goidelic" title="Goidelic">Goidelic</a> sub-group (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_Language" title="Irish Language">Irish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manx_language" title="Manx language">Manx</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" title="Scottish Gaelic language">Scottish Gaelic</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Brittonic_languages" title="Brittonic languages">Brittonic</a> sub-group (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>, Welsh and <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a>, spoken in <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">north-western France</a>) are the only remaining <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic languages</a>—the last of their continental relations becoming extinct before the 7th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">Norman languages</a> of <a href="/wiki/Guern%C3%A9siais" title="Guernésiais">Guernésiais</a>, <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A8rriais" title="Jèrriais">Jèrriais</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarkese" title="Sarkese">Sarkese</a> spoken in the Channel Islands are similar to French. A <a href="/wiki/Cant_(language)" title="Cant (language)">cant</a>, called <a href="/wiki/Shelta" title="Shelta">Shelta</a>, is spoken by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_Traveller" title="Irish Traveller">Irish Travellers</a>, often as a means to conceal meaning from those outside the group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> However, English, sometimes in the form of <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a>, is the dominant language, with few monoglots remaining in the other languages of the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Norn_language" title="Norn language">Norn language</a> of <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a> became extinct around 1880.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_British_Isles" title="History of the British Isles">History of the British Isles</a></div>
<p>At the end of the last <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">ice age</a>, what are now the British Isles were joined to <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">the European mainland</a> as a mass of land extending north west from the modern-day northern coastline of France, Belgium and the Netherlands. Ice covered almost all of what is now Scotland, most of Ireland and Wales, and the hills of northern England. From 14,000 to 10,000 years ago, as the ice melted, sea levels rose separating Ireland from Great Britain and also creating the Isle of Man. About two to four millennia later, Great Britain became separated from the mainland. Britain probably became repopulated with people before the ice age ended and certainly before it became separated from the mainland. It is likely that Ireland became settled by sea after it had already become an island.</p>
<p>At the time of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, about two thousand years ago, various tribes, which spoke <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic dialects</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Insular_Celtic_languages" title="Insular Celtic languages">Insular Celtic</a> group, were inhabiting the islands. The Romans expanded their civilisation to control southern Great Britain but were impeded in advancing any further, building <a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_Wall" title="Hadrian's Wall">Hadrian's Wall</a> to mark the northern frontier of their empire in 122 AD. At that time, Ireland was populated by a people known as <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Hiberni</a>, the northern third or so of Great Britain by a people known as <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a> and the southern two thirds by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Britons_(historical)" title="Britons (historical)">Britons</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alfred_Jewel.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1879" data-file-width="1748" height="236" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Alfred_Jewel.jpg/220px-Alfred_Jewel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Alfred_Jewel.jpg/330px-Alfred_Jewel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Alfred_Jewel.jpg/440px-Alfred_Jewel.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alfred_Jewel.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Alfred Jewel (9th century)</div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxons</a> arrived as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fall_of_Rome" title="Fall of Rome">Roman power waned in the 5th century AD</a>. Initially, their arrival seems to have been at the invitation of the Britons as mercenaries to repulse incursions by the Hiberni and Picts. In time, Anglo-Saxon demands on the British became so great that they came to culturally dominate the bulk of southern Great Britain, though recent genetic evidence suggests Britons still formed the bulk of the population. This dominance creating what is now England and leaving culturally British enclaves only in <a href="/wiki/Hen_Ogledd" title="Hen Ogledd">the north of what is now England</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> and what is now known as Wales. Ireland had been unaffected by the Romans except, significantly, having been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christianised" title="Christianised">Christianised</a>, traditionally by the Romano-Briton, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Patrick" title="Saint Patrick">Saint Patrick</a>. As Europe, including Britain, descended into turmoil following the collapse of Roman civilisation, an era known as <a href="/wiki/Dark_Ages_(historiography)" title="Dark Ages (historiography)">the Dark Ages</a>, Ireland entered a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_age" title="Golden age">golden age</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-Scottish_mission" title="Hiberno-Scottish mission">responded with missions</a> (first to Great Britain and then to the continent), the founding of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monasteries" title="Monasteries">monasteries</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universities" title="Universities">universities</a>. These were later joined by <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_mission" title="Anglo-Saxon mission">Anglo-Saxon missions</a> of a similar nature.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking" title="Viking">Viking</a> invasions began in the 9th century, followed by more permanent settlements, particularly along the east coast of Ireland, the west coast of modern-day Scotland and the Isle of Man. Though the Vikings were eventually neutralised in Ireland, their influence remained in the cities of <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cork_(city)" title="Cork (city)">Cork</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limerick" title="Limerick">Limerick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waterford" title="Waterford">Waterford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wexford" title="Wexford">Wexford</a>. England however was slowly conquered around the turn of the first millennium AD, and eventually became a feudal possession of <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>. The relations between the descendants of Vikings in England and counterparts in <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>, in northern France, lay at the heart of a series of events that led to the <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Norman</a> <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">conquest of England in 1066</a>. The remnants of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Normandy" title="Duchy of Normandy">Duchy of Normandy</a>, which conquered England, remain associated to the English Crown as the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a> to this day. A century later the marriage of the future <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II of England</a> to <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_of_Aquitaine" title="Eleanor of Aquitaine">Eleanor of Aquitaine</a> created the <a href="/wiki/Angevin_Empire" title="Angevin Empire">Angevin Empire</a>, partially under the <a href="/wiki/List_of_French_monarchs" title="List of French monarchs">French Crown</a>. At the invitation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dermot_MacMurrough" title="Dermot MacMurrough">a provincial king</a> and under <a href="/wiki/Laudabiliter" title="Laudabiliter">the authority of</a> <a href="/wiki/Pope_Adrian_IV" title="Pope Adrian IV">Pope Adrian IV</a> (the only Englishman to be elected pope), the <a href="/wiki/Norman_invasion_of_Ireland" title="Norman invasion of Ireland">Angevins invaded Ireland</a> in 1169. Though initially intended to be kept as an independent kingdom, the failure of <a href="/wiki/Ruaidr%C3%AD_Ua_Conchobair" title="Ruaidrí Ua Conchobair">the Irish High King</a> to ensure the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Windsor_(1175)" title="Treaty of Windsor (1175)">Treaty of Windsor</a> led Henry II, as King of England, to rule as effective monarch under the title of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_of_Ireland" title="Lord of Ireland">Lord of Ireland</a>. This title was granted to his younger son but when Henry's heir unexpectedly died the title of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_England" title="King of England">King of England</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_of_Ireland" title="Lord of Ireland">Lord of Ireland</a> became entwined in one person.</p>

<p>By the <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">Late Middle Ages</a>, Great Britain was separated into the Kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Scotland" title="Kingdom of Scotland">Scotland</a>. Power in Ireland fluxed between <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_Ireland" title="Gaelic Ireland">Gaelic kingdoms</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hiberno-Norman" title="Hiberno-Norman">Hiberno-Norman lords</a> and the English-dominated <a href="/wiki/Lordship_of_Ireland" title="Lordship of Ireland">Lordship of Ireland</a>. A similar situation existed in the <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Wales" title="Principality of Wales">Principality of Wales</a>, which was slowly being annexed into the Kingdom of England by a series of laws. During the course of the 15th century, the Crown of England would assert a claim to the Crown of France, thereby also releasing the King of England as from being <a href="/wiki/Vassal" title="Vassal">vassal</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_France" title="King of France">King of France</a>. In 1534, King Henry VIII, at first having been a strong defender of Roman Catholicism in the face of the Reformation, separated from the Roman Church after failing to secure a divorce from the Pope. His response was to place the King of England as "the only Supreme Head in Earth of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>", thereby removing the authority of the Pope from the affairs of the English Church. Ireland, which had been held by the King of England as Lord of Ireland, but which strictly speaking had been a feudal possession of the Pope since the Norman invasion was declared <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">a separate kingdom</a> in personal union with England.</p>
<p>Scotland, meanwhile had remained an independent Kingdom. In 1603, that changed when the King of Scotland <a href="/wiki/Union_of_the_Crowns" title="Union of the Crowns">inherited the Crown of England</a>, and consequently the Crown of Ireland also. The subsequent 17th century was one of political upheaval, religious division and war. English colonialism in Ireland of the 16th century was extended by large-scale Scottish and English colonies in <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a>. Religious division heightened and the king in England came into conflict with parliament over his tolerance towards Catholicism. The resulting <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_of_the_Three_Kingdoms" title="War of the Three Kingdoms">War of the Three Kingdoms</a> led to a <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary_republic" title="Revolutionary republic">revolutionary republic</a> in England. Ireland, largely Catholic was mainly loyal to the king. Following defeat to the parliaments army, large scale land distributions from loyalist Irish nobility to English commoners in the service of the parliamentary army created a new <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Ascendancy" title="Protestant Ascendancy">Ascendancy</a> class which obliterated the remnants of Old English (Hiberno-Norman) and Gaelic Irish nobility in Ireland. The new ruling class was Protestant and English, whilst the populace was largely Catholic and Irish. This theme would influence Irish politics for centuries to come. When the monarchy was restored in England, the king found it politically impossible to restore the lands of former land-owners in Ireland. The "<a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution" title="Glorious Revolution">Glorious Revolution</a>" of 1688 repeated similar themes: a Catholic king pushing for religious tolerance in opposition to a Protestant parliament in England. The king's army was defeated at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Boyne" title="Battle of the Boyne">Battle of the Boyne</a> and at the militarily crucial <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Aughrim" title="Battle of Aughrim">Battle of Aughrim</a> in Ireland. Resistance held out, eventually forcing the guarantee of religious tolerance in the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Limerick" title="Treaty of Limerick">Treaty of Limerick</a>. However, the terms were never honoured and a new monarchy was installed.</p>
<p>The Kingdoms of England and Scotland were <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">unified in 1707</a> creating the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Kingdom of Great Britain</a>. Following an attempted republican <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1798_Rebellion" title="1798 Rebellion">revolution in Ireland in 1798</a>, the Kingdoms of Ireland and Great Britain were <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1800" title="Acts of Union 1800">unified in 1801</a>, creating the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom</a>. The Isle of Man and the Channel Islands remaining outside of the United Kingdom but with their ultimate good governance being the responsibility of the British Crown (effectively the British government). Although, the colonies of North America that would become the United States of America were lost by the start of the 19th century, the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> expanded rapidly elsewhere. A century later it would cover one third of the globe. Poverty in the United Kingdom remained desperate however and industrialisation in England led to terrible condition for the working class. Mass migrations following the <a href="/wiki/Great_Famine_(Ireland)" title="Great Famine (Ireland)">Irish Famine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Highland_Clearances" title="Highland Clearances">Highland Clearances</a> resulted in the distribution of the islands' population and culture throughout the world and a rapid de-population of Ireland in the second half of the 19th century. Most of Ireland seceded from the United Kingdom after the <a href="/wiki/Irish_War_of_Independence" title="Irish War of Independence">Irish War of Independence</a> and the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Anglo-Irish Treaty</a> (1919–1922), with the six counties that formed <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> remaining as an autonomous region of the UK.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Politics_in_the_British_Isles" title="Politics in the British Isles">Politics in the British Isles</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations" title="Ireland–United Kingdom relations">Ireland–United Kingdom relations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Politics of the United Kingdom">Politics of the United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Politics of the Republic of Ireland">Politics of the Republic of Ireland</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ireland-Isle_of_Man_relations" title="Ireland-Isle of Man relations">Ireland-Isle of Man relations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_the_Isle_of_Man" title="Politics of the Isle of Man">Politics of the Isle of Man</a>, <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Jersey" title="Politics of Jersey">Politics of Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Guernsey" title="Politics of Guernsey">Politics of Guernsey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Alderney" title="Politics of Alderney">Politics of Alderney</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Politics_of_Sark" title="Politics of Sark">Politics of Sark</a></div>

<p>There are two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sovereign_states" title="Sovereign states">sovereign states</a> in the isles: <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Northern_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland">United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland</a>. Ireland, sometimes called <i>the Republic of Ireland</i>, governs five sixths of the island of Ireland, with the remainder of the island forming <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. Northern Ireland is a part of the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland, usually shortened to simply <i>the United Kingdom</i>, which governs the remainder of the archipelago with the exception of the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a>. The Isle of Man and the two states of the Channel Islands, <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bailiwick_of_Guernsey" title="Bailiwick of Guernsey">Guernsey</a>, are known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crown_Dependencies" title="Crown Dependencies">Crown Dependencies</a>. They exercise constitutional rights of self-government and judicial independence;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gov.je_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-gov.je-61">[61]</a></sup> responsibility for international representation rests largely upon the UK (in consultation with the respective governments); and responsibility for defence is reserved by the UK. The United Kingdom is made up of four <a href="/wiki/Counties_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Counties of the United Kingdom">constituent parts</a>: England, Scotland and Wales, forming Great Britain, and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> in the north-east of the island of Ireland. Of these, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland have "<a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolved</a>" governments meaning that they have their own parliaments/assemblies and are self-governing with respect to certain areas set down by law. For <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judicial" title="Judicial">judicial</a> purposes, Scotland, Northern Ireland and <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a> (the latter being one entity) form separate legal jurisdiction, with there being no single law for the UK as a whole.</p>
<p>Ireland, the United Kingdom and the three Crown Dependencies are all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parliamentary_democracies" title="Parliamentary democracies">parliamentary democracies</a>, with their own separate parliaments. All parts of the United Kingdom return <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" title="Member of Parliament">members</a> to <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">parliament in London</a>. In addition to this, voters in Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland return members to a <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">parliament in Edinburgh</a> and to assemblies in <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">Cardiff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Belfast</a> respectively. Governance in the norm is by majority rule, however, Northern Ireland uses a system of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_sharing" title="Power sharing">power sharing</a> whereby <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_Ireland" title="Unionism in Ireland">unionists</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_nationalist" title="Irish nationalist">nationalists</a> share executive posts proportionately and where the assent of both groups are required for the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a> to make certain decisions. (In the context of Northern Ireland, unionists are those who want Northern Ireland to remain a part of the United Kingdom and nationalists are those who want Northern Ireland join with the rest of Ireland.) The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_monarch" title="British monarch">British monarch</a> is the head of state for the United Kingdom while in the Republic of Ireland the head of state is the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">President of Ireland</a>.</p>
<p>Ireland and the United Kingdom are both part of the European Union (EU). The Crown Dependencies are not a part of the EU however do participate in certain aspects that were negotiated as a part of the UK's accession to the EU.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gov.je_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-gov.je-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Neither the United Kingdom or Ireland are part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schengen_area" title="Schengen area">Schengen area</a>, that allow passport-free travel between EU members states. However, since the <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_Ireland" title="Partition of Ireland">partition of Ireland</a>, an informal free-travel area had existed across the region. In 1997, this area required formal recognition during the course of negotiations for the <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam_Treaty" title="Amsterdam Treaty">Amsterdam Treaty</a> of the European Union and is now known as the <a href="/wiki/Common_Travel_Area" title="Common Travel Area">Common Travel Area</a>.</p>
<p>Reciprocal arrangements allow British and Irish citizens full voting rights in the two states. Exceptions to this are presidential elections and <a href="/wiki/Amendments_to_the_Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Amendments to the Constitution of Ireland">constitutional referendums</a> in the Republic of Ireland, for which there is no comparable franchise in the other states. In the United Kingdom, these pre-date European Union law, and in both jurisdictions go further than that required by European Union law. Other EU nationals may only vote in local and European Parliament elections while resident in either the UK or Ireland. In 2008, a <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Justice_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Justice (United Kingdom)">UK Ministry of Justice</a> report investigating how to strengthen the British sense of citizenship proposed to end this arrangement arguing that, "the right to vote is one of the hallmarks of the political status of citizens; it is not a means of expressing closeness between countries."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, some civil bodies are organised throughout the islands as a whole. For example the <a href="/wiki/Samaritans_(charity)" title="Samaritans (charity)">Samaritans</a>, which is deliberately organised without regard to national boundaries on the basis that a service which is not political or religious should not recognise sectarian or political divisions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RNLI" title="RNLI">RNLI</a>, the life boats service, is also organised throughout the islands as a whole, covering the waters of the United Kingdom, Ireland, the Isle of Man, and the Channel Islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Peace_Process" title="Northern Ireland Peace Process">Northern Ireland Peace Process</a> has led to a number of unusual arrangements between the Republic of Ireland, Northern Ireland and the United Kingdom. For example, citizens of Northern Ireland are entitled to the choice of Irish or British citizenship or both and the Governments of <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland" title="Government of Ireland">Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> consult on matters not devolved to the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Executive" title="Northern Ireland Executive">Northern Ireland Executive</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Executive" title="Northern Ireland Executive">Northern Ireland Executive</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland" title="Government of Ireland">Government of Ireland</a> also meet as the <a href="/wiki/North/South_Ministerial_Council" title="North/South Ministerial Council">North/South Ministerial Council</a> to develop policies common across the island of Ireland. These arrangements were made following the 1998 <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British.E2.80.93Irish_Council">British–Irish Council</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Council" title="British–Irish Council">British–Irish Council</a></div>
<p>Another body established under the Good Friday Agreement, the British–Irish Council, is made up of all of the states and territories of the British Isles. The <a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Parliamentary_Assembly" title="British–Irish Parliamentary Assembly">British–Irish Parliamentary Assembly</a> (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>Tionól Pharlaiminteach na Breataine agus na hÉireann</i></span>) predates the British–Irish Council and was established in 1990. Originally it comprised 25 members of the <a href="/wiki/Oireachtas" title="Oireachtas">Oireachtas</a>, the Irish parliament, and 25 members of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">parliament of the United Kingdom</a>, with the purpose of building mutual understanding between members of both legislatures. Since then the role and scope of the body has been expanded to include representatives from the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a>, the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Jersey" title="States of Jersey">States of Jersey</a>, the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Guernsey" title="States of Guernsey">States of Guernsey</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Tynwald" title="High Court of Tynwald">High Court of Tynwald</a> (Isle of Man).</p>
<p>The Council does not have executive powers but meets biannually to discuss issues of mutual importance. Similarly, the Parliamentary Assembly has no legislative powers but investigates and collects witness evidence from the public on matters of mutual concern to its members. Reports on its findings are presented to the Governments of Ireland and the United Kingdom. During the February 2008 meeting of the British–Irish Council, it was agreed to set up a standing secretariat that would serve as a permanent 'civil service' for the Council.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Leading on from developments in the British–Irish Council, the chair of the British–Irish Inter-Parliamentary Assembly, <a href="/wiki/Niall_Blaney" title="Niall Blaney">Niall Blaney</a>, has suggested that the body should shadow the British–Irish Council's work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>

<p>The United Kingdom and Ireland have separate media, although British television, newspapers and magazines are widely available in Ireland,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> giving people in Ireland a high level of familiarity with cultural matters in the United Kingdom. Irish newspapers are also available in the UK, and Irish state and private television is widely available in Northern Ireland.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Certain reality TV shows have embraced the whole of the islands, for example <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_X_Factor_(UK)" title="The X Factor (UK)">The X Factor</a></i>, seasons 3, 4 and 7 of which featured auditions in Dublin and were open to Irish voters, whilst the show previously known as <i>Britain's Next Top Model</i> became <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Britain_and_Ireland%27s_Next_Top_Model" title="Britain and Ireland's Next Top Model">Britain and Ireland's Next Top Model</a></i> in 2011. A few cultural events are organised for the island group as a whole. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Costa_Book_Awards" title="Costa Book Awards">Costa Book Awards</a> are awarded to authors resident in the UK or Ireland. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercury_Music_Prize" title="Mercury Music Prize">Mercury Music Prize</a> is handed out every year to the best album from a British or Irish musician or group.</p>
<p>Many globally popular sports had modern rules codified in the British Isles, including <a href="/wiki/History_of_golf" title="History of golf">golf</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_association_football" title="History of association football">association football</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_cricket" title="History of cricket">cricket</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rugby_football" title="Rugby football">rugby</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_snooker" title="History of snooker">snooker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Darts" title="Darts">darts</a>, as well as many minor sports such as <a href="/wiki/Croquet" title="Croquet">croquet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bowls" title="Bowls">bowls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pitch_and_putt" title="Pitch and putt">pitch and putt</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_water_polo" title="History of water polo">water polo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_handball" title="Gaelic handball">handball</a>. A number of sports are popular throughout the British Isles, the most prominent of which is <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">association football</a>. While this is organised separately in different national associations, leagues and national teams, even within the UK, it is a common passion in all parts of the islands. <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">Rugby union</a> is also widely enjoyed across the islands with four national teams from <a href="/wiki/England_national_rugby_union_team" title="England national rugby union team">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland_national_rugby_union_team" title="Ireland national rugby union team">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland_national_rugby_union_team" title="Scotland national rugby union team">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales_national_rugby_union_team" title="Wales national rugby union team">Wales</a>. The <a href="/wiki/British_and_Irish_Lions" title="British and Irish Lions">British and Irish Lions</a> is a team chosen from each national team and undertakes tours of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_hemisphere" title="Southern hemisphere">southern hemisphere</a> rugby playing nations every four years. Ireland play as a united team, represented by players from both Northern Ireland and the Republic. These national rugby teams play each other each year for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triple_Crown_(Rugby_Union)" title="Triple Crown (Rugby Union)">Triple Crown</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/Six_Nations_Championship" title="Six Nations Championship">Six Nations Championship</a>. Also since 2001 the professional club teams of Ireland, Scotland, Wales and Italy compete against each other in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinness_Pro12" title="Guinness Pro12">Guinness Pro12</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ryder_Cup" title="Ryder Cup">Ryder Cup</a> in <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a> was originally played between a United States team and a team representing Great Britain and Ireland. From 1979 onwards this was expanded to include the whole of Europe.</p>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Heathrow_Airport" title="London Heathrow Airport">London Heathrow Airport</a> is Europe's busiest <a href="/wiki/Airport" title="Airport">airport</a> in terms of passenger traffic and the Dublin-London route was once the busiest air route in Europe,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> and it remains the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World%27s_busiest_passenger_air_routes#Europe" title="World's busiest passenger air routes">busiest route</a> out of Heathrow. The <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> and the southern <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> are the busiest seaways in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Channel_Tunnel" title="Channel Tunnel">Channel Tunnel</a>, opened in 1994, links Great Britain to France and is the second-longest rail tunnel in the world.</p>
<p>The idea of building a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_Sea_tunnel" title="Irish Sea tunnel">tunnel under the Irish Sea</a> has been raised since 1895,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> when it was first investigated. Several potential Irish Sea tunnel projects have been proposed, most recently the <i>Tusker Tunnel</i> between the ports of <a href="/wiki/Rosslare_Europort" title="Rosslare Europort">Rosslare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fishguard" title="Fishguard">Fishguard</a> proposed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Institute_of_Engineers_of_Ireland" title="The Institute of Engineers of Ireland">The Institute of Engineers of Ireland</a> in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> A rail tunnel was proposed in 1997 on a different route, between <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holyhead" title="Holyhead">Holyhead</a>, by British engineering firm Symonds. Either tunnel, at 50 mi (80 km), would be by far the longest in the world, and would cost an estimated £15 billion or €20 billion. A proposal in 2007,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> estimated the cost of building a bridge from <a href="/wiki/County_Antrim" title="County Antrim">County Antrim</a> in Northern Ireland to <a href="/wiki/Galloway" title="Galloway">Galloway</a> in Scotland at £3.5bn (€5bn).</p>
