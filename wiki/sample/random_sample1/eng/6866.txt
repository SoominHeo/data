<p><b>Brittle</b> is a type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Confection" title="Confection">confection</a> consisting of flat broken pieces of hard <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> candy embedded with nuts such as <a href="/wiki/Pecan" title="Pecan">pecans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Almond" title="Almond">almonds</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanuts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It has many variations around the world, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pasteli" title="Pasteli">pasteli</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <i>croquant</i> in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gozinaki" title="Gozinaki">gozinaki</a> in <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gachak&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gachak (page does not exist)">gachak</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Punjab" title="Punjab">Punjab</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>), <a href="/wiki/Chikki" title="Chikki">chikki</a> in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <i>kotkoti</i> in <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In parts of the Middle East, brittle is made with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pistachios" title="Pistachios">pistachios</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> while many Asian countries use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sesame_seed" title="Sesame seed">sesame seeds</a> and peanuts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Peanut brittle is the most popular brittle recipe in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The term brittle first appears in print in 1892, though the candy itself has been around for much longer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Traditionally, a mixture of sugar and water is heated to the <a href="/wiki/Candy_making#Sugar_stages" title="Candy making">hard crack stage</a> corresponding to a temperature of approximately 300 °F (149 °C), although some recipes also call for ingredients such as corn syrup and salt in the first step.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Nuts are mixed with the <a href="/wiki/Caramelization" title="Caramelization">caramelized</a> sugar. At this point spices, <a href="/wiki/Leavening_agent" title="Leavening agent">leavening agents</a>, and often <a href="/wiki/Peanut_butter" title="Peanut butter">peanut butter</a> or butter are added. The hot candy is poured out onto a flat surface for cooling, traditionally a granite or marble slab. The hot candy may be troweled to uniform thickness. When the brittle cools, it is broken into pieces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
