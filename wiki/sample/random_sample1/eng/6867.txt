<p><b>British American Tobacco plc</b> (<b>BAT</b>) is a British <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational</a> tobacco company headquartered in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is one of the world’s five largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tobacco_companies" title="Tobacco companies">tobacco companies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>BAT has a market-leading position in over 50 countries and operations in around 180 countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whoweare_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-whoweare-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Its four largest-selling brands are <a href="/wiki/Dunhill_(cigarette)" title="Dunhill (cigarette)">Dunhill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lucky_Strike" title="Lucky Strike">Lucky Strike</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kent_(cigarette)" title="Kent (cigarette)">Kent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pall_Mall_(cigarette)" title="Pall Mall (cigarette)">Pall Mall</a>, with others including <a href="/wiki/Kool_(cigarette)" title="Kool (cigarette)">Kool</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benson_%26_Hedges" title="Benson &amp; Hedges">Benson &amp; Hedges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rothmans_International" title="Rothmans International">Rothmans</a>.</p>
<p>BAT has a primary listing on the <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">London Stock Exchange</a> and is a constituent of the <a href="/wiki/FTSE_100_Index" title="FTSE 100 Index">FTSE 100 Index</a>. It has a secondary listing on the <a href="/wiki/JSE_Limited" title="JSE Limited">Johannesburg Stock Exchange</a> and on the <a href="/wiki/Nairobi_Securities_Exchange" title="Nairobi Securities Exchange">Nairobi Securities Exchange</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_American_Tobacco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1902_to_2000">1902 to 2000</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_American_Tobacco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1902 to 2000">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BuckDuke.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="344" height="203" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/BuckDuke.jpg/140px-BuckDuke.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/BuckDuke.jpg/210px-BuckDuke.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/BuckDuke.jpg/280px-BuckDuke.jpg 2x" width="140"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BuckDuke.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/James_Buchanan_Duke" title="James Buchanan Duke">James Buchanan Duke</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The company was formed in 1902, when the United Kingdom's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_Tobacco_Company" title="Imperial Tobacco Company">Imperial Tobacco Company</a> and the <a href="/wiki/American_Tobacco_Company" title="American Tobacco Company">American Tobacco Company</a> of the United States agreed to form a joint venture, the British-American Tobacco Company Ltd.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-earlyyears_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-earlyyears-7">[7]</a></sup> The parent companies agreed not to trade in each other's domestic territory and to assign trademarks, export businesses and overseas subsidiaries to the joint venture. <a href="/wiki/James_Buchanan_Duke" title="James Buchanan Duke">James Buchanan Duke</a> became its chairman and the British American Tobacco business began life in countries as diverse as Canada, China, Germany, South Africa, New Zealand and Australia, but not in the United Kingdom or in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-earlyyears_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-earlyyears-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In China, BAT inherited a factory in the <a href="/wiki/Pudong" title="Pudong">Pudong</a> district of Shanghai from <a href="/wiki/W.D._%26_H.O._Wills" title="W.D. &amp; H.O. Wills">W.D. &amp; H.O. Wills</a>, one of the precursor companies of Imperial Tobacco.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BERKSHIRE_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-BERKSHIRE-8">[8]</a></sup> Under the management of James Augustus Thomas from <a href="/wiki/Rockingham,_North_Carolina" title="Rockingham, North Carolina">Rockingham, North Carolina</a>, USA, by 1919 the Shanghai factory was producing more than 243 million cigarettes per week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DUKE_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-DUKE-9">[9]</a></sup> Thomas worked closely with the local Wing Tai Vo Tobacco Company, which developed into BAT's principal Chinese partner after its success with the "Ruby Queen" cigarette brand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1911 the American Tobacco Company sold its share of the company. Imperial Tobacco gradually reduced its shareholding, but it was not until 1980 that it divested its remaining interests in the company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-earlyyears_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-earlyyears-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>At its peak, in 1937 BAT manufactured and distributed 55 billion cigarettes in China. The company's assets were seized by the Japanese in 1941 following their <a href="/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War" title="Second Sino-Japanese War">1937 invasion</a>. In 1953 the company was ejected from China following the foundation of the People's Republic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BERKSHIRE_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-BERKSHIRE-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1976 the group companies were reorganised under a new holding company, <b>B.A.T Industries</b>. In 1994 BAT acquired its former parent, American Tobacco Company (though reorganised after anti-trust proceedings). This brought the <a href="/wiki/Lucky_Strike" title="Lucky Strike">Lucky Strike</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pall_Mall_(cigarette)" title="Pall Mall (cigarette)">Pall Mall</a> brands into BAT's portfolio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1999 it merged with <a href="/wiki/Rothmans_International" title="Rothmans International">Rothmans International</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> which included a share in a factory in Burma. This made it the target of criticism from human rights groups. It sold its share of the factory on 6 November 2003 after an "exceptional request" from the British government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2002, BAT lost a lawsuit about the right to sell cigarettes under the <a href="/wiki/Marlboro_(cigarette)" title="Marlboro (cigarette)">Marlboro</a> brand name in the UK. It had acquired Rothmans, which had previously bought a licence to use the name from Philip Morris. Philip Morris' attorneys invoked a get-out clause for the case of a major change of ownership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003, BAT acquired <b>Ente Tabacchi Italiani (ETI)</b> S.p.A, Italy's state tobacco company. The important acquisition would elevate BAT to the number two position in Italy, the second largest tobacco market in the European Union. The scale of the enlarged operations would bring significant opportunities to compete and grow ETI's local brands and BAT's international brands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2003, BAT acquired a 67.8% holding in the Serbian tobacco company <b>Duvanska Industrija Vranje (DIV)</b>, allowing local manufacture of its brands, freeing them from import duties. In the longer term, export opportunities are planned as neighbouring countries in south east Europe developed free trade agreements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2004 the U.S. business of British American Tobacco (Brown &amp; Williamson) was combined with that of R. J. Reynolds Tobacco Company (R. J. Reynolds), under the R. J. Reynolds name. R. J. Reynolds and Brown &amp; Williamson were the second and third-ranking U.S. tobacco companies prior to the combination. When they combined, R. J. Reynolds became a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Reynolds_American" title="Reynolds American">Reynolds American</a>, with BAT holding a 42% share. In January 2007, BAT closed its remaining UK production plant in <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a> with the loss of over 600 jobs. However, the global Research and Development operation and some financial functions will continue on the site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Then in 2008 BAT acquired Turkey's state-owned cigarette maker <a href="/wiki/Tekel" title="Tekel">Tekel</a>. In July 2008, BAT acquired the cigarette and <a href="/wiki/Snus" title="Snus">snus</a> operations of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scandinavian_Tobacco_Company" title="Scandinavian Tobacco Company">Scandinavian Tobacco Company</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>BAT acquired 60% of Indonesia's <a href="/wiki/Bentoel_Group" title="Bentoel Group">Bentoel Group</a> in 2009<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> before increasing its stake to 100% the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2011 BAT acquired the <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombian</a> company Productora Tabacalera de Tabacos S.A. (Protabaco).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2016, BAT offered to buy the remaining 57.8 percent of U.S. cigarette maker <a href="/wiki/Reynolds_American" title="Reynolds American">Reynolds American</a> in a $47 billion takeover that would create the world's biggest listed tobacco company with brands including Newport, Lucky Strike and Pall Mall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reynolds_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reynolds-22">[22]</a></sup> In January 2017, Reynolds agreed to an increased $49.4 billion deal, expected to close in the third quarter of the year after approval from both companies' boards of directors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Former">Former</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_American_Tobacco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Former">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>BAT has diversified into various fields at different times in its history. Its U.S. retail division, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BATUS_Inc." title="BATUS Inc.">BATUS Retail Group</a>, acquired <a href="/wiki/Gimbels" title="Gimbels">Gimbels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kohl%27s" title="Kohl's">Kohl's</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saks_Fifth_Avenue" title="Saks Fifth Avenue">Saks Fifth Avenue</a> in the 1970s and <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Field%27s" title="Marshall Field's">Marshall Field's</a> and its divisions in 1982. The United Kingdom retail chain <a href="/wiki/Argos_(retailer)" title="Argos (retailer)">Argos</a> was purchased in 1979. Kohl's grocery stores were sold to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Great_Atlantic_and_Pacific_Tea_Company" title="The Great Atlantic and Pacific Tea Company">A&amp;P</a> in 1983. In 1986, BATUS sold the Kohl's department stores and two Marshall Field's divisions, <a href="/wiki/The_Crescent_(department_store)" title="The Crescent (department store)">The Crescent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frederick_%26_Nelson" title="Frederick &amp; Nelson">Frederick &amp; Nelson</a>; BATUS closed Gimbels the same year, with many locations being absorbed by sister division Marshall Field's, as well as Allied Stores' Stern's and Pomeroy's divisions. In 1990 Marshall Field's was sold to Dayton Hudson Corporation (now <a href="/wiki/Target_Corporation" title="Target Corporation">Target Corporation</a>), <a href="/wiki/Ivey%27s" title="Ivey's">Ivey's</a> (another Marshall Field's division) was sold to <a href="/wiki/Dillard%27s" title="Dillard's">Dillard's</a>, Saks Fifth Avenue was sold to <a href="/wiki/Investcorp" title="Investcorp">Investcorp S.A.</a>, and Argos was demerged (Argos was acquired by previous parent company <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GUS_plc" title="GUS plc">GUS plc</a> in 1998).</p>
<p>The group was a major financial services company with the acquisitions of <a href="/wiki/Eagle_Star_Insurance" title="Eagle Star Insurance">Eagle Star</a> (1984),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Allied_Dunbar" title="Allied Dunbar">Allied Dunbar</a> (1985) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farmers_Group,_Inc." title="Farmers Group, Inc.">Farmers Group, Inc.</a> (1988). Around 1996 British American Tobacco merged their financial operations into a single operating unit, British American Financial Services (BAFS). This division was merged with Zurich Insurance Company in 1998 to form the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zurich_Financial_Services" title="Zurich Financial Services">Zurich Financial Services Group</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> B.A.T still owns the minority interest in Zurich.</p>

<p>Nicandro Durante became the chief executive in March 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sponsorships">Sponsorships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_American_Tobacco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sponsorships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>BAT have found many imaginative ways over the years to keep its brands in the public eye. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ITC_Limited" title="ITC Limited">ITC Limited</a>, in which BAT holds a minority share, as recently as 1996 secured an arrangement to sponsor the <a href="/wiki/Cricket_World_Cup" title="Cricket World Cup">Cricket World Cup</a> which was branded the "Wills World Cup" and thereby achieved a high level of brand recognition for the Wills cigarette brand in India where young cricket fans were a key target market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>BAT also sponsor the London Symphony Orchestra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1997 BAT brought its participation in the sport to new levels with the purchase of the <a href="/wiki/Tyrrell_Racing" title="Tyrrell Racing">Tyrrell</a> team for approximately £30 million. The team raced as Tyrrell for the 1998 season before being renamed as <a href="/wiki/British_American_Racing" title="British American Racing">British American Racing</a> (BAR).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although Formula One is an exceptionally expensive sport, for BAT the high cost of running an F1 team was justified as a promotional expense because there were few other opportunities for brand promotion. However, in 2005 a European Union (EU) directive was brought into force which required national governments to legislate to prevent tobacco sponsorship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004 BAR announced that technology partner <a href="/wiki/Honda" title="Honda">Honda</a> had purchased a 45% stake and in September 2005 it announced that Honda would be buying the remaining 55% stake. The team raced as <a href="/wiki/Honda_in_Formula_One" title="Honda in Formula One">Honda Racing F1 Team</a> in 2006, the last year of the Lucky Strike sponsorship before leaving the sport. For the <a href="/wiki/2006_Formula_One_season" title="2006 Formula One season">2006 season</a>, the team was renamed as Honda F1 Racing Team, with BAT only advertised at a couple of races. All links between the two companies were severed for <a href="/wiki/2007_Formula_One_season" title="2007 Formula One season">2007</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_American_Tobacco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Nigerian federal government filed a lawsuit against BAT and two other tobacco companies in 2007. Nigeria is seeking $42.4 billion, $34.4 billion of which the government seeks in anticipation of the future cost of treating Nigerians for tobacco-related illnesses. They are also seeking $1.04 billion as a fine for the companies' advertising and marketing campaign allegedly targeting Nigerian youth, and has asked the companies to fund an awareness campaign to educate young people about the dangers of their product. Several Nigerian state governments have filed similar petitions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In 2008 the company was the subject of a <a href="/wiki/BBC_Two" title="BBC Two">BBC Two</a> documentary, in which <a href="/wiki/Duncan_Bannatyne" title="Duncan Bannatyne">Duncan Bannatyne</a> investigated the marketing practices of the company in Africa and specifically the way the company targets younger Africans with branded music events, competitions and the sale of single cigarette sticks. Many of the practices uncovered by Bannatyne appeared to break BAT's own code of conduct and company standards. Towards the end of the programme, Bannatyne interviewed Dr Chris Proctor, Head of Science and Regulation, in which Proctor admitted that advertisements targeting children from three African countries were 'disappointing'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In many of these undeveloped countries, the awareness of health risks from smoking is very low or nonexistent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2001, BAT invested $7.1m in North Korean state-owned enterprise called the Korea Sogyong Trading Corporation, which employs 200 people in Pyongyang to produce up to two billion cigarettes a year. The operation is run by BAT's Singapore Division. Brands of cigarettes produced are Kumgansan, Craven A and Viceroy. BAT claims that the cigarettes are produced only for consumption in North Korea, although there are allegations that the cigarettes are smuggled for sale overseas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>British American Tobacco was declared the winner of the 2008 <a href="/wiki/Roger_Award" title="Roger Award">Roger Award</a>, the award for the worst transnational corporation operating in New Zealand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>British American Tobacco spent more than €700,000 <a href="/wiki/Lobbying" title="Lobbying">lobbying</a> the EU in 2008, up to four times as much as the company declared on the EU's register of interest representatives, according to a report by <a href="/wiki/Corporate_Europe_Observatory" title="Corporate Europe Observatory">Corporate Europe Observatory</a>. The report argues that BAT's hidden lobbying activities, which are clearly not in the public interest, should be exposed to public scrutiny.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canadian_class_action_lawsuit">Canadian class action lawsuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_American_Tobacco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Canadian class action lawsuit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The three largest Canadian tobacco companies, Imperial Tobacco Canada (a division of British American Tobacco), <a href="/wiki/Japan_Tobacco_International" title="Japan Tobacco International">JTI-Macdonald Corp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benson_%26_Hedges" title="Benson &amp; Hedges">Rothmans Benson &amp; Hedges</a>, are the subject of the largest class action lawsuit in Canadian history. The case started on 12 March 2012 in <a href="/wiki/Quebec_Superior_Court" title="Quebec Superior Court">Quebec Superior Court</a>, and the companies face a potential payout of C$27 billion (US $27.30 billion) in damages and penalties. In addition, a number of Canadian provinces are teaming up to sue tobacco companies to recover healthcare costs caused by smoking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 1 June 2015, Quebec Superior Court Justice Brian Riordan has awarded more than $15 billion to Quebec smokers in a landmark case that pitted them against three Canadian cigarette giants, including JTI-Macdonald Corp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australian_lawsuit">Australian lawsuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_American_Tobacco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Australian lawsuit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During 2012 British American Tobacco, along with Philip Morris and Imperial Tobacco, sued the Australian Commonwealth government. At the High Court, they argued that the Commonwealth's plain packaging legislation was unconstitutional because it usurped the companies' intellectual property rights and good will on other than just terms. However, the challenge was unsuccessful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="HMRC_Fine_for_Oversupply">HMRC Fine for Oversupply</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_American_Tobacco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: HMRC Fine for Oversupply">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 2014, the HMRC fined BAT £650,000 after it was found to be oversupplying its tobacco products in the Belgium market with the likelihood these products would illegally find themselves back into the UK market, with UK excise taxes not paid. The event highlighted a tobacco-smuggling issue that many anti-tobacco activists have been attempting to bring to light for years. Following several investigations, the HMRC reportedly seized more than 1.4 billion cigarettes and 330 tons of hand rolling tobacco in 2013-2014. BAT denied all claims and described the allegation and fine as “unjustified”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bribery_in_Rwanda.2C_Burundi.2C_Kenya">Bribery in Rwanda, Burundi, Kenya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_American_Tobacco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Bribery in Rwanda, Burundi, Kenya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In late November 2015, an episode of BBC’s Panorama program alleged that BAT was bribing officials in Rwanda, Burundi and Kenya in exchange for their limiting the implementation of the WHO’s Framework Convention on Tobacco Control in their respective countries. The episode showed documents provided by whistleblower Paul Hopkins, who worked for BAT in Kenya for 13 years. BAT denied the claims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pakistani_Lobbying_Efforts">Pakistani Lobbying Efforts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_American_Tobacco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Pakistani Lobbying Efforts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In April 2015 medical experts and anti-tobacco campaigners accused Philip Barton, the British High Commissioner to Pakistan, of lobbying for BAT interests. Pictures were released showing Barton attending a meeting on March 13 in Islamabad, where BAT executives attempted to convince the Pakistani Finance and Health Minister to prevent plans to print large health warnings on cigarette packets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> This activity was deemed contrary to FCO policy. It followed an earlier incident when the British Ambassador to Panama was reprimanded for similar activity on BAT’s behalf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
