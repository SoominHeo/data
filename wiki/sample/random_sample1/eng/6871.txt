<p><b>Vladimir Sviatoslavich the Great</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_East_Slavic_language" title="Old East Slavic language">Old East Slavic</a>: <span lang="orv">Володимѣръ Свѧтославичь</span>, <i>Volodiměrъ Svętoslavičь</i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" title="Old Norse language">Old Norse</a> as <i>Valdamarr Sveinaldsson</i>, <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">Ukrainian</a>: <span lang="uk">Володимир</span>, <i>Volodymyr</i>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Влади́мир</span>, <i>Vladimir</i>, <a href="/wiki/Belarusian_language" title="Belarusian language">Belarusian</a>: <span lang="be">Уладзiмiр</span>, <i>Uladzimir</i>; c. 958 – 15 July 1015, <a href="/wiki/Berestove" title="Berestove">Berestove</a>) was a <a href="/wiki/Prince" title="Prince">prince</a> of <a href="/wiki/Veliky_Novgorod" title="Veliky Novgorod">Novgorod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grand_prince" title="Grand prince">grand prince</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kiev" title="Kiev">Kiev</a>, and ruler of <a href="/wiki/Kievan_Rus%27" title="Kievan Rus'">Kievan Rus'</a> from 980 to 1015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Vladimir's father was prince <a href="/wiki/Sviatoslav_I_of_Kiev" title="Sviatoslav I of Kiev">Sviatoslav</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Rurik_dynasty" title="Rurik dynasty">Rurik dynasty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> After the death of his father in 972, Vladimir, who was then prince of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Novgorod" title="Novgorod">Novgorod</a>, was forced to flee to <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> in 976 after his brother <a href="/wiki/Yaropolk_I_of_Kiev" title="Yaropolk I of Kiev">Yaropolk</a> had murdered his other brother <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oleg_of_Drelinia" title="Oleg of Drelinia">Oleg</a> and conquered <a href="/wiki/Kievan_Rus%27" title="Kievan Rus'">Rus'</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, with the help from his relative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ladejarl" title="Ladejarl">Ladejarl</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H%C3%A5kon_Sigurdsson" title="Håkon Sigurdsson">Håkon Sigurdsson</a>, ruler of <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, he assembled a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Varangian" title="Varangian">Varangian</a> army and reconquered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Novgorod" title="Novgorod">Novgorod</a> from <a href="/wiki/Yaropolk_I_of_Kiev" title="Yaropolk I of Kiev">Yaropolk</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> By 980 Vladimir had consolidated the <a href="/wiki/Kievan_Rus%27" title="Kievan Rus'">Kievan realm</a> from modern-day <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> and had solidified the frontiers against incursions of Bulgarian, Baltic, and Eastern nomads. Originally a follower of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slavic_paganism" title="Slavic paganism">Slavic paganism</a>, Vladimir converted to <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> in 988<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Christianization_of_Kievan_Rus%27" title="Christianization of Kievan Rus'">Christianized the Kievan Rus'</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica.com_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-britannica.com-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_to_power">Rise to power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_the_Great&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Rise to power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in 958, Vladimir was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_son" title="Natural son">natural son</a> and youngest son of <a href="/wiki/Sviatoslav_I_of_Kiev" title="Sviatoslav I of Kiev">Sviatoslav I of Kiev</a> by his housekeeper <a href="/wiki/Malusha" title="Malusha">Malusha</a>. Malusha is described in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_saga" title="Norse saga">Norse sagas</a> as a prophetess who lived to the age of 100 and was brought from her cave to the palace to predict the future. Malusha's brother <a href="/wiki/Dobrynya" title="Dobrynya">Dobrynya</a> was Vladimir's tutor and most trusted advisor. Hagiographic tradition of dubious authenticity also connects his childhood with the name of his grandmother, <a href="/wiki/Olga_of_Kiev" title="Olga of Kiev">Olga of Kiev</a>, who was Christian and governed the capital during Sviatoslav's frequent military campaigns. His place of birth is identified by different authors either as <a href="/wiki/Budyatychi" title="Budyatychi">Budyatychi</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Volyn_Oblast" title="Volyn Oblast">Volyn Oblast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diba_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Diba-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> or Budnik (<a class="extiw" href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%91%D1%83%D0%B4%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%BA_(%D0%B4%D0%B5%D1%80%D0%B5%D0%B2%D0%BD%D1%8F)" title="ru:Будник (деревня)">ru:Будник (деревня)</a>; modern <a href="/wiki/Pskov_Oblast" title="Pskov Oblast">Pskov Oblast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alexandrov_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alexandrov-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Transferring his capital to <a href="/wiki/Pereyaslavets" title="Pereyaslavets">Pereyaslavets</a> in 969, Sviatoslav designated Vladimir ruler of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Novgorod_the_Great" title="Novgorod the Great">Novgorod the Great</a> but gave Kiev to his legitimate son <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yaropolk_of_Kiev" title="Yaropolk of Kiev">Yaropolk</a>. After Sviatoslav's death in 972, a fratricidal war erupted in 976 between Yaropolk and his younger brother <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oleg_of_Drelinia" title="Oleg of Drelinia">Oleg</a>, ruler of the <a href="/wiki/Drevlians" title="Drevlians">Drevlians</a>. In 977 Vladimir fled to his kinsman <a href="/wiki/Haakon_Sigurdsson" title="Haakon Sigurdsson">Haakon Sigurdsson</a>, ruler of <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, collecting as many <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> <a href="/wiki/Leidang" title="Leidang">warriors</a> as he could to assist him to recover Novgorod. On his return the next year, he marched against Yaropolk. On his way to Kiev he sent ambassadors to <a href="/wiki/Rogvolod" title="Rogvolod">Rogvolod</a> (Norse: Ragnvald), prince of <a href="/wiki/Polotsk" title="Polotsk">Polotsk</a>, to sue for the hand of his daughter <a href="/wiki/Rogneda_of_Polotsk" title="Rogneda of Polotsk">Rogneda</a> (Norse: Ragnhild). The high-born princess refused to affiance herself to the son of a bondswoman, so Vladimir attacked Polotsk, slew Rogvolod, and took Ragnhild by force. Polotsk was a key fortress on the way to Kiev, and capturing Polotsk and <a href="/wiki/Smolensk" title="Smolensk">Smolensk</a> facilitated the taking of Kiev in 978, where he slew Yaropolk by treachery and was proclaimed <a href="/wiki/Knyaz" title="Knyaz">knyaz</a> of all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kievan_Rus" title="Kievan Rus">Kievan Rus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Years_of_pagan_rule">Years of pagan rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_the_Great&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Years of pagan rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vladimir continued to expand his territories beyond his father's extensive domain. In 981, he seized the <a href="/wiki/Red_Ruthenia" title="Red Ruthenia">Cherven towns</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">Poles</a>; in 981–982 he suppressed a <a href="/wiki/Vyatichi" title="Vyatichi">Vyatichi</a> rebellion; in 983, he subdued the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yatvingians" title="Yatvingians">Yatvingians</a>; in 984, he conquered the <a href="/wiki/Radimichs" title="Radimichs">Radimichs</a>; and in 985, he conducted a military campaign against the <a href="/wiki/Volga_Bulgaria" title="Volga Bulgaria">Volga Bulgars</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> planting numerous fortresses and colonies on his way.</p>
<p>Although <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> spread in the region under Oleg's rule, Vladimir had remained a thoroughgoing pagan, taking eight hundred concubines (along with numerous wives) and erecting pagan statues and shrines to gods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> He may have attempted to reform Slavic <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">paganism</a> by establishing the thunder-god, <a href="/wiki/Perun" title="Perun">Perun</a>, as a supreme deity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Open abuse of the deities that most people in Rus' revered triggered widespread indignation. A mob killed the Christian Fyodor and his son Ioann (later, after the overall christening of Kievan Rus', people came to regard these two as the first Christian martyrs in Rus', and the Orthodox Church<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> set a day to commemorate them, July 25. Immediately after the murder of Fyodor and Ioann, early medieval Rus' saw persecutions against Christians, many of whom escaped or concealed their belief.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, Prince Vladimir mused over the incident long after, and not least for political considerations. According to the early Slavic chronicle called Tale of Bygone Years, which describes life in Kievan Rus' up to the year 1110, he sent his envoys throughout the civilized world to judge first hand the major religions of the time, Islam, Roman Catholicism, Judaism, and Byzantine Orthodoxy. They were most impressed with their visit to Constantinople, saying, "We knew not whether we were in Heaven or on Earth… We only know that God dwells there among the people, and their service is fairer than the ceremonies of other nations."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Christianization_of_the_Kievan_Rus.27">Christianization of the Kievan Rus'</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_the_Great&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Christianization of the Kievan Rus'">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Christianization_of_Kievan_Rus%27" title="Christianization of Kievan Rus'">Christianization of Kievan Rus'</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Primary_Chronicle" title="Primary Chronicle">Primary Chronicle</a> reports that in the year 987, after consultation with his <a href="/wiki/Boyar" title="Boyar">boyars</a>, Vladimir the Great sent envoys to study the religions of the various neighboring nations whose representatives had been urging him to embrace their respective faiths. The result is described by the <a href="/wiki/Nestor_the_Chronicler" title="Nestor the Chronicler">chronicler Nestor</a>. Of the <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> <a href="/wiki/Volga_Bulgaria" title="Volga Bulgaria">Bulgarians of the Volga</a> the envoys reported there is no gladness among them, only sorrow and a great stench. He also reported that Islam was undesirable due to its <a href="/wiki/Taboo" title="Taboo">taboo</a> against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverages</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pork" title="Pork">pork</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Vladimir remarked on the occasion: "Drinking is the joy of all Rus'. We cannot exist without that pleasure."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Ukrainian and Russian sources also describe Vladimir consulting with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> envoys and questioning them about their religion, but ultimately rejecting it as well, saying that their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Destruction_of_Jerusalem" title="Destruction of Jerusalem">loss of Jerusalem</a> was evidence that they had been abandoned by <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a>.</p>
<p>His emissaries also visited <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Orthodox</a> missionaries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Ultimately Vladimir settled on <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Christianity</a>. In the churches of the Germans his emissaries saw no beauty; but at <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, where the full festival ritual of the Byzantine Church was set in motion to impress them, they found their ideal: <i>"We no longer knew whether we were in heaven or on earth,"</i> they reported, describing a majestic <a href="/wiki/Divine_Liturgy" title="Divine Liturgy">Divine Liturgy</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hagia_Sophia" title="Hagia Sophia">Hagia Sophia</a>, <i>"nor such beauty, and we know not how to tell of it."</i> If Vladimir was impressed by this account of his envoys, he was even more attracted by the political gains of the Byzantine alliance.</p>

<p>In 988, having taken the town of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chersonesos_Taurica" title="Chersonesos Taurica">Chersonesos</a> in <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a>, he boldly negotiated for the hand of emperor <a href="/wiki/Basil_II" title="Basil II">Basil II</a>'s sister, <a href="/wiki/Anna_Porphyrogenita" title="Anna Porphyrogenita">Anna</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Never before had a <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> imperial princess, and one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Porphyrogenitos" title="Porphyrogenitos">"born in the purple"</a> at that, married a barbarian, as matrimonial offers of French kings and German emperors had been peremptorily rejected. In short, to marry the 27-year-old princess to a pagan Slav seemed impossible. Vladimir was baptized at Chersonesos, however, taking the Christian name of Basil out of compliment to his imperial brother-in-law; the sacrament was followed by his wedding to Anna. Returning to Kiev in triumph, he <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baptism_of_Kievan_Rus%27" title="Baptism of Kievan Rus'">destroyed pagan monuments</a> and established many churches, starting with a church dedicated to St. Basil,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Tithes" title="Church of the Tithes">Church of the Tithes</a> (989).</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab" title="Arab">Arab</a> sources, both Muslim and Christian, present a different story of Vladimir's conversion. <a href="/wiki/Yahya_of_Antioch" title="Yahya of Antioch">Yahya of Antioch</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Al-Rudhrawari&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Al-Rudhrawari (page does not exist)">al-Rudhrawari</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Makin" title="Al-Makin">al-Makin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shams_al-Din_al-Ansari_al-Dimashqi" title="Shams al-Din al-Ansari al-Dimashqi">Al-Dimashqi</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibn_al-Athir" title="Ibn al-Athir">ibn al-Athir</a> all give essentially the same account.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In 987, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bardas_Sclerus" title="Bardas Sclerus">Bardas Sclerus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bardas_Phokas_the_Younger" title="Bardas Phokas the Younger">Bardas Phocas</a> revolted against the Byzantine emperor Basil II. Both rebels briefly joined forces, but then Bardas Phocas proclaimed himself emperor on 14 September 987. Basil II turned to the Kievan Rus' for assistance, even though they were considered enemies at that time. Vladimir agreed, in exchange for a marital tie; he also agreed to accept Christianity as his religion and to Christianize his people. When the wedding arrangements were settled, Vladimir dispatched 6,000 troops to the Byzantine Empire, and they helped to put down the revolt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rus_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rus-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 988 and 991, he baptized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pecheneg" title="Pecheneg">Pecheneg</a> princes <a href="/wiki/Metiga" title="Metiga">Metiga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kuchug" title="Kuchug">Kuchug</a>, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Christian_reign">Christian reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_the_Great&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Christian reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vladimir then formed a great council out of his boyars and set his <a href="/wiki/Family_life_and_children_of_Vladimir_I" title="Family life and children of Vladimir I">twelve sons</a> over his subject principalities. According to the <a href="/wiki/Primary_Chronicle" title="Primary Chronicle">Primary Chronicle</a>, he founded the city of <a href="/wiki/Belgorod_Kievsky" title="Belgorod Kievsky">Belgorod</a> in 991. In 992 he went on a campaign against the Croats, most likely the <a href="/wiki/White_Croats" title="White Croats">White Croats</a> that lived on the border of modern <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>. This campaign was cut short by the attacks of the <a href="/wiki/Pechenegs" title="Pechenegs">Pechenegs</a> on and around <a href="/wiki/Kiev" title="Kiev">Kiev</a>.</p>
<p>In his later years he lived in a relative peace with his other neighbors: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boleslav_I_of_Poland" title="Boleslav I of Poland">Boleslav I of Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_I_of_Hungary" title="Stephen I of Hungary">Stephen I of Hungary</a>, and Andrikh the Czech (questionable character mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Primary_Chronicle" title="Primary Chronicle">A Tale of the Bygone Years</a>). After Anna's death, he married again, likely to a granddaughter of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otto_the_Great" title="Otto the Great">Otto the Great</a>.</p>
<p>In 1014 his son <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yaroslav_I_the_Wise" title="Yaroslav I the Wise">Yaroslav the Wise</a> stopped paying tribute. Vladimir decided to chastise the insolence of his son and began gathering troops against him. Vladimir fell ill, however, most likely of old age, and died at Berestovo, near Kiev. The various parts of his dismembered body were distributed among his numerous sacred foundations and were venerated as <a href="/wiki/Relic" title="Relic">relics</a>.</p>
<p>During his Christian reign, Vladimir lived the teachings of the Bible through acts of charity. He would hand out food and drink to the less fortunate, and made an effort to go out to the people who could not reach him. His work was based on the impulse to help one’s neighbors by sharing the burden of carrying their cross.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Significance_and_legacy">Significance and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_the_Great&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Significance and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1_hryvnia_2006_front.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="727" data-file-width="1367" height="117" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/1_hryvnia_2006_front.jpg/220px-1_hryvnia_2006_front.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/1_hryvnia_2006_front.jpg/330px-1_hryvnia_2006_front.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/1_hryvnia_2006_front.jpg/440px-1_hryvnia_2006_front.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1_hryvnia_2006_front.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Vladimir the Great portrait on obverse <a href="/wiki/Banknotes_of_the_Ukrainian_hryvnia" title="Banknotes of the Ukrainian hryvnia">₴1 bill</a> circa 2006</div>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox" title="Eastern Orthodox">Eastern Orthodox</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> churches celebrate the feast day of St. Vladimir on 15 July.</p>
<p>The town <a href="/wiki/Volodymyr-Volynskyi" title="Volodymyr-Volynskyi">Volodymyr-Volynskyi</a> in north-western Ukraine was founded by Vladimir and is named after him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The foundation of another town, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir,_Russia" title="Vladimir, Russia">Vladimir</a> in Russia, is usually attributed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vladimir_Monomakh" title="Vladimir Monomakh">Vladimir Monomakh</a>. However some researchers argue that it was also founded by Vladimir the Great.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/St_Volodymyr%27s_Cathedral" title="St Volodymyr's Cathedral">St Volodymyr's Cathedral</a>, one of the largest cathedrals in Kiev, is dedicated to Vladimir the Great, as was originally the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Kiev" title="University of Kiev">University of Kiev</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Imperial Russian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Order_of_St._Vladimir" title="Order of St. Vladimir">Order of St. Vladimir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Vladimir%27s_Orthodox_Theological_Seminary" title="Saint Vladimir's Orthodox Theological Seminary">Saint Vladimir's Orthodox Theological Seminary</a> in the United States are also named after him.</p>
<p>The memory of Vladimir was also kept alive by innumerable Russian folk ballads and legends, which refer to him as <i>Krasno Solnyshko</i> (the <i>Fair Sun</i>). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Varangian" title="Varangian">Varangian</a> period of Eastern Slavic history ceases with Vladimir, and the Christian period begins. The appropriation of Kievan Rus' as part of national history has also been a topic of contention in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ukrainophile" title="Ukrainophile">Ukrainophile</a> vs. <a href="/wiki/Russophilia" title="Russophilia">Russophile</a> schools of historiography since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_historiography" title="Soviet historiography">Soviet era</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>During his leadership, all branches of the economy prospered under him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> He was able to mint coins and regulated foreign affairs with other countries such as trade. Through trade he brought in Greek wines, Baghdad spices, and Arab horses to trade at the markets of Kiev. These advancements shaped the future of Russian foreign affairs.</p>
