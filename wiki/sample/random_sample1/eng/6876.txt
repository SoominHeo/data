<p><b>Vladimir Borisovich Kramnik</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Влади́мир Бори́сович Кра́мник</span>; born 25 June 1975) is a Russian <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a> <a href="/wiki/Grandmaster_(chess)" title="Grandmaster (chess)">Grandmaster</a>. He was the <a href="/wiki/Classical_World_Chess_Championship_2000" title="Classical World Chess Championship 2000">Classical World Chess Champion</a> from 2000 to 2006, and the undisputed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Chess_Champion" title="World Chess Champion">World Chess Champion</a> from 2006 to 2007. He has won three team gold medals and three individual medals at <a href="/wiki/Chess_Olympiad" title="Chess Olympiad">Chess Olympiads</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2000, he defeated <a href="/wiki/Garry_Kasparov" title="Garry Kasparov">Garry Kasparov</a> in a match played in London, and became the Classical World Chess Champion. In late 2004, Kramnik successfully defended his title against challenger <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P%C3%A9ter_L%C3%A9k%C3%B3" title="Péter Lékó">Péter Lékó</a> in a drawn <a href="/wiki/Classical_World_Chess_Championship_2004" title="Classical World Chess Championship 2004">match</a> played in <a href="/wiki/Brissago" title="Brissago">Brissago</a>, Switzerland. In October 2006, Kramnik, the Classical World Champion, defeated reigning <a href="/wiki/FIDE" title="FIDE">FIDE</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Chess_Champion" title="World Chess Champion">World Champion</a> <a href="/wiki/Veselin_Topalov" title="Veselin Topalov">Veselin Topalov</a> in a unification match, the <a href="/wiki/World_Chess_Championship_2006" title="World Chess Championship 2006">World Chess Championship 2006</a>. As a result, Kramnik became the first undisputed World Champion, holding both the FIDE and Classical titles, since Kasparov split from FIDE in 1993. In 2007, Kramnik lost the title to <a href="/wiki/Viswanathan_Anand" title="Viswanathan Anand">Viswanathan Anand</a>, who won the <a href="/wiki/World_Chess_Championship_2007" title="World Chess Championship 2007">World Chess Championship 2007</a> tournament ahead of Kramnik. He challenged Anand at the <a href="/wiki/World_Chess_Championship_2008" title="World Chess Championship 2008">World Chess Championship 2008</a> to regain his title, but lost. Nonetheless, he has remained a world-class player, and is the current world number three.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kramnik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vladimir Kramnik was born in the town of <a href="/wiki/Tuapse" title="Tuapse">Tuapse</a>, on the shores of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>. His father's birth name was Boris Sokolov, but he took his stepfather's surname when his mother (Vladimir's grandmother) remarried; his mother is Ukrainian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> As a child, Vladimir Kramnik studied in the chess school established by <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Botvinnik" title="Mikhail Botvinnik">Mikhail Botvinnik</a>. His first notable result in a major tournament was his gold medal win as first reserve for the Russian team in the <a href="/wiki/30th_Chess_Olympiad" title="30th Chess Olympiad">1992 Chess Olympiad in Manila</a>. His selection for the team caused some controversy in Russia at the time, as he was only sixteen years old and had not yet been awarded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Grandmaster" title="International Grandmaster">grandmaster</a> title, but his selection was supported by <a href="/wiki/Garry_Kasparov" title="Garry Kasparov">Garry Kasparov</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He scored eight wins, one <a href="/wiki/Draw_(chess)" title="Draw (chess)">draw</a>, and no losses.</p>
<p>The following year, Kramnik played in the very strong tournament in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linares_chess_tournament" title="Linares chess tournament">Linares</a>. He finished fifth, beating the then world number three, <a href="/wiki/Vassily_Ivanchuk" title="Vassily Ivanchuk">Vassily Ivanchuk</a>, along the way. He followed this up with a string of good results, but had to wait until 1995 for his first major tournament win at normal time controls, when he won the strong <a href="/wiki/Dortmund_Sparkassen_Chess_Meeting" title="Dortmund Sparkassen Chess Meeting">Dortmund tournament</a>, finishing it unbeaten.</p>
<p>In 1995, Kramnik served as a <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_chess#Second" title="Glossary of chess">second</a> for Kasparov in the <a href="/wiki/Classical_World_Chess_Championship_1995" title="Classical World Chess Championship 1995">Classical World Chess Championship 1995</a> match against challenger <a href="/wiki/Viswanathan_Anand" title="Viswanathan Anand">Viswanathan Anand</a>. Kasparov won the match 10½–7½.</p>
<p>In January 1996, Kramnik became the <a href="/wiki/List_of_FIDE_chess_world_number_ones" title="List of FIDE chess world number ones">world number-one</a> rated player; although having the same FIDE rating as Kasparov (2775), Kramnik became number one by having played more games during the rating period in question. This was the first time since December 1985 that Kasparov was not world number one, and Kramnik's six month stretch (January through June 1996) as world number one would be the only time from January 1986 through March 2006 where Kasparov was not world number one. By becoming number one, Kramnik became the youngest ever to reach world number one, breaking Kasparov's record; this record would stand for 14 years until being broken by <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Carlsen" title="Magnus Carlsen">Magnus Carlsen</a> in January 2010.</p>
<p>Kramnik continued to produce good results, including winning at Dortmund (outright or tied) ten times from 1995 to 2011. He is the second of only nine chess players to have reached a <a href="/wiki/Elo_rating_system" title="Elo rating system">rating</a> of 2800 (the first being Kasparov).</p>
<p>During his reign as world champion, Kramnik never regained the world number-one ranking, doing so only in January 2008 after he had lost the title to <a href="/wiki/Viswanathan_Anand" title="Viswanathan Anand">Viswanathan Anand</a>; as in 1996, Kramnik had the same FIDE rating as Anand (2799) but became number one due to more games played within the rating period. Kramnik's 12 years between world number-one rankings is the longest since the inception of the FIDE ranking system in 1971 (disputed with Fischer "freezed" rating).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chess_career">Chess career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kramnik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chess career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_setbacks">Early setbacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kramnik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early setbacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the mid- and late-90s, Kramnik, although considered one of the strongest players in the world, suffered several setbacks in his attempts to qualify for a World Championship match. In 1994, he lost a quarterfinal candidates match for the PCA championship to <a href="/wiki/Gata_Kamsky" title="Gata Kamsky">Gata Kamsky</a> 1½–4½, and later that year, lost a semifinal candidates match for the FIDE championship to <a href="/wiki/Boris_Gelfand" title="Boris Gelfand">Boris Gelfand</a> with the score 3½–4½. In 1998, Kramnik faced <a href="/wiki/Alexei_Shirov" title="Alexei Shirov">Alexei Shirov</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Candidates_Tournament" title="Candidates Tournament">Candidates match</a> for the right to play <a href="/wiki/Garry_Kasparov" title="Garry Kasparov">Garry Kasparov</a> for the Classical World Chess Championship, and lost 3½–5½. In 1999, Kramnik participated in the <a href="/wiki/FIDE" title="FIDE">FIDE</a> knockout championship in <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_Valley" title="Las Vegas Valley">Las Vegas</a>, and lost in the quarterfinals to <a href="/wiki/Michael_Adams_(chess_player)" title="Michael Adams (chess player)">Michael Adams</a> 2–4.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000_World_Championship">2000 World Championship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kramnik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2000 World Championship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Classical_World_Chess_Championship_2000" title="Classical World Chess Championship 2000">Classical World Chess Championship 2000</a></div>
<p>Suitable sponsorship was not found for a Kasparov–Shirov match, and it never took place. In 2000, sponsorship was secured for a Kasparov–Kramnik match instead. This was somewhat controversial, making Kramnik the first player since 1935 to play a world championship match without qualifying.</p>
<p>In 2000, Kramnik played a sixteen-game match against <a href="/wiki/Garry_Kasparov" title="Garry Kasparov">Garry Kasparov</a> in London, for the Classical Chess World Championship. Kramnik began the match as underdog, but his adoption of the <a href="/wiki/Ruy_Lopez#Berlin_Defence" title="Ruy Lopez">Berlin Defence</a> to Kasparov's <a href="/wiki/Ruy_Lopez" title="Ruy Lopez">Ruy Lopez</a> opening was very effective. With the white pieces, Kramnik pressed Kasparov hard, winning Games Two and Ten and overlooking winning continuations in Games Four and Six. Kasparov put up little fight thereafter, agreeing to short draws with the white pieces in Games 9 and 13. Kramnik won the match 8½–6½ without losing a game (this was only the second time in history that a World Champion had lost a match without winning a single game). This event marked the first time Kasparov had been beaten in a World Championship match.</p>
<p>Kramnik's performance won him the <a href="/wiki/Chess_Oscar" title="Chess Oscar">Chess Oscar</a> for 2000; this was the first time he had received the award.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_London">After London</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kramnik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: After London">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In October 2002, Kramnik competed in <a href="/wiki/Brains_in_Bahrain" title="Brains in Bahrain">Brains in Bahrain</a>, an eight-game match against the chess computer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_Fritz" title="Deep Fritz">Deep Fritz</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bahrain" title="Bahrain">Bahrain</a>. Kramnik started well, taking a 3–1 lead after four games. However, in game five, Kramnik made what was described as the worst blunder of his career, losing a knight in a position which was probably drawn. He quickly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resign_(chess)" title="Resign (chess)">resigned</a>. He also resigned game six after making a speculative sacrifice, although subsequent analysis showed that he had drawing chances in the final position. The last two games were drawn, and the match ended tied at 4–4.</p>
<p>In February 2004 Kramnik won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linares_chess_tournament" title="Linares chess tournament">Tournament of Linares</a> outright for the first time (he had tied for first with Kasparov in 2000), finishing undefeated with a +2 score, ahead of <a href="/wiki/Garry_Kasparov" title="Garry Kasparov">Garry Kasparov</a>, the world's highest-rated player at the time.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2004_title_defense">2004 title defense</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kramnik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2004 title defense">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Classical_World_Chess_Championship_2004" title="Classical World Chess Championship 2004">Classical World Chess Championship 2004</a></div>
<p>From 25 September 2004 until 18 October 2004, Kramnik retained his title as Classical World Chess Champion against challenger <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P%C3%A9ter_L%C3%A9k%C3%B3" title="Péter Lékó">Péter Lékó</a> at <a href="/wiki/Brissago" title="Brissago">Brissago</a>, Switzerland, by barely drawing the match in the last game. The 14-game match was poised in favor of Lékó right up until Kramnik won the final game, thus forcing a 7–7 draw and ensuring that Kramnik remained world champion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The prize fund was 1 million <a href="/wiki/Swiss_franc" title="Swiss franc">Swiss francs</a>, which was about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USD" title="USD">USD</a> $770,000 at the time. Because of the drawn result, the prize was split between the two players.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2006_Reunification_match">2006 Reunification match</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kramnik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2006 Reunification match">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FIDE_World_Chess_Championship_2006" title="FIDE World Chess Championship 2006">FIDE World Chess Championship 2006</a></div>
<p>When Garry Kasparov broke with <a href="/wiki/FIDE" title="FIDE">FIDE</a>, the federation governing professional chess, to play the 1993 World Championship with <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Short" title="Nigel Short">Nigel Short</a>, he created a rift in the chess world. In response, FIDE sanctioned a match between <a href="/wiki/Anatoly_Karpov" title="Anatoly Karpov">Anatoly Karpov</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jan_Timman" title="Jan Timman">Jan Timman</a> for the FIDE World Championship, which Karpov won. Subsequently, the chess world had seen two "champions": the "classical" championship, claiming lineage dating back to <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Steinitz" title="Wilhelm Steinitz">Steinitz</a>; and the FIDE endorsed champion.</p>
<p>When Kramnik defeated Kasparov and inherited Kasparov's title, he also inherited some controversies. Because the arrangements for the Kasparov Shirov match fell through, (it appears Shirov refused to play for what he considered too small a prize fund<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>) Kasparov decided to try to arrange a match with the highest rated player according to FIDE's rating list. At the time Anand was the highest rated player but Anand refused the match.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In the meantime Kramnik overtook Anand in rating and so he was offered the match. Kramnik accepted and ended up playing the match despite his loss of the qualifying match against Alexei Shirov in 1998.</p>
<p>At the next FIDE world championship (<a href="/wiki/FIDE_World_Chess_Championship_2005" title="FIDE World Chess Championship 2005">FIDE World Chess Championship 2005</a>), Kramnik refused to participate, but indicated his willingness to play a match against the winner to unify the world championship. After the tournament, negotiations began for a reunification match between Kramnik and the new FIDE World Champion—<a href="/wiki/Veselin_Topalov" title="Veselin Topalov">Veselin Topalov</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>.</p>
<p>In April 2006, FIDE announced a reunification match between Kramnik and Topalov—the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FIDE_World_Chess_Championship_2006" title="FIDE World Chess Championship 2006">FIDE World Chess Championship 2006</a>. The match took place in <a href="/wiki/Elista" title="Elista">Elista</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kalmykia" title="Kalmykia">Kalmykia</a>. After the first four games, Kramnik led 3–1 (out of a maximum of 12). After the fourth game, however, Topalov's coach/manager Silvio Danailov protested that Kramnik was using the toilet suspiciously frequently, implying that he was somehow receiving outside assistance whilst doing so. Topalov said that he would refuse to shake hands with Kramnik in the remaining games. The Appeals committee decided that the players' toilets be locked and that they be forced to use a shared toilet, accompanied by an assistant arbiter.</p>
<p>Kramnik refused to play the fifth game unless the original conditions agreed for the match were adhered to. As a result, the point was awarded to Topalov, reducing Kramnik's lead to 3–2. Kramnik stated that the appeals committee was biased and demanded that it be replaced. As a condition to continue the match, Kramnik insisted on playing the remaining games under the original conditions of the match <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">contract</a>, which allows use of the bathroom at the players' discretion.</p>
<p>The controversy resulted in a heavy volume of correspondence to <i>Chessbase</i> and other publications. The balance of views from fans was in support of Kramnik.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Prominent figures in the chess world, such as John Nunn, Yasser Seirawan, and Bessel Kok also sided with Kramnik.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The Russian and Bulgarian Chess Federations supported their respective players.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>After twelve regular games the match was tied 6–6, although Kramnik continued to dispute the result of the unplayed fifth game until the end of the match. On 13 October 2006 the result of this disputed game became irrelevant as Kramnik won the rapid tie-break by a score of 2½–1½.</p>
<p>Kramnik's victory helped him win the <a href="/wiki/Chess_Oscar" title="Chess Oscar">Chess Oscar</a> for 2006, the second of his career.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/World_Chess_Championship_2007" title="World Chess Championship 2007">World Chess Championship 2007</a></div>
<p>When Kramnik won the 2006 unification match, he also won Topalov's berth in the 2007 World Championship as the incumbent FIDE champion. Although the rationale behind his (and Garry Kasparov's) "classical" title is that the title should change hands by challenge match rather than by tournament, Kramnik stated that he would recognize the winner of this tournament as being the world champion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the tournament, held in September 2007, Kramnik and Anand drew both of their games but Kramnik finished second. The tournament, and the world championship, was won by <a href="/wiki/Viswanathan_Anand" title="Viswanathan Anand">Viswanathan Anand</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008_match">2008 match</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kramnik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2008 match">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/World_Chess_Championship_2008" title="World Chess Championship 2008">World Chess Championship 2008</a></div>
<p>Pursuant to the agreement reached before the 2007 tournament Kramnik and Anand played a match of the World Championship title in 2008 in <a href="/wiki/Bonn" title="Bonn">Bonn</a>. He fell victim to Anand's superior preparation, and lost three of the first six games (two with the white pieces). Kramnik's play gradually improved, and although he managed a 29 move victory in game 10,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> he did not win any other game, and lost the match to Anand by a score of 6½ to 4½ (three wins to Anand, one win to Kramnik, seven draws).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2009">2009</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kramnik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2009">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kramnik had exceptionally good results in 2009, winning once again in Dortmund and then winning the Category 21 (average Elo = 2763) <a href="/wiki/Tal_Memorial" title="Tal Memorial">Tal Memorial</a> in Moscow with 6/9 and a 2883 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Performance_rating_(chess)" title="Performance rating (chess)">rating performance</a> ahead of world champion Anand, <a href="/wiki/Vassily_Ivanchuk" title="Vassily Ivanchuk">Vassily Ivanchuk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Carlsen" title="Magnus Carlsen">Magnus Carlsen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Levon_Aronian" title="Levon Aronian">Levon Aronian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boris_Gelfand" title="Boris Gelfand">Boris Gelfand</a>, former FIDE world champion <a href="/wiki/Ruslan_Ponomariov" title="Ruslan Ponomariov">Ruslan Ponomariov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Leko" title="Peter Leko">Peter Leko</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Svidler" title="Peter Svidler">Peter Svidler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Morozevich" title="Alexander Morozevich">Alexander Morozevich</a>. At the time, the average Elo rating of the field made it the strongest tournament in history. Following this result, Kramnik stated that his goal was to regain the World Championship title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>He also participated in the London Chess Classic in December, finishing second to <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Carlsen" title="Magnus Carlsen">Magnus Carlsen</a>, losing their head-to-head encounter on the Black side of the <a href="/wiki/English_Opening" title="English Opening">English Opening</a>. Kramnik's performance in 2009 allowed his rating (average of July 2009 and January 2010 ratings) to be high enough to qualify for the <a href="/wiki/Candidates_Tournament" title="Candidates Tournament">Candidates Tournament</a> to determine the challenger for the <a href="/wiki/World_Chess_Championship_2012" title="World Chess Championship 2012">World Chess Championship 2012</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010">2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kramnik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2010">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kramnik began 2010 at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corus_chess_tournament" title="Corus chess tournament">Corus chess tournament</a> in the Netherlands, during which he defeated new world number one Carlsen with the Black pieces in their head-to-head encounter, ending Carlsen's 36-match unbeaten streak.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> A late loss to <a href="/wiki/Viswanathan_Anand" title="Viswanathan Anand">Viswanathan Anand</a> knocked him out of first place, and Kramnik finished with 8/13, tying for second place with <a href="/wiki/Alexei_Shirov" title="Alexei Shirov">Alexei Shirov</a> behind Carlsen's 8½ points.</p>
<p>In May 2010 it was revealed that Kramnik had aided <a href="/wiki/Viswanathan_Anand" title="Viswanathan Anand">Viswanathan Anand</a> in preparation for the <a href="/wiki/World_Chess_Championship_2010" title="World Chess Championship 2010">World Chess Championship 2010</a> against challenger <a href="/wiki/Veselin_Topalov" title="Veselin Topalov">Veselin Topalov</a>. Anand won the match 6½–5½ to retain the title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April–May 2010 he tied for 1st–3rd with <a href="/wiki/Shakhriyar_Mamedyarov" title="Shakhriyar Mamedyarov">Shakhriyar Mamedyarov</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gata_Kamsky" title="Gata Kamsky">Gata Kamsky</a> in the President's Cup in Baku and won the event on tie-break after all finished on 5/7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kramnik also participated in Dortmund, but had a subpar showing, losing to eventual champion <a href="/wiki/Ruslan_Ponomariov" title="Ruslan Ponomariov">Ruslan Ponomariov</a> and finishing in joint third place with 5/10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>He then participated in the Grand Slam Chess Masters preliminary tournament in Shanghai from September 3 to 8, where he faced world number four <a href="/wiki/Levon_Aronian" title="Levon Aronian">Levon Aronian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexei_Shirov" title="Alexei Shirov">Alexei Shirov</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wang_Hao_(chess_player)" title="Wang Hao (chess player)">Wang Hao</a>; the top two scorers qualified for the Grand Slam final supertournament from October 9 to 15 in Bilbao against Carlsen and Anand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Scoring 3/6, Kramnik tied for second place with Aronian behind the winner Shirov's 4½/6. In the blitz playoff, Kramnik defeated Aronian to qualify along with Shirov for the Grand Slam final.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shortly after qualifying for the last stage of the Grand Slam, Kramnik played on board one for the Russian team in the 2010 Olympiad. He scored +2–0=7.</p>
<p>Following the Olympiad, Kramnik participated in the Grand Slam Chess Masters final in Bilbao where he competed against Anand, Carlsen and Shirov. The average rating of the field was 2789, the highest in history. After defeating world number one Carlsen for the second consecutive time, and then Shirov in his first two games, Kramnik drew his final four games to finish in clear first with 4/6. This gave Kramnik the distinction of having won the two strongest tournaments in chess history.</p>
<p>Kramnik's attempt to defend his 2009 title at the Tal Memorial in Moscow ended with a 7th place, while he finished 5th in the London Chess Classic in England.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011">2011</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kramnik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 2011">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>2011 brought varied results. In Wijk aan Zee Kramnik shared fifth with <a href="/wiki/Maxime_Vachier-Lagrave" title="Maxime Vachier-Lagrave">Maxime Vachier-Lagrave</a>, and in the Candidates he was eliminated by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Grischuk" title="Alexander Grischuk">Alexander Grischuk</a>. He won Dortmund for the tenth time, with <a href="/wiki/L%C3%AA_Quang_Li%C3%AAm" title="Lê Quang Liêm">Lê Quang Liêm</a> in second place, and shared third behind <a href="/wiki/Peter_Svidler" title="Peter Svidler">Peter Svidler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Morozevich" title="Alexander Morozevich">Alexander Morozevich</a> in the Russian Superfinal. Kramnik won the third London Chess Classic with four wins and four draws, and a rating performance over 2900 Elo. <a href="/wiki/Hikaru_Nakamura" title="Hikaru Nakamura">Hikaru Nakamura</a> came second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> However, in the earlier 6th Tal Memorial 2011 Moscow he came 8th out of 10, with 2 losses (to Nepomniachtchi and Svidler) and 7 draws, with <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Carlsen" title="Magnus Carlsen">Magnus Carlsen</a> winning the overall tournament on tiebreak from <a href="/wiki/Levon_Aronian" title="Levon Aronian">Levon Aronian</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2012">2012</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kramnik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 2012">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kramnik played a friendly match against <a href="/wiki/Levon_Aronian" title="Levon Aronian">Levon Aronian</a>, which finished 3–3 (with a win for Aronian in a rapid game that didn't count as tiebreak). In Tal Memorial he shared fourth place behind <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Carlsen" title="Magnus Carlsen">Magnus Carlsen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fabiano_Caruana" title="Fabiano Caruana">Fabiano Caruana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Teimour_Radjabov" title="Teimour Radjabov">Teimour Radjabov</a>. He finished second in the <a href="/wiki/London_Chess_Classic" title="London Chess Classic">London Chess Classic</a> behind Carlsen.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2013">2013</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kramnik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: 2013">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kramnik played in the <a href="/wiki/World_Chess_Championship_2013#Candidates_Tournament" title="World Chess Championship 2013">2013 Candidates Tournament</a>, which took place in London, from 15 March to 1 April. He finished with +4−1=9, sharing the first place with Magnus Carlsen, who won due to having better tiebreaks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 2013 <a href="/wiki/Alekhine_Memorial" title="Alekhine Memorial">Alekhine Memorial</a> tournament, held from 20 April to 1 May, Kramnik finished seventh, with +2−2=5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 2013 <a href="/wiki/Tal_Memorial" title="Tal Memorial">Tal Memorial</a> tournament, held from 13 June to 23 June, Kramnik finished tenth out of ten, with +0−3=6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Chess_World_Cup_2013" title="Chess World Cup 2013">Chess World Cup 2013</a>, held in Norway from 11 August to 2 September, Kramnik finished in first place, defeating <a href="/wiki/Dmitry_Andreikin" title="Dmitry Andreikin">Dmitry Andreikin</a> in the four-game final match 2½–1½.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<p>He participated as one of 130 grandmasters at the combined World Rapid and Blitz Championships in Berlin that was organized by FIDE from 10 to 14 October. In the World Rapid Championship he remained unbeaten, winning five games of 15 and reaching the 6th place.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2016">2016</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kramnik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: 2016">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kramnik competed in the Dortmund Sparkassen Chess Meeting, held from 9 – 17 July and finished joint second with 4/7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>He played in the Tal Memorial (a ten-player <a href="/wiki/Round-robin_tournament" title="Round-robin tournament">round-robin tournament</a>) in Moscow from 26 September – 6 October. He finished sixth with 4.5/9 in the opening blitz round-robin on 25 September, meaning that he was given one more game to play with the black pieces than with the white in the classical tournament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> He finished joint fifth in the classical tournament, again with 4.5/9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>He participated in three of the four events of the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Chess_Tour" title="Grand Chess Tour">Grand Chess Tour</a> (he withdrew from the <a href="/wiki/Sinquefield_Cup" title="Sinquefield Cup">Sinquefield Cup</a> due to health issues and was replaced by Svidler<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>): Kramnik played the Paris Grand Chess Tour speed chess tournament, the Your Next Move Grand Chess Tour speed chess tournament, and the London Chess Classic classical chess tournament. All the events of the Grand Chess Tour were 10-player round robin tournaments.</p>
<p>The Paris Grand Chess Tour tournament was held from 9 – 12 June and was composed of a mixture of rapid and blitz games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Kramnik finished ninth with 5.5/18, above only comparatively low-rated wildcard player <a href="/wiki/Laurent_Fressinet" title="Laurent Fressinet">Laurent Fressinet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Your Next Move Grand Chess Tour tournament was held from 17 – 20 June in <a href="/wiki/Leuven" title="Leuven">Leuven</a>, Belgium. Like the Paris GCT, it was composed of a combination of rapid and blitz games. Kramnik finished joint eighth with 2.5/9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The London Chess Classic was held from 9 – 18 December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Kramnik finished joint third with 5/9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deep_Fritz_match">Deep Fritz match</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kramnik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Deep Fritz match">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kramnik played a six-game match against the computer program <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_Fritz" title="Deep Fritz">Deep Fritz</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bonn" title="Bonn">Bonn</a>, Germany from 25 November to 5 December 2006, losing 2–4 to the machine, with 2 losses and 4 draws. He received 500,000 <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">Euros</a> for playing and would have twice as much had he won the match. Deep Fritz version 10 ran on a computer containing two <a href="/wiki/Intel_Core_2" title="Intel Core 2">Intel Core 2 Duo</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CPU" title="CPU">CPUs</a>. Kramnik received a copy of the program in mid-October for testing, but the final version included an updated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opening_book" title="Opening book">opening book</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Except for limited updates to the opening book, the program was not allowed to be changed during the course of the match. The <a href="/wiki/Endgame_tablebase" title="Endgame tablebase">endgame tablebases</a> used by the program were restricted to five pieces<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> even though a complete six-piece tablebase was widely available.</p>
<p>The first game ended in a draw.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> A number of commentators claimed that Kramnik missed a win.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The second game was won by Deep Fritz, due to a mistake by Kramnik, who failed to defend against a threatened mate-in-one. <a href="/wiki/Susan_Polgar" title="Susan Polgar">Susan Polgar</a> called it the "<a href="/wiki/Blunder_(chess)#Deep_Fritz_vs._Vladimir_Kramnik" title="Blunder (chess)">blunder of the century</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The third, fourth and fifth games of the match ended in draws. In the last game Kramnik played the aggressive Sicilian Defense in an attempt to win with black and hence even the match, but was outplayed by Fritz. Kramnik was forced to resign, and Fritz won the match 4–2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 30 December 2006 Kramnik married French journalist Marie-Laure Germon. They have two children: daughter Daria, and son Vadim Vladimirovich.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kramnik has been diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/Ankylosing_spondylitis" title="Ankylosing spondylitis">ankylosing spondylitis</a>, an uncommon form of <a href="/wiki/Arthritis" title="Arthritis">arthritis</a>. It causes him great physical discomfort while playing. In January 2006, Kramnik announced that he would skip the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corus_Chess_Tournament" title="Corus Chess Tournament">Corus Chess Tournament</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wijk_aan_Zee" title="Wijk aan Zee">Wijk aan Zee</a> to seek out treatment for his arthritis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> He returned from treatment in June 2006, playing in the <a href="/wiki/37th_Chess_Olympiad" title="37th Chess Olympiad">37th Chess Olympiad</a>. He scored a +4 result, achieving the highest rating performance (2847) of the 1307 participating players.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assessment_and_legacy">Assessment and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kramnik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Assessment and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Playing_style">Playing style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kramnik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Playing style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Garry Kasparov described Kramnik's style as pragmatic and tenacious, in the latter similar to <a href="/wiki/Anatoly_Karpov" title="Anatoly Karpov">Anatoly Karpov</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> He is one of the toughest opponents to defeat, losing only one game in over one hundred games leading up to his match with Kasparov, including eighty consecutive games without loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Kasparov did not defeat Kramnik during their 2000 World Championship match, partly due to Kramnik's use of the <a href="/wiki/Ruy_Lopez#Berlin_Defence" title="Ruy Lopez">Berlin Defence</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ruy_Lopez" title="Ruy Lopez">Ruy Lopez</a>. Kramnik is renowned for his <a href="/wiki/Chess_endgame" title="Chess endgame">endgame</a> skills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contributions_to_chess">Contributions to chess</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kramnik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Contributions to chess">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kramnik has significantly shaped <a href="/wiki/Chess_theory#Opening_theory" title="Chess theory">opening theory in chess</a>. <a href="/wiki/Viswanathan_Anand" title="Viswanathan Anand">Viswanathan Anand</a> has said of him in 2012: "I don't know exactly how many lines he's established, but you get the impression that for the last 10 years we've only been using his ideas. ... His stamp on opening theory is much more significant than mine."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Kramnik's results with the white pieces against the <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Indian_Defence" title="King's Indian Defence">King's Indian Defence</a> made Kasparov drop the opening from his repertoire, and caused the opening to disappear from top-level play for many years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Kramnik's use of the Berlin Defence in his 2000 match against Kasparov led to a massive increase in the opening's popularity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Kramnik also revived the <a href="/wiki/Catalan_Opening" title="Catalan Opening">Catalan Opening</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
