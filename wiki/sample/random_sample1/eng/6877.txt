<p><b>Vladimir Alexandrovich Kryuchkov</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: Владимир Александрович Крючков) (29 February 1924 – 23 November 2007) was a <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> lawyer, diplomat and head of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KGB_(USSR)" title="KGB (USSR)">KGB</a>, member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Politburo_of_the_Central_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Politburo of the Central Committee of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Politburo</a> of the Central Committee of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CPSU" title="CPSU">CPSU</a>.</p>
<p>Initially working in the Soviet justice system as a prosecutor's assistant, Kryuchkov then graduated from the Diplomatic Academy of the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_(Soviet_Union)" title="Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Soviet Union)">Soviet Foreign Ministry</a> and became a diplomat. During his years in the foreign service, he met <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Andropov" title="Yuri Andropov">Yuri Andropov</a>, who became his main patron. From 1974 until 1988, Kryuchkov headed the foreign intelligence branch of the <a href="/wiki/KGB" title="KGB">KGB</a>, the <a href="/wiki/First_Chief_Directorate" title="First Chief Directorate">First Chief Directorate</a> (PGU). During these years the Directorate was involved in funding and supporting various communist, socialist and anti-colonial movements across the world, some of which came to power in their countries and established pro-Soviet governments; in addition, under Kryuchkov's leadership the Directorate had major triumphs in penetrating Western intelligence agencies, acquiring valuable <a href="/wiki/Technical_intelligence" title="Technical intelligence">scientific and technical intelligence</a> and perfecting the techniques of <a href="/wiki/Disinformation" title="Disinformation">disinformation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Active_measures" title="Active measures">active measures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> At the same time however, during Kryuchkov's tenure the Directorate became plagued with defectors, had major responsibility for encouraging the Soviet government to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_War_in_Afghanistan" title="Soviet War in Afghanistan">invade Afghanistan</a> and its ability to influence Western European communist parties diminished even further.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1988 until 1991, Kryuchkov served as the 7th Chairman of the KGB. He was the leader of the abortive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/August_coup" title="August coup">August coup</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GKChP" title="GKChP">GKChP</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_career">Early life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kryuchkov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kryuchkov was born in February 1924 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tsaritsyn" title="Tsaritsyn">Tsaritsyn</a> (later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stalingrad" title="Stalingrad">Stalingrad</a>, and now <a href="/wiki/Volgograd" title="Volgograd">Volgograd</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> to a working-class family. His parents were strong supporters of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a>. He joined the Communist Party in 1944 and became a full-time employee of the Communist Youth League (<a href="/wiki/Komsomol" title="Komsomol">Komsomol</a>). After earning a law degree, Kryuchkov embarked on a career in the Soviet justice system, working as an investigator for the prosecutor's office in his home city of Stalingrad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diplomatic_Service">Diplomatic Service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kryuchkov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Diplomatic Service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kryuchkov then joined the Soviet diplomatic service, stationed in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> until 1959. He then worked for the Communist Party Central Committee for eight years, before joining the <a href="/wiki/KGB" title="KGB">KGB</a> in 1967 together with his patron <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Andropov" title="Yuri Andropov">Yuri Andropov</a>. He was appointed head of the <a href="/wiki/First_Chief_Directorate" title="First Chief Directorate">First Chief Directorate</a> (FCD) in 1974 (the KGB Foreign Operations) and Deputy Chairman in 1978. In 1988, he was promoted to the rank of General of the Army and became KGB Chairman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levy_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Levy-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1989–1990, he was a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Politburo_of_the_CPSU_Central_Committee" title="Politburo of the CPSU Central Committee">Politburo</a>.</p>
<p>A political hard-liner, Kryuchkov was among the members of the Soviet intelligence community who misinterpreted the 1983 <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> exercise <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Able_Archer" title="Able Archer">Able Archer</a> as a prelude to a nuclear attack. Many historians, such as <a href="/wiki/Robert_Cowley" title="Robert Cowley">Robert Cowley</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Lewis_Gaddis" title="John Lewis Gaddis">John Lewis Gaddis</a>, believe the Able Archer incident was the closest the world has come to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_war" title="Nuclear war">nuclear war</a> since the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Missile_Crisis" title="Cuban Missile Crisis">Cuban Missile Crisis</a> of 1962.</p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Tretyakov_(intelligence_officer)" title="Sergei Tretyakov (intelligence officer)">Sergei Tretyakov</a>, Kryuchkov secretly sent US$50 billion worth of funds of the Communist Party to an unknown location in the lead up to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_Soviet_Union_(1985%E2%80%931991)" title="History of the Soviet Union (1985–1991)">collapse of the Soviet Union</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPO_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-WAPO-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/August_Coup_of_1991" title="August Coup of 1991">August Coup of 1991</a>, Kryuchkov was the initiator of creation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gang_of_Eight_(Soviet_Union)" title="Gang of Eight (Soviet Union)">The State Committee on the State of Emergency</a> (Государственный Комитет по Чрезвычайному Положению, ГКЧП) which arrested the President of USSR Mikhail Gorbachev. After the defeat of the Committee, Kryuchkov was imprisoned for his participation. However, in 1994 the <a href="/wiki/State_Duma" title="State Duma">State Duma</a> freed him in an <a href="/wiki/Amnesty" title="Amnesty">amnesty</a>. Kryuchkov was replaced as chairman of the KGB by <a href="/wiki/Leonid_Shebarshin" title="Leonid Shebarshin">Leonid Shebarshin</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Kryuchkov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kryuchkov died at the age of 83 on 23 November 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levy_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Levy-5">[5]</a></sup> His body was buried at the <a href="/wiki/Troyekurovskoye_Cemetery" title="Troyekurovskoye Cemetery">Troyekurovskoye Cemetery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>.</p>
