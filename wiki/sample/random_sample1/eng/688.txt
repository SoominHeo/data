<p><i><b>Miracle in Cell No. 7</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>: <span lang="ko-Hang">7번방의 선물</span>; <a href="/wiki/Revised_Romanization_of_Korean" title="Revised Romanization of Korean">RR</a>: <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Korean transliteration">7beonbangui Seonmul</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Literal_translation" title="Literal translation">lit</a>. "A Gift from Room 7") is a 2013 <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korean</a> film starring <a href="/wiki/Ryu_Seung-ryong" title="Ryu Seung-ryong">Ryu Seung-ryong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kal_So-won" title="Kal So-won">Kal So-won</a> and <a href="/wiki/Park_Shin-hye" title="Park Shin-hye">Park Shin-hye</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The film is a heartwarming comedy and family melodrama about a mentally challenged man wrongfully imprisoned for murder, who builds friendships with the hardened criminals in his cell, and in return they help him see his daughter again by smuggling her into the prison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film's early <a href="/wiki/Working_title" title="Working title">working title</a> was <i>December 23</i> (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>: <span lang="ko-Hang">12월 23일</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miracle_in_Cell_No._7&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lee Yong-go is a mentally impaired father with the intellect of a six-year-old, who lives in a run-down house along with his daughter Ye-seung. One day, he gets into a physical altercation with the police commissioner, who has just purchased the last <a href="/wiki/Sailor_Moon" title="Sailor Moon">Sailor Moon</a> backpack for his daughter, a gift Yong-go was saving up to buy for Ye-seung. Soon after, the police commissioner's daughter dies in a freak accident, in which she slips on ice and suffers a fatal blow to the back of her head while she is taking Yong-go to another store that sells the same backpack. When he tries to resuscitate her, a woman witnesses him and mistakes him to be molesting her. Yong-go is falsely accused of the abduction, murder, and rape of a minor. The police quickly took advantage of his disability and forced him to admit to the crimes, while ignoring exonerating evidence. Yong-go, who is sentenced to death, is imprisoned and assigned to Cell No. 7, the harshest cell in a maximum security prison.</p>
<p>At first, the other men in the cell, led by gang leader So Yang-ho, do not take kindly to Yong-go after reading in his file that he murdered and molested a child. However, when Yong-go saves Yang-ho from being fatally stabbed by a rival gang leader, Yang-ho repays the favor by smuggling Ye-seung into Cell No. 7. Over time, they come to believe in his innocence, and make it their goal to help reverse the verdict at Yong-go's retrial.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miracle_in_Cell_No._7&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film took 4.6 million admissions (the equivalent of more than <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$30 million</span>) in its first two weeks due to strong <a href="/wiki/Word_of_mouth" title="Word of mouth">word of mouth</a>. Despite the release of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Berlin_File" title="The Berlin File">The Berlin File</a></i>, it drew in 15% more audience members on its third week, with seat booking at 75.8%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> 32 days after its release, it became only the eighth film in Korean cinema history to break the 10 million mark in ticket sales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This was especially notable since <i>Miracle in Cell No. 7</i> had no big stars, and a modest budget with a break-even point of 1.7 million admissions, making its eventual profit the highest among the films that reached 10 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film exceeded 12 million viewers 46 days after its release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> On March 15, 2013, 52 days after its release, ticket sales reached 12.32 million, making it the <a href="/wiki/List_of_highest-grossing_films_in_South_Korea" title="List of highest-grossing films in South Korea">fifth highest grossing Korean film of all time</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Analysts say among the reasons for its success was the long <a href="/wiki/Korean_New_Year" title="Korean New Year">lunar new year</a> holiday and families going to see the film in groups. The film proved to be especially popular among middle-aged and elderly Koreans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
