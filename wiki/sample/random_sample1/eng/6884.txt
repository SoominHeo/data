<p><i><b>Black Hawk Down</b></i> is a 2001 British-American <a href="/wiki/War_film" title="War film">war film</a> co-produced and directed by <a href="/wiki/Ridley_Scott" title="Ridley Scott">Ridley Scott</a>. The screenplay by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ken_Nolan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ken Nolan (page does not exist)">Ken Nolan</a> is adapted from the <a href="/wiki/Non-fiction" title="Non-fiction">non-fiction</a> <a href="/wiki/Black_Hawk_Down_(book)" title="Black Hawk Down (book)">book of the same name</a> by <a href="/wiki/Mark_Bowden" title="Mark Bowden">Mark Bowden</a>, which in turn is based on a series of articles published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Philadelphia_Inquirer" title="The Philadelphia Inquirer">The Philadelphia Inquirer</a></i>. The 29-part series chronicled the events of a 1993 raid in <a href="/wiki/Mogadishu" title="Mogadishu">Mogadishu</a> by the U.S. military aimed at capturing faction leader <a href="/wiki/Mohamed_Farrah_Aidid" title="Mohamed Farrah Aidid">Mohamed Farrah Aidid</a> and the ensuing firefight, known as the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Mogadishu_(1993)" title="Battle of Mogadishu (1993)">Battle of Mogadishu</a>.</p>
<p>The film features a large <a href="/wiki/Ensemble_cast" title="Ensemble cast">ensemble cast</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Josh_Hartnett" title="Josh Hartnett">Josh Hartnett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ewan_McGregor" title="Ewan McGregor">Ewan McGregor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Bana" title="Eric Bana">Eric Bana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Sizemore" title="Tom Sizemore">Tom Sizemore</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Fichtner" title="William Fichtner">William Fichtner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jason_Isaacs" title="Jason Isaacs">Jason Isaacs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hardy" title="Tom Hardy">Tom Hardy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sam_Shepard" title="Sam Shepard">Sam Shepard</a>. It won two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Oscars</a> for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Film_Editing" title="Academy Award for Best Film Editing">Best Film Editing</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Sound" title="Academy Award for Best Sound">Best Sound Mixing</a> at the <a href="/wiki/74th_Academy_Awards" title="74th Academy Awards">74th Academy Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oscars2002_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oscars2002-2">[2]</a></sup> The movie was received positively by American film critics, but was strongly criticized by a number of other groups and military officials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Black_Hawk_Rising_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Black_Hawk_Rising-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Hawk_Down_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1993, following the ousting of the central government and start of a civil war, a major United Nations military operation in Somalia is authorized with a peacekeeping mandate. After the bulk of the peacekeepers are withdrawn, the Mogadishu-based militia loyal to <a href="/wiki/Mohamed_Farrah_Aidid" title="Mohamed Farrah Aidid">Mohamed Farrah Aidid</a> have declared war on the remaining UN personnel. In response, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Rangers" title="U.S. Army Rangers">U.S. Army Rangers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delta_Force" title="Delta Force">Delta Force</a> counter-terrorist operators, and <a href="/wiki/160th_Special_Operations_Aviation_Regiment_(Airborne)" title="160th Special Operations Aviation Regiment (Airborne)">160th SOAR</a> aviators are deployed to Mogadishu to capture Aidid, who has proclaimed himself president of the country.</p>
<p>To cement his power and subdue the population in the south, Aidid and his militia seize Red Cross food shipments, while the UN forces are powerless to directly intervene. Outside Mogadishu, Rangers and Delta Force capture <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osman_Atto" title="Osman Atto">Osman Ali Atto</a>, a faction leader selling arms to Aidid's militia. Shortly thereafter, a mission is planned to capture Omar Salad Elmi and Abdi Hassan Awale Qeybdiid, two of Aidid's top advisers.</p>
<p>The U.S. forces include experienced men as well as new recruits, including PFC Todd Blackburn and a desk clerk, SPC Grimes, going on his first mission. When his Lieutenant is removed from duty after having an epileptic seizure, Staff Sergeant Matthew Eversmann is placed in command of Ranger <a href="/wiki/Chalk_(military)" title="Chalk (military)">Chalk</a> Four, his first command.</p>
<p>The operation begins and Delta Force operators capture Aidid's advisers inside the target building. The Rangers and helicopters escorting the ground-extraction convoy take heavy fire, while Eversmann's Chalk Four is dropped a block away by mistake. Blackburn is severely injured when he falls from one of the <a href="/wiki/Sikorsky_UH-60_Black_Hawk" title="Sikorsky UH-60 Black Hawk">Black Hawk</a> helicopters, so three Humvees led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Staff_Sergeant#United_States" title="Staff Sergeant">SSG</a> Jeff Struecker are detached from the convoy to return Blackburn to the UN-held <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mogadishu_Airport" title="Mogadishu Airport">Mogadishu Airport</a>.</p>
<p>SGT Dominick Pilla is shot and killed just as Struecker's column departs, and shortly thereafter Black Hawk <i>Super Six-One</i>, piloted by CWO Clifton "Elvis" Wolcott, is shot down by a rocket-propelled grenade and crashes. Both pilots are killed, the two crew chiefs are wounded, and one Delta Force sniper on board escapes in another helicopter.</p>
<p>The ground forces are rerouted to converge on the crash site. The Somali militia erects roadblocks, and LTC Danny McKnight's Humvee column is unable to reach the crash site, while sustaining heavy casualties. Meanwhile, two Ranger Chalks, including Eversmann's unit, reach Super-Six One's crash site and set up a defensive perimeter to await evacuation with the two wounded men and the fallen pilots. In the interim, <i>Super Six-Four</i>, piloted by CWO Michael Durant, is also shot down by an RPG and crashes several blocks away.</p>
<p>With CPT Mike Steele's Rangers pinned down and sustaining heavy casualties, no ground forces can reach <i>Super Six-Four</i> 's crash site nor reinforce the Rangers defending <i>Super Six-One</i>. Two Delta Force snipers, SFC Randy Shughart and MSG Gary Gordon are inserted by helicopter to <i>Super Six-Four</i> 's crash site, where they find Durant still alive. The site is eventually overrun, Gordon and Shughart are killed, and Durant is captured by Aidid's militia.</p>
<p>McKnight's column gives up the attempt to reach <i>Six-One'</i>s crash site and returns to base with their prisoners and the casualties. The men prepare to go back to extract the Rangers and the fallen pilots, and MG Garrison sends LTC Joe Cribbs to ask for reinforcements from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/10th_Mountain_Division_(United_States)" title="10th Mountain Division (United States)">10th Mountain Division</a>, including Malaysian and Pakistani armored units.</p>
<p>As night falls, Aidid's militia launch a sustained assault on the trapped Americans at <i>Super Six-One'</i>s crash site. The militants are held off throughout the night by strafing runs and rocket attacks from <a href="/wiki/MD_Helicopters_MH-6_Little_Bird" title="MD Helicopters MH-6 Little Bird">AH-6J Little Bird</a> helicopter gunships, until the 10th Mountain Division's relief column is able to reach and save the American soldiers. The wounded and casualties are evacuated in the vehicles, but a handful of Rangers and Delta Force soldiers are forced to run from the crash site back to the stadium, in the Pakistani Compound UN Safe Zone.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_production">Background and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Hawk_Down_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Background and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adapting <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Hawk_Down_(book)" title="Black Hawk Down (book)">Black Hawk Down: a Story of Modern War</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Mark_Bowden" title="Mark Bowden">Mark Bowden</a> was the idea of director <a href="/wiki/Simon_West" title="Simon West">Simon West</a>, who suggested to <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Bruckheimer" title="Jerry Bruckheimer">Jerry Bruckheimer</a> that he should buy the film rights and let West direct. West moved on to direct <i><a href="/wiki/Lara_Croft:_Tomb_Raider" title="Lara Croft: Tomb Raider">Lara Croft: Tomb Raider</a></i> (2001) instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ken_Nolan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ken Nolan (page does not exist)">Ken Nolan</a> was credited as screenwriter, and others contributed uncredited: Mark Bowden wrote an adaptation of his own book, Steven Gaghan was hired to do a rewrite, <a href="/wiki/Steven_Zaillian" title="Steven Zaillian">Steven Zaillian</a> and Ezna Sands rewrote the majority of the Gaghan and Nolan's work, Sam Shepard (MGen. Garrison) wrote some of his dialogue, and <a href="/wiki/Eric_Roth" title="Eric Roth">Eric Roth</a> wrote Josh Hartnett and Eric Bana's concluding speeches. Ken Nolan was on set for four months rewriting his script and the previous work by Gaghan, Zaillian, and Bowden. He was given sole screenwriting credit by a <a href="/wiki/Writers_Guild_of_America" title="Writers Guild of America">WGA</a> committee.</p>
<p>Filming began in March 2001 in <a href="/wiki/Sal%C3%A9" title="Salé">Salé</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, and concluded in late June.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The book relied on a dramatization of participant accounts, which were the basis of the movie. SPC John Stebbins was renamed as fictional "John Grimes." Stebbins had been convicted by court martial, in 1999, for sexually assaulting his daughter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Mark Bowden said the Pentagon, ever sensitive about public image decided to alter factual history by requesting the change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Bowden wrote early screenplay drafts, before Bruckheimer gave it to screenwriter Nolan. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/POW" title="POW">POW</a>-captor conversation, between pilot Mike Durant and militiaman Firimbi, is from a Bowden script draft.</p>
<p>For military verisimilitude, the Ranger actors took a one-week Ranger familiarization course at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Benning" title="Fort Benning">Fort Benning</a>, the Delta Force actors took a two-week commando course from the 1st Special Warfare Training Group at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Bragg" title="Fort Bragg">Fort Bragg</a>, and Ron Eldard and the actors playing 160th SOAR helicopter pilots were lectured by captured aviator <a href="/wiki/Michael_Durant" title="Michael Durant">Michael Durant</a> at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Campbell" title="Fort Campbell">Fort Campbell</a>.</p>
<p>The U.S. Army supplied the <a href="/wiki/Materiel" title="Materiel">materiel</a> and the helicopters from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/160th_Special_Operations_Aviation_Regiment" title="160th Special Operations Aviation Regiment">160th Special Operations Aviation Regiment</a>. Most pilots (e.g., Keith Jones, who speaks some dialogue) had participated in the historic battle on October 3–4, 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rubin_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rubin-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the last day of their week-long Army Ranger orientation at Fort Benning, the actors who portrayed the Rangers received letters slipped under their doors. It thanked them for their hard work, and asked them to "tell our story true", signed with the names of the men who died in the Mogadishu firefight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rubin_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rubin-8">[8]</a></sup> A platoon of Rangers from B-3/75 did the <a href="/wiki/Fast-roping" title="Fast-roping">fast-roping</a> scenes and appeared as extras; John Collette, a Ranger Specialist during the battle, served as a stunt performer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many of the actors bonded with the soldiers who trained them for their roles. Actor Tom Sizemore said, "What really got me at training camp was the <a href="/wiki/Ranger_Creed" title="Ranger Creed">Ranger Creed</a>. I don't think most of us can understand that kind of mutual devotion. It's like having 200 best friends and every single one of them would die for you".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rubin_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rubin-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the filmmakers considered filming in <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>, they found the city of <a href="/wiki/Amman" title="Amman">Amman</a> too built up and landlocked. Scott and production designer <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Max" title="Arthur Max">Arthur Max</a> subsequently turned to <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, where they had previously worked on <i><a href="/wiki/Gladiator_(2000_film)" title="Gladiator (2000 film)">Gladiator</a></i>. Scott preferred that urban setting for authenticity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rubin_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Rubin-8">[8]</a></sup> Most of the film was photographed in the cities of <a href="/wiki/Rabat" title="Rabat">Rabat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sal%C3%A9" title="Salé">Salé</a>; the Task Force Ranger base sequences were filmed at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C3%A9nitra" title="Kénitra">Kénitra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>To keep the film at a manageable length, 100 key figures in the book were condensed to 39. The movie also does not feature any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Somali_people" title="Somali people">Somali</a> actors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFTBBH_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-SFTBBH-11">[11]</a></sup> Additionally, no Somali consultants were hired for accuracy, according to writer Bowden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFSACC_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFSACC-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film features soldiers wearing helmets with their last names on them. Although this was not accurate, director Ridley Scott used this device to help the audience distinguish among the characters because "they all look the same once the uniforms are on".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Hawk_Down_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office_performance">Box office performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Hawk_Down_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Box office performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Black Hawk Down</i> had a limited release in four theaters on December 28, 2001, in order to be eligible for the 2001 Oscars. It earned $179,823 in its first weekend, averaging $44,956 per theater. On January 11, 2002, the release expanded to 16 theaters and continued to do well with a weekly gross of $1,118,003 and an average daily per theater gross of $9,982. On January 18, 2002, the film had its <a href="/wiki/Wide_release" title="Wide release">wide release</a>, opening at 3,101 theaters and earning $28,611,736 in its first wide release weekend to finish <a href="/wiki/List_of_2002_box_office_number-one_films_in_the_United_States" title="List of 2002 box office number-one films in the United States">first at the box office</a> for the weekend. Opening on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King,_Jr._Day" title="Martin Luther King, Jr. Day">Martin Luther King holiday</a>, the film grossed $5,014,475 on the holiday of Monday, January 21, 2002, for a 4-day weekend total of $33,628,211. Only <i><a href="/wiki/Titanic_(1997_film)" title="Titanic (1997 film)">Titanic</a></i> had previously grossed more money over the Martin Luther King holiday weekend. <i>Black Hawk Down</i> finished first at the box office during its first three weeks of wide release. When the film was pulled from theatres on April 14, 2002, after its 15th week, it had grossed $108,638,746 domestically and $64,350,906 overseas for a worldwide total of $172,989,651.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoxOfficeMojo_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-BoxOfficeMojo-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Hawk_Down_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film received many positive reviews from mainstream critics. <i><a href="/wiki/Empire_(film_magazine)" title="Empire (film magazine)">Empire</a></i> magazine gave it a verdict of "ambitious, sumptuously framed, and frenetic, <i>Black Hawk Down</i> is nonetheless a rare find of a war movie which dares to turn genre convention on its head".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Film critic Mike Clark of <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> wrote that the film "extols the sheer professionalism of America's elite Delta Force – even in the unforeseen disaster that was 1993's Battle of Mogadishu," and praised Scott's direction: "in relating the conflict, in which 18 Americans died and 70-plus were injured, the standard getting-to-know-you war-film characterizations are downplayed. While some may regard this as a shortcoming, it is, in fact, a virtue".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clark_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clark-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>It has a 76% "Certified Fresh" rating on <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, with its critics consensus being: "Though it's light on character development and cultural empathy, Black Hawk Down is a visceral, pulse-pounding portrait of war, elevated by Ridley Scott's superb technical skill."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It has a rating of 74 on <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, indicating "generally favorable reviews".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film has had a small cultural legacy, which has been studied academically by media analysts dissecting how media reflects American perceptions of war. <i>Newsweek</i> writer Evan Thomas considered the movie one of the most culturally significant films of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_W._Bush_presidency" title="George W. Bush presidency">George W. Bush presidency</a>. He suggested that although the film was presented as being anti-war, it was at its core pro-war. He further wrote that "though it depicted a shameful defeat, the soldiers were heroes willing to die for their brothers in arms[...] The movie showed brutal scenes of killing, but also courage, stoicism and honor[...] The overall effect was stirring, if slightly pornographic, and it seemed to enhance the desire of Americans for a thumping war to avenge <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">9/11</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stephen A. Klien, writing in <i>Critical Studies in Media Communication,</i> argued that the film's sensational rendering of war had the effect of encouraging audiences to empathize with the film's pro-soldier leitmotif. He suggested that this in turn served to "conflate personal support of American soldiers with support of American military policy" and discourage "critical public discourse concerning justification for and execution of military interventionist policy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

It also received three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award" title="BAFTA Award">BAFTA Award</a> nominations: Best Cinematography, Best Sound and Best Editing.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies_and_inaccuracies">Controversies and inaccuracies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Hawk_Down_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Controversies and inaccuracies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Soon after <i>Black Hawk Down</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s release, the Somali Justice Advocacy Center in California denounced what they felt was its brutal and dehumanizing depiction of Somalis and called for its boycott.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Black_Hawk_Rising_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Black_Hawk_Rising-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a radio interview, <a href="/wiki/Brendan_Sexton_III" title="Brendan Sexton III">Brendan Sexton</a>, an actor who briefly appeared in the movie, said the version of the film which made it onto theater screens significantly differed from the one recounted in the original script. According to him, many scenes asking hard questions of the US regarding the violent realities of war and the true purpose of their mission in Somalia were cut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a review featured in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, film critic <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Mitchell" title="Elvis Mitchell">Elvis Mitchell</a> expressed dissatisfaction with the film's "lack of characterization", and opined that the film "reeks of glumly staged racism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Owen_Gleiberman" title="Owen Gleiberman">Owen Gleiberman</a> and Sean Burns, the film critics for the mainstream magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> and the alternative newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Weekly" title="Philadelphia Weekly">Philadelphia Weekly</a></i>, respectively, echoed the sentiment that the depiction was racist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>American film critic <a href="/wiki/Wheeler_Winston_Dixon" title="Wheeler Winston Dixon">Wheeler Winston Dixon</a> also found the film's "absence of motivation and characterization" disturbing, and wrote that while American audiences might find the film to be a "paean to patriotism", other audiences might find it to be a "deliberately hostile enterprise"; nevertheless, Dixon lauded the film's "spectacular display of pyrotechnics coupled with equally adroit editing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jerry_Bruckheimer" title="Jerry Bruckheimer">Jerry Bruckheimer</a>, the film's producer, rejected such claims on <i><a href="/wiki/The_O%27Reilly_Factor" title="The O'Reilly Factor">The O'Reilly Factor</a></i>, putting them down to political correctness in part due to <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a>'s liberal leanings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Somali nationals charge that the African actors chosen to play the Somalis in the film do not resemble the culturally unique features of the <a href="/wiki/Horn_of_Africa" title="Horn of Africa">Horn of Africa</a> nor does the language they communicate in sound like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afro-Asiatic_languages" title="Afro-Asiatic languages">Afro-Asiatic</a> tongue spoken by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Somali_people" title="Somali people">Somali people</a>, and claim the abrasive way lines are delivered and lack of authenticy regarding Somali culture fails to capture the tone, mannerisms and spirit of actual life in Somalia. No Somali actors were used in the movie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFTBBH_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-SFTBBH-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an interview with the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>, the faction leader <a href="/wiki/Osman_Ali_Atto" title="Osman Ali Atto">Osman Ali Atto</a> said that many aspects of the film are factually incorrect. Taking exception with the ostentatious character chosen to portray him Ali Atto claimed he neither looks like the actor who portrayed him, smoke cigars, or wear earrings,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WTDFSF_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-WTDFSF-25">[25]</a></sup> all later confirmed by SEAL Team Six sniper <a href="/wiki/Howard_E._Wasdin" title="Howard E. Wasdin">Howard E. Wasdin</a> in his 2012 memoirs. Wasdin also indicated that while the character in the movie ridiculed his captors, in reality Atto seemed concerned that Wasdin and his men had been sent to kill rather than apprehend him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wasdin_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wasdin-26">[26]</a></sup> Atto additionally stated that he had not been consulted about the project or approached for permission, and that the film sequence re-enacting his arrest contained several inaccuracies:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WTDFSF_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-WTDFSF-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>First of all when I was caught on 21 September, I was only travelling with one <a href="/wiki/Fiat_124" title="Fiat 124">Fiat 124</a>, not three vehicles as it shows in the film[...] And when the helicopter attacked, people were hurt, people were killed[...] The car we were travelling in, (and) I have got proof, it was hit at least 50 times. And my colleague Ahmed Ali was injured on both legs[...] I think it was not right, the way they portrayed both the individual and the action. It was not right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WTDFSF_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-WTDFSF-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Navy SEAL Wasdin similarly remarked that while olive green military rigger's tape was used to mark the roof of the car in question in the movie, his team in actuality managed to track down Atto's whereabouts using a much more sophisticated technique involving the implantation of a homing device (This was hidden in a cane presented to Atto as a gift from a contact who routinely met with him, which eventually led the team directly to the faction leader.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wasdin_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wasdin-26">[26]</a></sup>).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysian</a> military officials whose own troops were involved in the fighting have likewise raised complaints regarding the film's accuracy. Retired Brigadier-General Abdul Latif Ahmad, who at the time commanded Malaysian forces in Mogadishu, told the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AFP_news_agency" title="AFP news agency">AFP news agency</a> that Malaysian moviegoers would be under the wrong impression that the real battle was fought by the Americans alone with Malaysian troops relegated to "mere bus drivers to ferry them out". The film does portray Malaysians contributing to the battle from their vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pervez_Musharraf" title="Pervez Musharraf">General Pervez Musharraf</a>, who later became President of <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> after a coup, similarly accused the filmmakers of not crediting the work done by the Pakistani soldiers. In his autobiography <i><a href="/wiki/In_the_Line_of_Fire:_A_Memoir" title="In the Line of Fire: A Memoir">In the Line of Fire: A Memoir</a></i>, Musharraf wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The outstanding performance of the Pakistani troops under adverse conditions is very well known at the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">UN</a>. Regrettably, the film <i>Black Hawk Down</i> ignores the role of Malaysia and Pakistan in Somalia. When U.S. troops were trapped in the thickly populated Madina Bazaar area of Mogadishu, it was the Seventh <a href="/wiki/Frontier_Force_Regiment" title="Frontier Force Regiment">Frontier Force Regiment</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Pakistan_Army" title="Pakistan Army">Pakistan Army</a> that reached out and extricated them. The bravery of the U.S. troops notwithstanding, we deserved equal, if not more, credit; but the filmmakers depicted the incident as involving only Americans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
