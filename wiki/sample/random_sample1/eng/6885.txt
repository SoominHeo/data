<p><b>Black Mountain College</b>, a school founded in 1933 in <a href="/wiki/Black_Mountain,_North_Carolina" title="Black Mountain, North Carolina">Black Mountain, North Carolina</a> (near Asheville, North Carolina), was a new kind of college in the United States in which the study of art was seen to be central to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberal_arts" title="Liberal arts">liberal arts education</a>, and in which <a href="/wiki/John_Dewey" title="John Dewey">John Dewey</a>'s principles of education played a major role. Many of the school's students and faculty were influential in the arts or other fields, or went on to become influential. Although notable even during its short life, the school closed in 1957 after only 24 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The history and legacy of Black Mountain College are preserved and extended through <a href="/wiki/Black_Mountain_College_Museum_%2B_Arts_Center" title="Black Mountain College Museum + Arts Center">Black Mountain College Museum + Arts Center</a>, in downtown Asheville, NC.</p>
<p>The school's Lake Eden campus, used from 1941 to 1957, is now part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camp_Rockmont" title="Camp Rockmont">Camp Rockmont</a>, a summer camp for boys, and the adjacent Lake Eden Events &amp; Lodging.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Mountain_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Founded in 1933 by <a href="/wiki/John_Andrew_Rice" title="John Andrew Rice">John Andrew Rice</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Theodore_Dreier&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Theodore Dreier (page does not exist)">Theodore Dreier</a>, Frederick Georgia, and Ralph Lounsbury, all dismissed faculty members of <a href="/wiki/Rollins_College#Firing_of_John_Andrew_Rice_and_required_loyalty_pledge" title="Rollins College">Rollins College</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Black Mountain was experimental by nature and committed to an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interdisciplinary" title="Interdisciplinary">interdisciplinary</a> approach, attracting a faculty that included many of America's leading visual artists, composers, poets, and designers, like <a href="/wiki/Buckminster_Fuller" title="Buckminster Fuller">Buckminster Fuller</a>, who developed the <a href="/wiki/Geodesic_dome" title="Geodesic dome">geodesic dome</a>.</p>
<p>Operating in a relatively isolated rural location with little budget, Black Mountain College inculcated an informal and collaborative spirit and over its lifetime attracted a venerable roster of instructors. Some of the innovations, relationships, and unexpected connections formed at Black Mountain would prove to have a lasting influence on the postwar American art scene, high culture, and eventually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pop_culture" title="Pop culture">pop culture</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Buckminster_Fuller" title="Buckminster Fuller">Buckminster Fuller</a> met student <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Snelson" title="Kenneth Snelson">Kenneth Snelson</a> at Black Mountain, and the result was their first <a href="/wiki/Geodesic_dome" title="Geodesic dome">geodesic dome</a> (improvised out of Venetian blind slats in the school's back yard); <a href="/wiki/Merce_Cunningham" title="Merce Cunningham">Merce Cunningham</a> formed his dance company; and <a href="/wiki/John_Cage" title="John Cage">John Cage</a> staged his first <a href="/wiki/Happening" title="Happening">happening</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarris2002226_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarris2002226-4">[4]</a></sup> (the term itself is traceable to Cage's student <a href="/wiki/Allan_Kaprow" title="Allan Kaprow">Allan Kaprow</a>, who applied it later to such events).</p>
<p>Not a haphazardly conceived venture, Black Mountain College was a consciously directed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberal_arts" title="Liberal arts">liberal arts</a> school that grew out of the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_education" title="Progressive education">progressive education</a> movement. In its day it was a unique educational experiment for the artists and writers who conducted it. The college was active at a time when other Southern colleges and universities remained segregated, and a decade before <a href="/wiki/Brown_v._Board_of_Education" title="Brown v. Board of Education">Brown v. Board of Education</a>, an African American woman named <a href="/wiki/Alma_Stone_Williams" title="Alma Stone Williams">Alma Stone Williams</a> is considered by some to be the first black student to enroll in an all-white institution of higher education in the South during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jim_Crow" title="Jim Crow">Jim Crow</a> era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_Pioneer_in_Southern_Racial_Integration_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-A_Pioneer_in_Southern_Racial_Integration-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Additionally, the College was an important incubator for the American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avant_garde" title="Avant garde">avant garde</a>. Black Mountain proved to be an important precursor to and prototype for many of the alternative colleges of today ranging from <a href="/wiki/College_of_the_Atlantic" title="College of the Atlantic">College of the Atlantic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Naropa_University" title="Naropa University">Naropa University</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz" title="University of California, Santa Cruz">University of California, Santa Cruz</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marlboro_College" title="Marlboro College">Marlboro College</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evergreen_State_College" title="Evergreen State College">Evergreen State College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_College" title="Hampshire College">Hampshire College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shimer_College" title="Shimer College">Shimer College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prescott_College" title="Prescott College">Prescott College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goddard_College" title="Goddard College">Goddard College</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_College_West" title="World College West">World College West</a> (1973-1992), and <a href="/wiki/New_College_of_Florida" title="New College of Florida">New College of Florida</a>, among others, including <a href="/wiki/Warren_Wilson_College" title="Warren Wilson College">Warren Wilson College</a> located just minutes down the road from where Black Mountain College was located. <a href="/wiki/Bennington_College" title="Bennington College">Bennington College</a> was founded the year before Black Mountain College based on the same philosophy.</p>
<p>For the first eight years, the college rented the YMCA Blue Ridge Assembly buildings south of Black Mountain, North Carolina. In 1941, it moved across the valley to its own campus at Lake Eden where it remained until its closing in 1956. The property was later purchased and converted to an ecumenical Christian boys' residential summer camp (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camp_Rockmont" title="Camp Rockmont">Camp Rockmont</a>), which later became a long-time location of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Mountain_Festival&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Black Mountain Festival (page does not exist)">Black Mountain Festival</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Eden_Arts_Festival" title="Lake Eden Arts Festival">Lake Eden Arts Festival</a>. A number of the original structures are still in use as lodgings or administrative facilities.</p>
<p>The college suspended classes by court order in 1957. This was due to debts not sustained by the decreased number of students. In 1962, the school's books were finally closed, with all debts covered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The college was featured in the <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Sparks" title="Nicholas Sparks">Nicholas Sparks</a> novel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Longest_Ride" title="The Longest Ride">The Longest Ride</a></i> (2013) and <a href="/wiki/The_Longest_Ride_(film)" title="The Longest Ride (film)">2015 movie</a>.</p>

<p>The college ran summer institutes from 1944 until its closing in 1956. It was however influential to the founding of the <a href="/wiki/Free_University_of_New_York" title="Free University of New York">Free University of New York</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Black_Mountain_poets">Black Mountain poets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Mountain_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Black Mountain poets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Various avant-garde poets (subsequently known as the <a href="/wiki/Black_Mountain_poets" title="Black Mountain poets">Black Mountain poets</a>) were drawn to the school through the years, most notably <a href="/wiki/Charles_Olson" title="Charles Olson">Charles Olson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Duncan_(poet)" title="Robert Duncan (poet)">Robert Duncan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denise_Levertov" title="Denise Levertov">Denise Levertov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Williams_(poet)" title="Jonathan Williams (poet)">Jonathan Williams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ed_Dorn" title="Ed Dorn">Ed Dorn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Creeley" title="Robert Creeley">Robert Creeley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarris2002225_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarris2002225-15">[15]</a></sup> Creeley was hired to teach and to edit the <i>Black Mountain Review</i> in 1955, and when he left two years later for San Francisco, he became the link between the Black Mountain poets and the poets of the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Renaissance" title="San Francisco Renaissance">San Francisco Renaissance</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Mountain_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Black Mountain College is the subject of the museum exhibition <i>Leap Before You Look: Black Mountain College 1933-1957,</i> which opened at the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Contemporary_Art,_Boston" title="Institute of Contemporary Art, Boston">Institute of Contemporary Art, Boston</a> on October 10, 2015. The show was curated by <a href="/wiki/Helen_Molesworth" title="Helen Molesworth">Helen Molesworth</a> with Ruth Erickson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Art_in_America_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Art_in_America-16">[16]</a></sup> The show exhibited at the <a href="/wiki/Hammer_Museum" title="Hammer Museum">Hammer Museum</a> February 21, 2016 to May 15, 2016, which was organized by Anne Ellegood, senior curator, with MacKenzie Stevens, curatorial assistant and January Parkos Arnall, curatorial assistant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
