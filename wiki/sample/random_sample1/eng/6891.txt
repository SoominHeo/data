<p><i><b>Black-ish</b></i> (stylized as <i><b><span style="font-style:normal;">black</span><span style="font-weight:normal;">ish</span></b></i>) is an American <a href="/wiki/Sitcom" title="Sitcom">sitcom</a> starring <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Anderson" title="Anthony Anderson">Anthony Anderson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tracee_Ellis_Ross" title="Tracee Ellis Ross">Tracee Ellis Ross</a> broadcast on <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-andreeva1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-andreeva1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variety1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-variety1-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Single-camera_setup" title="Single-camera setup">single-camera</a> comedy centers on an <a href="/wiki/African-American_upper_class" title="African-American upper class">upper-middle-class African-American</a> family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-episodes_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-episodes-3">[3]</a></sup> The series premiered on September 24, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> On March 3, 2016, the show was renewed for a third season, which premiered on September 21, 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Since the second season premiere, the show has received critical acclaim, receiving many awards and nominations including a <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Actress_%E2%80%93_Television_Series_Musical_or_Comedy" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Actress – Television Series Musical or Comedy">Golden Globe Award for Best Actress</a> for Tracee Ellis Ross, <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy</a> and Golden Globe nominations for Outstanding Comedy Series and a TCA Award for Outstanding Achievement in Comedy.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cast_and_characters">Cast and characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-ish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cast and characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_cast">Main cast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-ish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Main cast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anthony_Anderson" title="Anthony Anderson">Anthony Anderson</a> as Andre "Dre" Johnson Sr., an advertising executive who tries to pass on some of his urban culture to his seemingly uninterested children. His alma mater is <a href="/wiki/Howard_University" title="Howard University">Howard University</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tracee_Ellis_Ross" title="Tracee Ellis Ross">Tracee Ellis Ross</a> as Dr. Rainbow "Bow" Johnson, who is Dre's wife. She is a biracial anesthesiologist who was raised by <a href="/wiki/Hippie" title="Hippie">hippies</a>. Her alma mater is <a href="/wiki/Brown_University" title="Brown University">Brown University</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yara_Shahidi" title="Yara Shahidi">Yara Shahidi</a> as Zoey Johnson, Dre and Rainbow's 17-year-old daughter. She is the attractive, popular, entitled, stylish, and socially active member of the Johnson family. She is typically apathetic to any dealings with her family and is always treated as the world's most perfect teenager, despite her generally shallow personality and tendencies of selfishness towards her siblings, especially Junior.</li>
<li>Marcus Scribner as Andre ("Junior") Johnson Jr., Dre and Rainbow's 16-year-old son. He is a so-called "nerd" who is confused by the world around him and frequently subjected to disdain and pettiness at the hands of his more shallow and obnoxious father and siblings.</li>
<li>Miles Brown as Jack Johnson, Dre and Rainbow's upbeat 8-year-old son, fraternal twin of Diane, who idolizes his father.</li>
<li>Marsai Martin as Diane Johnson, Dre and Rainbow's 8-year-old daughter, fraternal twin of Jack, who considers herself smarter and more mature than her twin brother.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jenifer_Lewis" title="Jenifer Lewis">Jenifer Lewis</a> as Ruby Johnson (recurring season 1; starring season 2-present), Dre's mother.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Jeff Meacham as Josh Oppenhol (recurring season 1, 3—present; starring season 2), Dre's co-worker.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Peter_Mackenzie" title="Peter Mackenzie">Peter Mackenzie</a> as Leslie Stevens (recurring seasons 1—2; starring season 3—present), Dre's boss and co-owner of Stevens &amp; Lido.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-ish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_and_casting">Development and casting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-ish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Development and casting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Black-ish</i> first appeared on the development slate at ABC in October 2013, when it was reported that the project, which would star <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Anderson" title="Anthony Anderson">Anthony Anderson</a>, had received a script commitment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> On January 16, 2014, ABC greenlit the <a href="/wiki/Television_pilot" title="Television pilot">pilot episode</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Two weeks later, <a href="/wiki/Larry_Wilmore" title="Larry Wilmore">Larry Wilmore</a> joined the show as showrunner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In mid-February, <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Fishburne" title="Laurence Fishburne">Laurence Fishburne</a> was cast as the father of Anderson's character, and <a href="/wiki/Tracee_Ellis_Ross" title="Tracee Ellis Ross">Tracee Ellis Ross</a> signed on as the female lead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-ish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On May 8, 2014, ABC picked up the pilot to the series for the 2014–15 television season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-andreeva1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-andreeva1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variety1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-variety1-2">[2]</a></sup> A few days later, Anderson announced that Larry Wilmore would be stepping down as showrunner early in the show's run due to his forthcoming late night show, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nightly_Show_with_Larry_Wilmore" title="The Nightly Show with Larry Wilmore">The Nightly Show with Larry Wilmore</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 7, 2015, ABC renewed the series for a second season.</p>
<p>On March 3, 2016, ABC renewed the series for a third season.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-ish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Black-ish</i> has been met with generally positive reviews from critics. <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> gives season 1 a rating of 86% based on 56 reviews, with an average rating of 7.3/10. The site's consensus states, "Although it seems uncertain of its target audience, <i>Black-ish</i> ingratiates with a diverse cast and engaging cultural issues."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> gave season 1 a score of 77 out of 100, based on 30 critics, indicating "generally favorable reviews".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Rolling Stone's December 4, 2014 issue called it "one of the only new network comedies worth watching," praising in particular Laurence Fishburne's performance. <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Anderson" title="Anthony Anderson">Anthony Anderson</a>'s performance was met with critical acclaim, earning him a nomination for the <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Lead_Actor_in_a_Comedy_Series" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actor in a Comedy Series">Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actor in a Comedy Series</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_and_political_commentary_content">Social and political commentary content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-ish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Social and political commentary content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Racism">Racism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-ish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Racism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Black-ish addresses not only the racism that the Johnsons face as an upper-middle class African-American family, but also includes the racism African-Americans from a variety of backgrounds face in America. The "Pilot" episode starts off the series by introducing Dre's fear that his kids are too assimilated to their primarily white surroundings and losing their black culture. The episode also addresses racism African-Americans face in the workplace when Dre gets excited for a promotion at his advertising agency, which turns out to be for Senior Vice President of the Urban Division. In response, Dre questions, "Did they just put me in charge of black stuff?" This episode raises the question of where the line is drawn so that you are not defined by your race but your culture still remains relevant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 25th episode, "The Word", Jack performs <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Gold_Digger_(Kanye_West_song)" title="Gold Digger (Kanye West song)">Gold Digger</a>" at school and says the <a href="/wiki/Nigger#The_N-word_euphemism" title="Nigger">N-word</a>. The rest of the episode discusses the generational and multicultural perspectives of the word and how it has a different meaning to different people, even between different African-Americans. That different meaning comes with different guidelines and regulations for the use of the N-word based on the speaker, the context, and the audience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="LGBTQ.2B">LGBTQ+</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-ish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: LGBTQ+">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 22nd episode, "Please Don't Ask, Please Don't Tell". Dre's sister, Rhonda, is introduced. Rhonda is a lesbian but never officially came out to her family. Family members just gradually figured it out because of Rhonda's live-in girlfriend, however, no one ever acknowledges it. Andre admits that homosexuality is a topic that most African-Americans prefer to avoid, which is why he never discusses it with his sister. This leads to Rhonda not inviting any of her family members to her wedding and Andre finally talking to Rhonda about her sexuality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Police_Brutality">Police Brutality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-ish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Police Brutality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 40th episode, "Hope", the show tackles <a href="/wiki/Police_brutality" title="Police brutality">police brutality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black_Lives_Matter" title="Black Lives Matter">Black Lives Matter</a> as the family watches the news reporting about an unarmed young, black man's fatal run-in with police. Although the case was fictional, many real names, such as <a href="/wiki/Death_of_Freddie_Gray" title="Death of Freddie Gray">Freddie Gray</a> and <a href="/wiki/Death_of_Sandra_Bland" title="Death of Sandra Bland">Sandra Bland</a>, were included in the family's discussion. The debate format of the episode was able to address both sides of the situation and not completely villianize the police force. However, it leaned more towards the Black Lives Matter movement. The format also allowed for perspectives from different generations, backgrounds, and ideologies. The end of the episode revolved around a message of hope and the importance of protests, discussion, and attitudes when people are faced with tragedies from police brutality, assassinations, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
