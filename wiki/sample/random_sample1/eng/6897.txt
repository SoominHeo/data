<p><i><b>The Blair Witch Project</b></i> is a 1999 American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Found_footage_(pseudo-documentary)" title="Found footage (pseudo-documentary)">found footage</a> <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a> <a href="/wiki/Psychological_horror" title="Psychological horror">psychological horror</a> <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">film</a> written, directed and edited by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Myrick" title="Daniel Myrick">Daniel Myrick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_S%C3%A1nchez_(director)" title="Eduardo Sánchez (director)">Eduardo Sánchez</a>. The film tells the fictional story of three student filmmakers (<a href="/wiki/Heather_Donahue" title="Heather Donahue">Heather Donahue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_C._Williams" title="Michael C. Williams">Michael C. Williams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Leonard" title="Joshua Leonard">Joshua Leonard</a>) who hike in the Black Hills near <a href="/wiki/Burkittsville,_Maryland" title="Burkittsville, Maryland">Burkittsville, Maryland</a> in 1994 to film a documentary about a local legend known as the Blair Witch. The three disappear, but their video and sound equipment (along with most of the footage they shot) is discovered a year later; the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Found_footage_(pseudo-documentary)" title="Found footage (pseudo-documentary)">recovered footage</a>" is the film the viewer is watching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the film received unexpected acclaim from critics, audience reception was polarized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reception_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reception-4">[4]</a></sup> It became a resounding box office success, grossing over <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>248 million worldwide,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> making it one of the most successful <a href="/wiki/Independent_film" title="Independent film">independent films</a> of all time. The DVD was released on October 26, 1999, and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray</a> edition was released on October 5, 2010.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Sequel" title="Sequel">sequel</a> was released on October 27, 2000 entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Shadows:_Blair_Witch_2" title="Book of Shadows: Blair Witch 2">Book of Shadows: Blair Witch 2</a></i>. Another sequel was planned for the following year, but did not materialize. On September 2, 2009, it was announced that Sánchez and Myrick were pitching the third film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blair_Witch_(video_games)" title="Blair Witch (video games)">trilogy of video games</a> based on the films was released in 2000. The <a href="/wiki/Blair_Witch" title="Blair Witch"><i>Blair Witch</i> franchise</a> has since expanded to include various novels, dossiers, comic books and additional merchandise.</p>
<p>On July 22, 2016, an official trailer for a sequel to the film, directed by <a href="/wiki/Adam_Wingard" title="Adam Wingard">Adam Wingard</a> and entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Blair_Witch_(film)" title="Blair Witch (film)">Blair Witch</a></i>, was released at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Diego_Comic-Con_International" title="San Diego Comic-Con International">San Diego Comic-Con</a>. The film, produced by <a href="/wiki/Lionsgate_Films" title="Lionsgate Films">Lionsgate</a>, was released on September 16, 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes.com_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-latimes.com-7">[7]</a></sup> <i>Blair Witch</i> is a direct sequel to <i>The Blair Witch Project</i>, and does not acknowledge the events of <i>Book of Shadows: Blair Witch 2</i>. However, Wingard has said that although <i>Blair Witch</i> does not reference any of the events that transpired in <i>Book of Shadows: Blair Witch 2</i>, the film does not necessarily discredit the existence of <i>Book of Shadows: Blair Witch 2</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cinemablend.com_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-cinemablend.com-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fictional_legend">Fictional legend</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blair_Witch_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Fictional legend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The backstory for the movie is a legend fabricated by Sánchez and Myrick which is detailed in <i>The Curse of the Blair Witch</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Mockumentary" title="Mockumentary">mockumentary</a> broadcast on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SciFi_Channel" title="SciFi Channel">SciFi Channel</a> in 1999 prior to the release of <i>The Blair Witch Project</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-curseoftheblairwitch_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-curseoftheblairwitch-9">[9]</a></sup> Sánchez and Myrick also maintain a website which adds further details to the legend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Curse_of_the_Blair_Witch_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Curse_of_the_Blair_Witch-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The legend describes the killings and disappearances of some of the residents of Blair, Maryland (a fictitious town on the site of <a href="/wiki/Burkittsville,_Maryland" title="Burkittsville, Maryland">Burkittsville, Maryland</a>) from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/18th_Century" title="18th Century">18th</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/20th_Century" title="20th Century">20th centuries</a>. Residents blamed these occurrences on the ghost of Elly Kedward, a Blair resident accused of practicing witchcraft in 1785 and sentenced to death by <a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">exposure</a>. <i>The Curse of the Blair Witch</i> presents the legend as real, complete with manufactured newspaper articles, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newsreels" title="Newsreels">newsreels</a>, television news reports, and staged interviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-curseoftheblairwitch_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-curseoftheblairwitch-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blair_Witch_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In October 1994, film students <a href="/wiki/Heather_Donahue" title="Heather Donahue">Heather Donahue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_C._Williams" title="Michael C. Williams">Michael C. Williams</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Leonard" title="Joshua Leonard">Joshua Leonard</a> set out to produce a documentary about the fabled Blair Witch. They travel to Burkittsville, Maryland, formerly Blair, and interview residents about the legend. Locals tell them of Rustin Parr, a <a href="/wiki/Hermit" title="Hermit">hermit</a> who lived in the woods and kidnapped eight children in the 1940s. He took the children down to his basement in pairs and made one stand in the corner while he killed the other. After turning himself in to the police, Parr claimed that the spirit of Elly Kedward, a woman executed for witchcraft in the 18th century, forced him to commit the murders. The students then interview Mary Brown, a Burkittsville resident deemed insane by the public. She claims to have encountered the Blair Witch in person, describing her as a hairy half-human, half-animal beast.</p>
<p>On their second day, the students explore the woods in north Burkittsville to research the legend. Along the way, they meet two fishermen, one of whom warns them that the woods are haunted. He also tells them of a young girl named Robin Weaver who went missing in 1888. When she returned three days later, she talked about "an old woman whose feet never touched the ground." However, his companion is skeptical of the story. The students then hike to Coffin Rock, where five men were found <a href="/wiki/Human_sacrifice#Ritual_murder" title="Human sacrifice">ritualistically murdered</a> in the 19th century, their bodies later disappearing. The group then camps for the night.</p>
<p>The next day they move deeper into the woods despite being uncertain of their exact location. They eventually locate what appears to be an old cemetery with seven small <a href="/wiki/Cairn" title="Cairn">cairns</a>. They set up camp nearby and then return to the cemetery after dark. Josh accidentally disturbs a cairn and Heather hastily repairs it. Later that night, they hear the sound of twigs snapping from all directions but assume the noises are from animals or locals.</p>
<p>On day three, they attempt to hike back to the car, but are unable to find it before dark. Much to their frustrations, they set camp. That night, they again hear twigs snapping but fail to find the source of the noises.</p>
<p>On the fourth day they find three cairns have been built around their tent during the night, which unnerves them. As they continue, Heather realizes her map is missing, and Mike later reveals he kicked it into a creek the previous day out of frustration, which prompts Heather and Josh to attack him in a rage. They realize they are now lost and decide simply to head south. They eventually reach a section where they discover a multitude of humanoid stick figures suspended from trees, which further unsettles them. That night they hear sounds again, but this time includes the sounds of children laughing among other strange noises. When an unknown force shakes the tent, they flee in a panic and hide in the woods until dawn.</p>
<p>Upon returning to their tent, they find that their possessions have been rifled through, and Josh's equipment is covered with a translucent slime. As they continue, they begin to show signs of mentally giving up, and it is made worse when they come across a log on a river identical to one they crossed earlier. They realize they have walked in a circle, despite having traveled south all day, and once again make camp, completely demoralized at having wasted an entire day. Josh suffers a mental breakdown while holding the camera, taunting Heather for their circumstances and her constant recording of the events.</p>
<p>On the sixth morning, Heather and Mike awaken to find that Josh has disappeared. After trying in vain to find him, they slowly move on. That night, they hear Josh's agonized screams in the darkness but are unable to locate him. Mike and Heather theorize that Josh's screams are a fabrication by the witch in order to draw them out of their tent.</p>
<p>During their next day in the woods, Heather discovers a bundle of sticks tied with a piece of fabric from Josh's shirt outside her tent. As she searches through it, she finds blood-soaked scraps of Josh's shirt as well as teeth, hair, and what appears to be a piece of Josh's tongue. Although distraught by the discovery, she chooses not to tell Mike. That night, Heather records herself apologizing to her family, as well as to the families of Mike and Josh, taking full responsibility for their current predicament. She then breaks down and hyperventilates, realizing that something terrible is hunting them, and will ultimately take them.</p>
<p>Afterward, they again hear Josh's agonized cries for help and follow them to a derelict, abandoned house containing runic symbols and children's bloody hand-prints on the walls. Mike races upstairs in an attempt to find Josh while Heather follows. Both are still filming all their actions. Mike then says he hears Josh in the basement. He runs downstairs while a hysterical Heather struggles to keep up. Upon reaching the basement, something attacks Mike and causes him to drop the camera and go silent. Heather enters the basement screaming, and her camera captures Mike facing a corner. Something then attacks Heather, causing her to drop her camera and go silent as well. The footage continues for another moment then ends.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blair_Witch_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blair_Witch_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Blair Witch Project</i> was developed during 1993<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onion_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-onion-11">[11]</a></sup> by the filmmakers. Myrick and Sánchez came across the idea for the film after realizing that they found <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Documentaries" title="Documentaries">documentaries</a> on paranormal phenomena scarier than traditional horror films. The two decided to create a film that combined the styles of both.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stilllegend_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stilllegend-12">[12]</a></sup> The script began with a 35-page outline, with the dialogue to be improvised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onion_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-onion-11">[11]</a></sup> Accordingly, the directors advertised in <i><a href="/wiki/Backstage_(magazine)" title="Backstage (magazine)">Backstage</a></i> magazine for actors with strong improvisational abilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-real_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-real-13">[13]</a></sup> There was a very informal improvisational audition process to narrow the pool of 2,000 actors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-witch_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-witch-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-salon_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-salon-15">[15]</a></sup> In developing the mythology behind the film, the creators used many inspirations. Several character names are near-anagrams; Elly Kedward (The Blair Witch) is <a href="/wiki/Edward_Kelley" title="Edward Kelley">Edward Kelley</a>, a 16th-century mystic. Rustin Parr, the fictional 1940s child-murderer, began as an anagram for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rasputin" title="Rasputin">Rasputin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In talks with investors, they presented an eight-minute documentary along with newspapers and news footage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stone_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-stone-17">[17]</a></sup> This documentary, originally called <i>The Blair Witch Project: The Story of Black Hills Disappearances</i> was produced by <a href="/wiki/Haxan_Films" title="Haxan Films">Haxan Films</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Casting">Casting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blair_Witch_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Casting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to Heather Donahue, auditions for the film were held at Musical Theater Works in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, and ads were placed in the weekly <i><a href="/wiki/Backstage_(magazine)" title="Backstage (magazine)">Backstage</a></i> for an open audition. The advertisement noted a "completely improvised feature film" shot in a "wooded location." Donahue described the audition as Myrick and Sánchez posing her the question: "You've served seven years of a nine year sentence. Why should we let you out on parole?" to which Donahue was required to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Improvise" title="Improvise">improvise</a> a response.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craig_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-craig-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Joshua Leonard, also in response to audition calls through <i>Backstage</i>, claimed he was cast in the film due to his knowledge of how to run a camera, as there was not an omniscient camera filming the scenes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blair_Witch_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Principal_photography" title="Principal photography">Principal photography</a> for the film began in October 1997 and lasted eight days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-real_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-real-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Most of the film was shot in <a href="/wiki/Seneca_Creek_State_Park" title="Seneca Creek State Park">Seneca Creek State Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Montgomery_County,_Maryland" title="Montgomery County, Maryland">Montgomery County, Maryland</a>, although a few scenes were filmed in the real town of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burkittsville" title="Burkittsville">Burkittsville</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blairwitch_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-blairwitch-21">[21]</a></sup> Some of the townspeople interviewed in the film were not actors, and some were planted actors, unknown to the main cast. The final scenes were filmed at the historic <a href="/wiki/Griggs_House" title="Griggs House">Griggs House</a> in <a href="/wiki/Granite,_Maryland" title="Granite, Maryland">Granite, Maryland</a>. Donahue had never operated a camera before and spent two days in a "crash course." Donahue said she modeled her character after a director she had once worked with, citing the character's "self-assuredness" when everything went as planned, and confusion during crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>During filming, the actors were given clues as to their next location through messages given in milk crates found with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_Satellite" title="Global Positioning Satellite">Global Positioning Satellite</a> systems. They were given individual instructions that they would use to help improvise the action of the day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-real_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-real-13">[13]</a></sup> Teeth were obtained from a Maryland dentist for use as human remains in the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-real_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-real-13">[13]</a></sup> Influenced by producer <a href="/wiki/Gregg_Hale_(producer)" title="Gregg Hale (producer)">Gregg Hale</a>'s memories of his military training, in which "enemy soldiers" would hunt a trainee through wild terrain for three days, the directors moved the characters a long way during the day, harassing them by night and depriving them of food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stone_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-stone-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Almost 19 hours of usable footage was recorded which had to be edited down to 90 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-salon_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-salon-15">[15]</a></sup> The editing in post production took more than eight months. Originally it was hoped that the movie would make it on to cable television, and the filmmakers did not anticipate wide release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onion_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-onion-11">[11]</a></sup> The initial investment by the three filmmakers was about US$35,000. <a href="/wiki/Artisan_Entertainment" title="Artisan Entertainment">Artisan Entertainment</a> acquired the film for US$1.1 million but spent US$25 million to market it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The actors signed a "small" agreement to receive some of the profits from the film's release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-real_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-real-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>A list of production budget figures have circulated over the years, appearing as low as $20,000. In an interview with <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i>, Sánchez revealed that when principal photography first wrapped, approximately $20,000 to $25,000 had been spent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Other figures list a final budget ranging between $500,000 and $750,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Q.26A_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Q.26A-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing">Marketing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blair_Witch_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Marketing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The Blair Witch Project</i> is thought to be the first widely released film marketed primarily by internet. The film's official website featured faux police reports and "newsreel-style" interviews. These augmented the film's found footage device to spark debates across the internet over whether the movie was a real-life documentary or a work of fiction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> During screenings, the filmmakers made advertising efforts to promulgate the events in the film as factual, including the distribution of flyers at festivals such as the <a href="/wiki/Sundance_Film_Festival" title="Sundance Film Festival">Sundance Film Festival</a>, asking viewers to come forward with any information about the "missing" students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwpdigitalmedia.com_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-mwpdigitalmedia.com-29">[29]</a></sup> The campaign tactic was that viewers were being told, through missing persons posters, that the characters were missing while researching in the woods for the mythical Blair Witch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/IMDb" title="IMDb">IMDb</a> page also listed the actors as "missing, presumed dead" in the first year of the film's availability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The film's website contains materials of actors posing as police and investigators giving testimony about their casework, and shared childhood photos of the actors to add a sense of realism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> has opined that the <i>The Blair Witch Project</i> was the first film to go <a href="/wiki/Viral_marketing" title="Viral marketing">viral</a> despite having been produced before many of the technologies that facilitate such phenomena existed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blair_Witch_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blair_Witch_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Blair Witch Project</i> was premiered at the 1999 <a href="/wiki/Sundance_Film_Festival" title="Sundance Film Festival">Sundance Film Festival</a> and had a limited release on July 14, before going wide on July 30, 1999 after months of publicity, including a campaign by the studio to use the internet and suggest that the film was "a record of real events". The distribution strategy for the film was created and implemented by <a href="/wiki/Artisan_Entertainment" title="Artisan Entertainment">Artisan</a> studio executive <a href="/wiki/Steven_Rothenberg" title="Steven Rothenberg">Steven Rothenberg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thr_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-thr-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variety_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-variety-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film grossed $248.6 million worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mojo_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-mojo-36">[36]</a></sup> After reshoots, a new sound mix, experiments with different endings and other changes made by the studio, the film's final budget ended up between $500,000 and $750,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Q.26A_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Q.26A-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because the filming was done by the actors using hand-held cameras, much of the footage is <a href="/wiki/Shaky_camera" title="Shaky camera">shaky</a>, especially the final sequence in which a character is running down a set of stairs with the camera. Some audience members experienced <a href="/wiki/Motion_sickness" title="Motion sickness">motion sickness</a> and even vomited as a result.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blair_Witch_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<tr>
<td style="text-align: left;">"At a time when digital techniques can show us almost anything, <i>The Blair Witch Project</i> is a reminder that what really scares us is the stuff we can't see. The noise in the dark is almost always scarier than what makes the noise in the dark."</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td style="text-align: left;">—Roger Ebert, writing for the <i>Chicago Sun-Times</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert-38">[38]</a></sup></td>

<p>The film received wide acclaim from critics, although audience reception was polarized and divided.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reception_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reception-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> gives the film an approval rating of 86%, based on 155 reviews, with an average rating of 7.7/10. The site's critical consensus reads, "Full of creepy campfire scares, mock-doc <i>The Blair Witch Project</i> keeps audiences in the dark about its titular villain -- thus proving that imagination can be as scary as anything onscreen".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, the film holds a <a href="/wiki/Standard_score" title="Standard score">normalized score</a> 81 out of 100 based on 33 critics, indicating "universal acclaim".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film's "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Found_footage_(pseudo-documentary)" title="Found footage (pseudo-documentary)">found footage</a>" format received near-universal praise by critics and, though not the first in the found footage device, it has been declared a milestone in film history due to its critical and box office success. <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicago_Sun_Times" title="Chicago Sun Times">Chicago Sun Times</a></i> gave the film a total of 4 stars, calling it "an extraordinarily effective horror film".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert-38">[38]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Travers" title="Peter Travers">Peter Travers</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> praised the film, calling it "a groundbreaker in fright that reinvents scary for the new millennium".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Todd_McCarthy" title="Todd McCarthy">Todd McCarthy</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> wrote, "An intensely imaginative piece of conceptual filmmaking that also delivers the goods as a dread-drenched horror movie, <i>The Blair Witch Project</i> puts a clever modern twist on the universal fear of the dark and things that go bump in the night".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lisa_Schwarzbaum" title="Lisa Schwarzbaum">Lisa Schwarzbaum</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> gave a grade of "B", and wrote: "As a horror picture, <i>[The Blair Witch Project]</i> may not be much more than a cheeky game, a novelty with the cool, blurry look of an avant-garde artifact. But as a manifestation of multimedia synergy, it's pretty spooky".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Notwithstanding its international acclaim, few critics were less taken with the film. <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Sarris" title="Andrew Sarris">Andrew Sarris</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a></i> wrote, "<a href="/wiki/Daniel_Myrick" title="Daniel Myrick">Daniel Myrick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_S%C3%A1nchez_(director)" title="Eduardo Sánchez (director)">Eduardo Sánchez</a>'s <i>The Blair Witch Project</i> represents the ultimate triumph of the <a href="/wiki/Sundance_Film_Festival" title="Sundance Film Festival">Sundance</a> scam: Make a heartless home movie, get enough critics to blurb in near unison "scary," and watch the suckers flock to be fleeced. This fictional documentary within a pseudo-documentary form may be the most overrated, under-financed piece of film to come down the pike in a long time".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> A critic from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Christian_Science_Monitor" title="The Christian Science Monitor">The Christian Science Monitor</a></i> wrote, "The concept is clever, suggesting a new way to build horror-movie suspense without much on- camera gore. <i>[The Blair Witch Project]</i> would be better as a 30-minute short, though, since its shaky camera work and fuzzy images get monotonous after a while, and there's not much room for character development within the very limited plot".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> R. L. Schaffer of <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> gave a rating of 2 out of 10, and described it as "boring – really boring", and "a Z-grade, low-rent horror outing with no real scares into a genuine big-budget spectacle".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Film critic Michael Dodd has argued that the film is an embodiment of horror "modernizing its ability to be all-encompassing in expressing the fears of American society", acknowledging its status as the archetypal modern found footage feature, he noted that "In an age where anyone can film whatever they like, horror needn't be a cinematic expression of what terrifies the cinema-goer, it can simply be the medium through which terrors captured by the average American can be showcased".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> During 2008, <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> named <i>The Blair Witch Project</i> one of "the 100 best films from 1983 to 2008", ranking it at #99.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In 2006, <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Film_Critics_Association" title="Chicago Film Critics Association">Chicago Film Critics Association</a> listed it as one of the "Top 100 Scariest Movies", ranking it #12.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> It was listed on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filmcritic.com" title="Filmcritic.com">Filmcritic.com</a> as the "50th Best Movie Ending of All Time".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> In 2016, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i> listed it as one of the "10 Scariest Movies of All Time", ranking it #3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the film's release, critics have noted the influence of <i>The Blair Witch Project</i> has had on subsequent films similarly using the "found-footage" concept. These include the 2007 film <i><a href="/wiki/REC_(film)" title="REC (film)">REC</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> the 2008 film <i><a href="/wiki/Cloverfield" title="Cloverfield">Cloverfield</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> the 2009 film <i><a href="/wiki/Paranormal_Activity" title="Paranormal Activity">Paranormal Activity</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert-38">[38]</a></sup> the 2010 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Exorcism" title="The Last Exorcism">The Last Exorcism</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> and the 2012 films <i><a href="/wiki/Chronicle_(film)" title="Chronicle (film)">Chronicle</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/V/H/S" title="V/H/S">V/H/S</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> However, some critics have also noted the film as being strikingly similar to the 1980 film <i><a href="/wiki/Cannibal_Holocaust" title="Cannibal Holocaust">Cannibal Holocaust</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-46">[46]</a></sup> and the 1998 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Broadcast_(film)" title="The Last Broadcast (film)">The Last Broadcast</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> in terms of basic premise. Though <i>Cannibal Holocaust</i> director <a href="/wiki/Ruggero_Deodato" title="Ruggero Deodato">Ruggero Deodato</a> has acknowledged the similarities of <i>The Blair Witch Project</i> to his film, he criticized the publicity that the film received for being "an original production";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> advertisements for <i>The Blair Witch Project</i> also promoted the idea that the footage is genuine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onion_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-onion-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cinematic_and_literary_allusions">Cinematic and literary allusions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blair_Witch_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cinematic and literary allusions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the film, the Blair Witch is, according to legend, the ghost of Elly Kedward, a woman banished from the Blair Township (latter-day Burkittsville) for <a href="/wiki/Witchcraft" title="Witchcraft">witchcraft</a> in 1785. The directors incorporated that part of the legend, along with allusions to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salem_Witch_Trials" title="Salem Witch Trials">Salem Witch Trials</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Crucible" title="The Crucible">The Crucible</a></i>, to play on the themes of injustice done on those who were called witches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-witch_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-witch-14">[14]</a></sup> They were influenced by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shining_(film)" title="The Shining (film)">The Shining</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Alien_(film)" title="Alien (film)">Alien</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Omen" title="The Omen">The Omen</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Christensen" title="Benjamin Christensen">Benjamin Christensen</a>'s 1922 silent documentary <i><a href="/wiki/H%C3%A4xan" title="Häxan">Häxan</a></i>, after which the producers named their production company, Haxan Films. <i><a href="/wiki/Jaws_(film)" title="Jaws (film)">Jaws</a></i> was an influence as well, presumably because the witch was hidden from the viewer for the entirety of the film, forcing suspense from the unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onion_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-onion-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Soundtrack">Soundtrack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blair_Witch_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Soundtrack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>None of the songs featured on the film's official soundtrack entitled <i>The Blair Witch Project: Josh's Blair Witch Mix</i> actually appear in the film. However, "The Cellar" is played during the credits and the DVD menu. This collection of mostly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goth_rock" title="Goth rock">goth rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Industrial_music" title="Industrial music">industrial</a> tracks is supposedly from a mix tape made by ill-fated film student Joshua Leonard. In the story, the tape was found in his car after his disappearance. Some of the songs featured on the soundtrack were released after 1994, after the events of the film supposedly have taken place. Several of them feature dialogue from the film as well.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media_tie-ins">Media tie-ins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blair_Witch_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Media tie-ins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Blair_Witch" title="Blair Witch">Blair Witch</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Books">Books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blair_Witch_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In September 1999, D.A. Stern compiled <i>The Blair Witch Project: A Dossier</i>. Perpetuating the film's "true story" angle, the dossier consisted of fabricated police reports, pictures, interviews, and newspaper articles presenting the movie's premise as fact, as well as further elaboration on the Elly Kedward and Rustin Parr legends (an additional "dossier" was created for <i>Blair Witch 2</i>). Stern wrote the 2000 novel <i>Blair Witch: The Secret Confessions of Rustin Parr</i> and in 2004, revisited the franchise with the novel <i>Blair Witch: Graveyard Shift</i>, featuring all original characters and plot.</p>
<p>In May 1999, a <a href="/wiki/Photonovel" title="Photonovel">Photonovel</a> adaptation of <i>The Blair Witch Project</i> was written by Claire Forbes and was released by Fotonovel Publications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ComingSoon_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-ComingSoon-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Blair_Witch_Files"><i>The Blair Witch Files</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blair_Witch_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: The Blair Witch Files">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

The books center on Cade Merill, a fictional cousin of Heather Donahue, who investigates phenomena related to the Blair Witch in attempt to discover what really happened to Heather, Mike, and Josh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Files_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Files-65">[65]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comic_books">Comic books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blair_Witch_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Comic books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In August 1999, <a href="/wiki/Oni_Press" title="Oni Press">Oni Press</a> released a <a href="/wiki/One-shot_(comics)" title="One-shot (comics)">one-shot</a> comic promoting the film, simply titled <i>The Blair Witch Project</i>. Written by Jen Van Meter and drawn by <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Mireault" title="Bernie Mireault">Bernie Mireault</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guy_Davis_(comics)" title="Guy Davis (comics)">Guy Davis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Lee_Edwards" title="Tommy Lee Edwards">Tommy Lee Edwards</a>, the comic featured three short stories elaborating on the mythology of the Blair Witch. In mid-2000, the same group worked on a four-issue series called <i>The Blair Witch Chronicles</i>.</p>
<p>In October 2000, coinciding with the release of <i>Book of Shadows: Blair Witch 2</i>, <a href="/wiki/Image_Comics" title="Image Comics">Image Comics</a> released a one-shot called <i>Blair Witch: Dark Testaments</i>, drawn by <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Adlard" title="Charlie Adlard">Charlie Adlard</a> and written by <a href="/wiki/Ian_Edginton" title="Ian Edginton">Ian Edginton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ComingSoon_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-ComingSoon-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">Video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blair_Witch_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2000, <a href="/wiki/Gathering_of_Developers" title="Gathering of Developers">Gathering of Developers</a> released a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blair_Witch_(video_games)" title="Blair Witch (video games)">trilogy of computer games</a> based on the film, which greatly expanded on the myths first suggested in the film. The graphics engine and characters were all derived from the producer's earlier game <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturne_(Game)" title="Nocturne (Game)">Nocturne</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Each game, developed by a different team, focused on different aspects of the Blair Witch mythology: Rustin Parr, Coffin Rock, and Elly Kedward, respectively.</p>
<p>The trilogy received mixed reviews from critics, with most criticism being directed towards the very linear gameplay, clumsy controls and camera angles, and short length. The first volume, <i>Rustin Parr</i>, received the most praise, ranging from moderate to positive, with critics commending its storyline, graphics and atmosphere; some reviewers even claimed that the game was scarier than the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> The following volumes, <i>The Legend of Coffin Rock</i> and <i>The Elly Kedward Tale</i>, were less well-received, with <i><a href="/wiki/PC_Gamer" title="PC Gamer">PC Gamer</a></i> saying that Volume 2's "only saving grace was its cheap price",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> and calling Volume 3 "amazingly mediocre".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Documentary">Documentary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blair_Witch_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Documentary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Woods Movie</i> (2015) is a feature-length documentary exploring the production of <i>The Blair Witch Project</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> For this documentary, director Russ Gomm interviewed the original film's producer, <a href="/wiki/Gregg_Hale_(producer)" title="Gregg Hale (producer)">Gregg Hale</a>, and directors, <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_S%C3%A1nchez_(director)" title="Eduardo Sánchez (director)">Eduardo Sánchez</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Myrick" title="Daniel Myrick">Daniel Myrick</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blair_Witch_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The Blair Witch Project</i> was released on <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> on October 26, 1999. It contains a number of special features, including documentary <i>Curse of the Blair Witch</i> featurette, a 5-minute <i>newly discovered footage</i>, <a href="/wiki/Audio_commentary" title="Audio commentary">director and producer commentary</a>, production notes, cast and crew biographies, trailers, interactive menus and subtitles. The audio commentary presents directors <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Myrick" title="Daniel Myrick">Daniel Myrick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_S%C3%A1nchez_(director)" title="Eduardo Sánchez (director)">Eduardo Sánchez</a>, and producers Rob Cowie, Mike Monello and <a href="/wiki/Gregg_Hale_(producer)" title="Gregg Hale (producer)">Gregg Hale</a> discussing the film's production, from development to principal photography. The <i>Curse of the Blair Witch</i> feature provides an in-depth look inside the creation of the film. Other featurettes of the DVD include access to the film's official website, two teaser trailers, theatrical trailer, a trailer for the DVD release of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Stand_(miniseries)" title="The Stand (miniseries)">The Stand</a></i>, a map, and excerpts from the film's book and comic book tie-ins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray</a> release from <a href="/wiki/Lionsgate" title="Lionsgate">Lionsgate</a> was released on October 5, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Best_Buy" title="Best Buy">Best Buy</a> and Lionsgate had an exclusive release of the Blu-ray available on August 29, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-test-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequels">Sequels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blair_Witch_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Sequels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Sequel" title="Sequel">sequel</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Shadows:_Blair_Witch_2" title="Book of Shadows: Blair Witch 2">Book of Shadows: Blair Witch 2</a></i>, was released in the autumn of 2000. The film was poorly received by most critics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> A third installment announced that same year did not materialize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2015, Eduardo Sánchez revealed that he was still planning <i>Blair Witch 3</i> and that he considered the film "inevitable", but added that there was nothing to officially announce at that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 22, 2016, a surprise trailer of <i><a href="/wiki/Blair_Witch_(film)" title="Blair Witch (film)">Blair Witch</a></i>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Adam_Wingard" title="Adam Wingard">Adam Wingard</a>, a sequel to the first film, was released at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Diego_Comic-Con_International" title="San Diego Comic-Con International">San Diego Comic-Con</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> The film was originally marketed as <i>The Woods</i> so as to be an exclusive surprise announcement for those in attendance at the convention. The film, produced by <a href="/wiki/Lionsgate_Films" title="Lionsgate Films">Lionsgate</a>, is slated for a September 16, 2016 release<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes.com_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-latimes.com-7">[7]</a></sup> and stars <a href="/wiki/James_Allen_McCune" title="James Allen McCune">James Allen McCune</a> as the brother of the original film's Heather Donahue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> In September 2016, screenwriter <a href="/wiki/Simon_Barrett_(filmmaker)" title="Simon Barrett (filmmaker)">Simon Barrett</a> explained that in writing the new film he only considered material that was produced with the involvement of the original film's creative team (directors Daniel Myrick and Eduardo Sánchez, producer <a href="/wiki/Gregg_Hale_(producer)" title="Gregg Hale (producer)">Gregg Hale</a>, and production designer <a href="/wiki/Ben_Rock" title="Ben Rock">Ben Rock</a>) to be "<a href="/wiki/Canon_(fiction)" title="Canon (fiction)">canon</a>", and that he did not take any material produced without their direct involvement—such as the first sequel <i>Book of Shadows</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Blair_Witch#The_Blair_Witch_Files" title="Blair Witch">The Blair Witch Files</a></i>, a series of <a href="/wiki/Young_adult_fiction" title="Young adult fiction">young adult</a> novels—into consideration when writing the new sequel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cinemablend.com_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-cinemablend.com-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
