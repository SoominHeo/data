<p><b>Blake Edwards</b> (born <b>William Blake Crump</b>; July 26, 1922 – December 15, 2010) was an American film director, screenwriter, and producer.</p>
<p>Edwards began his career in the 1940s as an actor, but he soon began writing screenplays and radio scripts before turning to producing and directing in television and films. His best-known films include <i><a href="/wiki/Breakfast_at_Tiffany%27s_(film)" title="Breakfast at Tiffany's (film)">Breakfast at Tiffany's</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Days_of_Wine_and_Roses_(film)" title="Days of Wine and Roses (film)">Days of Wine and Roses</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/10_(film)" title="10 (film)">10</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Victor/Victoria" title="Victor/Victoria">Victor/Victoria</a></i>, and the hugely successful <a href="/wiki/The_Pink_Panther" title="The Pink Panther">Pink Panther</a> film series with British comedian <a href="/wiki/Peter_Sellers" title="Peter Sellers">Peter Sellers</a>. Often thought of as primarily a director of comedies, he also directed several drama, musical, and detective films. Late in his career, he transitioned to writing, producing, and directing for theater.</p>
<p>In 2004, he received an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honorary_Academy_Award" title="Honorary Academy Award">Honorary Academy Award</a> in recognition of his writing, directing, and producing an extraordinary body of work for the screen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blake_Edwards&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born William Blake Crump in <a href="/wiki/Tulsa,_Oklahoma" title="Tulsa, Oklahoma">Tulsa, Oklahoma</a>, he was the son of Donald and Lillian (Grommett) Crump (1897-1992).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> His father reportedly left the family before he was born. His mother married again, to Jack McEdwards,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> who became his stepfather. McEdwards was the son of <a href="/wiki/J._Gordon_Edwards" title="J. Gordon Edwards">J. Gordon Edwards</a>, a director of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silent_movie" title="Silent movie">silent movies</a>, and in 1925, he moved the family to Los Angeles and became a film production manager.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wakeman_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wakeman-5">[5]</a></sup> In an interview with the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Village_Voice" title="Village Voice">Village Voice</a></i> in 1971, Blake Edwards said that he had "always felt alienated, estranged from my own father, Jack McEdwards".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Village_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Village-6">[6]</a></sup> After attending grammar and high school in Los Angeles, Blake began taking jobs as an actor during World War II.</p>
<p>Edwards describes this period:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I worked with the best directors – <a href="/wiki/John_Ford" title="John Ford">Ford</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Wyler" title="William Wyler">Wyler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Otto_Preminger" title="Otto Preminger">Preminger</a> – and learned a lot from them. But I wasn't a very cooperative actor. I was a spunky, smart-assed kid. Maybe even then I was indicating that I wanted to give, not take, direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Village_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Village-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Edwards served in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">United States Coast Guard</a> during World War II, where he suffered a severe back injury, which left him in pain for years afterwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wakeman_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wakeman-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blake_Edwards&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Edwards' debut as a director came in 1952 on the television program <i><a href="/wiki/Four_Star_Playhouse" title="Four Star Playhouse">Four Star Playhouse</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1954–1955 television season, Edwards joined with <a href="/wiki/Richard_Quine" title="Richard Quine">Richard Quine</a> to create <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Rooney" title="Mickey Rooney">Mickey Rooney</a>'s first television series, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Mickey_Rooney_Show:_Hey,_Mulligan" title="The Mickey Rooney Show: Hey, Mulligan">The Mickey Rooney Show: Hey, Mulligan</a>,</i> a sitcom about a young studio page trying to become a serious actor. Edwards's hard-boiled private detective scripts for <i><a href="/wiki/Richard_Diamond,_Private_Detective" title="Richard Diamond, Private Detective">Richard Diamond, Private Detective</a></i> became NBC's answer to <a href="/wiki/Sam_Spade" title="Sam Spade">Sam Spade</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philip_Marlowe" title="Philip Marlowe">Philip Marlowe</a>, reflecting Edwards's unique humor. Edwards also created, wrote, and directed the 1959 TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Peter_Gunn" title="Peter Gunn">Peter Gunn</a>,</i> which starred <a href="/wiki/Craig_Stevens_(actor)" title="Craig Stevens (actor)">Craig Stevens</a>, with music by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Mancini" title="Henry Mancini">Henry Mancini</a>. In the same year, Edwards produced, with Mancini's musical theme, <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._Lucky_(TV_series)" title="Mr. Lucky (TV series)">Mr. Lucky</a>,</i> an adventure series on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Columbia_Broadcasting_System" title="Columbia Broadcasting System">CBS</a> starring <a href="/wiki/John_Vivyan" title="John Vivyan">John Vivyan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ross_Martin" title="Ross Martin">Ross Martin</a>. Mancini's association with Edwards continued in his film work, significantly contributing to their success.</p>
<dl>
<dt><i>Operation Petticoat</i> (1959)</dt>
</dl>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Operation_Petticoat" title="Operation Petticoat">Operation Petticoat</a></i> was Edwards's first big-budget movie as a director. The film, which starred <a href="/wiki/Tony_Curtis" title="Tony Curtis">Tony Curtis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cary_Grant" title="Cary Grant">Cary Grant</a>, became the "greatest box-office success of the decade for Universal [Studios]," and made Edwards a recognized director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wakeman_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wakeman-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt><i>Breakfast at Tiffany's</i> (1961)</dt>
</dl>
<p><i>Breakfast at Tiffany's</i>, based on the novel by <a href="/wiki/Truman_Capote" title="Truman Capote">Truman Capote</a>, is credited with establishing him as a "cult figure" with many critics. <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Sarris" title="Andrew Sarris">Andrew Sarris</a> called it the "directorial surprise of 1961", and it became a "romantic touchstone" for college students in the early 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wakeman_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wakeman-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt><i>Days of Wine and Roses</i> (1962)</dt>
</dl>
<p><i>Days of Wine And Roses</i>, a dark psychological film about the effects of alcoholism on a previously happy marriage, starred <a href="/wiki/Jack_Lemmon" title="Jack Lemmon">Jack Lemmon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lee_Remick" title="Lee Remick">Lee Remick</a>. It has been described as "perhaps the most unsparing tract against drink that Hollywood has yet produced, more pessimistic than <a href="/wiki/Billy_Wilder" title="Billy Wilder">Billy Wilder</a>'s <i>The Lost Weekend</i>". The film gave another major boost to Edwards's reputation as an important director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wakeman_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Wakeman-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Edwards's most popular films were comedies, the melodrama <i>Days of Wine and Roses</i> being a notable exception. His most dynamic and successful collaboration was with Peter Sellers in six of the movies in the <i>Pink Panther</i> series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MikeMoody_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-MikeMoody-8">[8]</a></sup> Five of those involved Edwards and Sellers in original material, while <i><a href="/wiki/Trail_of_the_Pink_Panther" title="Trail of the Pink Panther">Trail of the Pink Panther</a></i>, made after Sellers died in 1980, was made up of unused material from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pink_Panther_Strikes_Again" title="The Pink Panther Strikes Again">The Pink Panther Strikes Again</a></i>. He also worked with Sellers on the film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Party_(film)" title="The Party (film)">The Party</a></i>. Edwards later directed the comedy film <i><a href="/wiki/10_(film)" title="10 (film)">10</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Dudley_Moore" title="Dudley Moore">Dudley Moore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bo_Derek" title="Bo Derek">Bo Derek</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MikeMoody_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-MikeMoody-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt><i>Darling Lili</i> (1970)</dt>
</dl>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Darling_Lili" title="Darling Lili">Darling Lili</a></i>star, <a href="/wiki/Julie_Andrews" title="Julie Andrews">Julie Andrews</a> later married Edwards. While a few critics such as George Morris thought the film a major picture ("it synthesizes every major Edwards theme: the disappearance of gallantry and honor, the tension between appearances and reality, and the emotional, spiritual, moral, and psychological disorder" in such a world. Edwards used difficult cinematography techniques, including long-shot zooms, tracking, and focus distortion, to great effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wakeman_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Wakeman-5">[5]</a></sup>), the film failed badly with most critics and at the box office. At a cost of $17 million to make, few people went to see it, and the few who did were unimpressed. It brought <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a> to "the verge of financial collapse", and became an example of "self-indulgent extravagance" in filmmaking "that was ruining Hollywood."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wakeman_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-Wakeman-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt><i>Pink Panther</i> film series</dt>
</dl>
<p>Edwards is best known for directing most of the comedy film series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pink_Panther" title="The Pink Panther">The Pink Panther</a>,</i> all of those starring Peter Sellers as the inept Inspector Clouseau. The relationship between the director and the lead actor was considered a fruitful, yet complicated, with many disagreements during production. At various times in their film relationship, "he more than once swore off Sellers" as too hard to direct. However, in his later years, he admitted that working with Sellers was often irresistible:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"We clicked on comedy, and we were lucky we found each other, because we both had so much respect for it. We also had an ability to come up with funny things and great situations that had to be explored. But in that exploration there would oftentimes be disagreement. But I couldn't resist those moments when we jelled. And if you ask me who contributed most to those things, it couldn't have happened unless both of us were involved, even though it wasn't always happy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DGA_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-DGA-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The films were all highly profitable. <i>The Return of the Pink Panther</i> (1975), for example, cost just $2.5 million to make, but grossed $100 million, while <i>The Pink Panther Strikes Again</i> (1976), did even better.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wakeman_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-Wakeman-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Honorary Academy Award</dt>
</dl>
<p>In 2003, Edwards received an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honorary_Academy_Award" title="Honorary Academy Award">Honorary Academy Award</a> for cumulative achievements over the course of his film career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Silent-film_style">Silent-film style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blake_Edwards&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Silent-film style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Having grown up in Hollywood, the stepson of a studio production manager and stepgrandson of a silent-film director, Edwards had watched the films of the great silent-era comedians, including <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Chaplin" title="Charlie Chaplin">Charlie Chaplin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buster_Keaton" title="Buster Keaton">Buster Keaton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harold_Lloyd" title="Harold Lloyd">Harold Lloyd</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Laurel_and_Hardy" title="Laurel and Hardy">Laurel and Hardy</a>. Both Sellers and he appreciated and understood the comedy styles in silent films and tried to recreate them in their work together. After their immense success with the first two <i>Pink Panther</i> films, <i>The Pink Panther</i> (1963) and <i><a href="/wiki/A_Shot_in_the_Dark_(1964_film)" title="A Shot in the Dark (1964 film)">A Shot in the Dark</a></i> (1964), which adapted many silent-film aspects, including slapstick, they attempted to go even further in <i>The Party</i> (1968). The film has always had a cult following, and some critics and fans have considered it a "masterpiece in this vein" of silent comedy, though it did include minimal dialogue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kehr_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kehr-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blake_Edwards&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Edwards married his first wife, actress Patricia Walker, in 1953. They had two children, and divorced in 1967. She appeared in the comedy <i>All Ashore</i> (1953), for which Edwards was one of the screenwriters.</p>
<p>Edwards' second marriage from 1969 until his death was to <a href="/wiki/Julie_Andrews" title="Julie Andrews">Julie Andrews</a>. Andrews had a daughter, <a href="/wiki/Emma_Walton_Hamilton" title="Emma Walton Hamilton">Emma</a>, from her previous marriage, and the couple adopted two orphans from <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> in the early 1970s, Amelia Leigh and Joanna Lynne. Andrews appeared in a number of his films, including <i>Darling Lili</i>, <i>10</i>, <i>Victor/Victoria</i>, and the autobiographical satire <i><a href="/wiki/S.O.B._(film)" title="S.O.B. (film)">S.O.B.</a></i>, in which Andrews played a character who was a caricature of herself. In 1995, he wrote the book for <i><a href="/wiki/Victor/Victoria_(musical)" title="Victor/Victoria (musical)">the stage musical adaptation of Victor/Victoria</a></i>, also starring Andrews.</p>
<p>Edwards described his struggle with the illness <a href="/wiki/Chronic_fatigue_syndrome" title="Chronic fatigue syndrome">chronic fatigue syndrome</a> for 15 years in the documentary <i><a href="/wiki/I_Remember_Me" title="I Remember Me">I Remember Me</a></i> (2000).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blake_Edwards&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On December 15, 2010, Edwards died of complications of <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Saint_John%27s_Health_Center" title="Saint John's Health Center">Saint John's Health Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Monica,_California" title="Santa Monica, California">Santa Monica, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> His wife and children were at his side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MikeMoody_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-MikeMoody-8">[8]</a></sup> His death came after 15 years of suffering from chronic fatigue syndrome and depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blake_Edwards&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Edwards was greatly admired, as well as criticized, as a filmmaker during his career. On the negative side, general critique included this by American film author George Morris:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>It has been difficult for many critics to accept Blake Edwards as anything more than a popular entertainer. Edwards' detractors acknowledge his formal skill, but deplore the absence of profundity in his movies. Edwards' movies <i>are</i> slick and glossy, but their shiny surfaces reflect all too accurately the disposable values of contemporary life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wakeman_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-Wakeman-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Others, however, recognized him more for his significant achievements at different periods of his career. British film critic Peter Lloyd, for example, described Edwards, in 1971, as "the finest director working in the American commercial cinema at the present time." Edwards' biographers, <a href="/wiki/William_Luhr" title="William Luhr">William Luhr</a> and Peter Lehman,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> in an interview in 1974, called him "the finest American director working at this time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> They refer especially to the <i>Pink Panther'</i>s Clouseau, developed with the comedic skills of Peter Sellers, as a character "perfectly consistent" with his "absurdist view of the world, because he has no faith in anything and constantly adapts." Critic Stuart Byron calls his early <i>Pink Panther</i> films "two of the best comedies an American has ever made." Polls taken at the time showed that his name, as a director, was a rare "marketable commodity" in Hollywood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wakeman_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-Wakeman-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Edwards himself described one of the secrets to success in the film industry:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>For someone who wants to practise his art in this business, all you can hope to do, as <i>S.O.B.</i> says, is stick to your guns, make the compromises you must, and hope that somewhere along the way you acquire a few good friends who understand. And keep half a conscience."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wakeman_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-Wakeman-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
