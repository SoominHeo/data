<p><b>Blaine Devon Anderson</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feud_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Feud-1">[1]</a></sup> is a fictional character from the American musical comedy-drama television series <i><a href="/wiki/Glee_(TV_series)" title="Glee (TV series)">Glee</a></i>. Played by <a href="/wiki/Darren_Criss" title="Darren Criss">Darren Criss</a>, Blaine was introduced in the <a href="/wiki/Never_Been_Kissed_(Glee)" title="Never Been Kissed (Glee)">sixth episode</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Glee_(season_2)" title="Glee (season 2)">second season</a> as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Openly_gay" title="Openly gay">openly gay</a> lead singer of the Dalton Academy Warblers, a rival show choir to New Directions, the show's primary musical group. Blaine initially served as a mentor for New Directions member <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Hummel" title="Kurt Hummel">Kurt Hummel</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chris_Colfer" title="Chris Colfer">Chris Colfer</a>). Chemistry between the two, combined with fan support for the couple, led series co-creator <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Murphy_(writer)" title="Ryan Murphy (writer)">Ryan Murphy</a> to pair them romantically. Their relationship has been well received by critics, and they have been named "one of the most beloved TV couples of the millennium" by Jarett Wieselman of the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Post" title="New York Post">New York Post</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYP_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYP-2">[2]</a></sup> At the beginning of the third season, Blaine transfers to McKinley High and joins New Directions; concurrently, Criss was promoted from recurring guest star to the show's main cast.</p>
<p>Criss auditioned for <i>Glee</i> several times before being cast as Blaine, including for the lead role of <a href="/wiki/Finn_Hudson" title="Finn Hudson">Finn Hudson</a>. He believed he would be ill-suited to that character, but identifies with Blaine, having been raised among the "gay community".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kurt.27s_new_squeeze_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-kurt.27s_new_squeeze-3">[3]</a></sup> He plays Blaine as charismatic and confident, and finds his youthful self-acceptance a fitting counterpoint to common media portrayals of gay characters.</p>
<p>As the Warblers' lead vocalist and subsequent New Directions member, Blaine has performed a number of songs on the television series. His first, a <a href="/wiki/Cover_version" title="Cover version">cover version</a> of "<a href="/wiki/Teenage_Dream_(Katy_Perry_song)" title="Teenage Dream (Katy Perry song)">Teenage Dream</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Katy_Perry" title="Katy Perry">Katy Perry</a>, became the fastest-selling <i>Glee</i> single, reached number eight on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>, and was certified gold in the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bill20101127_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-bill20101127-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riaa_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-riaa-5">[5]</a></sup> Tracks by the Warblers have sold over 1.3 million copies. The songs became popular enough to warrant a Warbler soundtrack album, <i><a href="/wiki/Glee:_The_Music_Presents_the_Warblers" title="Glee: The Music Presents the Warblers">Glee: The Music Presents the Warblers</a></i>. Blaine has received mostly positive reviews from critics; Criss has been awarded a Rising Star accolade from the Gay and Lesbian Entertainment Critics Association for his portrayal. While the Blaine–Kurt ("Klaine") relationship has been met with acclaim, an episode storyline in which Blaine questioned his sexuality attracted negative reviews for undermining his previous characterization as a confident gay teenager.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Storylines">Storylines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blaine_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Storylines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_2">Season 2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blaine_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Season 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Blaine Anderson is introduced in the episode "<a href="/wiki/Never_Been_Kissed_(Glee)" title="Never Been Kissed (Glee)">Never Been Kissed</a>" as the lead soloist of the Dalton Academy Warblers <a href="/wiki/A_cappella" title="A cappella">a cappella</a> musical group. He meets <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Hummel" title="Kurt Hummel">Kurt Hummel</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chris_Colfer" title="Chris Colfer">Chris Colfer</a>), a member of the rival <a href="/wiki/Glee_club" title="Glee club">glee club</a> New Directions. When Kurt asks if Blaine is gay, Blaine matter-of-factly says he is; Kurt tells Blaine that he is being bullied at school for being gay, and Blaine reveals that he too was harassed at his old school, so he transferred to Dalton Academy, which enforces a no-bullying policy. Blaine befriends Kurt, and helps him stand up to his tormentor, <a href="/wiki/Dave_Karofsky" title="Dave Karofsky">Dave Karofsky</a> (<a href="/wiki/Max_Adler_(actor)" title="Max Adler (actor)">Max Adler</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NY_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NY-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN-NeverBeenKissed_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-NeverBeenKissed-7">[7]</a></sup> When the threats and violence against Kurt reach a dangerous level, he transfers to Dalton Academy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He falls in love with Blaine,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> who is initially oblivious to Kurt's feelings even as their friendship grows. Blaine enlists Kurt's help to serenade his crush Jeremiah (Alexander Nifong), the assistant manager at a local Gap store. Jeremiah is subsequently fired and rebuffs Blaine. Kurt confesses his feelings, and Blaine tells Kurt that he cares for him, but is terrible at romance and does not want to risk damaging their friendship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kurt and Blaine attend a party hosted by New Directions co-captain <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Berry" title="Rachel Berry">Rachel Berry</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lea_Michele" title="Lea Michele">Lea Michele</a>). The attendees play <a href="/wiki/Spin_the_bottle" title="Spin the bottle">spin the bottle</a>, which results in Rachel and Blaine kissing. In the aftermath, Blaine wonders whether he might be bisexual, and goes on a date with Rachel. When she kisses him again while they are both sober, he concludes that he is indeed gay, which relieves Kurt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sc_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-sc-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>After learning of Kurt’s ignorance of sexual matters, Blaine visits Kurt's father, <a href="/wiki/Burt_Hummel" title="Burt Hummel">Burt</a> (<a href="/wiki/Mike_O%27Malley" title="Mike O'Malley">Mike O'Malley</a>), and prompts him to give Kurt "<a href="/wiki/The_birds_and_the_bees" title="The birds and the bees">the talk</a>" about sex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sexy_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sexy-12">[12]</a></sup> As the Warblers prepare to perform at the Regional show choir competition, Kurt admits that he is jealous of how many solos Blaine gets. At a subsequent group meeting, Kurt arrives late and announces that the group's mascot canary is dead; he sings "<a href="/wiki/Blackbird_(Beatles_song)" title="Blackbird (Beatles song)">Blackbird</a>" in honor of the bird. While Kurt is singing, Blaine has a revelation, and later tells Kurt that he reciprocates his feelings and kisses him. At Regionals, the two sing a duet of "Candles" by <a href="/wiki/Hey_Monday" title="Hey Monday">Hey Monday</a>. The Warblers lose to New Directions, but while Kurt is very disappointed, Blaine tells him that even though they lost, in reality, they won each other which makes losing Regionals worth it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> After Kurt transfers back to McKinley, he invites Blaine to be his date at his junior prom in "<a href="/wiki/Prom_Queen_(Glee)" title="Prom Queen (Glee)">Prom Queen</a>"; both are shocked when Kurt wins Prom Queen due to having received an overwhelming number of unwanted write-in votes in the secret balloting. Karofsky, the Prom King, leaves to avoid dancing with a guy in the traditional dance between King and Queen, and Kurt dances with Blaine instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prom_Queen_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prom_Queen-14">[14]</a></sup> After Kurt returns from Nationals in New York, he and Blaine admit their love for one another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_York_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-New_York-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_3">Season 3</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blaine_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Season 3">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the first episode of the <a href="/wiki/Glee_(season_3)" title="Glee (season 3)">third season</a>, "<a href="/wiki/The_Purple_Piano_Project" title="The Purple Piano Project">The Purple Piano Project</a>", Blaine transfers to McKinley High at the beginning of his junior year to be closer to Kurt, who is a senior, and joins New Directions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Purple_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Purple-16">[16]</a></sup> He later auditions for the role of Bernardo in the school musical <i><a href="/wiki/West_Side_Story" title="West Side Story">West Side Story</a></i>, so as not to compete against Kurt who wants to play the male lead, Tony, but is cast as Tony himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unicorn_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Unicorn-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Asian_F_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Asian_F-18">[18]</a></sup> In the episode "<a href="/wiki/The_First_Time_(Glee)" title="The First Time (Glee)">The First Time</a>", Blaine is pursued by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sebastian_Smythe" title="Sebastian Smythe">Sebastian Smythe</a> (<a href="/wiki/Grant_Gustin" title="Grant Gustin">Grant Gustin</a>), a new Dalton Academy Warbler. Kurt and Blaine meet up with Sebastian at a gay bar, Blaine gets drunk, and afterward tries to get Kurt to have sex with him in the car. Kurt refuses, they have a fight, and Blaine decides to walk home. After the <i>West Side Story</i> opening night, they apologize to each other, and decide to go to Blaine's house. They are later shown in bed together, apparently having just had sex for the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-First_Time_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-First_Time-19">[19]</a></sup> When New Directions and the Warblers informally compete in "<a href="/wiki/Michael_(Glee)" title="Michael (Glee)">Michael</a>" to determine which club can perform <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a>'s music at the upcoming show choir Regionals, Sebastian throws a slushie containing rock salt at Kurt, but Blaine interposes himself and is hit in the eye; his cornea is badly scratched and requires surgery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michael_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Michael-20">[20]</a></sup> His eye heals, and he is back in time for New Directions to defeat the Warblers at Regionals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-On_My_Way_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-On_My_Way-21">[21]</a></sup> Blaine's older brother Cooper (<a href="/wiki/Matt_Bomer" title="Matt Bomer">Matt Bomer</a>), a successful actor in commercials, visits Ohio, and the two achieve a rapprochement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Big_Brother_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Brother-22">[22]</a></sup> Blaine's relationship with Kurt is later strained when Kurt text-flirts with a boy he met while preparing for his NYADA audition, and by Kurt's eagerness to leave for New York after graduation, which would separate the two at least until Blaine graduated the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dance_with_Somebody_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dance_with_Somebody-23">[23]</a></sup> The two patch things up, the glee club wins at Nationals, and the pair is still a couple at the end of the school year, though Blaine is still uneasy about the prolonged physical separation facing them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodbye_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goodbye-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_4">Season 4</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blaine_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Season 4">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the first episode of the <a href="/wiki/Glee_(season_4)" title="Glee (season 4)">fourth season</a>, "<a href="/wiki/The_New_Rachel" title="The New Rachel">The New Rachel</a>", Blaine becomes the lead singer of New Directions and successfully prompts Kurt to follow his New York City dreams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_Rachel_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-New_Rachel-25">[25]</a></sup> In addition, Blaine successfully runs for senior class president with <a href="/wiki/Sam_Evans" title="Sam Evans">Sam</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chord_Overstreet" title="Chord Overstreet">Chord Overstreet</a>), and the two subsequently develop a friendship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Makeover_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Makeover-26">[26]</a></sup> Kurt inadvertently pulls away from Blaine due to his Vogue.com internship; distraught and feeling isolated from his friends, Blaine cheats on Kurt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Makeover_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Makeover-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Break_Up_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Break_Up-27">[27]</a></sup> After confessing to Kurt of his infidelity, Kurt severs all ties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Break_Up_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Break_Up-27">[27]</a></sup> New Directions' Nationals trophy is stolen by Hunter Clarington (<a href="/wiki/Nolan_Gerard_Funk" title="Nolan Gerard Funk">Nolan Gerard Funk</a>), the new captain of the Dalton Academy Warblers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dynamic_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dynamic-28">[28]</a></sup> When Blaine goes to Dalton Academy to retrieve it, Hunter and Sebastian attempt to seduce Blaine into returning to the Warblers. Blaine becomes conflicted, believing that he does not belong in New Directions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dynamic_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dynamic-28">[28]</a></sup> Sam ultimately convinces him that, despite having done a bad thing to Kurt, Blaine is still a good person and an important member of New Directions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dynamic_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dynamic-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kurt begins to mend their relationship in "<a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving_(Glee)" title="Thanksgiving (Glee)">Thanksgiving</a>", just before New Directions loses at Sectionals to the Warblers, and they spend Christmas together in New York City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thanksgiving_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thanksgiving-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Actually_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Actually-30">[30]</a></sup> Though he and Kurt continue to be on good terms, Blaine finds himself developing a crush on his best friend, Sam, which he knows will come to nothing as he knows Sam is not gay; the two of them team up to find evidence that the Warblers cheated at Sectionals, which means New Directions will be competing at Regionals. He ends up going to the Sadie Hawkins dance with <a href="/wiki/Tina_Cohen-Chang" title="Tina Cohen-Chang">Tina Cohen-Chang</a> (<a href="/wiki/Jenna_Ushkowitz" title="Jenna Ushkowitz">Jenna Ushkowitz</a>), who has developed a crush on him, but as friends only.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sadie_Hawkins_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sadie_Hawkins-31">[31]</a></sup> When Kurt comes to Lima for the wedding of glee club director <a href="/wiki/Will_Schuester" title="Will Schuester">Will</a> (<a href="/wiki/Matthew_Morrison" title="Matthew Morrison">Matthew Morrison</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Emma_Pillsbury" title="Emma Pillsbury">Emma</a> (<a href="/wiki/Jayma_Mays" title="Jayma Mays">Jayma Mays</a>)—which Emma flees—he and Blaine make out beforehand, and sleep together afterward, though they do not resume a permanent relationship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-I_Do_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-I_Do-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Blaine had briefly joined the Cheerios when it looked like New Directions would be disbanding after their Sectionals loss, and cheerleading coach <a href="/wiki/Sue_Sylvester" title="Sue Sylvester">Sue</a> (<a href="/wiki/Jane_Lynch" title="Jane Lynch">Jane Lynch</a>) blackmails Blaine into rejoining; Blaine and Sam hatch up a plan to bring Sue down from the inside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feud_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Feud-1">[1]</a></sup> Blaine eventually confesses to Sam that he has feelings for him, to which Sam assures him that he is somewhat flattered by Blaine's honesty and attraction, and that it would not change the fact that Blaine is still Sam's best friend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guilty_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guilty-33">[33]</a></sup> Blaine still loves Kurt and asks Burt for his permission to propose to Kurt, but Burt tells him they're too young to marry, and advises him to wait.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wonder-ful_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wonder-ful-34">[34]</a></sup> Blaine remains determined, however, and while shopping for a ring, he meets Jan (<a href="/wiki/Patty_Duke" title="Patty Duke">Patty Duke</a>), a lesbian jeweler who has been with her partner Liz (<a href="/wiki/Meredith_Baxter" title="Meredith Baxter">Meredith Baxter</a>) for over thirty years. Jan offers to be a mentor to him, and Blaine and Kurt later have dinner with Jan and Liz, where Jan and Liz explain how their relationship evolved over the years and their experience with the growing mainstream acceptance of gay people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-All_or_Nothing_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-All_or_Nothing-35">[35]</a></sup> New Directions wins at Regionals, and Will and Emma get married immediately afterward, with the glee club, plus some graduates including Kurt, in attendance. Blaine is shown, after the ceremony is over, holding a jewelry box behind his back.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-All_or_Nothing_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-All_or_Nothing-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_5">Season 5</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blaine_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Season 5">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the season premiere, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Love_Love_Love_(Glee)" title="Love Love Love (Glee)">Love Love Love</a>", Blaine and Kurt agree to be boyfriends again. Blaine still wants to marry Kurt, and stages an elaborate and successful marriage proposal at Dalton Academy where he and Kurt first met, accompanied by New Directions, and all their rival show choir groups, including the Warblers. He auditions for NYADA and is accepted. New Directions comes in second at Nationals, and is disbanded by Sue for not being champions. Blaine graduates as class valedictorian, and then moves to New York to be with Kurt. They start off living together, but though they remain engaged, Blaine ultimately moves out because the two of them realize they still need their own space. The relationship between them goes through rocky patches, including Blaine's insecurity when Kurt becomes popular at school, and when an influential socialite and NYADA supporter hears Blaine perform and takes an interest in his future career, though she is not impressed by Kurt and ultimately tries to break them up, though she fails and ultimately supports them both. Their engagement is strengthened by having weathered these storms, and Blaine moves back in with Kurt.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_6">Season 6</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blaine_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Season 6">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Blaine returns to Lima after Kurt ended their engagement, having become so despondent that his schoolwork suffered and he was cut by NYADA. He becomes the coach of the Dalton Academy Warblers, and begins dating Dave Karofsky after a chance encounter at the local gay bar. Kurt, having realized he still loves Blaine and regretting that he ended the engagement, arranges for his NYADA off-campus semester to be in Lima, helping Rachel to coach a reinstated New Directions—Will had left McKinley High to coach Vocal Adrenaline. Unfortunately, by the time he arrives, Blaine and Karofsky are already a couple, and there is further strain between Kurt and Blaine as coaches of rival show choirs. In "<a href="/wiki/The_Hurt_Locker,_Part_Two" title="The Hurt Locker, Part Two">The Hurt Locker, Part Two</a>", Sue, who "ships Klaine" and is desperate for Kurt and Blaine to reunite, locks them both in a fake elevator and refuses to let them leave until they kiss. After resisting for a great many hours, Blaine and Kurt share a passionate kiss, but do not reunite afterward. In "<a href="/wiki/Transitioning_(Glee)" title="Transitioning (Glee)">Transitioning</a>", Blaine sings a duet with Kurt, "Somebody Loves You", and afterward, kisses Kurt. The following day, Blaine, knowing that he is still in love with Kurt, breaks up with Karofsky; however, Kurt is still seeing an older man, Walter. In "<a href="/wiki/A_Wedding_(Glee)" title="A Wedding (Glee)">A Wedding</a>", Kurt tells Walter that he is going to Brittany and Santana's wedding with Blaine, not him, and on Walter's advice, returns to Blaine; the two again become a couple. At the wedding, Brittany insists that Kurt and Blaine get married alongside her and Santana. Though skeptical at first, Kurt and Blaine agree and get married, with Burt officiating the joint ceremony. Dalton Academy burns down in "<a href="/wiki/The_Rise_and_Fall_of_Sue_Sylvester" title="The Rise and Fall of Sue Sylvester">The Rise and Fall of Sue Sylvester</a>", and the Warblers who transfer to McKinley are accepted into New Directions, with Blaine joining Rachel and Kurt as the coaches of the combined glee club. In the series finale, "<a href="/wiki/Dreams_Come_True_(Glee)" title="Dreams Come True (Glee)">Dreams Come True</a>", after New Directions wins Nationals, Blaine and Kurt leave for New York, with Blaine attending <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">NYU</a> and Kurt returning to NYADA along with a reinstated Rachel. The episode jumps ahead to 2020, and Blaine and Kurt are shown to be actors and a celebrity married couple; they also visit schools to entertain and talk about acceptance. Rachel, who is married to Jesse, is pregnant with Blaine and Kurt's child.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Casting_and_creation">Casting and creation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blaine_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Casting and creation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Blaine is portrayed by actor <a href="/wiki/Darren_Criss" title="Darren Criss">Darren Criss</a>, who had auditioned for <i>Glee</i> several times for several roles before the character Blaine was created.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TTO_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-TTO-36">[36]</a></sup> He originally auditioned to play <a href="/wiki/Finn_Hudson" title="Finn Hudson">Finn Hudson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Billboard2_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Billboard2-37">[37]</a></sup> Criss made several audition videos for <i>Glee</i>, of which he posted only one to the social networking website <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MySpace" title="MySpace">MySpace</a> where he sang a cover of Bill Withers' song Lean on Me. Although the series writers have seen them, he considers them supplemental to his main audition, which was for series creator <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Murphy_(writer)" title="Ryan Murphy (writer)">Ryan Murphy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TTO_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-TTO-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ-38">[38]</a></sup> Murphy had not seen any of Criss's previous auditions, but knew he was the right actor for Blaine as soon as he saw this one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AE_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-AE-39">[39]</a></sup> The actor cut his long hair before auditioning, to suit the "serious and preppy" role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prior to the production of season two, rumors circulated that upcoming episodes would feature a love interest for Kurt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Out_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Out-40">[40]</a></sup> Initial fan and media speculation suggested that <a href="/wiki/Sam_Evans" title="Sam Evans">Sam Evans</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chord_Overstreet" title="Chord Overstreet">Chord Overstreet</a>) was created to fill this role; however, according to Overstreet, Sam's storyline quickly paired him with <a href="/wiki/Quinn_Fabray" title="Quinn Fabray">Quinn Fabray</a> (<a href="/wiki/Dianna_Agron" title="Dianna Agron">Dianna Agron</a>) as a result of the chemistry the producers detected between him and Agron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In September 2010, it was confirmed that Criss would join the show in a "much-talked-about new gay role".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-darren_criss_cast_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-darren_criss_cast-42">[42]</a></sup> Media speculation suggested that Blaine would be Kurt's new love interest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Out_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Out-40">[40]</a></sup> His original character description was: "a cute and charismatic gay student from a rival Glee club named the Dalton Academy Warblers—will maintain a strictly platonic friendship with McKinley High's most out and proud pupil. But could that change as the season progresses? Yes, it could."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-darren_criss_cast_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-darren_criss_cast-42">[42]</a></sup> Murphy stated that Blaine's arc would be a significant one. He explained, "He sort of becomes Kurt's mentor and then maybe love [...] Kurt really admires him and respects him."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following his first few appearances, it was reported that Criss had been confirmed as a series regular for the remainder of season two and for <a href="/wiki/Glee_(season_3)" title="Glee (season 3)">season three</a> of <i>Glee</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> This was based on comments by Murphy, who said: "Darren has become such a sensation in one week, which I love. I think there's a hunger for him and a positive relationship role model. He’ll definitely continue through the year and longer."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AE_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-AE-39">[39]</a></sup> However, the actor later denied that his role had been upgraded and said: "It's never really been officially confirmed to me. I think the option is there and that they want to keep Blaine around."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Murphy revealed that Blaine may join New Directions during the third season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prom_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-prom-46">[46]</a></sup> Criss hoped that this would not come to pass, as he enjoys being at Dalton Academy, but conceded: "it's not my call. I'm happy to serve whatever story they want."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> For the <a href="/wiki/Glee_(season_3)" title="Glee (season 3)">third season</a> of <i>Glee</i>, Criss was promoted to a series regular,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CrissShumJr.Regulars_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-CrissShumJr.Regulars-48">[48]</a></sup> and Blaine did join New Directions when he transferred to McKinley High to be with Kurt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Characterization">Characterization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blaine_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Characterization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:32em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 85%; background-color: #c6dbf7;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"This is the first time I'd really seen an out student that was so young and innocent and really struggling with the big ordeal that it is to be an out student at such an early age. When other shows present the gay character thing, it's typically been in much more adult situations, like gay men living in New York or closeted men who are married and struggling with that ordeal, or having sex on the subway, but never really the core of the journey of defining your sexuality. Blaine offers a beautiful counter to that and makes such a great addition to the many-colored palette that is <i>Glee</i>."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">—Criss on Blaine<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Upon Blaine's first appearance, Criss described his character as being a "very charismatic, put-together, composed guy." He stated that although Blaine is gay, he is not "overly <a href="/wiki/Effeminacy" title="Effeminacy">queeny</a>, and not too <a href="/wiki/Butch_and_femme" title="Butch and femme">butch</a> either," and that while his sexuality is a "huge part of who he is", it is not a major facet of how he wishes to be perceived. Due to similarities which stem from their shared sexuality, Blaine "sees a lot of himself in Kurt, in terms of experiences and the way they feel about the world around them. He feels the need to impart his knowledge, be a source of strength for him, and really help him through what he's going through."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kurt.27s_new_squeeze_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-kurt.27s_new_squeeze-3">[3]</a></sup> Initially, Criss felt that the most important element of his character was to give Kurt "someone he can relate to", and demonstrate that Kurt could have "a young out male friend, a support system".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Out_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-Out-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Criss discussed his personal connection with Blaine in an interview with <i><a href="/wiki/Vanity_Fair_(magazine)" title="Vanity Fair (magazine)">Vanity Fair</a></i>. He explained that he grew up among the "gay community", being with theater performers, so was raised without a concept of sexuality being an issue. Criss stated that, although he identifies as straight, "it really doesn't come into play with me in this role. As an actor, your objective is always to play the scene. And this in case, he happens to be a gay teen."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kurt.27s_new_squeeze_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-kurt.27s_new_squeeze-3">[3]</a></sup> Talk show host and media personality <a href="/wiki/Ellen_DeGeneres" title="Ellen DeGeneres">Ellen DeGeneres</a> deemed Blaine "a very confident gay teen, which is something you don't see much on television."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellen_Youtube_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ellen_Youtube-51">[51]</a></sup> However, Amy Reiter of the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> assessed that "despite the image he projects, he, too, is just a kid trying to figure things out as he goes along."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Criss feels that Blaine's confidence is an important aspect of his character, as it is rare for gay teenagers on television to be so "sure of themselves." He hopes that "all the kids struggling with this issue can look to a guy like Blaine and feel [inspired] by his confidence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships">Relationships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blaine_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Relationships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Kurt–Blaine relationship, sometimes referred to by the <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> "Klaine" by <i>Glee</i> fans and the media,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> developed slowly. As the series showrunner, Murphy felt tasked with keeping the two apart as long as possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Out_40-3"><a href="#cite_note-Out-40">[40]</a></sup> He was initially unsure whether the relationship would become a romantic one, and intended to gauge public response to their friendship before planning future developments. He commented, "Part of me thinks he should be the boyfriend, part of me thinks he should just be the mentor. I didn't want to decide that until we got into sort of the middle of the season."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AE_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-AE-39">[39]</a></sup> In December 2010, Colfer said, "Fans really want it to happen. It's funny how many people want to see these boys hook up. We'll see."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Based on the characters' chemistry and the "immediate outcry" from fans who wanted to see them as a couple, Murphy decided to have Blaine become Kurt's love interest. Criss noted: "We all want to see Kurt happy, and like all great love stories, if you have two people that can be together you've got to hold it up."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Considering Blaine and Kurt's potential future together, Murphy planned to treat them the same as all other <i>Glee</i> relationships, by making their pairing "as flawed and as exposed as everyone else's."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Out_40-4"><a href="#cite_note-Out-40">[40]</a></sup> This sentiment was re-iterated by executive producer <a href="/wiki/Brad_Falchuk" title="Brad Falchuk">Brad Falchuk</a>, after the characters kissed for the first time. He revealed that their relationship would not run smoothly, and observed that once couples start dating, "Everything goes to hell."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prom_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-prom-46">[46]</a></sup> Colfer suggested that Kurt returning to McKinley may cause difficulties in their relationship, but noted "Distance makes the heart grow fonder, right? That’s what they tell me. So even if they do go through some bumps in the road, it would be very realistic."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prom_46-2"><a href="#cite_note-prom-46">[46]</a></sup> In a Q&amp;A with <a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a> on the day "Born This Way" ran with Kurt's McKinley return, Criss stated that Blaine and Kurt were "in the honeymoon stages" of their relationship, and would still be "at the end of the [second] season".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BillboardQA_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-BillboardQA-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a July 2012 interview with <i><a href="/wiki/E!_News" title="E! News">E! News</a></i>, Colfer said, "I would like to do something besides say 'I love you,' and I think Darren [Criss] and I agree on that. We're ready for the next step. They've been together for a while. Let's throw some spice and drama into that." Colfer quipped that he did not know what was in store for the couple, "I hear mixed things. I hear they're still together but then maybe they're breaking up."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enews_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-enews-58">[58]</a></sup> In September 2012, Criss seconded Colfer, "We're like an old married couple now. Let's shake it up!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>

<p>As Blaine, Criss features in many musical performances, which have been released as <a href="/wiki/Single_(music)" title="Single (music)">singles</a>, available for <a href="/wiki/Music_download" title="Music download">download</a>. His first performance, "<a href="/wiki/Teenage_Dream_(Katy_Perry_song)" title="Teenage Dream (Katy Perry song)">Teenage Dream</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Katy_Perry" title="Katy Perry">Katy Perry</a>, was featured on the soundtrack album <i><a href="/wiki/Glee:_The_Music,_Volume_4" title="Glee: The Music, Volume 4">Glee: The Music, Volume 4</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vol4_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-vol4-61">[61]</a></sup> It reached number eight on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> in the week ending November 27, 2010,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bill20101127_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-bill20101127-4">[4]</a></sup> and was the best-selling song in the U.S. that week, selling 214,000 copies: the largest figure for a <i>Glee</i> title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> It was only the second U.S.-certified gold single in the show's history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riaa_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-riaa-5">[5]</a></sup> Both <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> Jillian Mapes and Erica Futterman of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> deemed "Teenage Dream" the best song of "Never Been Kissed", the episode in which it featured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JM_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-JM-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EF_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-EF-64">[64]</a></sup> The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daily_News_(New_York)" title="Daily News (New York)">Daily News</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> Anthony Benigno gave the song an "A", and noted that it was "flawless" and improved on Perry's original.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AB_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-AB-65">[65]</a></sup> The performance was nominated for the Best Gay Moment of the Year and Favorite Music Video awards at the 2010 <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AfterEllen.com_and_AfterElton.com" title="AfterEllen.com and AfterElton.com">AfterElton.com</a></i> Visibility Awards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEV_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-AEV-66">[66]</a></sup> A <i>Billboard</i> cover-feature on Criss noted that the performance "arguably ushered in the trend of more current pop hits being reworked by the [<i>Glee</i>] cast."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MH_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-MH-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later songs performed by Blaine and the Warblers became popular enough to warrant a Warbler soundtrack album, <i>Glee: The Music Presents the Warblers</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The tracks had sold over 1.3 million copies as singles by the time the album was released.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-holly_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-holly-69">[69]</a></sup> Blaine performed the lead vocal on covers of <a href="/wiki/Train_(band)" title="Train (band)">Train</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Hey,_Soul_Sister" title="Hey, Soul Sister">Hey, Soul Sister</a>", which peaked at number 32 in Canada,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Bills,_Bills,_Bills" title="Bills, Bills, Bills">Bills, Bills, Bills</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Destiny%27s_Child" title="Destiny's Child">Destiny's Child</a>, which reached number 44 in the U.S.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vday_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vday-73">[73]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Robin_Thicke" title="Robin Thicke">Robin Thicke</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/When_I_Get_You_Alone" title="When I Get You Alone">When I Get You Alone</a>", which got to number 47 on the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vday_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vday-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Criss downplayed his performance of "Bills, Bills, Bills", and jested that he ought to issue the group's lead singer, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beyonc%C3%A9_Knowles" title="Beyoncé Knowles">Beyoncé Knowles</a>, an apology for his cover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Blaine also led on "<a href="/wiki/Silly_Love_Songs" title="Silly Love Songs">Silly Love Songs</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Wings_(band)" title="Wings (band)">Wings</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Maroon_5" title="Maroon 5">Maroon 5</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Misery_(Maroon_5_song)" title="Misery (Maroon 5 song)">Misery</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zap16_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-zap16-77">[77]</a></sup> which reached 45 and 52 respectively in the U.S.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vday_73-2"><a <p>Blaine duetted with Kurt on <a href="/wiki/Frank_Loesser" title="Frank Loesser">Frank Loesser</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Baby,_It%27s_Cold_Outside" title="Baby, It's Cold Outside">Baby, It's Cold Outside</a>", which was included on <i><a href="/wiki/Glee:_The_Music,_The_Christmas_Album" title="Glee: The Music, The Christmas Album">Glee: The Music, The Christmas Album</a></i> and reached number 53 in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> It was reportedly the most downloaded track of the album, and called "by far the gayest thing that has ever been on TV" by Colfer. Murphy expressed his pride in the number for "push[ing] the envelope a bit."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BICO_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-BICO-82">[82]</a></sup> Jessica Ronayne of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zap2it" title="Zap2it">Zap2it</a></i> called the duet one of the Christmas episode's few saving graces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JessicaRonayne_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-JessicaRonayne-83">[83]</a></sup> Blaine and Kurt also duetted on <a href="/wiki/Neon_Trees" title="Neon Trees">Neon Trees</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Animal_(Neon_Trees_song)" title="Animal class="reference" id="cite_ref-CB_78-1"><a href="#cite_note-CB-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> In an April 2011 interview, Criss stated that he did not feel established enough within the cast to make song suggestions, but had mentioned liking "Animal" to Murphy, who included it in a script soon thereafter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MH_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-MH-67">[67]</a></sup> Blaine shared a duet of "<a href="/wiki/Don%27t_You_Want_Me" title="Don't You Want Me">Don't You Want Me</a>" by <a href="/wiki/The_Human_League" title="The Human League">The Human League</a> with Rachel, which Reiter deemed a musical highlight of the episode "<a href="/wiki/Blame_It_on_the_Alcohol" title="Blame It on the Alcohol">Blame It on the Alcohol</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amyreiter2_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-amyreiter2-87">[87]</a></sup> It was included on <i><a <p>Over the course of the season, Blaine's musical performances leading the Warblers became so prevalent that he was called out for it on-screen. Criss offered the insight: "Blaine definitely had his moment in the sun. I think it's time to focus back on the characters that fans of the show really know and love. Completely objectively from watching the show, I was like, 'Why does Blaine get all these songs? This is ridiculous. I want to hear other people doing stuff.' I think we're focusing a little more back on New Directions and taking a little bit of a break from the Warblers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MH_67-2"><a href="#cite_note-MH-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the first episode of <a href="/wiki/Glee_(season_3)" title="Glee (season 3)">season three</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Glee_(TV_series)" title="Glee (TV series)">Glee</a></i>, "<a href="/wiki/The_Purple_Piano_Project" title="The Purple Piano Project">The Purple Piano Project</a>", Blaine sings "<a href="/wiki/It%27s_Not_Unusual" title="It's Not Unusual">It's Not Unusual</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Tom_Jones_(singer)" title="Tom Jones (singer)">Tom Jones</a> when he transfers to McKinley and quits the Warblers for New Directions. After successfully auditioning later in the season for the school's production of <i><a href="/wiki/West_Side_Story" title="West Side Story">West Side Story</a></i> with "Something's Coming" from the musical, he sings "<a href="/wiki/Tonight_(1956_song)" title="Tonight (1956 song)">Tonight</a>" in rehearsal and "One Hand, One Heart" in performance, both with his co-star Rachel. He sings lead or co-lead on several songs with New Directions, including "<a href="/wiki/Last_Friday_Night_(T.G.I.F.)" title="Last Friday Night (T.G.I.F.)">Last Friday Night (T.G.I.F.)</a>"; "<a href="/wiki/Fuckin%27_Perfect" title="Fuckin' Perfect">Perfect</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Let_It_Snow!_Let_It_Snow!_Let_It_Snow!" title="Let It Snow! Let It Snow! Let It Snow!">Let It Snow! Let It Snow! <p>In special tribute episode to <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a>, "<a href="/wiki/Michael_(Glee)" title="Michael (Glee)">Michael</a>", Blaine sings "<a href="/wiki/Wanna_Be_Startin%27_Somethin%27" title="Wanna Be Startin' Somethin'">Wanna Be Startin' Somethin'</a>". Futterman wrote that Blaine's "slick showmanship" made him a "natural for lead vocals",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/TVLine" title="TVLine">TVLine</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Michael Slezak called it "the best use of Darren Criss' voice on Glee in quite some time" and gave it an "A−". <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> Joseph Brannigan Lynch also gave it an "A−", and said Criss "captured the excitement and the spunky bravado of the original".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a>'s Kevin P. Sullivan was another fan of "the sheer awesomeness of Darren Criss" on the song, and characterized it as "a tribute that's more respectful than any other in the episode".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> In the fourteenth episode of the third season, "<a href="/wiki/On_My_Way_(Glee)" title="On My Way (Glee)">On My Way</a>", Blaine performs "<a href="/wiki/Cough_Syrup_(song)" title="Cough Syrup (song)">Cough Syrup</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Young_the_Giant" title="Young the Giant">Young the Giant</a>, which is sung during the <a href="/wiki/Dave_Karofsky" title="Dave Karofsky">Dave Karofsky</a> suicide sequence. Futterman said Blaine "flawlessly deliver[ed] the vocal".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> Lynch called it a "chilling rendition" that was "hard to shake" and gave it an <span class="nowrap">"A−",</span> the same grade given by Slezak, who wrote, "taken on its own, Blaine's vocal was strong and passionate—perhaps better than the original".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> Bobby Hankinson of the <i><a href="/wiki/Houston_Chronicle" title="Houston Chronicle">Houston Chronicle</a></i> said it was the "best" of the episode, and added that the "scene was really, really well done and carried maximum emotional punch".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> In the following episode, "<a href="/wiki/Big_Brother_(Glee)" title="Big Brother (Glee)">Big Brother</a>", Blaine shares a duet of <a href="/wiki/Gotye" title="Gotye">Gotye</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Somebody_That_I_Used_to_Know" title="Somebody That I Used to Know">Somebody That I Used to Know</a>" with his brother Cooper (<a href="/wiki/Matt_Bomer" title="Matt Bomer">Matt Bomer</a>). Crystal Bell of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HuffPost_TV" title="HuffPost TV">HuffPost TV</a></i> called it the "highlight of the episode", and it was Hankinson's favorite performance: "Bomer and Criss did a fantastic job bringing it to life".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> Futterman said that "it was initially disconcerting to envision" two brothers singing about a "former love", but she noted that it "was less weird in context".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> In its first week, the cover sold 152,000 digital downloads in the US, and was number twenty-six on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-US_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-US-99">[99]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blaine_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Blaine has received mostly positive reviews from television critics. Ellen DeGeneres praised Criss's portrayal, and described him as one of <i>Glee</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> breakout stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellen_Youtube_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ellen_Youtube-51">[51]</a></sup> <i>Entertainment Weekly</i> named him one of the breakout stars of 2010, with the comment: "It took about 2 minutes and 11 seconds on <i>Glee</i> for Darren Criss to turn into a bona fide Gleek phenomenon. [...] And it doesn't hurt that his character Blaine's warm relationship with the show's other openly gay character, Chris Colfer's Kurt, continues to resonate with fans."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> In November 2010, the publication also named Criss the series' best guest-star to date, and praised him for "seamlessly entering the fold with his charm and great voice."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> Ronayne deemed him "a much better addition to the show than <a href="/wiki/Chord_Overstreet" title="Chord Overstreet">Chord Overstreet</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JessicaRonayne_83-1"><a href="#cite_note-JessicaRonayne-83">[83]</a></sup> Following his <i>Glee</i> debut, Criss won the We're Wilde About You Rising Star Award at the 2011 Dorian Awards, presented by the Gay and Lesbian Entertainment Critics Association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> He also won the 2011 Teen Choice Award for Choice TV Breakout Star.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HolRep_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-HolRep-104">[104]</a></sup></p>
<p>Indecision over his sexuality in the episode "<a href="/wiki/Blame_It_on_the_Alcohol" title="Blame It on the Alcohol">Blame It on the Alcohol</a>" drew some negative reviews of Blaine. Reiter noted that, "So much of Blaine's charm has been his certainty about who he is." Though she found his subplot with Rachel "fun", she commented: "Blaine's overwrought speech in the coffee shop after Rachel asks him out just felt off-key. Saying 'bye' to the Blaine sexual-confusion storyline wouldn't make us angry at all."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amyreiter2_87-1"><a href="#cite_note-amyreiter2-87">[87]</a></sup> Todd VanDerWerff of <i><a href="/wiki/The_A.V._Club" title="The A.V. Club">The A.V. Club</a></i> also criticised the storyline, which, he wrote, "seemed like it might be an interesting, complicated look at teenage <a href="/wiki/Human_sexuality" title="Human sexuality">sexuality</a> and how it can seem formed but might be more fluid than most teens would give it credit for, then lost its nerve and took the easy way out."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-avclub_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-avclub-105">[105]</a></sup> <i>AfterElton.com</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> Chris O'Guinn felt that the only aspect of the storyline handled acceptably was Blaine questioning whether he was bisexual, rather than outright straight. He criticized the shallow examination of his feelings, and wrote: "Bisexuality is such a contentious issue that it should not be fumbled this way. For a character to say 'maybe I'm bi' in one scene and then the next say, 'nope, definitely gay' is almost cruel in how dismissive it is toward bisexuals by trivializing the intense confusion that many bisexual people feel in coming to terms with their orientation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> In a more positive review, Canning opined that Blaine and Kurt's conversation about sexuality was "great". He was "glad it wasn't an easy talk for either of them" and said that it "felt very real for kids in this situation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN-BIOTA_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-BIOTA-107">[107]</a></sup></p>

<p>Blaine and Kurt's relationship has been generally well received. They were named Favorite TV Couple at the 2010 <i>AfterElton.com</i> Visibility Awards,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEV_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-AEV-66">[66]</a></sup> and have been lauded for "leading the way" in representing gay teenagers on television by <i>Entertainment Weekly</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BICO_82-1"><a href="#cite_note-BICO-82">[82]</a></sup> Jarett Wieselman of the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Post" title="New York Post">New York Post</a></i> referred to them as "one of the most beloved TV couples of the millennium".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYP_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYP-2">[2]</a></sup> When they "finally solidified what their relationship actually is" in the episode "<a href="/wiki/Silly_Love_Songs_(Glee)" title="Silly Love Songs (Glee)">Silly Love Songs</a>", <i><a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> Robert Canning noted that his opinion of Blaine improved. He commented: "I love that the [serenading of Jeremiah] blew up in Blaine's face. The character has always come off to me as annoyingly arrogant, but "Silly Love Songs" humanized the guy. Guess I may have to like him now."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-108">[108]</a></sup> Their first kiss, in the episode "<a href="/wiki/Original_Song" title="Original Song">Original Song</a>", was met with critical acclaim. Mark Perigard of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Boston_Herald" title="The Boston Herald">The Boston Herald</a></i> wrote, "It was utterly, sweetly romantic, and Criss sold the hell out of the moment. It's long overdue and it will silence the growing legion of critics out there who were unhappy with the pace of this story."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BostonH_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-BostonH-109">[109]</a></sup> <i>Entertainment Weekly</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> Mandi Bierly was so impressed with the scene that she was initially concerned it may be a dream sequence, "because we don't get romantic, unapologetic first kisses like that between young gay characters on network TV." She wrote, "Relationships aren't easy. They’ll make mistakes. But if they stay true to themselves, and continue to be as open and honest with each other, we're in for something special."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MB_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-MB-110">[110]</a></sup> Kevin Fallon of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Atlantic" title="The Atlantic">The Atlantic</a></i> thought the kiss was "sweet", and stated that he was pleased that it attracted no controversy whatsoever.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> Aly Semigran of MTV praised the interaction between Blaine and Kurt. She called the kiss scene a "sweet, real and, shockingly, un-hyped moment", and praised Criss and Colfer for "handl[ing] it with dignity and honesty".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MTV_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-MTV-112">[112]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Lesley Goldberg of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i> was pleased Blaine's season three transfer to McKinley in the first episode increased Criss' screen time with Colfer and the New Directions cast, she stated that the move "screams of co-dependency."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> In contrast, <i>Entertainment Weekly</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> Abby West found it romantic, and noted: "Blaine and Kurt, with their budding love and witty, pseudo-urbane ways, are my favorite couple to watch. ... I look forward to hearing [Blaine] sing more, watching him bump up against the insular Glee clubbers, and seeing him show off his non-uniform attire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> Several reviewers were unhappy with the second episode's revelation that Blaine was a junior, not a senior like Kurt, as had been implied in the previous season. VanDerWerff wrote that Blaine "seems to have simultaneously gotten younger and had a complete personality transplant over the summer", <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> Rae Votta noted "the continuity-bending plot point that he's somehow a Junior and not a Senior like his boyfriend", and Samantha Urban of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dallas_Morning_News" title="The Dallas Morning News">The Dallas Morning News</a></i> allowed her exasperation to show: "Oh really, <i>Glee</i>? Blaine's a junior? Blaine's younger than Kurt? Fine. FINE."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AV302_115-0"><a href="#cite_note-AV302-115">[115]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBRV302_116-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBRV302-116">[116]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dallas302_117-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dallas302-117">[117]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kurt and Blaine make the decision to have sex for the first time in "<a href="/wiki/The_First_Time_(Glee)" title="The First Time (Glee)">The First Time</a>" episode; many critics were enthusiastic about the fact that a gay couple was being given such a storyline. Canning said that Kurt and Blaine's "attempts to get a little wild", and "trying to grow up faster than they should", were "the better parts of the episode as they felt the most realistic".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN-TheFirstTime_118-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-TheFirstTime-118">[118]</a></sup> Futterman praised their departure from the bar as a "very faithful and honest scene".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RS_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-RS-119">[119]</a></sup> VanDerWerff and <a href="/wiki/BuddyTV" title="BuddyTV">BuddyTV</a> contributor John Kubicek both had issues with Blaine's characterization. The latter asserted that he "just behaves however the writers need him to behave in order for the scene to work", while the former said that Blaine's season three storyline "hasn’t been bad by any means, but it does feel like Darren Criss is playing someone who’s quite a bit different from the guy he was playing last season".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buddy_120-0"><a href="#cite_note-Buddy-120">[120]</a></sup> Bell was impressed by the way the characters' relationship "inspires gay youth in a way that we haven't seen on network television yet" and called them "amazing role models for all teens", and <i>The Atlantic</i> writer Kevin Fallon said it was "remarkable" and a "milestone" that "the decision by gay teen characters to lose their virginities is given equal weight to that of a straight couple".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL_121-0"><a href="#cite_note-ATL-121">[121]</a></sup></p>
<p>Discussing public response to his character, Criss stated that he particularly enjoyed comments from "people from parts of the world who are maybe not as exposed to certain ideologies", but had reconsidered their stance on relationships and human rights as a result of the Blaine–Kurt storyline. He called this response "phenomenal", and said: "I was a straight kid growing up in a very gay community and it's something that I've had to watch so many friends have to struggle with and have no place to go to identify in kind of a grander media culture. To be a small piece of that machine is incredibly wonderful."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup></p>
