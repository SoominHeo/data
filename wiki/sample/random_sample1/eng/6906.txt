<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Blue white screen</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>The <b>blue-white screen</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Genetic_screen" title="Genetic screen">screening technique</a> that allows for the rapid and convenient detection of recombinant bacteria in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vector_DNA" title="Vector DNA">vector</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Molecular_cloning" title="Molecular cloning">molecular cloning</a> experiments. <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> of interest is ligated into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vector_DNA" title="Vector DNA">vector</a>. The vector is then <a href="/wiki/Transformation_(genetics)" title="Transformation (genetics)">inserted</a> into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Competent_cell" title="Competent cell">competent host cell</a> viable for transformation, which are then grown in the presence of <a href="/wiki/X-gal" title="X-gal">X-gal</a>. Cells transformed with vectors containing <a href="/wiki/Recombinant_DNA" title="Recombinant DNA">recombinant DNA</a> will produce white colonies; cells transformed with non-recombinant plasmids (i.e. only the vector) grow into blue colonies. This method of screening is usually performed using a suitable <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacterial strain</a>, but other organisms such as yeast may also be used.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_white_screen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Molecular_cloning" title="Molecular cloning">Molecular cloning</a> is one of the most commonly used procedures in <a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">molecular biology</a>. A gene of interest may be inserted into a plasmid vector via <a href="/wiki/DNA_ligase" title="DNA ligase">ligation</a>, and the plasmid is then transformed into <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i> cells. However, not all the plasmids transformed into cells may contain the desired gene insert, and checking each individual colony for the presence of the insert is time-consuming, therefore a method for the detection of the insert would be useful for making this procedure less time- and labor-intensive. One of the early methods developed for the detection of insert is blue-white screening which allows for identification of successful products of cloning reactions through the colour of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterial" title="Bacterial">bacterial</a> colony.</p>
<p>The method is based on the principle of α-complementation of the <a href="/wiki/Beta-galactosidase" title="Beta-galactosidase">β-galactosidase</a> gene. This phenomenon of α-complementation was first demonstrated in work done by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Agnes_Ullmann&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Agnes Ullmann (page does not exist)">Agnes Ullmann</a> in the laboratory of <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Jacob" title="François Jacob">François Jacob</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Monod" title="Jacques Monod">Jacques Monod</a>, where the function of an inactive mutant β-galactosidase with deleted sequence was shown to be rescued by a fragment of β-galactosidase in which that same sequence, the α-donor peptide, is still intact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Langley <i>et al.</i> showed that the mutant non-functional <a href="/wiki/Beta-galactosidase" title="Beta-galactosidase">β-galactosidase</a> was lacking in part of its N-terminus with its residues 11—41 deleted, but it may be complemented by a peptide formed of residues 3—90 of β-galactosidase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/M13_bacteriophage" title="M13 bacteriophage">M13 filamentous phage</a> containing sequence coding for the first 145 amino acid was later constructed by <i>Messing et al.</i>, and α-complementation via the use of a vector was demonstrated by the formation of blue plaques when cells containing the inactive protein were infected by the phage and then grown in plates containing X-gal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The pUC series of plasmid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cloning_vectors" title="Cloning vectors">cloning vectors</a> by Vieira and Messing was developed from the M13 system and were the first plasmids constructed to take advantage of this screening method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In this method, DNA ligated into the plasmid disrupts the α peptide and therefore the complementation process, and no functional β-galactosidase can form. Cells transformed with plasmid containing an insert therefore form white colonies, while cells transformed with plasmid without an insert form blue colonies; result of a successful ligation can thus be easily identified by the white coloration of cells formed from the unsuccessful blue ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sambrook_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-sambrook-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_mechanism">Molecular mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_white_screen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Molecular mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Blue_white_assay_Ecoli.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="706" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Blue_white_assay_Ecoli.jpg/250px-Blue_white_assay_Ecoli.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Blue_white_assay_Ecoli.jpg/375px-Blue_white_assay_Ecoli.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Blue_white_assay_Ecoli.jpg/500px-Blue_white_assay_Ecoli.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Blue_white_assay_Ecoli.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A schematic representation of the Blue-white assay, used to screen for recombinant vectors</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Beta-galactosidase" title="Beta-galactosidase">β-galactosidase</a> is a protein encoded by the <i>lacZ</i> gene of the <a href="/wiki/Lac_operon" title="Lac operon"><i>lac</i> operon</a>, and it exists as a <a href="/wiki/Homotetramer" title="Homotetramer">homotetramer</a> in its active state. However, a mutant β-galactosidase derived from the M15 strain of <i>E. coli</i> has its N-terminal residues 11—41 deleted and this mutant, the ω-peptide, is unable to form a tetramer and is inactive. This mutant form of protein however may return fully to its active tetrameric state in the presence of an N-terminal fragment of the protein, the α-peptide. The rescue of function of the mutant β-galactosidase by the α-peptide is called α-complementation.</p>
<p>In this method of screening, the host <i>E. coli</i> strain carries the <i>lacZ</i> deletion mutant (<i>lacZΔM15</i>} which contains the ω-peptide, while the plasmids used carry the <i>lacZα</i> sequence which encodes the first 59 residues of β-galactosidase, the α-peptide. Neither is functional by itself. However, when the two peptides are expressed together, as when a plasmid containing the <i>lacZα</i> sequence is transformed into a <i>lacZΔM15</i> cells, they form a functional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%92-galactosidase" title="Β-galactosidase">β-galactosidase</a> enzyme.</p>
<p>The blue/white screening method works by disrupting this α-complementation process. The plasmid carries within the <i>lacZα</i> sequence an internal <a href="/wiki/Multiple_cloning_site" title="Multiple cloning site">multiple cloning site</a> (MCS). This MCS within the <i>lacZα</i> sequence can be cut by restriction enzymes so that the foreign DNA may be inserted within the <i>lacZ</i>α gene, thereby disrupting the gene that produces α-peptide. Consequently, in cells containing the plasmid with an insert, no functional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%92-galactosidase" title="Β-galactosidase">β-galactosidase</a> may be formed.</p>
<p>The presence of an active β-galactosidase can be detected by <a href="/wiki/X-gal" title="X-gal">X-gal</a>, a colourless <a href="/wiki/Structural_analog" title="Structural analog">analog</a> of lactose that may be cleaved by β-galactosidase to form 5-bromo-4-chloro-indoxyl, which then spontaneously dimerizes and oxidizes to form a bright blue insoluble pigment 5,5'-dibromo-4,4'-dichloro-indigo. This results in a characteristic blue colour in cells containing a functional β-galactosidase. Blue colonies therefore show that they may contain a vector with an uninterrupted <i>lacZα</i> (therefore no insert), while white colonies, where X-gal is not hydrolyzed, indicate the presence of an insert in <i>lacZα</i> which disrupts the formation of an active β-galactosidase.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Practical_considerations">Practical considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_white_screen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Practical considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The correct type of <a href="/wiki/Vector_(molecular_biology)" title="Vector (molecular biology)">vector</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Competent_cell" title="Competent cell">competent cells</a> are important considerations when planning a blue white screen. The plasmid must contain the <i>lacZ</i>α, and examples of such plasmids are <a href="/wiki/PUC19" title="PUC19">pUC19</a> and <a href="/wiki/PBluescript" title="PBluescript">pBluescript</a>. The <i>E. coli</i> cell should contain the mutant <i>lacZ</i> gene with deleted sequence (i.e. <i>lacZΔM15</i>), and some of the commonly used cells with such genotype are JM109, DH5α, and XL1-Blue.</p>
<p>It should also be understood that the lac operon is affected by the presence of glucose. The protein <a href="/wiki/PEP_group_translocation" title="PEP group translocation">EIIA<sup>Glc</sup></a>, which is involved in glucose import, shuts down lactose permease when glucose is being transported into the cell. The media used in agar plate therefore should not include glucose.</p>
<p>X-gal is light-sensitive and therefore its solution and plates containing X-gal should be stored in the dark. <a href="/wiki/Isopropyl_%CE%B2-D-1-thiogalactopyranoside" title="Isopropyl β-D-1-thiogalactopyranoside">Isopropyl β-D-1-thiogalactopyranoside</a> (IPTG), which functions as the inducer of the <i>lac</i> operon, may be used in the media to enhance the expression of LacZ.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drawbacks">Drawbacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_white_screen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Drawbacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some white colonies may not contain the desired recombinant plasmid for a number of reasons. The ligated DNA may not be the correct one or not properly ligated, and it is possible for some linearized vector to be transformed, its ends "repaired" and ligated together such that no LacZα is produced and no blue colonies may be formed. Mutation can also lead to the α-fragment not being expressed. A colony with no vector at all will also appear white, and may sometimes appear as satellite colonies after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotic</a> used has been depleted. It is also possible that blue colonies may contain the insert. This occurs when the insert is "in frame" with the LacZα gene and a STOP codon is absent in the insert. This can lead to the expression of a fusion protein that has a functional LacZα if its structure is not disrupted. The correct recombinant construct can sometimes give lighter blue colonies which may complicate its identification.</p>
