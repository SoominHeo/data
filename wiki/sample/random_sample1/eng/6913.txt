<p>A <b>blini</b> (sometimes spelled <b>bliny</b>) or, rarely, <b>blin (more accurate as a single form of the noun)</b> is a thin pancake, traditionally made from <a href="/wiki/Buckwheat" title="Buckwheat">buckwheat</a> flour<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and served with <a href="/wiki/Sour_cream" title="Sour cream">sour cream</a>, <a href="/wiki/Butter" title="Butter">butter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caviar" title="Caviar">caviar</a>, and other garnishes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ahd_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ahd-1">[1]</a></sup> Should not be confused with blintzes ("blinchik" in Russian) also known as crepes or <a href="/wiki/Palatschinke" title="Palatschinke">palatschinke</a>.</p>
<p>Some English dictionaries record usage of the forms <i>blin</i> as singular and <i>blini</i> or <i>bliny</i> as plural, which correspond to the originally Russian forms, but some dictionaries consider this usage so rare in English that they do not mention <i>blin</i> at all and only record the widespread modern regular usage of <i>blini</i> for the singular and <i>blinis</i> for the plural, for example the American Heritage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ahd_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ahd-1">[1]</a></sup> MacMillan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and Vocabulary.com<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> dictionaries. Some cookbooks and restaurants use <i>blin</i> and <i>blintchick</i> as in Russian to refer to <a href="/wiki/Cr%C3%AApe" title="CrÃªpe">crÃªpes</a>.</p>
<p>Blintzes are apparently an offshoot (an evolved or variant form) of blini. They are thin pancakes usually made of wheat flour (not buckwheat), folded to form a casing (as for cheese or fruit) and then sautÃ©ed or baked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Blinis were considered by early Slavic people in pre-<a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> times to be a symbol of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>, due to their round form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia-6">[6]</a></sup> They were traditionally prepared at the end of winter to honor the rebirth of the new sun (Butter Week, or <a href="/wiki/Maslenitsa" title="Maslenitsa">Maslenitsa</a>, also called "pancake week").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia-6">[6]</a></sup> This tradition was adopted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodoxy" title="Eastern Orthodoxy">Orthodox church</a> and is carried on to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pancake_Day" title="Pancake Day">present day</a>. Blini were also served at <a href="/wiki/Wake_(ceremony)" title="Wake (ceremony)">wakes</a> to commemorate the recently deceased.</p>
<p>Traditional Russian blinis are made with yeasted batter, which is left to rise and then diluted with cold or boiling water or <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a>. When diluted with boiling water, they are referred to as zavarniye bliny. Traditionally, blinis are baked in a <a href="/wiki/Russian_oven" title="Russian oven">Russian oven</a>. The process of preparing blinis is still referred to as baking in Russian, even though they are nowadays pan-fried, like pancakes. French <a href="/wiki/Cr%C3%AApe" title="CrÃªpe">crÃªpes</a> made from unyeasted batter (usually made of flour, milk, and eggs) are also common in Russia. All kinds of flour is used, from <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buckwheat" title="Buckwheat">buckwheat</a> to <a href="/wiki/Oatmeal" title="Oatmeal">oatmeal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">millet</a>, although wheat is currently the most popular.</p>
<p>Blinis were popularized in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> by Eastern European Jewish immigrants who used them in <a href="/wiki/Jewish_cuisine" title="Jewish cuisine">Jewish cuisine</a>. While not part of any specific religious rite in <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>, blinis stuffed with a <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">cheese</a> filling and then fried in oil are served on holidays such as <a href="/wiki/Hanukkah" title="Hanukkah">Chanukah</a> (as oil played a pivotal role in the miracle of the Chanukah story) and <a href="/wiki/Shavuot" title="Shavuot">Shavuot</a> (when dairy dishes are traditionally served within the <a href="/wiki/Ashkenazi_Jews" title="Ashkenazi Jews">Ashkenazi</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Minhag" title="Minhag">minhag</a></i>). Blinis, also called "blinchiki" in Russian, are ordinarily stuffed before frying a second time. Fillings include chocolate, mushrooms, meat, rice, mashed potatoes, and cheese.</p>

<p>Some ways that blinis are prepared and served include the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Blinis made from <a href="/wiki/Batter_(cooking)" title="Batter (cooking)">batter</a> containing various additions such as grated <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potato</a> or <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apple</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raisins" title="Raisins">raisins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia-6">[6]</a></sup> Such blinis are quite common in <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a> and are more solidly filled than the spongy pancakes usually eaten in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.</li>
<li>Blinis covered with <a href="/wiki/Butter" title="Butter">butter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sour_cream" title="Sour cream">sour cream</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jam" title="Jam">jam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Caviar" title="Caviar">caviar</a> (whitefish, salmon, or traditional sturgeon caviar). They may be folded or rolled into a tube.</li>
<li>Blintzes. A filling such as jam, fruit, potato, <a href="/wiki/Quark_(dairy_product)" title="Quark (dairy product)">quark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cottage_cheese" title="Cottage cheese">cottage cheese</a> or <a href="/wiki/Farmer_cheese" title="Farmer cheese">farmer cheese</a>, cooked <a href="/wiki/Ground_meat" title="Ground meat">ground meat</a>, cooked <a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">chicken</a>, and even chopped mushrooms, bean sprouts, cabbage, and onions (for a Chinese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eggroll" title="Eggroll">eggroll</a>-type blintz) is rolled or enveloped into a pre-fried blintz and then the blintz is lightly re-fried, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saut%C3%A9eing" title="SautÃ©eing">sautÃ©ed</a>, or baked. Such blintzes are also called <i>nalysnyky</i> (Ukrainian: <i>Ð½Ð°Ð»Ð¸ÑÐ½Ð¸ÐºÐ¸</i>) or <i>blinchiki</i> (Russian: <i>Ð±Ð»Ð¸Ð½ÑÐ¸ÐºÐ¸</i>) or <i>krokiety</i> (Polish). The caviar filling is popular during Russian-style <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cocktail_parties" title="Cocktail parties">cocktail parties</a>.</li>
<li><b>Buckwheat blinis</b> are part of traditional <a href="/wiki/Russian_cuisine" title="Russian cuisine">Russian cuisine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia-6">[6]</a></sup> They are also widespread in <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia-6">[6]</a></sup> where they are sometimes known as <i>hrechanyky</i> (Ukrainian: <i>Ð³ÑÐµÑÐ°Ð½Ð¸ÐºÐ¸</i>), and Lithuania's <a href="/wiki/Dz%C5%ABkija" title="DzÅ«kija">DzÅ«kija</a> region, the only region in the country where buckwheat is grown, where they are called <i>grikiÅ³ blynai</i>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Blinis are the traditional meal in <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a> on Fat Tuesday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
