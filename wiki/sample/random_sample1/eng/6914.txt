<p><b>Blizzard Entertainment, Inc.</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Video_game_developer" title="Video game developer">video game developer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Video_game_publisher" title="Video game publisher">publisher</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Irvine,_California" title="Irvine, California">Irvine, California</a>, and is currently a subsidiary of American company <a href="/wiki/Activision_Blizzard" title="Activision Blizzard">Activision Blizzard</a>. The company was founded on February 8, 1991, under the name <b>Silicon &amp; Synapse</b> by three graduates of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Los_Angeles" title="University of California, Los Angeles">University of California, Los Angeles</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCLAHonorDinner2006_11_06_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-UCLAHonorDinner2006_11_06-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Morhaime" title="Michael Morhaime">Michael Morhaime</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frank_Pearce_(businessman)" title="Frank Pearce (businessman)">Frank Pearce</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allen_Adham" title="Allen Adham">Allen Adham</a>. The company originally concentrated primarily on the creation of game <a href="/wiki/Porting" title="Porting">ports</a> for other studios before beginning development of their own software in 1993 with the development of games like <i><a href="/wiki/Rock_n%27_Roll_Racing" title="Rock n' Roll Racing">Rock n' Roll Racing</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_Vikings" title="The Lost Vikings">The Lost Vikings</a></i>. In 1994 the company became <b>Chaos Studios</b>, then <b>Blizzard Entertainment, Inc.</b> after being acquired by distributor <a href="/wiki/Davidson_%26_Associates" title="Davidson &amp; Associates">Davidson &amp; Associates</a>.</p>
<p>Shortly thereafter, Blizzard shipped out what would become their first breakthrough smash hit <i><a href="/wiki/Warcraft:_Orcs_%26_Humans" title="Warcraft: Orcs &amp; Humans">Warcraft: Orcs &amp; Humans</a></i>. Blizzard then went on to create several other successful video games, including other <i><a href="/wiki/Warcraft" title="Warcraft">Warcraft</a></i> sequels, the <i><a href="/wiki/Diablo_(series)" title="Diablo (series)">Diablo</a></i> series, the <i><a href="/wiki/StarCraft" title="StarCraft">StarCraft</a></i> series, and also in 2004 the popular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MMORPG" title="MMORPG">MMORPG</a> <i><a href="/wiki/World_of_Warcraft" title="World of Warcraft">World of Warcraft</a></i>. Their most recent projects include: the first expansion for <i><a href="/wiki/Diablo_III" title="Diablo III">Diablo III</a>—<a href="/wiki/Diablo_III:_Reaper_of_Souls" title="Diablo III: Reaper of Souls">Reaper of Souls</a>;</i> the online <a href="/wiki/Collectible_card_game" title="Collectible card game">collectible card game</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearthstone:_Heroes_of_Warcraft" title="Hearthstone: Heroes of Warcraft">Hearthstone</a></i>; the sixth expansion for <i><a href="/wiki/World_of_Warcraft" title="World of Warcraft">World of Warcraft</a>—</i><a href="/wiki/World_of_Warcraft:_Legion" title="World of Warcraft: Legion"><i>Legion</i></a>; the "Hero Brawler" <i><a href="/wiki/Heroes_of_the_Storm" title="Heroes of the Storm">Heroes of the Storm</a></i>; the third and final expansion for <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/StarCraft_II" title="StarCraft II">StarCraft II</a>—<a href="/wiki/StarCraft_II:_Legacy_of_the_Void" title="StarCraft II: Legacy of the Void">Legacy of the Void</a></i>; and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multiplayer" title="Multiplayer">multiplayer</a> <a href="/wiki/First-person_shooter" title="First-person shooter">first-person shooter</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Overwatch_(video_game)" title="Overwatch (video game)">Overwatch</a></i>.</p>
<p>On July 9, 2008, <a href="/wiki/Activision" title="Activision">Activision</a> officially merged with <a href="/wiki/Vivendi_Games" title="Vivendi Games">Vivendi Games</a>, culminating in the inclusion of the Blizzard brand name in the title of the resulting holding company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Activision_Blizzard_Close_English_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Activision_Blizzard_Close_English-5">[5]</a></sup> On July 25, 2013, Activision Blizzard announced the purchase of 429 million shares from majority owner Vivendi. As a result, Activision Blizzard became a completely independent company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Blizzard Entertainment hosts conventions for fans to meet and to promote their games: the <a href="/wiki/BlizzCon" title="BlizzCon">BlizzCon</a> in California, United States, and the <a href="/wiki/Blizzard_Worldwide_Invitational" title="Blizzard Worldwide Invitational">Blizzard Worldwide Invitational</a> in other countries, including France and South Korea.</p>

<p>Blizzard Entertainment was founded by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Morhaime" title="Michael Morhaime">Michael Morhaime</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allen_Adham" title="Allen Adham">Allen Adham</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frank_Pearce_(businessman)" title="Frank Pearce (businessman)">Frank Pearce</a> as <b>Silicon &amp; Synapse</b> on February 8, 1991, a year after<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCLAHonorDinner2006_11_06_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-UCLAHonorDinner2006_11_06-4">[4]</a></sup> all three had received their <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">bachelor's degrees</a> from UCLA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blizz-10th-anniversary_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-blizz-10th-anniversary-7">[7]</a></sup> In the early days the company focused on creating game <a href="/wiki/Porting" title="Porting">ports</a> for other studios. Ports include titles such as <i><a href="/wiki/J.R.R._Tolkien%27s_The_Lord_of_the_Rings,_Vol._I_(1990_video_game)" title="J.R.R. Tolkien's The Lord of the Rings, Vol. I (1990 video game)">J.R.R. Tolkien's The Lord of the Rings, Vol. I</a></i> and <i>Battle Chess II: Chinese Chess</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blizz_tline_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-blizz_tline-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1993, the company developed games such as <i><a href="/wiki/Rock_n%27_Roll_Racing" title="Rock n' Roll Racing">Rock n' Roll Racing</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_Vikings" title="The Lost Vikings">The Lost Vikings</a></i> (published by <a href="/wiki/Interplay_Entertainment" title="Interplay Entertainment">Interplay Productions</a>).</p>
<p>In early 1994 they were acquired by distributor <a href="/wiki/Davidson_%26_Associates" title="Davidson &amp; Associates">Davidson &amp; Associates</a> for $6.75 million ($10.9 million today).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> That same year the company briefly changed its name to <b>Chaos Studios,</b> before finally settling on Blizzard Entertainment after it was discovered that another company with the Chaos name already existed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Shortly thereafter, Blizzard shipped their breakthrough hit <i><a href="/wiki/Warcraft:_Orcs_%26_Humans" title="Warcraft: Orcs &amp; Humans">Warcraft: Orcs &amp; Humans</a></i>.</p>
<p>Blizzard has changed hands several times since then: Davidson was acquired along with <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Entertainment" title="Sierra Entertainment">Sierra On-Line</a> by a company called <a href="/wiki/CUC_International" title="CUC International">CUC International</a> in 1996; CUC then merged with a hotel, real-estate, and car-rental franchiser called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HFS_Corporation" title="HFS Corporation">HFS Corporation</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Cendant" title="Cendant">Cendant</a> in 1997. In 1998 it became apparent that CUC had engaged in accounting fraud for years before the merger; Cendant's stock lost 80% of its value over the next six months in the ensuing widely discussed accounting scandal. The company sold its consumer software operations, Sierra On-line which included Blizzard, to French publisher <a href="/wiki/Havas" title="Havas">Havas</a> in 1998, the same year Havas was purchased by <a href="/wiki/Vivendi" title="Vivendi">Vivendi</a>. Blizzard was part of the <a href="/wiki/Vivendi_Games" title="Vivendi Games">Vivendi Games</a> group of Vivendi. In July 2008 Vivendi Games merged with <a href="/wiki/Activision" title="Activision">Activision</a>, using Blizzard's name in the resulting company, <a href="/wiki/Activision_Blizzard" title="Activision Blizzard">Activision Blizzard</a>.</p>
<p>In 1996, Blizzard acquired Condor Games, which had been working on the game <i><a href="/wiki/Diablo_(video_game)" title="Diablo (video game)">Diablo</a></i> for Blizzard at the time. Condor was renamed <a href="/wiki/Blizzard_North" title="Blizzard North">Blizzard North</a>, and has since developed hit games <i><a href="/wiki/Diablo_(video_game)" title="Diablo (video game)">Diablo</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Diablo_II" title="Diablo II">Diablo II</a></i>, and its expansion pack <i><a href="/wiki/Diablo_II:_Lord_of_Destruction" title="Diablo II: Lord of Destruction">Diablo II: Lord of Destruction</a></i>. Blizzard North was located in <a href="/wiki/San_Mateo,_California" title="San Mateo, California">San Mateo, California</a>; the company originated in <a href="/wiki/Redwood_City,_California" title="Redwood City, California">Redwood City, California</a>.</p>
<p>Blizzard launched their online gaming service <i><a href="/wiki/Battle.net" title="Battle.net">Battle.net</a></i> in January 1997 with the release of their action-<a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">RPG</a> <i>Diablo</i>. In 2002, Blizzard was able to reacquire rights for three of its earlier Silicon &amp; Synapse titles (<a href="/wiki/The_Lost_Vikings" title="The Lost Vikings">The Lost Vikings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_n%27_Roll_Racing" title="Rock n' Roll Racing">Rock n' Roll Racing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blackthorne" title="Blackthorne">Blackthorne</a>) from <a href="/wiki/Interplay_Entertainment" title="Interplay Entertainment">Interplay Entertainment</a> and re-release them under <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Advance" title="Game Boy Advance">Game Boy Advance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 2004, Blizzard opened European offices in the Paris suburb of <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A9lizy-Villacoublay" title="Vélizy-Villacoublay">Vélizy</a>, Yvelines, France, responsible for the European in-game support of <i><a href="/wiki/World_of_Warcraft" title="World of Warcraft">World of Warcraft</a></i>. On November 23, 2004, Blizzard released World of Warcraft, its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MMORPG" title="MMORPG">MMORPG</a> offering. On May 16, 2005, Blizzard announced the <a href="/wiki/Takeover" title="Takeover">acquisition</a> of <a href="/wiki/Swingin%27_Ape_Studios" title="Swingin' Ape Studios">Swingin' Ape Studios</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Video_game_developer" title="Video game developer">video game developer</a> which had been developing <i><a href="/wiki/StarCraft:_Ghost" title="StarCraft: Ghost">StarCraft: Ghost</a></i>. The company was then merged into Blizzard's other teams after <i>StarCraft: Ghost</i> was "postponed indefinitely". On August 1, 2005, Blizzard announced the <a href="/wiki/Consolidation_(business)" title="Consolidation (business)">consolidation</a> of Blizzard North into the headquarters at 131 Theory in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UC_Irvine" title="UC Irvine">UC Irvine</a>'s University Research Park in <a href="/wiki/Irvine,_California" title="Irvine, California">Irvine, California</a>. In 2007, Blizzard moved their headquarters to 16215 Alton Parkway in <a href="/wiki/Irvine,_California" title="Irvine, California">Irvine, California</a>.</p>
<p><i>World of Warcraft</i> was the fourth released game set in the fantasy <a href="/wiki/Warcraft" title="Warcraft"><i>Warcraft</i> universe</a>, which was first introduced by <i><a href="/wiki/Warcraft:_Orcs_%26_Humans" title="Warcraft: Orcs &amp; Humans">Warcraft: Orcs &amp; Humans</a></i> in 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Blizzard announced <i>World of Warcraft</i> on September 2, 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The game was released on November 23, 2004, on the 10th anniversary of the <i>Warcraft</i> <a href="/wiki/Media_franchise" title="Media franchise">franchise</a>.</p>
<p>The first expansion set of the game, <i><a href="/wiki/World_of_Warcraft:_The_Burning_Crusade" title="World of Warcraft: The Burning Crusade">The Burning Crusade</a></i>, was released on January 16, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crusade_Release_Date_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crusade_Release_Date-15">[15]</a></sup> The second expansion set, <i><a href="/wiki/World_of_Warcraft:_Wrath_of_the_Lich_King" title="World of Warcraft: Wrath of the Lich King">Wrath of the Lich King</a></i>, was released on November 13, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wrath_Release_Date_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wrath_Release_Date-16">[16]</a></sup> The third expansion set, <i><a href="/wiki/World_of_Warcraft:_Cataclysm" title="World of Warcraft: Cataclysm">Cataclysm</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> was released on December 7, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/World_of_Warcraft:_Mists_of_Pandaria" title="World of Warcraft: Mists of Pandaria">Mists of Pandaria</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/World_of_Warcraft:_Warlords_of_Draenor" title="World of Warcraft: Warlords of Draenor">Warlords of Draenor</a></i>, were released respectively on September 25, 2012 and November 13, 2014. The most recent expansion, <a href="/wiki/World_of_Warcraft:_Legion" title="World of Warcraft: Legion">Legion</a>, was released on August 30, 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Having peaked at 12 million monthly subscriptions in 2010, <i>World of Warcraft</i> subscriptions sunk to 6.8 million in 2014, the lowest number since the end of 2006, prior to <i><a href="/wiki/World_of_Warcraft:_The_Burning_Crusade" title="World of Warcraft: The Burning Crusade">The Burning Crusade</a></i> expansion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> However, <i>World of Warcraft</i> is still the world's most-subscribed MMORPG,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wrath_Release_Date_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wrath_Release_Date-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mmogchart_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-mmogchart-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and holds the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Record" title="Guinness World Record">Guinness World Record</a> for the most popular MMORPG by subscribers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In April 2008, <i>World of Warcraft</i> was estimated to hold 62 percent of the MMORPG subscription market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In 2008, Blizzard was honored at the 59th Annual <a href="/wiki/Technology_%26_Engineering_Emmy_Award" title="Technology &amp; Engineering Emmy Award">Technology &amp; Engineering Emmy Awards</a> for the creation of <i>World of Warcraft</i>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mike_Morhaime" title="Mike Morhaime">Mike Morhaime</a> accepted the award.</p>
<p>In 2012 Blizzard had 4,700 employees,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra-dice-2012_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra-dice-2012-3">[3]</a></sup> with offices across 11 cities including <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Texas" title="Austin, Texas">Austin, Texas</a>, and countries around the globe. As of June 2015, the company's headquarters in Irvine, California had 2,622 employees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_franchises">Main franchises</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard_Entertainment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Main franchises">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Currently, Blizzard has four main franchises in the gaming industry: <i><a href="/wiki/Warcraft" title="Warcraft">Warcraft</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Diablo_(series)" title="Diablo (series)">Diablo</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/StarCraft" title="StarCraft">StarCraft</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Overwatch_(video_game)" title="Overwatch (video game)">Overwatch</a></i>. Multiple games have been released for each game series except for <i>Overwatch</i>. Each franchise is supported by other media based around the intellectual property for each franchise such as novels, collectible card games, comics and video shorts. Blizzard Entertainment announced in 2006 that they will be producing a <a href="/wiki/Warcraft_(film)" title="Warcraft (film)"><i>Warcraft</i> live-action movie</a>. The movie is directed by <a href="/wiki/Duncan_Jones" title="Duncan Jones">Duncan Jones</a>, financed and produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legendary_Pictures" title="Legendary Pictures">Legendary Pictures</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Entertainment" title="Atlas Entertainment">Atlas Entertainment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Film4_Productions" title="Film4 Productions">Film4</a>, and distributed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Pictures" title="Universal Pictures">Universal Pictures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> It was released in June 2016.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unreleased_games">Unreleased games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard_Entertainment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Unreleased games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Notable unreleased titles include <i><a href="/wiki/Warcraft_Adventures:_Lord_of_the_Clans" title="Warcraft Adventures: Lord of the Clans">Warcraft Adventures: Lord of the Clans</a></i>, which was cancelled on May 22, 1998, <i>Shattered Nations</i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/StarCraft:_Ghost" title="StarCraft: Ghost">StarCraft: Ghost</a></i>, which was "Postponed indefinitely" on March 24, 2006 after being in <a href="/wiki/Development_hell" title="Development hell">development hell</a> for much of its lifespan. After seven years of development, Blizzard revealed the cancellation of an unannounced MMO codenamed <i><a href="/wiki/Titan_(Blizzard_Entertainment_project)" title="Titan (Blizzard Entertainment project)">"Titan"</a></i> on September 23, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The company also has a history of declining to set release dates, choosing to instead take as much time as needed, generally saying a given product is "done when it's done."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Pax Imperia II</i> was originally announced as a title to be published by Blizzard. Blizzard eventually dropped <i>Pax Imperia II</i>, though, when it decided it might be in conflict with their other space strategy project, which became known as <i><a href="/wiki/StarCraft" title="StarCraft">StarCraft</a></i>. THQ eventually contracted with Heliotrope and released the game in 1997 as <i><a href="/wiki/Pax_Imperia:_Eminent_Domain" title="Pax Imperia: Eminent Domain">Pax Imperia: Eminent Domain</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard_Entertainment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Warden_client">Warden client</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard_Entertainment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Warden client">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Blizzard has made use of a special form of software known as the 'Warden Client'. The Warden client is known to be used with Blizzard's Online Games such as <i>Diablo</i> and <i>World of Warcraft</i>, and the Terms of Service contain a clause consenting to the Warden software's RAM scans while a Blizzard game is running.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Warden client scans a small portion of the <a href="/wiki/Code_segment" title="Code segment">code segment</a> of running processes in order to determine whether any third-party programs are running. The goal of this is to detect and address players who may be attempting to run unsigned code or third party programs in the game. This determination of third party programs is made by <a href="/wiki/Hash_function" title="Hash function">hashing</a> the scanned <a href="/wiki/String_(computer_science)" title="String (computer science)">strings</a> and comparing the hashed value to a list of hashes assumed to correspond to banned third party programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The Warden's reliability in correctly discerning legitimate versus illegitimate actions was called into question when a large scale incident happened when many Linux users were banned after an update to Warden caused it to incorrectly detect <a href="/wiki/Cedega_(software)" title="Cedega (software)">Cedega</a> as a cheat program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Blizzard issued a statement claiming they had correctly identified and restored all accounts and credited them with 20 days play.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Warden scans all processes running on a computer, not just the game, and could possibly run across what would be considered private information and other <a href="/wiki/Personally_identifiable_information" title="Personally identifiable information">personally identifiable information</a>. It is because of these peripheral scans that Warden has been accused of being <a href="/wiki/Spyware" title="Spyware">spyware</a> and has run afoul of controversy among <a href="/wiki/Privacy" title="Privacy">privacy</a> advocates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Battle.net_2.0">Battle.net 2.0</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard_Entertainment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Battle.net 2.0">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battle.net" title="Battle.net">Battle.net</a></div>
<p>Blizzard released its revamped Battle.net service in 2009. This service allows people who have purchased Blizzard products (<i><a href="/wiki/StarCraft_(video_game)" title="StarCraft (video game)">StarCraft</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/StarCraft_II:_Wings_of_Liberty" title="StarCraft II: Wings of Liberty">StarCraft II</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Diablo_II" title="Diablo II">Diablo II</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Warcraft_III:_Reign_of_Chaos" title="Warcraft III: Reign of Chaos">Warcraft III</a></i>, as well as their expansions) to download digital copies of games they have purchased, without needing any physical media.</p>
<p>On November 11, 2009, Blizzard required all <i>World of Warcraft</i> accounts to switch over to Battle.net Accounts. This transition now means that all current Blizzard titles can be accessed, downloaded, and played with a singular Battle.net login.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Battle.net 2.0 is the new platform for matchmaking service for Blizzard games which offers players a host of additional features. Players will now be able to track their friend's achievements, view match history, avatars, etc. Players will also be able to unlock a wide range of achievements (rewards for completing game content) for Blizzard games. This means players can enjoy new avatars, decals, badges etc, for their accounts.</p>
<p>The service also allows players to chat simultaneously with players from other Blizzard games. For example, players no longer need to create multiple user names or accounts for most Blizzard products. StarCraft II, Diablo III, Hearthstone, Overwatch, World of Warcraft and the Battle.net Launcher support the ability for users to cross communicate. This means that a player could be in a game of StarCraft II, and he/she may send or receive messages from friends playing World of Warcraft or Diablo III. To enable cross game communication players need to become either Battletag or Real ID friends.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Privacy_controversy_and_Real_ID">Privacy controversy and Real ID</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard_Entertainment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Privacy controversy and Real ID">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On July 6, 2010, Blizzard announced that they were changing the way their forums worked to require that users identify themselves with their real name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The reaction from the community was overwhelmingly negative with multiple game magazines calling the change "foolhardy"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> and an "Epic Fail".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> It resulted in a significant user response on the Blizzard forums, including one thread on the issue reaching over 11,000 replies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news.bbc.co.uk_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-news.bbc.co.uk-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> This included personal details of a Blizzard employee who gave his real name "to show it wasn't a big deal".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Shortly after revealing his real name, forum users posted personal information including his phone number, picture, age, and home address.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news.bbc.co.uk_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-news.bbc.co.uk-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some technology media outlets suggested that displaying real names through Real ID is a good idea and would benefit both Battle.net and the Blizzard community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> But others were worried that Blizzard were opening their fans up to real-life dangers such as stalking, harassment, and employment issues, since a simple Internet search by someone's employer can reveal their online activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news.bbc.co.uk_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-news.bbc.co.uk-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>Blizzard initially responded to some of the concerns by saying that the changes would not be retroactive to previous posts, that parents could set up the system so that minors cannot post, and that posting to the forums is optional.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-holisky1_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-holisky1-61">[61]</a></sup> However, due to the huge negative response, Blizzard President <a href="/wiki/Michael_Morhaime" title="Michael Morhaime">Michael Morhaime</a> issued a statement rescinding the plan to use real names on Blizzard's forums for the time being.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Apart from the negative side effects of Real ID relating to privacy, the new addition boasts new features for current Blizzard titles. For instance, real names for friends, cross-realm and cross-game chat, rich presence and broadcasts are included with the Real ID system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_disputes">Legal disputes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard_Entertainment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Legal disputes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="StarCraft_privacy_lawsuit"><i>StarCraft</i> privacy lawsuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard_Entertainment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: StarCraft privacy lawsuit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1998, Donald P. Driscoll, an <a href="/wiki/Albany,_California" title="Albany, California">Albany</a>, California attorney filed a suit on behalf of Intervention, Inc., a California consumer group against Blizzard Entertainment for "unlawful business practices" for the action of collecting data from a user's computer without their permission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="FreeCraft"><i>FreeCraft</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard_Entertainment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: FreeCraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Stratagus" title="Stratagus">Stratagus</a></div>
<p>On June 20, 2003, Blizzard issued a <a href="/wiki/Cease_and_desist" title="Cease and desist">cease and desist</a> letter to the developers of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_source" title="Open source">open source</a> clone of the Warcraft engine called <i>FreeCraft</i>, claiming <a href="/wiki/Trademark_infringement" title="Trademark infringement">trademark infringement</a>. This hobby project had the same gameplay and characters as <i>Warcraft II</i>, but came with different graphics and music.</p>
<p>As well as a similar name, <i>FreeCraft</i> enabled gamers to use <i>Warcraft II</i> graphics, provided they had the <i>Warcraft II</i> CD. The programmers of the clone shut down their site without challenge. Soon after that the developers regrouped to continue the work by the name of <i><a href="/wiki/Stratagus" title="Stratagus">Stratagus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_of_Warcraft_Private_Server_Complications">World of Warcraft Private Server Complications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard_Entertainment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: World of Warcraft Private Server Complications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On December 5, 2008, Blizzard issued a <a href="/wiki/Cease_and_desist" title="Cease and desist">cease and desist</a> letter to many administrators of high population <i><a href="/wiki/World_of_Warcraft" title="World of Warcraft">World of Warcraft</a></i> private servers (essentially slightly altered hosting servers of the actual <a href="/wiki/World_of_Warcraft" title="World of Warcraft">World of Warcraft</a> game, that players do not have to pay for). Blizzard used the <a href="/wiki/Digital_Millennium_Copyright_Act" title="Digital Millennium Copyright Act">Digital Millennium Copyright Act</a> to influence many private servers to fully shut down and cease to exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> In 2008, a private server by the name of ChaosCrusade was served with a DMCA notification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founder_Electronics_infringement_lawsuit">Founder Electronics infringement lawsuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard_Entertainment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Founder Electronics infringement lawsuit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On August 14, 2007, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beijing_University" title="Beijing University">Beijing University</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Founder_Electronics" title="Founder Electronics">Founder Electronics</a> Co., Ltd. sued Blizzard Entertainment Limited for copyright infringement claiming 100 million <a href="/wiki/Chinese_yuan" title="Chinese yuan">yuan</a> in damages. The lawsuit alleged the Chinese edition of World of Warcraft reproduced a number of Chinese typefaces made by Founder Electronics without permission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="MDY_Industries.2C_LLC_v._Blizzard_Entertainment.2C_Inc.">MDY Industries, LLC v. Blizzard Entertainment, Inc.</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard_Entertainment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: MDY Industries, LLC v. Blizzard Entertainment, Inc.">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Glider_(bot)" title="Glider (bot)">Glider (bot)</a></div>
<p>On July 14, 2008, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_District_of_Arizona" title="United States District Court for the District of Arizona">United States District Court for the District of Arizona</a> ruled on the case <i><a href="/wiki/MDY_Industries,_LLC_v._Blizzard_Entertainment,_Inc." title="MDY Industries, LLC v. Blizzard Entertainment, Inc.">MDY Industries, LLC v. Blizzard Entertainment, Inc.</a></i>. The Court found that MDY was liable for <a href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement" title="Copyright infringement">copyright infringement</a> since users of its <a href="/wiki/Glider_(bot)" title="Glider (bot)">Glider</a> bot program were breaking the End User License Agreement and Terms of Use for <i><a href="/wiki/World_of_Warcraft" title="World of Warcraft">World of Warcraft</a></i>. MDY Industries appealed the judgment of the district court, and a judgment was delivered by the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals on December 14, 2010, in which the summary judgment against MDY for contributory copyright infringement was reversed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Nevertheless, they ruled that the bot violated the <a href="/wiki/Digital_Millennium_Copyright_Act" title="Digital Millennium Copyright Act">DMCA</a> and the case was sent back to the district court for review in light of this decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blizzard_Entertainment.2C_Inc._v._Valve_Corporation">Blizzard Entertainment, Inc. v. Valve Corporation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard_Entertainment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Blizzard Entertainment, Inc. v. Valve Corporation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shortly after <a href="/wiki/Valve_Corporation" title="Valve Corporation">Valve Corporation</a> filed its trademark for "Dota" to secure the franchising rights for <i><a href="/wiki/Dota_2" title="Dota 2">Dota 2</a></i>, DotA-Allstars, LLC, run by former contributors to the games's predecessor, <i><a href="/wiki/Defense_of_the_Ancients" title="Defense of the Ancients">Defense of the Ancients</a></i>, filed an opposing trademark in August 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> DotA All-Stars, LLC was sold to Blizzard Entertainment in 2011. After the opposition was overruled in Valve's favor, Blizzard filed an opposition against Valve in November 2011, citing their license agreement with developers, as well as their ownership of DotA-Allstars, LLC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Blizzard conceded their case in May 2012, however, giving Valve undisputed commercial rights to <i>Dota</i>, while Blizzard would rename their <i>StarCraft II: Heart of the Swarm</i> mod <i>"Blizzard All-Stars"</i>, which would become the stand-alone game, <i><a href="/wiki/Heroes_of_the_Storm" title="Heroes of the Storm">Heroes of the Storm</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_companies">Related companies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard_Entertainment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Related companies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Over the years, some former Blizzard employees have moved on and established gaming companies of their own:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Flagship_Studios" title="Flagship Studios">Flagship Studios</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> creators of <i><a href="/wiki/Hellgate:_London" title="Hellgate: London">Hellgate: London</a></i>, also worked on <i><a href="/wiki/Mythos_(video_game)" title="Mythos (video game)">Mythos</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/ArenaNet" title="ArenaNet">ArenaNet</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> creators of the <i><a href="/wiki/Guild_Wars" title="Guild Wars">Guild Wars</a></i> franchise.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ready_at_Dawn" title="Ready at Dawn">Ready at Dawn Studios</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> creators of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Order:_1886" title="The Order: 1886">The Order: 1886</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Daxter_(video_game)" title="Daxter (video game)">Daxter</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/God_of_War:_Chains_of_Olympus" title="God of War: Chains of Olympus">God of War: Chains of Olympus</a></i> and an <i><a href="/wiki/%C5%8Ckami" title="Ōkami">Ōkami</a></i> port for the <a href="/wiki/Wii" title="Wii">Wii</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Red_5_Studios" title="Red 5 Studios">Red 5 Studios</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> currently working on <i><a href="/wiki/Firefall_(video_game)" title="Firefall (video game)">Firefall</a></i>, a free to play game <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MMOG" title="MMOG">MMOG</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Castaway_Entertainment" title="Castaway Entertainment">Castaway Entertainment</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> now defunct, after working on a game similar to the <i>Diablo</i> series, <i>Djinn</i>.</li>
<li>Click Entertainment,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> creators of <i><a href="/wiki/Throne_of_Darkness" title="Throne of Darkness">Throne of Darkness</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carbine_Studios" title="Carbine Studios">Carbine Studios</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> released a massively multiplayer title <i><a href="/wiki/WildStar_(video_game)" title="WildStar (video game)">WildStar</a></i>.</li>
<li>Turpitude Design,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> founded by <a href="/wiki/Stieg_Hedlund" title="Stieg Hedlund">Stieg Hedlund</a>.</li>
<li>Hyboreal Games,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> founded by <a href="/wiki/Michio_Okamura" title="Michio Okamura">Michio Okamura</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Runic_Games" title="Runic Games">Runic Games</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> founded by Travis Baldree, Erich Schaefer, and Max Schaefer; creators of <i><a href="/wiki/Torchlight" title="Torchlight">Torchlight</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Trion_Worlds" title="Trion Worlds">Trion Worlds</a>, released an MMORPG, <i><a href="/wiki/Rift_(video_game)" title="Rift (video game)">Rift</a></i> and an <a href="/wiki/Massively_multiplayer_online_game#First-person_shooter" title="Massively multiplayer online game">MMOFPS</a> entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Defiance_(video_game)" title="Defiance (video game)">Defiance</a></i> with a <a href="/wiki/Multiplayer_online_battle_arena" title="Multiplayer online battle arena">MOBA</a> game, <i><a href="/wiki/End_of_Nations" title="End of Nations">End of Nations</a></i>, placed on indefinite hiatus.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Undead_Labs" title="Undead Labs">Undead Labs</a>, founded by <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Strain" title="Jeff Strain">Jeff Strain</a> and worked on the video game <i><a href="/wiki/State_of_Decay_(video_game)" title="State of Decay (video game)">State of Decay</a></i>.</li>
</ul>
