<p><b>Blixa Bargeld</b> (born <b>Christian Emmerich</b> on 12 January 1959) is a German-born musician active in a wide range of artistic fields. He is best known for his studio work and live performances with the groups <a href="/wiki/Einst%C3%BCrzende_Neubauten" title="Einst√ºrzende Neubauten">Einst√ºrzende Neubauten</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nick_Cave_and_the_Bad_Seeds" title="Nick Cave and the Bad Seeds">Nick Cave and the Bad Seeds</a>. His stage name comes from "Blixa", a German brand of blue felt pen, and <i>Bargeld</i>, which is German for "cash". Bargeld also refers to German <a href="/wiki/Dada" title="Dada">Dada</a> artist <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Theodor_Baargeld" title="Johannes Theodor Baargeld">Johannes Theodor Baargeld</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blixa_Bargeld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bargeld left school prior to completion and is <a href="/wiki/Autodidacticism" title="Autodidacticism">self-taught</a>. He revealed in 2010: "[I] would never have guessed when I was 13 that I would have become a professional musician. It was so far away as to become a reality in my personal life." Bargeld experimented with audio equipment as a teenager, including the disassembling of tape recorders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luke_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Luke-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bargeld is from the <a href="/wiki/Tempelhof" title="Tempelhof">Tempelhof</a> area of West Berlin<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gross_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gross-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-War_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-War-3">[3]</a></sup> and he moved out of his parents' home in the late 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gross_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gross-2">[2]</a></sup> A 2008 documentary featured him visiting his mother and talking to her about his childhood and the relationship he had with his parents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2008docu_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-2008docu-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blixa_Bargeld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1980, he founded the music group <a href="/wiki/Einst%C3%BCrzende_Neubauten" title="Einst√ºrzende Neubauten">Einst√ºrzende Neubauten</a> ("Collapsing New Buildings");<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> the first album he owned was by <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a>. He quickly moved onto German rock <a href="/wiki/Krautrock" title="Krautrock">Krautrock</a> acts such as <a href="/wiki/Kraftwerk" title="Kraftwerk">Kraftwerk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neu!" title="Neu!">Neu!</a> and <a href="/wiki/Can_(band)" title="Can (band)">Can</a>, which he described as his biggest influences at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luke_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Luke-1">[1]</a></sup> Bargeld spoke of the early days of Neubauten in 2010:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The starting point for Neubauten was more that we didn't have anything, so I didn't really have the choice to say 'I am doing this, I am doing that, or maybe I should play organ'. I didn't have any of these things, and I could not afford any of these things, and neither could anybody else in the group. It was more of the logical consequence of what can we obtain, and that's how it turned out. It certainly didn't start out as an artistic concept to say "let's do something different", it started as an extension of the live situation as it already was.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luke_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Luke-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>From 1983 to 2003, Bargeld was a long-time guitarist and backing vocalist in <a href="/wiki/Nick_Cave_and_the_Bad_Seeds" title="Nick Cave and the Bad Seeds">Nick Cave and the Bad Seeds</a>. Bargeld also sang lead vocals alongside <a href="/wiki/Nick_Cave" title="Nick Cave">Cave</a> on several songs, such as on "The Carny" and "The Weeping Song". Cave first saw Bargeld performing with Einst√ºrzende Neubauten on TV while <a href="/wiki/The_Birthday_Party_(band)" title="The Birthday Party (band)">The Birthday Party</a>, Cave's band at the time, were touring in Amsterdam. He described the music as "mournful", Bargeld as looking "destroyed", and his screams as: "a sound you would expect to hear from strangled cats or dying children".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>He is credited with playing guitar on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gun_Club" title="Gun Club">Gun Club</a> song, "Yellow Eyes", on their 1987 album <i>Mother Juno</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He also played on the album <i><a href="/wiki/Novice_(album)" title="Novice (album)">Novice</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Alain_Bashung" title="Alain Bashung">Alain Bashung</a> in 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the mid-90s Bargeld has appeared live with his solo <i>Rede/Speech Performances</i>. During these performances, usually supported by Neubauten's sound engineer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Boris_Wilsdorf&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Boris Wilsdorf (page does not exist)">Boris Wilsdorf</a>, he works with microphones, sound effects, overdubbing with the help of sampler loops, and speaks English or German. The performed pieces include a vocal creation of the DNA of an angel and a parody of a techno song.</p>
<p>In 2007 he started a collaborative project with <a href="/wiki/Alva_Noto" title="Alva Noto">Alva Noto</a> called <a href="/wiki/ANBB" title="ANBB">ANBB</a>, an abbreviation of Noto's and Bargeld's initials. An <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">EP</a>, <i>Ret Marut Handshake</i>, was released on 26 June 2010, followed later that year by a full-length album, <i>Mimikry</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2013, a collaboration with Italian composer Teho Teardo <i>Still Smiling</i> was released on the Specula record label. A music video for the song "Mi Scusi" was published on the Teho Teardo <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> channel and a corresponding Italian tour is scheduled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skinny_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Skinny-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In early October, Neubauten announced 24 November 2014 as the release date for their next album, <i>Lament</i>. <i>Lament</i> is described as a: "concept album based on a live performance and installation commissioned by the Flemish city of Diksmuide, Belgium to mark the centenary of the start of the First World War in 1914".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lam-12">[12]</a></sup> Bargeld explained in the official press release: "The Second World War is nothing but the elongation of the first one ‚Ä¶ As a child of the post Second World War era, and the resulting division of Germany and Berlin, I‚Äôm of course hugely influenced in my upbringing about the results of that".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lam-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bargeld explained in October 2014 that Neubauten is essentially a <a href="/wiki/Materialism" title="Materialism">materialistic</a> band, leading them to employ two scientific researchers to seek out material to support the development of <i>Lament</i> after the album received financial backing in August 2013. The band opened their 2014 European tour, in support of <i>Lament</i>, with a performance in <a href="/wiki/Diksmuide" title="Diksmuide">Diksmuide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, to mark the 100th anniversary of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-War_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-War-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style_and_influences">Style and influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blixa_Bargeld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Style and influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jennifer Shryane, in her book <i>Blixa Bargeld and Einst√ºrzende Neubauten: German Experimental Music. Evading do-re-mi</i> (2011), explores how the themes and threads of Bargeld‚Äôs work with Neubauten show even greater variation and experimentation in his performance work outside of the band. For example, the range extends from his surreal, electronic Dadaist-cabaret Rede and collaborations with Alva Noto, to his expert direction of Coetzee‚Äôs <i>Warten auf die Barbaren</i> (<i>Waiting for the Barbarians</i>) for the <a href="/wiki/Salzburg_Festival" title="Salzburg Festival">Salzburg Festival</a> in 2005, where he employed multi-layered symbolism through an ice-white setting and an interplay of voices, screams and noise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shryane‚Äôs book particularly examines his vocal strategies and his trademark scream (describing the scream in dance terms as the endless pirouette or unanticipated giant leap) and the labyrinthine concerns of his texts ‚Äì all through Artaudian performance theory (The Theatre and its Double, 1938). She also stresses Bargeld‚Äôs passionate stance on the socializing aspects of music (a la John Cage) citing his comment on Grundst√ºck that: "it‚Äôs the social aspects which are important for me".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Instruments">Instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blixa_Bargeld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bargeld's guitars of choice are a <a href="/wiki/Fender_Jaguar" title="Fender Jaguar">Fender Jaguar</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Fender_Mustang" title="Fender Mustang">Fender Mustang</a>, as seen on the concert DVD <i>God Is In The House</i> and at various media appearances. Initially he used a battered <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B6fner" title="H√∂fner">H√∂fner</a> Model 173 and a red H√∂fner Colorama II until they "broke down". After his effect pedals were stolen in the early 1980s, he relied exclusively on the Fender floating/dynamic tremolo (like the H√∂fner units)‚Äî which both raise and lower pitch ‚Äî Fender Twin amplifiers, metal slides, and changing his amp settings for each individual song, to create a unique guitar sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blixa_Bargeld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bargeld is married to American Erin Zhu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-War_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-War-3">[3]</a></sup> Together they developed the concept of web-based fan subscriptions as a new business model for musicians. The couple is featured among 37 other design and media figures in the 2010 book <i>Designing Media</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> by designer and IDEO co-founder <a href="/wiki/Bill_Moggridge" title="Bill Moggridge">Bill Moggridge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The couple resides with their daughter in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2008docu_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-2008docu-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spiegel2009_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-spiegel2009-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bargeld was a vegetarian for 30 years, but ceased the lifestyle option due to the difficulties he had practicing vegetarianism in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spiegel2009_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-spiegel2009-17">[17]</a></sup> He was also a smoker for a long period, but quit smoking in the early 2000s. The sound of Bargeld smoking a cigarette is a part of Einst√ºrzende Neubauten's song "Silence Is Sexy" (2000), which is featured on the <i><a href="/wiki/Silence_Is_Sexy" title="Silence Is Sexy">Silence Is Sexy</a></i> studio album.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
