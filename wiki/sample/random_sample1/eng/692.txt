<p>The <b>Rugby World Cup Sevens</b> is the premier stand-alone international <a href="/wiki/Rugby_sevens" title="Rugby sevens">rugby sevens</a> competition. The event is organised by the sport's governing body, <a href="/wiki/World_Rugby" title="World Rugby">World Rugby</a> (WR), and is contested every four years with tournaments for men's and women's national teams co-hosted at the same venues. The inaugural tournament was held in 1993 in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, the birthplace of rugby sevens. The winners of the men's tournament are awarded the <a href="/wiki/Melrose_Cup" title="Melrose Cup">Melrose Cup</a>, named after the Scottish town of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melrose,_Scotland" title="Melrose, Scotland">Melrose</a> where the first rugby sevens game was played.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The women's tournament was inaugurated at the 2009 Rugby World Cup Sevens held in <a href="/wiki/Dubai" title="Dubai">Dubai</a>.</p>
<p>New Zealand are the current men's and women's World Champions having won both tournaments in 2013. <a href="/wiki/Fiji_national_rugby_sevens_team" title="Fiji national rugby sevens team">Fiji</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_national_rugby_sevens_team" title="New Zealand national rugby sevens team">New Zealand</a> have won the men's tournament twice. <a href="/wiki/England_national_rugby_sevens_team" title="England national rugby sevens team">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales_national_rugby_sevens_team" title="Wales national rugby sevens team">Wales</a> have won a single tournament each, while both <a href="/wiki/Australia_national_rugby_sevens_team" title="Australia national rugby sevens team">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Africa_national_rugby_sevens_team" title="South Africa national rugby sevens team">South Africa</a> have reached tournament finals but not secured a title.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australia_women%27s_national_rugby_union_team_(sevens)" title="Australia women's national rugby union team (sevens)">Australia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Zealand_women%27s_national_rugby_union_team_(sevens)" title="New Zealand women's national rugby union team (sevens)">New Zealand</a> are the only nations to have been women's World Champions, having won the first and second Rugby World Cup Sevens tournaments respectively in 2009 and 2013.</p>

<p>In 2009, after the inclusion of <a href="/wiki/Rugby_sevens_at_the_Summer_Olympics" title="Rugby sevens at the Summer Olympics">rugby sevens at the Olympic Games</a> was announced, the International Rugby Board (IRB, now World Rugby) stated that their intention was to end the World Cup Sevens so that the Olympic Games would be the one pinnacle in a four-year cycle for rugby sevens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-espn20090527_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-espn20090527-2">[2]</a></sup> However, following consultation, the IRB announced that the competition would be retained, and integrated into the Olympic calendar, meaning that a meaningful elite level competition would take place every two years from 2016. The World Cup will be also markedly larger in terms of team numbers than the Olympic tournament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The first competition after Olympic integration is set to take place in <a href="/wiki/2018_Rugby_World_Cup_Sevens" title="2018 Rugby World Cup Sevens">2018</a>, which will entail a one-off five-year gap from the 2013 competition.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rugby_World_Cup_Sevens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Rugby World Cup Sevens originated with a proposal by the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Rugby_Union" title="Scottish Rugby Union">Scottish Rugby Union</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Rugby_Board" title="International Rugby Board">International Rugby Board</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The inaugural tournament was held at <a href="/wiki/Murrayfield_Stadium" title="Murrayfield Stadium">Murrayfield</a> in Edinburgh in 1993, and has been held every four years since. Hong Kong, which had played a major role in the international development of the Sevens game, hosted the <a href="/wiki/1997_Rugby_World_Cup_Sevens" title="1997 Rugby World Cup Sevens">1997 event</a>. The final was won by Fiji over South Africa. The <a href="/wiki/2001_Rugby_World_Cup_Sevens" title="2001 Rugby World Cup Sevens">2001 tournament</a> was held in <a href="/wiki/Mar_del_Plata" title="Mar del Plata">Mar del Plata</a>, Argentina. The <a href="/wiki/2005_Rugby_World_Cup_Sevens" title="2005 Rugby World Cup Sevens">2005 event</a> returned to Hong Kong.</p>
<p>The IRB made a submission to the <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> in 2005 for rugby sevens to become an Olympic sport. However, the submission failed because committee members felt IRB needed to improve promotion of the women's game.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> To that end, the IRB implemented the first women's Rugby World Cup Sevens tournament in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-espn20090813_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-espn20090813-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/2009_Rugby_World_Cup_Sevens" title="2009 Rugby World Cup Sevens">2009 Rugby World Cup Sevens</a> was held in <a href="/wiki/Dubai" title="Dubai">Dubai</a> during the first weekend of March 2009 and included a separate women's tournament. Cumulative attendance was 78,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-espn20090813_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-espn20090813-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prior to the inclusion of rugby sevens into the Olympic Games, the IRB stated that their intention would be to end the World Cup Sevens so that the Olympic Games would be the one pinnacle in a four-year cycle for Rugby Sevens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-espn20090527_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-espn20090527-2">[2]</a></sup> The adoption of rugby sevens and golf was recommended to the full International Olympic Committee council by its executive board in August 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The International Olympic Committee voted in 2009 for rugby sevens to become a medal sport at the 2016 Games in <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The IRB Council in 2010 awarded the hosting of the 2013 tournament to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moscow,_Russia" title="Moscow, Russia">Moscow, Russia</a> from a field of eight nations that had expressed formal interest in hosting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rwcsevens.com_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-rwcsevens.com-7">[7]</a></sup> The IRB intended that the exposure to rugby from hosting the World Cup Sevens would accelerate the growth of <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union_in_Russia" title="Rugby union in Russia">rugby in Russia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rwcsevens.com_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-rwcsevens.com-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The IRB had said the 2013 Rugby World Cup Sevens – featuring 24 men's teams and 16 women's teams – would be the last one. However, following feedback from its member unions, the IRB's general assembly voted for the tournament to continue. The principal concern is that Sevens at the Olympics would accommodate only 12 teams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The IRB announced on June 12, 2013 that the Rugby World Cup Sevens would continue after 2013, with the next tournament set for 2018, and for every four years after that.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Following the IRB's announcement, several nations officially announced their intention to bid to host the <a href="/wiki/2018_Rugby_World_Cup_Sevens" title="2018 Rugby World Cup Sevens">2018 tournament</a> – including the <a href="/wiki/USA_Rugby" title="USA Rugby">United States</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and Wales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>The 2001 tournament, held in Argentina, added another chapter to the legend of New Zealand's <a href="/wiki/Jonah_Lomu" title="Jonah Lomu">Jonah Lomu</a>. Lomu, used sparingly in pool play, received his opportunity when New Zealand captain <a href="/wiki/Eric_Rush" title="Eric Rush">Eric Rush</a> broke his leg in the last pool match. Lomu went on to score three tries in the final.</p>
<p>In 2005, <a href="/wiki/Waisale_Serevi" title="Waisale Serevi">Waisale Serevi</a> came out of international retirement to captain and lead Fiji to their second Melrose Cup.</p>
<p>At the 2009 tournament in <a href="/wiki/Dubai" title="Dubai">Dubai</a>, Wales, Samoa, Argentina and Kenya combined to stun the rugby world by defeating the traditional powerhouses of New Zealand, England, South Africa and Fiji in the quarter-finals, guaranteeing a new Melrose Cup winner. Wales and Argentina met in the final, with Wales triumphing 19–12. Wales' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taliesin_Selley" title="Taliesin Selley">Taliesin Selley</a> was named player of the tournament.</p>
<p>The top all-time try-scorer for the Rugby World Cup Sevens has been <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fijian</a> winger <a href="/wiki/Marika_Vunibaka" title="Marika Vunibaka">Marika Vunibaka</a>, who scored 23 tries in 3 of the Sevens World Cups he played in since his debut in 1997.</p>
