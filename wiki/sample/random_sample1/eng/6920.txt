<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Comparative method (linguistics)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, the <b>comparative method</b> is a technique for studying the development of languages by performing a feature-by-feature comparison of two or more languages with common descent from a shared ancestor, in order to extrapolate back to infer the properties of that ancestor. The comparative method may be contrasted with the method of <a href="/wiki/Internal_reconstruction" title="Internal reconstruction">internal reconstruction</a>, in which the internal development of a single language is inferred by the analysis of features within that language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Ordinarily both methods are used together to reconstruct prehistoric phases of languages, to fill in gaps in the historical record of a language, to discover the development of phonological, morphological, and other linguistic systems, and to confirm or refute hypothesized relationships between languages.</p>
<p>The comparative method was developed over the 19th century. Key contributions were made by the Danish scholars <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rasmus_Christian_Rask" title="Rasmus Christian Rask">Rasmus Rask</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karl_Verner" title="Karl Verner">Karl Verner</a> and the German scholar <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Grimm" title="Jacob Grimm">Jacob Grimm</a>. The first linguist to offer reconstructed forms from a <a href="/wiki/Proto-language" title="Proto-language">proto-language</a> was <a href="/wiki/August_Schleicher" title="August Schleicher">August Schleicher</a>, in his <i>Compendium der vergleichenden Grammatik der indogermanischen Sprachen</i>, originally published in 1861.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Here is Schleicher’s explanation of why he offered reconstructed forms:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>In the present work an attempt is made to set forth the inferred Indo-European original language side by side with its really existent derived languages. Besides the advantages offered by such a plan, in setting immediately before the eyes of the student the final results of the investigation in a more concrete form, and thereby rendering easier his insight into the nature of particular Indo-European languages, there is, I think, another of no less importance gained by it, namely that it shows the baselessness of the assumption that the non-Indian Indo-European languages were derived from Old-Indian (Sanskrit).</p>
</blockquote>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demonstrating_genetic_relationship">Demonstrating genetic relationship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comparative_method_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Demonstrating genetic relationship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The comparative method aims to prove that two or more historically <a href="/wiki/Attested_language" title="Attested language">attested languages</a> are descended from a single <a href="/wiki/Proto-language" title="Proto-language">proto-language</a> by comparing lists of <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognate</a> terms. From them, regular sound correspondences between the languages are established, and a sequence of regular <a href="/wiki/Sound_change" title="Sound change">sound changes</a> can then be postulated, which allows the proto-language to be <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_reconstruction" title="Linguistic reconstruction">reconstructed</a>. Relation is deemed certain only if at least a partial reconstruction of the common ancestor is feasible, and if regular sound correspondences can be established with chance similarities ruled out.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comparative_method_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Descent</i> is defined as transmission across the generations: children learn a language from the parents' generation and after being influenced by their peers transmit it to the next generation, and so on. For example, a continuous chain of speakers across the centuries links <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">Vulgar Latin</a> to all of its modern descendants.</p>
<p>Two languages are <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genetic_(linguistics)" title="Genetic (linguistics)">genetically</a> related</i> if they descended from the same <a href="/wiki/Proto-language" title="Proto-language">ancestor language</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> both come from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and therefore belong to the same family, the <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance languages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Having a large component of vocabulary from a certain origin is not sufficient to establish relatedness: for example, as a result of heavy <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">borrowing</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> into <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>, Modern Persian in fact takes more of its <a href="/wiki/Vocabulary" title="Vocabulary">vocabulary</a> from Arabic than from its direct ancestor, <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-Iranian_language" title="Proto-Indo-Iranian language">Proto-Indo-Iranian</a>, but Persian is still a member of the Indo-Iranian family and not considered "related" to Arabic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, it is possible for languages to have different degrees of relatedness. <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>, for example, is related to both <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>, but is more closely related to the former than it is to the latter. Although all three languages share a common ancestor, <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a>, English and German also share a more recent common ancestor, <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic_language" title="Proto-Germanic language">Proto-Germanic</a>, while Russian does not. Therefore, English and German are considered to belong to a different subgroup, the <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic languages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Shared retentions</i> from the parent language are not sufficient evidence of a sub-group. For example, German and Russian both retain from Proto-Indo-European a contrast between the <a href="/wiki/Dative_case" title="Dative case">dative case</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">accusative case</a>, which English has lost. However, this similarity between German and Russian is not evidence that German is more closely related to Russian than to English; it only means that the <i>innovation</i> in question—the loss of the accusative/dative distinction—happened more recently in English than the divergence of English from German. The division of related languages into sub-groups is more certainly accomplished by finding <i>shared linguistic innovations</i> differentiating them the parent language, rather than shared featured retained from the parent language.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_development_of_the_method">Origin and development of the method</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comparative_method_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origin and development of the method">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sajnovics_-_Demonstratio.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="551" data-file-width="365" height="332" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Sajnovics_-_Demonstratio.jpg/220px-Sajnovics_-_Demonstratio.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Sajnovics_-_Demonstratio.jpg/330px-Sajnovics_-_Demonstratio.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ee/Sajnovics_-_Demonstratio.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sajnovics_-_Demonstratio.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Title page of Sajnovic's 1770 work.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Languages have been compared since antiquity. For example, in the 1st century BC the Romans were aware of the similarities between Greek and Latin, which they explained mythologically, as the result of Rome being a Greek colony speaking a debased dialect. In the 9th or 10th century, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yehuda_Ibn_Quraysh" title="Yehuda Ibn Quraysh">Yehuda Ibn Quraysh</a> compared the phonology and morphology of Hebrew, Aramaic, and Arabic, but attributed this resemblance to the Biblical story of Babel, with Abraham, Isaac and Joseph retaining Adam's language, with other languages at various removes becoming more altered from the original Hebrew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In publications of 1647 and 1654, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marcus_van_Boxhorn" title="Marcus van Boxhorn">Marcus van Boxhorn</a> first described a rigid methodology for historical linguistic comparisons<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Driem_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Driem-9">[9]</a></sup> and proposed the existence of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-European" title="Indo-European">Indo-European</a> proto-language (which he called "Scythian") unrelated to Hebrew, but ancestral to Germanic, Greek, Romance, Persian, Sanskrit, Slavic, Celtic and Baltic languages. The Scythian theory was further developed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Andreas_J%C3%A4ger&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Andreas Jäger (page does not exist)">Andreas Jäger</a> (1686) and <a href="/wiki/William_Wotton" title="William Wotton">William Wotton</a> (1713), who made first forays to reconstruct this primitive common language. In 1710 and 1723, <a href="/wiki/Lambert_ten_Kate" title="Lambert ten Kate">Lambert ten Kate</a> first formulated the regularity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_law" title="Sound law">sound laws</a>, introducing among others, the term <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Root_vowel" title="Root vowel">root vowel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Driem_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Driem-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another early systematic attempt to prove the relationship between two languages on the basis of similarity of <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">lexicon</a> was made by the Hungarian <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A1nos_Sajnovics" title="János Sajnovics">János Sajnovics</a> in 1770, when he attempted to demonstrate the relationship between <a href="/wiki/Sami_languages" title="Sami languages">Sami</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a> (work that was later extended to the whole <a href="/wiki/Finno-Ugric_languages" title="Finno-Ugric languages">Finno-Ugric language family</a> in 1799 by his countryman <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samuel_Gyarmathi" title="Samuel Gyarmathi">Samuel Gyarmathi</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ssix_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ssix-10">[10]</a></sup> But the origin of modern <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguistics" title="Historical linguistics">historical linguistics</a> is often traced back to <a href="/wiki/William_Jones_(philologist)" title="William Jones (philologist)">Sir William Jones</a>, an English <a href="/wiki/Philology" title="Philology">philologist</a> living in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, who in 1786 made his famous <span class="nowrap">observation:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></span></p>
<blockquote>
<p>“The <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanscrit language</a>, whatever be its antiquity, is of a wonderful structure; more perfect than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">Greek</a>, more copious than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>, and more exquisitely refined than either, yet bearing to both of them a stronger affinity, both in the roots of verbs and the forms of grammar, than could possibly have been produced by accident; so strong indeed, that no philologer could examine them all three, without believing them to have sprung from some common source, which, perhaps, no longer exists. There is a similar reason, though not quite so forcible, for supposing that both the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_language" title="Gothic language">Gothick</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtick</a>, though blended with a very different idiom, had the same origin with the Sanscrit; and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Persian_language" title="Old Persian language">old Persian</a> might be added to the same family.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The comparative method developed out of attempts to reconstruct the proto-language mentioned by Jones, which he did not name, but subsequent linguists named <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> (PIE). The first professional comparison between the <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European languages</a> known then was made by the German linguist <a href="/wiki/Franz_Bopp" title="Franz Bopp">Franz Bopp</a> in 1816. Though he did not attempt a reconstruction, he demonstrated that Greek, Latin and Sanskrit shared a common structure and a common lexicon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karl_Wilhelm_Friedrich_von_Schlegel" title="Karl Wilhelm Friedrich von Schlegel">Friedrich Schlegel</a> in 1808 first stated the importance of using the eldest possible form of a language when trying to prove its relationships;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> in 1818, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rasmus_Christian_Rask" title="Rasmus Christian Rask">Rasmus Christian Rask</a> developed the principle of regular sound changes to explain his observations of similarities between individual words in the Germanic languages and their cognates in Greek and <span class="nowrap">Latin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></span> <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Grimm" title="Jacob Grimm">Jacob Grimm</a> - better known for his <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grimm%27s_Fairy_Tales" title="Grimm's Fairy Tales">Fairy Tales</a></i> - in <i>Deutsche Grammatik</i> (published 1819-37 in four volumes) made use of the comparative method in attempting to show the development of the <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic languages</a> from a common origin, the first systematic study of <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguistics" title="Historical linguistics">diachronic</a> language change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both Rask and Grimm were unable to explain apparent exceptions to the sound laws that they had discovered. Although <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Grassmann" title="Hermann Grassmann">Hermann Grassmann</a> explained one of these anomalies with the publication of <a href="/wiki/Grassmann%27s_law" title="Grassmann's law">Grassmann's law</a> in 1862,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> it was <a href="/wiki/Karl_Verner" title="Karl Verner">Karl Verner</a> who in 1875 made a methodological breakthrough when he identified a pattern now known as <a href="/wiki/Verner%27s_law" title="Verner's law">Verner's law</a>, the first sound law based on comparative evidence showing that a <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonological</a> change in one <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phoneme</a> could depend on other factors within the same word, such as the neighbouring phonemes and the position of the <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">accent</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> now called <i>conditioning environments</i>.</p>
<p>Similar discoveries made by the <i>Junggrammatiker</i> (usually translated as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neogrammarians" title="Neogrammarians">Neogrammarians</a>) at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Leipzig" title="University of Leipzig">University of Leipzig</a> in the late 1800s led them to conclude that all sound changes were ultimately regular, resulting in the famous statement by <a href="/wiki/Karl_Brugmann" title="Karl Brugmann">Karl Brugmann</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Osthoff" title="Hermann Osthoff">Hermann Osthoff</a> in 1878 that "sound laws have no exceptions".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This idea is fundamental to the modern comparative method, since the method necessarily assumes regular correspondences between sounds in related languages, and consequently regular sound changes from the proto-language. This <i>Neogrammarian Hypothesis</i> led to application of the comparative method to reconstruct <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European</a> being at that time by far the most well-studied language family. Linguists working with other families soon followed suit, and the comparative method quickly became the established method for uncovering linguistic relationships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ssix_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-ssix-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>There is no fixed set of steps to be followed in the application of the comparative method, but some steps are suggested by <a href="/wiki/Lyle_Campbell" title="Lyle Campbell">Lyle Campbell</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Terry_Crowley_(linguist)" title="Terry Crowley (linguist)">Terry Crowley</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> both authors of introductory texts in historical linguistics. The abbreviated summary below is based on their concepts of how to proceed.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">Borrowings</a> or <a href="/wiki/False_cognate" title="False cognate">false cognates</a> could skew or obscure the correct data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> For example, English <i>taboo</i> (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tæbu]</span>) is like the six Polynesian forms due to borrowing from Tongan into English, and not because of a genetic similarity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> This problem can usually be overcome by using basic vocabulary such as kinship terms, numbers, body parts, pronouns, and other basic terms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Nonetheless, even basic vocabulary can be sometimes borrowed. <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a>, for example, borrowed the word for "mother", <i>äiti</i>, from <a href="/wiki/Gothic_language" title="Gothic language">Gothic</a> <i>aiþei</i>.;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> borrowed the pronouns "they", "them", and "their(s)" from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" title="Old Norse language">Norse</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a> (along with various other <a href="/wiki/East_Asian_languages" title="East Asian languages">East Asian languages</a>) borrowed its numbers from <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>. An extreme case is represented by <a href="/wiki/Pirah%C3%A3_language" title="Pirahã language">Pirahã</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muran_languages" title="Muran languages">Muran language</a> of South America, which is controversially<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> claimed to have borrowed all its <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">pronouns</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nheengatu_language" title="Nheengatu language">Nheengatu</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Step_2.2C_establish_correspondence_sets">Step 2, establish correspondence sets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comparative_method_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Step 2, establish correspondence sets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The next step is to determine the regular sound correspondences exhibited by the potential cognates lists. For example, in the Polynesian data above, it is visible that words that contain <i>t</i> in most of the languages listed have cognates in Hawaiian with <i>k</i> in the same position. This is visible in multiple cognate sets: 'one', 'three', 'man', and 'taboo' all show this relationship. This situation is termed a <i>regular correspondence</i> between <i>k</i> in Hawaiian and <i>t</i> in the other Polynesian languages. Similarly, in those data a regular correspondence can be seen between Hawaiian and Rapanui <i>h</i>, Tongan and Samoan <i>f</i>, Maori <i>ɸ</i>, and Rarotongan <i>ʔ</i>.</p>
<p>Mere phonetic similarity, as between <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> <i>day</i> and <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>dies</i> (both with the same meaning), has no probative value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ltwo_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-ltwo-31">[31]</a></sup> English initial <i>d-</i> does not <i>regularly</i> match <span class="nowrap">Latin <i>d-</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bonetwoseven_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-bonetwoseven-32">[32]</a></sup></span>—it is not possible to assemble a large set of English and Latin non-borrowed cognates such that English <i>d</i> repeatedly and consistently corresponds to Latin <i>d</i> at the beginning of a word—and whatever sporadic matches can be observed are due either to chance (as in the above example) or to <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">borrowing</a> (for example, Latin <i>diabolus</i> and English <i>devil</i>, both ultimately of Greek origin<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>). English and Latin <i>do</i> exhibit a regular correspondence of <i>t-</i> : <i>d-</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bonetwoseven_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-bonetwoseven-32">[32]</a></sup> (where the notation "A : B" means "A corresponds to B"); for example,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>

<p>If there are many regular correspondence sets of this kind (the more the better), then a common origin becomes a virtual certainty, particularly if some of the correspondences are non-trivial or unusual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ltwothree_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-ltwothree-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Step_3.2C_discover_which_sets_are_in_complementary_distribution">Step 3, discover which sets are in complementary distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comparative_method_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Step 3, discover which sets are in complementary distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During the late 18th to late 19th century, two major developments improved the method's effectiveness.</p>
<p>First, it was found that many sound changes are conditioned by a specific <i>context</i>. For example, in both <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspiration_(phonetics)" title="Aspiration (phonetics)">aspirated</a> <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">stop</a> evolved into an unaspirated one, but only if a second aspirate occurred later in the same word;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> this is <a href="/wiki/Grassmann%27s_law" title="Grassmann's law">Grassmann's law</a>, first described for <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanskrit_grammarians" title="Sanskrit grammarians">Sanskrit grammarian</a> <a href="/wiki/P%C4%81%E1%B9%87ini" title="Pāṇini">Pāṇini</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and promulgated by <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Grassmann" title="Hermann Grassmann">Hermann Grassmann</a> in 1863.</p>
<p>Second, it was found that sometimes sound changes occurred in contexts that were later lost. For instance, in Sanskrit <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">velars</a> (<i>k</i>-like sounds) were replaced by <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">palatals</a> (<i>ch</i>-like sounds) whenever the following vowel was <i>*i</i> or <i>*e</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Subsequent to this change, all instances of <i>*e</i> were replaced by <i>a</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The situation would have been unreconstructable, had not the original distribution of <i>e</i> and <i>a</i> been recoverable from the evidence of other <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European languages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> For instance, <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> suffix <i>que</i>, "and", preserves the original <i>*e</i> vowel that caused the consonant shift in Sanskrit:</p>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verner%27s_Law" title="Verner's Law">Verner's Law</a>, discovered by <a href="/wiki/Karl_Verner" title="Karl Verner">Karl Verner</a> in about 1875, is a similar case: the <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">voicing</a> of consonants in <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic languages</a> underwent a change that was determined by the position of the old Indo-European <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">accent</a>. Following the change, the accent shifted to initial position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Verner solved the puzzle by comparing the Germanic voicing pattern with Greek and Sanskrit accent patterns.</p>
<p>This stage of the comparative method, therefore, involves examining the correspondence sets discovered in step 2 and seeing which of them apply only in certain contexts. If two (or more) sets apply in <a href="/wiki/Complementary_distribution" title="Complementary distribution">complementary distribution</a>, they can be assumed to reflect a single original <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phoneme</a>: "some sound changes, particularly conditioned sound changes, can result in a proto-sound being associated with more than one correspondence set".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Step_4.2C_reconstruct_proto-phonemes">Step 4, reconstruct proto-phonemes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comparative_method_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Step 4, reconstruct proto-phonemes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Typology assists in deciding what reconstruction best fits the data. For example, the voicing of voiceless stops between vowels is common, but not the devoicing of voiced stops there. If a correspondence <i>-t-</i> : <i>-d-</i> between vowels is found in two languages, the proto-<a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phoneme</a> is more likely to be <i>*-t-</i>, with a development to the voiced form in the second language. The opposite reconstruction would create a rare type.</p>
<p>However, unusual sound changes do occur. The <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> word for <i>two</i>, for example, is reconstructed as <i>*dwō</i>, which is reflected in <a href="/wiki/Classical_Armenian" title="Classical Armenian">Classical Armenian</a> as <i>erku</i>. Several other cognates demonstrate a regular change <i>*dw-</i> → <i>erk-</i> in Armenian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Similarly, in Bearlake, a dialect of the <a href="/wiki/Athabaskan_languages" title="Athabaskan languages">Athabaskan language</a> of <a href="/wiki/Slavey_language" title="Slavey language">Slavey</a>, there has been a sound change of Proto-Athabaskan <i>*ts</i> → Bearlake <i><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">kʷ</span></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> It is very unlikely that <i>*dw-</i> changed directly into <i>erk-</i> and <i>*ts</i> into <i><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">kʷ</span></i>, but instead they must have gone through several intermediate steps to arrive at the later forms. It is not phonetic similarity which matters when utilizing the comparative method, but regular sound correspondences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ltwo_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-ltwo-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principle_of_Economy" title="Principle of Economy">Principle of Economy</a>, the reconstruction of a proto-phoneme should require as few sound changes as possible to arrive at the modern reflexes in the daughter languages. For example, <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian languages</a> exhibit the following correspondence set:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>

<p>The simplest reconstruction for this set would be either <i>*m</i> or <i>*b</i>. Both <i>*m</i> → <i>b</i> and <i>*b</i> → <i>m</i> are likely. Because <i>m</i> occurs in five of the languages, and <i>b</i> in only one, if <i>*b</i> is reconstructed, then it is necessary to assume five separate changes of <i>*b</i> → <i>m</i>, whereas if <i>*m</i> is reconstructed, it is only necessary to assume a single change of <i>*m</i> → <i>b</i>. <i>*m</i> would be most economical. (This argument assumes that the languages other than Arapaho are at least partly independent of each other. If they all formed a common subgroup, the development <i>*b</i> → <i>m</i> would only have to be assumed having occurred once.)</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Step_5.2C_examine_the_reconstructed_system_typologically">Step 5, examine the reconstructed system typologically</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comparative_method_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Step 5, examine the reconstructed system typologically">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>An earlier voiceless aspirated row was removed on grounds of insufficient evidence. Since the mid-20th century, a number of linguists have argued that this phonology is implausible;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> that it is extremely unlikely for a language to have a voiced aspirated (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breathy_voice" title="Breathy voice">breathy voice</a>) series without a corresponding voiceless aspirated series. A potential solution was provided by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Gamkrelidze" title="Thomas Gamkrelidze">Thomas Gamkrelidze</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vyacheslav_Ivanov_(philologist)" title="Vyacheslav Ivanov (philologist)">Vyacheslav Ivanov</a>, who argued that the series traditionally reconstructed as plain voiced should in fact be reconstructed as <a href="/wiki/Glottalization" title="Glottalization">glottalized</a> — either <a href="/wiki/Implosive_consonant" title="Implosive consonant">implosive</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">(ɓ, ɗ, ɠ)</span> or <a href="/wiki/Ejective_consonant" title="Ejective consonant">ejective</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">(pʼ, tʼ, kʼ)</span>. The plain voiceless and voiced aspirated series would thus be replaced by just voiceless and voiced, with aspiration being a non-distinctive quality of both.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> This example of the application of linguistic typology to linguistic reconstruction has become known as the <a href="/wiki/Glottalic_theory" title="Glottalic theory">Glottalic Theory</a>. It has a large number of proponents but is not generally accepted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> As an alternative, the voiceless aspirated row was restored.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (December 2016)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The reconstruction of proto-sounds logically precedes the reconstruction of grammatical <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">morphemes</a> (word-forming affixes and inflectional endings), patterns of <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">declension</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjugation" title="Grammatical conjugation">conjugation</a>, and so on. The full reconstruction of an unrecorded protolanguage is an open-ended task.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations">Limitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comparative_method_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Limitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Problems_with_the_history_of_historical_linguistics">Problems with the history of historical linguistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comparative_method_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Problems with the history of historical linguistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The limitations of the comparative method were recognized by the very linguists who developed it,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> but it is still seen as a valuable tool. In the case of Indo-European, the method seemed to at least partially validate the centuries-old search for an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ursprache" title="Ursprache">Ursprache</a>, the original language. These others were presumed ordered in a <a href="/wiki/Family_tree" title="Family tree">family tree</a>, becoming the <a href="/wiki/Tree_model" title="Tree model">Tree model</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neogrammarians" title="Neogrammarians">neogrammarians</a>.</p>
<p>The archaeologists followed suit, attempting to find archaeological evidence of a culture or cultures that could be presumed to have spoken a <a href="/wiki/Proto-language" title="Proto-language">proto-language</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vere_Gordon_Childe" title="Vere Gordon Childe">Vere Gordon Childe</a>'s <i>The Aryans: a study of Indo-European origins</i>, 1926. Childe was a philologist turned archaeologist. These views culminated in the <i>Siedlungsarchaologie</i>, or "settlement-archaeology", of <a href="/wiki/Gustaf_Kossinna" title="Gustaf Kossinna">Gustaf Kossinna</a>, becoming known as "Kossinna's Law." He asserted that cultures represent ethnic groups, including their languages. It was rejected as a law in the post-World-War-II era. The fall of Kossinna's Law removed the temporal and spatial framework previously applied to many proto-languages. Fox concludes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>The Comparative Method <i>as such</i> is not, in fact, historical; it provides evidence of linguistic relationships to which we may give a historical interpretation. ...[Our increased knowledge about the historical processes involved] has probably made historical linguists less prone to equate the idealizations required by the method with historical reality. ...Provided we keep [the interpretation of the results and the method itself] apart, the Comparative Method can continue to be used in the reconstruction of earlier stages of languages.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Proto-languages can be verified in many historical instances, such as Latin. Although no longer a law, settlement-archaeology is known to be essentially valid for some cultures that straddle history and prehistory, such as the Celtic Iron Age (mainly Celtic) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mycenaean_civilization" title="Mycenaean civilization">Mycenaean civilization</a> (mainly Greek). None of these models can be or have been completely rejected, and yet none alone are sufficient.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Problems_with_the_neogrammarian_hypothesis">Problems with the neogrammarian hypothesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comparative_method_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Problems with the neogrammarian hypothesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The foundation of the comparative method, and of comparative linguistics in general, is the <a href="/wiki/Neogrammarian" title="Neogrammarian">Neogrammarians</a>' fundamental assumption that "sound laws have no exceptions." When it was initially proposed, critics of the Neogrammarians proposed an alternate position, summarized by the maxim "each word has its own history".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Several types of change do in fact alter words in non-regular ways. Unless identified, they may hide or distort laws and cause false perceptions of relationship.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Borrowing">Borrowing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comparative_method_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Borrowing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>All languages <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">borrow words</a> from other languages in various contexts. They are likely to have followed the laws of the languages from which they were borrowed rather than the laws of the borrowing language.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Areal_diffusion">Areal diffusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comparative_method_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Areal diffusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Borrowing on a larger scale occurs in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Areal_feature_(linguistics)" title="Areal feature (linguistics)">areal diffusion</a>, when features are adopted by contiguous languages over a geographical area. The borrowing may be <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonological</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">morphological</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lexeme" title="Lexeme">lexical</a>. A false proto-language over the area may be reconstructed for them or may be taken to be a third language serving as a source of diffused features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several areal features and other influences may converge to form a <a href="/wiki/Sprachbund" title="Sprachbund">sprachbund</a>, a wider region sharing features that appear to be related but are diffusional. For instance, the <a href="/wiki/Mainland_Southeast_Asia_linguistic_area" title="Mainland Southeast Asia linguistic area">Mainland Southeast Asia linguistic area</a> suggested several false classifications of such languages as <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">Vietnamese</a> before it was recognized.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Random_mutations">Random mutations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comparative_method_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Random mutations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Sporadic changes, such as irregular inflections, compounding, and abbreviation, do not follow any laws. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> words <i>palabra</i> ('word'), <i>peligro</i> ('danger') and <i>milagro</i> ('miracle') should have been <i>parabla</i>, <i>periglo</i>, <i>miraglo</i> by regular sound changes from the Latin <i>parabŏla</i>, <i>perīcŭlum</i> and <i>mīrācŭlum</i>, but the <i>r</i> and <i>l</i> changed places by sporadic <a href="/wiki/Metathesis_(linguistics)" title="Metathesis (linguistics)">metathesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Analogy">Analogy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comparative_method_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Analogy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Analogy#Linguistics" title="Analogy">Analogy</a> is the sporadic change of a feature to be like another feature in the same or a different language. It may affect a single word or be generalized to an entire class of features, such as a verb paradigm. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a> word for <i>nine</i>, by regular sound changes from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Slavic_language" title="Proto-Slavic language">Proto-Slavic</a>, should have been <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/nʲevʲatʲ/</span>, but is in fact <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/dʲevʲatʲ/</span>. It is believed that the initial <i><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">nʲ-</span></i> changed to <i><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">dʲ-</span></i> under influence of the word for "ten" in Russian, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/dʲesʲatʲ/</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gradual_application">Gradual application</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comparative_method_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Gradual application">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Students of contemporary language changes, such as <a href="/wiki/William_Labov" title="William Labov">William Labov</a>, note that even a systematic sound change is at first applied in an unsystematic fashion, with the percentage of its occurrence in a person's speech dependent on various social factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> The sound change gradually spreads, a process known as <a href="/wiki/Lexical_diffusion" title="Lexical diffusion">lexical diffusion</a>. While not invalidating the Neogrammarians' axiom that "sound laws have no exceptions", their gradual application shows that they do not always apply to all lexical items at the same time. Hock notes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> "While it probably is true in the long run every word has its own history, it is not justified to conclude as some linguists have, that therefore the Neogrammarian position on the nature of linguistic change is falsified."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Problems_with_the_Tree_Model">Problems with the Tree Model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comparative_method_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Problems with the Tree Model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The comparative method is used to construct a <a href="/wiki/Tree_model" title="Tree model">Tree model</a> (German <i>Stammbaum</i>) of language evolution,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> in which daughter languages are seen as branching from the <a href="/wiki/Proto-language" title="Proto-language">proto-language</a>, gradually growing more distant from it through accumulated <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonological</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morpho-syntactic" title="Morpho-syntactic">morpho-syntactic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">lexical</a> changes.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_presumption_of_a_well-defined_node">The presumption of a well-defined node</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comparative_method_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: The presumption of a well-defined node">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wave_model_Schmidt.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="409" data-file-width="334" height="367" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Wave_model_Schmidt.svg/300px-Wave_model_Schmidt.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Wave_model_Schmidt.svg/450px-Wave_model_Schmidt.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Wave_model_Schmidt.svg/600px-Wave_model_Schmidt.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wave_model_Schmidt.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wave_model_(linguistics)" title="Wave model (linguistics)">Wave Model</a> has been proposed as an alternative to the <a href="/wiki/Tree_model" title="Tree model">tree model</a> for representing language change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> In this <a href="/wiki/Venn_diagram" title="Venn diagram">Venn diagram</a>, each circle represents a “wave” or <a href="/wiki/Isogloss" title="Isogloss">isogloss</a>, i.e. the maximum geographical extension of a linguistic change as it propagated through the speaker population. These circles, which represent successive historical events of propagation, typically intersect. Each language in the family differs as to which isoglosses it belongs to, i.e. which innovations it reflects. The tree model presumes that all the circles should be nested, and never crosscut; but studies in <a href="/wiki/Dialectology" title="Dialectology">dialectology</a> and historical linguistics have shown this assumption to be usually wrong, suggesting that the wave-based approach may be more realistic than the tree model. A genealogical family in which isoglosses intersect is called a <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">dialect continuum</a> or a <i><a href="/wiki/Linkage_(linguistics)" title="Linkage (linguistics)">linkage</a></i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tree_model" title="Tree model">tree model</a> features nodes that are presumed to be distinct proto-languages existing independently in distinct regions during distinct historical times. The reconstruction of unattested proto-languages lends itself to that illusion: they cannot be verified and the linguist is free to select whatever definite times and places for them seem best. Right from the outset of Indo-European studies, however, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Young_(scientist)" title="Thomas Young (scientist)">Thomas Young</a> said:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>It is not, however, very easy to say what the definition should be that should constitute a separate language, but it seems most natural to call those languages distinct, of which the one cannot be understood by common persons in the habit of speaking the other … Still, however, it may remain doubtfull whether the Danes and the Swedes could not, in general, understand each other tolerably well … nor is it possible to say if the twenty ways of pronouncing the sounds, belonging to the Chinese characters, ought or ought not to be considered as so many languages or dialects… But, … the languages so nearly allied must stand next to each other in a systematic order…</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The assumption of uniformity in a proto-language, implicit in the comparative method, is problematic. Even in small language communities there are always <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialect differences</a>, whether based on area, gender, class, or other factors. The <a href="/wiki/Pirah%C3%A3_language" title="Pirahã language">Pirahã language</a> of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> is spoken by only several hundred people, but it has at least two different dialects, one spoken by men and one by women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Campbell points out:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>It is not so much that the comparative method 'assumes' no variation; rather, it is just that there is nothing built into the comparative method which would allow it to address variation directly....This assumption of uniformity is a reasonable idealization; it does no more damage to the understanding of the language than, say, modern reference grammars do which concentrate on a language's general structure, typically leaving out consideration of regional or social variation.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Different dialects, as they evolve into separate languages, remain in contact with one another and influence each other. Even after they are considered distinct, languages near to one another continue to influence each other, often sharing grammatical, phonological, and lexical innovations. A change in one language of a family may spread to neighboring languages; and multiple waves of change are communicated like waves across language and dialect boundaries, each with its own randomly delimited range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> If a language is divided into an inventory of features, each with its own time and range (<a href="/wiki/Isogloss" title="Isogloss">isoglosses</a>), they do not all coincide. History and prehistory may not offer a time and place for a distinct coincidence, as may be the case for <a href="/wiki/Italic_languages" title="Italic languages">proto-Italic</a>, in which case the proto-language is only a concept. However, Hock<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> observes:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The discovery in the late nineteenth century that <a href="/wiki/Isogloss" title="Isogloss">isoglosses</a> can cut across well-established linguistic boundaries at first created considerable attention and controversy. And it became fashionable to oppose a wave theory to a tree theory... Today, however, it is quite evident that the phenomena referred to by these two terms are complementary aspects of linguistic change...</p>
</blockquote>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Subjectivity_of_the_reconstruction">Subjectivity of the reconstruction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comparative_method_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Subjectivity of the reconstruction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The reconstruction of unknown proto-languages is inherently subjective.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Algonquian_language" title="Proto-Algonquian language">Proto-Algonquian</a> example above, the choice of <i>*m</i> as the parent <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phoneme</a> is only <i>likely</i>, not <i>certain</i>. It is conceivable that a Proto-Algonquian language with <i>*b</i> in those positions split into two branches, one which preserved <i>*b</i> and one which changed it to <i>*m</i> instead; and while the first branch only developed into <a href="/wiki/Arapaho_language" title="Arapaho language">Arapaho</a>, the second spread out wider and developed into all the other <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_peoples" title="Algonquian peoples">Algonquian</a> tribes. It is also possible that the nearest common ancestor of the <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian languages</a> used some other sound instead, such as <i>*p</i>, which eventually mutated to <i>*b</i> in one branch and to <i>*m</i> in the other. While examples of strikingly complicated and even circular developments are indeed known to have occurred (such as <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">PIE</a> <i>*t</i> &gt; Pre-Proto-Germanic <i>*þ</i> &gt; <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic_language" title="Proto-Germanic language">PG</a> <i>*ð</i> &gt; Proto-West-Germanic <i>*d</i> &gt; <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> <i>t</i> in <i>fater</i> &gt; Modern German <i>Vater</i>), in the absence of any evidence or other reason to postulate a more complicated development, the preference of a simpler explanation is justified by the principle of parsimony, also known as <a href="/wiki/Occam%27s_razor" title="Occam's razor">Occam's razor</a>. Since reconstruction involves many of these choices, some linguists prefer to view the reconstructed features as abstract representations of sound correspondences, rather than as objects with a historical time and place.</p>
<p>The existence of proto-languages and the validity of the comparative method is verifiable in cases where the reconstruction can be matched to a known language, which may only be known as a shadow in the <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanwords</a> of another language. For example, <a href="/wiki/Finnic_languages" title="Finnic languages">Finnic languages</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a> have borrowed many words from an early stage of <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic</a>, and the shape of the loans matches the forms that have been reconstructed for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" title="Proto-Germanic">Proto-Germanic</a>. Finnish <i>kuningas</i> 'king' and <i>kaunis</i> 'beautiful' match the Germanic reconstructions *<i>kuningaz</i> and *<i>skauniz</i> (&gt; German <i>König</i> 'king', <i>schön</i> 'beautiful').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Additional_models">Additional models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comparative_method_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Additional models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wave_model_(linguistics)" title="Wave model (linguistics)">Wave model</a> was developed in the 1870s as an alternative to the <a href="/wiki/Tree_model" title="Tree model">Tree model</a>, in order to represent the historical patterns of language diversification. Both the tree-based and the wave-based representations are compatible with the Comparative Method.</p>
<p>By contrast, some approaches are incompatible with the Comparative method, including <a href="/wiki/Glottochronology" title="Glottochronology">glottochronology</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_lexical_comparison" title="Mass lexical comparison">mass lexical comparison</a>. Most historical linguists consider these to be flawed and unreliable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
