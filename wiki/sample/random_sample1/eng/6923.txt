<p>The <b>Vienna Woods</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i><b>Wienerwald</b></i></span>) are forested highlands that form the northeastern foothills of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Limestone_Alps" title="Northern Limestone Alps">Northern Limestone Alps</a> in the states of <a href="/wiki/Lower_Austria" title="Lower Austria">Lower Austria</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vienna_(state)" title="Vienna (state)">Vienna</a>. The 45 kilometres (28 mi) long and 20–30 kilometres (12–19 mi) wide range of hills is heavily wooded and a popular recreation area with the Viennese.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vienna_Woods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Vienna Woods are bounded by the rivers <a href="/wiki/Triesting" title="Triesting">Triesting</a>, <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6lsen" title="Gölsen">Gölsen</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traisen_River" title="Traisen River">Traisen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a>, and are on the border of the <a href="/wiki/Mostviertel" title="Mostviertel">Mostviertel</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Industrieviertel" title="Industrieviertel">Industrieviertel</a>, two of the four quarters of Lower Austria. Reaching into the <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">city of Vienna</a> itself, they are a favourite outdoor destination for the densely populated area around the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vienna_Woods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The highest elevation in the Vienna Woods is <a href="/wiki/Sch%C3%B6pfl" title="Schöpfl">Schöpfl</a> at 893 metres (2,930 ft) above <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>, the location of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leopold_Figl_observatory" title="Leopold Figl observatory">Leopold Figl</a> <a href="/wiki/Observatory" title="Observatory">observatory</a>. Important rivers in the Vienna Woods are the <a href="/wiki/Wien_(river)" title="Wien (river)">Wien</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Schwechat_(river)" title="Schwechat (river)">Schwechat</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Triesting" title="Triesting">Triesting</a>. The northeasternmost <a href="/wiki/Leopoldsberg" title="Leopoldsberg">Leopoldsberg</a> overlooking the Danube and the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Basin" title="Vienna Basin">Vienna Basin</a> forms the eastern end of the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alpine</a> chain.</p>
<p>Even though the Vienna Woods are a <a href="/wiki/Protected_area" title="Protected area">protected landscape</a> and important recreational area, they are threatened by <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">urban sprawl</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Lainzer_Tiergarten" title="Lainzer Tiergarten">Lainzer Tiergarten</a>, an extensive park populated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boar" title="Boar">wild boar</a> and other woodland animals, as well as the <i>Schwarzenbergpark</i>, were created on the territory of the city of Vienna during the 19th century. The natural park <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandstein-Wienerwald" title="Sandstein-Wienerwald">Sandstein-Wienerwald</a></i> is near <a href="/wiki/Purkersdorf" title="Purkersdorf">Purkersdorf</a>, some 20 km (12 mi) west of Vienna. There are also some caves, such as the <i>Dreidärrischenhöhle</i> ("cave of the three deaf men").</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vienna_Woods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The mountain ranges of the Vienna Woods are the transition between the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Alps" title="Eastern Alps">Eastern Alps</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Carpathian_Mountains" title="Carpathian Mountains">Carpathians</a>, separated by the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> and some tectonic lines near Vienna. The northern area is part of the Alps' <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> zone, while its south is part of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Limestone_Alps" title="Northern Limestone Alps">Northern Limestone Alps</a>. In the east, the border is the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_line&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thermal line (page does not exist)">thermal line</a>, which forms a geological break line to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viennese_Basin" title="Viennese Basin">Viennese Basin</a>. <a href="/wiki/Beech" title="Beech">Beeches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oaks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hornbeam" title="Hornbeam">hornbeams</a> grow throughout the north of the Wienerwald <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a>; in the south are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer">conifers</a>, mostly <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fir" title="Fir">firs</a>. The <i>Föhrenberge</i> (Pine Mountains) natural park is in the latter region.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vienna_Woods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Vienna Woods have probably been populated since the 8th century. Under the influence of the <a href="/wiki/Pannonian_Avars" title="Pannonian Avars">Avars</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Slavs" title="Slavs">Slavic</a> population settled here after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lkerwanderung" title="Völkerwanderung">Völkerwanderung</a>, which may account for village names such as <a href="/wiki/D%C3%B6bling" title="Döbling">Döbling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liesing" title="Liesing">Liesing</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gablitz" title="Gablitz">Gablitz</a>. The Vienna Woods was a princely hunting ground, but from the 16th century onwards <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forestry</a> was practised. From 1840 industrial development encouraged increased settlement of the area. In 1870, plans were brought forward to clear most of the forest, but this caused widespread public resistance, in which <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Josef_Sch%C3%B6ffel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Josef Schöffel (page does not exist)">Josef Schöffel</a> was one of the most important figures.</p>
<p>In 1987, the governors of the states of <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lower_Austria" title="Lower Austria">Lower Austria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Burgenland" title="Burgenland">Burgenland</a> signed the <i>Vienna Woods Declaration</i> to protect nature in the region. The film actress <a href="/wiki/Hedy_Lamarr" title="Hedy Lamarr">Hedy Lamarr</a>, in keeping with her request for when she died, had her ashes spread in the Vienna Woods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
