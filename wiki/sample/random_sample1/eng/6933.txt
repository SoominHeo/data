<p><b>Vidin</b> (<a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a>: <span lang="bg">Видин</span>, <small>pronounced </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Bulgarian_and_Macedonian" title="Help:IPA for Bulgarian and Macedonian">[ˈvidin]</a></span>) is <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_and_towns_in_Bulgaria" title="List of cities and towns in Bulgaria">a port town</a> on the southern bank of the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> in north-western <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>. It is close to the borders with <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>, and is also the administrative centre of <a href="/wiki/Vidin_Province" title="Vidin Province">Vidin Province</a>, as well as of the Metropolitan of Vidin (since 870).</p>
<p>An agricultural and trade centre, Vidin has a fertile hinterland renowned for its wines.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vidin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name is archaically spelled as <i>Widdin</i> in English. Old name <i>Dunonia</i> itself meant "fortified hill" in <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic</a> with the typically <i>dun</i> found frequently in Celtic place names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vidin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vidin is the westernmost important Bulgarian Danube port and is situated on one of the southernmost sections of the river. The <a href="/wiki/New_Europe_Bridge" title="New Europe Bridge">New Europe Bridge</a>, completed in 2013, connects Vidin to the Romanian town of <a href="/wiki/Calafat" title="Calafat">Calafat</a> on the opposite bank of the Danube. Previously, a ferry located 2 km (1 mi) from the town was in use for that purpose.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vidin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vidin emerged at the place of an old <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> settlement known as <i>Dunonia</i>. The settlement evolved into a <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> fortified town called <i>Bononia</i>. The town grew into one of the important centres of the province of Upper <a href="/wiki/Moesia" title="Moesia">Moesia</a>, encompassing the territory of modern north-western Bulgaria and eastern <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>.</p>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Slavs" title="Slavs">Slavs</a> settled in the area, they called the town <i>Badin</i> or <i>Bdin</i>, where the modern name comes from. Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Anna_Komnene" title="Anna Komnene">Anna Komnene</a> refers to it as <i>Vidynē</i> (Βιδύνη) in the <a href="/wiki/Alexiad" title="Alexiad">Alexiad</a>.</p>

<p>Vidin's main landmark, the <a href="/wiki/Baba_Vida" title="Baba Vida">Baba Vida</a> fortress, was built in the period from the 10th to the 14th century. In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> Vidin used to be an important Bulgarian city, a bishop seat and capital of a large province. Between 971 and 976 the town was the center of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samuil_of_Bulgaria" title="Samuil of Bulgaria">Samuil</a>'s possessions while his brothers ruled to the south. In 1003 Vidin was seized by <a href="/wiki/Basil_II" title="Basil II">Basil II</a> after an eight-month siege because of the betrayal of the local bishop. Its importance once again rose during the <a href="/wiki/Second_Bulgarian_Empire" title="Second Bulgarian Empire">Second Bulgarian Empire</a> (1185–1422) and its <i><a href="/wiki/Despot_(court_title)" title="Despot (court title)">despots</a></i> were influential figures in the Empire and were on several occasions chosen for Emperors. From the mid 13th century it was ruled by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shishman_dynasty" title="Shishman dynasty">Shishman</a> family. In 1356, Bulgarian <a href="/wiki/Tsar" title="Tsar">Tsar</a> <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Alexander_of_Bulgaria" title="Ivan Alexander of Bulgaria">Ivan Alexander</a> isolated Vidin from the Bulgarian monarchy and appointed his son <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ivan_Stratsimir_of_Bulgaria" title="Ivan Stratsimir of Bulgaria">Ivan Stratsimir</a> (1356–1396) as absolute ruler of Vidin's new <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">city-state</a> - the <a href="/wiki/Tsardom_of_Vidin" title="Tsardom of Vidin">Tsardom of Vidin</a> (Bdin / Badin).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hungarian_occupation_of_Vidin">Hungarian occupation of Vidin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vidin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hungarian occupation of Vidin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_occupation_of_Vidin" title="Hungarian occupation of Vidin">Hungarian occupation of Vidin</a></div>
<p>In 1365, the <a href="/wiki/Tsardom_of_Vidin" title="Tsardom of Vidin">Tsardom of Vidin</a> <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_occupation_of_Vidin" title="Hungarian occupation of Vidin">was occupied</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magyars" title="Magyars">Magyar</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crusade" title="Crusade">crusaders</a>. Under <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Hungarian</a> rule, the city became known as <i>Bodony</i>, but the occupation was short-lived. In 1369, the Second Bulgarian empire drove out the Hungarian military, but in 1396 Vidin was occupied by a foreign force again.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Ottomans">The Ottomans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vidin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Ottomans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Widdin_Eyalet" title="Widdin Eyalet">Widdin Eyalet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sanjak_of_Vidin" title="Sanjak of Vidin">Sanjak of Vidin</a></div>
<p>The Ottomans went on to conquer the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Despotate#Bulgarian_Empire" title="Despotate">despotates</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dobrudzha" title="Dobrudzha">Dobrudzha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prilep" title="Prilep">Prilep</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velbazhd" title="Velbazhd">Velbazhd</a> as well. Vidin's independence did not last long. In 1396 the Ottomans invaded and turned Vidin into a sanjdak.</p>
<p>In the late years of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Ottoman_Bulgaria" title="History of Ottoman Bulgaria">Ottoman rule</a>, Vidin was the centre of Turkish rebel <a href="/wiki/Osman_Pazvanto%C4%9Flu" title="Osman Pazvantoğlu">Osman Pazvantoğlu</a>'s breakaway state.</p>
<p>In 1853, <i>The Times</i> of London reported that Widdin, as it was called, was</p>
<blockquote>
<p>a considerable town, with a population of about 26,000, and a garrison of 8,000 to 10,000 men. Widdin is one of the important fortified places of the military line of the Danube. It covers the approaches of <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Servia</a>, commands <a href="/wiki/Oltenia" title="Oltenia">Little Wallachia</a>, the defiles of <a href="/wiki/Transylvania" title="Transylvania">Transylvania</a>, and, above all, the opening of the road which leads through <a href="/wiki/Ni%C5%A1" title="Niš">Nissia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sofia,_Bulgaria" title="Sofia, Bulgaria">Sophia</a> on to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adrianople" title="Adrianople">Adrianople</a>. Its form is an irregular <a href="/wiki/Pentagon" title="Pentagon">pentagon</a>; it is strongly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bastions" title="Bastions">bastioned</a>, possesses a fortified castle, with two redoubts in the islands, and its defences are completed by an extensive <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marsh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_rule">Modern rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vidin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Modern rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Bulgarian_War" title="Serbo-Bulgarian War">Serbo-Bulgarian War</a> (1885), the town was besieged by a Serbian army.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vidin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vidin has a <a href="/wiki/Humid_subtropical_climate" title="Humid subtropical climate">humid subtropical climate</a> transforming to temperate continental climate. In the winter months, <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">inversions</a> are very common. The average annual temperature is 12.2 °C (54.0 °F).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethnic.2C_linguistic_and_religious_composition">Ethnic, linguistic and religious composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vidin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ethnic, linguistic and religious composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the latest 2011 census data, the individuals declared their ethnic identity were distributed as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turks_in_Bulgaria" title="Turks in Bulgaria">Turks</a>: 60 (0.1%)</li>
<li>Others: 199 (0.4%)</li>
<li>Indefinable: 280 (0.6%)</li>
<li>Undeclared: 3,647 (7.6%)</li>
</ul>
<p>Total: 48,071</p>
<p>There is minor number of Gypsies within the city limits. There are 3,335 in the city and 3,753 in the municipality, while the Bulgarians are 40,550 in the city and 54,546 in the municipality.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vidin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

The Vidin <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">Synagogue</a>, deserted after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> <a href="/wiki/Aliyah" title="Aliyah">emigration</a> to Israel.</div>

<p>Vidin maintains two well-preserved medieval fortresses, <a href="/wiki/Baba_Vida" title="Baba Vida">Baba Vida</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kaleto&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kaleto (page does not exist)">Kaleto</a>, as well as many old Orthodox churches such as St Pantaleimon, St Petka (both 17th century), and St Dimitar (<a href="/wiki/Demetrius_of_Thessaloniki" title="Demetrius of Thessaloniki">Demetrius of Thessaloniki</a>) (19th century), the Vidin <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">Synagogue</a> (1894), the <a href="/wiki/Osman_Pazvanto%C4%9Flu" title="Osman Pazvantoğlu">Osman Pazvantoğlu</a> <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">Mosque</a> and <a href="/wiki/Library" title="Library">library</a>, the late 18th-century Turkish ruler of north-western Bulgaria, the <a href="/wiki/Krastata_Kazarma" title="Krastata Kazarma">Krastata Kazarma</a> of 1798, and a number of old Renaissance buildings. Also remarkable is the theatre building which was the first Bulgarian theatre in "European model" and was built in 1891.</p>
<p>Another tourist attraction in the Vidin area is the town of <a href="/wiki/Belogradchik" title="Belogradchik">Belogradchik</a>, famous for its unique and impressive rock formations, the <a href="/wiki/Belogradchik_Rocks" title="Belogradchik Rocks">Belogradchik Rocks</a> and the medieval <a href="/wiki/Belogradchik_Fortress" title="Belogradchik Fortress">Belogradchik Fortress</a> and also the nearby <a href="/wiki/Magura_Cave" title="Magura Cave">Magura Cave</a> with its beautiful prehistoric cave paintings.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vidin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In Vidin is a border-station to neighbouring Romania via the Danube river. It was operated by ferryboats only until 14 June 2013 when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vidin%E2%80%93Calafat_Bridge" title="Vidin–Calafat Bridge">Vidin–Calafat Bridge</a> opened. Crossing by ferry was possible only every ½ hour with just five trucks per ferry. Ticket prices were €50 per truck and €12 per car.</p>
<p>The city has an airport (ICAO code <i>LBVD</i>) a few kilometres to the north-west; as of 2014<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Vidin&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there is no scheduled service, and the buildings are in a state of disrepair.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Landmarks">Landmarks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vidin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Landmarks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Close to the town lies a powerful medium wave broadcasting station (since 1973) whose signals can be easily received throughout Europe. It works on 576 kHz and on 1224 kHz with a power of 500 kW each. For transmission on 576 kHz a 259-metre-tall (850 ft) guyed mast equipped with a cage antenna at its lower part is used. The transmission of 576 kHz Radio Hristo Botev is on hold for now, because of the world financial crisis and it is unknown when it will resume broadcasting. A powerful FM transmitter on 88.2 MHz provides good coverage for Hristo Botev radio. Only 1224 kHz Radio Bulgaria remains atm. For the transmission on 1224 kHz four guyed masts, insulated against ground, which are each equipped with a cage antenna are used, which allows a switchable directional pattern.</p>
