<p>The <b>biretta</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>biretum, birretum</i></span>) is a square <a href="/wiki/Cap" title="Cap">cap</a> with three or four peaks or horns, sometimes surmounted by a tuft. Traditionally the three peaked biretta is worn by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic_Church_hierarchy" title="Catholic Church hierarchy">Roman Catholic clergy</a> and some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglican" title="Anglican">Anglican</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutheran" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a> clergy. The four peaked biretta is worn as academic dress by those holding a doctoral degree from a pontifical faculty or <a href="/wiki/Pontifical_university" title="Pontifical university">pontifical university</a>. Occasionally the biretta is worn by advocates in law courts, for instance the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advocates" title="Advocates">advocates</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biretta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:127px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bonete_Sammlung_Philippi.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1575" data-file-width="2000" height="98" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Bonete_Sammlung_Philippi.JPG/125px-Bonete_Sammlung_Philippi.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Bonete_Sammlung_Philippi.JPG/188px-Bonete_Sammlung_Philippi.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Bonete_Sammlung_Philippi.JPG/250px-Bonete_Sammlung_Philippi.JPG 2x" width="125"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bonete_Sammlung_Philippi.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The "Spanish version" of the biretta, from <a href="/wiki/The_Philippi_Collection" title="The Philippi Collection">The Philippi Collection</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The origins of the biretta are uncertain. It is mentioned as early as the tenth century. One possible origin is the academic cap of the high Middle Ages, which was soft and square. This is also the ancestor of the modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mortarboard" title="Mortarboard">mortarboard</a> used today in secular universities. The biretta seems to have become a more widely used as an ecclesiastical vestment after the synod of Bergamo, 1311, ordered the clergy to wear the "bireta on their heads after the manner of laymen."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The tuft or <a href="/wiki/Pom-pom" title="Pom-pom">pom</a> sometimes seen on the biretta was added later; the earliest forms of the biretta did not bear the device.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Liturgical_biretta">Liturgical biretta</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biretta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Liturgical biretta">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catholic_use">Catholic use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biretta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Catholic use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The biretta is used by all ranks of the Latin clergy from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_(Catholicism)" title="Cardinal (Catholicism)">cardinals</a> to <a href="/wiki/Priest" title="Priest">priests</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deacon" title="Deacon">deacons</a> and seminarians. Those worn by cardinals are scarlet red and made of silk. After the Second Vatican Council the ceremony of giving the <a href="/wiki/Galero" title="Galero">galero</a> to cardinals was replaced with giving the biretta. The biretta of a <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishop</a> is <a href="/wiki/Amaranth_(color)" title="Amaranth (color)">amaranth</a> in color, while those worn by priests, deacons, and seminarians are black. The <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">pope</a> does not make use of the biretta, instead wearing the more ancient <a href="/wiki/Camauro" title="Camauro">camauro</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">Pope Benedict XVI</a> has brought back into use.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance">Appearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biretta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Appearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Cardinals bear no tuft or "pom" (they are given their birettas and zuchettos by the Pope who elevated them in a ceremony named a <a href="/wiki/Papal_consistory" title="Papal consistory">consistory</a>- they will form a line, and kneel before him when receiving them), bishops bear a purple pom, priests who have been appointed as <a href="/wiki/Prelate" title="Prelate">prelates</a> to certain positions within the Vatican wear a black biretta with red pom, diocesan priests and deacons wear a black biretta with or without a black pom. It is often asserted that seminarians are only entitled to wear a biretta without a pom-pom, but there would seem to be no formal ruling on this point. Priests in monastic and mendicant religious orders that have their own habits (Benedictines, Franciscans, Dominicans, etc.) do not generally wear birettas: in most circumstances, even liturgical, the monastic hood took the place of the biretta. <a href="/wiki/Canons_Regular" title="Canons Regular">Canons Regular</a> generally do—for instance the canons of the <a href="/wiki/Premonstratensians" title="Premonstratensians">Order of Prémontré</a> wear a white biretta. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clerks_Regular" title="Clerks Regular">Clerks Regular</a> (that is, post-Renaissance religious orders primarily dedicated to priestly ministry, for instance the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuits" title="Jesuits">Jesuits</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redemptorists" title="Redemptorists">Redemptorists</a>) generally wear a black biretta with no tuft. Other priests who belong to various forms of community life, as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congregation_of_the_Oratory_of_St._Philip_Neri" title="Congregation of the Oratory of St. Philip Neri">Congregation of the Oratory of St. Philip Neri</a> for instance, generally also wear birettas, but without a pom.</p>
<p>The liturgical biretta has three peaks (four peaks however are the norm in Germany and the Netherlands), with the "peak-less" corner worn on the left side of the head. According to the 1913 <i><a href="/wiki/Catholic_Encyclopedia" title="Catholic Encyclopedia">Catholic Encyclopedia</a></i>, "It was formerly the rule that a priest should always wear it in giving absolution in confession, and it is probable that the ancient usage which requires an English judge assume the 'black cap' in pronouncing sentence of death is of identical origin."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The use of the biretta has not been abolished as a result of changes in the regulation of clerical dress and vesture following the <a href="/wiki/Second_Vatican_Council" title="Second Vatican Council">Second Vatican Council</a> and still remains the correct liturgical headgear for those in Holy Orders whilst "in choir", but its use has been made optional. Its use is prevalent among <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishops</a> and cardinals, and less so among other clergy. Some priests wear it during outdoor services such as burials or processions and, as is intended, during the celebration of Mass and other liturgical services. The biretta is also worn by a priest, deacon, subdeacon, and bishop in attendance at a Mass offered according to the rubrics for the Roman Missal of 1962.</p>

<p>Birettas are also occasionally worn by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Catholic" title="Anglo-Catholic">Anglo-Catholic</a> Anglican clergy, though is it generally considered a Romanism. <a href="/wiki/Canon_(priest)" title="Canon (priest)">Canons</a> and deans could wear a black biretta with a purple pom.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_cap" title="Canterbury cap">Canterbury cap</a> is of similar origin to the biretta, and, although seldom used since the early 20th century, has been considered a more authentically Anglican alternative to the biretta. The Canterbury cap has a soft, square top rather than the rigid horns that developed on the biretta.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Doctoral_degrees">Doctoral degrees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biretta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Doctoral degrees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_medieval_universities">Use in medieval universities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biretta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Use in medieval universities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the medieval university, the ceremony by which a new master or doctor received his degree included the birretatio, or imposition of the biretta. This was often given with a token book in recognition of the person's scholarship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The academic biretta developed into various styles of academic headgear on the European continent and in the British Isles. Today some secular universities still use the term, if not the actual biretta, to name their academic cap.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_by_pontifical_universities">Use by pontifical universities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biretta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Use by pontifical universities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>For those holding doctoral degrees from a pontifical university or faculty, whether ordained or lay, "the principal mark of a Doctor's dignity is the four horned biretta."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commencement_ceremony" title="Commencement ceremony">commencement ceremonies</a> and other academic settings, doctors of the four ecclesiastical disciplines (Philosophy, Theology, Canon Law, and Sacred Scriptures) from pontifical faculties and universities have a canonical right to wear the doctoral biretta as stated in the Codex Iuris Canonici, 1917, can. 1378, and explained in commentary 262 of the Commentarium Codicis Iuris Canonici as follows:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"262. Doctoratus ac Scentiae effectus canonici sic recensentur can. 1378...doctoribus seu gradum academicum in una ex quatuor supradictis facultatibus &lt;&lt;vide 261: philosophia, theologia, ius canonicum, Sacra Scriptura&gt;&gt; supremum obtinentibus, rite creatis, seu promotis regulariter post examen, iuxta « statuta a Sede Apostolica probata » (can. 1376, § 2) saltem quoad usum validum « facultatis ab eadem Aplca. Sede concessae » (can. 1377, § 1), deferendi, extra sacras functiones, (quarum nomine ad hunc eflectum non venit ex usu sacra praedicatio), nisi aliunde amplietur eis hoc ius quoad a) annulum etiam cum gemma « ipsis a iure huius canonis concessum » (can. 136, § 2), b) et biretum doctorale, (idest: cum quatuor apicibus) utpote insigne huius gradus ac diverso colore ornatum pro Facultate.:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Colors_of_doctorate_birettas">Colors of doctorate birettas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biretta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Colors of doctorate birettas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The color of the doctoral biretta given by ecclesiastical universities and faculties is normally black, with colored piping corresponding to the faculty of study in which the degree was granted:</p>
<ul>
<li>Theology (S.T.D.): Scarlet</li>
<li>Ministry (D.Min.): Crimson<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Canon Law (J.C.D.): Green</li>
<li>Philosophy (Ph.D.): Blue</li>
</ul>
<p>At one time, different universities had different practices concerning the color and style of the biretta itself. One author, nearly a century ago, reports that in his day the Roman universities gave a doctoral biretta in black silk,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Louvain gave a biretta with a colored tuft according to the academic discipline in which the doctorate was awarded,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and the Catholic University of America gave a velvet biretta with red tuft and trim to doctors of theology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The 'traditional' biretta at the <a href="/wiki/Pontifical_University_of_Saint_Thomas_Aquinas" title="Pontifical University of Saint Thomas Aquinas">Pontifical University of Saint Thomas Aquinas, <i>Angelicum</i></a>, is white, to correspond to the white Dominican habit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Also, the academic senate of the <i>Angelicum</i> in its May 2011 meeting indicated that the black biretta may be used with trim and pom in the color of the particular faculty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>A three-peaked black biretta with appropriately colored piping may be similarly used by those receiving the licentiate degree (S.T.L., Ph.L.).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Depictions_in_art_in_Catholicism">Depictions in art in Catholicism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biretta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Depictions in art in Catholicism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The doctoral biretta is sometimes seen in depictions of St. <a href="/wiki/Teresa_of_%C3%81vila" title="Teresa of Ávila">Teresa of Ávila</a>, because she was declared a doctor by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Salamanca" title="University of Salamanca">University of Salamanca</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This recognition is distinct from her status as a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_the_Church" title="Doctor of the Church">Doctor of the Church</a>. The doctoral biretta has been borrowed for depictions of another doctor of the Church, St. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9r%C3%A8se_de_Lisieux" title="Thérèse de Lisieux">Thérèse de Lisieux</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_by_women">Use by women</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biretta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Use by women">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg"><img alt="[icon]" data-file-height="31" data-file-width="44" height="14" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" width="20"/></a></td>
<td class="mbox-text"><span class="mbox-text-span"><b>This section needs expansion</b>. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biretta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small> <small><i>(August 2011)</i></small></span></td>

<p>The biretta was considered as possible headwear for female barristers in England and Wales. In 1922, immediately prior to the first lady being called to the Bar, there was discussion among the senior judges about what she should wear on her head. Darling J and Horridge J suggested the biretta, but were outvoted by the other nine judges present. As a result, female barristers wear the same unpowdered men's wig as male barristers, which completely covers the hair.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
