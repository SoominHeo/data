<p>The <b><i>Flakpanzer</i> IV "<i>Wirbelwind</i>"</b> (<b>Whirlwind</b> in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>) was a German <a href="/wiki/Self-propelled_anti-aircraft_weapon" title="Self-propelled anti-aircraft weapon">self-propelled anti-aircraft gun</a> based on the <a href="/wiki/Panzer_IV" title="Panzer IV">Panzer IV</a> tank. It was developed in 1944 as a successor to the earlier <i><a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6belwagen" title="Möbelwagen">Möbelwagen</a></i> self-propelled anti-aircraft gun.</p>
<p>In the first years of the war, the <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">German military forces</a> had less interest in developing self-propelled anti-aircraft guns, but as the Allies began to gain air superiority, the need for more mobile and better-armed self-propelled anti-aircraft guns increased. During the early summer of 1944, SS-<i><a href="/wiki/Hauptsturmf%C3%BChrer" title="Hauptsturmführer">Hauptsturmführer</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Karl_Wilhelm_Krause" title="Karl Wilhelm Krause">Karl Wilhelm Krause</a> with the <a href="/wiki/12th_SS_Panzer_Division_Hitlerjugend" title="12th SS Panzer Division Hitlerjugend">12th SS Panzer Division <i>Hitlerjugend</i></a> came up with the concept of the <i>Flakpanzer</i> IV <i>Wirbelwind</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Achtungpanzer.com_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Achtungpanzer.com-1">[1]</a></sup> He presented the concept to SS-<i><a href="/wiki/Obersturmbannf%C3%BChrer" title="Obersturmbannführer">Obersturmbannführer</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Max_W%C3%BCnsche" title="Max Wünsche">Max Wünsche</a>, commanding officer of the 12th SS Panzer Regiment and it was approved by Hitler.</p>
<p>The Panzer IV's turret was removed and replaced with an open-top, nine-sided turret which housed a quadruple <a href="/wiki/2_cm_Flak_30/38/Flakvierling#2_cm_Flakvierling_38" title="2 cm Flak 30/38/Flakvierling">2 cm Flakvierling 38 L/112.5</a>. A closed-top design would have been preferable, but this was not possible due to the heavy smoke generated by the four anti-aircraft guns. The shape of the turret earned it the nickname Keksdose ("Biscuit Tin").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Production of the tank was carried out by Ostbau Werke in <a href="/wiki/%C5%BBaga%C5%84" title="Żagań">Sagan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silesia" title="Silesia">Silesia</a>. However, in combat the 2 cm shells were felt to be insufficiently effective against aircraft, and so a more powerful successor was produced which eventually replaced it. Known as the <a href="/wiki/Ostwind" title="Ostwind"><i>Flakpanzer</i> IV <i>Ostwind</i></a> (East Wind), the successor was equipped with a single <a href="/wiki/3.7_cm_Flak_18/36/37/43#Flak_43" title="3.7 cm Flak 18/36/37/43">3.7 cm Flak 43</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Achtungpanzer.com_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Achtungpanzer.com-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated110_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated110-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>The combination of armor and rapid fire from the four guns of the <i>Wirbelwind</i> made it very effective against lightly armoured ground targets such as trucks and armored cars; infantry were particularly vulnerable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Between 87 and 105 <i>Wirbelwinds</i> were produced during the war, but due to discrepancies between the recorded production numbers at Ostbau Werke and <i><a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a></i> service records, the exact number will probably never be known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Achtungpanzer.com_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Achtungpanzer.com-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated110_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated110-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
