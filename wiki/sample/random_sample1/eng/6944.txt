<p><b>Beverly Atlee Cleary</b> (née <b>Bunn</b>; April 12, 1916) is an American writer of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chapter_books" title="Chapter books">children's</a> and <a href="/wiki/Young_adult_fiction" title="Young adult fiction">young adult fiction</a>. One of America's most successful living authors, 91 million copies of her books have been sold worldwide since her first book was published in 1950.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-springen_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-springen-1">[1]</a></sup> Some of her best known characters are <a href="/wiki/Henry_Huggins" title="Henry Huggins">Henry Huggins</a> and his dog <a href="/wiki/Ribsy" title="Ribsy">Ribsy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ramona_Quimby" title="Ramona Quimby">Ramona</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beezus_Quimby" title="Beezus Quimby">Beezus Quimby</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ralph_S._Mouse" title="Ralph S. Mouse">Ralph S. Mouse</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harper_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-harper-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>She won the 1981 <a href="/wiki/National_Book_Award_for_Young_People%27s_Literature" title="National Book Award for Young People's Literature">National Book Award</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/Ramona_and_Her_Mother" title="Ramona and Her Mother">Ramona and Her Mother</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nba1981_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-nba1981-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paper_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-paper-4">[a]</a></sup> and the 1984 <a href="/wiki/Newbery_Medal" title="Newbery Medal">Newbery Medal</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/Dear_Mr._Henshaw" title="Dear Mr. Henshaw">Dear Mr. Henshaw</a></i>. For her lifetime contributions to American literature, Cleary received the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Medal_of_Arts" title="National Medal of Arts">National Medal of Arts</a>, recognition as a <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress_Living_Legend" title="Library of Congress Living Legend">Library of Congress Living Legend</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Laura_Ingalls_Wilder_Medal" title="Laura Ingalls Wilder Medal">Laura Ingalls Wilder Medal</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Library_Service_to_Children" title="Association for Library Service to Children">Association for Library Service to Children</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cup_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-cup-5">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beverly_Cleary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Beverly Atlee Bunn was born on April 12, 1916, in <a href="/wiki/McMinnville,_Oregon" title="McMinnville, Oregon">McMinnville, Oregon</a>. Cleary was an only child<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paul_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-paul-6">[5]</a></sup> and lived on a farm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gibbs_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-gibbs-7">[6]</a></sup> Her mother was a school teacher and her father was a farmer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shepherd_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-shepherd-8">[7]</a></sup> When she was six years old, her family moved to <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland, Oregon</a>, where she attended elementary and high school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scholastic_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-scholastic-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In first grade, Cleary's teacher placed her in a group for struggling readers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scholastic_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-scholastic-9">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ulin_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ulin-10">[9]</a></sup> Cleary said, "The first grade was separated into three reading groups—Bluebirds, Redbirds, and Blackbirds. I was a Blackbird. To be a Blackbird was to be disgraced. I wanted to read, but somehow could not."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shepherd_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-shepherd-8">[7]</a></sup> With the help of a school librarian who introduced her to books she enjoyed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hewitt_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-hewitt-11">[10]</a></sup> Cleary caught up by third grade<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paul_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-paul-6">[5]</a></sup> and started to spend a lot of time reading and at the library.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scholastic_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-scholastic-9">[8]</a></sup> By sixth grade, a teacher suggested that Cleary should become a children's writer based on essays she'd written for class assignments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ulin_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-ulin-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>After high school, Cleary entered <a href="/wiki/Chaffey_College" title="Chaffey College">Chaffey Junior College</a> with aspirations of becoming a children's librarian. After two years at Chaffey, she was accepted at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_California_at_Berkeley" title="University of California at Berkeley">University of California at Berkeley</a>, where she earned a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_arts" title="Bachelor of arts">bachelor of arts</a> in English in 1938.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shepherd_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-shepherd-8">[7]</a></sup> She also met her future husband, Clarence Cleary, during her time at Berkeley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berkeley_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-berkeley-12">[11]</a></sup> In 1939, she graduated from the School of Library and Information Science at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Washington" title="University of Washington">University of Washington</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldsmith_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-goldsmith-13">[12]</a></sup> and accepted a year-long position as a children's librarian in <a href="/wiki/Yakima,_Washington" title="Yakima, Washington">Yakima, Washington</a>. Her parents disapproved of her relationship with Clarence T. Cleary, an auditor and accountant, so the couple eloped and were married in 1940.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berkeley_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-berkeley-12">[11]</a></sup> After the end of World War II, they settled in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carmel,_California" title="Carmel, California">Carmel, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nndb_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-nndb-14">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bowman_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-bowman-15">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beverly_Cleary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cleary obtained her baccalaureate and master's degree in Library Science at the University of Washington. After graduation she served as a children's librarian in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yakima_Washington" title="Yakima Washington">Yakima Washington</a> and then as the post librarian at the U.S. Army Hospital in <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland, California</a>. In 1942 she began working as a full-time writer for children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a children's librarian, Cleary empathized with her young patrons who had difficulty finding books with characters they could identify with<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gibbs_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-gibbs-7">[6]</a></sup> and she struggled to find enough books to suggest that would appeal to them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paul_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-paul-6">[5]</a></sup> After a few years of making recommendations and performing live storytelling in her role as a librarian, Cleary decided to start writing children's books about characters that young readers could relate to.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-warren_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-warren-18">[17]</a></sup> Cleary has said, “I believe in that ‘missionary spirit’ among children’s librarians. Kids deserve books of literary quality, and librarians are so important in encouraging them to read and selecting books that are appropriate.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hewitt_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-hewitt-11">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldsmith_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-goldsmith-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cleary's first book, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Huggins_(book)" title="Henry Huggins (book)">Henry Huggins</a></i> (1950), was accepted for immediate publication and was the first in a series of fictional <a href="/wiki/Chapter_book" title="Chapter book">chapter books</a> about Henry, his dog Ribsy, his neighborhood friend <a href="/wiki/Beezus_Quimby" title="Beezus Quimby">Beezus</a> and her little sister <a href="/wiki/Ramona_Quimby" title="Ramona Quimby">Ramona</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ulin_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-ulin-10">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nndb_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-nndb-14">[13]</a></sup> Like many of her later works, <i>Henry Huggins</i> is a novel about people living ordinary lives and is based on Cleary's own childhood experiences, the kids in her neighborhood growing up, as well as children she met while working as a librarian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gibbs_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-gibbs-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hewitt_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-hewitt-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cleary's first book to center a story on the Quimby sisters, <i><a href="/wiki/Beezus_and_Ramona" title="Beezus and Ramona">Beezus and Ramona</a></i>, was published in 1955.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> A publisher asked her to write a book about a kindergarten student. Cleary resisted, because she had not attended kindergarten, but later changed her mind after the birth of her twins.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> She has written two memoirs, <i>A Girl from Yamhill</i> (1988) and <i><a href="/wiki/My_Own_Two_Feet" title="My Own Two Feet">My Own Two Feet</a></i> (1995).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> During a 2011 interview for the <i>Los Angeles Times</i>, at age 95, Cleary stated, "I've had an exceptionally happy career."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ulin_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-ulin-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beverly_Cleary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pat Pflieger, professor of children's literature at West Chester University commented: "Cleary's books have lasted because she understands her audience. She knows they're sometimes confused or frightened by the world around them, and that they feel deeply about things that adults can dismiss."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thedailybeast.com_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-thedailybeast.com-21">[20]</a></sup> Eliza Dresang, professor in Children and Youth Services at the University of Washington Information School said, "Those books don't seem so radical now, but they were when she was writing them." Dresang also said the topics covered were portrayed with honesty and accuracy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> <i>Twentieth-Century Children's Writers</i> said, "Beverly Cleary's impact as a children's writer cannot be overestimated... her extraordinary talent in creating memorable young characters whose exuberant spirit and zest for life attract young and old readers alike."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20C_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-20C-23">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:210</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beverly_Cleary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1955 Cleary gave birth to twins, Malcolm and Marianne. Cleary has lived in an assisted living residence in Carmel Valley, California since before her husband's death in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cleary celebrated her 100th birthday on April 12, 2016, an event which was noted in several well-regarded news sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honors_and_legacy">Honors and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beverly_Cleary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Honors and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1975, Cleary won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laura_Ingalls_Wilder_Award" title="Laura Ingalls Wilder Award">Laura Ingalls Wilder Award</a> from the <a href="/wiki/American_Library_Association" title="American Library Association">American Library Association</a> for "substantial and lasting contributions to children's literature".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilder_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-wilder-30">[29]</a></sup> She was U.S. nominee for the biennial, international <a href="/wiki/Hans_Christian_Andersen_Award" title="Hans Christian Andersen Award">Hans Christian Andersen Award</a> in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ibby-nominee_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-ibby-nominee-31">[30]</a></sup> In April 2000 she was named <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress_Living_Legend" title="Library of Congress Living Legend">Library of Congress Living Legend</a> in the Writers and Artists category for her contributions to the cultural heritage of the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-childrenslit_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-childrenslit-32">[31]</a></sup> She received the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Medal_of_Arts" title="National Medal of Arts">National Medal of Arts</a> in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nea2003_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-nea2003-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cleary's books have been published in over 25 different languages and have been recognized by many awards and honors. <i><a href="/wiki/Dear_Mr._Henshaw" title="Dear Mr. Henshaw">Dear Mr. Henshaw</a></i> won the <a href="/wiki/Newbery_Medal" title="Newbery Medal">Newbery Medal</a> in 1984, and; Newbery Honors were conferred on <i><a href="/wiki/Ramona_and_Her_Father" title="Ramona and Her Father">Ramona and Her Father</a></i> in 1978 and <i><a href="/wiki/Ramona_Quimby,_Age_8" title="Ramona Quimby, Age 8">Ramona Quimby, Age 8</a></i> in 1982. She won the 1981 <a href="/wiki/National_Book_Award_for_Young_People%27s_Literature" title="National Book Award for Young People's Literature">National Book Award in category Children's Fiction (paperback)</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/Ramona_and_Her_Mother" title="Ramona and Her Mother">Ramona and Her Mother</a></i>; a William Allen White Children's Book award for <i><a href="/wiki/Socks_(novel)" title="Socks (novel)">Socks</a></i> (1973); the Catholic Library Association's Regina Medal (1980); and the Children's Book Council's Every Child Award (1985).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scholastic_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-scholastic-9">[8]</a></sup> Cleary's books have been read on PBS and ABC-TV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kidsreads_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-kidsreads-34">[33]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (April 2016)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 2012, <i>Ramona the Pest</i> was ranked number 24 among all-time children's novels in a survey published by <i><a href="/wiki/School_Library_Journal" title="School Library Journal">School Library Journal</a></i>, a monthly with primarily U.S. audience. <i>The Mouse and the Motorcycle</i> (89) and <i>Ramona and Her Father</i> (94) were also among the Top 100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SLJChapter2012_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-SLJChapter2012-35">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Publisher HarperCollins recognizes her birthday, April 12, as National Drop Everything and Read Day (D.E.A.R.), in promotion of <a href="/wiki/Sustained_silent_reading" title="Sustained silent reading">sustained silent reading</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DEAR_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-DEAR-36">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland, Oregon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_District_(Portland,_Oregon)" title="Hollywood District (Portland, Oregon)">Hollywood</a> branch of the <a href="/wiki/Multnomah_County_Library" title="Multnomah County Library">Multnomah County Library</a>, near where she lived as a child, commissioned a map of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Huggins" title="Henry Huggins">Henry Huggins</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Klickitat_Street" title="Klickitat Street">Klickitat Street</a> neighborhood for its lobby wall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garden_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-garden-37">[36]</a></sup> Statues of her characters Henry Huggins, the Huggins dog Ribsy, and Ramona Quimby can be found in Portland's <a href="/wiki/Grant_Park,_Portland,_Oregon" title="Grant Park, Portland, Oregon">Grant Park</a> in the Hollywood-Fernwood neighborhood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garden_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-garden-37">[36]</a></sup> In June 2008, the neighborhood's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K-8_school" title="K-8 school">K-8 school</a>, formerly Fernwood Grammar School and once attended by Cleary, was officially renamed <a href="/wiki/Beverly_Cleary_School" title="Beverly Cleary School">Beverly Cleary School</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stern_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-stern-38">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Washington_Information_School" title="University of Washington Information School">University of Washington Information School</a> completed fund-raising for the Beverly Cleary Endowed Chair for Children and Youth Services to honor her work and commitment to <a href="/wiki/Librarian" title="Librarian">librarianship</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldsmith_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-goldsmith-13">[12]</a></sup> In 2008, the school announced that she had been selected as the next recipient of the University's Alumnus Summa Laude Dignatus Award, the highest honor the University of Washington can bestow on a graduate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cleary has a 220-student residential hall at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a> named after her, called Beverly Cleary Hall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cleary has been mentioned as a major influence by other authors, including <a href="/wiki/Laurie_Halse_Anderson" title="Laurie Halse Anderson">Laurie Halse Anderson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Judy_Blume" title="Judy Blume">Judy Blume</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lauren_Myracle" title="Lauren Myracle">Lauren Myracle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jon_Scieszka" title="Jon Scieszka">Jon Scieszka</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup></p>
