<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Noncoding DNA</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<i><a href="/wiki/Utricularia_gibba" title="Utricularia gibba">Utricularia gibba</a></i> has only 3% noncoding DNA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DesignNTrend_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-DesignNTrend-1">[1]</a></sup></div>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Genomics" title="Genomics">genomics</a> and related disciplines, <b>noncoding DNA</b> sequences are components of an organism's <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> that do not <a href="/wiki/Genetic_code" title="Genetic code">encode</a> <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> sequences. Some noncoding DNA is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcription_(genetics)" title="Transcription (genetics)">transcribed</a> into functional <a href="/wiki/Non-coding_RNA" title="Non-coding RNA">non-coding RNA</a> molecules (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Transfer_RNA" title="Transfer RNA">transfer RNA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ribosomal_RNA" title="Ribosomal RNA">ribosomal RNA</a>, and <a href="/wiki/RNA_interference" title="RNA interference">regulatory RNAs</a>). Other functions of noncoding DNA include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcription_(genetics)" title="Transcription (genetics)">transcriptional</a> and <a href="/wiki/Translation_(biology)" title="Translation (biology)">translational</a> <a href="/wiki/Regulatory_sequence" title="Regulatory sequence">regulation</a> of protein-coding sequences, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scaffold_attachment_region" title="Scaffold attachment region">scaffold attachment regions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_replication" title="Origin of replication">origins of DNA replication</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centromeres" title="Centromeres">centromeres</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telomeres" title="Telomeres">telomeres</a>.</p>
<p>The amount of noncoding DNA varies greatly among species. Where only a small percentage of the genome is responsible for coding proteins, the percentage of the genome performing regulatory functions is growing. When there is much non-coding DNA, a large proportion appears to have no biological function for the organism, as theoretically predicted in the 1960s. Since that time, this non-functional portion has often been referred to as "<b>junk DNA</b>", a term that has elicited strong responses over the years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pennisi_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-pennisi-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The international Encyclopedia of DNA Elements (<a href="/wiki/ENCODE" title="ENCODE">ENCODE</a>) project uncovered, by direct biochemical approaches, that at least 80% of human genomic DNA has biochemical activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature489p57_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nature489p57-3">[3]</a></sup> Though this was not necessarily unexpected due to previous decades of research discovering many functional noncoding regions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Costa_non-coding_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Costa_non-coding-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nessa_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nessa-5">[5]</a></sup> some scientists criticized the conclusion for conflating biochemical activity with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_function" title="Biological function">biological function</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-observer_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-observer-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eddy_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-eddy-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doolittle2013_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-doolittle2013-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PalazzoGregory2014_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-PalazzoGregory2014-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-graur_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-graur-10">[10]</a></sup> Estimates for the biologically functional fraction of our genome based on <a href="/wiki/Comparative_genomics" title="Comparative genomics">comparative genomics</a> range between 8 and 15%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kellis_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-kellis-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rands_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rands-13">[13]</a></sup> However, others have argued against relying solely on estimates from comparative genomics due to its limited scope because non-coding DNA has been found to be involved in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epigenetic" title="Epigenetic">epigenetic</a> activity and complex <a href="/wiki/Gene_regulatory_network" title="Gene regulatory network">networks of genetic interactions</a>, being explored in <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_developmental_biology" title="Evolutionary developmental biology">evolutionary developmental biology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nessa_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nessa-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kellis_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-kellis-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extent_functionality_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-extent_functionality-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morris_Epigenetics_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morris_Epigenetics-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fraction_of_noncoding_genomic_DNA">Fraction of noncoding genomic DNA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Fraction of noncoding genomic DNA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The amount of total genomic DNA varies widely between organisms, and the proportion of coding and noncoding DNA within these genomes varies greatly as well. For example, it was originally suggested that over 98% of the <a href="/wiki/Human_genome" title="Human genome">human genome</a> does not encode protein sequences, including most sequences within <a href="/wiki/Intron" title="Intron">introns</a> and most <a href="/wiki/Intergenic_region" title="Intergenic region">intergenic DNA</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elgar_.26_Vavouri_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Elgar_.26_Vavouri-16">[16]</a></sup> whilst 20% of a typical <a href="/wiki/Prokaryote" title="Prokaryote">prokaryote</a> genome is noncoding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Costa_non-coding_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Costa_non-coding-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>While overall <a href="/wiki/Genome_size" title="Genome size">genome size</a>, and by extension the amount of noncoding DNA, are correlated to organism complexity, there are many exceptions. For example, the genome of the unicellular <i><a href="/wiki/Polychaos_dubium" title="Polychaos dubium">Polychaos dubium</a></i> (formerly known as <i>Amoeba dubia</i>) has been reported to contain more than 200 times the amount of DNA in humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gregory_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gregory-17">[17]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pufferfish" title="Pufferfish">pufferfish</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Takifugu" title="Takifugu">Takifugu</a> rubripes</i> genome is only about one eighth the size of the human genome, yet seems to have a comparable number of genes; approximately 90% of the <i>Takifugu</i> genome is noncoding DNA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elgar_.26_Vavouri_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Elgar_.26_Vavouri-16">[16]</a></sup> The extensive variation in nuclear genome size among eukaryotic species is known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-value_enigma" title="C-value enigma">C-value enigma</a> or C-value paradox.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wahls_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wahls-18">[18]</a></sup> Most of the genome size difference appears to lie in the noncoding DNA.</p>
<p>In 2013, a new "record" for the most efficient eukaryotic genome was discovered with <i><a href="/wiki/Utricularia_gibba" title="Utricularia gibba">Utricularia gibba</a></i>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bladderwort" title="Bladderwort">bladderwort</a> plant that has only 3% noncoding DNA and 97% of coding DNA. Parts of the noncoding DNA were being deleted by the plant and this suggested that noncoding DNA may not be as critical for plants, even though noncoding DNA is useful for humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DesignNTrend_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-DesignNTrend-1">[1]</a></sup> Other studies on plants have discovered crucial functions in portions noncoding DNA that were previously thought to be negligible and have added a new layer to the understanding of gene regulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_noncoding_DNA_sequences">Types of noncoding DNA sequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types of noncoding DNA sequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Conserved_non-coding_sequence" title="Conserved non-coding sequence">Conserved non-coding sequence</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Noncoding_functional_RNA">Noncoding functional RNA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Noncoding functional RNA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noncoding_RNA" title="Noncoding RNA">Noncoding RNAs</a> are functional <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a> molecules that are not translated into protein. Examples of noncoding RNA include <a href="/wiki/Ribosomal_RNA" title="Ribosomal RNA">ribosomal RNA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transfer_RNA" title="Transfer RNA">transfer RNA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piwi-interacting_RNA" title="Piwi-interacting RNA">Piwi-interacting RNA</a> and <a href="/wiki/MicroRNA" title="MicroRNA">microRNA</a>.</p>
<p>MicroRNAs are predicted to control the translational activity of approximately 30% of all protein-coding genes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammals" title="Mammals">mammals</a> and may be vital components in the progression or treatment of various diseases including <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">cardiovascular disease</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a> response to <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infection</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid19030926_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid19030926-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cis-_and_trans-regulatory_elements">Cis- and trans-regulatory elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cis- and trans-regulatory elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cis-regulatory_element" title="Cis-regulatory element">Cis-regulatory elements</a> are sequences that control the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcription_(genetics)" title="Transcription (genetics)">transcription</a> of a nearby gene. Many such elements are involved in the <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_developmental_biology" title="Evolutionary developmental biology">evolution and control of development</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carroll_2008_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll_2008-21">[21]</a></sup> Cis-elements may be located in <a href="/wiki/Directionality_(molecular_biology)#5.E2.80.B2-end" title="Directionality (molecular biology)">5'</a> or <a href="/wiki/Directionality_(molecular_biology)#3.E2.80.B2-end" title="Directionality (molecular biology)">3'</a> <a href="/wiki/Untranslated_region" title="Untranslated region">untranslated regions</a> or within <a href="/wiki/Intron" title="Intron">introns</a>. <a href="/wiki/Trans-regulatory_element" title="Trans-regulatory element">Trans-regulatory elements</a> control the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcription_(genetics)" title="Transcription (genetics)">transcription</a> of a distant gene.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Promoter_(biology)" title="Promoter (biology)">Promoters</a> facilitate the transcription of a particular gene and are typically <a href="/wiki/Upstream_and_downstream_(DNA)" title="Upstream and downstream (DNA)">upstream</a> of the coding region. <a href="/wiki/Enhancer_(genetics)" title="Enhancer (genetics)">Enhancer</a> sequences may also exert very distant effects on the transcription levels of genes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Introns">Introns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Introns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Intron" title="Intron">Introns</a> are non-coding sections of a gene, transcribed into the <a href="/wiki/Precursor_mRNA" title="Precursor mRNA">precursor mRNA</a> sequence, but ultimately removed by <a href="/wiki/RNA_splicing" title="RNA splicing">RNA splicing</a> during the processing to mature <a href="/wiki/Messenger_RNA" title="Messenger RNA">messenger RNA</a>. Many introns appear to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobile_genetic_element" title="Mobile genetic element">mobile genetic elements</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intron_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-intron-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Studies of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_I_intron" title="Group I intron">group I introns</a> from <i><a href="/wiki/Tetrahymena" title="Tetrahymena">Tetrahymena</a></i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protozoans" title="Protozoans">protozoans</a> indicate that some introns appear to be selfish genetic elements, neutral to the host because they remove themselves from flanking <a href="/wiki/Exon" title="Exon">exons</a> during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RNA_processing" title="RNA processing">RNA processing</a> and do not produce an expression bias between <a href="/wiki/Allele" title="Allele">alleles</a> with and without the intron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intron_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-intron-23">[23]</a></sup> Some introns appear to have significant biological function, possibly through <a href="/wiki/Ribozyme" title="Ribozyme">ribozyme</a> functionality that may regulate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TRNA" title="TRNA">tRNA</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RRNA" title="RRNA">rRNA</a> activity as well as protein-coding gene expression, evident in hosts that have become dependent on such introns over long periods of time; for example, the <i>trnL-intron</i> is found in all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_plant" title="Green plant">green plants</a> and appears to have been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertical_gene_transfer" title="Vertical gene transfer">vertically inherited</a> for several billions of years, including more than a billion years within <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplasts</a> and an additional 2–3 billion years prior in the <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacterial</a> ancestors of chloroplasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intron_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-intron-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pseudogenes">Pseudogenes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pseudogenes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pseudogene" title="Pseudogene">Pseudogenes</a> are DNA sequences, related to known <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a>, that have lost their protein-coding ability or are otherwise no longer <a href="/wiki/Gene_expression" title="Gene expression">expressed</a> in the cell. Pseudogenes arise from retrotransposition or genomic duplication of functional genes, and become "genomic fossils" that are nonfunctional due to <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">mutations</a> that prevent the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcription_(genetics)" title="Transcription (genetics)">transcription</a> of the gene, such as within the gene promoter region, or fatally alter the <a href="/wiki/Translation_(biology)" title="Translation (biology)">translation</a> of the gene, such as premature <a href="/wiki/Stop_codon" title="Stop codon">stop codons</a> or <a href="/wiki/Translational_frameshift" title="Translational frameshift">frameshifts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17568002_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17568002-24">[24]</a></sup> Pseudogenes resulting from the retrotransposition of an RNA intermediate are known as processed pseudogenes; pseudogenes that arise from the genomic remains of <a href="/wiki/Gene_duplication" title="Gene duplication">duplicated genes</a> or residues of inactivated genes are nonprocessed pseudogenes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17568002_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17568002-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>While <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dollo%27s_Law" title="Dollo's Law">Dollo's Law</a> suggests that the loss of function in pseudogenes is likely permanent, silenced genes may actually retain function for several million years and can be "reactivated" into protein-coding sequences<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and a substantial number of pseudogenes are actively transcribed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17568002_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17568002-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Because pseudogenes are presumed to change without evolutionary constraint, they can serve as a useful model of the type and frequencies of various spontaneous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genetic_mutation" title="Genetic mutation">genetic mutations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Petrov_.26_Hartl_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Petrov_.26_Hartl-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Repeat_sequences.2C_transposons_and_viral_elements">Repeat sequences, transposons and viral elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Repeat sequences, transposons and viral elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transposon" title="Transposon">Transposons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Retrotransposon" title="Retrotransposon">retrotransposons</a> are <a href="/wiki/Mobile_genetic_elements" title="Mobile genetic elements">mobile genetic elements</a>. Retrotransposon <a href="/wiki/Repeated_sequence_(DNA)" title="Repeated sequence (DNA)">repeated sequences</a>, which include <a href="/wiki/Retrotransposon#LINEs" title="Retrotransposon">long interspersed nuclear elements</a> (LINEs) and <a href="/wiki/Retrotransposon#SINEs" title="Retrotransposon">short interspersed nuclear elements</a> (SINEs), account for a large proportion of the genomic sequences in many species. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alu_sequence" title="Alu sequence">Alu sequences</a>, classified as a short interspersed nuclear element, are the most abundant mobile elements in the human genome. Some examples have been found of SINEs exerting transcriptional control of some protein-encoding genes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Endogenous_retrovirus" title="Endogenous retrovirus">Endogenous retrovirus</a> sequences are the product of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reverse_transcription" title="Reverse transcription">reverse transcription</a> of <a href="/wiki/Retrovirus" title="Retrovirus">retrovirus</a> genomes into the genomes of <a href="/wiki/Germ_cell" title="Germ cell">germ cells</a>. Mutation within these retro-transcribed sequences can inactivate the viral genome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Over 8% of the human genome is made up of (mostly decayed) endogenous retrovirus sequences, as part of the over 42% fraction that is recognizably derived of retrotransposons, while another 3% can be identified to be the remains of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transposon#Class_II:_DNA_transposons" title="Transposon">DNA transposons</a>. Much of the remaining half of the genome that is currently without an explained origin is expected to have found its origin in transposable elements that were active so long ago (&gt; 200 million years) that random mutations have rendered them unrecognizable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-humangenome_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-humangenome-32">[32]</a></sup> Genome size variation in at least two kinds of plants is mostly the result of retrotransposon sequences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Telomeres">Telomeres</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Telomeres">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Telomere" title="Telomere">Telomeres</a> are regions of repetitive DNA at the end of a <a href="/wiki/Chromosome" title="Chromosome">chromosome</a>, which provide protection from chromosomal deterioration during <a href="/wiki/DNA_replication" title="DNA replication">DNA replication</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Junk_DNA">Junk DNA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Junk DNA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "junk DNA" became popular in the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EhretdeHaller1963_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-EhretdeHaller1963-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/T._Ryan_Gregory" title="T. Ryan Gregory">T. Ryan Gregory</a>, a genomic biologist, David Comings was the first to discuss the nature of junk DNA explicitly in 1972, and he applied the term to all noncoding DNA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gregory_Evolution_Genome_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gregory_Evolution_Genome-37">[37]</a></sup> The term was formalized in 1972 by <a href="/wiki/Susumu_Ohno" title="Susumu Ohno">Susumu Ohno</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ohno_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ohno-38">[38]</a></sup> who noted that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutational_load" title="Mutational load">mutational load</a> from deleterious mutations placed an upper limit on the number of functional loci that could be expected given a typical mutation rate. Ohno hypothesized that mammal genomes could not have more than 30,000 loci under selection before the "cost" from the mutational load would cause an inescapable decline in fitness, and eventually extinction. This prediction remains robust, with the human genome containing approximately 20,000 genes. Another source for Ohno's theory was the observation that even closely related species can have widely (orders-of-magnitude) different genome sizes, which had been dubbed the <a href="/wiki/C-value" title="C-value">C-value paradox</a> in 1971.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eddy_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-eddy-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though the fruitfulness of the term "junk DNA" has been questioned on the grounds that it provokes a strong a priori assumption of total non-functionality and though some have recommended using more neutral terminology such as "noncoding DNA" instead;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gregory_Evolution_Genome_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gregory_Evolution_Genome-37">[37]</a></sup> "junk DNA" remains a label for the portions of a <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a> sequence for which no discernible <a href="/wiki/Function_(biology)" title="Function (biology)">function</a> has been identified and that through <a href="/wiki/Comparative_genomics" title="Comparative genomics">comparative genomics</a> analysis appear under no <a href="/wiki/Conserved_sequence" title="Conserved sequence">functional constraint</a> suggesting that the sequence itself has provided no <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adaptive advantage</a>. Since the late 70s it has become apparent that the majority of non-coding DNA in large genomes finds its origin in the <a href="/wiki/Selfish_DNA" title="Selfish DNA">selfish</a> amplification of <a href="/wiki/Transposable_element" title="Transposable element">transposable elements</a>, of which <a href="/wiki/Ford_Doolittle" title="Ford Doolittle">W. Ford Doolittle</a> and Carmen Sapienza in 1980 wrote in the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i>: "When a given DNA, or class of DNAs, of unproven phenotypic function can be shown to have evolved a strategy (such as transposition) which ensures its genomic survival, then no other explanation for its existence is necessary."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doolittle1980_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Doolittle1980-39">[39]</a></sup> The amount of junk DNA can be expected to depend on the rate of amplification of these elements and the rate at which non-functional DNA is lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In the same issue of <i>Nature</i>, <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Orgel" title="Leslie Orgel">Leslie Orgel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Crick" title="Francis Crick">Francis Crick</a> wrote that junk DNA has "little specificity and conveys little or no selective advantage to the organism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The term occurs mainly in <a href="/wiki/Popular_science" title="Popular science">popular science</a> and in a <a href="/wiki/Colloquialism" title="Colloquialism">colloquial</a> way in scientific publications, and it has been suggested that its connotations may have delayed interest in the biological functions of noncoding DNA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SA_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-SA-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several lines of evidence indicate that some "junk DNA" sequences are likely to have unidentified functional activity and that the process of <a href="/wiki/Exaptation" title="Exaptation">exaptation</a> of fragments of originally selfish or non-functional DNA has been commonplace throughout evolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In 2012, the <a href="/wiki/ENCODE" title="ENCODE">ENCODE</a> project, a research program supported by the <a href="/wiki/National_Human_Genome_Research_Institute" title="National Human Genome Research Institute">National Human Genome Research Institute</a>, reported that 76% of the human genome's noncoding DNA sequences were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcription_(genetics)" title="Transcription (genetics)">transcribed</a> and that nearly half of the genome was in some way accessible to genetic regulatory proteins such as <a href="/wiki/Transcription_factor" title="Transcription factor">transcription factors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pennisi_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-pennisi-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, the suggestion by ENCODE that over 80% of the human genome is biochemically functional has been criticized by other scientists,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-observer_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-observer-6">[6]</a></sup> who argue that neither accessibility of segments of the genome to transcription factors nor their transcription guarantees that those segments have biochemical function and that their transcription is <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">selectively advantageous</a>. Furthermore, the much lower estimates of functionality prior to ENCODE were based on genomic conservation estimates across mammalian lineages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eddy_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-eddy-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doolittle2013_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-doolittle2013-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PalazzoGregory2014_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-PalazzoGregory2014-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-graur_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-graur-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In response to such views, other scientists argue that the wide spread transcription and splicing that is observed in the human genome directly by biochemical testing is a more accurate indicator of genetic function than genomic conservation because conservation estimates are relative due to incredible variations in genome sizes of even closely related species, it is partially tautological, and these estimates are not based on direct testing for functionality on the genome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kellis_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-kellis-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extent_functionality_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-extent_functionality-14">[14]</a></sup> Conservation estimates may be used to provide clues to identify possible functional elements in the genome, but it does not limit or cap the total amount of functional elements that could possibly exist in the genome since elements that do things at the molecular level can be missed by comparative genomics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kellis_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-kellis-12">[12]</a></sup> Furthermore, much of the apparent junk DNA is involved in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epigenetic" title="Epigenetic">epigenetic</a> regulation and appears to be necessary for the development of complex organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nessa_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nessa-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extent_functionality_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-extent_functionality-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morris_Epigenetics_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morris_Epigenetics-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a 2014 paper, ENCODE researchers tried to address "the question of whether nonconserved but biochemically active regions are truly functional". They noted that in the literature, functional parts of the genome have been identified differently in previous studies depending on the approaches used. There have been three general approaches used to identify functional parts of the human genome: genetic approaches (which rely on changes in phenotype), evolutionary approaches (which rely on conservation) and biochemical approaches (which rely on biochemical testing and was used by ENCODE). All three have limitations: genetic approaches may miss functional elements that do not manifest physically on the organism, evolutionary approaches have difficulties using accurate multispecies sequence alignments since genomes of even closely related species vary considerably, and with biochemical approaches, though having high reproducibility, the biochemical signatures do not always automatically signify a function.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kellis_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-kellis-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>They noted that 70% of the transcription coverage was less than 1 transcript per cell. They noted that this "larger proportion of genome with reproducible but low biochemical signal strength and less evolutionary conservation is challenging to parse between specific functions and biological noise". Furthermore, assay resolution often is much broader than the underlying functional sites so some of the reproducibly “biochemically active but selectively neutral” sequences are unlikely to serve critical functions, especially those with lower-level biochemical signal. To this they added, "However, we also acknowledge substantial limitations in our current detection of constraint, given that some human-specific functions are essential but not conserved and that disease-relevant regions need not be selectively constrained to be functional." On the other hand, they argued that the 12–15% fraction of human DNA under functional constraint, as estimated by a variety of extrapolative evolutionary methods, may still be an underestimate. They concluded that in contrast to evolutionary and genetic evidence, biochemical data offer clues about both the molecular function served by underlying DNA elements and the cell types in which they act. Ultimately genetic, evolutionary, and biochemical approaches can all be used in a complementary way to identify regions that may be functional in human biology and disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kellis_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-kellis-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some critics have argued that functionality can only be assessed in reference to an appropriate <a href="/wiki/Null_hypothesis" title="Null hypothesis">null hypothesis</a>. In this case, the null hypothesis would be that these parts of the genome are non-functional and have properties, be it on the basis of conservation or biochemical activity, that would be expected of such regions based on our general understanding of <a href="/wiki/Molecular_evolution" title="Molecular evolution">molecular evolution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a>. According to these critics, until a region in question has been shown to have additional features, beyond what is expected of the null hypothesis, it should provisionally be labelled as non-functional.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PalazzoLee2015_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-PalazzoLee2015-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Functions_of_noncoding_DNA">Functions of noncoding DNA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Functions of noncoding DNA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many noncoding DNA sequences have important biological functions as indicated by <a href="/wiki/Comparative_genomics" title="Comparative genomics">comparative genomics</a> studies that report some regions of noncoding DNA that are highly <a href="/wiki/Conserved_sequence" title="Conserved sequence">conserved</a>, sometimes on time-scales representing hundreds of millions of years, implying that these noncoding regions are under strong <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolutionary</a> pressure and <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">positive selection</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ludwig_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ludwig-45">[45]</a></sup> For example, in the genomes of <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mice" title="Mice">mice</a>, which diverged from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_ancestor" title="Common ancestor">common ancestor</a> 65–75 million years ago, protein-coding DNA sequences account for only about 20% of conserved DNA, with the remaining 80% of conserved DNA represented in noncoding regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cobb_et_al_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cobb_et_al-46">[46]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linkage_mapping" title="Linkage mapping">Linkage mapping</a> often identifies chromosomal regions associated with a disease with no evidence of functional coding variants of genes within the region, suggesting that disease-causing genetic variants lie in the noncoding DNA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cobb_et_al_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cobb_et_al-46">[46]</a></sup> The significance of noncoding DNA mutations in cancer was explored in April 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Khurana_et_al_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Khurana_et_al-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Noncoding genetic polymorphisms have also been shown to play a role in infectious disease susceptibility, such as hepatitis C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Moreover, noncoding genetic polymorphisms were shown to contribute to susceptibility to Ewing sarcoma - a highly aggressive pediatric bone cancer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some specific sequences of noncoding DNA may be features essential to chromosome structure, <a href="/wiki/Centromere" title="Centromere">centromere</a> function and <a href="/wiki/Homology_(biology)" title="Homology (biology)">homolog</a> recognition in <a href="/wiki/Meiosis" title="Meiosis">meiosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to a comparative study of over 300 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prokaryotic" title="Prokaryotic">prokaryotic</a> and over 30 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eukaryotic" title="Eukaryotic">eukaryotic</a> genomes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> eukaryotes appear to require a minimum amount of non-coding DNA. This minimum amount can be predicted using a growth model for regulatory genetic networks, implying that it is required for regulatory purposes. In humans the predicted minimum is about 5% of the total genome.</p>
<p>There is evidence that a significant proportion (over 10%) of 32 mammalian genomes may function through the formation of specific <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid_secondary_structure" title="Nucleic acid secondary structure">RNA secondary structures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The study used <a href="/wiki/Comparative_genomics" title="Comparative genomics">comparative genomics</a> to identify compensatory DNA mutations that maintain RNA base-pairings, a distinctive feature of <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a> molecules. Over 80% of the genomic regions presenting evolutionary evidence of RNA structure conservation do not present strong DNA sequence conservation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protection_of_the_genome">Protection of the genome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Protection of the genome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">Mutation</a></div>
<p>Noncoding DNA separate genes from each other with long gaps, so mutation in one gene or part of a chromosome, for example deletion or insertion, does not have the frame shift effect (as in "<a href="/wiki/Frameshift_mutation" title="Frameshift mutation">frameshift mutation</a>") on the whole chromosome. When genome complexity is relatively high, like in the case of human genome, not only between different genes, but also inside many genes, there are gaps of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Introns" title="Introns">introns</a> to protect the entire coding segment and minimise the changes caused by mutation.</p>
<p>It has been suggested that non-coding DNA may serve to decrease the chance of gene disruption during <a href="/wiki/Chromosomal_crossover" title="Chromosomal crossover">chromosomal crossover</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetic_switches">Genetic switches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Genetic switches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some noncoding DNA sequences are genetic "switches" that regulate when and where genes are expressed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> For example, a long non-coding RNA (lncRNA) molecule was recently found to assist in preventing breast cancer, by stopping a genetic switch from getting stuck.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation_of_gene_expression">Regulation of gene expression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Regulation of gene expression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Regulation_of_gene_expression" title="Regulation of gene expression">Regulation of gene expression</a></div>
<p>Some noncoding DNA sequences determine the expression levels of various genes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Callaway_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Callaway-56">[56]</a></sup> In addition, some noncoding variants have been associated to variation in expression levels of mRNAs through mapping of <a href="/wiki/Expression_quantitative_trait_loci" title="Expression quantitative trait loci">Expression quantitative trait loci</a> (eQTLs). When eQTLs are combined with <a href="/wiki/Genome-wide_association_study" title="Genome-wide association study">Genome-wide association study</a> (GWAS), some independent risk variants for the same disease impacted on expression of the same mRNAs, or on mRNAs with similar functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transcription_factor_sites">Transcription factor sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Transcription factor sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Transcription_factor" title="Transcription factor">Transcription factor</a></div>
<p>Some noncoding DNA sequences determine where transcription factors attach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Callaway_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-Callaway-56">[56]</a></sup> A transcription factor is a protein that binds to specific non-coding DNA sequences, thereby controlling the flow (or transcription) of genetic information from DNA to mRNA. Transcription factors act at very different locations on the genomes of different people.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Operators">Operators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Operators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Operator_(biology)" title="Operator (biology)">Operator (biology)</a></div>
<p>An operator is a segment of DNA to which a <a href="/wiki/Repressor" title="Repressor">repressor</a> binds. A repressor is a DNA-binding protein that regulates the expression of one or more genes by binding to the operator and blocking the attachment of <a href="/wiki/RNA_polymerase" title="RNA polymerase">RNA polymerase</a> to the promoter, thus preventing transcription of the genes. This blocking of expression is called repression.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Enhancers">Enhancers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Enhancers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Enhancer_(genetics)" title="Enhancer (genetics)">Enhancer (genetics)</a></div>
<p>An enhancer is a short region of DNA that can be bound with proteins (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trans-acting_factors" title="Trans-acting factors">trans-acting factors</a>), much like a set of transcription factors, to enhance transcription levels of genes in a gene cluster.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Silencers">Silencers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Silencers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silencer_(DNA)" title="Silencer (DNA)">Silencer (DNA)</a></div>
<p>A silencer is a region of DNA that inactivates gene expression when bound by a regulatory protein. It functions in a very similar way as enhancers, only differing in the inactivation of genes.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Promoters">Promoters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Promoters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Promoter_(biology)" title="Promoter (biology)">Promoter (biology)</a></div>
<p>A promoter is a region of DNA that facilitates transcription of a particular gene. Promoters are typically located near the genes they regulate.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Insulators">Insulators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Insulators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Insulator_(genetics)" title="Insulator (genetics)">Insulator (genetics)</a></div>
<p>A genetic insulator is a boundary element that plays two distinct roles in gene expression, either as an enhancer-blocking code, or rarely as a barrier against condensed chromatin. An insulator in a DNA sequence is comparable to a linguistic <a href="/wiki/Word_divider" title="Word divider">word divider</a> such as a comma (,) in a sentence, because the insulator indicates where an enhanced or repressed sequence ends.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses_of_noncoding_DNA">Uses of noncoding DNA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Uses of noncoding DNA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Noncoding_DNA_and_evolution">Noncoding DNA and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Noncoding DNA and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shared sequences of apparently non-functional DNA are a major line of <a href="/wiki/Evidence_of_common_descent" title="Evidence of common descent">evidence of common descent</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TO-FAQ_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-TO-FAQ-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pseudogene sequences appear to accumulate mutations more rapidly than coding sequences due to a loss of selective pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Petrov_.26_Hartl_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Petrov_.26_Hartl-27">[27]</a></sup> This allows for the creation of mutant alleles that incorporate new functions that may be favored by natural selection; thus, pseudogenes can serve as raw material for <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> and can be considered "protogenes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Long_range_correlations">Long range correlations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Long range correlations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A statistical distinction between coding and noncoding DNA sequences has been found. It has been observed that nucleotides in non-coding DNA sequences display long range power law correlations while coding sequences do not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forensic_anthropology">Forensic anthropology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noncoding_DNA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Forensic anthropology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Police sometimes gather DNA as evidence for purposes of <a href="/wiki/Forensic_identification" title="Forensic identification">forensic identification</a>. As described in <i><a href="/wiki/Maryland_v._King" title="Maryland v. King">Maryland v. King</a></i>, a 2013 U.S. Supreme Court decision:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ussc_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-ussc-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>"The current standard for forensic DNA testing relies on an analysis of the chromosomes located within the nucleus of all human cells. “The DNA material in chromosomes is composed of ‘coding’ and ‘noncoding’ regions. The coding regions are known as genes and contain the information necessary for a cell to make proteins. . . . Non-protein coding regions . . . are not related directly to making proteins, [and] have been referred to as ‘junk’ DNA.” The adjective “junk” may mislead the lay person, for in fact this is the DNA region used with near certainty to identify a person.</p>
</blockquote>
