<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">State of emergency</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a> or division of government (i.e. on a municipal, provincial/state level) may declare that their area is in a <b>state of <a href="/wiki/Emergency" title="Emergency">emergency</a></b>. This means that the government can suspend and/or change some functions of the executive, the legislative and/or the judiciary during this period of time. It alerts citizens to change their normal behavior and orders government agencies to implement emergency plans. A government can declare a state of emergency during a time of natural or human-made disaster, during a period of civil unrest, or following a <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_war" title="Declaration of war">declaration of war</a> or situation of international/internal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armed_conflict" title="Armed conflict">armed conflict</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Justitium" title="Justitium">Justitium</a></i> is its equivalent in Roman law, where Senate could put forward <a href="/wiki/Senatus_consultum_ultimum" title="Senatus consultum ultimum">senatus consultum ultimum</a>.</p>
<p>It can also be used as a rationale (or pretext) for suspending rights and freedoms guaranteed under a country's <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitution</a> or <a href="/wiki/Basic_law" title="Basic law">basic law</a>. The procedure for and legality of doing so varies by country.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_international_law">Relationship with international law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Relationship with international law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Under <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a>, rights and freedoms may be suspended during a state of emergency; for example, a government can detain persons and hold them without trial. All rights that can be derogated from are listed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICCPR" title="ICCPR">International Covenant for Civil and Political Rights</a>. Non-derogable rights cannot be suspended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Non-derogable rights are listed in Article 4 of the ICCPR, they include the rights to freedom from arbitrary deprivation of liberty and to freedom from torture and/or ill-treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some countries have made it illegal to modify emergency law or the constitution during the emergency; other countries have the freedom to change any legislation or rights based constitutional frameworks at any time that the legislative chooses to do so. Constitutions are contracts between the government and the private individuals of that country. The International Covenant for Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR) is an international law document signed by states. Therefore, the Covenant only applies to persons acting in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Official_capacity" title="Official capacity">official capacity</a>, not private individuals. However, signatories to the Covenant are expected to integrate it into national legislation. The state of emergency (within the ICCPR framework) must be publicly declared and the Secretary-General of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> must be contacted immediately, to declare the reason for the emergency, the date on which the emergency is to start, the derogations that may take place, with the timeframe of the emergency and the date in which the emergency is expected to finish. Although this is common protocol stipulated by the ICCPR often this is not strictly followed; enforcement is better regulated by European Convention of human rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_and_viewpoints">Use and viewpoints</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use and viewpoints">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though fairly uncommon in democracies, <a href="/wiki/Dictatorship" title="Dictatorship">dictatorial</a> regimes often declare a state of emergency that is prolonged indefinitely for the life of the regime, or for extended periods of time so that derogations can be used to override human rights of their citizens usually protected by the International Covenant on Civil and political rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In some situations, <a href="/wiki/Martial_law" title="Martial law">martial law</a> is also declared, allowing the <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> greater authority to act. In other situations, emergency is not declared and de facto measures taken or decree-law adopted by the government. Ms. <a href="/wiki/Nicole_Questiaux" title="Nicole Questiaux">Nicole Questiaux</a> (France) and Mr. Leandro Despouy (Argentina), two consecutive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Special_Rapporteurs" title="United Nations Special Rapporteurs">United Nations Special Rapporteurs</a> have recommended to the international community to adopt the following "principles" to be observed during a state or de facto situation of emergency : Principles of Legality, Proclamation, Notification, Time Limitation, Exceptional Threat, Proportionality, Non-Discrimination, Compatibility, Concordance and Complementarity of the Various Norms of International Law (cf. "Question of Human Rights and State of Emergency", E/CN.4/Sub.2/1997/19, at Chapter II; see also <i><a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%A9tat_d%27exception" title="fr:état d'exception">état d'exception</a></i>).</p>
<p>Article 4 to the <a href="/wiki/International_Covenant_on_Civil_and_Political_Rights" title="International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights">International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights</a> (ICCPR), permits states to <a href="/wiki/Derogation" title="Derogation">derogate</a> from certain rights guaranteed by the ICCPR in "time of public emergency". Any measures derogating from obligations under the Covenant, however, must only be to the extent required by the exigencies of the situation, and must be announced by the State Party to the <a href="/wiki/Secretary-General_of_the_United_Nations" title="Secretary-General of the United Nations">Secretary-General of the United Nations</a>. The <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/American_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="American Convention on Human Rights">American Convention on Human Rights</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> have similar derogatory provisions. No derogation is permitted to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ILO_Conventions" title="ILO Conventions">International Labour Conventions</a>.</p>
<p>Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_theory" title="Political theory">political theorists</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Carl_Schmitt" title="Carl Schmitt">Carl Schmitt</a>, have argued that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_(philosophy)" title="Power (philosophy)">power</a> to decide the initiation of the state of emergency defines <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereignty</a> itself. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_of_Exception_(2005)" title="State of Exception (2005)"><i>State of Exception</i> (2005)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Agamben" title="Giorgio Agamben">Giorgio Agamben</a> criticized this idea, arguing that the mechanism of the state of emergency deprives certain people of their <a href="/wiki/Civil_and_political_rights" title="Civil and political rights">civil and political rights</a>, producing his interpretation of <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_sacer" title="Homo sacer">homo sacer</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Abuse">Abuse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Abuse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The state of emergency can, and often has been, <a href="/wiki/Abuse" title="Abuse">abused</a> by being invoked. An example would be to allow a state to suppress internal opposition without having to respect human rights. An example was the <a href="/wiki/1991_Soviet_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat_attempt" title="1991 Soviet coup d'état attempt">August 1991 attempted coup</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> (USSR) where the coup leaders invoked a state of emergency; the failure of the coup led to the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Dissolution of the Soviet Union">dissolution of the Soviet Union</a>.</p>
<p>Derogations by states having ratified or acceded to binding international agreements such as the ICCPR, the American and European Conventions on Human Rights and the International Labour Conventions are monitored by independent expert committees, regional Courts and other State Parties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Law_in_selected_countries">Law in selected countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Law in selected countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Argentina">Argentina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Argentina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Constitution, which has been amended several times, has always allowed for a state of emergency (literally <i>estado de sitio</i>, <i>state of siege</i>), to be declared if the constitution or the authorities it creates are endangered by internal unrest or foreign attack. This provision was much abused during dictatorships, with long-lasting states of siege giving the government a free hand to suppress opposition (as of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> state of emergencies had been declared 52 times by democratic and dictatorial governments, starting in 1854 shortly after the constitution came into force<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>). The <a href="/wiki/American_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="American Convention on Human Rights">American Convention on Human Rights</a> (Pacto de San José de Costa Rica), adopted in 1969 but only ratified by Argentina in 1984 immediately after the end of the <a href="/wiki/National_Reorganization_Process" title="National Reorganization Process">National Reorganization Process</a>, restricts abuse of the state of emergency by requiring any signatory nation declaring such a state to inform the other signatories of its circumstances and duration, and what rights are affected.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>State-of-emergency legislation differs in each state of Australia.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>, the premier can declare a state of emergency if there is a threat to employment, safety or public order.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The declaration expires after 30 days, and a resolution of either the upper or lower House of Parliament may revoke it earlier. Under the Public Safety Preservation Act, a declared state of emergency allows the premier to immediately make any desired regulations to secure public order and safety. However, these regulations expire if Parliament does not agree to continue them within 7 days. Also, under the Essential Services Act, the premier (or delegate) may operate or prohibit operation of, as desired, any essential service (e.g., transport, fuel, power, water, gas).</p>
<p>In regards to Emergency Management, regions (usually on a <a href="/wiki/Local_government_area" title="Local government area">local government area</a> basis) that have been affected by a <a href="/wiki/Natural_disaster" title="Natural disaster">natural disaster</a> are the responsibility of the state, until that state declares a State of Emergency where access to the Federal Emergency Fund becomes available to help respond to and recover from natural disasters. A State of Emergency does not apply to the whole state, but rather <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Districts" title="Districts">districts</a> or <a href="/wiki/Shire" title="Shire">shires</a>, where essential services may have been disrupted.</p>
<p>See also, <a href="/wiki/Exceptional_circumstances" title="Exceptional circumstances">Exceptional circumstances</a>; a term most commonly used in Australia with regard to emergency relief payments.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Extreme act that, in Brazil (<i>Estado de Sítio</i> or <i>Estado de Exceção</i>, in Portuguese), can be declared on the following circumstances:</p>
<ul>
<li>Serious disturbance with national impact;</li>
<li>Inefficiency on the Defense State previously decreed;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_war" title="Declaration of war">Declaration of war</a>;</li>
<li>Response to foreign armed aggression.</li>
</ul>
<p>The state of emergency could last for 30 days, being possible to extend it for more days in case of persistence of the reasons of exceptionality.</p>
<p>Only the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Brazil" title="President of Brazil">President</a> is able to declare or prorogate this State; after receiving formal authorization from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congresso_Nacional" title="Congresso Nacional">National Congress</a> and after consultation with the National Security Council or the Council of the Republic.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Canada" title="Politics of Canada">federal government of Canada</a> can use the <a href="/wiki/Emergencies_Act" title="Emergencies Act">Emergencies Act</a> to invoke a state of emergency. A national state of emergency automatically expires after 90 days, unless extended by the Governor-in-Council.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> There are different levels of emergencies: Public Welfare Emergency, Public Order Emergency, International Emergency, and War Emergency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Emergencies Act replaced the <a href="/wiki/War_Measures_Act" title="War Measures Act">War Measures Act</a> in 1988. The War Measures Act was invoked three times in Canadian history, most controversially during the 1970 <a href="/wiki/October_Crisis" title="October Crisis">October Crisis</a>, and also during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> (from 1914 to 1920, against threat of Communism) and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> (from 1942 to 1945, against perceived threat from Japanese Canadians following <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">Imperial Japan's</a> <a href="/wiki/Attack_on_Pearl_Harbor" title="Attack on Pearl Harbor">attack on Pearl Harbor</a>).</p>
<p>Under the current Emergency Act a state of emergency can also be declared by provincial, territorial, and municipal governments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In addition Canada's federal government and any of its provincial governments can suspend, for five years at a time, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charter_of_Rights_and_Freedoms" title="Charter of Rights and Freedoms">Charter</a> rights to fundamental freedoms in section 2, to legal rights in sections 7 through 14, and to equality rights in section 15 by legislation which invokes the notwithstanding clause, section 33, and therefore emergency powers can effectively be created even without using the Emergency Act.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Denmark">Denmark</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Denmark">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The police chief in a district can impose a zone in which people can be <a href="/wiki/Strip_search" title="Strip search">body searched</a> without a specific suspicion. Such an order must be issued in writing, published, and imposed for a limited period. The police law (article 6) regulates this area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The normal procedure calls for assisting the suspect to a private area and stripping them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>If the police feel that a situation involving a crowd of people can get out of hand, they can order the assembly to be dissolved and "pass the street" in the name of the king. People that after three such warnings are still part of the crowd can then without further warning be subjugated to <a href="/wiki/Mass_arrest" title="Mass arrest">mass arrest</a>. All people arrested can then be detained for 24 hours without charging them or taking them for a judge. This is called a <a href="/wiki/Preventive_detention" title="Preventive detention">precluding arrest</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Egypt">Egypt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Egypt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Emergency_law_in_Egypt" title="Emergency law in Egypt">Emergency law in Egypt</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egyptians</a> lived under an Emergency Law (Law No. 162 of 1958)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Egypt_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Egypt-15">[15]</a></sup> from 1967 to 2012, except for an 18-month break in 1980 and 1981. The emergency was imposed during the <a href="/wiki/Six-Day_War" title="Six-Day War">1967 Arab-Israeli War</a>, and reimposed following the <a href="/wiki/Assassination" title="Assassination">assassination</a> of President <a href="/wiki/Anwar_Sadat" title="Anwar Sadat">Anwar Sadat</a>. The law continuously extended every three years since 1981. Under the law, police powers were extended, constitutional rights suspended and <a href="/wiki/Censorship" title="Censorship">censorship</a> was legalized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shehata_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shehata-16">[16]</a></sup> The law sharply circumscribed any non-governmental political activity: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demonstration_(people)" title="Demonstration (people)">street demonstrations</a>, non-approved political organizations, and unregistered financial donations were formally banned. Some 17,000 people were detained under the law, and estimates of <a href="/wiki/Political_prisoner" title="Political prisoner">political prisoners</a> run as high as 30,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Al-Ahram_Weekly_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Al-Ahram_Weekly-17">[17]</a></sup> The emergency rule expired on May 31, 2012, and was put back in place in January 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-19">[19]</a></sup> Egypt declared a month-long national emergency on 14 August 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Egyptian presidency announced a one-month state of emergency across the country on August 14, 2013 and ordered the armed forces to help the Interior Ministry enforce security. The announcement made on state TV followed deadly countrywide clashes between supporters of deposed President Mohammed Morsi and the security forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/States_of_emergency_in_France" title="States of emergency in France">States of emergency in France</a></div>
<p>Three main provisions concern various kind of "state of emergency" in France: Article 16 of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_France" title="Constitution of France">Constitution of 1958</a> allows, in time of crisis, "extraordinary powers" to the president. Article 36 of the same constitution regulates "state of siege" (<i><a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89tat_de_si%C3%A8ge_(France)" title="fr:État de siège (France)">état de siège</a></i>). Finally, the Act of 3 April 1955 allows the proclamation, by the Council of Ministers, of the "state of emergency" (<i><a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89tat_d%27urgence_en_France" title="fr:État d'urgence en France">état d'urgence</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The distinction between article 16 and the 1955 Act concerns mainly the distribution of powers: whereas in article 16, the executive power basically suspend the regular procedures of the Republic, the 1955 Act permits a twelve-day state of emergency, after which a new law extending the emergency must be voted by the Parliament. These dispositions have been used at various times, in 1955, 1958, 1961, 1988, 2005, and 2015.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">Weimar Republic</a> constitution allowed states of emergency under <a href="/wiki/Article_48_(Weimar_Constitution)" title="Article 48 (Weimar Constitution)">Article 48</a> to deal with rebellions. Article 48 was often invoked during the 14-year life of the Republic, sometimes for no reason other than to allow the government to act when it was unable to obtain a <a href="/wiki/Parliament" title="Parliament">parliamentary</a> majority.</p>
<p>After the February 27, 1933 <a href="/wiki/Reichstag_fire" title="Reichstag fire">Reichstag fire</a>, an attack blamed on the <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> declared a state of emergency using Article 48, and then had President <a href="/wiki/Paul_von_Hindenburg" title="Paul von Hindenburg">von Hindenburg</a> sign the <a href="/wiki/Reichstag_Fire_Decree" title="Reichstag Fire Decree">Reichstag Fire Decree</a>, which suspended the <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Constitution" title="Weimar Constitution">Weimar Constitution</a> for the whole duration of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Reich" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a>: the Weimar Constitution was never actually repealed by <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>, but "indefinitely suspended". After the prohibition of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Germany" title="Communist Party of Germany">Communist Party of Germany</a> on March 1, 1933,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a> was free to vote in the March 23, <a href="/wiki/Enabling_Act_of_1933" title="Enabling Act of 1933">1933 Enabling Act</a>, which enabled Chancellor Adolf Hitler and his cabinet to enact laws without the participation of the Reichstag. These two laws implemented the <i><a href="/wiki/Gleichschaltung" title="Gleichschaltung">Gleichschaltung</a></i>, the Nazis' institution of <a href="/wiki/Totalitarianism" title="Totalitarianism">totalitarianism</a>.</p>
<p>In the postwar <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Federal Republic of Germany</a> the <i><a href="/wiki/German_Emergency_Acts" title="German Emergency Acts">Emergency Acts</a></i> state that some of the basic constitutional rights of the <a href="/wiki/Basic_Law_for_the_Federal_Republic_of_Germany" title="Basic Law for the Federal Republic of Germany">Basic Law</a> may be limited in case of a <a href="/wiki/State_of_Defence_(Germany)" title="State of Defence (Germany)">state of defence</a>, a state of tension, or an internal state of emergency or disaster (catastrophe). These amendments to the constitution were passed on May 30, 1968 despite fierce opposition by the so-called <i>extra-parliamentary opposition</i> (see <a href="/wiki/German_student_movement#Emergency_Acts" title="German student movement">German student movement</a> for details).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hong_Kong">Hong Kong</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Hong Kong">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Standing_Committee_of_the_National_People%27s_Congress" title="Standing Committee of the National People's Congress">Standing Committee of the National People's Congress</a> can declare a state of emergency and deploy troops from the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army_Hong_Kong_Garrison" title="People's Liberation Army Hong Kong Garrison">People's Liberation Army Hong Kong Garrison</a> under the Law of the People's Republic of China on the garrisoning of the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chief_Executive_of_Hong_Kong" title="Chief Executive of Hong Kong">Chief Executive of Hong Kong</a> along with the <a href="/wiki/Executive_Council_of_Hong_Kong" title="Executive Council of Hong Kong">Executive Council</a> can prohibit public gatherings, issue <a href="/wiki/Curfew" title="Curfew">curfew</a> orders, prohibit the movement of vessels or aircraft and appoint special constable all under Chapter 245 ("Public Order Ordinance") of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Law" title="Hong Kong Law">Hong Kong Law</a>.</p>
<p>Since 1997, no emergency measures have been enacted. Prior to that date, emergency measures were used for four major incidents:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_1956_riots" title="Hong Kong 1956 riots">Hong Kong 1956 riots</a> – with involvement of <a href="/wiki/British_Armed_Forces" title="British Armed Forces">British Armed Forces</a> and Hong Kong Police</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_1966_riots" title="Hong Kong 1966 riots">Hong Kong 1966 riots</a> – with involvement of British Forces and Hong Kong Police</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_1967_Leftist_Riots" title="Hong Kong 1967 Leftist Riots">Hong Kong 1967 Leftist Riots</a> – with involvement of British Forces and Hong Kong Police</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_1981_riots" title="Hong Kong 1981 riots">Hong Kong 1981 riots</a> – with involvement of Hong Kong Police</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hungary">Hungary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Hungary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungarian</a> Constitution, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Hungary" title="National Assembly of Hungary">National Assembly of Hungary</a> can declare state of emergency in case of armed rebellion or natural or industrial disaster. It expires after 30 days, but can be extended. Most civil rights can be suspended, but basic human rights (such as the right to live, the ban of torture, and freedom of religion) cannot.</p>
<p>During state of emergency, the Parliament cannot be disbanded.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iceland">Iceland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Iceland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Iceland" title="Constitution of Iceland">Icelandic constitution</a> provides no mechanism for state of emergency nor martial law.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/State_of_Emergency_in_India" title="State of Emergency in India">State of Emergency in India</a></div>
<p>In India, an external state of emergency was declared two times during wars:</p>
<ul>
<li>In 1962 <a href="/wiki/Sino-Indian_War" title="Sino-Indian War">Sino-Indian War</a></li>
<li>In 1971 <a href="/wiki/Indo-Pakistani_War_of_1971" title="Indo-Pakistani War of 1971">Indo-Pakistani War of 1971</a></li>
</ul>
<p>In 1975, <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_India" title="Prime Minister of India">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Indira_Gandhi" title="Indira Gandhi">Indira Gandhi</a> declared a <a href="/wiki/The_Emergency_(India)" title="The Emergency (India)">state of internal emergency</a> after she was indicted in a corruption scandal and ordered to vacate her seat in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_Parliament" title="Indian Parliament">Indian Parliament</a>, allowing herself to rule by <a href="/wiki/Decree" title="Decree">decree</a> until 1977. It all started after <a href="/wiki/Raj_Narain" title="Raj Narain">Raj Narain</a>, who had been defeated in parliamentary election by Indira Gandhi, lodged cases of election fraud and use of state machinery for election purposes against Gandhi in the <a href="/wiki/Allahabad_High_Court" title="Allahabad High Court">Allahabad High Court</a>. While some serious charges such as bribing voters and election malpractices were dropped, on 12 June 1975, Justice Jagmohanlal Sinha of the <a href="/wiki/Allahabad_High_Court" title="Allahabad High Court">Allahabad High Court</a> found the Prime Minister guilty on the charge of misuse of government machinery for her election campaign, such as using the state police to build a dais, availing the services of a government officer, Yashpal Kapoor (IAS), during the elections before he had resigned from his position, and use of electricity from the state electricity department. The court declared her election null and void and unseated her from her seat in the Lok Sabha. The court also banned her from contesting any election for an additional six years.</p>
<p>Protests led by J.P.Narayan, Raj Narain, Satyendra Narayan Sinha and Morarji Desai flooded the streets of Delhi close to the Parliament building and the Prime Minister's residence, and in response, Mrs Gandhi declared a state of emergency, suspending civil liberties. Political opposition was heavily suppressed during the emergency. The Government used police forces across the country to arrest thousands of protestors and strike leaders. J.P. Narayan, Raj Narain, Morarji Desai, Charan Singh, Jivatram Kripalani, Atal Bihari Vajpayee, L.K. Advani, Satyendra Narayan Sinha and other protest leaders were immediately arrested. Organizations such as the Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh, along with some opposition political parties were banned. Nearly two years later, confident about her chances of getting re-elected, <a href="/wiki/Indira_Gandhi" title="Indira Gandhi">Indira Gandhi</a> relaxed the emergency, released dissidents and announced elections.</p>
<p>In the ensuing election, <a href="/wiki/Raj_Narain" title="Raj Narain">Raj Narain</a> and his colleagues rode a popular wave of unrest to defeat her and her Government. <a href="/wiki/Raj_Narain" title="Raj Narain">Raj Narain</a> won decisively in the Prime Minister's home constituency of Rae Bareli, and for the first time a non-<a href="/wiki/Indian_National_Congress" title="Indian National Congress">Congress</a> government took office in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_India" title="Republic of India">Republic of India</a></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Ireland a state of national emergency may be declared by invoking Article 28 of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Constitution of Ireland">Constitution of Ireland</a>. According to Article 28.3.3., "no article of the Constitution may be invoked to invalidate any law enacted by the <a href="/wiki/Oireachtas" title="Oireachtas">Oireachtas</a> which is expressed to be for the purpose of securing the public safety and the preservation of the State in time of war or armed rebellion, or to nullify any act done or purporting to be done in time of war or armed rebellion in pursuance of any such law". The time of war or armed rebellion includes actions outside the state itself, and is not limited in time to the duration of the war or armed rebellion.</p>
<p>A state of emergency was declared in 1939 at the outbreak of World War II, though Ireland was not a participant (The period was and is referred to as <a href="/wiki/The_Emergency_(Ireland)" title="The Emergency (Ireland)">The Emergency</a> in Ireland). This state of emergency was not technically lifted until 1976, and was succeeded by a second state of emergency to deal with <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">the Troubles</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> and the recent assassination of the British ambassador to Ireland, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christopher_Ewart_Biggs" title="Christopher Ewart Biggs">Christopher Ewart Biggs</a>. The Troubles lasted until the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army" title="Irish Republican Army">IRA</a> ceasefire in 1994.</p>
<p>The Emergency Powers Act 1939 granted the government the power to govern through executive decree's called 'Emergency Powers Orders' that could cover any aspect of national life.</p>
<p>Some emergency powers do not require an act of parliament to pass, for example Section V of the <a href="/wiki/Offences_against_the_State_Acts_1939%E2%80%931998" title="Offences against the State Acts 1939–1998">Offences against the State Act</a> allows the government to declare that the section (which covers internment, arrest and search without a judicial warrant) is in effect by statutory instrument, and empowers any minister of state to issue an arrest warrant and determine how long the person in question is to be held for.</p>
<p>The only limitation placed on the government during a state of emergency (as with peacetime) is a prohibition on capital punishment.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Macau">Macau</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Macau">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Standing_Committee_of_the_National_People%27s_Congress" title="Standing Committee of the National People's Congress">Standing Committee of the National People's Congress</a> can declare a state of emergency and deploy troops from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army_Macau_Garrison" title="People's Liberation Army Macau Garrison">People's Liberation Army Macau Garrison</a> under the Article 14 of Macau's Basic Law on the defence of the Macau Special Administrative Region.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chief_Executive_of_Macau" title="Chief Executive of Macau">Chief Executive of Macau</a> can use the <a href="/wiki/Macau_national_security_law" title="Macau national security law">Macau national security law</a> to prohibit public gatherings, issue <a href="/wiki/Curfew" title="Curfew">curfew</a> orders, prohibit other activities perceived to be a threat against the Region or China.</p>
<p>Since 1999 no emergency measure have been enacted. Prior to 1999 emergency measures have been used for 1 major incident:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/12-3_incident" title="12-3 incident">12-3 incident</a> (1966) – with <a href="/wiki/Martial_law" title="Martial law">martial law</a> invoked and involvement of <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Army" title="Portuguese Army">Portuguese troops</a></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Malaysia">Malaysia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Malaysia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Malayan_Emergency" title="Malayan Emergency">Malayan Emergency</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, if the <a href="/wiki/Yang_di-Pertuan_Agong" title="Yang di-Pertuan Agong">Yang di-Pertuan Agong</a> (Monarch) is satisfied that a grave emergency exists whereby the security, or the economic life, or public order in the Federation or any part thereof is threatened, he may issue a Proclamation of Emergency making therein a declaration to that effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the history of Malaysia, a state of emergency was declared by the then-colonial government of Britain. The state of emergency lasted from 1948 until 1960 to deal with the <a href="/wiki/Malayan_Communist_Party" title="Malayan Communist Party">communists</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Chin_Peng" title="Chin Peng">Chin Peng</a>.</p>
<p>States of emergency were also declared during the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indonesia-Malaysia_confrontation" title="Indonesia-Malaysia confrontation">Konfrontasi</a></i> in 1962, the <a href="/wiki/1966_Sarawak_constitutional_crisis" title="1966 Sarawak constitutional crisis">1966 Sarawak constitutional crisis</a> and <a href="/wiki/1977_Kelantan_Emergency" title="1977 Kelantan Emergency">1977 Kelantan Emergency</a>.</p>
<p>When a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/May_13_incident" title="May 13 incident">race riot</a> broke out on May 13, 1969, a state of emergency was declared.</p>
<p>On August 11, 2005 a state of emergency was announced for the world's 13th largest <a href="/wiki/Port" title="Port">port</a>, <a href="/wiki/Port_Klang" title="Port Klang">Port Klang</a> and the <a href="/wiki/District" title="District">district</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kuala_Selangor" title="Kuala Selangor">Kuala Selangor</a> after air pollution there reached dangerous levels (defined as a value greater than 500 on the <a href="/wiki/Air_Pollution_Index" title="Air Pollution Index">Air Pollution Index</a> or API).</p>
<p>Thiery Rommel, the European Commission's envoy to Malaysia, told Reuters by telephone on November 13, 2007 (the last day of his mission) that, "Today, this country still lives under (a state of) emergency."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Although not officially proclaimed as a state of emergency, the Emergency Ordinance and the Internal Security Act had allowed detention for years without trial.</p>
<p>On June 23, 2013 a state of emergency was declared by Prime Minister Najib Abdul Razak for Muar and Ledang, Johor as smoke from land-clearing fires in Indonesia pushed air pollution index to above 750. This is the first time in years that air quality has dipped to hazardous level with conditions worsening in as dry weather persists and fires rage in Sumatra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Civil Defence Emergency Management Act 2002 gives the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Government of New Zealand">government</a> and local <a href="/wiki/City_council" title="City council">city council</a> the power to issue a state of emergency, either over the entire country or within a specific region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> This may suspend ordinary work and essential services if need be. The state of emergency in New Zealand expires on the commencement of the seventh day after the date on which it was declared, unless it is extended. However, the minister of civil defence or local mayor may lift the state of emergency after an initial review of the region's status.</p>
<ul>
<li>On 23 February 2011 at 11.28 am Minister of Civil Defence <a href="/wiki/John_Carter_(New_Zealand_politician)" title="John Carter (New Zealand politician)">John Carter</a> declared the first National State Of Emergency (for a civil defence emergency) in New Zealand's history in response to the February 22, <a href="/wiki/2011_Christchurch_earthquake" title="2011 Christchurch earthquake">2011 Christchurch earthquake</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emergency_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-emergency-27">[27]</a></sup> A local state of emergency was declared following the September 4, <a href="/wiki/2010_Canterbury_earthquake" title="2010 Canterbury earthquake">2010 Canterbury earthquake</a>.</li>
<li>A state of emergency was declared previously during the <a href="/wiki/1951_New_Zealand_waterfront_dispute" title="1951 New Zealand waterfront dispute">1951 waterfront dispute</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nigeria">Nigeria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Nigeria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Nigeria, a state of emergency is usually declared in times of great civil unrest. In recent years, it has specifically been implemented in reaction to terrorist attacks on Nigerians by the Islamic jihadist group <a href="/wiki/Boko_Haram" title="Boko Haram">Boko Haram</a>.</p>
<p>On 14 May 2013, <a href="/wiki/Goodluck_Jonathan" title="Goodluck Jonathan">Goodluck Jonathan</a> declared a state of emergency for the entire northeastern states of <a href="/wiki/Borno_State" title="Borno State">Borno</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yobe" title="Yobe">Yobe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adamawa_State" title="Adamawa State">Adamawa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> A more limited state of emergency had been declared on 31 December 2011 in parts of Yobe, Borno, <a href="/wiki/Plateau_State" title="Plateau State">Plateau</a> and <a href="/wiki/Niger_State" title="Niger State">Niger</a> states. This earlier declaration included the temporary shutdown of the international borders in those regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pakistan">Pakistan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Pakistan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/International_reaction_to_the_2007_Pakistani_state_of_emergency" title="International reaction to the 2007 Pakistani state of emergency">International reaction to the 2007 Pakistani state of emergency</a></div>
<p>In Pakistan, a state of emergency was declared five times in its history:</p>
<ul>
<li>In 1958 by President <a href="/wiki/Iskander_Mirza" title="Iskander Mirza">Iskander Mirza</a></li>
<li>In 1969 by President General <a href="/wiki/Yahya_Khan" title="Yahya Khan">Yahya Khan</a></li>
<li>In 1977 by President General <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Zia-ul-Haq" title="Muhammad Zia-ul-Haq">Muhammad Zia-ul-Haq</a></li>
<li>In 1998 by President <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Rafiq_Tarar" title="Muhammad Rafiq Tarar">Muhammad Rafiq Tarar</a></li>
<li>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2007_Pakistani_state_of_emergency" title="2007 Pakistani state of emergency">2007</a> by President General <a href="/wiki/Pervez_Musharraf" title="Pervez Musharraf">Pervez Musharraf</a></li>
</ul>
<p>The first three were regarded as the imposition of direct martial law.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Romania">Romania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Romania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, there are two types of states of emergency, each designed for a different type of situation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul>
<li>State of alert (<i>Stare de alertă</i> in <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a>): Non-military, can be enforced by a prefect. <a href="/wiki/Roadblock" title="Roadblock">Roadblocks</a> are enforced. Any utilitarian vehicle or equipment can be temporarily used by the state, without any restriction. Evacuation is not mandatory, unless extreme circumstances apply. Only <a href="/wiki/Emergency_medical_services" title="Emergency medical services">EMS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romanian_Police" title="Romanian Police">Police</a> and firefighting personnel are required to intervene. This situation can be enforced in case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_disasters" title="Natural disasters">natural disasters</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_unrest" title="Civil unrest">civil unrest</a>.</li>
<li>State of emergency (<i>stare de urgentă</i> in Romanian): Can only be enforced by the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Romania" title="President of Romania">President of Romania</a> with approval from <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Romania" title="Parliament of Romania">Parliament</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_of_Romania" title="Military of Romania">military</a> becomes the upper form of control in the country (under the rule of the president).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The civilian population is subject to strict regulations, imposed by the type of emergency.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> All private and public non-crucial activities are suspended.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Essential_services" title="Essential services">Essential services</a> might be disrupted. This situation can be enforced in case of extreme circumstances, such as a war.</li>
<li>Special zone of public safety (<i>Zonă specială de siguranță publică</i> in Romanian): Administrative, can be enforced by local police. This implies installation of road check-points and higher numbers in police and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanian_riot_police" title="Romanian riot police">gendarmes/ riot police</a> presence, patrolling the area. There is also a ban that restricts the right to travel for people in the area; any vehicle and individual transiting the zone are subject to screening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zona_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-zona-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The most well-known event in which the state of emergency has been enforced was because of <a href="/wiki/1977_Vrancea_earthquake" title="1977 Vrancea earthquake">1977 Vrancea earthquake</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The last instance in which the <i>special zone of public safety</i> was enforced was in December 8, 2013-ongoing, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Punge%C8%99ti,_Vaslui" title="Pungești, Vaslui">Pungești, Vaslui</a> following <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2012%E2%80%9313_Romanian_protests_against_shale_gas" title="2012–13 Romanian protests against shale gas">civil unrest in Pungești</a> from <a href="/wiki/Chevron_Corporation" title="Chevron Corporation">Chevron</a>'s plans to begin exploring shale-gas in the village.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> According to police officials, the special security zone will be maintained as long as there is conflict in the area that poses a threat to Chevron’s operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zona_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-zona-30">[30]</a></sup> This special security zone has faced domestic and international criticism for alleged human-rights abuses.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/State_of_Emergency_Act,_1997" title="State of Emergency Act, 1997">State of Emergency Act, 1997</a></div>
<p>States of emergency in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> are governed by section 37 of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_South_Africa" title="Constitution of South Africa">Constitution</a> and by the <a href="/wiki/State_of_Emergency_Act,_1997" title="State of Emergency Act, 1997">State of Emergency Act, 1997</a>. The <a href="/wiki/President_of_South_Africa" title="President of South Africa">President</a> may declare a state of emergency only when "the life of the nation is threatened by war, invasion, general insurrection, disorder, natural disaster or other public emergency" and if the ordinary laws and government powers are not sufficient to restore peace and order. The declaration is made by <a href="/wiki/Proclamation" title="Proclamation">proclamation</a> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Government_Gazette_of_South_Africa" title="Government Gazette of South Africa">Government Gazette</a></i> and may only apply from the time of publication, not retroactively. It can only continue for 21 days unless the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_South_Africa" title="National Assembly of South Africa">National Assembly</a> grants an extension, which may be for at most three months at a time. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_Courts_of_South_Africa" title="High Courts of South Africa">High Courts</a> have the power, subject to confirmation by the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Court_of_South_Africa" title="Constitutional Court of South Africa">Constitutional Court</a>, to determine the validity of the declaration of a state of emergency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sa-bor-handbook_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-sa-bor-handbook-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>During a state of emergency the President has the power to make emergency regulations "necessary or expedient" to restore peace and order and end the emergency. This power can be delegated to other authorities. Emergency measures can violate the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_of_Rights_(South_Africa)" title="Bill of Rights (South Africa)">Bill of Rights</a>, but only to a limited extent. Some rights are inviolable, including amongst others the rights to life and to human dignity; the prohibition of discrimination on the grounds of race, sex or religion; the prohibition of torture or inhuman punishment; and the right of accused people to a fair trial. Any violation of a constitutional right must be strictly required by the emergency. Emergency measures may not indemnify the government or individuals for illegal actions. They may impose criminal penalties, but not exceeding three years' imprisonment. They may not require military service beyond that required by the ordinary laws governing the defence force. An emergency measure may be disapproved by the National Assembly, in which case it lapses, and no emergency measure may interfere with the elections, powers or sittings of <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_South_Africa" title="Parliament of South Africa">Parliament</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Provincial_legislature_(South_Africa)" title="Provincial legislature (South Africa)">provincial legislatures</a>. The courts have the power to determine the validity of any emergency measure.</p>
<p>The constitution places strict limits on any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detention_without_trial" title="Detention without trial">detention without trial</a> during a state of emergency. A friend or family member of the detainee must be informed, and the name and place of detention must be published in the <i>Government Gazette</i>. The detainee must have access to a doctor and a legal representative. He or she must be brought before a court within at most ten days, for the court to determine whether the detention is necessary, and if not released may demand repeated review every ten days. At the court review the detainee must be allowed legal representation and must be allowed to appear in person. The provisions on detention without trial do not apply to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prisoners_of_war" title="Prisoners of war">prisoners of war</a> in an international conflict; instead they must be treated in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Conventions" title="Geneva Conventions">Geneva Conventions</a> and other international law.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spain">Spain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Spain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Spain, there are three degrees of state of emergency (<i>estado de emergencia</i> in Spanish): <i>alarma</i> (alarm or alert), <i>excepción</i> (exception[al circumstance]) and <i>sitio</i> (siege). They are named by the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Constitution_of_1978" title="Spanish Constitution of 1978">constitution</a>, which limits which rights may be suspended, but regulated by the "<a class="external text" href="http://noticias.juridicas.com/base_datos/Admin/lo4-1981.html" rel="nofollow">Ley Orgánica 4/1981</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Organic_Law_(Spain)" title="Organic Law (Spain)">Organic Law</a>).</p>
<p>On December 4, 2010, the first state of alert was declared following the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_air_traffic_controllers_strike" title="Spanish air traffic controllers strike">air traffic controllers strike</a>. It was the first time since the <a href="/wiki/Francoist_Spain" title="Francoist Spain">Francisco Franco's regime</a> that a state of emergency was declared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Switzerland">Switzerland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Switzerland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to Art. 185 of the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Federal_Constitution" title="Swiss Federal Constitution">Swiss Federal Constitution</a> The <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Federal_Council" title="Swiss Federal Council">Federal Council</a> (Bundesrat) can call up in their own competence military personnel of maximum 4000 <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a> for three weeks to safeguard inner or outer security (called Federal Intervention or Federal Execution, respectively). A larger number of soldiers or of a longer duration is subject to <a href="/wiki/Federal_Assembly_(Switzerland)" title="Federal Assembly (Switzerland)">parliamentary</a> decision. For deployments within Switzerland the principle of subsidiarity rules: as a first step, unrest has to be overcome with the aid of cantonal police units.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trinidad_and_Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Trinidad and Tobago">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A state of emergency was declared in 1970 during the <a href="/wiki/Black_Power_Revolution" title="Black Power Revolution">Black Power Revolution</a> by then Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Eric_Williams" title="Eric Williams">Eric Williams</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Jamaat_al_Muslimeen_coup_attempt" title="Jamaat al Muslimeen coup attempt">attempted state coup</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Jamaat_al_Muslimeen" title="Jamaat al Muslimeen">Jamaat al Muslimeen</a> against the NAR government of the then Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/A._N._R._Robinson" title="A. N. R. Robinson">A. N. R. Robinson</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> a state of emergency was declared during the coup attempt and for a period after the coup.</p>
<p>On August 4, 1995, a state of emergency was declared to remove the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Speakers_of_the_House_of_Representatives_of_Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="List of Speakers of the House of Representatives of Trinidad and Tobago">Speaker of the House</a> Occah Seepaul by Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Manning" title="Patrick Manning">Patrick Manning</a> during a constitutional crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The government had attempted to remove the speaker via a no-confidence motion, which failed. The state of emergency was used to remove the speaker using the emergency powers granted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Kamla_Persad-Bissessar" title="Kamla Persad-Bissessar">Kamla Persad-Bissessar</a> on 22 August 2011 at 8:00 pm announced a State of Emergency in an attempt to crack down on the trafficking of illegal drugs and firearms, in addition to gangs. The decision of the President, <a href="/wiki/George_Maxwell_Richards" title="George Maxwell Richards">George Maxwell Richards</a>, to issue the proclamation for the State of Emergency was debated in <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Parliament of Trinidad and Tobago">the country's Parliament</a> as required by the Constitution on September 2, 2011 and passed by the required simple majority of the House of Representatives. On September 4 the Parliament has extended the State Of Emergency for a further 3 months and ended in December 2011.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Turkey">Turkey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Turkey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Martial_law_and_state_of_emergency_in_Turkey" title="Martial law and state of emergency in Turkey">Martial law and state of emergency in Turkey</a></div>
<p>Since the foundation of the Republic of <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> in 1923 the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_Armed_Forces" title="Turkish Armed Forces">military</a> conducted three <a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d'état">coups d'état</a> and announced martial law. Martial law between 1978 and 1983 was replaced by a state of emergency that lasted until November 2002. The latest state of emergency was declared by President Erdogan on 20 July 2016 following a failed coup attempt on 15 July 2016 by a faction of the country's armed forces.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_United_Kingdom" title="The United Kingdom">the United Kingdom</a>, only the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Sovereign" title="British Sovereign">British Sovereign</a>, on the advice of the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Privy Council of the United Kingdom">Privy Council</a> is able to proclaim emergency regulations under the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Contingencies_Act_2004" title="Civil Contingencies Act 2004">Civil Contingencies Act 2004</a> in case of any seriously fatal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Threats" title="Threats">threats</a> to their human welfare, their human society, and their environment, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warfare" title="Warfare">warfare</a> or <a href="/wiki/Terrorism" title="Terrorism">terrorism</a>. These regulations last for seven days unless confirmed otherwise by <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament</a>. A state of emergency was last invoked in 1974 by Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Edward_Heath" title="Edward Heath">Edward Heath</a> in response to increasing <a href="/wiki/Industrial_action" title="Industrial action">industrial action</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Senate_Report_93-549" title="Senate Report 93-549">Senate Report 93-549</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insurrection_Act" title="Insurrection Act">Insurrection Act</a>, and <a href="/wiki/National_Emergencies_Act" title="National Emergencies Act">National Emergencies Act</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">Constitution</a> explicitly provides some emergency powers:</p>
<ul>
<li>Congress may authorize the government to call forth the militia to execute the laws, suppress an insurrection or repel an invasion.</li>
<li>Congress may authorize the government to suspend consideration of writs of <i><a href="/wiki/Habeas_corpus_in_the_United_States" title="Habeas corpus in the United States">habeas corpus</a></i> "when in cases of rebellion or invasion the public safety may require it."</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Felony" title="Felony">Felony</a> charges may be brought without presentment or <a href="/wiki/Grand_jury" title="Grand jury">grand jury</a> <a href="/wiki/Indictment" title="Indictment">indictment</a> in cases arising "in the militia, when in actual service in time of war or public danger."</li>
<li>A state government may engage in war without Congress's approval if "actually invaded, or in such imminent Danger as will not admit of delay."</li>
</ul>
<p>Aside from these, many provisions of law exist in various jurisdictions, which take effect only upon an executive declaration of emergency. The <a href="/wiki/National_Emergencies_Act" title="National Emergencies Act">National Emergencies Act</a> regulates this process at the federal level. It requires the President to specifically identify the provisions activated and to renew the declaration annually so as to prevent an arbitrarily broad or open-ended emergency. In all such cases, the government must continue to act within the limits of the law and constitution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>A state <a href="/wiki/Governor_(United_States)" title="Governor (United States)">governor</a> or local <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">mayor</a> may declare a state of emergency within his or her <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdiction</a>. This is common at the state level in response to natural disasters. The <a href="/wiki/Federal_Emergency_Management_Agency" title="Federal Emergency Management Agency">Federal Emergency Management Agency</a> maintains a system of assets, personnel and training to respond to such incidents. For example, on December 10, 2015, Washington state Governor Jay Inslee declared a state of emergency due to flooding and landslides caused by heavy rains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 1977 <a href="/wiki/International_Emergency_Economic_Powers_Act" title="International Emergency Economic Powers Act">International Emergency Economic Powers Act</a> allows the government to freeze assets, limit trade and confiscate property in response to an "unusual and extraordinary threat" to the United States that originates substantially outside of it. As of 2015 more than thirty emergencies under the IEEPA remain active regarding various subjects, the oldest of which was declared in 1979 with regard to the government of <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. Another ongoing national emergency, declared after the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a>, authorizes the president to retain or reactivate military personnel beyond their normal term of service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ongoing">Ongoing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_emergency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Ongoing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>On January 17, 2017, the outgoing President of Gambia <a href="/wiki/Yahya_Jammeh" title="Yahya Jammeh">Yahya Jammeh</a> declared a state of emergency, following military pressure from Nigeria, to accept the outcome of the <a href="/wiki/Gambian_presidential_election,_2016" title="Gambian presidential election, 2016">Gambian presidential election, 2016</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li>On October 8, 2016, the federal government of Ethiopia declared a state of emergency for six months after protests got out of control and many local and foreign investments got destroyed by the protesters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> declared a national emergency in response to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/September_11,_2001_attacks" title="September 11, 2001 attacks">September 11, 2001 attacks</a> that remains in effect. This emergency suspends certain provisions of law that limit the size of the military and the duration of military service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> declared a state of calamity on December 20, 2011, following the onslaught of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Washi" title="Tropical Storm Washi">Tropical Storm Washi</a> or Sendong which led to 1080 fatalities, 1079 people missing and nearly 1 billion pesos worth of damage to infrastructure and agriculture, in order to institute price freeze on heavily affected areas and to allow for the release of calamity funds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a> declared a state of emergency in <a href="/wiki/Meiktila" title="Meiktila">Meiktila</a> due to ongoing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sectarian" title="Sectarian">sectarian</a> violence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> declared a state of emergency in response to the <a href="/wiki/November_2015_Paris_attacks" title="November 2015 Paris attacks">November 2015 Paris attacks</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a> declared a state of emergency after pylons in Ukraine were blown up leaving 1,896,000 people without power.</li>
<li>Governor <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Brown" title="Jerry Brown">Jerry Brown</a> of the U.S state <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> declared a state of emergency on January 6, 2016 as a result of a massive <a href="/wiki/Aliso_Canyon_gas_leak" title="Aliso Canyon gas leak">methane gas leak</a> 25 miles northwest of Los Angeles at the Southern California Gas Company <a href="/wiki/Aliso_Canyon_Oil_Field" title="Aliso Canyon Oil Field">Aliso Canyon storage facility</a> near Porter Ranch, California.</li>
<li>In 2016, <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> declared a <a href="/wiki/2016_state_of_emergency_in_Venezuela" title="2016 state of emergency in Venezuela">state of emergency</a> due to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2014-16_Venezuelan_protests" title="2014-16 Venezuelan protests">mass protests</a> and further fueled by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impeachment_process_against_Dilma_Rousseff" title="Impeachment process against Dilma Rousseff">Impeachment process against Dilma Rousseff</a> which President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicolas_Maduro" title="Nicolas Maduro">Nicolas Maduro</a> believes is the result of an American conspiracy to overthrow him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-reuters-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guar_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-guar-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph-50">[50]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/President_of_Turkey" title="President of Turkey">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Recep_Tayyip_Erdo%C4%9Fan" title="Recep Tayyip Erdoğan">Recep Tayyip Erdoğan</a> declared a state of emergency on 20 July 2016 for three months after the <a href="/wiki/2016_Turkish_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat_attempt" title="2016 Turkish coup d'état attempt">failed coup attempt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soe_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-soe-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li>In May 5, 2016, the government of the <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazilian</a> state of <a href="/wiki/Esp%C3%ADrito_Santo" title="Espírito Santo">Espírito Santo</a> declared a state of emergency due to the <a href="/wiki/2014%E2%80%9316_Brazilian_drought" title="2014–16 Brazilian drought">2014–16 Brazilian drought</a>, which is affecting severely the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GazetaES_Drought_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-GazetaES_Drought-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
<li>Since 1948, Israel has been subject to a State of Emergency. Despite the conditions leading to its impositions having vanished, provisions of Israeli law, including those that impact on the day-to-day lives of Israeli citizens (such as allowing employers to allow work beyond normal hours outside certain sectors) have depended on the State of Emergency being in force.</li>
</ul>

<li>July 1, 2008 – <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolian</a> president <a href="/wiki/Nambaryn_Enkhbayar" title="Nambaryn Enkhbayar">Nambaryn Enkhbayar</a> declared a state of emergency in the capital <a href="/wiki/Ulaanbaatar" title="Ulaanbaatar">Ulaanbaatar</a> for four days after violent protests against the ex-communist <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_People%27s_Party" title="Mongolian People's Party">Mongolian People's Revolutionary Party</a> (MPRP). The MPRP had claimed a majority of seats in the <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_legislative_election,_2008" title="Mongolian legislative election, 2008">2008 parliamentary elections</a>, but was accused of fraud and vote rigging by the less-successful parties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></li>
<li>March 2008 – <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a> was in a state of emergency from March 2, 2008 to March 20, 2008, declared by President <a href="/wiki/Robert_Kocharyan" title="Robert Kocharyan">Robert Kocharyan</a> in response to <a href="/wiki/2008_Armenian_presidential_election_protests" title="2008 Armenian presidential election protests">protests</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_presidential_election,_2008" title="Armenian presidential election, 2008">2008 Armenian presidential elections</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></li>
<li>November 3, 2007 – <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> was in a state of emergency from November 3, 2007 to December 15, 2007. <a href="/wiki/President_of_Pakistan" title="President of Pakistan">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Pervez_Musharraf" title="Pervez Musharraf">Pervez Musharraf</a> declared emergency "to stop Pakistan from committing suicide". He lifted the state of emergency after he resigned from the <a href="/wiki/Pakistan_Army" title="Pakistan Army">army</a> and took the oath of office as a civilian <a href="/wiki/President_of_Pakistan" title="President of Pakistan">President of Pakistan</a>.</li>
<li>August 25, 2007 – Greece was in a state of emergency from August 25, 2007 to August 28, 2007 due to the highly destructive <a href="/wiki/2007_Greek_forest_fires" title="2007 Greek forest fires">forest fires</a> that occurred throughout the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></li>
<li>June 2007 – <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a> declared a state of emergency as the country was hit by the strongest named cyclone in the northern Indian Ocean, <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Gonu" title="Cyclone Gonu">Cyclone Gonu</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></li>
<li>January 2007 – <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara_County,_California" title="Santa Clara County, California">Santa Clara County, California</a> was under a state of emergency due to extremely cold temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></li>
<li>May 14, 2006 – The U.S. states of <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a> declared a state of emergency as a result of massive <a href="/wiki/New_England_Flood_of_May_2006" title="New England Flood of May 2006">flooding</a> from the strongest rains to hit the regions in almost 70 years.</li>
<li>October 13, 2006 – <a href="/wiki/Buffalo,_New_York" title="Buffalo, New York">Buffalo, United States</a>, declared a state of emergency when the "most devastating snow storm in U.S. history"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> hit the city. Schools and businesses were closed for a week, and Buffalo and surrounding towns and cities were declared major disaster areas by President Bush.</li>
<li>February 24, 2006 – the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> declared a state of emergency via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippine_Proclamation_1017" title="Philippine Proclamation 1017">Philippine Proclamation 1017</a> for one week until <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippine_Proclamation_1021" title="Philippine Proclamation 1021">Philippine Proclamation 1021</a>, in response to a supposed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coup" title="Coup">coup</a> against President <a href="/wiki/Gloria_Macapagal_Arroyo" title="Gloria Macapagal Arroyo">Gloria Macapagal Arroyo</a>'s government in the midst of the 20th anniversary of the 1986 <a href="/wiki/People_Power_Revolution" title="People Power Revolution">People Power Revolution</a> that toppled the rule of <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Marcos" title="Ferdinand Marcos">Ferdinand Marcos</a>.
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2006_state_of_emergency_in_the_Philippines" title="2006 state of emergency in the Philippines">2006 state of emergency in the Philippines</a></div>
</li>
<li>August 27, 2005 – In the U.S state <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> due to Hurricane Katrina</li>
<li>Mid-August 2005 – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sucumbios" title="Sucumbios">Sucumbios</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orellana_Province" title="Orellana Province">Orellana</a>, two provinces of <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, because of indigenous protests against oil firms</li>
<li>April 15, 2005 – <a href="/wiki/Quito" title="Quito">Quito</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capital_(political)" title="Capital (political)">capital</a> of Ecuador due to protests; lifted less than a day later, on April 17, 2005.</li>
<li>February to April 2005 – <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a></li>
<li>December 2004 – <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a> because of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2004_Indian_Ocean_earthquake" title="2004 Indian Ocean earthquake">tsunami</a>.</li>
<li>November 2004 – <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a></li>
<li>October 2004 – The <a href="/wiki/Gaza_Strip" title="Gaza Strip">Gaza Strip</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Bank" title="West Bank">West Bank</a></li>
<li>April 2004 – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ryongchon" title="Ryongchon">Ryongchon</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> following a major explosion.</li>
<li>November 2003 – <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a>, following weeks of civil unrest.</li>
<li>August 2003 – <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, United States, and <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, Canada, in response to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2003_North_America_blackout" title="2003 North America blackout">2003 North America blackout</a>.</li>
<li>August 2003 – <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a></li>
<li>August 2003 – Portugal, in response to forest fires.</li>
<li>July 2003 – Mexico, in response to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Nile_virus" title="West Nile virus">West Nile virus</a> outbreak .</li>
<li>May 2003 – <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a></li>
<li>April 2003 – <a href="/wiki/Mato_Grosso" title="Mato Grosso">Mato Grosso</a>, Brazil, in response to torrential rainfall (<i>estado de emergência</i>).</li>
<li>March 2003 – <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a> after assassination of <a href="/wiki/Zoran_%C4%90in%C4%91i%C4%87" title="Zoran Đinđić">Zoran Đinđić</a> (<i>vanredno stanje</i>).</li>
<li>January 2003 – <a href="/wiki/Canberra" title="Canberra">Canberra</a>, the national capital of Australia during the <a href="/wiki/2003_Canberra_bushfires" title="2003 Canberra bushfires">Canberra bushfires of 2003</a>.</li>
<li>September 2002 – Moscow, Russia, in response to smoke pollution from forest fires.</li>
<li>July 2002 – <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a> , in response to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/December_2001_riots_(Argentina)" title="December 2001 riots (Argentina)">public unrest</a>.</li>
<li>November 2001 – <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, in response to increased guerrilla activity.</li>
<li>November 30, 1999 – The U.S city of <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" title="Washington (U.S. state)">Washington</a>, stemming from protest of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WTO_Ministerial_Conference_of_1999" title="WTO Ministerial Conference of 1999">WTO Ministerial Conference of 1999</a> and police reaction to it.
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WTO_Ministerial_Conference_of_1999_protest_activity" title="WTO Ministerial Conference of 1999 protest activity">WTO Ministerial Conference of 1999 protest activity</a></div>
</li>
<li>May–June 1998 – <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> declares state of emergency, due to <a href="/wiki/May_1998_riots_of_Indonesia" title="May 1998 riots of Indonesia">May 1998 riots of Indonesia</a>.</li>
<li>March 2, 1997 – The 1997 unrest in <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a>, also known as the Lottery Uprising or Anarchy in Albania, was an uprising sparked by Ponzi scheme failures. Albania descended into anarchy and violence in which the government was toppled and some 2,000 people were killed. On March 1, Prime Minister Aleksandër Meksi resigned and on March 2 President <a href="/wiki/Sali_Berisha" title="Sali Berisha">Sali Berisha</a> declared a state of emergency.
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1997_rebellion_in_Albania" title="1997 rebellion in Albania">1997 rebellion in Albania</a></div>
</li>
<li>August 5, 1995 – <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a> to remove <a href="/wiki/List_of_Speakers_of_the_House_of_Representatives_of_Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="List of Speakers of the House of Representatives of Trinidad and Tobago">Speaker of the House</a> Occah Seapaul who refused to resign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyt_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nyt-72">[72]</a></sup></li>
<li>Winter 1995 – The U.S city of Sault Ste. Marie, Michigan after a severe snowstorm buried the city in 6 feet (1.8 m) of snow.</li>
<li>April–May 1992 – <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, United States. State of Emergency was declared in response of the <a href="/wiki/1992_Los_Angeles_riots" title="1992 Los Angeles riots">1992 Los Angeles riots</a>. After Rodney King was video taped being beaten by White police men, that was acquitted by jury at trial.</li>
<li>March 1992 – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Moldova" title="Republic of Moldova">Republic of Moldova</a>, in response to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_of_Transnistria" title="War of Transnistria">War of Transnistria</a></li>
<li>1992 to 2011 – <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> endures a 19-year state of emergency enacted at the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Algerian_Civil_War" title="Algerian Civil War">1992 coup</a>. The state of emergency, which suspended citizens' rights in lieu of military power, was lifted after the Algerian Government gave in to protestor demands during the 2011 <a href="/wiki/Arab_Spring" title="Arab Spring">Arab Spring</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>August 1991 – <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, enemies of <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a>'s reforms evoked the state of emergency because Gorbachev, according to them, was destroying both <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communism</a> and the state itself. The leader of the coup was <a href="/wiki/Gennady_Yanayev" title="Gennady Yanayev">Gennady Yanayev</a>.</li>
<li>July–August 1990 – Trinidad and Tobago declared a state of emergency when a group stormed Parliament and a TV Station holding government officials, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Minister" title="Prime Minister">Prime Minister</a> at ransom. See <a href="/wiki/Jamaat_al_Muslimeen_coup_attempt" title="Jamaat al Muslimeen coup attempt">Jamaat al Muslimeen coup attempt</a></li>
<li>July 1985 to February 1990 – <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, in response to increasing civil unrest and township violence opposing <a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">apartheid</a> rule.</li>
<li>1975 to 1977 – India. <a href="/wiki/Indira_Gandhi" title="Indira Gandhi">Indira Gandhi</a> declared a state of emergency in 1975 in response to political opposition and her own conviction on charges of electoral fraud. <a href="/wiki/The_Emergency_(India)" title="The Emergency (India)">The Emergency</a> lasted for 19 months</li>
<li>1972 to 1976 – <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>, due to ethnic and labor-related unrest. Elections were suspended during this period, and political rights were broadly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circumscribed" title="Circumscribed">circumscribed</a>.</li>
<li>1971 – <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, Australia in response to fears over increasing protest over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1971_Springbok_tour" title="1971 Springbok tour">1971 Springbok tour</a></li>
<li>1970 to 1972 – Trinidad and Tobago a state of emergency was declared to deal with the <a href="/wiki/Black_Power_Revolution" title="Black Power Revolution">Black Power Revolution</a> which also included a mutiny in the Military.</li>
<li>1972 – the United Kingdom in response to increasingly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Militant_(word)" title="Militant (word)">militant</a> <a href="/wiki/Industrial_action" title="Industrial action">industrial action</a>.</li>
<li>October 1970 – <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> in response to the <a href="/wiki/October_Crisis" title="October Crisis">October Crisis</a> kidnappings of government officials.</li>
<li>July 1967 – <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a>, United States in response to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/12th_Street_riot" title="12th Street riot">12th Street riot</a> started on Sunday morning during a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blind_pig" title="Blind pig">blind pig</a> <a href="/wiki/Police_raid" title="Police raid">raid</a>.</li>
<li>October 1962 – United States in response to the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Missile_Crisis" title="Cuban Missile Crisis">Cuban Missile Crisis</a>.</li>
<li>1948 to 1960 – <a href="/wiki/Malayan_Emergency" title="Malayan Emergency">Malayan Emergency</a> in Malaysia and Singapore</li>
<li>1958 – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malta_Colony" title="Malta Colony">Malta</a> due to riots against the British colonial government following Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Dom_Mintoff" title="Dom Mintoff">Dom Mintoff</a>'s resignation.</li>
<li>1950 to 1978 – United States due to the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, specifically the threat of "world conquest by communist imperialism."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-espinoza_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-espinoza-73">[73]</a></sup></li>
<li>1939 to 1952 – United States due to World War II</li>
<li>1941 to 1942 – Moscow due to the German advance to within 19 miles (31 km) of the city</li>
<li>March 18, 1907 – <a href="/wiki/Moldavia" title="Moldavia">Moldavia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wallachia" title="Wallachia">Wallachia</a> in Romania during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1907_Romanian_Peasants%27_Revolt" title="1907 Romanian Peasants' Revolt">1907 Romanian Peasants' Revolt</a>.</li>
</ul>
