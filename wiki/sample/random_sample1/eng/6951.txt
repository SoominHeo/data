<p><b>Visoki Dečani</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serbian_Cyrillic_language" title="Serbian Cyrillic language">Serbian Cyrillic</a>: <span lang="sr" xml:lang="sr"><span class="Unicode">Високи Дечани</span></span>, <a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">Albanian</a>: <span lang="sq" xml:lang="sq"><i>Manastiri i Deçanit</i></span>) is a major <a href="/wiki/Serbian_Orthodox_Church" title="Serbian Orthodox Church">Serbian Orthodox Christian</a> <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastery</a> located near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/De%C4%8Dani" title="Dečani">Dečani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kosovo" title="Kosovo">Kosovo</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-status_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-status-1">[a]</a></sup>. It was founded in 14th century by Serbian king <a href="/wiki/Stefan_De%C4%8Danski" title="Stefan Dečanski">Stefan Dečanski</a>.</p>
<p>The Visoki Dečani monastery is located by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/De%C4%8Danska_Bistrica" title="Dečanska Bistrica">Dečanska Bistrica</a> river gorge at the foot of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prokletije_Mountains" title="Prokletije Mountains">Prokletije Mountains</a>, in the region of <a href="/wiki/Metohija" title="Metohija">Metohija</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWorld_Heritage_Committee2003_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWorld_Heritage_Committee2003-3">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurkeMarch_2009_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkeMarch_2009-4">[3]</a></sup> It is located about 2 kilometres (1.2 mi) from the town of Dečani.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEElsie201072_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEElsie201072-5">[4]</a></sup> The monastery is managed by the Serbian Orthodox <a href="/wiki/Eparchy_of_Ra%C5%A1ka_and_Prizren" title="Eparchy of Raška and Prizren">Eparchy of Raška and Prizren</a>. The monastery has been under the legal protection of Serbia since 1947<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWorld_Heritage_Committee2003_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWorld_Heritage_Committee2003-3">[2]</a></sup> with a designation of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cultural_Monuments_of_Exceptional_Importance" title="Cultural Monuments of Exceptional Importance">Cultural Monument of Exceptional Importance</a></i>. The monastery is part of the World Heritage site named "<a href="/wiki/Medieval_Monuments_in_Kosovo" title="Medieval Monuments in Kosovo">Medieval Monuments in Kosovo</a>".</p>

<p>Construction began during the reign of Serbian King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stefan_Uro%C5%A1_III_De%C4%8Danski" title="Stefan Uroš III Dečanski">Stefan Uroš III Dečanski</a> in 1327 and the original founding charter from 1330 has been preserved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUNESCO2006_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUNESCO2006-6">[5]</a></sup> Dečanski's son, <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Du%C5%A1an" title="Stefan Dušan">Stefan Dušan</a>, seized the Serbian throne in 1331 and had his father strangled to death in the <a href="/wiki/Zve%C4%8Dan_Fortress" title="Zvečan Fortress">Zvečan Fortress</a> shortly afterwards. Dečanski was buried in the still incomplete Visoki Dečani monastery in 1331 and its construction was continued by Dušan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJudah200023_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJudah200023-7">[6]</a></sup> The monastery's main architect was Fra Vita, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franciscan" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> monk from the Montenegrin coastal town of <a href="/wiki/Kotor" title="Kotor">Kotor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEElsie201072_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEElsie201072-5">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJudah200023_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJudah200023-7">[6]</a></sup> Construction of the monastery lasted for a total of 8 years, and ended in 1335. The wooden throne of the <a href="/wiki/Hegumen" title="Hegumen">hegumen</a> was finished at around this time, and the church interior was decorated. Dečanski's carved wooden sarcophagus was finished in 1340.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUNESCO2006_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUNESCO2006-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="15th.E2.80.9319th_century">15th–19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visoki_De%C4%8Dani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 15th–19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bulgarian writer <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Tsamblak" title="Gregory Tsamblak">Gregory Tsamblak</a>, author of <i>The Life of Stefan Dečanski</i>, was the head (<i>hegoumenos</i>) of the monastery at the beginning of the 15th century. The painter-monk Longin spent two decades in the monastery during the second half of the 16th century and created 15 icons with depictions of the Great Feast and hermits, as well as his most celebrated work, the icon of Stefan Uroš. In the late 17th century, the Turks plundered the monastery, but inflicted no serious damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUNESCO2006_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUNESCO2006-6">[5]</a></sup> In 1819, archimandrite <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zaharija_De%C4%8Danac" title="Zaharija Dečanac">Zaharija Dečanac</a> became <a href="/wiki/Eparchy_of_Ra%C5%A1ka_and_Prizren" title="Eparchy of Raška and Prizren">Metropolitan of Raška and Prizren</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_and_21st_century">20th and 21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visoki_De%C4%8Dani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 20th and 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the monastery's treasures were plundered by the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Army" title="Austro-Hungarian Army">Austro-Hungarian Army</a>, which occupied Serbia between 1915 and 1918.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMitrovi.C4.872007230_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMitrovi.C4.872007230-8">[7]</a></sup> The monastery fell within the territory of the Italian-ruled <a href="/wiki/Albanian_Kingdom_(1939%E2%80%9343)" title="Albanian Kingdom (1939–43)">Albanian Kingdom</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, and was targeted for destruction by the Albanian nationalist <i><a href="/wiki/Balli_Komb%C3%ABtar" title="Balli Kombëtar">Balli Kombëtar</a></i> and Italian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fascist" title="Fascist">fascist</a> <a href="/wiki/Blackshirts" title="Blackshirts">blackshirts</a> in mid-1941. The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Italian_Army" title="Royal Italian Army">Royal Italian Army</a> responded by sending a group of soldiers to help protect the monastery from attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJudah2000131_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJudah2000131-9">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJudah2002287_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJudah2002287-10">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>The monastic treasure was exhibited in the rebuilt medieval refectory in 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWorld_Heritage_Committee2003_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWorld_Heritage_Committee2003-3">[2]</a></sup> The monastery's monks sheltered refugees of all ethnicities during the <a href="/wiki/Kosovo_War" title="Kosovo War">Kosovo War</a>, which lasted from March 1998 to June 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurkeMarch_2009_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkeMarch_2009-4">[3]</a></sup> On 7 May 1998, the corpses of two elderly Albanians were found 400 metres (1,300 ft) from the monastery. They were reportedly killed by the <a href="/wiki/Kosovo_Liberation_Army" title="Kosovo Liberation Army">Kosovo Liberation Army</a> (KLA) for allegedly collaborating with Serb forces. The KLA staged an attack not far from the monastery on 8 May, killing one person and wounding four others. That evening, Deçan's 300 remaining Serbs came to the monastery to seek shelter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJudah2002158_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJudah2002158-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Albanian civilians seeking refuge in the monastery returned to their homes following the withdrawal of Serb military personnel from Kosovo in June 1999. An Italian unit of the <a href="/wiki/Kosovo_Force" title="Kosovo Force">Kosovo Force</a> (KFOR) was subsequently assigned to guard the monastery, which was attacked on several occasions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJudah2002287_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJudah2002287-10">[9]</a></sup> Dozens of <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Romanis</a> sought sanctuary in the monastery over the next several months, fearing retaliatory attacks by their Albanian neighbours, who accused them of collaborating with the Serbs and looting Albanian homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJudah2002288_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJudah2002288-12">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>During <a href="/wiki/2004_unrest_in_Kosovo" title="2004 unrest in Kosovo">violent unrest</a> in Kosovo on 17 March 2004, KFOR defended the monastery from an Albanian mob trying to throw <a href="/wiki/Molotov_cocktail" title="Molotov cocktail">Molotov cocktails</a> at it. Several Albanians were shot and wounded in the clash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKingMason200614_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKingMason200614-13">[12]</a></sup> On 2 July 2004, the monastery was declared a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Educational,_Scientific_and_Cultural_Organization" title="United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization">United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization</a> (UNESCO).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEElsie201072_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEElsie201072-5">[4]</a></sup> UNESCO cited it as "an irreplaceable treasure, a place where traditions of Romanesque architecture meet artistic patterns of the Byzantine world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurkeMarch_2009_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkeMarch_2009-4">[3]</a></sup> The monastery, along with all other Serbian <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Monuments_in_Kosovo" title="Medieval Monuments in Kosovo">Medieval Monuments in Kosovo</a>, was added to the <a href="/wiki/List_of_World_Heritage_in_Danger" title="List of World Heritage in Danger">UNESCO list</a> of endangered World Heritage sites in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUNESCO2006_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUNESCO2006-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Suspected Kosovo Albanian insurgents hurled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hand_grenade" title="Hand grenade">hand grenades</a> at the monastery on 30 March 2007, but caused little damage. In recent years, the situation around the monastery has stabilized and it has reopened to visitors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurkeMarch_2009_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkeMarch_2009-4">[3]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serbian_President" title="Serbian President">Serbian President</a> <a href="/wiki/Boris_Tadi%C4%87" title="Boris Tadić">Boris Tadić</a> attended a service at the monastery in April 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBBC17_April_2009_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBBC17_April_2009-14">[13]</a></sup> U.S. <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> <a href="/wiki/Joe_Biden" title="Joe Biden">Joe Biden</a> visited the monastery the following month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETanner22_May_2009_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETanner22_May_2009-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architectural_features">Architectural features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visoki_De%C4%8Dani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Architectural features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The church has five-nave naos, a three-part <a href="/wiki/Iconostasis" title="Iconostasis">iconostasis</a>, and a three-nave <a href="/wiki/Parvise" title="Parvise">parvise</a>. With a dome, it is 26 m high.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Its outer walls are done in alternate layers of white and pink marble. The portals, windows, consoles, and capitals are richly decorated. Christ the Judge is shown surrounded by angels in the western part of the Church. Its twenty major cycles of fresco murals represent the largest preserved gallery of Serbian <a href="/wiki/Medieval_art" title="Medieval art">medieval art</a>, featuring over 1000 compositions and several thousand portraits.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visoki_De%C4%8Dani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
