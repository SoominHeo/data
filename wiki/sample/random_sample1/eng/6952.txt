<p>The <b>Bismarck Archipelago</b> is a group of islands off the northeastern coast of <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a> in the western <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> and is part of the <a href="/wiki/Islands_Region" title="Islands Region">Islands Region</a> of <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>. Its area is about 50,000 square km.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismarck_Archipelago&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first inhabitants of the <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> arrived around 33,000 years ago from New Guinea, either by boats across the <a href="/wiki/Bismarck_Sea" title="Bismarck Sea">Bismarck Sea</a> or via a temporary <a href="/wiki/Land_bridge" title="Land bridge">land bridge</a>, created by an uplift in the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a>. Later arrivals included the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lapita" title="Lapita">Lapita</a> people.</p>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> to visit these islands was <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> explorer <a href="/wiki/Willem_Schouten" title="Willem Schouten">Willem Schouten</a> in 1616.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The islands remained unsettled by western Europeans until they were annexed as part of the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Protectorate" title="Protectorate">protectorate</a> of <a href="/wiki/German_New_Guinea" title="German New Guinea">German New Guinea</a> in 1884. The area was named in honour of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Germany_(German_Reich)" title="Chancellor of Germany (German Reich)">Chancellor</a> <a href="/wiki/Otto_von_Bismarck" title="Otto von Bismarck">Otto von Bismarck</a>.</p>
<p>On 13 March 1888, a <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcano</a> erupted on <a href="/wiki/Ritter_Island" title="Ritter Island">Ritter Island</a> causing a <a href="/wiki/Megatsunami" title="Megatsunami">megatsunami</a>. Almost the entire volcano fell into the ocean, leaving a small <a href="/wiki/Crater_lake" title="Crater lake">crater lake</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Following the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Naval_and_Military_Expeditionary_Force" title="Australian Naval and Military Expeditionary Force">Australian Naval and Military Expeditionary Force</a> seized the islands in 1914 and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> later received a <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations_mandate" title="League of Nations mandate">League of Nations mandate</a> for the islands. They remained under Australian administration—interrupted only by <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> occupation during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>—until Papua New Guinea became independent in September 1975.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismarck_Archipelago&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Bismarck Archipelago includes mostly <a href="/wiki/High_island" title="High island">volcanic islands</a> with a total land area of 49,700 km<sup>2</sup> (19,189 sq mi). The archipelago encompasses the <a href="/wiki/Bismarck_Sea" title="Bismarck Sea">Bismarck Sea</a> and sits upon the <a href="/wiki/North_Bismarck_Plate" title="North Bismarck Plate">North Bismarck Plate</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Manus_Plate" title="Manus Plate">Manus Plate</a> and the <a href="/wiki/South_Bismarck_Plate" title="South Bismarck Plate">South Bismarck Plate</a>.</p>

<p>The passage of water between the islands of New Britain and New Ireland is called <b>St. George's Channel</b> after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._George%27s_Channel" title="St. George's Channel">St. George's Channel</a> in the British Isles between Wales and Ireland.</p>

Blackwell Publishing. <b>154</b> (3): 891. <a href="/wiki/Bibcode" title="Bibcode">Bibcode</a>:<a class="external text" href="http://adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2003GeoJI.154..891W" rel="nofollow">2003GeoJI.154..891W</a>. <a href="/wiki/Digital_object_identifier" title="Digital object identifier">doi</a>:<a class="external text" href="//dx.doi.org/10.1046%2Fj.1365-246X.2003.02016.x" rel="nofollow">10.1046/j.1365-246X.2003.02016.x</a>. <q>In the early morning of 1888 March 13, roughly 5 km<sup>3</sup> of Ritter Island Volcano fell violently into the sea northeast of New Guinea. This event, the largest lateral collapse of an island volcano to be recorded in historical time, flung devastating tsunami tens of metres high on to adjacent shores. Several hundred kilometres away, observers on New Guinea chronicled 3-min period waves up to 8 m high, that lasted for as long as 3 h. These accounts represent the best available first-hand information on tsunami generated by a major volcano lateral collapse. In this article, we simulate the Ritter Island landslide as constrained by a 1985 sonar survey of its debris field and compare predicted tsunami with historical observations. The best agreement occurs for landslides travelling at 40 m/s, but velocities up to 80 m/s cannot be excluded. The Ritter Island debris dropped little more than 800 m vertically and moved slowly compared with landslides that descend into deeper water. Basal friction block models predict that slides with shorter falls should attain lower peak velocities and that 40+ m/s is perfectly compatible with the geometry and runout extent of the Ritter Island landslide. The consensus between theory and observation for the Ritter Island waves increases our confidence in the existence of mega-tsunami produced by oceanic volcano collapses two to three orders of magnitude larger in scale.</q></cite><span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3ABismarck+Archipelago&amp;rft.atitle=Ritter+Island+Volcano+%94Lateral+Collapse+and+the+Tsunami+of+1888&amp;rft.au=Day%2C+Simon&amp;rft.aufirst=Steven+N.&amp;rft.aulast=Ward&amp;rft.date=2003-09&amp;rft.genre=article&amp;rft_id=info%3Abibcode%2F2003GeoJI.154..891W&amp;rft_id=info%3Adoi%2F10.1046%2Fj.1365-246X.2003.02016.x&amp;rft.issue=3&amp;rft.jtitle=Geophysical+Journal+International&amp;rft.pages=891&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Ajournal&amp;rft.volume=154"><span style="display:none;"> </span></span></span></li>
