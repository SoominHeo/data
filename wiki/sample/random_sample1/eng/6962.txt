<p><b>Bianca Cappello</b> (1548 – 20 October 1587) was an Italian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noblewoman" title="Noblewoman">noblewoman</a> who was the mistress, and afterward the second wife, of <a href="/wiki/Francesco_I_de%27_Medici,_Grand_Duke_of_Tuscany" title="Francesco I de' Medici, Grand Duke of Tuscany">Francesco I de' Medici, Grand Duke of Tuscany</a>. Her husband officially made her his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Duchess_of_Tuscany" title="Grand Duchess of Tuscany">consort</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bianca_Cappello&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alessandro_Allori_-_Bianca_Cappello_(Dorotheum).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="283" height="311" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Alessandro_Allori_-_Bianca_Cappello_%28Dorotheum%29.jpg/220px-Alessandro_Allori_-_Bianca_Cappello_%28Dorotheum%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d0/Alessandro_Allori_-_Bianca_Cappello_%28Dorotheum%29.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alessandro_Allori_-_Bianca_Cappello_(Dorotheum).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Portrait by <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Allori" title="Alessandro Allori">Alessandro Allori</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>She was born in Venice, in 1548, the daughter of Bartolomeo Cappello and Pellegrina Morosini, a member of one of the richest and noblest <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venetian</a> families, and was noted for her great beauty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the age of fifteen she fell in love with Pietro Bonaventuri, a young <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florentine</a> clerk in the firm of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salviati_(bankers)" title="Salviati (bankers)">Salviati</a>, and on 28 November 1563 escaped with him to Florence, where they were married. In 1564 she had a daughter named Virginia, or, according to other sources, Pellegrina. The Venetian government made every effort to have Bianca arrested and brought back but the <a href="/wiki/Cosimo_I_de%27_Medici,_Grand_Duke_of_Tuscany" title="Cosimo I de' Medici, Grand Duke of Tuscany">Grand Duke Cosimo I</a> intervened in her favour and she was left unmolested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, she did not get on well with her husband's family, who were very poor and made her do menial work, until at last her beauty attracted <a href="/wiki/Francesco_I_de%27_Medici,_Grand_Duke_of_Tuscany" title="Francesco I de' Medici, Grand Duke of Tuscany">Grand Prince Francesco</a>, son and <a href="/wiki/Heir_apparent" title="Heir apparent">heir apparent</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Grand_duke" title="Grand duke">grand duke</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although already married to <a href="/wiki/Joanna_of_Austria,_Grand_Duchess_of_Tuscany" title="Joanna of Austria, Grand Duchess of Tuscany">Joanna of Austria</a>, Francesco seduced Bianca and gave her jewels, money and other presents. Bonaventuri, Bianca's husband, was given court employment and consoled himself with other ladies until, in 1572, he was murdered in the streets of Florence in consequence of some amorous intrigue. It is possible that Bianca and Francesco were involved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the death of Cosimo in 1574 Francesco succeeded to the <a href="/wiki/Grand_duchy" title="Grand duchy">grand duchy</a>; he now installed Bianca in a palace (now known as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Palazzo_di_Bianca_Cappello&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Palazzo di Bianca Cappello (page does not exist)">Palazzo di Bianca Cappello</a>) close to his own and outraged his wife by flaunting his mistress before her. At this point, Francesco had no legitimate son to inherit the duchy; a child by Bianca, though illegitimate, would be a potential heir, and by extension would secure Bianca's own position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1576 she duly gave birth to Don <a href="/wiki/Antonio_de%27_Medici" title="Antonio de' Medici">Antonio de' Medici</a> (d. 1621), but his father, still hoping to have a legitimate son by his wife, refused to acknowledge him. Francesco and Joanna then produced a son, Grand Prince <a href="/wiki/Philip_de%27_Medici" title="Philip de' Medici">Philip de' Medici</a>, in 1577; the child survived the perilous months of early infancy, and Bianca's hopes of being anything more than a favoured mistress seemed dashed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage">Marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bianca_Cappello&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1578 Joanna died; a few months later Francesco secretly married Bianca, and on 10 June 1579, the marriage was publicly announced, and Antonio acknowledged as the Duke's son. Two days later, on the 12 June, Bianca was crowned the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Duchess_of_Tuscany" title="Grand Duchess of Tuscany">Grand Duchess of Tuscany</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Vecchio" title="Palazzo Vecchio">Palazzo Vecchio</a> in <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>. The Venetian government now put aside its resentment and was officially represented at the magnificent wedding festivities, for it saw in Bianca Cappello an instrument for cementing good relations with <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a>.</p>
<p>Bianca's position, however, was still not secure. The heir remained the young Grand Prince Philip; her own son by Francesco, though acknowledged, remained illegitimate, barred from inheriting the duchy. There would be no more children born of the relationship, and Bianca was aware that, if her husband were to die before her, she was lost, for his family, especially his brother <a href="/wiki/Ferdinando_I_de%27_Medici,_Grand_Duke_of_Tuscany" title="Ferdinando I de' Medici, Grand Duke of Tuscany">Cardinal Ferdinand</a>, hated her bitterly, as an adventuress and interloper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1582, however, Grand Prince Philip died. Francesco immediately began working on securing the succession for his remaining son, Antonio, having him legitimated and declared heir apparent, with the support of Philip II of Spain. As the mother of the heir, Bianca's position was far stronger: even if Francesco died before Antonio reached adulthood, Bianca would have a good claim to ruling as regent on her son's behalf, and her husband's family would give her more respect as the mother of the heir.</p>
<p>In October 1587, at the Villa Medici in <a href="/wiki/Poggio_a_Caiano" title="Poggio a Caiano">Poggio a Caiano</a>, Francesco and Bianca died on 19 and 20 October, possibly poisoned, or as some historians believe, from malarial fever. As Bianca wasn't an official member of the Medici family, Cardinal Ferdinand didn't allow her body to be buried in the Medici family tombs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Instead, Bianca was buried in an unmarked grave under the church of St. Lorenzo, which is located near the site of her death. In 2006, forensic and toxicology experts at the University of Florence reported evidence of arsenic poisoning in a study published in the British Medical Journal,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> but in 2010 evidence of the parasite <i>Plasmodium falciparum</i>, which causes malaria, was found in Francesco's remains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
