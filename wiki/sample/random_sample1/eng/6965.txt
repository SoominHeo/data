<p><b>Villarrica</b> (<span class="nowrap"><small>pronunciation:</small> <span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">VEE</span></span></b>-ə-<b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">REE</span></span></b>-kə</i></a></span>) (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><i>Volcán Villarrica</i></span>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mapudungun_language" title="Mapudungun language">Mapudungun</a>: <span lang="arn" xml:lang="arn"><i>Ruka Pillañ</i></span>) is one of <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>'s most active <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a>, rising above the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Villarrica" title="Lake Villarrica">lake</a> and <a href="/wiki/Villarrica,_Chile" title="Villarrica, Chile">town</a> of the same name, 750 km (470 mi) south of <a href="/wiki/Santiago" title="Santiago">Santiago</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It is also known as <b>Rucapillán</b>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mapudungun" title="Mapudungun">Mapuche</a> word meaning "Pillan's house". It is the westernmost of three large <a href="/wiki/Stratovolcano" title="Stratovolcano">stratovolcanoes</a> that trend NW-SW obliquely perpendicular to the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andean</a> chain along the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mocha-Villarrica_Fault_Zone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mocha-Villarrica Fault Zone (page does not exist)">Mocha-Villarrica Fault Zone</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dzierma_2012_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dzierma_2012-3">[3]</a></sup> along with <a href="/wiki/Quetrupill%C3%A1n" title="Quetrupillán">Quetrupillán</a> and the Chilean portion of <a href="/wiki/Lan%C3%ADn" title="Lanín">Lanín</a>, are protected within <a href="/wiki/Villarrica_National_Park" title="Villarrica National Park">Villarrica National Park</a>. Guided ascents are popular during summer months.</p>
<p>Villarrica, with its <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basaltic" title="Basaltic">basaltic</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andesitic" title="Andesitic">andesitic</a> composition, is one of a small number worldwide known to have an active (but in this case intermittent) <a href="/wiki/Lava_lake" title="Lava lake">lava lake</a> within its <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_crater" title="Volcanic crater">crater</a>. The volcano usually generates <a href="/wiki/Strombolian_eruption" title="Strombolian eruption">strombolian eruptions</a> with ejection of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incandescent" title="Incandescent">incandescent</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyroclast" title="Pyroclast">pyroclasts</a> and lava flows. Rainfall plus melted snow and <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glacier ice</a> can cause massive <a href="/wiki/Lahar" title="Lahar">lahars</a> (mud and debris flows), such as during the eruptions of 1964 and 1971.</p>
<p>Villarrica is one of 9 volcanoes currently monitored by the <a href="/wiki/Deep_Earth_Carbon_Degassing_Project" title="Deep Earth Carbon Degassing Project">Deep Earth Carbon Degassing Project</a>. The project is collecting data on the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulphur dioxide</a> emission rates from <a href="/wiki/Subaerial_eruption" title="Subaerial eruption">subaerial volcanoes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_geology">Geography and geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Villarrica_(volcano)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography and geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Villarica stands in the <a href="/wiki/Chilean_Central_Valley" title="Chilean Central Valley">Chilean Central Valley</a> as the westernmost of an alignment of three large <a href="/wiki/Stratovolcano" title="Stratovolcano">stratovolcanoes</a>. The alignment is attributed to the existence of an old fracture in the crust, the North West-South East trending <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mocha-Villarrica_Fault_Zone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mocha-Villarrica Fault Zone (page does not exist)">Mocha-Villarrica Fault Zone</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dzierma_2012_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dzierma_2012-3">[3]</a></sup> the other volcanoes in the chain, <a href="/wiki/Quetrupill%C3%A1n" title="Quetrupillán">Quetrupillán</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lan%C3%ADn" title="Lanín">Lanín</a>, are far less active. The alignment is unusual as it crosses the N-S running <a href="/wiki/Liqui%C3%B1e-Ofqui_Fault" title="Liquiñe-Ofqui Fault">Liquiñe-Ofqui Fault</a>, along which several active volcanoes are aligned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sernageom_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sernageom-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Villarrica covers an area of 400 km<sup>2</sup> and has an estimated volume of 250 km<sup>3</sup>. It contains volcanic caves and about 26 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scoria_cone" title="Scoria cone">scoria cones</a>. The constant degassing at the lava lake turns the otherwise quite effusive lava more viscous, heightening its explosive potential. Two large <a href="/wiki/Ignimbrite" title="Ignimbrite">ignimbrite</a> layers are visible; the Licán Ignimbrite and the more recent Pucón Ignimbrite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sernageom_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sernageom-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Villarrica emerged during the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Middle Pleistocene</a> and grew forming a large stratocone of similar dimensions to the current edifice. 100,000 years ago during the <a href="/wiki/Eemian" title="Eemian">Valdivia Interglacial</a> the ancestral Villarrica collapsed following an eruption and formed a large elliptical <a href="/wiki/Caldera" title="Caldera">caldera</a> of 6.5 and 4.2 km in diameter. During the <a href="/wiki/Llanquihue_glaciation" title="Llanquihue glaciation">Llanquihue glaciation</a> Villarrica produced <a href="/wiki/Pyroclastic_flow" title="Pyroclastic flow">pyroclastic flow</a> deposits, subglacial <a href="/wiki/Andesite" title="Andesite">andesite</a> lavas and <a href="/wiki/Dacite" title="Dacite">dacite</a> dykes. It collapsed once again 13,700 years ago forming a new smaller caldera, among other pyroclastic flows the Licán Ignimbrite has been related to this event. Beginning with the Licán Ignimbrite, generated just after the last deglaciation, activity continued in similar fashion. The Pucón Ignimbrite was ejected during a minor collapse of the uppermost stratocone 3,700 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glaciers">Glaciers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Villarrica_(volcano)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Glaciers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The upper part of Villarrica is permanently covered by snow and has some 40 km<sup>2</sup> of <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>, the largest of which is the Pichillancahue-Turbio Glacier situated on its southeastern flank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Ash from the eruptions can increase the <a href="/wiki/Ablation" title="Ablation">ablation</a> of snow and ice by absorption of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a>. Some ash coverings are thicker than 5 <a href="/wiki/Centimetre" title="Centimetre">cm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">insulate</a> the glacier, decreasing ablation instead of enhancing it. Between 1961 and 2003, Villarrica lost 25% of its glaciated surface and the glaciers shrank at an average rate of -0.4 km<sup>2</sup> each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Villarrica_(volcano)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Villarrica is popular for climbing with guided hikes to the crater from the town of Pucón, but these may be suspended due to cloud or volcanic activity. Helicopter over-flights have been available since 2007. In the winter (July–September) a ski resort operates on the northern slopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eruptive_history">Eruptive history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Villarrica_(volcano)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Eruptive history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Villarrica is one of Chile's most active volcanoes; eruptions have been recorded since the <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Chile" title="Conquest of Chile">conquest of Chile</a> and the founding of the city of <a href="/wiki/Villarrica,_Chile" title="Villarrica, Chile">Villarrica</a> in 1552. There are uncertainties in the eruptive record in the first half of the 17th century due to the <a href="/wiki/Mapuche" title="Mapuche">Mapuche</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huilliche" title="Huilliche">Huilliche</a> uprising which led to the surrender or abandonment of Spanish settlements during the <a href="/wiki/Destruction_of_the_Seven_Cities" title="Destruction of the Seven Cities">destruction of the Seven Cities</a>. The earliest recorded eruption occurred in 1558, followed by at least 65 others with assumed <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_Explosivity_Index" title="Volcanic Explosivity Index">Volcanic Explosivity Indexes</a> of 1-3.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1964_eruption">1964 eruption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Villarrica_(volcano)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1964 eruption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Villarica_Volcano_(aerial_view)1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1360" data-file-width="2048" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Villarica_Volcano_%28aerial_view%291.jpg/240px-Villarica_Volcano_%28aerial_view%291.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Villarica_Volcano_%28aerial_view%291.jpg/360px-Villarica_Volcano_%28aerial_view%291.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Villarica_Volcano_%28aerial_view%291.jpg/480px-Villarica_Volcano_%28aerial_view%291.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Villarica_Volcano_(aerial_view)1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Aerial view of Mount Villarrica.</div>

<p>In the two last weeks of February 1964 Villarrica produced small, violent lava effusions and tremors. On 1 March, at 2:45 am, it begun a <a href="/wiki/Strombolian_eruption" title="Strombolian eruption">strombolian eruption</a>, and residents of <a href="/wiki/Co%C3%B1aripe" title="Coñaripe">Coñaripe</a>, a wood-logging town, fled to the surrounding hills. Melting snow and ice from the Pichillancahue-Turbio Glacier combined with heavy rainfall to produce several <a href="/wiki/Lahar" title="Lahar">lahars</a>. Coñaripe, on the north-western shores of <a href="/wiki/Calafqu%C3%A9n_Lake" title="Calafquén Lake">Calafquén Lake</a>, had about half of its buildings destroyed, necessitating reconstruction further east.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1971_eruption">1971 eruption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Villarrica_(volcano)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1971 eruption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 29 October 1971, at 4:00 am, there were several explosions in the crater and a column of black and white smoke appeared. One month later, at quarter to midnight on 29 November, Villarrica begun a new eruptive cycle. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyroclast" title="Pyroclast">Pyroclasts</a> were ejected and the snow surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_crater" title="Volcanic crater">crater</a> melted. After a brief halt in activity lava pored from a new vent on the north-east side, melting considerable amounts of water and causing <a href="/wiki/Lahar" title="Lahar">lahars</a>. These lahars affected Pichaye, El Turbio, Collentañe, Minetué, Molco, Voipir and <a href="/wiki/Curarrehue" title="Curarrehue">Curarrehue</a>, all of them in the <a href="/wiki/Trancura_River" title="Trancura River">Trancura River</a> basin. Toxic <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_gas" title="Volcanic gas">sulphurous gases</a> made the air unbreathable in <a href="/wiki/Villarrica,_Chile" title="Villarrica, Chile">Villarrica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Puc%C3%B3n" title="Pucón">Pucón</a> where at least 15 deaths were reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>Approximately at 03:01 AM on March 3, 2015, it erupted,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biobiochile20150303_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-biobiochile20150303-14">[14]</a></sup> raising the alert to red up to 10 kilometers around. The eruption exceeded the height of the volcano, causing a loud explosion that woke the neighbors, and forcing the evacuation of 3,385 people in nearby communities.</p>
<p>On February 7, 2015, Villarrica triggered a yellow alert at both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Servicio_Nacional_de_Geolog%C3%ADa_y_Miner%C3%ADa" title="Servicio Nacional de Geología y Minería">Servicio Nacional de Geología y Minería</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/ONEMI" title="ONEMI">Onemi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Minor explosions and low emissions of ashes were visible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Ten days later, another eruption occurred. The National Service of Geology and Mining (Sernageomin) declared a yellow alert after volcanic and seismic activity increased again, while the National Emergency Office of the Interior Ministry (ONEMI) kept the yellow alert. The Southern Andean Volcano Observatory (OVDAS) indicated that a new imminent eruption would continue until 12:30 of March 19, 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> As of July 2015,the eruption is continuing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Volcanic gas emissions from this volcano are measured by a <a href="/wiki/Multi-Component_Gas_Analyzer_System" title="Multi-Component Gas Analyzer System">Multi-Component Gas Analyzer System</a>, which detects pre-eruptive degassing of rising magmas, improving <a href="/wiki/Prediction_of_volcanic_activity" title="Prediction of volcanic activity">prediction of volcanic activity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
