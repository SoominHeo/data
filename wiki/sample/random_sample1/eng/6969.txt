<p><b>Vientiane</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/j/ 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/j/ 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[vjɛ̃ˈtjan]</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Lao_language" title="Lao language">Lao</a>: <span lang="lo" xml:lang="lo"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E0%BA%A7%E0%BA%BD%E0%BA%87%E0%BA%88%E0%BA%B1%E0%BA%99" title="wikt:ວຽງຈັນ">ວຽງຈັນ</a></span>, <i>Viang chan</i>, <small>IPA: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Thai_and_Lao" title="Help:IPA for Thai and Lao">[wíəŋ tɕàn]</a></span>) is the <a href="/wiki/Capital_city" title="Capital city">capital</a> and largest city of <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a>, on the banks of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mekong_River" title="Mekong River">Mekong River</a> near the border with <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>. Vientiane became the capital in 1563 due to fears of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">Burmese</a> invasion, but ironically, the city was razed and looted to the ground in 1827 by the Siamese (Thai).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hist_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-hist-2">[2]</a></sup> Vientiane was the administrative capital during French rule and, due to economic growth in recent times, is now the economic centre of Laos.</p>
<p>The estimated population of the city is 760,000 (2015). The city hosted the <a href="/wiki/2009_Southeast_Asian_Games" title="2009 Southeast Asian Games">25th Southeast Asian Games</a> in December 2009 celebrating the 50 years of <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asian_Games" title="Southeast Asian Games">Southeast Asian Games</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vientiane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name of the city is derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P%C4%81li" title="Pāli">Pali</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liturgical_language" title="Liturgical language">liturgical language</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theravada_Buddhism" title="Theravada Buddhism">Theravada Buddhism</a>. Although the original meaning of the name of the city is "city of sandalwood", as shown by ancient Lao inscription which wrote according to etymology, unlike modern Lao which is written phonetically, in modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lao_Language" title="Lao Language">Lao</a>, the meaning of the name Vientiane is ambiguous.</p>
<p>Many, if not most, Lao people claim that the city's name means "city of the moon", while many also claim correctly that the city's name means "city of sandalwood" because the words for "moon" (<span lang="lo" xml:lang="lo">ຈັນ</span> or <span lang="lo" xml:lang="lo">ຈັນທຣ໌</span> from <i>chandra</i> <span lang="sa" xml:lang="sa">चन्द्र</span> in <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>) and "<a href="/wiki/Sandalwood" title="Sandalwood">sandalwood</a>" (ຈັນ or ຈັນທນ໌ from <i>chandana</i> <span lang="sa" xml:lang="sa">चन्दन</span> in Sanskrit) are written and pronounced identically as "chan" in modern Lao. Most academic and historic Lao sources claim that the city's name does in fact mean "city of sandalwood", reinforced by the city's <a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a> (<span lang="th" xml:lang="th">เวียงจันทน์</span>) and Khmer (<span lang="km" xml:lang="km">វៀងចន្ទន៍</span>) names both retain the etymological spelling, which indicates "city of sandalwood".</p>
<p>The romanised spelling "Vientiane" is of <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> origin, and reflects the difficulty the French had in pronouncing the hard "ch" sound in the Lao language. A common English-based spelling is "Viangchan", or occasionally "Wiangchan".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vientiane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The great Laotian epic, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phra_Lak_Phra_Lam" title="Phra Lak Phra Lam">Phra Lak Phra Lam</a>, claims that Prince Thattaradtha founded the city when he left the legendary Lao kingdom of <i>Muong Inthapatha Maha Nakhone</i> because he was denied the throne in favor of his younger brother. Thattaradtha founded a city called <i>Maha Thani Si Phan Phao</i> on the western banks of the <a href="/wiki/Mekong" title="Mekong">Mekong River</a>; this city was said to have later become today's <a href="/wiki/Udon_Thani" title="Udon Thani">Udon Thani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>. One day, a seven-headed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naga_(mythology)" title="Naga (mythology)">Naga</a> told Thattaradtha to start a new city on the east bank of the river opposite <i>Maha Thani Si Phan Phao</i>. The prince called this city <i>Chanthabuly Si Sattanakhanahud</i>; which was said to be the predecessor of modern Vientiane.</p>
<p>Contrary to the Phra Lak Phra Lam, most historians believe Vientiane was an early <a href="/wiki/Khmer_Empire" title="Khmer Empire">Khmer</a> settlement centered around a <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> temple, which the Pha That Luang would later replace. In the 11th and 12th centuries, the time when the <a href="/wiki/Lao_people" title="Lao people">Lao</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thai_people" title="Thai people">Thai people</a> are believed to have entered <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_China" title="Southern China">Southern China</a>, the few remaining Khmers in the area were either killed, removed, or assimilated into the Lao civilization, which would soon overtake the area.</p>
<p>In 1354, when <a href="/wiki/Fa_Ngum" title="Fa Ngum">Fa Ngum</a> founded the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Lan_Xang" title="Lan Xang">Lan Xang</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coedes_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coedes-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:223</sup> Viangchan became an important administrative city, even though it was not made the capital. King <a href="/wiki/Setthathirath" title="Setthathirath">Setthathirath</a> officially established it as the capital of Lan Xang in 1563, to avoid Burmese invasion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> When Lan Xang fell apart in 1707, it became an independent <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Vientiane" title="Kingdom of Vientiane">Kingdom of Vientiane</a>. In 1779, it was conquered by the Siamese general Phraya <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddha_Yodfa_Chulaloke" title="Buddha Yodfa Chulaloke">Chakri</a> and made a vassal of <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Siam</a>.</p>
<p>When King <a href="/wiki/Anouvong" title="Anouvong">Anouvong</a> raised an unsuccessful rebellion, it was obliterated by <a href="/wiki/Thai_people" title="Thai people">Siamese</a> armies in 1827. The city was burned to the ground and was looted of nearly all Laotian artifacts, including Buddha statues and people. Viangchan was in great disrepair, depopulated and disappearing into the forest, when the French arrived. It eventually passed to <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> rule in 1893. It became the capital of the French protectorate of Laos in 1899. The French rebuilt the city and rebuilt or repaired Buddhist temples such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pha_That_Luang" title="Pha That Luang">Pha That Luang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haw_Phra_Kaew" title="Haw Phra Kaew">Haw Phra Kaew</a>, and left many colonial buildings behind.</p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Viangchan fell with little resistance and was occupied by Japanese forces, under the command of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sako_Masanori&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sako Masanori (page does not exist)">Sako Masanori</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-5">[5]</a></sup> On 9 March 1945 French paratroopers arrived, and reoccupied the city on 24 April 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the <a href="/wiki/Laotian_Civil_War" title="Laotian Civil War">Laotian Civil War</a> broke out between the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Lao_Government" title="Royal Lao Government">Royal Lao Government</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pathet_Lao" title="Pathet Lao">Pathet Lao</a>, Vientiane became unstable. In August 1960, <a href="/wiki/Kong_Le" title="Kong Le">Kong Le</a> seized the capital and insisted that <a href="/wiki/Souvanna_Phouma" title="Souvanna Phouma">Souvanna Phouma</a> become prime minister. In mid-December, <a href="/wiki/Phoumi_Nosavan" title="Phoumi Nosavan">Phoumi Nosavan</a> then seized the capital, overthrew the Phouma Government, and installed <a href="/wiki/Boun_Oum" title="Boun Oum">Boun Oum</a> as prime minister. In mid-1975, Pathet Lao troops moved towards the city and Americans began evacuating the capital. On 23 August 1975, a contingent of 50 Pathet Lao women symbolically liberated the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6">[6]</a></sup> On 2 December 1975, the <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communist</a> party of the <a href="/wiki/Pathet_Lao" title="Pathet Lao">Pathet Lao</a> took over Vientiane, defeated the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Laos" title="Kingdom of Laos">Kingdom of Laos</a>, and renamed the country the Lao People's Democratic Republic, which ended the <a href="/wiki/Laotian_Civil_War" title="Laotian Civil War">Laotian Civil War</a>. The next day, an <a href="/wiki/Insurgency_in_Laos" title="Insurgency in Laos">Insurgency in Laos</a> began in the jungle, with the Pathet Lao fighting factions of Hmong and royalists.</p>
<p>Vientiane was the host of the incident-free <a href="/wiki/2009_Southeast_Asian_Games" title="2009 Southeast Asian Games">2009 Southeast Asian Games</a>. Eighteen competitions were dropped from the previous games held in Thailand, due to Laos' landlocked borders and the lack of adequate facilities in Vientiane.</p>

<p>Vientiane is on a bend of the <a href="/wiki/Mekong" title="Mekong">Mekong</a> River, at which point it forms the border with <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vientiane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Vientiane features a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_wet_and_dry_climate" title="Tropical wet and dry climate">tropical wet and dry climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen</a> <i>Aw</i>) with a distinct <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon season</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">dry season</a>. Vientiane’s dry season spans from November through March. April marks the onset of the monsoon which in Vientiane lasts about seven months. Vientiane tends to be hot and humid throughout the course of the year, though temperatures in the city tend to be somewhat cooler during the dry season than the wet season.</p>

<p>Although still a small city, the capital experiences a major influx of tourists. The city contains many temples and Buddhist monuments with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pha_That_Luang" title="Pha That Luang">Pha That Luang</a>, a Buddhist stupa, one of the most famous in Laos. It is the most important national cultural monument and very popular amongst foreign tourists. The original was built in 1566 by King <a href="/wiki/Setthathirath" title="Setthathirath">Setthathirath</a>, and was restored in 1953. The golden stupa is 45 metres tall and is believed to contain a relic of the Lord Buddha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another site that is also popular amongst tourists is <a href="/wiki/Wat_Si_Muang" title="Wat Si Muang">Wat Si Muang</a>. The temple was built on the ruins of a Khmer Hindu shrine, the remains of which can be seen behind the ordination hall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-12">[12]</a></sup> It was built in 1563 and is believed to be guarded by the spirit of a local girl called “Si". Legend says that Nang Si, who was pregnant at the time, leapt to her death as a sacrifice, just as the pillar was being lowered into the hole. In front of the temple stands a statue of King <a href="/wiki/Sisavang_Vong" title="Sisavang Vong">Sisavang Vong</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The memorial monument, <a href="/wiki/Patuxai" title="Patuxai">Patuxai</a>, began construction in 1957 and completed in 1968, is perhaps the most prominent landmark in the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-11">[11]</a></sup> While the <a href="/wiki/Arc_de_Triomphe" title="Arc de Triomphe">Arc de Triomphe</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> inspired the architecture, the design incorporates typical Lao motifs including “Kinnari”, a mythical bird woman. Energetic visitors can climb to the top of the monument, which reveals an panoramic view of the city.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Buddha_Park" title="Buddha Park">Buddha Park</a> was built in 1958 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luang_Pu_Bunleua_Sulilat" title="Luang Pu Bunleua Sulilat">Luang Pu Bunleua Sulilat</a> and contains a collection of Buddhist and Hindu sculptures, scattered amongst gardens and trees. The park was built about 28 kilometres south of Vientiane at the edge of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mekong_River" title="Mekong River">Mekong River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Vientiane is home to one of the three bowling alleys in Laos (the other two are in <a href="/wiki/Luang_Prabang" title="Luang Prabang">Luang Prabang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pakse" title="Pakse">Pakse</a>). There are many upper-class hotels in Vientiane.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vientiane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vientiane is the driving force behind economic change in Laos. In recent years, the city has experienced rapid economic growth from foreign investment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Lao_Securities_Exchange" title="Lao Securities Exchange">stock exchange</a> opened with two listed company stocks, with the cooperation of <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Thai%E2%80%93Lao_Friendship_Bridge" title="Thai–Lao Friendship Bridge">First Thai-Lao Friendship Bridge</a>, built in the 1990s, crosses the river 18 kilometres downstream of the city of <a href="/wiki/Nong_Khai" title="Nong Khai">Nong Khai</a> in Thailand, and is the major crossing between the two countries. The official name of the bridge was changed in 2007 by the addition of "First", after the Second Friendship Bridge linking <a href="/wiki/Mukdahan" title="Mukdahan">Mukdahan</a> in Thailand with <a href="/wiki/Savannakhet_Province" title="Savannakhet Province">Savannakhet</a> in Laos was opened early in 2007.</p>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metre_gauge" title="Metre gauge">metre gauge</a> railway link over the bridge was formally inaugurated on 5 March 2009, ending at <a href="/wiki/Thanaleng_Railway_Station" title="Thanaleng Railway Station">Thanaleng Railway Station</a>, in Dongphosy village (<a href="/wiki/Vientiane_Prefecture" title="Vientiane Prefecture">Vientiane Prefecture</a>), 20 km east of Vientiane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian-09_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-09-22">[22]</a></sup> As of November 2010, Lao officials plan to convert the station into a rail cargo terminal for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freight_trains" title="Freight trains">freight trains</a>, allowing cargo to be transported from Bangkok into Laos at a lower cost than would be possible with road transport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trweekly-nov10_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-trweekly-nov10-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="To_Thailand">To Thailand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vientiane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: To Thailand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Daily non-stop bus services run between Vientiane and Nong Khai, Udon Thani, and Khon Kaen.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="From_China">From China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vientiane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: From China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In October 2010, plans were announced for a 530 km high-speed railway linking Vientiane to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xishuangbanna" title="Xishuangbanna">Xishuangbanna</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a> Province in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> which was later modified to a high speed train from Boten to Vientiane with total distance of 421.243 km to be served by 21 stations including 5 major stations passing through 165 bridges (total length of 92.6 km) and 69 tunnels (total length of 186.9 km)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Construction on this line—as part of the longer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kunming_to_Singapore_Railway" title="Kunming to Singapore Railway">Kunming to Singapore Railway</a> began on 25 April 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-People-Daily_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-People-Daily-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_air">By air</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vientiane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: By air">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Vientiane is served by <a href="/wiki/Wattay_International_Airport" title="Wattay International Airport">Wattay International Airport</a> with international connections to other Asian countries. <a href="/wiki/Lao_Airlines" title="Lao Airlines">Lao Airlines</a> has regular flights to several domestic destinations in the country (including several flights daily to <a href="/wiki/Luang_Prabang" title="Luang Prabang">Luang Prabang</a>, plus a few flights weekly to other local destinations).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In Thailand, <a href="/wiki/Udon_Thani_International_Airport" title="Udon Thani International Airport">Udon Thani International Airport</a>, one of Wattay's main connections, is less than 90 km distant.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Healthcare">Healthcare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vientiane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Healthcare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The "Centre Medical de l'Ambassade de France" is available to the foreign community in Laos. The <a href="/wiki/Mahosot_Hospital" title="Mahosot Hospital">Mahosot Hospital</a> is an important local hospital in treating and researching diseases and is connected with the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a>. In 2011 the Alliance Clinic opened near the airport, with a connection to Thai hospitals. The Setthathirat International Clinic has foreign doctors. A free, 24/7 ambulance service is provided by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vientiane_Rescue&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vientiane Rescue (page does not exist)">Vientiane Rescue</a>, a volunteer-run rescue service established in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

Both Israel and Palestinians claim Jerusalem as capital. <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> contains the Israeli parliament and almost all Israeli government ministries. <a href="/wiki/Tel_Aviv" title="Tel Aviv">Tel Aviv</a> contains most foreign embassies in Israel; <a href="/wiki/Ramallah" title="Ramallah">Ramallah</a> is the administrative seat of the Palestinian Authority.<br/>
<b>+</b> According to the 1947 <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Republic_of_China" title="Constitution of the Republic of China">Constitution of the Republic of China</a>, Nanjing is the official capital of the ROC, with Taipei as the <a href="/wiki/Temporary_capital" title="Temporary capital">seat of government</a>. See also <a href="/wiki/Political_status_of_Taiwan" title="Political status of Taiwan">Political status of Taiwan</a>.</div>
