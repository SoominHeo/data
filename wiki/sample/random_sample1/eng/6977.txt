<p><i><b>Torreya</b></i> is a genus of <a href="/wiki/Pinophyta" title="Pinophyta">conifers</a> comprising six or seven species placed the family <a href="/wiki/Taxaceae" title="Taxaceae">Taxaceae</a>, though sometimes formerly placed in <a href="/wiki/Cephalotaxaceae" title="Cephalotaxaceae">Cephalotaxaceae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christenhusz2011_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christenhusz2011-2">[2]</a></sup> Four species are native to eastern <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>; the other two are native to <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. They are small to medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">evergreen</a> <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">trees</a> reaching 5–20 m, rarely 25 m, tall. Common names include <b>nutmeg yew</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> are spirally arranged on the shoots, but twisted at the base to lie in two flat ranks; they are linear, 2–8 cm long and 3–4 mm broad, hard in texture, with a sharp spine tip.</p>
<p><i>Torreya</i> can be either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_sexuality" title="Plant sexuality">monoecious</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_sexuality" title="Plant sexuality">dioecious</a>; when monoecious, the male and female <a href="/wiki/Conifer_cone" title="Conifer cone">cones</a> are often on different branches. The male (<a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a>) cones are 5–8 mm long, grouped in lines along the underside of a shoot. The female (<a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seed</a>) cones are single or grouped two to eight together on a short stem; minute at first, they mature in about 18 months to a <a href="/wiki/Drupe" title="Drupe">drupe</a>-like structure with the single large <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nut</a>-like seed 2–4 cm long surrounded by a fleshy covering, green to purple at full maturity. In some species, notably the <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Torreya_nucifera" title="Torreya nucifera">Torreya nucifera</a></i> ('kaya'), the seed is edible. Natural dispersal is thought to be aided by <a href="/wiki/Squirrel" title="Squirrel">squirrels</a> which bury the seeds for a winter food source; any seeds left uneaten are then able to germinate.</p>
<p>The genus is named after the American botanist <a href="/wiki/John_Torrey" title="John Torrey">John Torrey</a>.</p>

<p><i><a href="/wiki/Torreya_californica" title="Torreya californica">Torreya californica</a></i> (California torreya) is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemic_(ecology)" title="Endemic (ecology)">endemic</a> in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. It is the largest species, reaching 25 m tall. One species, <i><a href="/wiki/Torreya_clarnensis" title="Torreya clarnensis">T. clarnensis</a></i>, has been described from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Eocene" title="Middle Eocene">Middle Eocene</a> fossils found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clarno_Formation" title="Clarno Formation">Clarno Formation</a> of Central Oregon, USA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manchester1994_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Manchester1994-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Torreya_taxifolia" title="Torreya taxifolia">T. taxifolia</a></i> (Florida torreya or gopher wood) has a restricted habitat within <a href="/wiki/Torreya_State_Park" title="Torreya State Park">Torreya State Park</a>, along the east bank of the <a href="/wiki/Apalachicola_River" title="Apalachicola River">Apalachicola River</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a> and immediately adjacent southernmost <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>.</p>
