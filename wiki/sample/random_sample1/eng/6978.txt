<p><b>Ladan Bijani</b> and <b>Laleh Bijani</b> (in <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">لادن و لاله بیژنی</span>‎) (January 17, 1974 – July 8, 2003) were <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iranian</a> <a href="/wiki/Craniopagus_twins" title="Craniopagus twins">conjoined twin</a> sisters. They were joined at the head and died immediately after their complicated <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgical</a> separation. Coincidentally, the twins were born 100 years to the day of the deaths of <a href="/wiki/Chang_and_Eng_Bunker" title="Chang and Eng Bunker">Chang and Eng Bunker</a>, also conjoined twins, famously known as the "original" Siamese twins.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ladan_and_Laleh_Bijani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>They were born in <a href="/wiki/Firuzabad,_Fars" title="Firuzabad, Fars">Firuzabad</a>, a city in southwest <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, to Dadollah Bijani and Maryam Safari, members of a farming family from the nearby <a href="/wiki/Lohrasb" title="Lohrasb">Lohrasb</a> village. The Bijani sisters were lost in a hospital in 1979 after the doctors responsible for them had to suddenly leave for the United States during the <a href="/wiki/Iranian_Revolution" title="Iranian Revolution">revolution in Iran</a>. The Bijanis' parents did not find the sisters again until several years later in the city of <a href="/wiki/Karaj" title="Karaj">Karaj</a> near <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">Tehran</a>, where Dr. Alireza Safaian had adopted them. Even though Dadollah Bijani won custody against Safaian, the sisters chose to spend their childhood with Safaian.</p>
<p>Ladan wanted to be a lawyer, while Laleh wished to become a journalist; in the end, they settled on Ladan's choice. They studied law for four years at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tehran_University" title="Tehran University">Tehran University</a>. Most other personal decisions also had to meet each other's approval. For these and other reasons, they had wanted to be separated since they were children. Laleh hoped that she could then move to Tehran, the capital city of Iran, to study journalism, while Ladan wanted to continue with graduate studies in law and then move to <a href="/wiki/Shiraz" title="Shiraz">Shiraz</a>.</p>
<p>In addition, the sisters had different hobbies. While Laleh liked to, among other things, play computer games, Ladan preferred computer programming. Ladan also described Laleh as more introverted and herself quite talkative.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Separation">Separation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ladan_and_Laleh_Bijani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Separation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1996, they traveled to <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, trying to convince doctors there to separate them; the German doctors however declined to operate, saying that the risk of separation surgery would be too high for both of them.</p>
<p>In November 2002, after meeting Dr. <a href="/wiki/Keith_Goh" title="Keith Goh">Keith Goh</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singaporean</a> neurosurgeon who successfully separated the Shrestha sisters from Nepal (Ganga and Jamuna), who had previously also been joined at the head, the Bijani sisters travelled to Singapore to undergo the controversial operation. Even though they were warned by the doctors that the surgery to separate them would still be very risky, the sisters were very determined. Their decision to go ahead with the operation caused an international media blitz.</p>
<p>After eight months in the Southeast Asian country doing extensive <a href="/wiki/Psychiatry" title="Psychiatry">psychiatric</a> and legal evaluations, they underwent surgery on July 6, 2003, under the care of a large team of international specialists at <a href="/wiki/Raffles_Hospital" title="Raffles Hospital">Raffles Hospital</a>, composed of 28 surgeons (including neurosurgeon <a href="/wiki/Ben_Carson" title="Ben Carson">Ben Carson</a>) and more than 100 support staff working in shifts. A specially-designed chair was required that allowed the operation to be performed with both sisters in a sitting position.</p>
<p>The attempt to separate the twins turned out to be very difficult, because their <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brains</a> not only shared a major <a href="/wiki/Vein" title="Vein">vein</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Superior_sagittal_sinus" title="Superior sagittal sinus">superior sagittal sinus</a>), but had fused together. The separation was achieved on July 8, 2003, but it was announced then that the twins were in critical condition, both having lost a large volume of <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> due to complications of the operation.</p>
<p>The separation stage of the surgery completed at 13:30 (Singapore time, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a> +8), but there was significant blood loss during the blood vessel repairing process, and Ladan Bijani died at around 14:30 on the operating table; her sister Laleh died about 90 minutes later. Their deaths were announced by the chairman of Raffles Hospital, Dr. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Loo_Choon_Yong&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Loo Choon Yong (page does not exist)">Loo Choon Yong</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Burials">Burials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ladan_and_Laleh_Bijani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Burials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The sisters were buried in accordance to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiite" title="Shiite">Shiite</a> Muslim traditions in separate <a href="/wiki/Tomb" title="Tomb">tombs</a>, side by side, in <a href="/wiki/Lohrasb" title="Lohrasb">Lohrasb</a>. The sisters willed their property to blind and orphaned children.</p>
<p>A documentary about the operation was broadcast soon after. Later, people named January 17 as Hope's day.</p>
