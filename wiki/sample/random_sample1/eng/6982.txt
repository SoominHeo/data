<p>A <b>visual novel</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">ビジュアルノベル</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>bijuaru noberu</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interactive" title="Interactive">interactive</a> game,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> featuring mostly static graphics, most often using <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a>-style art or occasionally live-action stills (and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Interactive_movie" title="Interactive movie">video footage</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1up_novel_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-1up_novel-3">[3]</a></sup> As the name might suggest, they resemble mixed-media novels. <b>Sound novels</b> and <b>kinetic novels</b> are types of visual novels, although visual novels have its own distinction between the others.</p>
<p>In Japanese terminology, a distinction is often made between visual novels (abbreviated NVL, derived from visual NoVeL), which consist predominantly of narration and have very few interactive elements, and adventure games (abbreviated AVG, or ADV derived from ADVenture), a form of <a href="/wiki/Adventure_game" title="Adventure game">adventure game</a> which may incorporate problem-solving and other types of gameplay. This distinction is normally lost outside Japan, where both NVLs and ADVs are commonly referred to as "visual novels" by international fans. Visual novels and ADVs are especially prevalent in Japan, where they made up nearly 70% of the <a href="/wiki/PC_game" title="PC game">PC game</a> titles released in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Visual novels are often produced for <a href="/wiki/Video_game_console" title="Video game console">video game consoles</a>, and the more popular games have occasionally been <a href="/wiki/Porting" title="Porting">ported</a> to such systems. The more famous visual novels are also often adapted into the <a href="/wiki/Light_novel" title="Light novel">light novel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a> or <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a> formats. The market for visual novels outside of East Asia is small, though a number of <a href="/wiki/List_of_anime_based_on_video_games" title="List of anime based on video games">anime based on visual novels</a> are popular among anime fans in the Western world.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gameplay">Gameplay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_novel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gameplay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Visual novels are distinguished from other game types by their generally minimal gameplay. Typically the majority of player interaction is limited to clicking to keep the text, graphics and sound moving (many recent games offer "play" or "fast-forward" toggles that make this unnecessary), while making narrative choices along the way.</p>
<p>Most visual novels have multiple storylines and more than one ending; the mechanic in these cases typically consists of intermittent multiple-choice decision points, where the player selects a direction in which to take the game. This style of gameplay is similar to story-driven <a href="/wiki/Interactive_fiction" title="Interactive fiction">interactive fiction</a>, or the shorter and less detailed real-life <a href="/wiki/Gamebook" title="Gamebook">gamebook</a> books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-klug_194-7_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-klug_194-7-5">[5]</a></sup> Many fans of visual novels hold them up as exceptions to the relatively weak storytelling in video games overall.</p>
<p>Some visual novels do not limit themselves into merely interactive fictions, but also incorporate other elements into them. An example of this is <i><a href="/wiki/Symphonic_Rain" title="Symphonic Rain">Symphonic Rain</a></i>, where the player is required to play a musical instrument of some sort, and attain a good score in order to advance. Usually such an element is related as a <a href="/wiki/Plot_device" title="Plot device">plot device</a> in the game.</p>
<p>Some shorter works do not contain any decision points at all. Most examples of this sort are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doujinshi" title="Doujinshi">fan-created</a>. Fan-created novel games are reasonably popular; there are a number of free game engines and construction kits aimed at making them easy to construct, most notably <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NScripter" title="NScripter">NScripter</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KiriKiri" title="KiriKiri">KiriKiri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ren%27Py" title="Ren'Py">Ren'Py</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-renpy_hp_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-renpy_hp-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many visual novels use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seiy%C5%AB" title="Seiyū">voice actors</a> to provide voices for the characters in the game. Often, the protagonist is left unvoiced, even when the rest of the characters are fully voiced. This is to aid the player in identifying with the protagonist and to avoid having to record large amounts of dialogue, as the main character typically has the most speaking lines due to the branching nature of visual novels.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Branching_narratives">Branching narratives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_novel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Branching narratives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nonlinear_gameplay" title="Nonlinear gameplay">Non-linear</a> branching storylines are a common trend in visual novels, which frequently use multiple branching storylines to achieve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multiple_endings" title="Multiple endings">multiple different endings</a>, allowing non-linear freedom of choice along the way. Decision points within a visual novel often present players with the option of altering the course of events during the game, leading to many different possible outcomes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> An acclaimed example is <i><a href="/wiki/Zero_Escape:_Virtue%27s_Last_Reward" title="Zero Escape: Virtue's Last Reward">Zero Escape: Virtue's Last Reward</a></i>, where nearly every action and dialogue choice can lead to entirely new branching paths and endings. Each path only reveals certain aspects of the overall storyline and it is only after uncovering all the possible different paths and outcomes, through multiple playthroughs, that everything comes together to form a coherent well-written story.</p>
<p>The branching path stories found in visual novels represent an evolution of the <i>Choose Your Own Adventure</i> concept. The digital medium allows significant improvements, such as being able to fully explore multiple aspects and perspectives of a story. Another improvement is having hidden decision points that are automatically determined based on the player's past decisions. In <i><a href="/wiki/Fate/stay_night" title="Fate/stay night">Fate/stay night</a></i>, for example, the way the <a href="/wiki/Player_character" title="Player character">player character</a> behaved towards <a href="/wiki/Non-player_character" title="Non-player character">non-player characters</a> during the course of the game affects the way they react to the player character in later scenes, such as whether or not they choose to help in life-or-death situations. This would be far more difficult to track with physical books. More importantly, visual novels do not face the same length restrictions as a physical book. For example, the total word count of the English <a href="/wiki/Fan_translation" title="Fan translation">fan translation</a> of <i>Fate/stay night</i>, taking all the branching paths into account, exceeds that of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings" title="The Lord of the Rings">The Lord of the Rings</a></i>. This significant increase in length allows visual novels to tell stories as long and complex as those often found in traditional novels, while still maintaining a branching path structure, and allowing them to focus on deep stories with mature themes and consistent plots in a way which <i>Choose Your Own Adventure</i> books were unable to do due to their physical limitations. Visual novels with non-branching plots, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Higurashi_When_They_Cry" title="Higurashi When They Cry">Higurashi When They Cry</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Planetarian:_The_Reverie_of_a_Little_Planet" title="Planetarian: The Reverie of a Little Planet">Planetarian: The Reverie of a Little Planet</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muv-Luv_Alternative" title="Muv-Luv Alternative">Muv-Luv Alternative</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Digital:_A_Love_Story" title="Digital: A Love Story">Digital: A Love Story</a></i> are rare exceptions within the genre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-klug_194-7_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-klug_194-7-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-renpy_hp_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-renpy_hp-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many visual novels often revolve almost entirely around character interactions and <a href="/wiki/Dialog_tree" title="Dialog tree">dialogue choices</a>, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Ace_Attorney" title="Ace Attorney">Ace Attorney</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Tokimeki_Memorial" title="Tokimeki Memorial">Tokimeki Memorial</a></i>, usually featuring complex branching dialogues and often presenting the player's possible responses word-for-word as the <a href="/wiki/Player_character" title="Player character">player character</a> would say them. Such titles revolving around relationship-building, including visual novels as well as dating <a href="/wiki/Simulation" title="Simulation">simulations</a> such as <i>Tokimeki Memorial</i> and some <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing video games</a> such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shin_Megami_Tensei:_Persona" title="Shin Megami Tensei: Persona">Shin Megami Tensei: Persona</a></i>, often give choices that have a different number of associated "mood points" which influence a player character's relationship and future conversations with a non-player character. These games often feature a <a href="/wiki/Persistent_world" title="Persistent world">day-night cycle</a> with a time scheduling system that provides context and relevance to character interactions, allowing players to choose when and if to interact with certain characters, which in turn influences their responses during later conversations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>As early as 1983, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portopia_Renzoku_Satsujin_Jiken" title="Portopia Renzoku Satsujin Jiken">Portopia Serial Murder Case</a></i> featured non-linear elements, which included traveling between different areas in a generally <a href="/wiki/Open_world" title="Open world">open world</a>, a branching dialogue conversation system where the story develops through entering commands and receiving responses from other characters, and making choices that determine the dialogues and order of events as well as alternate outcomes, though there is only one true culprit while the others are <a href="/wiki/Red_herring" title="Red herring">red herrings</a>. It also features a phone that could be used to dial any number to contact several <a href="/wiki/Non-player_character" title="Non-player character">non-player characters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Retro-85_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Retro-85-10">[10]</a></sup> The game was well received in Japan for its well-told storyline and surprising <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twist_ending" title="Twist ending">twist ending</a>, and for allowing multiple ways to achieve objectives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Retro_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Retro-11">[11]</a></sup> Another more non-linear early example was <i>Mirrors</i>, released by Soft Studio Wing for the PC-8801 and <a href="/wiki/FM_Towns" title="FM Towns">FM Towns</a> computers in 1990; it featured a branching narrative, multiple endings, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Book_(CD_standard)" title="Red Book (CD standard)">audio CD</a> music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is not uncommon for visual novels to have morality systems. A well-known example is the 2005 title <i><a href="/wiki/School_Days_(visual_novel)" title="School Days (visual novel)">School Days</a></i>, an animated visual novel that <a href="/wiki/Kotaku" title="Kotaku">Kotaku</a> describes as going well beyond the usual "black and white choice systems" (referring to video games such as <i><a href="/wiki/Mass_Effect" title="Mass Effect">Mass Effect</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Fallout_3" title="Fallout 3">Fallout 3</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/BioShock" title="BioShock">BioShock</a></i>) where you "pick a side and stick with it" while leaving "the expansive middle area between unexplored." <i>School Days</i> instead encourages players to explore the grey, neutral middle-ground in order to view the more interesting, "bad" endings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>A common feature used in visual novels is having multiple protagonists giving different perspectives on the story. <a href="/wiki/C%27s_Ware" title="C's Ware">C's Ware</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eve_Burst_Error" title="Eve Burst Error">EVE Burst Error</a></i> (1995) introduced a unique twist to the system by allowing the player to switch between both protagonists at any time during the game, instead of finishing one protagonist's scenario before playing the other. <i>EVE Burst Error</i> often requires the player to have both protagonists co-operate with each other at various points during the game, with choices in one scenario affecting the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <i>Fate/stay night</i> is another example that features multiple perspectives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-klug_194-7_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-klug_194-7-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chunsoft" title="Chunsoft">Chunsoft</a> sound novels such as <i><a href="/wiki/Machi_(video_game)" title="Machi (video game)">Machi</a></i> (1998) and <i><a href="/wiki/428:_F%C5%ABsa_Sareta_Shibuya_de" title="428: Fūsa Sareta Shibuya de">428: Fūsa Sareta Shibuya de</a></i> (2008) develop this concept further, by allowing the player to alternate between the perspectives of several or more different characters, making choices with one character that have consequences for other characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1up_novel_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-1up_novel-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-destructoid428_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-destructoid428-15">[15]</a></sup> <i>428</i> in particular features up to 85 different possible endings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-destructoid428_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-destructoid428-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other notable examples of non-linear storytelling include <a href="/wiki/ELF_Corporation" title="ELF Corporation">ELF</a>'s most famous visual novel, <i><a href="/wiki/YU-NO:_A_girl_who_chants_love_at_the_bound_of_this_world" title="YU-NO: A girl who chants love at the bound of this world">YU-NO: A girl who chants love at the bound of this world</a></i> (1996), which featured a science fiction plot revolving around <a href="/wiki/Time_travel" title="Time travel">time travel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parallel_universe_(fiction)" title="Parallel universe (fiction)">parallel universes</a>. The player travels between parallel worlds using a Reflector device, which employs a limited number of stones to mark a certain position as a returning location, so that if the player decides to retrace their steps, they can go to an alternate universe to the time they have used a Reflector stone. The game also implemented an original system called ADMS, or Automatic Diverge Mapping System, which displays a screen that the player can check at any time to see the direction in which they are heading along the branching plot lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Similar systems have later been employed in the 2010 role-playing games, <i><a href="/wiki/Radiant_Historia" title="Radiant Historia">Radiant Historia</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Radiant-Historia_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Radiant-Historia-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Portable" title="PlayStation Portable">PSP</a> version of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tactics_Ogre" title="Tactics Ogre">Tactics Ogre</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="RPG_hybrids">RPG hybrids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_novel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: RPG hybrids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There have often been <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing video games</a> that feature visual novel style elements. A well-known example in the West is the <i><a href="/wiki/Lost_Odyssey" title="Lost Odyssey">Lost Odyssey</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing video game</a> that features a series of visual novel style flashback sequences called "A Thousand Years of Dreams".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1up_novel_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-1up_novel-3">[3]</a></sup> These sequences were penned by an award-winning <a href="/wiki/Japanese_literature" title="Japanese literature">Japanese short story</a> writer, Kiyoshi Shigematsu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-develop_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-develop-20">[20]</a></sup> Another title is the <a href="/wiki/Arc_System_Works" title="Arc System Works">Arc System Works</a> <a href="/wiki/Fighting_game" title="Fighting game">fighting game</a> series <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blazblue" title="Blazblue">Blazblue</a>, which plays off of a complex fantasy setting where a one hundred-year period is reset indefinitely with many variables. The many branching story lines in Story Mode can serve as stand-alone stories, but players must consider them together along with Arcade Mode stories to be able to fully understand the universe.</p>
<p>Another successful example is <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">Sega</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Sakura_Wars" title="Sakura Wars">Sakura Wars</a></i> series, which combined <a href="/wiki/Tactical_role-playing_game" title="Tactical role-playing game">tactical role-playing game</a> combat with visual novel elements, introducing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real-time_game" title="Real-time game">real-time</a> <a href="/wiki/Nonlinear_gameplay#Branching_storylines" title="Nonlinear gameplay">branching choice</a> system where, during an event or conversation, the player must choose an action or dialogue choice within a time limit, or to not respond at all within that time. The player's choice, or lack thereof, affects the <a href="/wiki/Player_character" title="Player character">player character</a>'s relationship with other characters and in turn the characters' performance in battle, the direction of the storyline, and the ending. Later games in the series added several variations, including an action gauge that can be raised up or down depending on the situation, and a gauge that the player can manipulate using the <a href="/wiki/Analog_stick" title="Analog stick">analog stick</a> depending on the situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rpgamer_sakura_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-rpgamer_sakura-21">[21]</a></sup> The success of <i>Sakura Wars</i> led to a wave of games that combine role-playing and visual novel elements, including <i><a href="/wiki/Thousand_Arms" title="Thousand Arms">Thousand Arms</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Riviera:_The_Promised_Land" title="Riviera: The Promised Land">Riviera: The Promised Land</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Luminous_Arc" title="Luminous Arc">Luminous Arc</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> A similar type of real-time conversation system also appeared in the Sega-published <a href="/wiki/Action_role-playing_game" title="Action role-playing game">action role-playing game</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Alpha_Protocol" title="Alpha Protocol">Alpha Protocol</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Another example is <i><a href="/wiki/Sentou_Gakuen" title="Sentou Gakuen">Sentou Gakuen</a></i>, which combined elements of both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MMORPG" title="MMORPG">MMORPGs</a> and visual novels.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_novel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The visual novel genre has evolved a style somewhat different from print novels. In general, visual novels are more likely to be narrated in the first person than the third, and to present events from the point of view of only one character. It is fairly common for the primary structural unit to be the day rather than the chapter, with formulaic awakenings and returnings to bed framing each day's events. There are of course many exceptions to these generalizations.</p>
<p>In the typical visual novel, the graphics comprise a set of generic backgrounds (normally just one for each location in the game), with character <a href="/wiki/Sprite_(computer_graphics)" title="Sprite (computer graphics)">sprites</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">立ち絵</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>tachi-e</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> superimposed on these; the perspective is usually first-person, with the protagonist remaining unseen. At certain key moments in the plot, special <i>event CG</i> <a href="/wiki/Computer_graphics" title="Computer graphics">computer graphics</a> are displayed instead; these are more detailed images, drawn specially for that scene rather than being composed from predefined elements, which often use more cinematic camera angles and include the protagonist. These event CGs can usually be viewed at any time once they have been "unlocked" by finding them in-game; this provides a motivation to replay the game and try making different decisions, as it is normally impossible to view all special events on a single play-through.</p>
<p>Up until the 1990s, the majority of visual novels utilized <a href="/wiki/Pixel_art" title="Pixel art">pixel art</a>. This was particularly common on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NEC_PC-9801" title="NEC PC-9801">NEC PC-9801</a> format, which showcased what is considered to be some of the best pixel art in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_video_games" title="History of video games">history of video games</a>, with a popular example being <i><a href="/wiki/Policenauts" title="Policenauts">Policenauts</a></i> in 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Retro_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Retro-11">[11]</a></sup> There have also been visual novels that use live-action stills or video footage, such as several <i>Sound Novel</i> games by <a href="/wiki/Chunsoft" title="Chunsoft">Chunsoft</a>. The most successful example is <i><a href="/wiki/Machi_(video_game)" title="Machi (video game)">Machi</a></i>, one of the most celebrated games in Japan, where it was voted No. 5 in a 2006 <i><a href="/wiki/Famitsu" title="Famitsu">Famitsu</a></i> reader poll of top 100 games of all time. The game resembled a live-action television drama, but allowing players to explore multiple character perspectives and affect the outcomes. Another successful example is <i><a href="/wiki/428:_F%C5%ABsa_Sareta_Shibuya_de" title="428: Fūsa Sareta Shibuya de">428: Fūsa Sareta Shibuya de</a></i>, which received a perfect score of 40 out of 40 from <i><a href="/wiki/Famitsu" title="Famitsu">Famitsu</a></i> magazine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1up_novel_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-1up_novel-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Content_and_genre">Content and genre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_novel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Content and genre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Adventure_game" title="Adventure game">Adventure game</a></div>
<p>Many visual novels are centered on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drama_film" title="Drama film">drama</a>, particularly themes involving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romantic_love" title="Romantic love">romance</a> or family, but visual novels centered on science fiction, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fantasy_fiction" title="Fantasy fiction">fantasy fiction</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Horror_fiction" title="Horror fiction">horror fiction</a> are not uncommon.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Erotic_content">Erotic content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_novel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Erotic content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Eroge" title="Eroge">Eroge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sex_and_nudity_in_video_games" title="Sex and nudity in video games">Sex and nudity in video games</a></div>
<p>Many visual novels also qualify as <a href="/wiki/Eroge" title="Eroge">eroge</a>, an abbreviation of 'erotic game'. These games feature sexually explicit imagery that are accessed by completing certain routes in the game, most often depicting the game's protagonist having sex with one of the game's other characters. Like other pornographic media in Japan, scenes depicting genitalia are censored in their original Japanese releases, only becoming uncensored if the game is licensed outside Japan. Certain eroge titles receive re-releases which exclude explicit content in order to be sold to a younger audience, such as ports to consoles or handheld systems where sexually explicit content is not allowed, and storylines referring to aforementioned sex scenes are often omitted from adaptations into other media, unless that media is also pornographic in nature, such as a <a href="/wiki/Hentai" title="Hentai">hentai</a> anime.</p>
<p>Traditionally, PC-based visual novels have contained risque scenes even if the overall focus is not erotic (similar to the "<a href="/wiki/Sex_in_film" title="Sex in film">obligatory sex scene</a>" in <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_States" title="Cinema of the United States">Hollywood</a> <a href="/wiki/Action_film" title="Action film">action films</a>). However, the vast majority of console ports do not contain adult material, and a number of recent PC games have also been targeted at the all-age market; for example, all of <a href="/wiki/Key_(company)" title="Key (company)">Key</a>'s titles come in family-friendly versions, although the content might still not be appropriate for children, and three have never contained adult content at all. Also, all of <a href="/wiki/KID" title="KID">KID</a>'s titles are family-friendly.</p>
<p>However, some of these games are later re-released with the addition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erotic" title="Erotic">erotic</a> scenes, or have a sequel with such. For example, <i><a href="/wiki/Little_Busters!" title="Little Busters!">Little Busters!</a></i> was first released as an all-ages visual novel, but a version with erotic scenes titled <i><a href="/wiki/Little_Busters!" title="Little Busters!">Little Busters! Ecstasy</a></i> came out later, and though <i><a href="/wiki/Clannad_(visual_novel)" title="Clannad (visual novel)">Clannad</a></i> is also all-ages, its spinoff <i><a href="/wiki/Tomoyo_After:_It%27s_a_Wonderful_Life" title="Tomoyo After: It's a Wonderful Life">Tomoyo After: It's a Wonderful Life</a></i> is not.</p>
<p>Often, the beginning of the eroge will be dedicated to introducing the characters and developing the protagonist's relationship with them, before the protagonist sexually interacts with other characters, for example, <a href="/wiki/Lump_of_Sugar" title="Lump of Sugar">Lump of Sugar</a> games such as <a href="/wiki/Tayutama:_Kiss_on_my_Deity" title="Tayutama: Kiss on my Deity">Tayutama: Kiss on my Deity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hello,_Good-bye" title="Hello, Good-bye">Hello, Good-bye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gaku_Ou:_The_Royal_Seven_Stars" title="Gaku Ou: The Royal Seven Stars">Gaku Ou: The Royal Seven Stars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sekai_to_Sekai_no_Mannaka_de" title="Sekai to Sekai no Mannaka de">Sekai to Sekai no Mannaka de</a> and <a href="/wiki/Everlasting_Summer" title="Everlasting Summer">Everlasting Summer</a> do this. The effect it has on the reader is the H-scenes (sex scenes) will feel more emotional and impactful on the two (or possibly more) characters.</p>
<p>Some of Japan's earliest adventure games were <a href="/wiki/Eroge" title="Eroge">erotic</a> <a href="/wiki/Bish%C5%8Djo_game" title="Bishōjo game">bishōjo games</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/Koei" title="Koei">Koei</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pesimo_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pesimo-24">[24]</a></sup> In 1982, they released <i><a href="/wiki/Night_Life_(video_game)" title="Night Life (video game)">Night Life</a></i>, the first commercial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adult_video_game" title="Adult video game">erotic computer game</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Retro_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Retro-11">[11]</a></sup> It was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graphic_adventure_game" title="Graphic adventure game">graphic adventure</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-25">[25]</a></sup> with sexually explicit images.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Retro_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Retro-11">[11]</a></sup> That same year, they released another erotic title, <i>Danchi Tsuma no Yūwaku</i> (<i>Seduction of the Condominium Wife</i>), which was an early adventure game with colour graphics, owing to the eight-color palette of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NEC_PC-8001" title="NEC PC-8001">NEC PC-8001</a> computer. It became a hit, helping Koei become a major software company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pesimo_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pesimo-24">[24]</a></sup> Other now-famous companies such as <a href="/wiki/Enix" title="Enix">Enix</a>, <a href="/wiki/Square_(company)" title="Square (company)">Square</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nihon_Falcom" title="Nihon Falcom">Nihon Falcom</a> also produced similar erotic games in the early 1980s before they became famous for their <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing video games</a>. While some early erotic games meaningfully integrate the erotic content into a thoughtful and mature storyline, others often used it as a flimsy excuse for pornography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Retro_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Retro-11">[11]</a></sup> The Japanese game Pai Touch! involves the protagonist gaining the ability to change the size of girls' breasts, and the adventures that ensue in trying to choose which girl to use the power on the most.</p>
<p>Another subgenre is called "nukige" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">抜きゲー</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, in which sexual gratification of the player is the main focus of the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_fiction">Science fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_novel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Science fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1986, <a href="/wiki/Square_(company)" title="Square (company)">Square</a> released the science fiction adventure game <i><a href="/wiki/Suish%C5%8D_no_Dragon" title="Suishō no Dragon">Suishō no Dragon</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Entertainment_System" title="Nintendo Entertainment System">NES console</a>. The game featured several innovations, including the use of <a href="/wiki/Computer_animation" title="Computer animation">animation</a> in many of the scenes rather than still images,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Square-Enix_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Square-Enix-27">[27]</a></sup> and an interface resembling that of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point-and-click" title="Point-and-click">point-and-click</a> interface for a console, like <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portopia_Renzoku_Satsujin_Jiken" title="Portopia Renzoku Satsujin Jiken">Portopia Serial Murder Case</a></i>, but making use of visual icons rather than text-based ones to represent various actions. Like the NES version of <i>Portopia Serial Murder Case</i>, it featured a cursor that could be moved around the screen using the <a href="/wiki/D-pad" title="D-pad">D-pad</a> to examine the scenery, though the cursor in <i>Suishō no Dragon</i> was also used to click on the action icons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Square-Enix_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Square-Enix-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-itmedia-dragon_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-itmedia-dragon-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hideo_Kojima" title="Hideo Kojima">Hideo Kojima</a> (of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_Gear_(series)" title="Metal Gear (series)">Metal Gear</a></i> fame) was inspired by <i>Portopia Serial Murder Case</i> to enter the video game industry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kasavin_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kasavin-29">[29]</a></sup> and later produced his own <a href="/wiki/Adventure_game" title="Adventure game">adventure games</a>. After completing the <a href="/wiki/Stealth_game" title="Stealth game">stealth game</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Metal_Gear_(video_game)" title="Metal Gear (video game)">Metal Gear</a></i>, his first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graphic_adventure" title="Graphic adventure">graphic adventure</a> was released by <a href="/wiki/Konami" title="Konami">Konami</a> the following year: <i><a href="/wiki/Snatcher_(video_game)" title="Snatcher (video game)">Snatcher</a></i> (1988), an ambitious <a href="/wiki/Cyberpunk" title="Cyberpunk">cyberpunk</a> detective novel, graphic adventure, that was highly regarded at the time for pushing the boundaries of video game storytelling, cinematic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cut_scene" title="Cut scene">cut scenes</a>, and mature content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1UP-Kojima_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-1UP-Kojima-30">[30]</a></sup> It also featured a <a href="/wiki/Apocalyptic_and_post-apocalyptic_fiction" title="Apocalyptic and post-apocalyptic fiction">post-apocalyptic</a> science fiction setting, an <a href="/wiki/Amnesia" title="Amnesia">amnesiac</a> protagonist, and some <a href="/wiki/Light_gun_shooter" title="Light gun shooter">light gun shooter</a> segments. It was praised for its graphics, soundtrack, high quality writing comparable to a novel, voice acting comparable to a film or <a href="/wiki/Radio_drama" title="Radio drama">radio drama</a>, and in-game computer database with optional documents that flesh out the game world. The <a href="/wiki/Sega_CD" title="Sega CD">Sega CD</a> version of <i>Snatcher</i> was for a long time the only major visual novel game to be released in America, where it, despite low sales, gained a <a href="/wiki/Cult_following" title="Cult following">cult following</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kalata-Policenauts_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kalata-Policenauts-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following <i><a href="/wiki/Metal_Gear_2:_Solid_Snake" title="Metal Gear 2: Solid Snake">Metal Gear 2: Solid Snake</a></i>, Kojima produced his next graphic adventure, <i><a href="/wiki/Policenauts" title="Policenauts">Policenauts</a></i> (1994), a point-and-click adventure notable for being an early example of extensive voice recording in video games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN-Kojima_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-Kojima-32">[32]</a></sup> It also featured a <a href="/wiki/Hard_science_fiction" title="Hard science fiction">hard science fiction</a> setting, a theme revolving around space exploration, a plot inspired by the ancient <a href="/wiki/Japanese_literature" title="Japanese literature">Japanese tale</a> of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urashima_Taro" title="Urashima Taro">Urashima Taro</a></i>, and some occasional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full_motion_video_based_game" title="Full motion video based game">full-motion video</a> cut scenes. The gameplay was largely similar to <i>Snatcher</i>, but with the addition of a point-and-click interface and some <a href="/wiki/First-person_shooter" title="First-person shooter">first-person shooter</a> segments. <i>Policenauts</i> also introduced summary screens, which act to refresh the player's memory of the plot upon reloading a <a href="/wiki/Saved_game" title="Saved game">saved game</a> (save), an element Kojima would later use in <i><a href="/wiki/Metal_Gear_Solid" title="Metal Gear Solid">Metal Gear Solid</a></i>. The <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_(console)" title="PlayStation (console)">PlayStation</a> version of <i>Policenauts</i> could also read the <a href="/wiki/Memory_card" title="Memory card">memory card</a> and give some <a href="/wiki/Easter_egg" title="Easter egg">easter egg</a> dialogues if a save file of Konami's <a href="/wiki/Dating_sim" title="Dating sim">dating sim</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Tokimeki_Memorial" title="Tokimeki Memorial">Tokimeki Memorial</a></i> is present, a technique Kojima would also later use in <i>Metal Gear Solid</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kalata-Policenauts_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kalata-Policenauts-31">[31]</a></sup> From 1997 to 1999, Kojima developed the three <i><a href="/wiki/Tokimeki_Memorial_(series)" title="Tokimeki Memorial (series)">Tokimeki Memorial Drama Series</a></i> titles, which were adaptations of <i>Tokimeki Memorial</i> in a visual novel adventure game format.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Other acclaimed examples of science fiction visual novels include <a href="/wiki/ELF_Corporation" title="ELF Corporation">ELF</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/YU-NO:_A_girl_who_chants_love_at_the_bound_of_this_world" title="YU-NO: A girl who chants love at the bound of this world">Yu-No</a></i> (1996) and <a href="/wiki/5pb." title="5pb.">5pb.</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Chaos;Head" title="Chaos;Head">Chaos;Head</a></i> (2008) and <i><a href="/wiki/Steins;Gate" title="Steins;Gate">Steins;Gate</a></i> (2009).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nakige">Nakige</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_novel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nakige">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A popular subgenre of visual novels is the <i>nakige</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">泣きゲー</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>"crying game"</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, which , as opposed to <i>utsuge</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">鬱ゲー</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>"depressing game"</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, still usually has a happy ending. The main purpose of such a game is to make the player feel for the characters and to make them cry because of emotional scenarios which serve to have a bigger impact on the player after the game is over. These games often follow a similar formula: a comedic first half, with a heart-warming romantic middle, followed by a tragic separation, and finally (though not always) an emotional reunion. This formula was influenced primarily by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaf_(company)" title="Leaf (company)">Leaf's</a> visual novel <i><a href="/wiki/To_Heart" title="To Heart">To Heart</a></i>, released in 1997, and was further developed in the 1998 title <i><a href="/wiki/One:_Kagayaku_Kisetsu_e" title="One: Kagayaku Kisetsu e">One: Kagayaku Kisetsu e</a></i>, developed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tactics_(company)" title="Tactics (company)">Tactics</a>. After <i>One</i> was complete, the development team quit Tactics to form <a href="/wiki/Key_(company)" title="Key (company)">Key</a> where they developed their first title <i><a href="/wiki/Kanon_(visual_novel)" title="Kanon (visual novel)">Kanon</a></i>, also based upon this formula. According to Satoshi Todome in his book, <i>A History of Adult Games</i>, <i>Kanon</i> was "heavily hyped [and] had gamers impatient until its release. It was only one game released by Key so far, and yet [it] had already sent major shockwaves around the industry. And yet another game [<i>Air</i>], two years later, sent even more shockwaves. <i><a href="/wiki/Air_(visual_novel)" title="Air (visual novel)">Air</a></i> was equally hyped and well received."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history-of-adult-games_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-of-adult-games-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Key's "crying game" formula used successfully in <i>One</i> and <i>Kanon</i> was later adopted by other visual novel companies to create their own "crying games". Examples of this include: <i><a href="/wiki/Kana:_Little_Sister" title="Kana: Little Sister">Kana: Little Sister</a></i> (1999) by Digital Object, the <i><a href="/wiki/Memories_Off" title="Memories Off">Memories Off</a></i> series (1999 onwards) by <a href="/wiki/KID" title="KID">KID</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D.C.:_Da_Capo" title="D.C.: Da Capo">D.C.: Da Capo</a></i> (2002) by <a href="/wiki/Circus_(company)" title="Circus (company)">Circus</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Wind:_A_Breath_of_Heart" title="Wind: A Breath of Heart">Wind: A Breath of Heart</a></i> (2002) by <a href="/wiki/Minori_(company)" title="Minori (company)">Minori</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Snow_(visual_novel)" title="Snow (visual novel)">Snow</a></i> (2003) by Studio Mebius (under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visual_Art%27s" title="Visual Art's">Visual Art's</a>).</p>
<p>One of the most acclaimed visual novels of this subgenre was Key's <i><a href="/wiki/Clannad_(visual_novel)" title="Clannad (visual novel)">Clannad</a></i>, written by <a href="/wiki/Jun_Maeda" title="Jun Maeda">Jun Maeda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Y%C5%ABichi_Suzumoto" title="Yūichi Suzumoto">Yūichi Suzumoto</a>, and Kai and Tōya Okano. Released in 2004, its story revolved around the central theme of the value of having a family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pre-Clannad_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-pre-Clannad-35">[35]</a></sup> It was voted the best <a href="/wiki/Bish%C5%8Djo_game" title="Bishōjo game">bishōjo game</a> of all time in a poll held by <i><a href="/wiki/Dengeki_G%27s_Magazine" title="Dengeki G's Magazine">Dengeki G's Magazine</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> It served as the basis for a media franchise, with successful adaptations into a <a href="/wiki/Light_novel" title="Light novel">light novel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clannad_(film)" title="Clannad (film)">animated film</a>, and acclaimed <a href="/wiki/List_of_Clannad_episodes" title="List of Clannad episodes">anime series</a>.</p>
<p>In 2008, several of Key's visual novels were voted in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dengeki" title="Dengeki">Dengeki</a></i> poll of the ten most tear-inducing games of all time, including <i><a href="/wiki/Clannad_(visual_novel)" title="Clannad (visual novel)">Clannad</a></i> at No. 2, <i><a href="/wiki/Kanon_(visual_novel)" title="Kanon (visual novel)">Kanon</a></i> at No. 4, <i><a href="/wiki/Air_(visual_novel)" title="Air (visual novel)">Air</a></i> at No. 7, and <i><a href="/wiki/Little_Busters!" title="Little Busters!">Little Busters!</a></i> at No. 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In 2011, several visual novels were also voted in <i><a href="/wiki/Famitsu" title="Famitsu">Famitsu</a>'</i>s poll of 20 most tear-inducing games of all time, with <i>Clannad</i> at No. 4, <i><a href="/wiki/Steins;Gate" title="Steins;Gate">Steins;Gate</a></i> at No. 6, <i>Air</i> at No. 7, <i>Little Busters!</i> at No. 10, and <i><a href="/wiki/428:_F%C5%ABsa_Sareta_Shibuya_de" title="428: Fūsa Sareta Shibuya de">428: Fūsa Sareta Shibuya de</a></i> at No. 14.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Horror">Horror</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_novel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Horror">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Higurashi_When_They_Cry" title="Higurashi When They Cry">Higurashi no Naku Koro ni</a></i> (<i>When They Cry</i>) was a 2002 <a href="/wiki/Survival_horror" title="Survival horror">horror-themed</a> visual novel by <a href="/wiki/07th_Expansion" title="07th Expansion">07th Expansion</a>, influenced by the "crying game" subgenre. <a href="/wiki/Ryukishi07" title="Ryukishi07">Ryukishi07</a> of 07th Expansion mentioned in 2004 how he was influenced by <a href="/wiki/Key_(company)" title="Key (company)">Key</a>'s works during the planning of <i>Higurashi no Naku Koro ni</i>. He played their games, as well as other visual novels, as a reference and analyzed them to try to determineon why they were so popular. He decided that the secret was that the stories would start with ordinary, enjoyable days, but then a sudden occurrence would happen leading the player to cry from shock. He used a similar model as the basis for <i>Higurashi</i> but instead of leading the player to cry, Ryukishi07 wanted to scare the player with the addition of horror elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryukishi07-blog04_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ryukishi07-blog04-39">[39]</a></sup> Other examples of horror-themed visual novels include: <i><a href="/wiki/Animamundi:_Dark_Alchemist" title="Animamundi: Dark Alchemist">Animamundi: Dark Alchemist</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Higanbana_no_Saku_Yoru_ni" title="Higanbana no Saku Yoru ni">Higanbana no Saku Yoru ni</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umineko_no_Naku_Koro_ni" title="Umineko no Naku Koro ni">Umineko no Naku Koro ni</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ookami_Kakushi" title="Ookami Kakushi">Ookami Kakushi</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imabikisou" title="Imabikisou">Imabikisou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saya_no_Uta" title="Saya no Uta">Saya no Uta</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Corpse_Party" title="Corpse Party">Corpse Party</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="D.C5.8Djinshi_games_.28D.C5.8Djin_soft.29">Dōjinshi games (Dōjin soft)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_novel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Dōjinshi games (Dōjin soft)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/D%C5%8Djin_soft" title="Dōjin soft">Dōjin soft</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/D%C5%8Djinshi" title="Dōjinshi">Dōjinshi</a> (同人誌, often transliterated as <i>doujinshi</i>) is the Japanese term for self-published (fan-made) works. This includes (but is not limited to) <i><a href="/wiki/D%C5%8Djin_soft" title="Dōjin soft">dōjin games</a> (同人ゲーム)</i>, also sometimes called <i>dōjin soft (同人ソフト)</i>. These visual novel-style games are created as <a href="/wiki/Fan_labor" title="Fan labor">fan-made works</a> based on pre-existing <i><a href="/wiki/Fandom" title="Fandom">fandoms</a></i> (usually <a href="/wiki/Anime_and_manga_fandom" title="Anime and manga fandom">anime and manga</a>, but also for tv shows or even other pre-existing games and visual novels). Dōjinshi games are often based on romance (or <i><a href="/wiki/Shipping_(fandom)" title="Shipping (fandom)">shipping</a></i>) between two characters, known as an <i><a href="/wiki/Otome_game" title="Otome game">otome game</a> (乙女ゲーム)</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Dating_sim" title="Dating sim">dating sim</a></i>; sometimes becoming sexual (or <i><a href="/wiki/Hentai" title="Hentai">hentai</a></i>), known as an <i><a href="/wiki/Eroge" title="Eroge">eroge</a> (エロゲ, a portmanteau of <b>ero</b>tic <b>ga</b>me: (エロチックゲーム))</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Translations">Translations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_novel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Translations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to the year 2000, few Japanese visual novels were translated into other languages. As with the visual novel genre in general, a majority of titles released for the PC have been <a href="/wiki/Eroge" title="Eroge">eroge</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Hirameki" title="Hirameki">Hirameki</a>'s now-discontinued <a href="/wiki/AnimePlay" title="AnimePlay">AnimePlay</a> series a notable exception. As of 2014, <a href="/wiki/JAST_USA" title="JAST USA">JAST USA</a> and <a href="/wiki/MangaGamer" title="MangaGamer">MangaGamer</a> are the two most prolific publishers of translated visual novels for the PC; both primarily release eroge, but have begun to diversify into the all-ages market in recent years, with titles such as <a href="/wiki/Steins;Gate" title="Steins;Gate">Steins;Gate</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Higurashi_no_Naku_Koro_ni" title="Higurashi no Naku Koro ni">Higurashi no Naku Koro ni</a> respectively. In addition to official commercial translations, a vibrant <a href="/wiki/Fan_translation" title="Fan translation">fan translation</a> scene exists, which has translated many free visual novels (such as <i><a href="/wiki/Narcissu" title="Narcissu">Narcissu</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/True_Remembrance" title="True Remembrance">True Remembrance</a></i>) and a few commercial works (such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umineko_no_Naku_Koro_ni" title="Umineko no Naku Koro ni">Umineko no Naku Koro ni</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Policenauts" title="Policenauts">Policenauts</a></i>) into English. Fan translations of Japanese visual novels into languages other than English such as Chinese, French, German and Russian are commonplace as well.</p>
<p>English translations of Japanese visual novels on video game consoles were rare until the release of the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DS" title="Nintendo DS">Nintendo DS</a>, though some games with visual novel elements had been published in the Western world before then, such as <a href="/wiki/Hideo_Kojima" title="Hideo Kojima">Hideo Kojima</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Snatcher_(video_game)" title="Snatcher (video game)">Snatcher</a></i>. Following the success of mystery titles for the Nintendo DS such as <a href="/wiki/Capcom" title="Capcom">Capcom</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Ace_Attorney" title="Ace Attorney">Ace Attorney</a></i> series (which began on the <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Advance" title="Game Boy Advance">Game Boy Advance</a> in 2001), Cing's <i><a href="/wiki/Hotel_Dusk:_Room_215" title="Hotel Dusk: Room 215">Hotel Dusk</a></i> series (beginning in 2006),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kalata-Snatcher_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kalata-Snatcher-40">[40]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Level-5_(video_game_company)" title="Level-5 (video game company)">Level-5</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Professor_Layton" title="Professor Layton">Professor Layton</a></i> series (beginning in 2007),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Attorney-Layton_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Attorney-Layton-41">[41]</a></sup> Japanese visual novels have been published in other countries more frequently. The success of these games has sparked a resurgence in the <a href="/wiki/Adventure_game" title="Adventure game">adventure game</a> genre outside Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kalata-Snatcher_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kalata-Snatcher-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/GameSpot" title="GameSpot">GameSpot</a> has credited <i><a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Wright:_Ace_Attorney" title="Phoenix Wright: Ace Attorney">Phoenix Wright: Ace Attorney</a></i> in particular for revitalizing the adventure game genre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The success of the <i>Ace Attorney</i> series was followed soon after by the even greater success of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Level-5_(video_game_company)" title="Level-5 (video game company)">Level-5</a>'s <i>Professor Layton</i> in 2007. Both have since become some of the best selling adventure game franchises, with <i>Ace Attorney</i> selling over 3.9 million units worldwide and <i>Professor Layton</i> selling over 9.5 million units worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Attorney-Layton_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Attorney-Layton-41">[41]</a></sup> Their success has led to an increase in Japanese visual novels being localized for release outside Japan, including: <a href="/wiki/KID" title="KID">KID</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Ever_17:_The_Out_of_Infinity" title="Ever 17: The Out of Infinity">Ever 17: The Out of Infinity</a></i> (2002), Cing's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Another_Code" title="Another Code">Another Code</a></i> series (2005 onwards), <a href="/wiki/Marvelous_Entertainment" title="Marvelous Entertainment">Marvelous Entertainment</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Lux-Pain" title="Lux-Pain">Lux-Pain</a></i> (2008), <a href="/wiki/Chunsoft" title="Chunsoft">Chunsoft</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/999:_Nine_Hours,_Nine_Persons,_Nine_Doors" title="999: Nine Hours, Nine Persons, Nine Doors">999: Nine Hours, Nine Persons, Nine Doors</a></i> (2010), and Capcom's <i><a href="/wiki/Ghost_Trick:_Phantom_Detective" title="Ghost Trick: Phantom Detective">Ghost Trick: Phantom Detective</a></i> (2010).</p>
