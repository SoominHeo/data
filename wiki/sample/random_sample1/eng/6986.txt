<p>A <b>business park</b> or <b>office park</b> is an area of land in which many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Office_building" title="Office building">office buildings</a> are grouped together. <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">All of the work that goes on is <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">commercial</a>, not large-scale <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industrial</a> nor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Residential" title="Residential">residential</a>.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The first office park opened in <a href="/wiki/Mountain_Brook,_Alabama" title="Mountain Brook, Alabama">Mountain Brook, Alabama</a>, in the early 1950s to avoid racial tension in city centers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>These are popular in many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suburban" title="Suburban">suburban</a> locations, where development is cheaper because of the lower land costs and the lower building costs for building wider, not necessarily higher. Some businesses prefer the larger floorplates as more efficient, reducing time lost moving between floors. They are also often located near motorways or main roads for easy access.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The impact of these areas on the urban fabric has been criticized:</p>
<ul>
<li>Spaces escape the control of the built realm: voids between fragments of unconnected residential schemes, gaps between urbanized zones, abandoned farmland, etc. A new approach to spacial organization arises with the ease that characterizes any new consumer good, an approach which questions the conventional references of urbanism: the so-called 'commercial, industrial, business and theme park'.</li>
<li>The urbanized park originally sprang from the hybridization of the <a href="/wiki/Garden_city_movement" title="Garden city movement">garden-city</a> and Anglo-Saxon <a href="/wiki/Campus" title="Campus">university campus</a> models. It adopted the former's low-rise buildings and attention to free spaces as a way of shaping the environment, and the latter's autonomous constructions. In sum, parks are thematic precincts of autonomous architectural set pieces arranged around parking lots and communal services, and are situated at the most accessible points of the metropolitan road network.</li>
<li>(<cite class="citation journal">José María Ezquiaga (November–December 1998). "The City: Folds and Pieces". <i>AV Monographs</i>. <b>74</b>: 4–11.</cite><span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3ABusiness+park&amp;rft.atitle=The+City%3A+Folds+and+Pieces&amp;rft.au=Jos%C3%A9+Mar%C3a+Ezquiaga&amp;rft.date=1998-11%2F1998-12&amp;rft.genre=article&amp;rft.jtitle=AV+Monographs&amp;rft.pages=4-11&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Ajournal&amp;rft.volume=74"><span style="display:none;"> </span></span>)</li>
</ul>
