<p><b>Bertalan Bicskei</b> (17 September 1944 – 16 July 2011) was a <a href="/wiki/Hungarians" title="Hungarians">Hungarian</a> <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> <a href="/wiki/Goalkeeper_(association_football)" title="Goalkeeper (association football)">goalkeeper</a> and manager.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Active_career">Active career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bertalan_Bicskei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Active career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a youth player, he played for the most famous Hungarian club side, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferencvaros" title="Ferencvaros">Ferencvaros</a>, and he also honed his skills in <a href="/wiki/Budapest_Honv%C3%A9d_FC" title="Budapest Honvéd FC">Honved</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MTK_Budapest" title="MTK Budapest">MTK Budapest</a>, both Premier Division Hungarian sides. As a goalkeeper of great talent, he played many games for the <a href="/wiki/Hungary_national_football_team" title="Hungary national football team">National Youth Team of Hungary</a>, as well as the 13 and A senior National teams. After his playing days were over, he devoted much of his time to coaching and teaching young soccer players on the highest level.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Team_manager">Team manager</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bertalan_Bicskei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Team manager">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From 1977 to 1982, he worked as the coaching director for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MTK_Budapest" title="MTK Budapest">MTK Budapest</a>'s youth programme, and from 1982 to 1986 he was the Youth Coach Director for the <a href="/wiki/Hungary_national_football_team" title="Hungary national football team">Hungarian national football team</a>. As one of his greatest achievements on that level, under his coaching and guidance, the <a href="/wiki/Hungary_national_football_team" title="Hungary national football team">Under-18 National Team of Hungary</a>, captured the European Youth Championship in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>, in 1984.</p>
<p>Before moving on to the senior level, Bicskei successfully completed the, work, and graduated at the world-renowned soccer academy in <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> in 1986. He is one of just a few <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungarian</a>-born coaches who was able to earn one of the most respected coaching diplomas in the world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> While in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, he also studied philosophy, and learned to speak excellent <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>.</p>
<p>In 1987/88 Bicskei become head coach of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honved_Budapest" title="Honved Budapest">Honved Budapest</a>, (that year, Honved won the National title and the Hungarian cup) and his professional career was off to a flying start. In 1989 he, was selected to coach the <a href="/wiki/Hungary_national_football_team" title="Hungary national football team">senior national team of Hungary</a>. In 1991 he could not refuse a challenging offer from <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> and he became a head coach of <a href="/wiki/Busan_IPark" title="Busan IPark">Daewoo Royals</a>. Under his direction, the club won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1991_K-League" title="1991 K-League">1991 national title</a> and he was voted, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K-League_Manager_of_the_Year_Award" title="K-League Manager of the Year Award">Manager of the year</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>. From 1992 to 1994 he coached club <a href="/wiki/FC_Luzern" title="FC Luzern">FC Luzern</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>. In the 1995/96 he returned home briefly to coach <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MTK_Budapest" title="MTK Budapest">MTK Budapest</a>.</p>
<p>Bicskei packed his suitcase again in 1995, this time to <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> where he coached the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Riyadh" title="Al-Riyadh">Al-Riyadh</a> club in the 1995/96 seasons. During his stay in <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>, he started working on his book, "Preparation of Young Footballs", a soccer achievement that not only complements, but also challenges everything that was written thus far on teaching young soccer players to achieve the highest level. After returning home, he coached <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honved_Budapest" title="Honved Budapest">Honved Budapest</a> once again, 1996/97 seasons, and he was technical director at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RABA_ETO_Gyor" title="RABA ETO Gyor">RABA ETO Gyor</a> in the 1998/99 seasons.</p>
<p>Between 1998 and 2001 he served his second stint as the national coach of Hungary,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> later becoming the technical director of the Hungarian Football Federation between 2006 and mid-2008.</p>
<p>In the second half of 2004, the <a href="/wiki/Football_Association_of_Malaysia" title="Football Association of Malaysia">Football Association of Malaysia</a> selected Bertalan Bicskei, to succeed <a href="/wiki/Allan_Harris" title="Allan Harris">Allan Harris</a>. Bicskei led the national side to third place at the regional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tiger_Cup" title="Tiger Cup">Tiger Cup</a> competition, but was demoted to youth development duties by FAM for his actions during a friendly against <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> in <a href="/wiki/Penang" title="Penang">Penang</a> on 8 June 2005. Bicskei, disgusted by the standard of officiating, threw a bottle onto the pitch before confronting a Singapore player. In September 2005 the contract was terminated after mutual agreement.</p>
<p>Bicksei was given the job of <a href="/wiki/Liberia_national_football_team" title="Liberia national football team">Liberia</a>'s national coach in July 2010 on a four-year contract, but had to take a leave of absence in February 2011 after being poisoned in Liberia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>He has an adopted son called Wilson.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bertalan_Bicskei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 16 July 2011, Bicskei died in <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a> after a long battle with illness. He was 67 years old. The death of Bicskei was described as a loss to "a very important part of Hungarian football".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
