<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Witt group</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>In mathematics, a <b>Witt group</b> of a field, named after <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Witt" title="Ernst Witt">Ernst Witt</a>, is an <a href="/wiki/Abelian_group" title="Abelian group">abelian group</a> whose elements are represented by symmetric bilinear forms over the field.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Witt_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fix a field <i>k</i> of characteristic not two. All <a href="/wiki/Vector_space" title="Vector space">vector spaces</a> will be assumed to be finite-dimensional. We say that two spaces equipped with <a href="/wiki/Symmetric_bilinear_form" title="Symmetric bilinear form">symmetric bilinear forms</a> are <b>equivalent</b> if one can be obtained from the other by adding a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metabolic_quadratic_space" title="Metabolic quadratic space">metabolic quadratic space</a>, that is, zero or more copies of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperbolic_plane_(quadratic_forms)" title="Hyperbolic plane (quadratic forms)">hyperbolic plane</a>, the non-degenerate two-dimensional symmetric bilinear form with a norm 0 vector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MB14_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-MB14-1">[1]</a></sup> Each class is represented by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Core_form" title="Core form">core form</a> of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Witt_decomposition" title="Witt decomposition">Witt decomposition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L30_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-L30-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <b>Witt group of k</b> is the abelian group <i>W</i>(<i>k</i>) of equivalence classes of non-degenerate symmetric bilinear forms, with the group operation corresponding to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthogonal_direct_sum" title="Orthogonal direct sum">orthogonal direct sum</a> of forms. It is additively generated by the classes of one-dimensional forms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MH65_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-MH65-3">[3]</a></sup> Although classes may contain spaces of different dimension, the parity of the dimension is constant across a class and so rk : <i>W</i>(<i>k</i>) → <b>Z</b>/2<b>Z</b> is a homomorphism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MH66_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-MH66-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The elements of finite order in the Witt group have <a href="/wiki/Order_(group_theory)" title="Order (group theory)">order</a> a power of 2;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L37_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-L37-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MB72_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-MB72-6">[6]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Torsion_subgroup" title="Torsion subgroup">torsion subgroup</a> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kernel_(group_theory)" title="Kernel (group theory)">kernel</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Functorial" title="Functorial">functorial</a> map from <i>W</i>(<i>k</i>) to <i>W</i>(<i>k</i><sup>py</sup>), where <i>k</i><sup>py</sup> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pythagorean_closure" title="Pythagorean closure">Pythagorean closure</a> of <i>k</i>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam260_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lam260-7">[7]</a></sup> it is generated by the <a href="/wiki/Pfister_form" title="Pfister form">Pfister forms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam395_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lam395-8">[8]</a></sup> If <i>k</i> is not formally real, then the Witt group is <a href="/wiki/Torsion_group" title="Torsion group">torsion</a>, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exponent_(group_theory)" title="Exponent (group theory)">exponent</a> a power of 2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L35_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-L35-9">[9]</a></sup> The <b>height</b> of the field <i>k</i> is the exponent of the torsion in the Witt group, if this is finite, or ∞ otherwise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam395_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lam395-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ring_structure">Ring structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Witt_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ring structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Witt group of <i>k</i> can be given a <a href="/wiki/Commutative_ring" title="Commutative ring">commutative ring</a> structure, by using the <a href="/wiki/Tensor_product" title="Tensor product">tensor product</a> of two bilinear forms to define the ring product. This is sometimes called the <b>Witt ring</b> <i>W</i>(<i>k</i>), though the term "Witt ring" is often also used for a completely different ring of <a href="/wiki/Witt_vector" title="Witt vector">Witt vectors</a>.</p>
<p>To discuss the structure of this ring we assume that <i>k</i> is of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Characteristic_(field)" title="Characteristic (field)">characteristic</a> not equal to 2, so that we may identify symmetric bilinear forms and quadratic forms.</p>
<p>The kernel of the rank mod 2 homomorphism is a <a href="/wiki/Prime_ideal" title="Prime ideal">prime ideal</a>, <i>I</i>, of the Witt ring<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MH66_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-MH66-4">[4]</a></sup> termed the <i>fundamental ideal</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L31_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-L31-10">[10]</a></sup> The ring homomorphisms from <i>W</i>(<i>k</i>) to <b>Z</b> correspond to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Field_ordering" title="Field ordering">field orderings</a> of <i>k</i>, by taking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signature_(quadratic_form)" title="Signature (quadratic form)">signature</a> with respective to the ordering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L31_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-L31-10">[10]</a></sup> The Witt ring is a <a href="/wiki/Jacobson_ring" title="Jacobson ring">Jacobson ring</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L35_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-L35-9">[9]</a></sup> It is a <a href="/wiki/Noetherian_ring" title="Noetherian ring">Noetherian ring</a> if and only if there are finitely many <a href="/wiki/Square_class" title="Square class">square classes</a>; that is, if the squares in <i>k</i> form a subgroup of finite <a href="/wiki/Index_of_a_subgroup" title="Index of a subgroup">index</a> in the multiplicative group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam32_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lam32-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>If <i>k</i> is not formally real, the fundamental ideal is the only prime ideal of <i>W</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L33_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-L33-12">[12]</a></sup> and consists precisely of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nilpotent_element" title="Nilpotent element">nilpotent elements</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L35_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-L35-9">[9]</a></sup> <i>W</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Local_ring" title="Local ring">local ring</a> and has <a href="/wiki/Krull_dimension" title="Krull dimension">Krull dimension</a> 0.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam280_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lam280-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>If <i>k</i> is real, then the nilpotent elements are precisely those of finite additive order, and these in turn are the forms all of whose signatures are zero;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L36_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-L36-14">[14]</a></sup> <i>W</i> has Krull dimension 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam280_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lam280-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>If <i>k</i> is a real <a href="/wiki/Pythagorean_field" title="Pythagorean field">Pythagorean field</a> then the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero-divisor" title="Zero-divisor">zero-divisors</a> of <i>W</i> are the elements for which some signature is zero; otherwise, the zero-divisors are exactly the fundamental ideal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L37_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-L37-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam282_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lam282-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>If <i>k</i> is an ordered field with positive cone <i>P</i> then <a href="/wiki/Sylvester%27s_law_of_inertia" title="Sylvester's law of inertia">Sylvester's law of inertia</a> holds for quadratic forms over <i>k</i> and the <i>signature</i> defines a ring homomorphism from <i>W</i>(<i>k</i>) to <b>Z</b>, with kernel a <a href="/wiki/Prime_ideal" title="Prime ideal">prime ideal</a> <i>K<sub>P</sub></i>. These prime ideals are in <a href="/wiki/Bijection" title="Bijection">bijection</a> with the orderings <i>X<sub>k</sub></i> of <i>k</i> and constitute the minimal prime ideal <a href="/wiki/Spectrum_of_a_ring" title="Spectrum of a ring">spectrum</a> MinSpec <i>W</i>(<i>k</i>) of <i>W</i>(<i>k</i>). The bijection is a <a href="/wiki/Homeomorphism" title="Homeomorphism">homeomorphism</a> between MinSpec <i>W</i>(<i>k</i>) with the <a href="/wiki/Zariski_topology" title="Zariski topology">Zariski topology</a> and the set of orderings <i>X<sub>k</sub></i> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harrison_topology" title="Harrison topology">Harrison topology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>n</i>-th power of the fundamental ideal is additively generated by the <i>n</i>-fold <a href="/wiki/Pfister_form" title="Pfister form">Pfister forms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam316_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lam316-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Witt_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The Witt ring of <b>C</b>, and indeed any <a href="/wiki/Algebraically_closed_field" title="Algebraically closed field">algebraically closed field</a> or <a href="/wiki/Quadratically_closed_field" title="Quadratically closed field">quadratically closed field</a>, is <b>Z</b>/2<b>Z</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam34_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lam34-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Witt ring of <b>R</b> is <b>Z</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam34_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lam34-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Witt ring of a finite field <b>F</b><sub><i>q</i></sub> with <i>q</i> odd is <b>Z</b>/4<b>Z</b> if <i>q</i> is 3 mod 4 and isomorphic to the group ring (<b>Z</b>/2<b>Z</b>)[<i>F*</i>/<i>F*</i><sup>2</sup>] if <i>q</i> = 1 mod 4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam37_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lam37-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Witt ring of a <a href="/wiki/Local_field" title="Local field">local field</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximal_ideal_of_a_valuation" title="Maximal ideal of a valuation">maximal ideal</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ideal_norm" title="Ideal norm">norm</a> congruent to 1 modulo 4 is isomorphic to the group ring (<b>Z</b>/2<b>Z</b>)[<i>V</i>] where <i>V</i> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Klein_4-group" title="Klein 4-group">Klein 4-group</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam152_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lam152-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Witt ring of a local field with maximal ideal of norm congruent to 3 modulo 4 is (<b>Z</b>/4<b>Z</b>)[<i>C</i><sub>2</sub>] where <i>C</i><sub>2</sub> is a cyclic group of order 2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam152_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lam152-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Witt ring of <b>Q</b><sub>2</sub> is of order 32 and is given by<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam166_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lam166-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Invariants">Invariants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Witt_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Invariants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Certain invariants of a quadratic form can be regarded as functions on Witt classes. We have seen that dimension mod 2 is a function on classes: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discriminant_of_a_quadratic_form" title="Discriminant of a quadratic form">discriminant</a> is also well-defined. The <a href="/wiki/Hasse_invariant_of_a_quadratic_form" title="Hasse invariant of a quadratic form">Hasse invariant of a quadratic form</a> is again a well-defined function on Witt classes with values in the <a href="/wiki/Brauer_group" title="Brauer group">Brauer group</a> of the field of definition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam119_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lam119-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brauer.E2.80.93Wall_group">Brauer–Wall group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Witt_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Brauer–Wall group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Witt_ring_of_a_number_field">Witt ring of a number field</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Witt_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Witt ring of a number field">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Let <i>K</i> be a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Number_field" title="Number field">number field</a>. For quadratic forms over <i>K</i>, there is a <a href="/wiki/Hasse_invariant_of_a_quadratic_form" title="Hasse invariant of a quadratic form">Hasse invariant</a> ±1 for every <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finite_place" title="Finite place">finite place</a> corresponding to the <a href="/wiki/Hilbert_symbol" title="Hilbert symbol">Hilbert symbols</a>. The invariants of a form over a number field are precisely the dimension, discriminant, all local Hasse invariants and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signature_of_a_quadratic_form" title="Signature of a quadratic form">signatures</a> coming from real embeddings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CP16_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-CP16-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

Let [<i>a</i>, <i>b</i>] be the sequence of Hilbert symbols: it satisfies the conditions on <i>f</i> just stated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CP167_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-CP167-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Witt_ring_and_Milnor.27s_K-theory">Witt ring and Milnor's K-theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Witt_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Witt ring and Milnor's K-theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Let <i>k</i> be a field of characteristic not equal to <i>2</i>. The powers of the ideal <i>I</i> of forms of even dimension ("fundamental ideal") in <i>W(k)</i> form a descending <a href="/wiki/Filtration" title="Filtration">filtration</a> and one may consider the associated graded ring, that is the direct sum of quotients <i>I<sup>n</sup>/I<sup>(n+1)</sup></i>. Let <i>⟨a⟩</i> be the quadratic form <i>ax<sup>2</sup></i> considered as an element of the Witt ring. Then <i>⟨a⟩-⟨1⟩</i> is an element of <i>I</i> and correspondingly a product of the form</p>
<p><i>&lt;&lt;a<sub>1</sub>,...,a<sub>n</sub>&gt;&gt;=(⟨a<sub>1</sub>⟩-⟨1⟩)...(⟨a<sub>n</sub>⟩-⟨1⟩)</i></p>
<p>is an element of <i>I<sup>n</sup></i>. <a href="/wiki/John_Milnor" title="John Milnor">John Milnor</a> in a 1970 paper <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> proved that the mapping from <i>(k*)<sup>n</sup></i> to <i>I<sup>n</sup>/I<sup>(n+1)</sup></i> that sends <i>(a<sub>1</sub>,...,a<sub>n</sub>)</i> to <i>&lt;&lt;a<sub>1</sub>,...,a<sub>n</sub>&gt;&gt;</i> is multi-linear and maps Steinberg elements (elements such that for some <i>i</i>, <i>j</i> such that <i>i≠j</i> one has <i>a<sub>i</sub>+a<sub>j</sub>=1</i>) to zero. This means that this mapping defines a homomorphism from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milnor_ring" title="Milnor ring">Milnor ring</a> of <i>k</i> to the graded Witt ring. Milnor showed also that this homomorphism sends elements divisible by <i>2</i> to zero and that it is surjective. In the same paper he made a conjecture that this homomorphism is an isomorphism for all fields <i>k</i> (of characteristic different from <i>2</i>). This became known as the Milnor's conjecture on quadratic forms.</p>
<p>It was proved in 1996 (published in 2007) by Dmitry Orlov, Alexander Vishik and <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Voevodsky" title="Vladimir Voevodsky">Vladimir Voevodsky</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> leading to a rise of new understanding of the structure of quadratic forms over general fields.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grothendieck-Witt_ring">Grothendieck-Witt ring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Witt_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Grothendieck-Witt ring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <b>Grothendieck-Witt ring</b> <i>GW</i> is a related construction generated by isometry classes of nonsingular quadratic spaces with addition given by orthogonal sum and multiplication given by tensor product. Since two spaces that differ by a hyperbolic plane are not identified in <i>GW</i>, the inverse for the addition needs to be introduced formally through the construction that was discovered by Grothendieck (see <a href="/wiki/Grothendieck_group" title="Grothendieck group">Grothendieck group</a>). There is a natural homomorphism <i>GW</i> → <b>Z</b> given by dimension: a field is <a href="/wiki/Quadratically_closed_field" title="Quadratically closed field">quadratically closed</a> if and only if this is an isomorphism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam34_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lam34-18">[18]</a></sup> The hyperbolic spaces generate an ideal in <i>GW</i> and the Witt ring <i>W</i> is the quotient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam28_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lam28-36">[36]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exterior_power" title="Exterior power">exterior power</a> gives the Grothendieck-Witt ring the additional structure of a <a href="/wiki/%CE%9B-ring" title="Λ-ring">λ-ring</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GMS63_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-GMS63-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_2">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Witt_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The Grothendieck-Witt ring of <b>C</b>, and indeed any <a href="/wiki/Algebraically_closed_field" title="Algebraically closed field">algebraically closed field</a> or <a href="/wiki/Quadratically_closed_field" title="Quadratically closed field">quadratically closed field</a>, is <b>Z</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam34_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lam34-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Grothendieck-Witt ring of <b>R</b> is isomorphic to the group ring <b>Z</b>[<i>C</i><sub>2</sub>], where <i>C</i><sub>2</sub> is a cyclic group of order 2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam34_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lam34-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Grothendieck-Witt ring of any finite field of odd characteristic is <b>Z</b> ⊕ <b>Z</b>/2<b>Z</b> with trivial multiplication in the second component.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam36_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lam36-38">[38]</a></sup> The element <i>(1, 0)</i> corresponds to the quadratic form <i>⟨a⟩</i> where <i>a</i> is not a square in the finite field.</li>
<li>The Grothendieck-Witt ring of a local field with maximal ideal of norm congruent to 1 modulo 4 is isomorphic to <b>Z</b> ⊕ (<b>Z</b>/2<b>Z</b>)<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam152_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lam152-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Grothendieck-Witt ring of a local field with maximal ideal of norm congruent to 3 modulo 4 it is <b>Z</b> ⊕<b>Z</b>/4<b>Z</b> ⊕ <b>Z</b>/2<b>Z</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lam152_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lam152-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grothendieck-Witt_ring_and_motivic_stable_homotopy_groups_of_spheres">Grothendieck-Witt ring and motivic stable homotopy groups of spheres</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Witt_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Grothendieck-Witt ring and motivic stable homotopy groups of spheres">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It was shown recently by <a href="/wiki/Fabien_Morel" title="Fabien Morel">Fabien Morel</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> that the Grothendieck-Witt ring of a perfect field is isomorphic to the motivic stable homotopy group of spheres π<sub>0,0</sub>(S<sup>0,0</sup>) (see "<a href="/wiki/A%C2%B9_homotopy_theory" title="A¹ homotopy theory">A¹ homotopy theory</a>").</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Witt_equivalence">Witt equivalence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Witt_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Witt equivalence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two fields are said to be <b>Witt equivalent</b> if their Witt rings are isomorphic.</p>
<p>For global fields there is a local-to-global principle: two global fields are Witt equivalent if and only if there is a bijection between their places such that the corresponding local fields are Witt equivalent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In particular, two number fields <i>K</i> and <i>L</i> are Witt equivalent if and only if there is a bijection <i>T</i> between the places of <i>K</i> and the places of <i>L</i> and a group isomorphism <i>t</i> between their <a href="/wiki/Square_class" title="Square class">square-class groups</a>, preserving degree 2 Hilbert symbols. In this case the pair (<i>T</i>,<i>t</i>) is called a <b>reciprocity equivalence</b> or a <b>degree 2 Hilbert symbol equivalence</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Some variations and extensions of this condition, such as "tame degree <i>l</i> Hilbert symbol equivalence", have also been studied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Generalizations">Generalizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Witt_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Generalizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/L-theory" title="L-theory">L-theory</a></div>
<p>Witt groups can also be defined in the same way for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skew-symmetric_form" title="Skew-symmetric form">skew-symmetric forms</a>, and for <a href="/wiki/Quadratic_form" title="Quadratic form">quadratic forms</a>, and more generally <a href="/wiki/%CE%95-quadratic_form" title="Ε-quadratic form">ε-quadratic forms</a>, over any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/*-ring" title="*-ring">*-ring</a> <i>R</i>.</p>
<p>The resulting groups (and generalizations thereof) are known as the even-dimensional symmetric <a href="/wiki/L-theory" title="L-theory"><i>L</i>-groups</a> <i>L</i><sup>2<i>k</i></sup>(<i>R</i>) and even-dimensional quadratic <i>L</i>-groups <i>L</i><sub>2<i>k</i></sub>(<i>R</i>). The quadratic <i>L</i>-groups are 4-periodic, with <i>L</i><sub>0</sub>(<i>R</i>) being the Witt group of (1)-quadratic forms (symmetric), and <i>L</i><sub>2</sub>(<i>R</i>) being the Witt group of (-1)-quadratic forms (skew-symmetric); symmetric <i>L</i>-groups are not 4-periodic for all rings, hence they provide a less exact generalization.</p>
<p><i>L</i>-groups are central objects in <a href="/wiki/Surgery_theory" title="Surgery theory">surgery theory</a>, forming one of the three terms of the <a href="/wiki/Surgery_exact_sequence" title="Surgery exact sequence">surgery exact sequence</a>.</p>
