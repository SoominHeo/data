<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Nondestructive testing</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Nondestructive testing</b> or <b>Non-destructive testing</b> (<b>NDT</b>) is a wide group of analysis techniques used in science and technology industry to evaluate the properties of a material, component or system without causing damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cartz_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cartz-1">[1]</a></sup> The terms <b>Nondestructive examination</b> (<b>NDE</b>), <b>Nondestructive inspection</b> (<b>NDI</b>), and <b>Nondestructive evaluation</b> (<b>NDE</b>) are also commonly used to describe this technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Because NDT does not permanently alter the article being inspected, it is a highly valuable technique that can save both money and time in product evaluation, troubleshooting, and research. Common NDT methods include <a href="/wiki/Ultrasonic_testing" title="Ultrasonic testing">ultrasonic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic-particle_inspection" title="Magnetic-particle inspection">magnetic-particle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_penetrant_testing" title="Liquid penetrant testing">liquid penetrant</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiographic_testing" title="Radiographic testing">radiographic</a>, remote visual inspection (RVI), <a href="/wiki/Eddy-current_testing" title="Eddy-current testing">eddy-current testing</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cartz_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cartz-1">[1]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_interferometry" title="Optical interferometry">low coherence interferometry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> NDT is commonly used in <a href="/wiki/Forensic_engineering" title="Forensic engineering">forensic engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_engineering" title="Mechanical engineering">mechanical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_engineering" title="Petroleum engineering">petroleum engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Systems_engineering" title="Systems engineering">systems engineering</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aeronautical_engineering" title="Aeronautical engineering">aeronautical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cartz_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cartz-1">[1]</a></sup> Innovations in the field of nondestructive testing have had a profound impact on <a href="/wiki/Medical_imaging" title="Medical imaging">medical imaging</a>, including on <a href="/wiki/Echocardiography" title="Echocardiography">echocardiography</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_ultrasonography" title="Medical ultrasonography">medical ultrasonography</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Digital_radiography" title="Digital radiography">digital radiography</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods">Methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nondestructive_testing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>NDT methods may rely upon use of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a>, and inherent properties of materials to examine samples. This includes some kinds of <a href="/wiki/Microscopy" title="Microscopy">microscopy</a> to examine external surfaces in detail, although sample preparation techniques for <a href="/wiki/Metallography" title="Metallography">metallography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Optical_microscope" title="Optical microscope">optical microscopy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electron_microscope" title="Electron microscope">electron microscopy</a> are generally destructive as the surfaces must be made smooth through polishing or the sample must be electron transparent in thickness. The inside of a sample can be examined with penetrating radiation, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-rays" title="X-rays">X-rays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neutron_imaging" title="Neutron imaging">neutrons</a> or <a href="/wiki/Terahertz_nondestructive_evaluation" title="Terahertz nondestructive evaluation">terahertz radiation</a>. Sound waves are utilized in the case of ultrasonic testing. Contrast between a defect and the bulk of the sample may be enhanced for visual examination by the unaided eye by using liquids to penetrate <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(material)" title="Fatigue (material)">fatigue</a> cracks. One method (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_penetrant_testing" title="Liquid penetrant testing">liquid penetrant testing</a>) involves using dyes, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorescent" title="Fluorescent">fluorescent</a> or non-fluorescent, in fluids for non-magnetic materials, usually metals. Another commonly used NDT method used on ferrous materials involves the application of fine iron particles (either liquid or dry dust) that are applied to a part while it is in an externally magnetized state (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic-particle_inspection" title="Magnetic-particle inspection">magnetic-particle testing</a>). The particles will be attracted to leakage fields within the test object, and form on the objects surface. Magnetic particle testing can reveal surface &amp; some sub-surface defects within the part. <a href="/wiki/Thermoelectric_effect" title="Thermoelectric effect">Thermoelectric effect</a> (or use of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seebeck_effect" title="Seebeck effect">Seebeck effect</a>) uses thermal properties of an alloy to quickly and easily characterize many alloys. The <a href="/wiki/Chemical_test" title="Chemical test">chemical test</a>, or chemical spot test method, utilizes application of sensitive chemicals that can indicate the presence of individual alloying elements. Electrochemical methods, such as <a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_fatigue_crack_sensor" title="Electrochemical fatigue crack sensor">electrochemical fatigue crack sensors</a>, utilize the tendency of metal structural material to oxidize readily in order to detect progressive damage.</p>
<p>Analyzing and documenting a non-destructive failure mode can also be accomplished using a <a href="/wiki/High-speed_camera" title="High-speed camera">high-speed camera</a> recording continuously (movie-loop) until the failure is detected. Detecting the failure can be accomplished using a sound detector or stress gauge which produces a signal to trigger the high-speed camera. These high-speed cameras have advanced recording modes to capture some non-destructive failures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> After the failure the high-speed camera will stop recording. The capture images can be played back in <a href="/wiki/Slow_motion" title="Slow motion">slow motion</a> showing precisely what happen before, during and after the non-destructive event, image by image.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nondestructive_testing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>NDT is used in a variety of settings that covers a wide range of industrial activity, with new NDT methods and applications, being continuously developed. Non-destructive testing methods are routinely applied in industries where a failure of a component would cause significant hazard or economic loss, such as in transportation, pressure vessels, building structures, piping, and hoisting equipment.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weld_verification">Weld verification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nondestructive_testing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Weld verification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In manufacturing, <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welds</a> are commonly used to join two or more metal parts. Because these connections may encounter loads and <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(material)" title="Fatigue (material)">fatigue</a> during <a href="/wiki/Product_lifetime" title="Product lifetime">product lifetime</a>, there is a chance that they may fail if not created to proper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specification" title="Specification">specification</a>. For example, the base metal must reach a certain temperature during the welding process, must cool at a specific rate, and must be welded with compatible materials or the joint may not be strong enough to hold the parts together, or cracks may form in the weld causing it to fail. The typical welding defects (lack of fusion of the weld to the base metal, cracks or porosity inside the weld, and variations in weld density) could cause a structure to break or a pipeline to rupture.</p>
<p>Welds may be tested using NDT techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Industrial_radiography" title="Industrial radiography">industrial radiography</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_CT_scanning" title="Industrial CT scanning">industrial CT scanning</a> using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-rays" title="X-rays">X-rays</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_rays" title="Gamma rays">gamma rays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ultrasonic_testing" title="Ultrasonic testing">ultrasonic testing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_penetrant_testing" title="Liquid penetrant testing">liquid penetrant testing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_particle_inspection" title="Magnetic particle inspection">magnetic particle inspection</a> or via <a href="/wiki/Eddy_current" title="Eddy current">eddy current</a>. In a proper weld, these tests would indicate a lack of cracks in the radiograph, show clear passage of sound through the weld and back, or indicate a clear surface without penetrant captured in cracks.</p>
<p>Welding techniques may also be actively monitored with acoustic emission techniques before production to design the best set of parameters to use to properly join two materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UltrasonicMethods_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-UltrasonicMethods-6">[6]</a></sup> In the case of high stress or safety critical welds, weld monitoring will be employed to confirm the specified welding parameters (arc current, arc voltage, travel speed, heat input etc.) are being adhered to those stated in the welding procedure. This verifies the weld as correct to procedure prior to nondestructive evaluation and metallurgy tests.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structural_mechanics">Structural mechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nondestructive_testing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Structural mechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Structure can be complex systems that undergo different loads during their lifetime, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Lithium-ion_battery" title="Lithium-ion battery">Lithium-ion batteries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Some complex structures, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turbo_machinery" title="Turbo machinery">turbo machinery</a> in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid-fuel_rocket" title="Liquid-fuel rocket">liquid-fuel rocket</a>, can also cost millions of dollars. Engineers will commonly model these structures as coupled second-order systems, approximating dynamic structure components with <a href="/wiki/Spring_(device)" title="Spring (device)">springs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">masses</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dashpot" title="Dashpot">dampers</a>. The resulting sets of differential equations are then used to derive a transfer function that models the behavior of the system.</p>
<p>In NDT, the structure undergoes a dynamic input, such as the tap of a hammer or a controlled impulse. Key properties, such as <a href="/wiki/Displacement_(vector)" title="Displacement (vector)">displacement</a> or <a href="/wiki/Acceleration" title="Acceleration">acceleration</a> at different points of the structure, are measured as the corresponding output. This output is recorded and compared to the corresponding output given by the transfer function and the known input. Differences may indicate an inappropriate model (which may alert engineers to unpredicted instabilities or performance outside of tolerances), failed components, or an inadequate <a href="/wiki/Control_system" title="Control system">control system</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiography_in_medicine">Radiography in medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nondestructive_testing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Radiography in medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>As a system, the <a href="/wiki/Human_body" title="Human body">human body</a> is difficult to model as a complete <a href="/wiki/Transfer_function" title="Transfer function">transfer function</a>. Elements of the body, however, such as <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bones</a> or molecules, have a known response to certain radiographic inputs, such as <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">x-rays</a> or <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">magnetic resonance</a>. Coupled with the controlled introduction of a known element, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barium_swallow" title="Barium swallow">digested barium</a>, radiography can be used to image parts or functions of the body by measuring and interpreting the response to the radiographic input. In this manner, many <a href="/wiki/Bone_fracture" title="Bone fracture">bone fractures</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">diseases</a> may be detected and localized in preparation for treatment. X-rays may also be used to examine the interior of mechanical systems in manufacturing using NDT techniques, as well.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_events_in_early_academic_and_industrial_NDT">Notable events in early academic and industrial NDT</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nondestructive_testing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Notable events in early academic and industrial NDT">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>1854 Hartford, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>: a boiler at the Fales and Gray Car works explodes, killing 21 people and seriously injuring 50. Within a decade, the State of Connecticut passes a law requiring annual inspection (in this case visual) of boilers.</li>
<li>1880 - 1920 The "Oil and Whiting" method of crack detection<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> is used in the railroad industry to find cracks in heavy steel parts. (A part is soaked in thinned oil, then painted with a white coating that dries to a powder. Oil seeping out from cracks turns the white powder brown, allowing the cracks to be detected.) This was the precursor to modern liquid penetrant tests.</li>
<li>1895 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Conrad_R%C3%B6ntgen" title="Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen">Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen</a> discovers what are now known as X-rays. In his first paper he discusses the possibility of flaw detection.</li>
<li>1920 Dr. H. H. Lester begins development of industrial radiography for metals.</li>
<li>1924 — Lester uses radiography to examine castings to be installed in a Boston Edison Company steam pressure power plant.</li>
<li>1926 The first electromagnetic eddy current instrument is available to measure material thicknesses.</li>
<li>1927 - 1928 Magnetic induction system to detect flaws in railroad track developed by Dr. Elmer Sperry and H.C. Drake.</li>
<li>1929 Magnetic particle methods and equipment pioneered (A.V. DeForest and F.B. Doane.)</li>
<li>1930s Robert F. Mehl demonstrates radiographic imaging using gamma radiation from Radium, which can examine thicker components than the low-energy <a href="/wiki/X-ray_generator" title="X-ray generator">X-ray machines</a> available at the time.</li>
<li>1935 - 1940 Liquid penetrant tests developed (Betz, Doane, and DeForest)</li>
<li>1935 - 1940s <a href="/wiki/Eddy_current" title="Eddy current">Eddy current</a> instruments developed (H.C. Knerr, C. Farrow, Theo Zuschlag, and Fr. F. Foerster).</li>
<li>1940 - 1944 <a href="/wiki/Ultrasonic_testing" title="Ultrasonic testing">Ultrasonic test</a> method developed in USA by Dr. <a href="/wiki/Floyd_Firestone" title="Floyd Firestone">Floyd Firestone</a>, who applies for a U.S. invention patent for same on May 27, 1940 and is issued the U.S. patent as grant no. 2,280,226 on April 21, 1942. Extracts from the first two paragraphs of this seminal patent for a nondestructive testing method succinctly describe the basics of ultrasonic testing. "My invention pertains to a device for detecting the presence of inhomogeneities of density or elasticity in materials. For instance if a casting has a hole or a crack within it, my device allows the presence of the flaw to be detected and its position located, even though the flaw lies entirely within the casting and no portion of it extends out to the surface. ... The general principle of my device consists of sending high frequency vibrations into the part to be inspected, and the determination of the time intervals of arrival of the direct and reflected vibrations at one or more stations on the surface of the part." Medical <a href="/wiki/Echocardiography" title="Echocardiography">echocardiography</a> is an offshoot of this technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>1946 First neutron radiographs produced by Peters.</li>
<li>1950 The <a href="/wiki/Schmidt_hammer" title="Schmidt hammer">Schmidt Hammer</a> (also known as "Swiss Hammer") is invented. The instrument uses the world’s first patented non-destructive testing method for concrete.</li>
<li>1950 J. Kaiser introduces acoustic emission as an NDT method.</li>
</ul>
<p><i>(Basic Source for above: Hellier, 2001) Note the number of advancements made during the WWII era, a time when industrial quality control was growing in importance.</i></p>
<ul>
<li>1963 Frederick G. Weighart's<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_F._McNulty_(U.S._radio_engineer)" title="James F. McNulty (U.S. radio engineer)">James F. McNulty (U.S. radio engineer)</a>’s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> co-invention of <a href="/wiki/Digital_radiography" title="Digital radiography">Digital radiography</a> is an offshoot of the pairs development of nondestructive test equipment at Automation Industries, Inc., then, in El Segundo, California. See James F. McNulty also at article <a href="/wiki/Ultrasonic_testing" title="Ultrasonic testing">Ultrasonic testing</a>.</li>
<li>1996 Rolf Diederichs founded the first Open Access NDT Journal in the Internet. Today the Open Access NDT Database NDT.net</li>
<li>2008 Academia NDT International has been officially founded and has its base office in Brescia (Italy) www.academia-ndt.org</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personnel_training.2C_qualification_and_certification">Personnel training, qualification and certification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nondestructive_testing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Personnel training, qualification and certification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Successful and consistent application of nondestructive testing techniques depends heavily on personnel training, experience and integrity. Personnel involved in application of industrial NDT methods and interpretation of results should be certified, and in some industrial sectors certification is enforced by law or by the applied codes and standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nondestructive_testing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following definitions for <i>qualification</i> and <i>certification</i> are given in <a href="/wiki/DIN_EN_ISO_9712" title="DIN EN ISO 9712">ISO 9712</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISO9712_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ISO9712-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Certification:</b> "Procedure, used by the certification body to confirm that the qualification requirements for a method, level and sector have been fulfilled, leading to the issuing of a certificate".</li>
<li><b>Qualification:</b> "Demonstration of physical attributes, knowledge, skill, training and experience required to properly perform NDT tasks".</li>
</ul>
<p>In US standards and codes, while a very similar definition of qualification is included in ASNT SNT-TC-1A, certification is simply defined as: "Written testimony of qualification".</p>
<p>In the aerospace sector, EN 4179:2009 contains the following definitions:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EN4179_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-EN4179-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Certification:</b> Written statement by an employer that an individual has met the applicable requirements of this standard.</li>
<li><b>Qualification:</b> The skills, training, knowledge, examinations, experience and visual capability required for personnel to properly perform to a particular level.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Training">Training</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nondestructive_testing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Training">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Non-Destructive Testing (NDT) training is provided for people working in many industries. It is generally necessary that the <a href="/wiki/Candidate" title="Candidate">candidate</a> successfully completes a theoretical and practical training program, as well as have performed several hundred hours of practical application of the particular method they wish to be trained in. At this point, they may pass a certification examination. While online training has become more popular, many certifying bodies will require additional practical training.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Certification_schemes">Certification schemes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nondestructive_testing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Certification schemes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are two approaches in personnel certification:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li><b>Employer Based Certification</b>: Under this concept the employer compiles their own <i>Written Practice</i>. The written practice defines the responsibilities of each level of certification, as implemented by the company, and describes the training, experience and examination requirements for each level of certification. In industrial sectors the written practices are usually based on recommended practice SNT-TC-1A of the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_for_Nondestructive_Testing" title="American Society for Nondestructive Testing">American Society for Nondestructive Testing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> ANSI standard CP-189 outlines requirements for any written practice that conforms to the standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> For aviation, space, and defense (ASD) applications NAS 410 sets further requirements for NDT personnel, and is published by AIA - Aerospace Industries Association, which is made up of US aerospace airframe and powerplant manufacturers. This is the basis document for EN 4179<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EN4179_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-EN4179-17">[17]</a></sup> and other (USA) NIST-recognized aerospace standards for the Qualification and Certification (employer-based) of Nondestructive Testing personnel. NAS 410 also sets the requirements also for "National NDT Boards", which allow and proscribe personal certification schemes. NAS 410 allows ASNT Certification as a portion of the qualifications needed for ASD certification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Personal Central Certification</b>: The concept of central certification is that an NDT operator can obtain certification from a central certification authority, that is recognized by most employers, third parties and/or government authorities. Industrial standards for central certification schemes include ISO 9712,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISO9712_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-ISO9712-16">[16]</a></sup> and ANSI/ASNT CP-106<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> (used for the ASNT ACCP <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> scheme). Certification under these standards involves training, work experience under supervision and passing a written and practical examination set up by the independent certification authority. EN 473<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EN473_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-EN473-24">[24]</a></sup> was another central certification scheme, very similar to ISO 9712, which was withdrawn when CEN replaced it with <i>EN ISO 9712</i> in 2012.</li>
</ol>
<p>In the United States employer based schemes are the norm, however central certification schemes exist as well. The most notable is <i>ASNT Level III</i> (established in 1976-1977), which is organized by the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_for_Nondestructive_Testing" title="American Society for Nondestructive Testing">American Society for Nondestructive Testing</a> for Level 3 NDT personnel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <i>NAVSEA 250-1500</i> is another US central certification scheme, specifically developed for use in the naval nuclear program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Central certification is more widely used in the European Union, where certifications are issued by <a href="/wiki/Personnel_certification_body" title="Personnel certification body">accredited bodies</a> (independent organizations conforming to ISO 17024 and accredited by a national accreditation authority like UKAS). The <a href="/wiki/Pressure_Equipment_Directive" title="Pressure Equipment Directive">Pressure Equipment Directive</a> (97/23/EC) actually enforces central personnel certification for the initial testing of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam_boiler" title="Steam boiler">steam boilers</a> and some categories of <a href="/wiki/Pressure_vessel" title="Pressure vessel">pressure vessels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Piping" title="Piping">piping</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> European Standards harmonized with this directive specify personnel certification to EN 473. Certifications issued by a national NDT society which is a member of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=European_Federation_of_NDT&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="European Federation of NDT (page does not exist)">European Federation of NDT</a> (EFNDT) are mutually acceptable by the other member societies <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> under a multilateral recognition agreement.</p>
<p>Canada also implements an ISO 9712 central certification scheme, which is administered by <a href="/wiki/Natural_Resources_Canada" title="Natural Resources Canada">Natural Resources Canada</a>, a government department.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Aerospace" title="Aerospace">aerospace</a> sector worldwide sticks to employer based schemes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In America it is based mostly on AIA-NAS-410 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and in the European Union on the equivalent and very similar standard EN 4179.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EN4179_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-EN4179-17">[17]</a></sup> However EN 4179:2009 includes an option for central qualification and certification by a <i>National aerospace NDT board</i> or <i>NANDTB</i> (paragraph 4.5.2).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Levels_of_certification">Levels of certification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nondestructive_testing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Levels of certification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most NDT personnel certification schemes listed above specify three "levels" of qualification and/or certification, usually designated as <i>Level 1</i>, <i>Level 2</i> and <i>Level 3</i> (although some codes specify Roman numerals, like <i>Level II</i>). The roles and responsibilities of personnel in each level are generally as follows (there are slight differences or variations between different codes and standards):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISO9712_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-ISO9712-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EN4179_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-EN4179-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Level 1</b> are <a href="/wiki/Technician" title="Technician">technicians</a> qualified to perform only specific <a href="/wiki/Calibration" title="Calibration">calibrations</a> and tests under close supervision and direction by higher level personnel. They can only report test results. Normally they work following specific work instructions for testing procedures and rejection criteria.</li>
<li><b>Level 2</b> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Engineers" title="Engineers">engineers</a> or experienced technicians who are able to set up and calibrate testing equipment, conduct the inspection according to codes and standards (instead of following work instructions) and compile work instructions for Level 1 technicians. They are also authorized to report, interpret, evaluate and document testing results. They can also supervise and train Level 1 technicians. In addition to testing methods, they must be familiar with applicable codes and standards and have some knowledge of the manufacture and service of tested products.</li>
<li><b>Level 3</b> are usually specialized engineers or very experienced technicians. They can establish NDT techniques and procedures and interpret codes and standards. They also direct NDT laboratories and have central role in personnel certification. They are expected to have wider knowledge covering materials, fabrication and product technology.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nondestructive_testing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The standard US terminology for Nondestructive testing is defined in standard ASTM E-1316.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-E1316_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-E1316-34">[34]</a></sup> Some definitions may be different in European standard EN 1330.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Indication </dt>
<dd>The response or evidence from an examination, such as a blip on the screen of an instrument. Indications are classified as <i>true</i> or <i>false</i>. <i>False indications</i> are those caused by factors not related to the principles of the testing method or by improper implementation of the method, like film damage in radiography, electrical interference in ultrasonic testing etc. <i>True indications</i> are further classified as <i>relevant</i> and <i>non relevant</i>. <i>Relevant indications</i> are those caused by flaws. <i>Non relevant indications</i> are those caused by known features of the tested object, like gaps, threads, case hardening etc.</dd>
<dt>Interpretation </dt>
<dd>Determining if an indication is of a type to be investigated. For example, in electromagnetic testing, indications from metal loss are considered flaws because they should usually be investigated, but indications due to variations in the material properties may be harmless and nonrelevant.</dd>
<dt>Flaw </dt>
<dd>A type of discontinuity that must be investigated to see if it is rejectable. For example, porosity in a weld or metal loss.</dd>
<dt>Evaluation </dt>
<dd>Determining if a flaw is rejectable. For example, is porosity in a weld larger than acceptable by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Mechanical_Engineers#ASME_Codes_and_Standards" title="American Society of Mechanical Engineers">code</a>?</dd>
<dt>Defect </dt>
<dd>A flaw that is rejectable — i.e. does not meet acceptance criteria. Defects are generally removed or repaired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-E1316_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-E1316-34">[34]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reliability_and_statistics">Reliability and statistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nondestructive_testing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Reliability and statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Probability of detection (POD) tests are a standard way to evaluate a nondestructive testing technique in a given set of circumstances, for example "What is the POD of lack of fusion flaws in pipe welds using manual ultrasonic testing?" The POD will usually increase with flaw size. A common error in POD tests is to assume that the percentage of flaws detected is the POD, whereas the percentage of flaws detected is merely the first step in the analysis. Since the number of flaws tested is necessarily a limited number (non-infinite), statistical methods must be used to determine the POD for all possible defects, beyond the limited number tested. Another common error in POD tests is to define the statistical sampling units (test items) as flaws, whereas a true sampling unit is an item that may or may not contain a flaw.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Guidelines for correct application of statistical methods to POD tests can be found in ASTM E2862 Standard Practice for Probability of Detection Analysis for Hit/Miss Data and MIL-HDBK-1823A Nondestructive Evaluation System Reliability Assessment, from the U.S. Department of Defense Handbook.</p>
