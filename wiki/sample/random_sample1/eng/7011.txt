<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Flight instruments</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Flight instruments</b> are the instruments in the cockpit of an aircraft that provide the pilot with information about the flight situation of that aircraft, such as <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a>, <a href="/wiki/Airspeed" title="Airspeed">airspeed</a> and direction. They improve safety by allowing the pilot to fly the aircraft in level flight, and make turns, without a reference outside the aircraft such as the horizon. <a href="/wiki/Visual_flight_rules" title="Visual flight rules">Visual flight rules</a> (VFR) require an airspeed indicator, an altimeter, and a compass or other suitable magnetic direction indicator. <a href="/wiki/Instrument_flight_rules" title="Instrument flight rules">Instrument flight rules</a> (IFR) additionally require a gyroscopic pitch-bank (artificial horizon), direction (directional gyro) and rate of turn indicator, plus a slip-skid indicator, adjustable altimeter, and a clock. Flight into <a href="/wiki/Instrument_meteorological_conditions" title="Instrument meteorological conditions">Instrument meteorological conditions</a> (IMC) require <a href="/wiki/Radio_navigation" title="Radio navigation">radio navigation</a> instruments for precise takeoffs and landings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faa_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-faa-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:3-1</sup></p>
<p>The term is sometimes used loosely as a synonym for <a href="/wiki/Cockpit" title="Cockpit">cockpit instruments</a> as a whole, in which context it can include engine instruments, navigational and communication equipment. Many modern aircraft have <a href="/wiki/Electronic_flight_instrument_system" title="Electronic flight instrument system">electronic flight instrument systems</a>.</p>
<p>Most regulated aircraft have these flight instruments as dictated by the US <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Federal_Regulations" title="Code of Federal Regulations">Code of Federal Regulations</a>, Title 14, Part 91. They are grouped according to <a href="/wiki/Pitot-static_system" title="Pitot-static system">pitot-static system</a>, <a href="/wiki/Compass" title="Compass">compass</a> systems, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gyroscopic" title="Gyroscopic">gyroscopic</a> instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faa_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-faa-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:3-1</sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pitot-Static_Systems">Pitot-Static Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pitot-Static Systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Altimeter">Altimeter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Altimeter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Altimeter" title="Altimeter">Altimeter</a></div>
<dl>
<dd>
<div class="floatleft"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:3-Pointer_Altimeter.svg"><img alt="3-Pointer Altimeter.svg" data-file-height="650" data-file-width="638" height="61" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/3-Pointer_Altimeter.svg/60px-3-Pointer_Altimeter.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/3-Pointer_Altimeter.svg/90px-3-Pointer_Altimeter.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/3-Pointer_Altimeter.svg/120px-3-Pointer_Altimeter.svg.png 2x" width="60"/></a></div>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>The altimeter shows the aircraft's altitude above sea-level by measuring the difference between the pressure in a stack of <a href="/wiki/Pressure_measurement#Aneroid" title="Pressure measurement">aneroid</a> capsules inside the altimeter and the atmospheric pressure obtained through the <a href="/wiki/Static_pressure" title="Static pressure">static</a> system. It is adjustable for local barometric pressure which must be set correctly to obtain accurate altitude readings. As the aircraft ascends, the capsules expand and the static pressure drops, causing the altimeter to indicate a higher altitude. The opposite effect occurs when descending. With the advancement in aviation and increased altitude ceiling the altimeter dial had to be altered for use both at higher and lower altitudes. Hence when the needles were indicating lower altitudes i.e. the first 360 degree operation of the pointers was delineated by the appearance of a small window with oblique lines warning the pilot that he/she is nearer to the ground. This modification was introduced in the early sixties after the recurrence of air accidents caused by the confusion in the pilot's mind. At higher altitudes the window will disappear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faa_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-faa-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:3-3</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Airspeed_indicator">Airspeed indicator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Airspeed indicator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Airspeed_indicator" title="Airspeed indicator">Airspeed indicator</a></div>
<dl>
<dd>
<div class="floatleft"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Airspeed_indicator.svg"><img alt="Airspeed indicator.svg" data-file-height="362" data-file-width="360" height="60" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Airspeed_indicator.svg/60px-Airspeed_indicator.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Airspeed_indicator.svg/90px-Airspeed_indicator.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Airspeed_indicator.svg/120px-Airspeed_indicator.svg.png 2x" width="60"/></a></div>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>The airspeed indicator shows the aircraft's speed (usually in <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">knots</a> ) relative to the surrounding air. It works by measuring the ram-air pressure in the aircraft's <a href="/wiki/Pitot_tube" title="Pitot tube">Pitot tube</a> relative to the ambient static pressure. The <a href="/wiki/Indicated_airspeed" title="Indicated airspeed">Indicated airspeed</a> (IAS) must be corrected for nonstandard pressure and temperature in order to obtain the <a href="/wiki/True_airspeed" title="True airspeed">True airspeed</a> (TAS). The instrument is color coded to indicate important airspeeds such as the stall speed, never-exceed airspeed, or safe <a href="/wiki/Flap_(aeronautics)" title="Flap (aeronautics)">flap</a> operation speeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faa_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-faa-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:3-7 to 3-8</sup></p>
<p><br/></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vertical_speed_indicator">Vertical speed indicator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vertical speed indicator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertical_speed_indicator" title="Vertical speed indicator">Vertical speed indicator</a></div>
<dl>
<dd>
<div class="floatleft"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vertical_speed_indicator.PNG"><img alt="Vertical speed indicator.PNG" data-file-height="463" data-file-width="463" height="60" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Vertical_speed_indicator.PNG/60px-Vertical_speed_indicator.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Vertical_speed_indicator.PNG/90px-Vertical_speed_indicator.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Vertical_speed_indicator.PNG/120px-Vertical_speed_indicator.PNG 2x" width="60"/></a></div>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>The VSI (also sometimes called a <a href="/wiki/Variometer" title="Variometer">variometer</a>, or rate of climb indicator) senses changing air pressure, and displays that information to the pilot as a rate of climb or descent in feet per minute, meters per second or knots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faa_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-faa-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:3-8 to 3-9</sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compass_Systems">Compass Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Compass Systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_compass">Magnetic compass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Magnetic compass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Compass" title="Compass">Compass</a></div>
<dl>
<dd>
<div class="floatleft"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aero_Magnetic_Compass.jpg"><img alt="Aero Magnetic Compass.jpg" data-file-height="334" data-file-width="323" height="62" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Aero_Magnetic_Compass.jpg/60px-Aero_Magnetic_Compass.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Aero_Magnetic_Compass.jpg/90px-Aero_Magnetic_Compass.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Aero_Magnetic_Compass.jpg/120px-Aero_Magnetic_Compass.jpg 2x" width="60"/></a></div>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>The compass shows the aircraft's heading relative to magnetic north. Errors include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compass_variation" title="Compass variation">Variation</a>, or the difference between magnetic and true direction, and Deviation, caused by the electrical wiring in the aircraft, which requires a Compass Correction Card. Additionally, the compass is subject to Dip Errors. While reliable in steady level flight it can give confusing indications when <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_compass_turns" title="Aircraft compass turns">turning</a>, climbing, descending, or accelerating due to the inclination of the Earth's magnetic field. For this reason, the <a href="/wiki/Heading_indicator" title="Heading indicator">heading indicator</a> is also used for aircraft operation, but periodically calibrated against the compass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faa_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-faa-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:3-9 to 3-13, 3-19</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gyroscopic_Systems">Gyroscopic Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Gyroscopic Systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Attitude_Indicator">Attitude Indicator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Attitude Indicator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Attitude_indicator" title="Attitude indicator">Attitude indicator</a></div>
<dl>
<dd>
<div class="floatleft"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Attitude_indicator_level_flight.svg"><img alt="Attitude indicator level flight.svg" data-file-height="420" data-file-width="420" height="60" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Attitude_indicator_level_flight.svg/60px-Attitude_indicator_level_flight.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Attitude_indicator_level_flight.svg/90px-Attitude_indicator_level_flight.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Attitude_indicator_level_flight.svg/120px-Attitude_indicator_level_flight.svg.png 2x" width="60"/></a></div>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>The attitude indicator (also known as an <i>artificial horizon</i>) shows the aircraft's relation to the horizon. From this the pilot can tell whether the wings are level (<a href="/wiki/Flight_dynamics" title="Flight dynamics">roll</a>) and if the aircraft nose is pointing above or below the horizon (<a href="/wiki/Flight_dynamics" title="Flight dynamics">pitch</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faa_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-faa-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:3-18 to 3-19</sup> This is a primary instrument for instrument flight and is also useful in conditions of poor visibility. Pilots are trained to use other instruments in combination should this instrument or its power fail.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heading_Indicator">Heading Indicator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Heading Indicator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Heading_indicator" title="Heading indicator">Heading indicator</a></div>
<dl>
<dd>
<div class="floatleft"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Heading_indicator.svg"><img alt="Heading indicator.svg" data-file-height="340" data-file-width="340" height="60" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Heading_indicator.svg/60px-Heading_indicator.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Heading_indicator.svg/90px-Heading_indicator.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Heading_indicator.svg/120px-Heading_indicator.svg.png 2x" width="60"/></a></div>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>The heading indicator (also known as the directional gyro, or DG) displays the aircraft's heading with respect to magnetic north when set with a compass. Bearing friction causes drift errors from <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">precession</a>, which must be periodically corrected by calibrating the instrument to the magnetic compass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faa_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-faa-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:3-19 to 3-20</sup> In many advanced aircraft (including almost all jet aircraft), the heading indicator is replaced by a <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_situation_indicator" title="Horizontal situation indicator">horizontal situation indicator</a> (HSI) which provides the same heading information, but also assists with navigation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Turn_Indicator">Turn Indicator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Turn Indicator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Turn_and_slip_indicator" title="Turn and slip indicator">Turn and slip indicator</a></div>
<dl>
<dd>
<div class="floatleft"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Turn_coordinator_-_coordinated.svg"><img alt="Turn coordinator - coordinated.svg" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="538" height="60" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Turn_coordinator_-_coordinated.svg/60px-Turn_coordinator_-_coordinated.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Turn_coordinator_-_coordinated.svg/90px-Turn_coordinator_-_coordinated.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Turn_coordinator_-_coordinated.svg/120px-Turn_coordinator_-_coordinated.svg.png 2x" width="60"/></a></div>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>These include the Turn-and-Slip Indicator and the Turn Coordinator, which indicate rotation about the <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_principal_axes" title="Aircraft principal axes">longitudinal axis</a>. They include an <a href="/wiki/Inclinometer" title="Inclinometer">inclinometer</a> to indicate if the aircraft is in <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_flight" title="Coordinated flight">Coordinated flight</a>, or in a <a href="/wiki/Slip_(aerodynamics)" title="Slip (aerodynamics)">Slip</a> or <a href="/wiki/Skid_(aerodynamics)" title="Skid (aerodynamics)">Skid</a>. Additional marks indicate a <a href="/wiki/Standard_rate_turn" title="Standard rate turn">Standard rate turn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faa_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-faa-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:3-20 to 3-22</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_Director_Systems">Flight Director Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Flight Director Systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_situation_indicator" title="Horizontal situation indicator">Horizontal situation indicator</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attitude_Director_Indicator" title="Attitude Director Indicator">Attitude Director Indicator</a></div>
<p>These include the Horizontal Situation Indicator (HSI) and Attitude Director Indicator (ADI). The HSI combines the magnetic compass with navigation signals and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glide_slope" title="Glide slope">Glide slope</a>. The navigation information comes from a <a href="/wiki/VHF_omnidirectional_range" title="VHF omnidirectional range">VOR</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Localizer" title="Localizer">Localizer</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">GPS</a>. The ADI is an Attitude Indicator with computer-driven steering bars, a task reliever during instrument flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faa_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-faa-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:3-22 to 3-23,7-10</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Navigational_Systems">Navigational Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Navigational Systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Very-High_Frequency_Omnidirectional_Range_.28VOR.29">Very-High Frequency Omnidirectional Range (VOR)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Very-High Frequency Omnidirectional Range (VOR)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dd>
<div class="floatleft"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vor_indicator.png"><img alt="Vor indicator.png" data-file-height="196" data-file-width="200" height="59" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Vor_indicator.png/60px-Vor_indicator.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Vor_indicator.png/90px-Vor_indicator.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Vor_indicator.png/120px-Vor_indicator.png 2x" width="60"/></a></div>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>The VOR indicator instrument includes a <a href="/wiki/Course_deviation_indicator" title="Course deviation indicator">Course deviation indicator</a> (CDI), Omnibearing Selector (OBS), TO/FROM indicator, and Flags. The CDI shows an aircraft's lateral position in relation to a selected radial track. It is used for orientation, tracking to or from a station, and course interception.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faa_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-faa-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:7-8 to 7-11</sup></p>
<p><br/></p>
<p><br/></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nondirectional_Radio_Beacon_.28NDB.29">Nondirectional Radio Beacon (NDB)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Nondirectional Radio Beacon (NDB)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Non-directional_beacon" title="Non-directional beacon">Non-directional beacon</a></div>
<dl>
<dd>
<div class="floatleft"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Adf_rmi.jpg"><img alt="Adf rmi.jpg" data-file-height="180" data-file-width="184" height="59" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Adf_rmi.jpg/60px-Adf_rmi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Adf_rmi.jpg/90px-Adf_rmi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Adf_rmi.jpg/120px-Adf_rmi.jpg 2x" width="60"/></a></div>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automatic_direction_finder" title="Automatic direction finder">Automatic direction finder</a> (ADF) indicator instrument can be a fixed-card, movable card, or a <a href="/wiki/Radio_direction_finder#RMI" title="Radio direction finder">Radio magnetic indicator</a> (RMI). An RMI is remotely coupled to a gyrocompass so that it automatically rotates the azimuth card to represent aircraft heading.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faa_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-faa-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:7-3 to 7-4</sup> While simple ADF displays may have only one needle, a typical RMI has two, coupled to different ADF receivers, allowing for <a href="/wiki/Position_fixing" title="Position fixing">position fixing</a> using one instrument.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Layout">Layout</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Layout">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Six_flight_instruments.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="927" data-file-width="1503" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Six_flight_instruments.JPG/250px-Six_flight_instruments.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Six_flight_instruments.JPG/375px-Six_flight_instruments.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Six_flight_instruments.JPG/500px-Six_flight_instruments.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>

Six basic instruments in a light twin-engine <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">airplane</a> arranged in a "basic-T". </div>
<p>Most aircraft are equipped with a standard set of <b>flight instruments</b> which give the pilot information about the aircraft's attitude, airspeed, and altitude.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="T_arrangement">T arrangement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: T arrangement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most US aircraft built since the 1940s have flight instruments arranged in a standardized pattern called the "T" arrangement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Natola_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Natola-2">[2]</a></sup> The attitude indicator is in the top center, airspeed to the left, altimeter to the right and heading indicator under the attitude indicator. The other two, turn-coordinator and vertical-speed, are usually found under the airspeed and altimeter, but are given more latitude in placement. The magnetic compass will be above the instrument panel, often on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windscreen" title="Windscreen">windscreen</a> centerpost. In newer aircraft with <a href="/wiki/Glass_cockpit" title="Glass cockpit">glass cockpit</a> instruments the layout of the displays conform to the basic T arrangement.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Standard_instrument_set_up_in_a_cessna_172.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Standard_instrument_set_up_in_a_cessna_172.jpg/220px-Standard_instrument_set_up_in_a_cessna_172.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Standard_instrument_set_up_in_a_cessna_172.jpg/330px-Standard_instrument_set_up_in_a_cessna_172.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Standard_instrument_set_up_in_a_cessna_172.jpg/440px-Standard_instrument_set_up_in_a_cessna_172.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Standard_instrument_set_up_in_a_cessna_172.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Typical instrument configuration of a Cessna 172</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1929, Jimmy Doolittle became the first pilot to take off, fly and land an airplane using instruments alone, without a view outside the cockpit. In 1937, the British <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> (RAF) chose a set of six essential flight instruments<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> which would remain the standard panel used for flying in <a href="/wiki/Instrument_meteorological_conditions" title="Instrument meteorological conditions">instrument meteorological conditions</a> (IMC) for the next 20 years. They were:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Altimeter" title="Altimeter">altimeter</a> (feet)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Airspeed_indicator" title="Airspeed indicator">airspeed indicator</a> (knots)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turn_and_bank_indicator" title="Turn and bank indicator">turn and bank indicator</a> (turn direction and coordination)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertical_speed_indicator" title="Vertical speed indicator">vertical speed indicator</a> (feet per minute)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_horizon" title="Artificial horizon">artificial horizon</a> (attitude indication)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Directional_gyro" title="Directional gyro">directional gyro / heading indicator</a> (degrees)</li>
</ul>
<p>This panel arrangement was incorporated into all RAF aircraft built to <a href="/wiki/List_of_Air_Ministry_specifications" title="List of Air Ministry specifications">official specification</a> from 1938, such as the <a href="/wiki/Miles_Master" title="Miles Master">Miles Master</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawker_Hurricane" title="Hawker Hurricane">Hawker Hurricane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Supermarine_Spitfire" title="Supermarine Spitfire">Supermarine Spitfire</a>, and 4-engined <a href="/wiki/Avro_Lancaster" title="Avro Lancaster">Avro Lancaster</a> and <a href="/wiki/Handley_Page_Halifax" title="Handley Page Halifax">Handley Page Halifax</a> heavy bombers, but not the earlier light single-engined <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Tiger_Moth" title="De Havilland Tiger Moth">Tiger Moth</a> trainer, and minimized the type-conversion difficulties associated with blind flying, since a pilot trained on one aircraft could quickly become accustomed to any other if the instruments were identical.</p>
<p>This basic six set, also known as a "six pack",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6pack_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-6pack-4">[4]</a></sup> was also adopted by commercial aviation. After the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a> the arrangement was changed to: (top row) airspeed, artificial horizon, altimeter, (bottom row) turn and bank indicator, heading indicator, vertical speed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_development">Further development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Further development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Of the old basic six instruments, the turn and bank indicator is now obsolete. The instrument was included, but it was of little use in the first generation of jet airliners. It was removed from many aircraft prior to glass cockpits becoming available. With an improved artificial horizon, including gyros and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flight_director_(aviation)" title="Flight director (aviation)">flight directors</a>, the turn and bank indicator became needless except when performing certain types of aerobatics (which would not be intentionally performed in <a href="/wiki/Instrument_meteorological_conditions" title="Instrument meteorological conditions">IMC</a> to begin with). But the other five flight instruments, sometimes known as "the big five", are still included in all cockpits. The way of displaying them has changed over time, though. In glass cockpits the flight instruments are shown on monitors. But the display is not shown by numbers, but as images of analog instruments. The artificial horizon is given a central place in the monitor, with a heading indicator just below (usually this is displayed only as a part of the compass). The indicated airspeed, altimeter, and vertical speed indicator are displayed as columns with the indicated airspeed and altitude to the right of the horizon and the vertical speed to the left in the same pattern as in most older style "clock cockpits".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Different_significance_and_some_other_instrumentation">Different significance and some other instrumentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Different significance and some other instrumentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In good weather a pilot can fly by looking out the window. However, when flying in cloud or at night at least one gyroscopic instrument is necessary to orientate the aircraft, being either an artificial horizon, turn and slip, or a gyro compass.</p>
<p>The vertical speed indicator, or VSI, is more of "a good help" than absolutely essential. On jet aircraft it displays the vertical speed in thousands of feet per minute, usually in the range -6 to +6. The gyrocompass can be used for navigation, but it is indeed a flight instrument as well. It is needed to control the adjustment of the heading, to be the same as the heading of the landing runway. Indicated airspeed, or IAS, is the second most important instrument and indicates the airspeed very accurately in the range of 45 to 250 knots. At higher altitude a MACH-meter is used instead, to prevent the aircraft from overspeed. An instrument called <a href="/wiki/True_airspeed" title="True airspeed">true airspeed</a>, or TAS, exists on some aircraft. TAS shows airspeed in knots in the range from 200 knots and higher (It is like the Mach-meter: not really a flight instrument). The altimeter displays the altitude in feet, but must be corrected to local air pressure at the landing airport. The altimeter may be adjusted to show an altitude of zero feet on the runway, but far more common is to adjust the altimeter to show the actual altitude when the aircraft has landed. In the latter case pilots must keep the runway elevation in mind. However a radio altimeter (displaying the height above the ground if lower than around 2000–2500 feet) has been standard for decades. This instrument is however not among the "big five", but must still be considered as a flight instrument.</p>
