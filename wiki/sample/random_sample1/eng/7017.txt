<p><b>Big in Japan</b> is an expression historically used to describe western based musical groups who achieve success in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> but not necessarily in other parts of the world.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Original_usage">Original usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_in_Japan_(phrase)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Original usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The phrase began to appear on several major Japanese foreign-rock magazines, especially the <i>Music Life</i> magazine, in the late 1970s, and in most cases, the "big in Japan" artists became popular in Japan due to being featured by <i>Music Life</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The concept predated the phrase; <a href="/wiki/Neil_Sedaka" title="Neil Sedaka">Neil Sedaka</a> made it big in Japan with "<a href="/wiki/One_Way_Ticket_(Neil_Sedaka_song)" title="One Way Ticket (Neil Sedaka song)">One Way Ticket</a>" before breaking through in his native United States, while <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Osmond" title="Jimmy Osmond">Jimmy Osmond</a>, typically a side show to his older brothers <a href="/wiki/The_Osmonds" title="The Osmonds">The Osmonds</a> in North America and Europe, cut several tracks in Japanese and received several gold records for his recordings.</p>
<p>In the late 20th century, notable "big in Japan" artists included several <a href="/wiki/Arena_rock" title="Arena rock">stadium rock</a> bands from the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">metal</a> artists from Northern European countries such as <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, and especially <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> (e.g. the rock band <a href="/wiki/Hanoi_Rocks" title="Hanoi Rocks">Hanoi Rocks</a>), <a href="/wiki/Eurobeat" title="Eurobeat">eurobeat</a> artists from <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and especially <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, and UK rock<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> artists.</p>
<p>Some bands have used their popularity in Japan as a springboard to break into other audiences. Notably, the <a href="/wiki/Power_pop" title="Power pop">power pop</a> group <a href="/wiki/Cheap_Trick" title="Cheap Trick">Cheap Trick</a>, which had been known as the "American Beatles" in Japan for their appeal, achieved widespread success with their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multi-platinum" title="Multi-platinum">multi-platinum</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live_album" title="Live album">live album</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Cheap_Trick_at_Budokan" title="Cheap Trick at Budokan">Cheap Trick at Budokan</a></i>. The band had previously struggled to break into the mainstream American market with their earlier albums. Furthermore, like Cheap Trick, some bands have lost their "big in Japan" titles after gaining popularity in their respective homelands. The most notable example is <a href="/wiki/Queen_(band)" title="Queen (band)">Queen</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Bon_Jovi" title="Bon Jovi">Bon Jovi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ongen_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ongen-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>For example, <a href="/wiki/Scorpions_(band)" title="Scorpions (band)">Scorpions</a> initially had only mediocre success in Europe and the United States,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> yet were "Big in Japan", as evidenced by their 1978 tour of the country and the double live album <i><a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Tapes_(album)" title="Tokyo Tapes (album)">Tokyo Tapes</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> Another example is <a href="/wiki/The_Ventures" title="The Ventures">The Ventures</a>, a band formed in 1959 and touring Japan each year since 1965, having logged over 2,000 concerts there by 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Homan_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Homan-6">[5]</a></sup> "Being 'Big in Japan' turned into a positive sign of their closeness to the hearts of Japanese people, with the band embedded in national and local rock cultures."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Homan_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Homan-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The phrase was used as the name of <a href="/wiki/Big_in_Japan_(band)" title="Big in Japan (band)">a UK punk band</a> active from 1977 to 1982 (whose name inspired the title of <a href="/wiki/Big_in_Japan_(Alphaville_song)" title="Big in Japan (Alphaville song)">a 1984 hit single</a> by pop band <a href="/wiki/Alphaville_(band)" title="Alphaville (band)">Alphaville</a>) and was the name of the lead track on the Grammy-winning 1999 album <i><a href="/wiki/Mule_Variations" title="Mule Variations">Mule Variations</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Tom_Waits" title="Tom Waits">Tom Waits</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Mockumentary" title="Mockumentary">mockumentary</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/This_is_Spinal_Tap" title="This is Spinal Tap">This is Spinal Tap</a></i> parodies this phenomenon when the band schedules a Japanese tour after discovering that their single "Sex Farm" is inexplicably selling very well there.</p>
<p>American band Mountain reformed for a successful tour of Japan in 1973. A live album titled <i>Twin Peaks</i> was released in 1974. Mountain bass player and vocalist Felix Pappalardi then worked with the Japanese band Creation in 1976.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_in_Japan_(phrase)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Avril_Lavigne" title="Avril Lavigne">Avril Lavigne</a> has remained immensely popular in Japan, years after her popularity waned in North America, and in April 2014 released the single and music video "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hello_Kitty_(Avril_Lavigne_song)" title="Hello Kitty (Avril Lavigne song)">Hello Kitty</a>" as a thank you to her Japanese fans.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alexandra_Stan" title="Alexandra Stan">Alexandra Stan</a>, whose song "<a href="/wiki/Mr._Saxobeat" title="Mr. Saxobeat">Mr. Saxobeat</a>" sold over 10,000,000 copies worldwide, is still popular in Japan, charting her three albums within top 40.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_usage">Other usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_in_Japan_(phrase)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It has also been used in sports, for instance, to describe <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> players who join Japanese clubs at the end of their careers, such as <a href="/wiki/Daryl_Spencer" title="Daryl Spencer">Daryl Spencer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Small_in_Japan.22">"Small in Japan"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_in_Japan_(phrase)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title='Edit section: "Small in Japan"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The derivative phrase "small in Japan", originally used for <a href="/wiki/AC/DC" title="AC/DC">AC/DC</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> has been used since the early 1980s. In general, a small-in-Japan artist holds significant popularity in the Western world (in most cases the United States), and visits Japan many times to promote himself/herself, yet is almost unknown and unsuccessful in Japan despite being heavily featured by Japanese music media.</p>
<p>In Japanese culture, the phrase "small in Japan" is also used to describe Japanese celebrities who are unknown, unsuccessful or "forgotten" in Japan but making their ways outside Japan. The phrase has been used to refer to certain musicians such as <a href="/wiki/Dir_En_Grey" title="Dir En Grey">Dir En Grey</a>, certain <a href="/wiki/Professional_wrestling" title="Professional wrestling">professional wrestlers</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Yoshihiro_Tajiri" title="Yoshihiro Tajiri">Yoshihiro Tajiri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yoshi_Tatsu" title="Yoshi Tatsu">Yoshitatsu</a>, certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fashion_model" title="Fashion model">fashion models</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Ai_Tominaga" title="Ai Tominaga">Ai Tominaga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tao_Okamoto" title="Tao Okamoto">Tao Okamoto</a>, and all the <a href="/wiki/Miss_Universe" title="Miss Universe">Miss Universe</a> contestants from Japan, most of whom are former unsuccessful fashion models, including <a href="/wiki/Kurara_Chibana" title="Kurara Chibana">Kurara Chibana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Riyo_Mori" title="Riyo Mori">Riyo Mori</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aj_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-aj-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In one exceptional case, <i>Digital Arts</i> magazine has used the phrase to describe the <a href="/wiki/Xbox_(console)" title="Xbox (console)">Xbox</a>, a videogame console that was a success all over the world except in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
