<p><b>Viktor Ahn</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Виктор Ан</span>; born November 23, 1985), also known as <b>Ahn Hyun-soo</b> (<a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>: <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">안현수</span>) and <b>Victor An</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">Korean</a>-born <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> <a href="/wiki/Short_track_speed_skating" title="Short track speed skating">short track speed skating</a> athlete. After competing for <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> since childhood, in 2011 he became a <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> citizen and now races for the Russian team. One of the most accomplished short track speed skaters of all time, Ahn won three gold medals and a bronze medal in <a href="/wiki/2006_Winter_Olympics" title="2006 Winter Olympics">2006 Winter Olympics</a> held in <a href="/wiki/Turin" title="Turin">Turin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, becoming the most successful athlete there. He has also won three gold medals and one bronze medal in the <a href="/wiki/2014_Winter_Olympics" title="2014 Winter Olympics">2014 Winter Olympics</a> held in <a href="/wiki/Sochi" title="Sochi">Sochi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. He is also a six-time <a href="/wiki/World_Short_Track_Speed_Skating_Championships" title="World Short Track Speed Skating Championships">Overall World Champion</a> for 2003–2007 and 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-worlds_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-worlds-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After winning gold in Sochi, Ahn explained his reasons for joining <a href="/wiki/Russia_at_the_2014_Winter_Olympics" title="Russia at the 2014 Winter Olympics">the Russian team</a> saying, "I wanted to train in the best possible environment and I proved my decision was not wrong." As expected, a gold-winning athlete leaving the national team caused public uproar in South Korea. However, it was aimed not at Ahn, but at the country's skating union. Most South Korean fans in a poll said they understood Ahn's decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Ahn received the <a href="/wiki/Order_%22For_Merit_to_the_Fatherland%22" title='Order "For Merit to the Fatherland"'>The Order For Merit to the Fatherland Award</a> 4th class with Russian President <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Putin" title="Vladimir Putin">Vladimir Putin</a> handing the state awards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OMF2014_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-OMF2014-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>Ahn first began skating in elementary school. The first time he watched the sport on television was during the <a href="/wiki/1994_Winter_Olympics" title="1994 Winter Olympics">1994 Winter Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lillehammer" title="Lillehammer">Lillehammer</a> where one of his heroes, <a href="/wiki/Chae_Ji-hoon" title="Chae Ji-hoon">Chae Ji-hoon</a>, took gold in the 500 m and silver in the 1000 m for South Korea. Incidentally, these were the Games where Russia achieved a national record of 11 Olympic golds, a feat that he himself would help to repeat twenty years later. His coach, <a href="/wiki/Kim_Ki-hoon" title="Kim Ki-hoon">Kim Ki-hoon</a>, was a three-time Olympic gold medalist who scouted Ahn and continues to train him. He trains ten hours every day from techniques, speed, and endurance to video analysis.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viktor_Ahn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2002_Olympics">2002 Olympics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viktor_Ahn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2002 Olympics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He participated in the <a href="/wiki/2002_Winter_Olympics" title="2002 Winter Olympics">2002 Winter Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">Salt Lake City</a>, making the finals of the 1000 meter short course event but returned home without a medal after a controversial fall involving <a href="/wiki/Apolo_Ohno" title="Apolo Ohno">Apolo Ohno</a>, <a href="/wiki/Li_Jiajun" title="Li Jiajun">Li Jiajun</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mathieu_Turcotte" title="Mathieu Turcotte">Mathieu Turcotte</a> allowed Australian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steven_Bradbury_(speed_skater)" title="Steven Bradbury (speed skater)">Steve Bradbury</a> to sweep the gold medal.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_Olympics">After the Olympics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viktor_Ahn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: After the Olympics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ahn won the world junior short track championship in 2002, and finished second to <a href="/wiki/Kim_Dong-sung" title="Kim Dong-sung">Kim Dong-sung</a> at his first senior-level <a href="/wiki/2002_World_Short_Track_Speed_Skating_Championships" title="2002 World Short Track Speed Skating Championships">world championship</a> competition the same year, almost duplicating Kim's feat of winning both the Junior and Senior World Championship titles in 1997. Ahn Hyun-soo finished first in the overall World Cup rankings during 2003–2004 and 2005–2006 seasons.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2006_Olympics">2006 Olympics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viktor_Ahn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2006 Olympics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/South_Korea_at_the_2006_Winter_Olympics" title="South Korea at the 2006 Winter Olympics">2006 Winter Olympics</a>, Ahn won gold medals in the 1500 m and 1000 m events. He set a new <a href="/wiki/Olympic_record" title="Olympic record">Olympic record</a> time of 1:26.739 in the 1000 m, finishing ahead of teammate <a href="/wiki/Lee_Ho-suk" title="Lee Ho-suk">Lee Ho-suk</a> and rival Ohno. Ahn also won gold in Men's 5000 m relay along with teammates <a href="/wiki/Lee_Ho-suk" title="Lee Ho-suk">Lee Ho-suk</a> (이호석), <a href="/wiki/Seo_Ho-jin" title="Seo Ho-jin">Seo Ho-jin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Song_Suk-woo" title="Song Suk-woo">Song Suk-woo</a>. He became the second Korean athlete ever to win three gold medals in one Olympics. (<a href="/wiki/Jin_Sun-yu" title="Jin Sun-yu">Jin Sun-yu</a> (진선유) accomplished this earlier on the same day.) He also won a bronze medal in the 500 m event. Rarely taking the lead position throughout the entire race, Ahn's strategy is to follow the leaders, then sprint using an outside passing lane (or occasionally an inside passing lane if the opportunity arises) with only two or three laps to go. In the 5000 m relay at the 2006 Winter Olympics, Ahn made a brilliant pass around defending Olympic champion Canada on the final lap, helping Korea earn the victory. Sports commentators often refer to his "patented outside pass" due to its consistency and tremendous effectiveness.</p>
<p>Ahn Hyun-soo won a total of 4 medals in the 2006 Games, an unprecedented result by any athlete in his sport. He is the first Korean man to win at least 3 medals in a single Winter Olympics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_Olympics_2">After the Olympics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viktor_Ahn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: After the Olympics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite being disqualified in the finals of the 500 m and 3000 m events at the <a href="/wiki/2006_World_Short_Track_Speed_Skating_Championships" title="2006 World Short Track Speed Skating Championships">2006 World Championships</a> in <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a>, Ahn was able to claim the championship with victories in both the 1000 m and 1500 m events and became the overall world champion with 68 points followed by countryman <a href="/wiki/Lee_Ho-suk" title="Lee Ho-suk">Lee Ho-suk</a> with 60 points.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conflict">Conflict</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viktor_Ahn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conflict">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/2006_World_Short_Track_Speed_Skating_Championships" title="2006 World Short Track Speed Skating Championships">2006 World Championships</a>, Ahn flew back to South Korea. At <a href="/wiki/Incheon_International_Airport" title="Incheon International Airport">Incheon International Airport</a>, Ahn's father, Ahn Ki-Won, had a loud quarrel with the vice president of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Korean_Skating_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Korean Skating Union (page does not exist)">Korean Skating Union</a> (KSU), claiming that the coach did not associate with Ahn and conspired with other skaters to prevent Ahn from winning the title of overall champion.</p>
<p>The South Korean short track team was split into two groups, in one of which Ahn was being coached by the women's coach due to conflicts with the men's coach. The tensions had risen so high that the skaters refused to dine in the same room, sit next to each other on the plane, or even share the same floor with each other. Ahn and <a href="/wiki/Lee_Ho-suk" title="Lee Ho-suk">Lee Ho-suk</a> used to attend the same high school together, and even shared a room last year in skating camps, but since then have rarely spoken to each other.</p>
<p>Ahn mentioned on his personal website that the pressure was too much for him and he contemplated quitting the sport. Due to the issue, KSU stated that starting next season, the team would be united under one head coach to prevent deleterious rivalries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_conflict">After the conflict</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viktor_Ahn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: After the conflict">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/2007_World_Short_Track_Speed_Skating_Championships" title="2007 World Short Track Speed Skating Championships">2007 World Championships</a> held in <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> from March 9 to March 11, 2007, Ahn won his fifth world championship, finishing first in the 1000 m and in the 5000 m relay with teammates, <a href="/wiki/Sung_Si-bak" title="Sung Si-bak">Sung Si-bak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Song_Kyung-taek" title="Song Kyung-taek">Song Kyung-taek</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kim_Hyun-kon" title="Kim Hyun-kon">Kim Hyun-kon</a>. He also won silver in the 3000 m behind countryman, Song Kyung-taek, and won two bronze medals in the 500 m and the 1500 m. He is the first man to win five world championships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-worlds_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-worlds-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ahn is the only male short track skater to have won at least three consecutive world championships (having won five in a row); Canadian short track legend <a href="/wiki/Marc_Gagnon" title="Marc Gagnon">Marc Gagnon</a> has won four times, but his titles did not come back to back.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_2008_season_injury">Early 2008 season injury</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viktor_Ahn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Early 2008 season injury">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On January 16, 2008, the Korean Skating Union (KSU) announced that Ahn had injured his knee after colliding with a fence during national team training at the Korea Training Center in Taeneung. After being sent to the hospital, the injury was diagnosed as a fractured knee.</p>
<p>Due to the injury, KSU announced that Ahn would not be competing in the <a href="/wiki/International_Skating_Union" title="International Skating Union">ISU</a> Samsung World Cup Series 5 and 6 in Quebec City and Salt Lake City, respectively. It was also reported that he would not be competing in the <a href="/wiki/2008_World_Short_Track_Speed_Skating_Championships" title="2008 World Short Track Speed Skating Championships">2008 World Championships</a> in Gangeung or the 2008 World Team Championships in <a href="/wiki/Harbin" title="Harbin">Harbin</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. As a result of the unexpected injury, it was clear that Ahn would be unable to defend his sixth World title, leaving his countrymen <a href="/wiki/Lee_Ho-suk" title="Lee Ho-suk">Lee Ho-suk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Song_Kyung-taek" title="Song Kyung-taek">Song Kyung-taek</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lee_Seung-hoon" title="Lee Seung-hoon">Lee Seung-hoon</a> to make up the ground. After undergoing three surgeries, his rehabilitation period was predicted to be around 2–3 months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>After eight months off the ice, a Korean news article reported on September 5, 2008 that Ahn was back training, undergoing approximately two hours of physical reinforcement and skating along with around five hours of rehabilitation accompanied by muscular power training. The article also reported that Ahn was eyeing the <a href="/wiki/2010_Winter_Olympics" title="2010 Winter Olympics">2010 Winter Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because of his injury, Ahn did not compete in the <a href="/wiki/2009_World_Short_Track_Speed_Skating_Championships" title="2009 World Short Track Speed Skating Championships">2009 World Championships</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> which took place March 6–8.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010_Winter_Olympics">2010 Winter Olympics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viktor_Ahn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2010 Winter Olympics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the South Korean National Team Trials, which ultimately determine the Fall World Cup and Olympic Teams, Ahn was unable to qualify, finishing 7th in overall points (because Ahn didn't compete in the last two World Cup seasons, he needed to finish in the top three in overall points to qualify). Due to his inability to qualify, he did not compete in the <a href="/wiki/2010_Winter_Olympics" title="2010 Winter Olympics">2010 Winter Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Despite this, Ahn competed in the <a href="/wiki/2014_Winter_Olympics" title="2014 Winter Olympics">2014 Winter Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sochi" title="Sochi">Sochi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> as a Russian citizen.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Moving_to_Russia">Moving to Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viktor_Ahn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Moving to Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ahn was the leader in the 3000 meters for the 2011–2012 season. He was also in the first place 5000 meter relay team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2014_Winter_Olympics">2014 Winter Olympics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viktor_Ahn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 2014 Winter Olympics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>At the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2014_Sochi_winter_Olympics" title="2014 Sochi winter Olympics">2014 Sochi winter Olympics</a>, Ahn won the bronze medal in the <a href="/wiki/Short_track_speed_skating_at_the_2014_Winter_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_1500_metres" title="Short track speed skating at the 2014 Winter Olympics – Men's 1500 metres">1500 m event</a>. He won the first short track speed skating medal that Russia has earned while competing as Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UTSD-2014-02-10_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-UTSD-2014-02-10-10">[10]</a></sup> Ahn then won the first Russian gold medal in short track, winning the <a href="/wiki/Short_track_speed_skating_at_the_2014_Winter_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_1000_metres" title="Short track speed skating at the 2014 Winter Olympics – Men's 1000 metres">1000 m event</a>, leading the first Russian 1-2 finish in short track, with <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Grigorev" title="Vladimir Grigorev">Vladimir Grigorev</a> winning silver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> On the 21st of February Ahn won his 7th overall and 5th Winter Olympic gold medal when he finished first in the 500 m men's final.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> With that gold medal, he became the first short track skater to win all four Olympic golds, the 500 m, 1000 m, 1500 m, 5000 m relay. He also became the short track speed skater with the most Olympic gold medals, with 5,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> which increased to 6 with a 5000 m relay win later the same day. With that gold, he became the short tracker with the most Olympic medals, at 8, tied with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apolo_Anton_Ohno" title="Apolo Anton Ohno">Apolo Anton Ohno</a>; he also became the short tracker with the most Olympic Gold medals, at 6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_Olympics_3">After the Olympics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viktor_Ahn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: After the Olympics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After finishing his career as a skater, Ahn will coach short track for Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UTSD-2014-02-10_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-UTSD-2014-02-10-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Russian_citizenship">Russian citizenship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viktor_Ahn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Russian citizenship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ahn trained in Russia and has received Russian citizenship to compete for Russia in the <a href="/wiki/Short_track_speed_skating_at_the_2014_Winter_Olympics" title="Short track speed skating at the 2014 Winter Olympics">2014 Sochi Olympics</a>. He won a bronze medal in the 1500 meters and a gold medal in the 1000 meters and the 500 meters. Ahn's father stated that the decision was due to lack of support from the South Korean skating association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Prior to moving to Russia, Ahn did not know Russian, and Ahn had no familial ties to Russia. He had considered competing for the United States, but found that the process for gaining Russian citizenship was much easier. He chose "Viktor" as his Russian name as it derived from <a href="/wiki/Victory" title="Victory">Victory</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and to pay tribute to <a href="/wiki/Viktor_Tsoi" title="Viktor Tsoi">Viktor Tsoi</a>, a Soviet rock star of Korean descent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> His new Russian family name is Antonov but it is not often used in international media.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In South Korea, a furor erupted over the loss of Ahn to Team Russia, after his participation in the <a href="/wiki/2014_Winter_Olympics" title="2014 Winter Olympics">2014 Winter Olympics</a>. Several newspapers reported the scorn of the Korean public and newspaper editors on the actions of the skating federation. The minister of sport and president of South Korea have both promised action in rooting out corruption and feuding at the organization that may have led to his "defection," in a bid to clean it up in preparation for the <a href="/wiki/2018_Winter_Olympics" title="2018 Winter Olympics">2018 Winter Olympics</a> in South Korea. The Korean public is generally supportive of Ahn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viktor_Ahn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ahn is married to Korean Woo Na-ri (<a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>: <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">우나리</span>, born 1984), who moved to Russia in 2013 and received the Russian citizenship. Na-ri was a former fan of Ahn and was even member of a fan club. Ahn said, that her presence and care helped him adapt to Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> They have a daughter, Jane Ahn (born 29 December 2015).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
