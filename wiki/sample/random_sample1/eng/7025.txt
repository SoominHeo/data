<div class="hatnote" role="note">This name uses <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Slavic_naming_customs" title="Eastern Slavic naming customs">Eastern Slavic naming customs</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> is <i>Stepanovich</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">family name</a> is <i>Chernomyrdin</i>.</div>
<p><b>Viktor Stepanovich Chernomyrdin</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Ви́ктор Степа́нович Черномы́рдин</span>, <small>IPA: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Russian" title="Help:IPA for Russian">[ˈvʲiktər sʲtʲɪˈpanəvʲɪtɕ tɕɪrnɐˈmɨrdʲɪn]</a></span>; 9 April 1938 – 3 November 2010) was a Russian politician. He was the first chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Gazprom" title="Gazprom">Gazprom</a> energy company and the longest-serving <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Russia" title="Prime Minister of Russia">Prime Minister of Russia</a> (1992–1998) based on consecutive years. He was a key figure in Russian politics in the 1990s, and a participant in the Russian transition from a <a href="/wiki/Planned_economy" title="Planned economy">planned</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Market_economy" title="Market economy">market</a> economy. From 2001 to 2009, he was Russia's ambassador to <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>. After that he was designated as a presidential adviser.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kremlin110609_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-kremlin110609-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chernomyrdin is known in Russia and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_countries_where_Russian_is_an_official_language" title="List of countries where Russian is an official language">Russian-speaking countries</a> for his unique language style, containing numerous <a href="/wiki/Malapropism" title="Malapropism">malapropisms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntactic</a> errors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dosuga.net_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-dosuga.net-2">[2]</a></sup> Many of his sayings became <a href="/wiki/Aphorism" title="Aphorism">aphorisms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Idiom" title="Idiom">idioms</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian language</a>, the most famous being his expression <i>"We wanted the best, but it turned out like always."</i> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Хотели как лучше, а получилось как всегда</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dushenko.ru_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-dushenko.ru-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chernomyrdin died on 3 November 2010 after a long illness. He was buried beside his wife in <a href="/wiki/Novodevichy_Cemetery" title="Novodevichy Cemetery">Novodevichy Cemetery</a> on 5 November, and his funeral was broadcast live on Russian <a href="/wiki/Television_in_Russia" title="Television in Russia">federal TV channels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rbc_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-rbc-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viktor_Chernomyrdin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chernomyrdin was born in Chernyi Otrog, Orenburg Oblast, Russian SFSR. His father was a labourer and Viktor was one of five children. Chernomyrdin completed school education in 1957 and found employment as a mechanic in an <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">oil refinery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Orsk" title="Orsk">Orsk</a>. He worked there until 1962, except for two years of <a href="/wiki/Conscription#Russia" title="Conscription">compulsory military service</a> from 1957 to 1960. His other occupations on the plant during this period included machinist, operator and chief of technical installations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>He became a member of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Communist Party of the Soviet Union">CPSU</a> in 1961. In 1962, he was admitted to Kuybyshev Industrial Institute (which was later renamed <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Samara_Polytechnical_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Samara Polytechnical Institute (page does not exist)">Samara Polytechnical Institute</a>). In his entrance exams he performed very poorly. He failed the math sections of the test and had to take the exam again, getting a C. He got only one B in Russian language, and Cs in the other tests. He was admitted only because of very low competition. In 1966, he graduated from the institute. In 1972, he completed further studies at the Department of Economics of the Union-wide Polytechnic Institute by correspondence.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>Chernomyrdin began developing his career as a politician when he worked for the CPSU in <a href="/wiki/Orsk" title="Orsk">Orsk</a> between 1967 and 1973. In 1973, he was appointed the director of the natural gas refining plant in <a href="/wiki/Orenburg" title="Orenburg">Orenburg</a>, a position which he held until 1978. Between 1978 and 1982, Chernomyrdin worked in the heavy industry arm of the CPSU <a href="/wiki/Central_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Central Committee of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Central Committee</a>.</p>
<p>In 1982, he was appointed deputy Minister of the natural gas industries of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. Concurrently, beginning from 1983, he directed Glavtyumengazprom, an industry association for natural gas resource development in <a href="/wiki/Tyumen_Oblast" title="Tyumen Oblast">Tyumen Oblast</a>. During 1985–1989 he was the minister of gas industries.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Founder_of_Gazprom">Founder of Gazprom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viktor_Chernomyrdin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Founder of Gazprom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In August 1989, under the leadership of Chernomyrdin, the Ministry of Gas Industry was transformed into the <i>State Gas Concern</i>, <a href="/wiki/Gazprom" title="Gazprom">Gazprom</a>, which became the country's first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_corporation" title="State corporation">state-corporate enterprise</a>. Chernomyrdin was elected its first chairman. The company was still controlled by the state, but now the control was exercised through shares of stock, 100% of which were owned by the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faq_jsc_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-faq_jsc-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marshall_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-marshall-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>When the Soviet Union dissolved in late 1991, assets of the former Soviet state in the gas sector were transferred to newly created national companies such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ukrgazprom" title="Ukrgazprom">Ukrgazprom</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turkmengazprom" title="Turkmengazprom">Turkmengazprom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Gazprom kept assets located in the territory of Russia, and was able to secure a monopoly in the gas sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marshall_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-marshall-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gazprom's political influence increased markedly after Russian President <a href="/wiki/Boris_Yeltsin" title="Boris Yeltsin">Boris Yeltsin</a> appointed the company's chairman Chernomyrdin as his <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Russia" title="Prime Minister of Russia">Prime Minister</a> in 1992. <a href="/wiki/Rem_Viakhirev" title="Rem Viakhirev">Rem Viakhirev</a> took Chernomyrdin's place as Chairman both of the Board of Directors and of the Managing Committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marshall_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-marshall-6">[6]</a></sup> Gazprom was one of the backbones of the country's economy in 1990s, though the company underperformed during that decade. In the 2000s, however, Gazprom became the largest extractor of natural gas in the world and the largest <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Russia" title="Economy of Russia">Russian company</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prime_Minister_of_Russia">Prime Minister of Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viktor_Chernomyrdin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Prime Minister of Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In May 1992, <a href="/wiki/Boris_Yeltsin" title="Boris Yeltsin">Boris Yeltsin</a> appointed Chernomyrdin as Deputy Prime Minister in charge of fuel and energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-8">[8]</a></sup> On 14 December 1992, Chernomyrdin was confirmed by the VII Congress of People's Deputies of Russia as Prime Minister. In April 1995, he formed a political bloc called <a href="/wiki/Our_Home_%E2%80%93_Russia" title="Our Home – Russia">Our Home – Russia</a>, which was aimed at becoming the central force in the parliament, but failed in this, gaining only 10% of votes.</p>
<p>In 1995, Chernomyrdin signed a decree calling for the development of a national strategy for tiger conservation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 18 June 1995, as a result of <a href="/wiki/Shamil_Basayev" title="Shamil Basayev">Shamil Basayev</a>-led terrorists <a href="/wiki/Budyonnovsk_hospital_hostage_crisis" title="Budyonnovsk hospital hostage crisis">taking over 1500 people hostage</a> in <a href="/wiki/Budyonnovsk" title="Budyonnovsk">Budyonnovsk</a>, negotiations between Chernomyrdin and Basayev led to a compromise which became a turning point for the <a href="/wiki/First_Chechen_War" title="First Chechen War">First Chechen War</a>. In exchange for the hostages, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_government" title="Russian government">Russian government</a> agreed to halt military actions in Chechnya and begin a series of negotiations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>When Boris Yeltsin was undergoing a heart operation on 6 November 1996, Chernomyrdin served as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acting_President_of_the_Russian_Federation" title="Acting President of the Russian Federation">Acting President</a> for 23 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HoffmanWashingtonPost_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-HoffmanWashingtonPost-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chernomyrdin remained Prime Minister until his sudden dismissal on 23 March 1998. Following the <a href="/wiki/1998_Russian_financial_crisis" title="1998 Russian financial crisis">1998 Russian financial crisis</a> in August, Yeltsin re-appointed Chernomyrdin as Prime Minister, and attempted to groom him as his successor. However, the <a href="/wiki/State_Duma" title="State Duma">Duma</a> twice refused to confirm Chernomyrdin as the head of the government. Rather than to risk the third rejection and thus to force the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_parliament#Russia" title="Dissolution of parliament">dissolution of the State Duma</a> and political crisis, Chernomyrdin withdrew his nomination and the President propose a more popular <a href="/wiki/Yevgeny_Primakov" title="Yevgeny Primakov">Yevgeny Primakov</a> to <a href="/wiki/Yevgeny_Primakov%27s_Cabinet" title="Yevgeny Primakov's Cabinet">form new cabinet</a>.</p>

<p>During the <a href="/wiki/NATO_bombing_of_Yugoslavia" title="NATO bombing of Yugoslavia">NATO bombing of Yugoslavia</a> in 1999 Chernomyrdin was a special representative of Russia in Yugoslavia. It was he who persuaded <a href="/wiki/Slobodan_Milo%C5%A1evi%C4%87" title="Slobodan Milošević">Slobodan Milošević</a> to agree to an armistice and to place <a href="/wiki/Kosovo" title="Kosovo">Kosovo</a> under UN control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lenta_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-lenta-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 1999 Chernomyrdin was <a href="/wiki/Russian_legislative_election,_1999" title="Russian legislative election, 1999">elected</a> a member of the <a href="/wiki/State_Duma" title="State Duma">State Duma</a>. In May 2001, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Putin" title="Vladimir Putin">Vladimir Putin</a> appointed Chernomyrdin <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ambassador_of_Russia_to_Ukraine" title="Ambassador of Russia to Ukraine">Ambassador of Russia to Ukraine</a>. This action was interpreted by some Russian media agencies as a move to distance Chernomyrdin from the centre of <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Russia" title="Politics of Russia">Russian politics</a>. In 2003, he dismissed talk of an apology for the <a href="/wiki/Holodomor" title="Holodomor">Holodomor Famine</a> made by the Soviet Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2009 Chernomyrdin again strained the <a href="/wiki/Russia%E2%80%93Ukraine_relations" title="Russia–Ukraine relations">relations between Ukraine and Russia</a> when he in an interview said "It is impossible to come to an agreement on anything with the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ukraine" title="Government of Ukraine">Ukrainian leadership</a>. If different people come in, we'll see". The <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_(Ukraine)" title="Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Ukraine)">Ukrainian foreign ministry</a> said in a response it could declare Chernomyrdin "<a href="/wiki/Persona_non_grata" title="Persona non grata">persona non grata</a>" over the row.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 11 June 2009, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_President" title="Russian President">Russian President</a> <a href="/wiki/Dmitry_Medvedev" title="Dmitry Medvedev">Dmitry Medvedev</a> relieved Chernomyrdin as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ambassadors_of_Russia" title="Ambassadors of Russia">Russian Ambassador</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kiev" title="Kiev">Kiev</a>, and appointed him as "presidential adviser and special presidential representative on economic cooperation with CIS member countries".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kremlin110609_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-kremlin110609-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In a parting shot at the Ukrainian government, Chernomyrdin stated that Russia should not apologise to <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> over voicing its suspicions about Ukraine being unable to pay for its natural gas, and further stated that Russia wants Ukraine to pay for the gas it consumes, and hence Russia is right to be concerned about the solvency of the Ukrainian state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viktor_Chernomyrdin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Chernomyrdin died on the morning of 3 November 2010<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-8">[8]</a></sup> after a long illness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rbc_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-rbc-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lenta_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-lenta-13">[13]</a></sup> The exact cause and the place of death were not announced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lenta_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-lenta-13">[13]</a></sup> According to people close to Chernomyrdin, such as popular singer <a href="/wiki/Lev_Leshchenko" title="Lev Leshchenko">Lev Leshchenko</a>, the former Prime Minister was deeply affected by the death of his wife Valentina, seven and a half months earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chernomyrdin was buried beside his wife in <a href="/wiki/Novodevichy_Cemetery" title="Novodevichy Cemetery">Novodevichy Cemetery</a> on 5 November 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> On 3 November <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_President" title="Russian President">Russian President</a> <a href="/wiki/Dmitry_Medvedev" title="Dmitry Medvedev">Dmitry Medvedev</a> signed an <a href="/wiki/Decree_of_the_President_of_Russia" title="Decree of the President of Russia">order</a> to show Chernomyrdin's <a href="/wiki/Funeral" title="Funeral">funeral</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Live_television" title="Live television">live broadcast</a> on Russian <a href="/wiki/Television_in_Russia" title="Television in Russia">federal TV</a> channels<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rbc_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-rbc-4">[4]</a></sup> (only the funerals of the former President <a href="/wiki/Boris_Yeltsin" title="Boris Yeltsin">Boris Yeltsin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_Alexy_II" title="Patriarch Alexy II">Patriarch Alexy II</a> were granted the same right in recent years). The head of the <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Administration_of_Russia" title="Presidential Administration of Russia">Presidential Administration of Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sergey_Naryshkin" title="Sergey Naryshkin">Sergey Naryshkin</a>, supervised the funeral ceremony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rbc_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-rbc-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Condolences on the death of Chernomyrdin were voiced on 3 November 2010 by Russian President <a href="/wiki/Dmitry_Medvedev" title="Dmitry Medvedev">Dmitry Medvedev</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Prime_Minister" title="Russian Prime Minister">Russian Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Putin" title="Vladimir Putin">Vladimir Putin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ukrainian_President" title="Ukrainian President">Ukrainian President</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viktor_Yanukovich" title="Viktor Yanukovich">Viktor Yanukovich</a> and other state figures in Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lenta2_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-lenta2-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sayings">Sayings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Viktor_Chernomyrdin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sayings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In Russian-speaking countries, Chernomyrdin is famous for his numerous <a href="/wiki/Malapropism" title="Malapropism">malapropisms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntactically</a> incorrect speech.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dosuga.net_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-dosuga.net-2">[2]</a></sup> His idioms received the name <i>Chernomyrdinki</i>, and are somewhat comparable to <a href="/wiki/Bushism" title="Bushism">Bushisms</a> in style and effect.</p>
<p>One of his expressions <i>"We wanted to do it better, but it turned out as always"</i> (<i>Хотели как лучше, а получилось как всегда</i> in Russian) about the economic reforms in Russia became a very popular proverb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dushenko.ru_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-dushenko.ru-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The phrase was uttered after a <a href="/wiki/Monetary_reform_in_Russia,_1993" title="Monetary reform in Russia, 1993">highly unsuccessful monetary exchange</a> performed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Bank_of_the_Russian_Federation" title="Central Bank of the Russian Federation">Russian Central Bank</a> in July 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among his other sayings were:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dosuga.net_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-dosuga.net-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>We have completed all the items: from A to B.</li>
<li>Better than vodka there is nothing worse.</li>
<li>The direction we have is one – the correct one.</li>
<li>A government is not that body which you can do with a tongue as you wish.</li>
<li>Better to be the head of a fly than the buttocks of an elephant.</li>
<li>27 millions were left with no arms, no leg, and without nothing else.</li>
<li>The soul hurts for grandchildren and the country.</li>
<li>You got to think what to understand.</li>
<li>Always we in Russia have something erect that we don't need instead of what is needed.</li>
<li>The principles that were principled were non-principled.</li>
<li>There is still time to save the face. Later we will be forced to save some other parts of a body.</li>
<li>Wine we need for health, and the health we need to drink vodka.</li>
<li>My life has passed in the atmosphere of oil and gas.</li>
<li>I can talk to anyone in any language, but that is the skill I try not to use.</li>
<li>Accuse of what? Of corruption? Whom? Me? Who? USA? What, did they, there, just wake up?</li>
<li>I don't draw and can't draw, but if I do draw something, it'll be more than you can handle.</li>
<li>We need to do what our people need, not what we are doing now.</li>
<li>The country we have – enough for her by skipping to do jumping.</li>
<li>Here is what can happen when somebody's starting to reason.</li>
<li>It has never been like this and now it is exactly the same again.</li>
<li>I am not going to speak much, otherwise I'll again say something.</li>
<li>The victims of the catastrophe should be personally liquidated.</li>
<li>You think it is simply too far for me... It is far from simple for me!</li>
<li>I feel today something of some kind in the air: there - who to whom, who to what...</li>
</ul>
