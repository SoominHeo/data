<p><b>Viktor Ivanovich Patsayev</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Ви́ктор Ива́нович Паца́ев</span>; 19 June 1933 – 30 June 1971)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> was a <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">cosmonaut</a> who flew on the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_11" title="Soyuz 11">Soyuz 11</a> mission and had the unfortunate distinction of being part of the second crew to die during a <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">space flight</a>. On board the space station <a href="/wiki/Salyut_1" title="Salyut 1">Salyut 1</a> he operated the Orion 1 Space Observatory (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orion_1_and_Orion_2_Space_Observatories" title="Orion 1 and Orion 2 Space Observatories">Orion 1 and Orion 2 Space Observatories</a>), he became the first man to operate a telescope outside the Earth’s atmosphere.</p>
<p>After a normal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Re-entry" title="Re-entry">re-entry</a>, the capsule was opened and the crew was found dead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It was discovered that a valve had opened just prior to leaving orbit that had allowed the capsule's atmosphere to <a href="/wiki/Space_exposure" title="Space exposure">vent away into space</a>, suffocating the crew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> One of Patsayev's hands was found to be bruised, and he may have been trying to shut the valve manually at the time he lost consciousness.</p>
<p>Patsayev's ashes were inturned in the <a href="/wiki/Kremlin_Wall_Necropolis" title="Kremlin Wall Necropolis">Kremlin Wall</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Red_Square" title="Red Square">Red Square</a> in Moscow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He was posthumously awarded the title of <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Hero of the Soviet Union">Hero of the Soviet Union</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Lenin" title="Order of Lenin">Order of Lenin</a> and the title of <a href="/wiki/Pilot-Cosmonaut_of_the_USSR" title="Pilot-Cosmonaut of the USSR">Pilot-Cosmonaut of the USSR</a>. The lunar crater <i><a href="/wiki/Patsaev_(crater)" title="Patsaev (crater)">Patsaev</a></i> and the minor planet <a href="/wiki/1791_Patsayev" title="1791 Patsayev">1791 Patsayev</a> are named for him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
