<p><b>Victor Franz Hess</b> (24 June 1883 – 17 December 1964) was an <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austrian</a>-<a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel laureate in physics</a>, who discovered <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-obit-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Victor_Francis_Hess&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was born to Vinzenz Hess and Serafine Edle von Grossbauer-Waldstätt, in <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schloss_Waldstein_(Deutschfeistritz)" title="de:Schloss Waldstein (Deutschfeistritz)">Waldstein Castle</a>, near Peggau in <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Styria" title="Duchy of Styria">Styria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> on 24 June 1883. His father was a royal forester in Prince <a href="/wiki/Louis_of_Oettingen-Wallerstein" title="Louis of Oettingen-Wallerstein">Louis of Oettingen-Wallerstein</a>'s service. He attended secondary school at <a href="/wiki/Graz" title="Graz">Graz</a> Gymnasium from 1893 to 1901.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobel_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-nobel-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Angelo_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Angelo-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1901 to 1905 Hess was an undergraduate student at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Graz" title="University of Graz">University of Graz</a>, and continued postgraduate studies in physics until he received his PhD there in 1910. He worked as Assistant under <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Meyer_(physicist)" title="Stefan Meyer (physicist)">Stefan Meyer</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_Radium_Research,_Vienna" title="Institute for Radium Research, Vienna">Institute for Radium Research</a>, Viennese Academy of Sciences, from 1910 to 1920.</p>
<p>In 1920 he married Marie Bertha Warner Breisky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-obit-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hess took a leave of absence in 1921 and travelled to the United States, working at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Radium_Corporation" title="United States Radium Corporation">United States Radium Corporation</a>, in <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, and as Consulting Physicist for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Bureau_of_Mines" title="US Bureau of Mines">US Bureau of Mines</a>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_DC" title="Washington DC">Washington DC</a>. In 1923, he returned to the University of Graz, and was appointed the Ordinary Professor of Experimental Physics in 1925. The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Innsbruck" title="University of Innsbruck">University of Innsbruck</a> appointed him Professor, and Director Institute of Radiology, in 1931.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobel_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-nobel-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hess relocated to the United States with his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jewish</a> wife in 1938, in order to escape <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> persecution. The same year <a href="/wiki/Fordham_University" title="Fordham University">Fordham University</a> appointed him Professor of Physics, and he later became a naturalized United States citizen in 1944.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTimes_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marquis_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marquis-5">[5]</a></sup> His wife died of cancer in 1955.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-obit-1">[1]</a></sup> The same year he married Elizabeth M. Hoenke, the woman who nursed Berta at the end of her life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTimes_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was a practicing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.deutsche-biographie.de/sfz31861.html" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>, and in 1946 he wrote on the topic of the relationship between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_and_religion" title="Science and religion">science and religion</a> in his article <i>"My Faith"</i>, in which he explained why he believed in God.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He retired from Fordham University in 1958 and he died on 17 December 1964, in <a href="/wiki/Mount_Vernon,_New_York" title="Mount Vernon, New York">Mount Vernon, New York</a> from <a href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Parkinson's disease">Parkinson's disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-obit-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmic_rays">Cosmic rays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Victor_Francis_Hess&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cosmic rays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hessballon.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1018" data-file-width="876" height="232" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Hessballon.jpg/200px-Hessballon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Hessballon.jpg/300px-Hessballon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Hessballon.jpg/400px-Hessballon.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hessballon.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Hess (center) at work</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Between 1911 and 1913, Hess undertook the work that won him the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1936. For many years, scientists had been puzzled by the levels of ionizing radiation measured in the atmosphere. The assumption at the time was that the radiation would decrease as the distance from the earth, the source of the radiation, increased. The <a href="/wiki/Electroscope" title="Electroscope">electroscopes</a> previously used gave an approximate measurement of the radiation, but indicated that at greater altitude in the atmosphere the level of radiation might actually be higher than that on the ground. Hess approached this mystery first by greatly increasing the precision of the measuring equipment, and then by personally taking the equipment aloft in a balloon. He systematically measured the radiation at altitudes up to 5.3 km during 1911-12. The daring flights were made both at day and during the night, at significant risk to himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Angelo_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Angelo-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The result of Hess's meticulous work was published in the Proceedings of the Viennese Academy of Sciences, and showed the level of radiation decreased up to an altitude of about 1 km, but above that the level increased considerably, with the radiation detected at 5 km being about twice that at sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> His conclusion was that there was radiation penetrating the atmosphere from outer space, and his discovery was confirmed by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Andrews_Millikan" title="Robert Andrews Millikan">Robert Andrews Millikan</a> in 1925, who gave the radiation the name "<a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a>". Hess's discovery opened the door to many new discoveries in <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">particle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_physics" title="Nuclear physics">nuclear physics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Angelo_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Angelo-3">[3]</a></sup> In particular, both the <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positron</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Muon" title="Muon">muon</a> were first discovered in cosmic rays by <a href="/wiki/Carl_David_Anderson" title="Carl David Anderson">Carl David Anderson</a>. Hess and Anderson shared the 1936 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a>.</p>
