<p><b>Lake Victoria</b> (<i><span lang="luo" xml:lang="luo">Nam Lolwe</span></i> in <a href="/wiki/Luo_languages" title="Luo languages">Luo</a>; <i><span lang="lg" xml:lang="lg">Nalubaale</span></i> in <a href="/wiki/Luganda" title="Luganda">Luganda</a>; <i><span lang="rw" xml:lang="rw">Nyanza</span></i> in <a href="/wiki/Kinyarwanda" title="Kinyarwanda">Kinyarwanda</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Bantu_languages" title="Bantu languages">Bantu languages</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WDL1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-WDL1-1">[1]</a></sup> is one of the <a href="/wiki/African_Great_Lakes" title="African Great Lakes">African Great Lakes</a>. The lake was named after <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> by the explorer <a href="/wiki/John_Hanning_Speke" title="John Hanning Speke">John Hanning Speke</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Briton</a> to document it. Speke accomplished this in 1858, while on an expedition with <a href="/wiki/Richard_Francis_Burton" title="Richard Francis Burton">Richard Francis Burton</a> to locate the <a href="/wiki/Nile#Search_for_the_source_of_the_Nile" title="Nile">source of the Nile River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>With a surface area of approximately 68,800 km<sup>2</sup> (26,600 sq mi),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fishery_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fishery-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Lake Victoria is Africa's largest lake by area, the world's largest <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical</a> lake,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and the world's second largest <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a> lake by surface area, after <a href="/wiki/Lake_Superior" title="Lake Superior">Lake Superior</a> in North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In terms of volume, Lake Victoria is the world's <a href="/wiki/List_of_lakes_by_volume" title="List of lakes by volume">ninth largest continental lake</a>, containing about 2,750 cubic kilometres (2.23<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>9</sup> acre·ft) of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holtzman_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holtzman-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lake Victoria receives its water primarily from direct rainfall and thousands of small <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">streams</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Kagera_River" title="Kagera River">Kagera River</a> is the largest river flowing into this lake, with its <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">mouth</a> on the lake's western shore. Lake Victoria is drained solely by the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile River</a> near <a href="/wiki/Jinja,_Uganda" title="Jinja, Uganda">Jinja, Uganda</a>, on the lake's northern shore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fishery_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fishery-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lake Victoria occupies a shallow <a href="/wiki/Depression_(geology)" title="Depression (geology)">depression</a> in Africa; it has a maximum depth of 84 m (276 ft) and an average depth of 40 m (130 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNEP99_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNEP99-9">[9]</a></sup> Its <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">catchment area</a> covers 184,000 km<sup>2</sup> (71,000 sq mi). The lake has a shoreline of 7,142 km (4,438 mi) when digitized at the 1:25,000 level,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> with islands constituting 3.7 percent of this length,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hickling61_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hickling61-11">[11]</a></sup> and is divided among three countries: <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a> (6 percent or 4,100 km<sup>2</sup> or 1,600 sq mi), <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a> (45 percent or 31,000 km<sup>2</sup> or 12,000 sq mi), and <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a> (49 percent or 33,700 km<sup>2</sup> or 13,000 sq mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>During its geological history, Lake Victoria went through changes ranging from its present shallow depression, through to what may have been a series of much smaller lakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hickling61_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hickling61-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Core_sample" title="Core sample">Geological cores</a> taken from its bottom show Lake Victoria has dried up completely at least three times since it formed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reader_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reader-13">[13]</a></sup> These drying cycles are probably related to <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">past ice ages</a>, which were times when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> declined globally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reader_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reader-13">[13]</a></sup> Lake Victoria last dried out 17,300 years ago, and it refilled beginning about 14,700 years ago. Geologically, Lake Victoria is relatively young – about 400,000 years old – and it formed when westward-flowing rivers were dammed by an upthrown crustal block.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reader_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Reader-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>This geological history probably contributed to the dramatic <a href="/wiki/Cichlid" title="Cichlid">cichlid</a> speciation that characterises its ecology, as well as that of other African Great Lakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Some researchers dispute this, arguing that while Lake Victoria was at its lowest between 18,000 and 14,000 years ago, and it dried out at least once during that time, there is no evidence of remnant ponds or marshes persisting within the desiccated basin. If such features existed, then they would have been small, shallow, turbid, and/or saline, and therefore markedly different from the lake to which today's species are adapted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>Its outflow controlled by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nalubaale_Power_Station" title="Nalubaale Power Station">Nalubaale Power Station</a> and associated barrages, the lake itself is much less vulnerable than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endorheic" title="Endorheic">endorheic</a> lakes to drops in rainfall, but the level of precipitation has a direct impact on the chief water supply of the main agricultural lands to the north in <a href="/wiki/South_Sudan" title="South Sudan">South Sudan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrology_and_limnology">Hydrology and limnology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Victoria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hydrology and limnology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lake Victoria receives 80 percent of its water from direct rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hickling61_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hickling61-11">[11]</a></sup> Average evaporation on the lake is between 2.0 and 2.2 metres (6.6 and 7.2 ft) per year, almost double the precipitation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riparian" title="Riparian">riparian</a> areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In the Kenya Sector, the main influent rivers are the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sio_River&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sio River (page does not exist)">Sio</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nzoia" title="Nzoia">Nzoia</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yala_River&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yala River (page does not exist)">Yala</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nyando_River&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nyando River (page does not exist)">Nyando</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sondu_Miriu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sondu Miriu (page does not exist)">Sondu Miriu</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mogusi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mogusi (page does not exist)">Mogusi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Migori" title="Migori">Migori</a>. Combined, these rivers contribute far more water to the lake than does the largest single river which enters the lake from the west, the <a href="/wiki/Kagera_River" title="Kagera River">Kagera River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rift.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="593" data-file-width="481" height="271" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Rift.svg/220px-Rift.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Rift.svg/330px-Rift.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Rift.svg/440px-Rift.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rift.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Lake Victoria and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Rift_Valley" title="Great Rift Valley">Great Rift Valley</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The only outflow from Lake Victoria is the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile River</a>, which exits the lake near Jinja, Uganda. In terms of contributed water, this makes Lake Victoria the principal source of the longest branch of the Nile; however, the most distal source of the Nile Basin, and therefore the ultimate <a href="/wiki/Nile#Sources" title="Nile">source of the Nile</a>, is more often considered to be one of the tributary rivers of the Kagera River (the exact tributary remains undetermined), and which originates in either <a href="/wiki/Rwanda" title="Rwanda">Rwanda</a> or <a href="/wiki/Burundi" title="Burundi">Burundi</a>. The uppermost section of the Nile is generally known as the Victoria Nile until it reaches <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Albert_(Africa)" title="Lake Albert (Africa)">Lake Albert</a>. Although it is a part of the same river system known as the <a href="/wiki/White_Nile" title="White Nile">White Nile</a> and is occasionally referred to as such, strictly speaking this name does not apply until after the river crosses the Uganda border into South Sudan to the north.</p>
<p>The lake exhibits <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophic conditions</a>. In 1990–1991, oxygen concentrations in the mixed layer were higher than in 1960–1961, with nearly continuous oxygen supersaturation in surface waters. Oxygen concentrations in <a href="/wiki/Hypolimnion" title="Hypolimnion">hypolimnetic waters</a> (i.e. the layer of water that lies below the <a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">thermocline</a>, is noncirculating, and remains perpetually cold) were lower in 1990–1991 for a longer period than in 1960–1961, with values of less than 1 mg per litre (&lt; 0.4 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grain_(mass)" title="Grain (mass)">gr</a>/cu ft) occurring in water as shallow as 40 metres (130 ft) compared with a shallowest occurrence of greater than 50 metres (160 ft) in 1961. The changes in oxygenation are considered consistent with measurements of higher algal biomass and productivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> These changes have arisen for multiple reasons: successive burning within its basin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> soot and ash from which has been deposited over the lake's wide area; from increased nutrient inflows via rivers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OchumbaKibaara_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-OchumbaKibaara-20">[20]</a></sup> and from increased pollution associated with settlement along its shores.</p>
<p>The extinction of <a href="/wiki/Cichlid" title="Cichlid">cichlids</a> in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Haplochromis" title="Haplochromis">Haplochromis</a></i> has also been blamed on the lake's eutrophication. The fertility of tropical waters depends on the rate at which nutrients can be brought into solution. The influent rivers of Lake Victoria provide few nutrients to the lake in relation to its size. Because of this, most of Lake Victoria's nutrients are thought to be locked up in lake-bottom deposits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hickling61_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hickling61-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beauchamp54_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beauchamp54-21">[21]</a></sup> By itself, this vegetative matter decays slowly. Animal flesh decays considerably faster, however, so the fertility of the lake is dependent on the rate at which these nutrients can be taken up by fish and other organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beauchamp54_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beauchamp54-21">[21]</a></sup> There is little doubt that <i>Haplochromis</i> played an important role in returning detritus and plankton back into solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lucy_Richardson_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lucy_Richardson-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KaufmanOchumba93_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-KaufmanOchumba93-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldschmidt93_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goldschmidt93-24">[24]</a></sup> With some 80% of <i>Haplochromis</i> species feeding off detritus, and equally capable of feeding off one another, they represented a tight, internal recycling system, moving nutrients and biomass both vertically and horizontally through the water column, and even out of the lake via predation by humans and terrestrial animals. The removal of <i>Haplochromis</i>, however, may have contributed to the increasing frequency of <a href="/wiki/Algal_bloom" title="Algal bloom">algal blooms</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OchumbaKibaara_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-OchumbaKibaara-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KaufmanOchumba93_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-KaufmanOchumba93-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldschmidt93_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goldschmidt93-24">[24]</a></sup> which may in turn be responsible for mass <a href="/wiki/Fish_kill" title="Fish kill">fish kills</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OchumbaKibaara_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-OchumbaKibaara-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>The lake is considered a shallow lake considering its large geographic area with a maximum depth of approximately 80 meters and an average depth of almost exactly 40 meters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> A 2016 project digitized ten-thousand points and created the first true bathymetric map of the lake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The deepest part of the lake is offset to the east of the lake near Kenya and the lake is generally shallower in the west along the Ugandan shoreline and the south along the Tanzanina shoreline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fisheries">Fisheries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Victoria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fisheries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fishing_on_Lake_Victoria" title="Fishing on Lake Victoria">Fishing on Lake Victoria</a></div>
<p>Lake Victoria supports Africa's largest inland <a href="/wiki/Fishery" title="Fishery">fishery</a> (as of 1997).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Geheb97_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Geheb97-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_issues">Environmental issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Victoria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Environmental issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_issue" title="Environmental issue">environmental issues</a> are associated with Lake Victoria.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Invasive_species">Invasive species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Victoria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Invasive species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The introduction of exotic fish species, especially the <a href="/wiki/Nile_perch" title="Nile perch">Nile perch</a>, has altered the <a href="/wiki/Freshwater_ecosystem" title="Freshwater ecosystem">freshwater ecosystem</a> of the lake and driven several hundred species of native <a href="/wiki/Cichlid" title="Cichlid">cichlids</a> to near or total <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a>. In the 1950s, a proposal to increase fish catches in the lake by introducing the Nile perch (mbutta or Sangara) was adamantly opposed by scientists who feared that the lack of a natural predator for the non-native species would result in the imminent destruction of the lake's bountiful ecosystem.</p>
<p>Despite the controversy, a colonial fisheries officer was ordered to clandestinely put the Nile perch into the portion of the lake that is in Uganda in 1952. Thereafter, it was introduced intentionally in both 1962 and 1963. And by 1964 Mbutta or Sangara was recorded in Tanzania, by 1970 it was well established in Kenya, and by the early 1980s it was abundant throughout Tanzania, Kenya and Uganda, the three countries surrounding the lake.</p>
<p>Now in fifty years' time or less, virtually the entire natural, biological "wealth" unique to Lake Victoria has been destroyed. At one time, the lake contained over 350 endemic species of haplochromines whose extraordinary diversity, and speed of evolution, were inspiring to scientists concerned with the forces that create and maintain the richness of life everywhere. Today, only a handful of species struggle to exist, threatened on one hand by an invasive species (the Nile perch) and on the other hand by the lake's changing conditions.</p>
<p>Due to the presence of the Nile perch, the natural balance of the lake's ecosystem has been disrupted. The food chain is being altered and in some cases, broken by the indiscriminate eating habits of the Nile perch. The subsequent decrease in the member of algae-eating fish allows the algae to grow at an alarming rate, thereby choking the lake. The increasing amounts of algae, in turn, increase the amount of detritus (dead plant material) that falls to the deeper portions of the lake before decomposing. As a by-product of this the oxygen levels in the deeper layer of water are being depleted. Without oxygen, any aerobic life (such as fish) cannot exist in the deeper parts of the lake, forcing all life to exist within a narrow range of depth. In this way, the Nile perch has degraded the diverse and thriving ecosystem that was once Lake Victoria. The abundance of aquatic life is not the only dependent of the lake: more than thirty million people in Tanzania, Kenya and Uganda rely on the lake for its natural resources.</p>
<p>The fishing industry, in particular, is also suffering. With traditional food sources all but extinct, and nets continuously damaged from the sheer force of the Nile perch, local fisheries have to abandon their work. The costs of scarce wood that is burned to dry the fish and of large processing plants that are built to prepare the fish for market preclude many fisheries from entering the Nile perch market. Only fisheries with large financial standings have been able to switch to catching the fish. As the Nile perch's food supply dwindles over time, so does its population, making these fisheries unsustainable. Having no natural predators in the lake and a plethora of food, The Nile perch flourished, often reaching up to 240 kg. Such eating habits are no longer sustainable. The fish has caused mass extinctions among haplochromine populations. And with few available food sources remaining, the Nile perch has taken to cannibalism, with the larger fish feasting on the smaller ones.</p>
<p>Hundreds of endemic species that evolved under the special conditions offered by the protection of Lake Victoria have been lost due to extinction, and several more are still threatened. Their loss is devastating for the lake, the fields of ecology, genetics and evolution biology, and more evidently, for the local fisheries. Local fisheries once depended on catching the lungfish, tilapia, carp and catfish that comprise the local diet. Today, the composition and yields of such fish catches are virtually negligible. Extensive fish kills, Nile perch, loss of habitat and overfishing have caused many fisheries to collapse and many protein sources to be unavailable at the market for local consumption. Few fisheries, though, have been able to make the switch to catching the Nile perch, since that requires a significant amount of capital resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_hyacinth_invasion">Water hyacinth invasion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Victoria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Water hyacinth invasion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Water_hyacinth_in_Lake_Victoria" title="Water hyacinth in Lake Victoria">Water hyacinth in Lake Victoria</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NdereIsland3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/NdereIsland3.jpg/220px-NdereIsland3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/NdereIsland3.jpg/330px-NdereIsland3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/NdereIsland3.jpg/440px-NdereIsland3.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NdereIsland3.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_hyacinth" title="Water hyacinth">hyacinth</a>-choked lakeshore at <a href="/wiki/Ndere_Island" title="Ndere Island">Ndere Island</a>, Lake Victoria, <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_hyacinth" title="Water hyacinth">Water hyacinth</a> has become a major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invasive_plant_species" title="Invasive plant species">invasive plant species</a> in Lake Victoria. The release of large amounts of untreated wastewater (sewage), agricultural and industrial runoff directly into Lake Victoria over the past 30 years, has greatly increased the nutrient levels of nitrogen and phosphorus in the lake "triggering massive growth of exotic Water hyacinth, which colonised the lake in the late 1990s".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luilo_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Luilo-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> This invasive weed creates anoxic (total depletion of oxygen levels) conditions in the lake inhibiting decomposing plant material, raising toxicity and disease levels to both fish and people. At the same time the plant's mat or "web" creates a barrier for boats and ferries to maneuver, impedes access to the shoreline, interferes with hydroelectric power generation, and blocks the intake of water for industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luilo_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Luilo-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kateregga.2C_E._.26_Sterner.2C_T._32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kateregga.2C_E._.26_Sterner.2C_T.-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Albright.2C_T.P..2C_et_al._35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Albright.2C_T.P..2C_et_al.-35">[35]</a></sup> On the other hand, Water hyacinth mats can potentially have a positive effect on fish life in that they create a barrier to overfishing and allow for fish growth, there has even been the reappearance of some fish species thought to have been extinct in recent years. However, the overall effects of the Water hyacinth are still unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kateregga.2C_E._.26_Sterner.2C_T._32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kateregga.2C_E._.26_Sterner.2C_T.-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Growth of the Water hyacinth in Lake Victoria has been tracked since 1993, reaching its maxima biomass in 1997 and then declining again by the end of 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kateregga.2C_E._.26_Sterner.2C_T._32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kateregga.2C_E._.26_Sterner.2C_T.-32">[32]</a></sup> Greater growth was observed in the northern part of the lake, in relatively protected areas, which may be linked to current and weather patterns and could also be due to the climate and water conditions, which are more suitable to the plants growth (as there are large urban areas to the north end of the lake, in Uganda).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Albright.2C_T.P..2C_et_al._35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Albright.2C_T.P..2C_et_al.-35">[35]</a></sup> The invasive weed was first attempted to be controlled by hand, removed manually from the lake, however, re-growth occurred quickly. Public awareness exercises were also conducted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Albright.2C_T.P..2C_et_al._35-2"><a href="#cite_note-Albright.2C_T.P..2C_et_al.-35">[35]</a></sup> More recently, measures have been used such as the introduction of natural insect predators, including two different Water hyacinth weevils and large harvesting and chopping boats, which seem to be much more effective in eliminating the Water hyacinth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Albright.2C_T.P..2C_et_al._35-3"><a href="#cite_note-Albright.2C_T.P..2C_et_al.-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other factors which may have contributed to the decline of the Water hyacinth in Lake Victoria include varying weather patterns, such as El Nino during the last few months of 1997 and first six months of 1998 bringing with it higher levels of water in the lake and thus dislodging the plants. Heavy winds and rains along with their subsequent waves may have also damaged the plants during this same time frame. The plants may not have been destroyed however, simply moved to another location. Additionally, the water quality and nutrient supply, temperature and other environmental factors could have played a role. Overall the timing of decline could be linked to all of these factors and perhaps together, in combination, they were more effective than any one deterrent would have been by itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Albright.2C_T.P..2C_et_al._35-4"><a href="#cite_note-Albright.2C_T.P..2C_et_al.-35">[35]</a></sup> The Water hyacinth is in remission and this trend could be permanent if control efforts are continued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollution">Pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Victoria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Density_evolution_Victoria.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2347" data-file-width="3139" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Density_evolution_Victoria.png/220px-Density_evolution_Victoria.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Density_evolution_Victoria.png/330px-Density_evolution_Victoria.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Density_evolution_Victoria.png/440px-Density_evolution_Victoria.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Density_evolution_Victoria.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Population density around Lake Victoria</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Pollution of Lake Victoria is mainly due to discharge of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raw_sewage" title="Raw sewage">raw sewage</a> into the lake, dumping of domestic and industrial waste, and fertiliser and chemicals from farms.</p>
<p>The Lake Victoria basin while generally rural has many major centres of population. Its shores in particular are dotted with the key cities and towns, including <a href="/wiki/Kisumu" title="Kisumu">Kisumu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kisii,_Kenya" title="Kisii, Kenya">Kisii</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Homa_Bay" title="Homa Bay">Homa Bay</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>; <a href="/wiki/Kampala" title="Kampala">Kampala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jinja,_Uganda" title="Jinja, Uganda">Jinja</a> and <a href="/wiki/Entebbe" title="Entebbe">Entebbe</a> in <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>; and Bukoba, <a href="/wiki/Mwanza" title="Mwanza">Mwanza</a> and <a href="/wiki/Musoma" title="Musoma">Musoma</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>. These cities and towns also are home to many factories that discharge some chemicals directly into the lake or its influent rivers. Large parts of these urban areas also discharge untreated (raw) sewage into the river, increasing its <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a> that in turn is helping to increase the invasive water hyacinth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-earthobservatory.nasa.gov_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-earthobservatory.nasa.gov-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_Data">Environmental Data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Victoria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Environmental Data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of 2016, an environmental data repository exists for <a class="external text" href="https://dataverse.harvard.edu/dataverse/LakeVicFish" rel="nofollow">Lake Victoria</a>. The repository contains shoreline, bathymetry, pollution, temperature, wind vector, and other important data for both the lake and the wider Basin.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_exploration">History and exploration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Victoria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: History and exploration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_National_Archives_UK_-_CO_1069-3-183.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="388" data-file-width="518" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/The_National_Archives_UK_-_CO_1069-3-183.jpg/220px-The_National_Archives_UK_-_CO_1069-3-183.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/The_National_Archives_UK_-_CO_1069-3-183.jpg/330px-The_National_Archives_UK_-_CO_1069-3-183.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/The_National_Archives_UK_-_CO_1069-3-183.jpg/440px-The_National_Archives_UK_-_CO_1069-3-183.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_National_Archives_UK_-_CO_1069-3-183.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bismarck Rock</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first recorded information about Lake Victoria comes from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab" title="Arab">Arab</a> traders plying the inland routes in search of <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ivory" title="Ivory">ivory</a>, other precious commodities, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave" title="Slave">slaves</a>. An excellent <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">map</a>, known as the <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_al-Idrisi" title="Muhammad al-Idrisi">Muhammad al-Idrisi</a> map from the calligrapher who developed it and dated from the 1160s, clearly depicts an accurate representation of Lake Victoria, and attributes it as the source of the Nile.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Africa_Lake_Victoria_10_006.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1728" data-file-width="2304" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Africa_Lake_Victoria_10_006.jpg/220px-Africa_Lake_Victoria_10_006.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Africa_Lake_Victoria_10_006.jpg/330px-Africa_Lake_Victoria_10_006.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Africa_Lake_Victoria_10_006.jpg/440px-Africa_Lake_Victoria_10_006.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Africa_Lake_Victoria_10_006.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The lake as it is visible from the shores of the Speke Resort in Kampala, Uganda</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The lake was first sighted by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_ethnic_groups" title="European ethnic groups">European</a> in 1858 when the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_explorers" title="List of explorers">explorer</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Hanning_Speke" title="John Hanning Speke">John Hanning Speke</a> reached its southern shore while on his journey with <a href="/wiki/Richard_Francis_Burton" title="Richard Francis Burton">Richard Francis Burton</a> to explore central Africa and locate the Great Lakes. Believing he had found the source of the Nile on seeing this "vast expanse of open water" for the first time, Speke named the lake after <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a>. Burton, who had been recovering from illness at the time and resting further south on the shores of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tanganyika" title="Lake Tanganyika">Lake Tanganyika</a>, was outraged that Speke claimed to have proved his discovery to have been the true source of the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a>, which Burton regarded as still unsettled. A very public quarrel ensued, which not only sparked a great deal of intense debate within the scientific community of the day, but also much interest by other explorers keen to either confirm or refute Speke's discovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNB_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the late 1860s, the famous British <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explorer" title="Explorer">explorer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">missionary</a> <a href="/wiki/David_Livingstone" title="David Livingstone">David Livingstone</a> failed in his attempt to verify Speke's discovery, instead pushing too far west and entering the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_Congo" title="River Congo">River Congo</a> system instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Ultimately, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welsh-American" title="Welsh-American">Welsh-American</a> explorer <a href="/wiki/Henry_Morton_Stanley" title="Henry Morton Stanley">Henry Morton Stanley</a>, on an expedition funded by the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Herald" title="New York Herald">New York Herald</a> newspaper, confirmed the truth of Speke's discovery, circumnavigating the lake and reporting the great outflow at <a href="/wiki/Ripon_Falls" title="Ripon Falls">Ripon Falls</a> on the lake's northern shore.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nalubaale_Dam">Nalubaale Dam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Victoria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Nalubaale Dam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nalubaale_Power_Station" title="Nalubaale Power Station">Nalubaale Power Station</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:From_top_of_Nalubaale_Power_Station.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/From_top_of_Nalubaale_Power_Station.jpg/220px-From_top_of_Nalubaale_Power_Station.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/From_top_of_Nalubaale_Power_Station.jpg/330px-From_top_of_Nalubaale_Power_Station.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/From_top_of_Nalubaale_Power_Station.jpg/440px-From_top_of_Nalubaale_Power_Station.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:From_top_of_Nalubaale_Power_Station.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Nalubaale_Hydroelectric_Power_Station" title="Nalubaale Hydroelectric Power Station">Nalubaale Hydroelectric Power Station</a> in <a href="/wiki/Njeru" title="Njeru">Njeru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The only outflow for Lake Victoria is at Jinja, Uganda, where it forms the Victoria Nile. The water since at least 12,000 years ago drained across a natural rock weir. In 1952, engineers acting for the government of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Uganda" title="British Uganda">British Uganda</a> blasted out the weir and reservoir to replace it with an artificial barrage to control the level of the lake and reduce the gradual erosion of the rock weir. A standard for mimicking the old rate of outflow called the "agreed curve" was established, setting the maximum flow rate at 300 to 1,700 <a href="/wiki/Cubic_metre_per_second" title="Cubic metre per second">cubic metres per second</a> (392–2,224 <a href="/wiki/Cubic_yard" title="Cubic yard">cu yd</a>/sec) depending on the lake's water level.</p>
<p>In 2002, Uganda completed a second <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric" title="Hydroelectric">hydroelectric</a> complex in the area, the <a href="/wiki/Kiira_Hydroelectric_Power_Station" title="Kiira Hydroelectric Power Station">Kiira Hydroelectric Power Station</a>, with <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> assistance. By 2006, the water levels in Lake Victoria had reached an 80-year low, and Daniel Kull, an independent hydrologist living in <a href="/wiki/Nairobi" title="Nairobi">Nairobi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, calculated that Uganda was releasing about twice as much water as is allowed under the agreement,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and was primarily responsible for recent drops in the lake's level.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Victoria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lake_Victoria_ferries" title="Lake Victoria ferries">Lake Victoria ferries</a></div>
<p>Since the 1900s, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Victoria_ferries" title="Lake Victoria ferries">Lake Victoria ferries</a> have been an important means of transport between <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>. The main ports on the lake are <a href="/wiki/Kisumu" title="Kisumu">Kisumu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mwanza" title="Mwanza">Mwanza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bukoba" title="Bukoba">Bukoba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Entebbe" title="Entebbe">Entebbe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Port_Bell" title="Port Bell">Port Bell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jinja,_Uganda" title="Jinja, Uganda">Jinja</a>. Until <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kenyan_independence" title="Kenyan independence">Kenyan independence</a> in 1963, the fastest and newest ferry, <a href="/wiki/MV_Victoria_(1959)" title="MV Victoria (1959)">MV <i>Victoria</i></a>, was designated a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Mail_Ship" title="Royal Mail Ship">Royal Mail Ship</a>. In 1966, <a href="/wiki/Train_ferry" title="Train ferry">train ferry</a> services between Kenya and Tanzania were established with the introduction of <a href="/wiki/MV_Uhuru" title="MV Uhuru">MV <i>Uhuru</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/MV_Umoja" title="MV Umoja">MV <i>Umoja</i></a>. The ferry <a href="/wiki/MV_Bukoba" title="MV Bukoba">MV <i>Bukoba</i></a> sank in the lake on May 21, 1996, with a loss of between 800 and 1,000 lives, making it one of Africa's worst maritime disasters.</p>
