<p><i><b>Victoria</b></i> is a genus of water-lilies, in the plant family <a href="/wiki/Nymphaeaceae" title="Nymphaeaceae">Nymphaeaceae</a>, with very large green leaves that lie flat on the water's surface. <i><a href="/wiki/Victoria_amazonica" title="Victoria amazonica">Victoria amazonica</a></i> has a leaf that is up to 3 metres (9.8 ft) in diameter, on a stalk up to 8 metres (26 ft) in length. The genus name was given in honour of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victoria_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Victoria of the United Kingdom">Queen Victoria</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Victoria_amazonica" title="Victoria amazonica">Victoria amazonica</a></i> is native to the shallow waters of the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon River</a> basin, such as oxbow lakes and bayous. It is depicted in the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Guyana" title="Coat of arms of Guyana">Guyanese coat of arms</a>. The flowers are white the first night they are open and become pink the second night. They are up to 40 cm in diameter, and are pollinated by <a href="/wiki/Scarabaeidae" title="Scarabaeidae">scarab beetles</a>.</p>
<p>Another species, <i><a href="/wiki/Victoria_cruziana" title="Victoria cruziana">Victoria cruziana</a></i>, in the <a href="/wiki/Paran%C3%A1_River" title="Paraná River">Parana</a>-<a href="/wiki/Paraguay_River" title="Paraguay River">Paraguay</a> basin, is only slightly smaller, with the underside of the leaves purple rather than the red of <i>V. amazonica</i>, and covered with a peachlike fuzz lacking in <i>V. amazonica</i>. <i>V. cruziana</i> opens its flowers at dusk.</p>
<p>The first published description of the genus was by <a href="/wiki/John_Lindley" title="John Lindley">John Lindley</a> in 1837, based on specimens returned from <a href="/wiki/British_Guiana" title="British Guiana">British Guiana</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Schomburgk" title="Robert Schomburgk">Robert Schomburgk</a>. Lindley named the genus after the new Queen, Victoria, and the species <i>Victoria regia</i>. An earlier account of the species, <i>Euryale amazonica</i> <a href="/wiki/Eduard_Friedrich_Poeppig" title="Eduard Friedrich Poeppig">Poeppig</a>, in 1832 described an affinity with <i><a href="/wiki/Euryale_ferox" title="Euryale ferox">Euryale ferox</a></i>. A collection and description was also made by the French botanist <a href="/wiki/Aim%C3%A9_Bonpland" title="Aimé Bonpland">Aimé Bonpland</a> in 1825.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPNI_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPNI-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knotts_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Knotts-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The leaf of <i>Victoria</i> is able to support quite a large weight due to the plant's structure, although the leaf itself is quite delicate: so much so that "a straw held 6 inches above and dropped perpendicularly upon it would readily pass through it".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ripley1861p992_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ripley1861p992-3">[3]</a></sup> To counter the fragile nature of the leaf, the weight needs to be distributed across the surface through mechanical means, such as a sheet of plywood. This allows the leaf to support up to 32 kilograms (71 lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ripley1861p992_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ripley1861p992-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
