<p>In <a href="/wiki/Folklore_of_the_United_States" title="Folklore of the United States">American folklore</a>, <b>Bigfoot</b> (also known as <b>Sasquatch</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Simian" title="Simian">simian</a>-like creature<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soucy_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Soucy-2">[2]</a></sup> that is said to inhabit forests, mainly in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a>. Bigfoot is usually described as a large, hairy, <a href="/wiki/Bipedalism" title="Bipedalism">bipedal</a> <a href="/wiki/Humanoid" title="Humanoid">humanoid</a>. The term <i>sasquatch</i> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglicization" title="Anglicization">Anglicized</a> derivative of the <a href="/wiki/Halkomelem" title="Halkomelem">Halkomelem</a> word <i>sásq'ets</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bright_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bright-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scientists discount the existence of Bigfoot and consider it to be a combination of <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a>, misidentification, and <a href="/wiki/Hoax" title="Hoax">hoax</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> rather than a living animal, because of the lack of physical evidence and the large numbers of creatures that would be necessary to maintain a breeding population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skepdic_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-skepdic-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-breedingpop_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-breedingpop-8">[8]</a></sup> Occasional new reports of sightings sustain a small group of self-described investigators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_Radford_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-SI_Radford-9">[9]</a></sup> Many reports of sightings are attributed to being various animals, particularly <a href="/wiki/American_black_bear" title="American black bear">black bears</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>Individuals claiming to have seen Bigfoot describe it as a large, hairy, muscular, bipedal ape-like creature, roughly 2–3 metres (6 ft 7 in–9 ft 10 in) covered in hair described as black, dark brown, or dark reddish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skepdic_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-skepdic-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-10">[10]</a></sup> Some descriptions include details such as large eyes, a pronounced <a href="/wiki/Supraorbital_ridge" title="Supraorbital ridge">brow ridge</a>, and a large, low-set forehead. The top of the head has been described as rounded and crested, similar to the <a href="/wiki/Sagittal_crest" title="Sagittal crest">sagittal crest</a> of the male <a href="/wiki/Gorilla" title="Gorilla">gorilla</a>, with a strong, unpleasant smell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The enormous footprints for which the creature is named are claimed to be as large as 24 inches (60 cm) long and 8 inches (20 cm) wide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-10">[10]</a></sup> Some footprint casts have also contained claw marks, making it likely that they came from known animals, such as bears, which have five toes and claws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_Nickell_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-SI_Nickell-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Proponents of Bigfoot's existence claim that the creature is <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivorous</a> and mainly <a href="/wiki/Nocturnality" title="Nocturnality">nocturnal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wild_men" title="Wild men">Wild men</a> stories are found among the indigenous peoples of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a> Coast. <a href="/wiki/Grover_Krantz" title="Grover Krantz">Grover Krantz</a> writes, "Native stories that can confidently be related to the sasquatch occur throughout the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a>. Their distribution corresponds to the area where White Man accounts<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> are concentrated."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Daegling" title="David Daegling">David Daegling</a>, the legends existed before there was a single name for the creature;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dae04_p28_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dae04_p28-16">[16]</a></sup> and that they differed in their details both regionally and between families in the same community; and that similar stories of wild men are found on every continent except <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dae04_p28_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dae04_p28-16">[16]</a></sup> Ecologist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Michael_Pyle" title="Robert Michael Pyle">Robert Pyle</a> argues that most cultures have human-like giants in their folk history: "We have this need for some larger-than-life creature."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodavage_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goodavage-17">[17]</a></sup> Each language had its own name for the creature featured in the local version of such legends. Many names meant something along the lines of "wild man" or "hairy man", although other names described common actions it was said to perform, <i>e.g.</i>, eating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clams" title="Clams">clams</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mil07_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mil07-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Members of the <a href="/wiki/Lummi" title="Lummi">Lummi</a> tell tales about <i>Ts'emekwes</i>, the local version of Bigfoot. The stories are similar to each other in the general descriptions of <i>Ts'emekwes</i>, but details about the creature's diet and activities differed between family stories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some regional versions contained more nefarious creatures. The <i>stiyaha</i> or <i>kwi-kwiyai</i> were a nocturnal race that children were told not to say the names of lest the monsters hear and come to carry off a person—sometimes to be killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rigsby_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rigsby-20">[20]</a></sup> In 1847, Paul Kane reported stories by the native people about <i><a href="/wiki/Skookum#monsters" title="Skookum">skoocooms</a></i>: a race of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cannibalistic" title="Cannibalistic">cannibalistic</a> wildmen living on the peak of <a href="/wiki/Mount_St._Helens" title="Mount St. Helens">Mount St. Helens</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_Nickell_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-SI_Nickell-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Less-menacing versions exist, such as the one recorded by Reverend <a href="/wiki/Elkanah_Walker" title="Elkanah Walker">Elkanah Walker</a>. In 1840, Walker, a Protestant missionary, recorded stories of giants among the Native Americans living near present-day <a href="/wiki/Spokane,_Washington" title="Spokane, Washington">Spokane, Washington</a>. The Indians said that these giants lived on and around the peaks of nearby mountains and stole <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> from the fishermen's nets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Local stories were compiled by Indian Agent J. W. Burns in a series of Canadian newspaper articles in the 1920s recounting stories told to him by the <a href="/wiki/Sts%27Ailes_people" title="Sts'Ailes people">Sts'Ailes people</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chehalis,_British_Columbia" title="Chehalis, British Columbia">Chehalis</a> and others. The Sts'Ailes maintain, as do other indigenous peoples of the region, that the Sasquatch are very real and take great umbrage when it is suggested that they are legendary. According to Sts'Ailes eyewitness accounts, the Sasquatch prefer to avoid white men, and speak the "Douglas language", i.e. <i><a href="/wiki/Lillooet_language" title="Lillooet language">Ucwalmicwts</a></i>, the language of the people at <a href="/wiki/Port_Douglas,_British_Columbia" title="Port Douglas, British Columbia">Port Douglas, British Columbia</a> at the head of <a href="/wiki/Harrison_Lake" title="Harrison Lake">Harrison Lake</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> It was Burns who first borrowed the term Sasquatch from the <a href="/wiki/Halkomelem" title="Halkomelem">Halkomelem</a> <i>sásq'ets</i> (<small>IPA: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[ˈsæsqʼəts]</a></span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bright_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bright-3">[3]</a></sup> and used it in his articles to describe a hypothetical single type of creature reflected in the stories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_Nickell_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-SI_Nickell-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mil07_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mil07-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>A story told to <a href="/wiki/Charles_Hill-Tout" title="Charles Hill-Tout">Charles Hill-Tout</a> by Chief Mischelle of the <a href="/wiki/Nlaka%27pamux" title="Nlaka'pamux">Nlaka'pamux</a> at <a href="/wiki/Lytton,_British_Columbia" title="Lytton, British Columbia">Lytton, British Columbia</a> in 1898 gives another <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salishan" title="Salishan">Salishan</a> variant of the name, meaning "the benign-faced-one".</p>
<p>Each language had its own name for the local version. Many names meant something along the lines of "wild man" or "hairy man" although other names described common actions it was said to perform, <i>e.g.</i>, eating clams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mil07_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mil07-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spotted_Elk.2C_bears_and_the_origin_of_the_.22Bigfoot.22_name">Spotted Elk, bears and the origin of the "Bigfoot" name</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bigfoot1890s.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="413" data-file-width="361" height="252" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Bigfoot1890s.png/220px-Bigfoot1890s.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Bigfoot1890s.png/330px-Bigfoot1890s.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/de/Bigfoot1890s.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bigfoot1890s.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
1895 article describing a giant grizzly bear named "Bigfoot".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Had_Slain_His_Thousand_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Had_Slain_His_Thousand-25">[25]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The name "Bigfoot" for the creature appeared in the late 19th century. <a href="/wiki/Spotted_Elk" title="Spotted Elk">Spotted Elk</a>, also called Chief Big Foot, was a well-known Lakota leader killed during the <a href="/wiki/Wounded_Knee_Massacre" title="Wounded Knee Massacre">Wounded Knee Massacre</a> in 1890. Famous in his time, his name likely inspired the name of two fabled attacking bears. In the late 19th and early 20th centuries at least two enormous marauding <a href="/wiki/Grizzly_bear" title="Grizzly bear">grizzly bears</a> nicknamed "Bigfoot" were widely noted in the press, perhaps inspiring the common name of the ape-creature and may be a source of confusion in early stories. The first grizzly bear Bigfoot was reportedly killed near Fresno, California in 1895 after killing sheep for 15 years; his weight was estimated at 2,000 pounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Had_Slain_His_Thousand_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Had_Slain_His_Thousand-25">[25]</a></sup> The second grizzly bear Bigfoot was active in Idaho in the 1890s and 1900s, between the Snake and Salmon rivers, and was attributed with nearly supernatural powers. "Nearly twice the size of an ordinary grizzly, Bigfoot for years has levied his tribute of prime steers and no one has been found brave enough or clever enough to catch or kill him. With a single blow of his giant paw he kills the largest and best animal he can find and he usually takes the pick of a herd. He makes a single meal of the animal, and it is usually a meal that would provide a camp full of men for a week, and disappears, never to return to that locality again that season."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The Idaho Bigfoot was shot and killed in 1902 near Pierce City, credited with killing 1,000 cattle in his lifetime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sightings">Sightings</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Reported_Bigfoot_sightings_(updated).svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1750" data-file-width="1750" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Reported_Bigfoot_sightings_%28updated%29.svg/220px-Reported_Bigfoot_sightings_%28updated%29.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Reported_Bigfoot_sightings_%28updated%29.svg/330px-Reported_Bigfoot_sightings_%28updated%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Reported_Bigfoot_sightings_%28updated%29.svg/440px-Reported_Bigfoot_sightings_%28updated%29.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Reported_Bigfoot_sightings_(updated).svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Distribution of reported Bigfoot sightings in North America.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>About one-third of all claims of Bigfoot sightings are located in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a>, with the remaining reports spread throughout the rest of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_Nickell_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-SI_Nickell-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Car08_p118_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Car08_p118-29">[29]</a></sup> Most reports are considered mistakes or hoaxes, even by researchers who maintain that Bigfoot exists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Radford2012_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Radford2012-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>As Bigfoot has become better known and a <a href="/wiki/Bigfoot_in_popular_culture" title="Bigfoot in popular culture">phenomenon in popular culture</a>, sightings have spread throughout North America. In addition to the Pacific Northwest, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes_region" title="Great Lakes region">Great Lakes region</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">Southeastern United States</a> have had many reports of Bigfoot sightings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The debate over the legitimacy of Bigfoot sightings reached a peak in the 1970s, and Bigfoot has been regarded as the first widely popularized example of pseudoscience in American culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McLeod_2009_4_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-McLeod_2009_4-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_explanations_for_sightings">Proposed explanations for sightings</span></h2>
<p>Various types of creatures have been suggested to explain both the sightings and what type of creature Bigfoot would be. The scientific community typically attributes sightings to either hoaxes or misidentification of known animals and their tracks, particularly <a href="/wiki/American_black_bear" title="American black bear">black bears</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holmes2009_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holmes2009-33">[33]</a></sup> While <a href="/wiki/Cryptozoology" title="Cryptozoology">cryptozoologists</a> generally explain Bigfoot as an unknown ape, some attribute the phenomenon to other causes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Misidentification">Misidentification</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Croped_BFRO_image.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="283" data-file-width="351" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/63/Croped_BFRO_image.jpg/220px-Croped_BFRO_image.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/63/Croped_BFRO_image.jpg/330px-Croped_BFRO_image.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/6/63/Croped_BFRO_image.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Croped_BFRO_image.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A 2007 photo of an unidentified animal the Bigfoot Research Organization claims is a "juvenile Sasquatch"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-debate_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-debate-35">[35]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Game_Commission" title="Pennsylvania Game Commission">Pennsylvania Game Commission</a> said that the photos that the Bigfoot Field Researchers Organization said showed a juvenile Bigfoot were of a bear with <a href="/wiki/Mange" title="Mange">mange</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap_bigfoot_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap_bigfoot-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Meldrum" title="Jeffrey Meldrum">Jeffrey Meldrum</a>, on the other hand, said that the limb proportions of the suspected juvenile in question were not bear-like, and stated that he felt they were "more like a chimpanzee."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hoaxes">Hoaxes</span></h3>
<p>Both Bigfoot believers and non-believers agree that many of the reported sightings are hoaxes or misidentified animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_Radford_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-SI_Radford-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bigfoot sightings or footprints have, in some cases, been shown to be hoaxes. Author Jerome Clark argues that the <a href="/wiki/Jacko_hoax" title="Jacko hoax">Jacko Affair</a>, involving an 1884 newspaper report of an apelike creature captured in British Columbia, was a hoax. Citing research by John Green, who found that several contemporary British Columbia newspapers regarded the alleged capture as very dubious, Clark notes that the <i>Mainland Guardian</i> of <a href="/wiki/New_Westminster" title="New Westminster">New Westminster</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, wrote, "Absurdity is written on the face of it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 14, 2005, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Biscardi" title="Tom Biscardi">Tom Biscardi</a>, a long-time Bigfoot enthusiast and CEO of Searching for Bigfoot Inc., appeared on the <i><a href="/wiki/Coast_to_Coast_AM" title="Coast to Coast AM">Coast to Coast AM</a></i> paranormal radio show and announced that he was "98% sure that his group will be able to capture a Bigfoot which they have been tracking in the <a href="/wiki/Happy_Camp,_California" title="Happy Camp, California">Happy Camp, California</a> area."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biscardi_C2C_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Biscardi_C2C-40">[40]</a></sup> A month later, Biscardi announced on the same radio show that he had access to a captured Bigfoot and was arranging a <a href="/wiki/Pay-per-view" title="Pay-per-view">pay-per-view</a> event for people to see it. Biscardi appeared on <i>Coast to Coast AM</i> again a few days later to announce that there was no captive Bigfoot. Biscardi blamed an unnamed woman for misleading him and the show's audience for being gullible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biscardi_C2C_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Biscardi_C2C-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 9, 2008, <a href="/wiki/Rick_Dyer" title="Rick Dyer">Rick Dyer</a> and Matthew Whitton posted a video to <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> claiming that they had discovered the body of a dead Sasquatch in a forest in northern <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>. Tom Biscardi was contacted to investigate. Dyer and Whitton received $50,000 from Searching for Bigfoot, Inc. as a <a href="/wiki/Good_faith" title="Good faith">good faith</a> gesture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The story of the men claims was covered by many major news networks, including <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/ABC_News" title="ABC News">ABC News</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox_News_Channel" title="Fox News Channel">Fox News</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Soon after a press conference, the alleged Bigfoot body arrived in a block of ice in a freezer with the Searching for Bigfoot team. When the contents were thawed, it was discovered that the hair was not real, the head was hollow, and the feet were rubber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Dyer and Whitton subsequently admitted it was a hoax after being confronted by Steve Kulls, executive director of SquatchDetective.com.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2012, a man in Montana was killed by a car while perpetrating a Bigfoot hoax using a <a href="/wiki/Ghillie_suit" title="Ghillie suit">ghillie suit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2014, Rick Dyer, perpetrator of a previous Bigfoot hoax, said he had killed a Bigfoot creature in September 2012 outside of San Antonio, Texas. He said he had scientific tests performed on the body, "from DNA tests to 3D optical scans to body scans. It is the real deal. It's Bigfoot, and Bigfoot's here, and I shot it, and now I'm proving it to the world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> He stated that he intended to take the body, which he had kept in a hidden location, on tour across North America in 2014. He released photos of the body and a video showing a few individuals' reactions to seeing it,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> but never released any of the tests or scans. He refused to disclose the test results or provide biological samples, although he stated that the DNA results, which were done by an undisclosed lab, could not identify any known animal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Dyer stated he would reveal the body and tests on February 9 at a news conference at Washington University,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bigfoot_Reveal_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bigfoot_Reveal-54">[54]</a></sup> but the test results were never made available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bigfoot_On_Tour_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bigfoot_On_Tour-55">[55]</a></sup> After the <a href="/wiki/Phoenix,_Arizona" title="Phoenix, Arizona">Phoenix</a> tour, the body traveled to <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bigfoot_On_Tour_Houston_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bigfoot_On_Tour_Houston-56">[56]</a></sup> On March 28, 2014, Dyer admitted on his <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> page that his "Bigfoot corpse" was another hoax. He had paid Chris Russel of Twisted Toy Box to manufacture the prop, which he nicknamed "Hank", from latex, foam, and camel hair. Dyer earned approximately <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US</a>$60,000 from the tour of this second fake Bigfoot corpse. He maintains that he did kill a Bigfoot, but states that he did not take the real body on tour for fear that it would be stolen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gigantopithecus"><i>Gigantopithecus</i></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gigantopithecus_blacki_mandible_010112.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2718" data-file-width="2490" height="273" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Gigantopithecus_blacki_mandible_010112.jpg/250px-Gigantopithecus_blacki_mandible_010112.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Gigantopithecus_blacki_mandible_010112.jpg/375px-Gigantopithecus_blacki_mandible_010112.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Gigantopithecus_blacki_mandible_010112.jpg/500px-Gigantopithecus_blacki_mandible_010112.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gigantopithecus_blacki_mandible_010112.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Fossil jaw of <i>Gigantopithecus blacki</i>, an extinct primate</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Bigfoot proponents <a href="/wiki/Grover_Krantz" title="Grover Krantz">Grover Krantz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_H._Bourne" title="Geoffrey H. Bourne">Geoffrey H. Bourne</a> believed that Bigfoot could be a <a href="/wiki/Relict_(biology)" title="Relict (biology)">relict population</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Gigantopithecus" title="Gigantopithecus">Gigantopithecus</a></i>. According to Bourne, all <i>Gigantopithecus</i> fossils were found in Asia, and, as many species of animals migrated across the <a href="/wiki/Beringia" title="Beringia">Bering land bridge</a>, it is not unreasonable to assume that <i>Gigantopithecus</i> might have as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Gigantopithecus</i> fossils have not been found in the Americas. The only recovered fossils are of <a href="/wiki/Mandible" title="Mandible">mandibles</a> and teeth, leaving uncertainty about <i>Gigantopithecus's</i> locomotion. Krantz has argued, based on his extrapolation of the shape of its mandible, that <i><a href="/wiki/Gigantopithecus" title="Gigantopithecus">Gigantopithecus blacki</a></i> could have been bipedal. However, the relevant part of the mandible is not present in any fossils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> An alternative view is that <i>Gigantopithecus</i> was <a href="/wiki/Quadrupedalism" title="Quadrupedalism">quadrupedal</a>, and it has been said that <i>Gigantopithecus's</i> enormous mass would have made it difficult for it to adopt a bipedal gait.</p>
<p>Matt Cartmill presents another view regarding the <i>Gigantopithecus</i> hypothesis: "The trouble with this account is that <i>Gigantopithecus</i> was not a hominin and maybe not even a <a href="/wiki/Crown_group" title="Crown group">crown group</a> hominoid; yet the physical evidence implies that Bigfoot is an upright biped with buttocks and a long, stout, permanently adducted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hallux" title="Hallux">hallux</a>. These are hominin autapomorphies, not found in other mammals or other bipeds. It seems unlikely that <i>Gigantopithecus</i> would have evolved these uniquely <a href="/wiki/Hominini" title="Hominini">hominin</a> traits in parallel."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bernard G. Campbell wrote: "That <i>Gigantopithecus</i> is in fact extinct has been questioned by those who believe it survives as the Yeti of the Himalayas and the Sasquatch of the north-west American coast. But the evidence for these creatures is not convincing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extinct_hominidae">Extinct hominidae</span></h3>
<p>A species of <i><a href="/wiki/Paranthropus" title="Paranthropus">Paranthropus</a></i>, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Paranthropus_robustus" title="Paranthropus robustus">Paranthropus robustus</a></i>, with its crested skull and bipedal gait, was suggested by <a href="/wiki/Primatology" title="Primatology">primatologist</a> <a href="/wiki/John_R._Napier" title="John R. Napier">John R. Napier</a> and anthropologist Gordon Strasenburg as a possible candidate for Bigfoot's identity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> despite the fact that fossils of <i>Paranthropus</i> are found only in Africa.</p>
<p>Michael Rugg, of the Bigfoot Discovery Museum, presented a comparison between human, <i>Gigantopithecus</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Meganthropus" title="Meganthropus">Meganthropus</a></i> skulls (reconstructions made by <a href="/wiki/Grover_Krantz" title="Grover Krantz">Grover Krantz</a>) in episodes 131 and 132 of the Bigfoot Discovery Museum Show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> He favorably compares a modern tooth suspected of coming from a Bigfoot to the <i>Meganthropus</i> fossil teeth, noting the worn enamel on the <a href="/wiki/Occlusion_(dentistry)" title="Occlusion (dentistry)">occlusal</a> surface. The <i>Meganthropus</i> fossils originated from Asia, and the tooth was found near Santa Cruz, California.</p>
<p>Some suggest <a href="/wiki/Neanderthal" title="Neanderthal">Neanderthal</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_erectus" title="Homo erectus">Homo erectus</a></i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_heidelbergensis" title="Homo heidelbergensis">Homo heidelbergensis</a></i> to be the creature, but no remains of any of those species have been found in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_view">Scientific view</span></h2>
<p>The evidence that does exist supporting the survival of such a large, prehistoric ape-like creature has been attributed to hoaxes or delusion rather than to sightings of a genuine creature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skepdic_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-skepdic-7">[7]</a></sup> In a 1996 <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> article, Washington State zoologist John Crane said, "There is no such thing as Bigfoot. No data other than material that's clearly been fabricated has ever been presented."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodavage_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goodavage-17">[17]</a></sup> In addition, scientists cite the fact that Bigfoot is alleged to live in regions unusual for a large, nonhuman primate, i.e., temperate latitudes in the northern hemisphere; all recognized apes are found in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> of Africa and Asia.</p>
<p>Mainstream scientists do not consider the subject of Bigfoot an area of credible science<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> and there have been a limited number of formal scientific studies of Bigfoot.</p>
<p>Evidence such as the 1967 Patterson–Gimlin film has provided "no supportive data of any scientific value".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>As with other similar beings, climate and food supply issues would make such a creature's survival in reported habitats unlikely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sj.C3.B6gren.2C_Bengt_1980_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sj.C3.B6gren.2C_Bengt_1980-68">[68]</a></sup> Great apes have not been found in the fossil record in the Americas, and no Bigfoot remains are known to have been found. The breeding population of such an animal would be so large that it would account for many more purported sightings than currently occur, making the existence of such an animal an almost certain impossibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-breedingpop_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-breedingpop-8">[8]</a></sup> In the 1970s, when Bigfoot experts were frequently given high-profile media coverage, Mcleod writes that the scientific community generally avoided lending credence to the theories by debating them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McLeod_2009_4_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-McLeod_2009_4-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Researchers">Researchers</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ivan_T._Sanderson" title="Ivan T. Sanderson">Ivan T. Sanderson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Heuvelmans" title="Bernard Heuvelmans">Bernard Heuvelmans</a> have spent parts of their career searching for Bigfoot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sci99_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sci99-69">[69]</a></sup> Later scientists who researched the topic included <a href="/wiki/Carleton_S._Coon" title="Carleton S. Coon">Carleton S. Coon</a>, George Allen Agogino and <a href="/wiki/William_Charles_Osman_Hill" title="William Charles Osman Hill">William Charles Osman Hill</a>, although they came to no definite conclusions and later drifted from this research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Regal2008_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Regal2008-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Meldrum" title="Jeffrey Meldrum">Jeffrey Meldrum</a> has said that the fossil remains of an ancient giant ape called <i>Gigantopithecus</i> could turn out to be ancestors of today's commonly known Bigfoot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> John Napier asserts that the scientific community's attitude towards Bigfoot stems primarily from insufficient evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> Other scientists who have shown varying degrees of interest in the creature are <a href="/wiki/Patterson%E2%80%93Gimlin_film#David_J._Daegling_and_Daniel_O._Schmitt" title="Patterson–Gimlin film">David J. Daegling</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_Schaller" title="George Schaller">George Schaller</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodavage_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Goodavage-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-latimes-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Russell_Mittermeier" title="Russell Mittermeier">Russell Mittermeier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daris_Swindler" title="Daris Swindler">Daris Swindler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Esteban_Sarmiento" title="Esteban Sarmiento">Esteban Sarmiento</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Carleton_S._Coon" title="Carleton S. Coon">Carleton S. Coon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formal_studies">Formal studies</span></h3>
<p>The first scientific study of available evidence was conducted by John Napier and published in his book, <i>Bigfoot: The Yeti and Sasquatch in Myth and Reality,</i> in 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> Napier wrote that if a conclusion is to be reached based on scant extant "'hard' evidence," science must declare "Bigfoot does not exist."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> However, he found it difficult to entirely reject thousands of alleged tracks, "scattered over 125,000 square miles" or to dismiss all "the many hundreds" of eyewitness accounts. Napier concluded, "I am convinced that Sasquatch exists, but whether it is all it is cracked up to be is another matter altogether. There must be <i>something</i> in north-west America that needs explaining, and that something leaves man-like footprints."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1974, the <a href="/wiki/National_Wildlife_Federation" title="National Wildlife Federation">National Wildlife Federation</a> funded a field study seeking Bigfoot evidence. No formal federation members were involved and the study made no notable discoveries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beginning in the late 1970s, physical anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Grover_Krantz" title="Grover Krantz">Grover Krantz</a> published several articles and four book-length treatments of Sasquatch. However, his work was found to contain multiple scientific failings including falling for hoaxes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p>A study published in the <a href="/wiki/Journal_of_Biogeography" title="Journal of Biogeography">Journal of Biogeography</a> in 2009 by J.D. Lozier et al. used ecological niche modeling on reported sightings of Bigfoot, using their locations to infer Bigfoot's preferred ecological parameters. They found a very close match with the ecological parameters of the <a href="/wiki/American_black_bear" title="American black bear">American black bear</a>, <i>Ursus americanus</i>. They also note that an upright bear looks much like Bigfoot's purported appearance and consider it highly improbable that two species should have very similar ecological preferences, concluding that Bigfoot sightings are likely sightings of black bears.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lozier2009_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lozier2009-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the first systematic genetic analysis of 30 hair samples that were suspected to be from bigfoot, yeti, sasquatch, almasty or other anomalous primates, only one was found to be primate in origin, and that was identified as human. A joint study by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_Cantonal_de_Zoologie" title="Musée Cantonal de Zoologie">Lausanne's Cantonal Museum of Zoology</a> and published in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proceedings_of_the_Royal_Society_B" title="Proceedings of the Royal Society B">Proceedings of the Royal Society B</a> in 2014, the team used a previously published cleaning method to remove all surface contamination and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribosomal" title="Ribosomal">ribosomal</a> <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> 12S fragment of the sample was sequenced and then compared to <a href="/wiki/GenBank" title="GenBank">GenBank</a> to identify the species origin. The samples submitted were from different parts of the world, including the United States, Russia, the Himalayas, and Sumatra. Other than one sample of human origin, all but two are from common animals. Black and brown bear accounted for most of the samples, other animals include cow, horse, dog/wolf/coyote, sheep, goat, raccoon, porcupine, deer and tapir. The last two samples were thought to match a fossilized genetic sample of a 40,000 year old polar bear of the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> epoch;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> however, a later study disputes this finding. In the second paper, tests identified the hairs as being from a rare type of brown bear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bigfoot_claims">Bigfoot claims</span></h2>
<p>After what <i><a href="/wiki/The_Huffington_Post" title="The Huffington Post">The Huffington Post</a></i> described as "a five-year study of purported Bigfoot (also known as Sasquatch) DNA samples,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HuffingtonPost_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-HuffingtonPost-88">[88]</a></sup> Texas veterinarian Melba Ketchum and her team announced that they had found proof that the Sasquatch "is a human relative that arose approximately 15,000 years ago as a hybrid cross of modern <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_sapiens" title="Homo sapiens">Homo sapiens</a></i> with an unknown primate species". Ketchum called for this to be recognized officially, saying that "Government at all levels must recognize them as an indigenous people and immediately protect their human and Constitutional rights against those who would see in their physical and cultural differences a 'license' to hunt, trap, or kill them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicholson_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nicholson-89">[89]</a></sup> Failing to find a <a href="/wiki/Scientific_journal" title="Scientific journal">scientific journal</a> that would publish their results, Ketchum announced on February 13, 2013 that their research had been published in the <i>DeNovo Journal of Science</i>. <i>The Huffington Post</i> discovered that the journal's domain had been registered anonymously only nine days before the announcement. The only edition of DeNovo was listed as Volume 1, Issue 1, and its only content was the Bigfoot research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicholson_89-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nicholson-89">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discovery.com_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-Discovery.com-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bigfoot_organizations">Bigfoot organizations</span></h2>
<p>There are several organizations dedicated to the research and investigation of Bigfoot sightings in the United States. The oldest and largest is the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bigfoot_Field_Researchers_Organization&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bigfoot Field Researchers Organization (page does not exist)">Bigfoot Field Researchers Organization</a> (BFRO).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BFRO_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-BFRO-92">[92]</a></sup> The BFRO also provides a free database to individuals and other organizations. Their website includes reports from across North America that have been investigated by researchers to determine credibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2016, the University of New Mexico at Gallup held a two-day Bigfoot conference, at a cost of $7,000 in university funds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bigfoot_in_popular_culture" title="Bigfoot in popular culture">Bigfoot in popular culture</a></div>
<p>Bigfoot has had a demonstrable impact as a <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">popular culture</a> phenomenon. It has "become entrenched in American popular culture and it is as viable an icon as Michael Jordan" with more than forty-five years having passed since reported sightings in California, and neither an animal nor "a satisfying explanation as to why folks see giant hairy men that don't exist".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup></p>
<p>When asked for her opinion of Bigfoot in a September 27, 2002, interview on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Public_Radio" title="National Public Radio">National Public Radio</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Science_Friday" title="Science Friday">Science Friday</a>", <a href="/wiki/Jane_Goodall" title="Jane Goodall">Jane Goodall</a> said "I'm sure they exist", and later said, chuckling, "Well, I'm a romantic, so I always wanted them to exist", and finally, "You know, why isn't there a body? I can't answer that, and maybe they don't exist, but I want them to."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPR_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-96">[96]</a></sup> In 2012, when asked again by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huffington_Post" title="Huffington Post">Huffington Post</a>, Goodall said "I'm fascinated and would actually love them to exist," adding, "Of course, it's strange that there has never been a single authentic hide or hair of the Bigfoot, but I've read all the accounts."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup></p>
