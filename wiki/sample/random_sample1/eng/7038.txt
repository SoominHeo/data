<p>The <b>Academy of Fine Arts Vienna</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Akademie der bildenden Künste Wien</i></span>) is a public <a href="/wiki/Art_school" title="Art school">art school</a> of higher education in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academy_of_Fine_Arts_Vienna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Academy of Fine Arts Vienna was founded in 1692 as a private academy modelled on the <a href="/wiki/Accademia_di_San_Luca" title="Accademia di San Luca">Accademia di San Luca</a> and the Parisien <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_de_peinture_et_de_sculpture" title="Académie de peinture et de sculpture">Académie de peinture et de sculpture</a> by the court-painter <a href="/wiki/Peter_Strudel" title="Peter Strudel">Peter Strudel</a>, who became the <i>Praefectus Academiae Nostrae</i>. In 1701 he was ennobled by Emperor <a href="/wiki/Joseph_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Joseph I, Holy Roman Emperor">Joseph I</a> as <i><a href="/wiki/Freiherr" title="Freiherr">Freiherr</a></i> (Baron) of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Empire</a>. With his death in 1714, the academy temporarily closed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aktsaal_der_Wiener_Akademie_(1787,_Martin_Ferdinand_Quadal).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1661" data-file-width="2365" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Aktsaal_der_Wiener_Akademie_%281787%2C_Martin_Ferdinand_Quadal%29.jpg/220px-Aktsaal_der_Wiener_Akademie_%281787%2C_Martin_Ferdinand_Quadal%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Aktsaal_der_Wiener_Akademie_%281787%2C_Martin_Ferdinand_Quadal%29.jpg/330px-Aktsaal_der_Wiener_Akademie_%281787%2C_Martin_Ferdinand_Quadal%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Aktsaal_der_Wiener_Akademie_%281787%2C_Martin_Ferdinand_Quadal%29.jpg/440px-Aktsaal_der_Wiener_Akademie_%281787%2C_Martin_Ferdinand_Quadal%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>On 20 January 1725, Emperor <a href="/wiki/Charles_VI,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles VI, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles VI</a> appointed the Frenchman <a href="/wiki/Jacob_van_Schuppen" title="Jacob van Schuppen">Jacob van Schuppen</a> as Prefect and Director of the Academy, which was refounded as the <i>k.k. Hofakademie der Maler, Bildhauer und Baukunst</i> (Imperial and Royal Court Academy of painters, sculptors and architecture). Upon Charles' death in 1740, the academy at first declined, however during the rule of his daughter Empress <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maria_Theresa_of_Austria" title="Maria Theresa of Austria">Maria Theresa</a>, a new statute reformed the academy in 1751. The prestige of the academy grew during the deanships of <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo_Unterberger" title="Michelangelo Unterberger">Michelangelo Unterberger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Troger" title="Paul Troger">Paul Troger</a>, and in 1767 the archduchesses <a href="/wiki/Archduchess_Maria_Anna_of_Austria_(1738%E2%80%931789)" title="Archduchess Maria Anna of Austria (1738–1789)">Maria Anna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maria_Carolina_of_Austria" title="Maria Carolina of Austria">Maria Carolina</a> were made the first Honorary Members. In 1772, there were further reforms to the organisational structure. Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Wenzel_Anton,_Prince_of_Kaunitz-Rietberg" title="Wenzel Anton, Prince of Kaunitz-Rietberg">Wenzel Anton Kaunitz</a> integrated all existing art schools into the <i>k.k. vereinigten Akademie der bildenden Künste</i> (Imperial and Royal Unified Academy of Fine Arts). The word "vereinigten" (unified) was later dropped. In 1822 the art cabinet grew significantly with the bequest of honorary member <a href="/wiki/Anton_Franz_de_Paula_Graf_Lamberg-Sprinzenstein" title="Anton Franz de Paula Graf Lamberg-Sprinzenstein">Anton Franz de Paula Graf Lamberg-Sprinzenstein</a>. His collection still forms the backbone of the art on display.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1872 Emperor <a href="/wiki/Franz_Joseph_I_of_Austria" title="Franz Joseph I of Austria">Franz Joseph I of Austria</a> approved a statute making the academy the supreme government authority for the arts. A new building was constructed according to plans designed by the faculty <a href="/wiki/Theophil_Hansen" title="Theophil Hansen">Theophil Hansen</a> in the course of the layout of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ringstra%C3%9Fe" title="Ringstraße">Ringstraße</a> boulevard. On 3 April 1877, the present-day building on Schillerplatz in the <a href="/wiki/Innere_Stadt" title="Innere Stadt">Innere Stadt</a> district was inaugurated, the interior works, including ceiling frescos by <a href="/wiki/Anselm_Feuerbach" title="Anselm Feuerbach">Anselm Feuerbach</a>, continued until 1892. In 1907 and 1908, young <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>, who had come from <a href="/wiki/Linz" title="Linz">Linz</a>, was twice denied admission to the drawing class. He stayed in Vienna, subsisting on his orphan allowance, and tried unsuccessfully to continue his profession as an artist. Soon he had withdrawn into poverty and started selling amateur paintings, mostly <a href="/wiki/Watercolor_painting" title="Watercolor painting">watercolours</a>, for meagre sustenance until he left Vienna for <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> in May 1913.</p>

<p>During the Austrian <i><a href="/wiki/Anschluss" title="Anschluss">Anschluss</a></i> to <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> from 1938–1945, the academy was forced to heavily reduce its number of Jewish staff. After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the academy was reconstituted in 1955 and its autonomy reconfirmed. It has had <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a> status since 1998, but retained its original name. It is currently the only Austrian university that doesn't have the word "university" in its name.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academy_of_Fine_Arts_Vienna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The academy is divided into the following institutes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>Institute for Fine Arts</i>, which houses thirteen departments: Abstract Painting; Art and Digital Media; Art and Photography; Arts and Research; Conceptual Art; Contextual Painting; Expanded Pictorial Space; Figurative Painting; Graphic Arts and Printmaking Techniques; Object Sculpture; Performative Art - Sculpture; Video and Video-installation; Textual Sculpture<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Institute for Art Theory and Cultural Studies</i> (art theory, philosophy, history);</li>
<li><i>Institute for Conservation and Restoration</i>;</li>
<li><i>Institute for Natural Sciences and Technologies in Art</i>;</li>
<li><i>Institute for Secondary School Teaching Degrees</i> (craft, design, textile arts);</li>
<li><i>Institute for Art and Architecture</i>.</li>
</ul>
<p>The Academy currently has about 900 students, almost a quarter of which are foreign students. Its faculty includes "stars" such as <a href="/wiki/Peter_Sloterdijk" title="Peter Sloterdijk">Peter Sloterdijk</a>. Its library houses approx. 110,000 volumes and its "etching cabinet" (<i>Kupferstichkabinett</i>) has about 150,000 drawings and prints. The collection is one of the biggest in Austria, and is used for academic purposes, although portions are also open to the general public.</p>
