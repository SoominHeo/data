<p>The <b>Kunsthistorisches Museum</b> (<a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>: "Museum of Art History", also often referred to as the "Museum of Fine Arts") is an art museum in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>. Housed in its festive palatial building on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ringstra%C3%9Fe" title="Ringstraße">Ringstraße</a>, it is crowned with an octagonal <a href="/wiki/Dome" title="Dome">dome</a>. The term <i>Kunsthistorisches Museum</i> applies to both the institution and the main building. It is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_art_museums" title="List of largest art museums">largest</a> art museum in the country.</p>
<p>It was opened around 1891 at the same time as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naturhistorisches_Museum" title="Naturhistorisches Museum">Naturhistorisches Museum</a></i>, by Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franz_Joseph_I" title="Franz Joseph I">Franz Joseph I</a> of <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a>. The two museums have similar exteriors and face each other across <a href="/wiki/Maria-Theresien-Platz" title="Maria-Theresien-Platz">Maria-Theresien-Platz</a>. Both buildings were built between 1871 and 1891 according to plans drawn up by <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Semper" title="Gottfried Semper">Gottfried Semper</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karl_Freiherr_von_Hasenauer" title="Karl Freiherr von Hasenauer">Karl Freiherr von Hasenauer</a>.</p>
<p>The two Ringstraße museums were commissioned by the Emperor in order to find a suitable shelter for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habsburg" title="Habsburg">Habsburgs</a>' formidable art collection and to make it accessible to the general public. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fa%C3%A7ade" title="Façade">façade</a> was built of <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a>. The building is rectangular in shape, and topped with a dome that is 60 meters high. The inside of the building is lavishly decorated with marble, stucco ornamentations, gold-leaf, and paintings.</p>

<div class="hatnote selfreference">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Paintings_of_the_Kunsthistorisches_Museum" title="Category:Paintings of the Kunsthistorisches Museum">Category:Paintings of the Kunsthistorisches Museum</a>.</div>
<p>The museum's primary collections are those of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habsburg" title="Habsburg">Habsburgs</a>, particularly from the <a href="/wiki/Portrait" title="Portrait">portrait</a> and <a href="/wiki/Armour" title="Armour">armour</a> collections of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Austria" title="Ferdinand II of Austria">Ferdinand of Tirol</a>, the collections of Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rudolph_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Rudolph II, Holy Roman Emperor">Rudolph II</a> (the largest part of which is, however, scattered), and the collection of paintings of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archduke_Leopold_Wilhelm" title="Archduke Leopold Wilhelm">Archduke Leopold Wilhelm</a>, of which his Italian paintings were first documented in the <i><a href="/wiki/Theatrum_Pictorium" title="Theatrum Pictorium">Theatrum Pictorium</a></i>.</p>
<p>Notable works in the picture gallery include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jan_van_Eyck" title="Jan van Eyck">Jan van Eyck</a>: <i><a href="/wiki/Portrait_of_Cardinal_Niccol%C3%B2_Albergati" title="Portrait of Cardinal Niccolò Albergati">Portrait of Cardinal Niccolò Albergati</a></i> (c. 1431)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a>: <i><a href="/wiki/Adoration_of_the_Trinity" title="Adoration of the Trinity">Adoration of the Trinity</a></i> (1511)</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_events">Recent events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kunsthistorisches_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Recent events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the museum's most important objects, the <i><a href="/wiki/Cellini_Salt_Cellar" title="Cellini Salt Cellar">Cellini Salt Cellar</a></i> sculpture by <a href="/wiki/Benvenuto_Cellini" title="Benvenuto Cellini">Benvenuto Cellini</a>, was stolen on May 11, 2003 and recovered on January 21, 2006, in a box buried in a forest near the town of <a href="/wiki/Zwettl" title="Zwettl">Zwettl</a>, Austria. It was featured in an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Museum_Secrets" title="Museum Secrets">Museum Secrets</a></i> on the <a href="/wiki/History_(U.S._TV_channel)" title="History (U.S. TV channel)">History Channel</a>. It had been the biggest <a href="/wiki/Art_theft" title="Art theft">art theft</a> in Austrian history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The museum is the subject of Johannes Holzhausen's documentary film <i>The Great museum</i> (2014), filmed over two years in the run up to the re-opening of the newly renovated and expanded Kunstkammer rooms in 2013.</p>
