<p>The <b>Vienna Boys' Choir</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Wiener Sängerknaben</i></span> ) or <b>Vienna Choir Boys</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Choir" title="Choir">choir</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boy_sopranos" title="Boy sopranos">boy sopranos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alto" title="Alto">altos</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>. It is one of the best known <a href="/wiki/Boys%27_choir" title="Boys' choir">boys' choirs</a> in the world. The boys are selected mainly from Austria, but also from many other countries.</p>
<p>The choir is a private, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-profit_organization" title="Non-profit organization">not-for-profit organization</a>. There are approximately 100 choristers between the ages of ten and fourteen. The boys are divided into four touring choirs, named after Austrian composers <a href="/wiki/Anton_Bruckner" title="Anton Bruckner">Bruckner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Haydn" title="Joseph Haydn">Haydn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Mozart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Schubert" title="Franz Schubert">Schubert</a>, which combined perform about 300 concerts each year before almost 500,000 people. Each group tours for about nine to eleven weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsk_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-wsk-1">[1]</a></sup>Some pieces include "Good Morning" and "Merry Christmas from Vienna Boys."</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vienna_Boys%27_Choir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The choir is the modern-day descendant of the boys' choirs of the Viennese Court, dating back to the late <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. The choir was, for practical purposes, established by a letter from Emperor <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Maximilian I, Holy Roman Emperor">Maximilian I</a> of Habsburg on 30 June 1498, instructing court officials to employ a singing master, two basses and six boys. <a href="/wiki/Jurij_Slatkonja" title="Jurij Slatkonja">Jurij Slatkonja</a> became the director of the ensemble. The role of the choir (numbering between 24 and 26) was to provide musical accompaniment for the church mass Additionally, the <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Haydn" title="Joseph Haydn">Haydn</a> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Haydn" title="Michael Haydn">brothers</a> were members of the <a href="/wiki/St._Stephen%27s_Cathedral,_Vienna" title="St. Stephen's Cathedral, Vienna">St. Stephen's Cathedral</a> choir, directed at the time by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georg_Reutter_II" title="Georg Reutter II">Georg Reutter II</a> who used this choir in his duties for the imperial court which at the time had no boy choristers of its own.</p>
<p>Over the centuries, the choir has worked with many composers, including <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Isaac" title="Heinrich Isaac">Heinrich Isaac</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Hofhaimer" title="Paul Hofhaimer">Hofhaimer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heinrich_Ignaz_Biber" title="Heinrich Ignaz Biber">Biber</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johann_Fux" title="Johann Fux">Fux</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Caldara" title="Antonio Caldara">Caldara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christoph_Willibald_Gluck" title="Christoph Willibald Gluck">Gluck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Salieri" title="Antonio Salieri">Salieri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Mozart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franz_Schubert" title="Franz Schubert">Franz Schubert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anton_Bruckner" title="Anton Bruckner">Bruckner</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsk_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-wsk-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1920, following the fall of the Austrian Empire, the Hofkapelle (court orchestra) was disbanded. However, the <a href="/wiki/Rector_(ecclesiastical)" title="Rector (ecclesiastical)">rector</a> at the time, Josef Schnitt, sought a continuation of the tradition. In 1924 the Vienna Boys' Choir was officially founded, and it has evolved into a professional music group. The choir adopted the now-famous blue and white <a href="/wiki/Sailor_suit" title="Sailor suit">sailor suit</a>, replacing the imperial military cadet uniform that included a <a href="/wiki/Dagger" title="Dagger">dagger</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The composer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HK_Gruber" title="HK Gruber">HK Gruber</a> is one of the graduates of the reformed choir.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Since 1948 the <a href="/wiki/Palais_Augarten" title="Palais Augarten">Palais Augarten</a> has served as their rehearsal venue and boarding school, which goes from <a href="/wiki/Kindergarten" title="Kindergarten">kindergarten</a> level up to middle school level.</p>
<p>In 1961, Walt Disney filmed <i><a href="/wiki/Almost_Angels" title="Almost Angels">Almost Angels</a></i>, a fictional drama about (and starring) the Vienna Boys' Choir, set and filmed in the Palais Augarten. It was Disney who, for cinematographic reasons, persuaded the Austrian government to allow the boys to legally wear the Austrian national emblem on the breast of their uniform, a tradition that continues to this day.</p>
<p>Gerald Wirth became the choir's artistic director in 2001. However, since then, the choir has come under pressure to modernize and has faced criticism of their musical standards, leading to a split with the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_State_Opera" title="Vienna State Opera">Vienna State Opera</a>. The choir has for the first time had to advertise for recruits after a rival choir school was established by <a href="/wiki/Ioan_Holender" title="Ioan Holender">Ioan Holender</a>, director of the opera company. He complained of both falling standards and poor communication with the choir. He said that the State Opera sometimes trained boys for particular stage roles, only to find out on the day of performance that they were unavailable as they had gone on tour with the choir. Some boys were attracted to the rival choir school by the prospect of a more relaxed atmosphere and of performance fees being paid directly to them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The Vienna Boys' Choir has sought to update its image, recording pop music selections and adopting an alternative uniform to the sailor suits used since the 1920s, allowing the boys to dance as they sing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> After Eugen Jesser died in May 2008, Walter Nettig.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Gerald Wirth is its present director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsk_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-wsk-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, following <a href="/wiki/Sexual_abuse" title="Sexual abuse">sexual abuse</a> allegations from two former choristers stemming from the late 1960s and early 1980s, the Vienna Boys' Choir opened a confidential phone and e-mail hotline to allow others to come forward. Eight possible victims came forward saying they were abused, either by staff or other choir members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
