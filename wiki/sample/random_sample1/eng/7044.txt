<p>The <b>Lion Monument</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Löwendenkmal</i></span>), or the <b>Lion of Lucerne</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Rock_relief" title="Rock relief">rock relief</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lucerne" title="Lucerne">Lucerne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, designed by <a href="/wiki/Bertel_Thorvaldsen" title="Bertel Thorvaldsen">Bertel Thorvaldsen</a> and hewn in 1820–21 by Lukas Ahorn. It commemorates the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swiss_Guard" title="Swiss Guard">Swiss Guards</a> who <a href="/wiki/10_August_(French_Revolution)" title="10 August (French Revolution)">were massacred in 1792 during the French Revolution</a>, when revolutionaries stormed the <a href="/wiki/Tuileries_Palace" title="Tuileries Palace">Tuileries Palace</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">Mark Twain</a> praised the sculpture of a mortally-wounded <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lion</a> as "the most mournful and moving piece of stone in the world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twain_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-twain-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lion_Monument&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/10_August_(French_Revolution)" title="10 August (French Revolution)">10 August (French Revolution)</a></div>
<p>From the early 17th century, a <a href="/wiki/Regiment" title="Regiment">regiment</a> of <a href="/wiki/Swiss_mercenaries#Service_in_the_French_army" title="Swiss mercenaries">Swiss Guards</a> had served as part of the <a href="/wiki/Maison_du_Roi" title="Maison du Roi">Royal Household</a> of France. On 6 October 1789, King <a href="/wiki/Louis_XVI_of_France" title="Louis XVI of France">Louis XVI</a> had been forced to move with his family from the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Versailles" title="Palace of Versailles">Palace of Versailles</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Tuileries_Palace" title="Tuileries Palace">Tuileries Palace</a> in Paris. In June 1791 he tried to <a href="/wiki/Flight_to_Varennes" title="Flight to Varennes">flee to Montmédy</a> near the frontier, where troops under royalist officers were concentrated. In the 1792 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/10th_of_August_(French_Revolution)" title="10th of August (French Revolution)">10th of August Insurrection</a>, revolutionaries stormed the palace. Fighting broke out spontaneously after the Royal Family had been escorted from the Tuileries to take refuge with the <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_(France)" title="Legislative Assembly (France)">Legislative Assembly</a>. The Swiss Guards ran low on ammunition and were overwhelmed by superior numbers. A note written by the King half an hour after firing had commenced has survived, ordering the Swiss to retire and return to their barracks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>Delivered in the middle of the fighting, this was only acted on after their position had become untenable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of the Swiss Guards defending the Tuileries, more than six hundred were killed during the fighting or massacred after surrender. An estimated two hundred more died in prison of their wounds or were killed during the <a href="/wiki/September_Massacres" title="September Massacres">September Massacres</a> that followed. Apart from about a hundred Swiss who escaped from the Tuileries, the only survivors of the regiment were a 300 strong detachment which, with the King's authorization, had been sent to Normandy to escort grain convoys a few days before August 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The Swiss officers were mostly amongst those massacred, although Major <a href="/wiki/Karl_Josef_von_Bachmann" title="Karl Josef von Bachmann">Karl Josef von Bachmann</a> — in command at the Tuileries —was formally tried and <a href="/wiki/Guillotine" title="Guillotine">guillotined</a> in September, still wearing his red uniform coat. Two surviving Swiss officers achieved senior rank under <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Memorial">Memorial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lion_Monument&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Memorial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The initiative to create the monument was taken by Karl Pfyffer von Altishofen, an officer of the Guards who had been on leave in Lucerne at that time of the fight. He began collecting money in 1818. The monument was designed by <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a> sculptor <a href="/wiki/Bertel_Thorvaldsen" title="Bertel Thorvaldsen">Bertel Thorvaldsen</a>, and finally hewn in 1820–21 by Lukas Ahorn, in a former <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> <a href="/wiki/Quarry" title="Quarry">quarry</a> near Lucerne. Carved into the cliff face, the monument measures ten meters in length and six meters in height.</p>
<p>The monument is dedicated <i>Helvetiorum Fidei ac Virtuti</i> ("To the loyalty and bravery of the Swiss"). The dying lion is portrayed impaled by a <a href="/wiki/Spear" title="Spear">spear</a>, covering a <a href="/wiki/Shield" title="Shield">shield</a> bearing the <a href="/wiki/Fleur-de-lis" title="Fleur-de-lis">fleur-de-lis</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_monarchy" title="French monarchy">French monarchy</a>; beside him is another shield bearing the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Switzerland" title="Coat of arms of Switzerland">coat of arms of Switzerland</a>. The inscription below the sculpture lists the names of the officers and gives the approximate numbers of soldiers who died (DCCLX = 760), and survived (CCCL = 350).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The monument is described by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Carlyle" title="Thomas Carlyle">Thomas Carlyle</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_French_Revolution:_A_History" title="The French Revolution: A History">The French Revolution: A History</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The pose of the lion was copied in 1894 by Thomas M. Brady (1849–1907)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> for his <i>Lion of Atlanta</i> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oakland_Cemetery_(Atlanta,_Georgia)#Confederate_section" title="Oakland Cemetery (Atlanta, Georgia)">Oakland Cemetery</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlanta,_Georgia" title="Atlanta, Georgia">Atlanta, Georgia</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mark_Twain_on_the_monument">Mark Twain on the monument</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lion_Monument&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mark Twain on the monument">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The Lion lies in his lair in the perpendicular face of a low cliff — for he is carved from the <a href="/wiki/Rock-cut_architecture" title="Rock-cut architecture">living rock</a> of the cliff. His size is colossal, his attitude is noble. His head is bowed, the broken spear is sticking in his shoulder, his protecting paw rests upon the lilies of France. Vines hang down the cliff and wave in the wind, and a clear stream trickles from above and empties into a pond at the base, and in the smooth surface of the pond the lion is mirrored, among the water-lilies.<br/>
<br/>
Around about are green trees and grass. The place is a sheltered, reposeful woodland nook, remote from noise and stir and confusion — and all this is fitting, for lions do die in such places, and not on granite pedestals in public squares fenced with fancy iron railings. The Lion of Lucerne would be impressive anywhere, but nowhere so impressive as where he is.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Mark Twain, <a href="/wiki/A_Tramp_Abroad" title="A Tramp Abroad">A Tramp Abroad</a>, 1880</cite></div>
</blockquote>
