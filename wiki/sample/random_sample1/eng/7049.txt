<p><b>William McGuire</b> "<b>Bill</b>" <b>Bryson</b> <span style="font-size:85%;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Officer_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Officer of the Order of the British Empire">OBE</a> <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">FRS</a></span> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/Ë/ primary stress follows">Ë</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/aÉª/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aÉª</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/Én/ 'on' in 'button'">Én</span></span>/</a></span></span>; born December 8, 1951) is a best-selling Anglo-American author of books on travel, the English language, science, and other non-fiction topics. Born in the United States, he has been a resident of Britain for most of his adult life, returning to the United States between 1995 and 2003. He served as the chancellor of <a href="/wiki/Durham_University" title="Durham University">Durham University</a> from 2005 to 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imdb_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-imdb-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bryson came to prominence in the United Kingdom with the publication of <i><a href="/wiki/Notes_from_a_Small_Island" title="Notes from a Small Island">Notes from a Small Island</a></i> (1995), an exploration of Britain, and its accompanying television series. He received widespread recognition again with the publication of <i><a href="/wiki/A_Short_History_of_Nearly_Everything" title="A Short History of Nearly Everything">A Short History of Nearly Everything</a></i> (2003), a book widely acclaimed for its accessible communication of science.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Bryson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bryson was born in <a href="/wiki/Des_Moines,_Iowa" title="Des Moines, Iowa">Des Moines, Iowa</a>, the son of Agnes Mary (nÃ©e McGuire) and sports journalist <a href="/wiki/Bill_Bryson_Sr." title="Bill Bryson Sr.">Bill Bryson Sr.</a></p>
<blockquote>
<p>"I come from Des Moines, Iowa. Somebody had to."<br/>

<p>His mother was of Irish descent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He had an older brother, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bryson" title="Michael Bryson">Michael</a> (1942â2012), and a sister, Mary Jane Elizabeth. In 2006 Bryson published <i><a href="/wiki/The_Life_and_Times_of_the_Thunderbolt_Kid" title="The Life and Times of the Thunderbolt Kid">The Life and Times of the Thunderbolt Kid</a></i>, a humorous account of his childhood years in Des Moines.</p>
<p>Bryson attended <a href="/wiki/Drake_University" title="Drake University">Drake University</a> for two years before dropping out in 1972, deciding instead to backpack around Europe for four months. He returned to Europe the following year with a high-school friend, Matt Angerer (the pseudonymous Stephen Katz).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Bryson wrote about some of his experiences from this trip in his book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neither_Here_Nor_There:_Travels_in_Europe" title="Neither Here Nor There: Travels in Europe">Neither Here Nor There: Travels in Europe</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Move_to_the_United_Kingdom">Move to the United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Bryson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Move to the United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Bryson first visited Britain in 1973 during his tour of Europe<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and decided to stay after landing a job working in a psychiatric hospital<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-belfasttelegraph.co.uk_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-belfasttelegraph.co.uk-8">[8]</a></sup>âthe now-defunct <a href="/wiki/Holloway_Sanatorium" title="Holloway Sanatorium">Holloway Sanatorium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Water" title="Virginia Water">Virginia Water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a>. He met a nurse there named Cynthia Billen, whom he married.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-belfasttelegraph.co.uk_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-belfasttelegraph.co.uk-8">[8]</a></sup> They moved to Bryson's hometown of Des Moines, Iowa in 1975 so that Bryson could complete his college degree at Drake University. In 1977 they settled in Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DesMoinesRegister2014_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-DesMoinesRegister2014-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>He worked as a <a href="/wiki/Journalist" title="Journalist">journalist</a>, first for the <i><a href="/wiki/Bournemouth_Daily_Echo" title="Bournemouth Daily Echo">Bournemouth Evening Echo</a></i> and eventually became chief copy editor of the business section of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> and then deputy national news editor of the business section of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i>. He left journalism in 1987, three years after the birth of his third child. Bryson started writing independently and in 1990 their fourth child, Samuel, was born.</p>
<p>He has moved around the UK and lived in <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Water" title="Virginia Water">Virginia Water</a> (Surrey), Purewell (Dorset), <a href="/wiki/Burton,_Dorset" title="Burton, Dorset">Burton</a> (Dorset), <a href="/wiki/Kirkby_Malham" title="Kirkby Malham">Kirkby Malham</a> (North Yorkshire, in the 1980s and '90s), and the Old Rectory in <a href="/wiki/Wramplingham" title="Wramplingham">Wramplingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a> (2003-2013).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> He currently<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (October 2016)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> lives in rural Hampshire and maintains a small flat in South Kensington, London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-belfasttelegraph.co.uk_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-belfasttelegraph.co.uk-8">[8]</a></sup> From 1995-2003 he lived in <a href="/wiki/Hanover,_New_Hampshire" title="Hanover, New Hampshire">Hanover, New Hampshire</a>, USA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although able to apply for <a href="/wiki/British_nationality_law" title="British nationality law">British citizenship</a>, Bryson said in 2010 that he had declined a citizenship test, declaring himself "too cowardly" to take it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> However, in 2014, he said that he was preparing to take it<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and in the prologue to his 2015 book <a href="/wiki/The_Road_to_Little_Dribbling:_More_Notes_From_a_Small_Island" title="The Road to Little Dribbling: More Notes From a Small Island">The Road to Little Dribbling: More Notes From a Small Island</a> he describes doing so, in Eastleigh. His citizenship award was in Winchester and he now holds dual nationality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-belfasttelegraph.co.uk_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-belfasttelegraph.co.uk-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writings">Writings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Bryson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Writings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While living in the US in the 1990s Bryson wrote a <a href="/wiki/Column_(periodical)" title="Column (periodical)">column</a> for a British newspaper for several years, reflecting on humorous aspects of his repatriation in the United States. These columns were selected and adapted to become his book <i><a href="/wiki/Notes_from_a_Big_Country" title="Notes from a Big Country">I'm a Stranger Here Myself</a></i>, alternatively titled <i>Notes from a Big Country</i> in Britain, Canada, and Australia. During his time in the United States, Bryson decided to walk the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Trail" title="Appalachian Trail">Appalachian Trail</a> with his friend Stephen Katz (a pseudonym), about which he wrote the book <i><a href="/wiki/A_Walk_in_the_Woods_(book)" title="A Walk in the Woods (book)">A Walk in the Woods</a></i>. In the 2015 film adaptation of <i><a href="/wiki/A_Walk_in_the_Woods_(film)" title="A Walk in the Woods (film)">A Walk in the Woods</a></i>, Bryson is portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Academy Award</a> winner <a href="/wiki/Robert_Redford" title="Robert Redford">Robert Redford</a> and Katz is portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Nick_Nolte" title="Nick Nolte">Nick Nolte</a> (Bryson is portrayed as being much older than he was at the time of his actual walk).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT1999_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT1999-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003, in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/World_Book_Day" title="World Book Day">World Book Day</a>, British voters chose Bryson's book <i><a href="/wiki/Notes_from_a_Small_Island" title="Notes from a Small Island">Notes from a Small Island</a></i> as that which best sums up British identity and the state of the nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In the same year, he was appointed a Commissioner for <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a>.</p>
<p>His <a href="/wiki/Popular_science" title="Popular science">popular science</a> book, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Short_History_of_Nearly_Everything" title="A Short History of Nearly Everything">A Short History of Nearly Everything</a></i> is 500 pages long and explores not only the histories and current statuses of the sciences, but also reveals their humble and often humorous beginnings. Although one "top scientist" is alleged to have jokingly described the book as "annoyingly free of mistakes",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chancellor_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-chancellor-16">[16]</a></sup> Bryson himself makes no such claim and a list of some reported errors in the book is available online.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2006, Bryson interviewed the then British prime minister, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a>, on the state of science and education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bryson has also written two popular works on the history of the English languageÂ â <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mother_Tongue" title="The Mother Tongue">The Mother Tongue</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Made_in_America_(book)" title="Made in America (book)">Made in America</a></i>Â â and, more recently, an update of his guide to usage, <i><a href="/wiki/Bryson%27s_Dictionary_of_Troublesome_Words" title="Bryson's Dictionary of Troublesome Words">Bryson's Dictionary of Troublesome Words</a></i> (published in its first edition as <i>The Penguin Dictionary of Troublesome Words</i> in 1983).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Litigation">Litigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Bryson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Litigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2012 Bryson sued his agent, Jed Mattes Inc., in New York County Supreme Court claiming they "failed to perform some of the most fundamental duties of an agent".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The case was settled out of court, with part of the settlement being that Bryson agree not to discuss it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards.2C_positions_and_honours">Awards, positions and honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Bryson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Awards, positions and honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bill_Bryson_Chancellor_crop.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="681" data-file-width="513" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Bill_Bryson_Chancellor_crop.JPG/170px-Bill_Bryson_Chancellor_crop.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Bill_Bryson_Chancellor_crop.JPG/255px-Bill_Bryson_Chancellor_crop.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Bill_Bryson_Chancellor_crop.JPG/340px-Bill_Bryson_Chancellor_crop.JPG 2x" width="170"/></a>

<p>In 2005 Bryson was appointed <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_(education)" title="Chancellor (education)">chancellor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Durham_University" title="Durham University">Durham University</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chancellor_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-chancellor-16">[16]</a></sup> succeeding the late <a href="/wiki/Peter_Ustinov" title="Peter Ustinov">Sir Peter Ustinov</a>, and became more active with student activities than is common for holders of that post, even appearing in a Durham student film and promoting <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Litter_pick&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Litter pick (page does not exist)">litter picks</a> in the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> He had praised Durham as "a perfect little city" in <i>Notes from a Small Island</i>. In October 2010, it was announced that Bryson would step down at the end of 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2007, he became the president of the <a href="/wiki/Campaign_to_Protect_Rural_England" title="Campaign to Protect Rural England">Campaign to Protect Rural England</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> His first area of focus in this role was the establishment of an anti-littering campaign across England. He discussed the future of the countryside with <a href="/wiki/Richard_Mabey" title="Richard Mabey">Richard Mabey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sue_Clifford" title="Sue Clifford">Sue Clifford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Crane" title="Nicholas Crane">Nicholas Crane</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Girling" title="Richard Girling">Richard Girling</a> at CPRE's Volunteer Conference in November 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bryson has received numerous awards for his ability to communicate science with passion and enthusiasm. In 2004, he won the prestigious <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_Prizes_for_Science_Books" title="Royal Society Prizes for Science Books">Aventis Prize</a> for best general science book that year, with <i><a href="/wiki/A_Short_History_of_Nearly_Everything" title="A Short History of Nearly Everything">A Short History of Nearly Everything</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-descartes_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-descartes-26">[26]</a></sup> In 2005, the book won the EU <a href="/wiki/Descartes_Prize" title="Descartes Prize">Descartes Prize</a> for science communication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-descartes_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-descartes-26">[26]</a></sup> In 2005 he received the President's Award from the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Chemistry" title="Royal Society of Chemistry">Royal Society of Chemistry</a> for advancing the cause of the chemical sciences. In 2007, he won the Bradford Washburn Award from the Museum of Science in Boston, MA for contributions to the popularization of science. In 2012, he received the Kenneth B. Myer Award from the <a href="/wiki/Florey_Institute_of_Neuroscience_and_Mental_Health" title="Florey Institute of Neuroscience and Mental Health">Florey Institute of Neuroscience</a> in Melbourne, Australia.</p>
<p>With the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Chemistry" title="Royal Society of Chemistry">Royal Society of Chemistry</a> the Bill Bryson prize for Science Communication was established in 2005. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The competition engages students from around the world in explaining science to non-experts.</p>
<p>He was awarded an honorary <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">OBE</a> for his contribution to literature on 13 December 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The following year, he was awarded the <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce_Award" title="James Joyce Award">James Joyce Award</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Literary_and_Historical_Society_(University_College_Dublin)" title="Literary and Historical Society (University College Dublin)">Literary and Historical Society</a> of <a href="/wiki/University_College_Dublin" title="University College Dublin">University College Dublin</a>. After he received <a href="/wiki/British_nationality_law" title="British nationality law">British Citizenship</a> his OBE was made substantive.</p>
<p>In 2011 he won the Golden Eagle Award from the Outdoor Writers and Photographers Guild.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> On 22 November 2012, Durham University officially renamed the <a href="/wiki/Durham_University_Library" title="Durham University Library">Main Library</a> the Bill Bryson Library for his contributions as the university's 11th chancellor (2005â11).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bryson was elected an Honorary <a href="/wiki/List_of_Fellows_of_the_Royal_Society_elected_in_2013" title="List of Fellows of the Royal Society elected in 2013">Fellow of the Royal Society (FRS) in 2013</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frs_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-frs-32">[32]</a></sup> becoming the first non-Briton upon whom this honour has been conferred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> His biography at the Society reads: "Bill Bryson is a popular author who is driven by a deep curiosity for the world we live in. Bill's books and lectures demonstrate an abiding love for science and an appreciation for its social importance. His international bestseller, <i>A Short History of Nearly Everything</i> (2003), is widely acclaimed for its accessible communication of science and has since been adapted for children."</p>
<p>In 2006 <a href="/wiki/Frank_Cownie" title="Frank Cownie">Frank Cownie</a>, the mayor of Des Moines, awarded Bryson the <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_the_City" title="Freedom of the City">key to the city</a> and announced that 21 October 2006 would be known as "Bill Bryson, The Thunderbolt Kid, Day".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2007, he was the Schwartz Visiting Fellow at the <a href="/wiki/Pomfret_School" title="Pomfret School">Pomfret School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
