<p><b>William Mervin "Billy" Mills</b>, also known as <i>Makata Taka Hela</i> (born June 30, 1938), is the second <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a> (after <a href="/wiki/Jim_Thorpe" title="Jim Thorpe">Jim Thorpe</a>) to win an <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic</a> <a href="/wiki/Gold_medal" title="Gold medal">gold medal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He accomplished this feat in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athletics_at_the_1964_Summer_Olympics_-_Men%27s_10000_metres" title="Athletics at the 1964 Summer Olympics - Men's 10000 metres">10,000 meter run</a> (6.2 mi) at the <a href="/wiki/1964_Summer_Olympics" title="1964 Summer Olympics">1964 Tokyo Olympics</a>, becoming the only person from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_hemisphere" title="Western hemisphere">Western hemisphere</a> to win the Olympic gold in this event. His 1964 victory is considered one of the greatest Olympic upsets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USMChisory_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-USMChisory-3">[3]</a></sup> A former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Marine" title="United States Marine">United States Marine</a>, Billy Mills is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Oglala_Lakota" title="Oglala Lakota">Oglala</a> <a href="/wiki/Lakota_people" title="Lakota people">Lakota</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sioux" title="Sioux">Sioux</a>) <a href="/wiki/Tribe" title="Tribe">Tribe</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Billy_Mills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>William Mervin Mills was born in <a href="/wiki/Pine_Ridge,_South_Dakota" title="Pine Ridge, South Dakota">Pine Ridge, South Dakota</a>, and was raised on the impoverished <a href="/wiki/Pine_Ridge_Indian_Reservation" title="Pine Ridge Indian Reservation">Pine Ridge Indian Reservation</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oglala_Sioux" title="Oglala Sioux">Oglala Sioux</a> people. His given native name, Makata Taka Hela, loosely means "love your country."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He was orphaned when he was twelve years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FT_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FT-5">[5]</a></sup> Mills took up running while attending the Haskell Institute, which is now known as <a href="/wiki/Haskell_Indian_Nations_University" title="Haskell Indian Nations University">Haskell Indian Nations University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lawrence,_Kansas" title="Lawrence, Kansas">Lawrence, Kansas</a>. Both a boxer and a runner in his youth, Mills gave up boxing to focus on running.</p>
<p>He attended the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kansas" title="University of Kansas">University of Kansas</a> on an athletic scholarship. He was named a <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">NCAA</a> <a href="/wiki/All-America" title="All-America">All-America</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cross-country_runner" title="Cross-country runner">cross-country runner</a> three times and in 1960 he won the individual title in the <a href="/wiki/Big_Eight_Conference" title="Big Eight Conference">Big Eight</a> cross-country championship. The University of Kansas track team won the 1959 and 1960 outdoor national championships while Mills was on the team.</p>
<p>After graduating with a degree in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_Education" title="Physical Education">Physical Education</a>, Mills entered the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">United States Marine Corps</a>. He was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_First_Lieutenant" title="US First Lieutenant">First Lieutenant</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_Forces_Reserve" title="Marine Forces Reserve">Marine Corps Reserve</a> when he competed in the 1964 Olympics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USMChisory_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-USMChisory-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1964_Olympics">1964 Olympics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Billy_Mills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1964 Olympics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BillyMills_Crossing_Finish_Line_1964Olympics.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="532" data-file-width="678" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/BillyMills_Crossing_Finish_Line_1964Olympics.jpg/200px-BillyMills_Crossing_Finish_Line_1964Olympics.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/BillyMills_Crossing_Finish_Line_1964Olympics.jpg/300px-BillyMills_Crossing_Finish_Line_1964Olympics.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/BillyMills_Crossing_Finish_Line_1964Olympics.jpg/400px-BillyMills_Crossing_Finish_Line_1964Olympics.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BillyMills_Crossing_Finish_Line_1964Olympics.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Billy Mills breaks the tape in the 10,000 m in the 1964 Olympics. <i>U.S. Marine Corps photo</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Mills qualified for the <a href="/wiki/1964_Summer_Olympics" title="1964 Summer Olympics">1964 Summer Olympics</a> on the U.S. Track and Field Team in the 10,000 meter and the <a href="/wiki/Marathon" title="Marathon">marathon</a>. The favorite in 1964 for the 10,000 m was <a href="/wiki/Ron_Clarke" title="Ron Clarke">Ron Clarke</a> of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, who held the world record. The runners expected to challenge him were defending champion <a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Bolotnikov" title="Pyotr Bolotnikov">Pyotr Bolotnikov</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Murray_Halberg" title="Murray Halberg">Murray Halberg</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, who had won the 5,000 m in <a href="/wiki/1960_Summer_Olympics" title="1960 Summer Olympics">1960</a>.</p>
<p>Mills was a virtual unknown. He had finished second in the U.S. Olympic trials. His time in the preliminaries was a full minute slower than Clarke's. Clarke set the tone of the race. His tactic of surging every other lap appeared to be working. Halfway through the race, only four runners were still with Clarke: <a href="/wiki/Mohammed_Gammoudi" title="Mohammed Gammoudi">Mohammed Gammoudi</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mamo_Wolde" title="Mamo Wolde">Mamo Wolde</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kokichi_Tsuburaya" title="Kokichi Tsuburaya">Kokichi Tsuburaya</a> of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, and Mills. Tsuburaya, the local favorite, lost contact first, then Wolde. With two laps to go, only two runners were still with Clarke. On paper, it seemed to be Clarke's race. He had run a world record time of 28:15.6, while neither Gammoudi nor Mills had ever run under 29 minutes.</p>
<p>Mills and Clarke were running together with Gammoudi right behind as they entered the final lap. They were lapping other runners and, down the backstretch, Clarke was boxed in. He pushed Mills once, then again. Then Gammoudi pushed them both and surged into the lead as they rounded the final curve. Clarke recovered and began chasing Gammoudi while Mills appeared to be too far back to be in contention. Clarke failed to catch Gammoudi, but Mills pulled out to lane 4 and sprinted past them both. His winning time of 28:24.4 was almost 50 seconds faster than he had run before and set a new Olympic record for the event. No American had ever before won the 10,000 m, nor has any other American come seriously close until <a href="/wiki/Galen_Rupp" title="Galen Rupp">Galen Rupp</a> took the silver in the 2012 London Olympics.</p>
<p>American television viewers were able to hear the surprise and drama as <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> expert analyst Dick Bank<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> screamed "Look at Mills! Look at Mills!" over the more sedate play-by-play announcer <a href="/wiki/Bud_Palmer" title="Bud Palmer">Bud Palmer</a>, who seemed to miss what was unfolding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> For bringing that drama to the coverage, Bank was fired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the race, Mills talked with Clarke and asked if he was straining as hard as he could on the final straightaway to the finish, to which Clarke replied, "Yes." Mills has stated that he tried to be relaxed during his final kick to the finish line and felt that helped him pass both Gammoudi and Clarke. Both Clarke and Mills ran the marathon at the 1964 Olympics after the 10,000 m event. Clarke finished in 9th place, and Mills finished in 14th, in a respectable 2:22:55.4, approximately two-and-a-half minutes behind Clarke and about 10 minutes behind winner <a href="/wiki/Abebe_Bikila" title="Abebe Bikila">Abebe Bikila</a>.</p>

<p>Mills later set U.S. records for 10,000 m (28:17.6) and the three-mile run, and had a 5,000 m best of 13:41.4. In 1965, he and Gerry Lindgren both broke the <a href="/wiki/World_record" title="World record">world record</a> for the six-mile run when they finished in a tie at the <a href="/wiki/Amateur_Athletic_Union" title="Amateur Athletic Union">AAU</a> <a href="/wiki/USA_Outdoor_Track_and_Field_Championships" title="USA Outdoor Track and Field Championships">National Championships</a>, running 27:11.6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-running_career">Post-running career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Billy_Mills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post-running career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mills is the co-founder of the nonprofit group <a href="/wiki/Running_Strong_for_American_Indian_Youth" title="Running Strong for American Indian Youth">Running Strong for American Indian Youth</a> with Eugene Krizek. The aim of Running Strong is to help American Indian people fulfill their basic needs -food, water, and shelter- while also helping their communities gain self-sufficiency and self-esteem. He now acts as a spokesperson for the group and travels the country speaking out for and on behalf of Native American communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Mills charity work also includes diabetes prevention and management education for adults and especially for youth. Being a diabetes patient himself Mills helps people with diabetes learn how to maintain a healthy lifestyle and improve their lives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_and_honors">Legacy and honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Billy_Mills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy and honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>In 1976, Mills was inducted into the United States <a href="/wiki/National_Track_and_Field_Hall_of_Fame" title="National Track and Field Hall of Fame">National Track and Field Hall of Fame</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 1984, he was one of a select group of former American Olympians given the honor of carrying the Olympic flag into the Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum at the opening ceremony of the Games of the XXIII Olympiad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 1984, he was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Olympic_Hall_of_Fame" title="United States Olympic Hall of Fame">U.S. Olympic Hall of Fame</a>.</li>
<li>He has also been inducted into the <a href="/wiki/National_Distance_Running_Hall_of_Fame" title="National Distance Running Hall of Fame">National Distance Running Hall of Fame</a>, the Kansas Hall of Fame, the South Dakota Hall of Fame, the San Diego Hall of Fame, and the National High School Hall of Fame.</li>
<li>Mills is the subject of the 1983 movie <i><a href="/wiki/Running_Brave" title="Running Brave">Running Brave</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FT_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-FT-5">[5]</a></sup> in which he is portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Robby_Benson" title="Robby Benson">Robby Benson</a>.</li>
<li>Mills has produced a book, <i>Lessons of a Lakota</i>, made speaking tours, and sponsored some events. He lives near Sacramento, California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FT_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-FT-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> awarded Mills the 2012 <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Citizens_Medal" title="Presidential Citizens Medal">Presidential Citizens Medal</a>, for his work with the Running Strong for American Indian Youth organization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2014, Mills was awarded the NCAA's highest honor, the <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt_Award" title="Theodore Roosevelt Award">Theodore Roosevelt Award</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2014, the Virginia State Senate passed a joint resolution commending Mills on the 50th Anniversary of his Olympic win.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Anti-Defamation_League" title="Anti-Defamation League">Anti-Defamation League</a> honored Mills as a 2014 ADL In Concert Against Hate Honoree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2015, The <a href="/wiki/President%27s_Council_on_Fitness,_Sports,_and_Nutrition" title="President's Council on Fitness, Sports, and Nutrition">President's Council on Fitness, Sports, and Nutrition</a> awarded Mills the President's Council Lifetime Achievement Award, which is given each year to up to 5 individuals "whose careers have greatly contributed to the advancement or promotion of physical activity, fitness, sports, or nutrition nationwide." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>Mills was one of several athletes featured on the August 18, 2016 <a href="/wiki/On_Being" title="On Being">On Being</a> program "Running as a Spiritual Practice." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
