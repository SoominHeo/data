<p><b>Billie Joe Armstrong</b> (born February 17, 1972)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17_February_2016_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-17_February_2016-1">[1]</a></sup> is an American musician, singer, songwriter and actor who is best known as the lead vocalist, primary songwriter, and guitarist of the <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Green_Day" title="Green Day">Green Day</a>, which he co-founded with <a href="/wiki/Mike_Dirnt" title="Mike Dirnt">Mike Dirnt</a>. He is also a guitarist and vocalist for the punk rock band <a href="/wiki/Pinhead_Gunpowder" title="Pinhead Gunpowder">Pinhead Gunpowder</a> and provides lead vocals for Green Day's side projects <a href="/wiki/Foxboro_Hot_Tubs" title="Foxboro Hot Tubs">Foxboro Hot Tubs</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Network" title="The Network">The Network</a>.</p>
<p>Raised in <a href="/wiki/Rodeo,_California" title="Rodeo, California">Rodeo, California</a>, Armstrong developed an interest in music at a young age, and recorded his first song at the age of five. He met Mike Dirnt while attending elementary school, and the two instantly bonded over their mutual interest in music, forming the band Sweet Children when the two were 15 years old. The band changed its name to Green Day, and would later achieve massive commercial success. Armstrong has also pursued musical projects outside of Green Day's work, including numerous collaborations with other musicians.</p>
<p>In 1997, to coincide with the release of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nimrod_(Green_Day_album)" title="Nimrod (Green Day album)">Nimrod</a></i>, Armstrong founded <a href="/wiki/Adeline_Records" title="Adeline Records">Adeline Records</a> in Oakland to help support other bands releasing music and signed bands such as <a href="/wiki/The_Frustrators" title="The Frustrators">The Frustrators</a>, <a href="/wiki/AFI_(band)" title="AFI (band)">AFI</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dillinger_Four" title="Dillinger Four">Dillinger Four</a>. The record company has since come under the management of Pat Magnarella.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span></h2>
<p>Billie Joe Armstrong was born in <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland, California</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and raised in nearby <a href="/wiki/Rodeo,_California" title="Rodeo, California">Rodeo</a>, the youngest of six children of Andrew "Andy" Armstrong and Ollie Jackson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RS_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-RS-4">[4]</a></sup> His father worked as a jazz musician and truck driver for <a href="/wiki/Safeway_Inc." title="Safeway Inc.">Safeway Inc.</a> to support his family. He died of <a href="/wiki/Esophageal_cancer" title="Esophageal cancer">esophageal cancer</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RS_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-RS-4">[4]</a></sup> on September 10, 1982, when Armstrong was 10. The song "<a href="/wiki/Wake_Me_Up_When_September_Ends" title="Wake Me Up When September Ends">Wake Me Up When September Ends</a>" is a memorial to his father. He has five older siblings: David, Alan, Marci, Hollie, and Anna. His mother worked at Rod's Hickory Pit<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RS_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-RS-4">[4]</a></sup> restaurant in <a href="/wiki/El_Cerrito,_California" title="El Cerrito, California">El Cerrito</a>, where Armstrong and Dirnt played their first gig in 1987. Armstrong is of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scotch-Irish_American" title="Scotch-Irish American">Scotch-Irish</a>, <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people" title="Scottish people">Scottish</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_people" title="Italian people">Italian</a> (Sicilian), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_people" title="Spanish people">Spanish</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_people" title="German people">German</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Welsh_people" title="Welsh people">Welsh</a> descent.</p>
<p>Armstrong's interest in music started at a young age. He attended Hillcrest Elementary School in Rodeo, where a teacher encouraged him to record a song titled "Look for Love" at the age of five on the Bay Area label Fiat Records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RS_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-RS-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> After his father died, his mother married a man whom her children disliked, which resulted in Armstrong's further retreat into music.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>At the age of 10, Armstrong met <a href="/wiki/Mike_Dirnt" title="Mike Dirnt">Mike Dirnt</a> in the school cafeteria and they immediately bonded over their love of music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RS_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-RS-4">[4]</a></sup> He became interested in <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk rock</a> after being introduced to punk rock by his brothers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Armstrong has also cited <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a>-based bands <a href="/wiki/The_Replacements_(band)" title="The Replacements (band)">The Replacements</a> and <a href="/wiki/H%C3%BCsker_D%C3%BC" title="Hüsker Dü">Hüsker Dü</a> as major musical influences. Armstrong and <a href="/wiki/Mike_Dirnt" title="Mike Dirnt">Mike Dirnt</a>'s first live performance under the name Green Day was in <a href="/wiki/Davis,_California" title="Davis, California">Davis</a>, a town approximately an hour's drive northeast of the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay" title="San Francisco Bay">San Francisco Bay</a> area.</p>
<p>Along with Hillcrest Elementary, Armstrong attended Carquinez Middle School and <a href="/wiki/John_Swett_High_School" title="John Swett High School">John Swett High School</a>, both in <a href="/wiki/Crockett,_California" title="Crockett, California">Crockett</a>, and later transferred to <a href="/wiki/Pinole_Valley_High_School" title="Pinole Valley High School">Pinole Valley High School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pinole,_California" title="Pinole, California">Pinole</a>. On his 18th birthday (February 17, 1990), he dropped out to pursue his musical career.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Normal_180914_1994-XX-XX_-_Green_Day_Live.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="326" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Normal_180914_1994-XX-XX_-_Green_Day_Live.jpg/170px-Normal_180914_1994-XX-XX_-_Green_Day_Live.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Normal_180914_1994-XX-XX_-_Green_Day_Live.jpg/255px-Normal_180914_1994-XX-XX_-_Green_Day_Live.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4f/Normal_180914_1994-XX-XX_-_Green_Day_Live.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Normal_180914_1994-XX-XX_-_Green_Day_Live.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Armstrong performing in 1994</div>

<p>In 1986, aged 14, Armstrong formed a band called Sweet Children with his childhood friend <a href="/wiki/Mike_Dirnt" title="Mike Dirnt">Mike Dirnt</a>. In the beginning, Dirnt and Armstrong both played guitar, with <a href="/wiki/John_Kiffmeyer" title="John Kiffmeyer">John Kiffmeyer</a>, also known as Al Sobrante, on drums,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metropolis_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-metropolis-7">[7]</a></sup> and Sean Hughes on bass. After a few performances, Hughes left the band in 1988; Dirnt then began playing bass and they became a three-piece band. They changed their name to Green Day in April 1989, choosing the name because of their fondness for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marijuana" title="Marijuana">marijuana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1989, Armstrong provided lead guitar and backing vocals on three songs for <a href="/wiki/The_Lookouts" title="The Lookouts">The Lookouts</a>' final EP <i><a href="/wiki/IV_(The_Lookouts_album)" title="IV (The Lookouts album)">IV</a></i>.</p>
<p>That same year, Green Day released their debut EP <i><a href="/wiki/1,000_Hours" title="1,000 Hours">1,000 Hours</a></i> through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lookout!_Records" title="Lookout! Records">Lookout! Records</a>. They recorded their debut studio album <i><a href="/wiki/39/Smooth" title="39/Smooth">39/Smooth</a></i> and the extended play <i><a href="/wiki/Slappy" title="Slappy">Slappy</a></i> in 1990, which were later combined with <i>1,000 Hours</i> into the compilation <i><a href="/wiki/1,039/Smoothed_Out_Slappy_Hours" title="1,039/Smoothed Out Slappy Hours">1,039/Smoothed Out Slappy Hours</a></i> in 1991.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%A9_Cool" title="Tré Cool">Tré Cool</a> became Green Day's drummer in late 1990 after Sobrante left in order to go to college. Cool made his debut on Green Day's second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Kerplunk_(album)" title="Kerplunk (album)">Kerplunk</a></i> (1991).</p>
<p>In 1993, Armstrong played live several times with California punk band <a href="/wiki/Rancid_(band)" title="Rancid (band)">Rancid</a>. Rancid lead singer, <a href="/wiki/Tim_Armstrong" title="Tim Armstrong">Tim Armstrong</a>, asked Billie Joe Armstrong to join his band, but he refused owing to the progress with Green Day. However, Billie Joe Armstrong was credited as a co-writer on Rancid's 1993 song, <a href="/wiki/Radio_Radio_Radio" title="Radio Radio Radio">Radio</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dookie_and_mainstream_success"><i>Dookie</i> and mainstream success</span></h3>
<p>With their next album, <i><a href="/wiki/Dookie" title="Dookie">Dookie</a></i> (1994), the band broke through into the mainstream, and have remained one of the most popular rock bands of the 1990s and 2000s with over 60 million records sold worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000-present:_continued_success.2C_collaborations_and_musical_theater">2000-present: continued success, collaborations and musical theater</span></h3>
<p>Armstrong has collaborated with many artists. He co-wrote <a href="/wiki/The_Go-Go%27s" title="The Go-Go's">The Go-Go's</a> 2001 song "<a href="/wiki/Unforgiven_(The_Go-Go%27s_song)" title="Unforgiven (The Go-Go's song)">Unforgiven</a>". He has also co-written songs with <a href="/wiki/Penelope_Houston" title="Penelope Houston">Penelope Houston</a> ("The Angel and The Jerk" and "New Day"), and sung backing vocals with <a href="/wiki/Melissa_Auf_der_Maur" title="Melissa Auf der Maur">Melissa Auf der Maur</a> on <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Adams" title="Ryan Adams">Ryan Adams</a>' "Do Miss America" (where they acted as the backing band for <a href="/wiki/Iggy_Pop" title="Iggy Pop">Iggy Pop</a> on his <i><a href="/wiki/Skull_Ring" title="Skull Ring">Skull Ring</a></i> album ("Private Hell" and "Supermarket")). Armstrong produced an album for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Riverdales" title="The Riverdales">The Riverdales</a>. He was part of the side project <a href="/wiki/The_Network" title="The Network">The Network</a>, which existed from 2003 to 2005 and released the album <i><a href="/wiki/Money_Money_2020" title="Money Money 2020">Money Money 2020</a></i>.</p>
<p>Hoping to clear his head and develop new ideas for songs, Armstrong traveled to New York City alone for a few weeks, renting a small apartment in the <a href="/wiki/East_Village,_Manhattan" title="East Village, Manhattan">East Village</a> of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spitz150_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spitz150-10">[10]</a></sup> He spent much of this time taking long walks and participating in jam sessions in the basement of Hi-Fi, a bar in Manhattan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spitz151_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spitz151-11">[11]</a></sup> However, the friends he made during this time drank too much for his liking, which was the catalyst for Armstrong's return to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bay_Area" title="Bay Area">Bay Area</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spitz151_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spitz151-11">[11]</a></sup> After returning home, Armstrong was arrested on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DUI" title="DUI">DUI</a> charges on January 5, 2003, and released on $1,200 bail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spitz151_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Spitz151-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2004, Green Day debuted <i><a href="/wiki/American_Idiot" title="American Idiot">American Idiot</a></i>, their first <a href="/wiki/Rock_opera" title="Rock opera">rock opera</a>. The album has sold more than 15,000,000 copies worldwide, fueled by the hit singles "<a href="/wiki/American_Idiot_(song)" title="American Idiot (song)">American Idiot</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Boulevard_of_Broken_Dreams_(Green_Day_song)" title="Boulevard of Broken Dreams (Green Day song)">Boulevard of Broken Dreams</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Wake_Me_Up_When_September_Ends" title="Wake Me Up When September Ends">Wake Me Up When September Ends</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 2009 Green Day released <i><a href="/wiki/21st_Century_Breakdown" title="21st Century Breakdown">21st Century Breakdown</a></i>, the band's second rock opera, which was another commercial success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009, <i>American Idiot</i> was turned into a musical on Broadway, also called <i><a href="/wiki/American_Idiot_(musical)" title="American Idiot (musical)">American Idiot</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The musical won two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tony_Awards" title="Tony Awards">Tony Awards</a>. Armstrong appeared in <i>American Idiot</i> in the role of St. Jimmy twice, in late 2010<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and early 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, Armstrong appeared on Season 3 of NBC's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Voice_(U.S.)" title="The Voice (U.S.)">The Voice</a></i> as an assistant mentor for <a href="/wiki/Christina_Aguilera" title="Christina Aguilera">Christina Aguilera</a>'s team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Armstrong wrote songs for <i>These Paper Bullets</i>, a rock musical adaptation of <i><a href="/wiki/Much_Ado_About_Nothing" title="Much Ado About Nothing">Much Ado About Nothing</a></i>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yale_Repertory_Theater" title="Yale Repertory Theater">Yale Repertory Theater</a> premiered the show in March 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Armstrong also collaborated with the <a href="/wiki/Comedy_hip_hop" title="Comedy hip hop">comedy hip hop</a> group <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lonely_Island" title="Lonely Island">Lonely Island</a> in their song "I Run NY" from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wack_Album" title="The Wack Album">The Wack Album</a></i> released on June 7, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lonely_Island.27s_The_Wack_Album_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lonely_Island.27s_The_Wack_Album-20">[20]</a></sup> He starred alongside <a href="/wiki/Leighton_Meester" title="Leighton Meester">Leighton Meester</a> in the 2014 film <i><a href="/wiki/Like_Sunday,_Like_Rain" title="Like Sunday, Like Rain">Like Sunday, Like Rain</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, Armstrong and singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Norah_Jones" title="Norah Jones">Norah Jones</a> released the album <i><a href="/wiki/Foreverly" title="Foreverly">Foreverly</a></i>, consisting of covers of songs from <a href="/wiki/The_Everly_Brothers" title="The Everly Brothers">The Everly Brothers</a>' album <i><a href="/wiki/Songs_Our_Daddy_Taught_Us" title="Songs Our Daddy Taught Us">Songs Our Daddy Taught Us</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stereogum_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-stereogum-22">[22]</a></sup> The first single from the album, "Long Time Gone", was released on October 23.</p>
<p>In 2014 Armstrong joined The Replacements for a number of shows beginning on April 19 at Coachella. Frontman Paul Westerberg had been suffering with back problems and spent the majority of the gig lying on a sofa while Armstrong helped play his parts. Westerberg referred to Billie Joe as an "expansion of the band".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Armstrong joined The Replacements on stage again at the Shaky Knees music festival in Atlanta in May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2014 Armstrong moved with his son Joseph to New York<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and began working on another acting role, in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Ordinary_World_(film)" title="Ordinary World (film)">Ordinary World</a></i>. It will be Armstrong's first lead acting role, and centers on the mid-life crisis of a husband and father who attempts to revisit his punk past. The film will include new songs written and performed by Armstrong.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Instruments">Instruments</span></h2>

<p>Armstrong's first guitar was a Cherry Red Hohner acoustic, which his father bought for him. He then received his first electric guitar, a <a href="/wiki/Fernandes_Guitars" title="Fernandes Guitars">Fernandes Stratocaster</a> that he named "Blue" when he was eleven. His mother got "Blue" from <a href="/wiki/George_Cole_(musician)" title="George Cole (musician)">George Cole</a> who taught Armstrong electric guitar for 10 years. Armstrong says in a 1995 <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a> interview, "Basically, it wasn't like guitar lessons because I never really learned how to read music. So he just taught me how to put my hands on the thing." Cole bought the guitar new from David Margen of the band <a href="/wiki/Santana_(band)" title="Santana (band)">Santana</a>. Cole gave Armstrong a Bill Lawrence Humbucking pickup and told him to install the pickup in the bridge position. After the pickup was destroyed at <a href="/wiki/Woodstock_%2794" title="Woodstock '94">Woodstock '94</a>, Armstrong then used a Duncan JB model. "Armstrong fetishized his teacher's guitar, partly because the blue instrument had a sound quality and <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Van_Halen" title="Eddie Van Halen">Van Halen</a>-worthy fluidity he couldn't get from his little red Hohner. He prized it mostly, however, because of his relationship with Cole, another father figure after the death of Andy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> He toured with this guitar from the band's early days and still uses it to this day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> "Blue" also appears in a number of its music videos such as "<a href="/wiki/Longview_(song)" title="Longview (song)">Longview</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Basket_Case_(song)" title="Basket Case (song)">Basket Case</a>", "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brain_Stew/Jaded" title="Brain Stew/Jaded">Brain Stew/Jaded</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Hitchin%27_a_Ride_(Green_Day_song)" title="Hitchin' a Ride (Green Day song)">Hitchin' a Ride</a>", and most recently in "<a href="/wiki/Minority_(Green_Day_song)" title="Minority (Green Day song)">Minority</a>".</p>

<p>Today, Armstrong mainly uses <a href="/wiki/Gibson_Guitar_Corporation" title="Gibson Guitar Corporation">Gibson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fender_Musical_Instruments_Corporation" title="Fender Musical Instruments Corporation">Fender</a> guitars. Twenty of his Gibson guitars are <a href="/wiki/Gibson_Les_Paul_Junior" title="Gibson Les Paul Junior">Les Paul Junior</a> models from the mid- to late-1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> His Fender collection includes: <a href="/wiki/Fender_Stratocaster" title="Fender Stratocaster">Stratocaster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fender_Jazzmaster" title="Fender Jazzmaster">Jazzmaster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fender_Telecaster" title="Fender Telecaster">Telecaster</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Gretsch" title="Gretsch">Gretsch</a> hollowbody and his copies of "Blue". He states that his favorite guitar is a 1956 Gibson Les Paul Junior he calls "Floyd". He bought this guitar in 2000 just before recording their album <i><a href="/wiki/Warning_(Green_Day_album)" title="Warning (Green Day album)">Warning</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Armstrong also has two of his own <a href="/wiki/Gibson_Les_Paul_Junior" title="Gibson Les Paul Junior">Les Paul Junior</a> signature models from Gibson. The first has been in production since 2006 and is modeled closely after "Floyd", Armstrong's original 1956 Les Paul Junior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The second began production in 2012 and is a TV Yellow double-cutaway Junior. Both models include a Gibson "H-90" pickup, exclusive to Armstrong's models. Gibson has also released an extremely limited run of acoustic signature guitars.</p>
<p>He plays several other instruments as well. He recorded harmonica and <a href="/wiki/Mandolin" title="Mandolin">mandolin</a> parts in the past (<i><a href="/wiki/Nimrod_(album)" title="Nimrod (album)">Nimrod</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Warning_(Green_Day_album)" title="Warning (Green Day album)">Warning</a></i>), piano parts on <i><a href="/wiki/21st_Century_Breakdown" title="21st Century Breakdown">21st Century Breakdown</a></i> (2009), <i><a href="/wiki/American_Idiot:_The_Original_Broadway_Cast_Recording" title="American Idiot: The Original Broadway Cast Recording">American Idiot: The Original Broadway Cast Recording</a></i> (2011) and <i><a href="/wiki/%C2%A1Tr%C3%A9!" title="¡Tré!">¡Tré!</a></i> (2012), and plays drums live from time to time.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span></h2>
<p>Armstrong met his first serious girlfriend, Erica Paleno (also known as Arica Pelino), inside <a href="/wiki/924_Gilman_Street" title="924 Gilman Street">the Gilman music club</a> on his sixteenth birthday. Erica became known as the "first official Green Day fan" as she listened to the first four-track recordings by Billie and Sean, encouraging the band, touring with them and acting as occasional photographer for the band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Erica inspired many of Green Day's well known songs including Christie Road which was written for her by Armstrong about the local tracks where the couple would sneak out to and meet at.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> When Armstrong began living in punk houses and warehouses at the age of seventeen, including the warehouse above a West Oakland brothel which ultimately inspired the song "Welcome to Paradise"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spitz.2C_M_2006.2C_p48_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spitz.2C_M_2006.2C_p48-37">[37]</a></sup> Erica would often stay with him despite the dangers, "I would stay with him sometimes in these warehouses full of crusty punks".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spitz.2C_M_2006.2C_p48_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spitz.2C_M_2006.2C_p48-37">[37]</a></sup> The couple split in late 1991. Erica is the sister of former Green Day touring member (2004-2005) Mike Pelino and sister-in-law of Janna White, wife of Green Day guitarist Jason White.</p>
<p>Shortly after his split with Erica, Armstrong met a woman he has identified only as "Amanda" at <a href="/wiki/924_Gilman_Street" title="924 Gilman Street">Gilman</a>, and they started dating. Amanda produced and distributed her own zine and was an iron-willed feminist, which enthralled Armstrong.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Amanda, however, was unimpressed with Armstrong and though the couple dated for some time she ultimately dumped him in 1993<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and joined the <a href="/wiki/Peace_Corps" title="Peace Corps">Peace Corps</a>. Armstrong has written many songs about Amanda, both during their relationship and afterwards, including "She", "Good Riddance", "Stuart And The Ave", "Sassafrass Roots", "Amanda", "She's A Rebel", "Extraordinary Girl" and "Whatsername". The hero, "Whatsername", on the album <i>American Idiot</i>, and in the <i>American Idiot</i> musical, is based on Amanda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1990, Armstrong met Adrienne Nesser (<a href="/wiki/Steve_Nesser" title="Steve Nesser">Steve Nesser</a>'s sister) at one of Green Day's early performances in Minneapolis, Minnesota. They married on July 2, 1994; the day after their wedding, Adrienne discovered that she was pregnant. Armstrong's son, Joseph Marciano Armstrong, who was born on February 28, 1995, plays drums in a <a href="/wiki/Berkeley,_California" title="Berkeley, California">Berkeley</a>-based band named <a href="/wiki/SWMRS" title="SWMRS">SWMRS</a>. Billie Joe's second son Jakob Danger (born September 12, 1998) is a guitarist and singer/songwriter who released his first material online in 2015. In a February 2014 interview with <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> Armstrong described how his sudden marriage and having a son was "mental"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingstone.com_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone.com-41">[41]</a></sup> saying, "I was very impulsive at that time. I think that impulsive behavior was meant to counteract the chaos in my life."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingstone.com_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone.com-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sexuality">Sexuality</span></h3>
<p>Armstrong has identified himself as <a href="/wiki/Bisexuality" title="Bisexuality">bisexual</a>, saying in a 1995 interview with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Advocate" title="The Advocate">The Advocate</a></i>, "I think I've always been bisexual. I mean, it's something that I've always been interested in. I think people are born bisexual, and it's just that our parents and society kind of veer us off into this feeling of 'Oh, I can't.' They say it's taboo. It's ingrained in our heads that it's bad, when it's not bad at all. It's a very beautiful thing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> In a later interview for <i><a href="/wiki/Out_(magazine)" title="Out (magazine)">Out</a></i> magazine's April 2010 issue, Armstrong stated: "There were a lot of people who didn't accept it, who were <a href="/wiki/Homophobia" title="Homophobia">homophobic</a>." Armstrong continued, saying, "The fact that it's an issue is kind of phobic within itself. At some point, you gotta think, this should be something that's just accepted." Armstrong added: "I don't really classify myself as anything. And when it comes to sex, there are parts of me that are very shy and conservative."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In February 2014, Armstrong again discussed his bisexuality in a <i>Rolling Stone</i> article about <i>Dookie</i>, a record which Armstrong described as "touch[ing] on bisexuality a lot."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingstone.com_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone.com-41">[41]</a></sup> Armstrong discussed the song "Coming Clean" stating, "It was a song about questioning myself. There are these other feelings you may have about the same sex, the opposite sex, especially being in Berkeley and San Francisco then. People are acting out what they're feeling: gay, bisexual, transgender, whatever. And that opens up something in society that becomes more acceptable. Now we have gay marriage becoming recognized... I think it's a process of discovery. I was willing to try anything." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingstone.com_41-3"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone.com-41">[41]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_views">Political views</span></h3>
<p>Armstrong supported <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> during the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2008" title="United States presidential election, 2008">2008</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2012" title="United States presidential election, 2012">2012 presidential elections</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2016" title="United States presidential election, 2016">2016 presidential election</a>, Armstrong supported <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Sanders" title="Bernie Sanders">Bernie Sanders</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_ventures">Business ventures</span></h3>
<p>In 1997, Armstrong co-founded <a href="/wiki/Adeline_Records" title="Adeline Records">Adeline Records</a>, a rock and punk rock record label that is now managed by Pat Magnarella, Green Day's manager.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adeline_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Adeline-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2015, Armstrong opened <i>Broken Guitars</i>, a guitar shop in Oakland, California with fellow <a href="/wiki/Pinhead_Gunpowder" title="Pinhead Gunpowder">Pinhead Gunpowder</a> member and longtime <a href="/wiki/Green_Day" title="Green Day">Green Day</a> associate, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Schneider_(musician)" title="Bill Schneider (musician)">Bill Schneider</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2015, Armstrong along with <a href="/wiki/Mike_Dirnt" title="Mike Dirnt">Mike Dirnt</a>, launched a coffee company, <i><a href="/wiki/Oakland_Coffee" title="Oakland Coffee">Oakland Coffee Works</a></i>. The company sells organic coffee beans and is said to be the first company to use mass-produced compostable bags and pods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Incidents">Incidents</span></h3>
<p>On September 21, 2012, during a Green Day performance at <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_Valley" title="Las Vegas Valley">Las Vegas</a>' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IHeartRadio_music_festival" title="IHeartRadio music festival">IHeartRadio music festival</a>, Armstrong became agitated onstage and stopped the band's set midway through their performance of the 1994 hit song "<a href="/wiki/Basket_Case_(song)" title="Basket Case (song)">Basket Case</a>". In an expletive-filled rant, Armstrong criticized the event's promoters for allegedly cutting short the band's performance before smashing his guitar and storming off stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The band later issued a statement apologizing for the incident and clarifying that their set had not actually been cut short.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The incident occurred just four days prior to the release of Green Day's ninth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/%C2%A1Uno!" title="¡Uno!">¡Uno!</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 23, 2012, two days following the incident at the iHeartRadio Music Festival, Green Day announced that Armstrong was seeking treatment for an unspecified substance abuse problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Billie_Joe_Armstrong_enters_treatment_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Billie_Joe_Armstrong_enters_treatment-54">[54]</a></sup> As a result, scheduled appearances on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jimmy_Kimmel_Live" title="Jimmy Kimmel Live">Jimmy Kimmel Live</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ellen_DeGeneres_Show" title="The Ellen DeGeneres Show">The Ellen DeGeneres Show</a></i> were canceled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> According to Claudia Suarez Wright, Tre Cool's ex-wife and the mother of Armstrong's <a href="/wiki/Godparent" title="Godparent">godson</a>, Armstrong had been drinking heavily in Las Vegas prior to the iHeartMusic Festival, following approximately one year of sobriety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Armstrong gave an interview to <i>Rolling Stone</i> in March 2013 in which he admitted, "I've been trying to get sober since 1997, right around Nimrod".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rs2013_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-rs2013-58">[58]</a></sup> Armstrong discussed how during the 21st Century Breakdown tour in 2009–2010, "There were meltdowns on that tour that were huge".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rs2013_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-rs2013-58">[58]</a></sup> Armstrong detailed his addiction and in particular how it had escalated in the months prior to the release of the Trilogy albums and the performance at iHeart stating that during the summer while touring in Europe, "I was at my pill-taking height at that time, medicating the shit out of myself".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-59">[59]</a></sup> Armstrong gave details of a gig at Irving Plaza in New York just over a week before the iHeart incident he "Threw back four or five beers before we went on and probably had four or five when we played. Then I drank my body weight in alcohol after that. I ended up hungover on the West Side Highway, laying in a little park."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-59">[59]</a></sup> Green Day subsequently canceled all remaining concert dates for 2012 and early 2013 as Armstrong continued dealing with his personal problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> In late December, the band announced they would return to touring at the end of March.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Armstrong later admitted that the substances he had been abusing were alcohol and prescription pills for anxiety and insomnia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
