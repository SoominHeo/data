<p><b>Panzerkampfwagen VIII Maus</b> ("Mouse") was a <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> <a href="/wiki/Super-heavy_tank" title="Super-heavy tank">super-heavy tank</a> completed in late 1944. It is the heaviest fully enclosed <a href="/wiki/Armoured_fighting_vehicle" title="Armoured fighting vehicle">armoured fighting vehicle</a> ever built. Five were ordered, but only two hulls and one turret were completed before the testing grounds were captured by the advancing Soviet forces.</p>
<p>These two prototypes – one with, and the other without a turret – underwent trials in late 1944. The complete vehicle was 10.2 metres (33 ft 6 in) long, 3.71 metres (12 ft 2 in) wide and 3.63 metres (11.9 ft) high. Weighing 188 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_tons" title="Metric tons">metric tons</a>, the Maus's main armament was the <a href="/wiki/Krupp" title="Krupp">Krupp</a>-designed 128 mm KwK 44 L/55 gun, based on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/12.8_cm_Pak_44" title="12.8 cm Pak 44">12.8 cm Pak 44</a> anti-tank field artillery piece also used in the <a href="/wiki/Casemate" title="Casemate">casemate</a>-type <i><a href="/wiki/Jagdtiger" title="Jagdtiger">Jagdtiger</a></i> tank destroyer, with a coaxial 75 mm KwK 44 L/36.5 gun. The 128 mm gun was powerful enough to destroy all <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied</a> armoured fighting vehicles then in service, some at ranges exceeding 3,500 metres (3,800 yd).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Achtung_Panzer.2C_Maus_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Achtung_Panzer.2C_Maus-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The principal problem in the design of the <i>Maus</i> was developing an engine and drivetrain which was powerful enough to propel the tank, yet small enough to fit inside it — as it was meant to use the same sort of "hybrid drive", using an internal-combustion engine to operate an <a href="/wiki/Electric_generator" title="Electric generator">electric generator</a> to power its tracks with electric motor units, much as its <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Porsche" title="Ferdinand Porsche">Ferdinand Porsche</a>-designed predecessors, the <a href="/wiki/VK_3001_(P)" title="VK 3001 (P)">VK 3001 (P)</a>, <a href="/wiki/VK_4501_(P)" title="VK 4501 (P)">VK 4501 (P)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Elefant" title="Elefant">Elefant</a> had. The drive train was electrical, designed to provide a maximum speed of 20 kilometres per hour (12 mph) and a minimum speed of 1.5 kilometres per hour (0.93 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Panzer_Tracts_6-3_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Panzer_Tracts_6-3-3">[3]</a></sup> However, during actual field testing, the maximum speed achieved on hard surfaces was 13 kilometres per hour (8.1 mph) with full motor field, and by weakening the motor field to a minimum, a top speed of 22 kilometres per hour (14 mph) was achieved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-German_tanks_of_WW2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-German_tanks_of_WW2-4">[4]</a></sup> The vehicle's weight made it unable to utilize most bridges, instead it was intended to <a href="/wiki/Ford_(crossing)" title="Ford (crossing)">ford</a> to a depth of 2 metres (6 ft 7 in) or submerge up to a depth of 8 metres (26 ft 3 in) and use a <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_snorkel" title="Vehicle snorkel">snorkel</a> to cross rivers.</p>
<p>The Maus was intended to punch holes through enemy defences in the manner of an immense "breakthrough tank", whilst taking almost no damage to any components.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panzer_VIII_Maus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The basic design known as the <i>VK100.01</i> / <i>Porsche Type 205</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyc._German_WW2.2C_Maus_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Encyc._German_WW2.2C_Maus-5">[5]</a></sup> was suggested by Ferdinand Porsche to <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> in June 1942, who subsequently approved it. Work on the design began in earnest; the first prototype, to be ready in 1943 was initially to receive the name <i>Mammut</i> (<a href="/wiki/Mammoth" title="Mammoth">Mammoth</a>). This was reportedly changed to <i>Mäuschen</i> (Little Mouse) in December 1942 and finally to <i>Maus</i> (Mouse) in February 1943, which became the most common name for this tank.</p>
<p>The <i>Maus</i> was designed from the start to use the <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_vehicle_drivetrain#Series_hybrid" title="Hybrid vehicle drivetrain">"electric transmission"</a> design which Ferdinand Porsche had used in the <a href="/wiki/VK_4501_(P)" title="VK 4501 (P)">VK 4501 (P)</a>, his unsuccessful attempt to win the production contract for the <a href="/wiki/Tiger_I" title="Tiger I">Tiger</a>. The initial powerplant was the Daimler-Benz MB 509 gasoline engine, an adaptation of Germany's largest displacement (at 44.5 litres/2,717 in³) inverted V12 aircraft engine, the <a href="/wiki/Daimler-Benz_DB_603" title="Daimler-Benz DB 603">Daimler-Benz DB 603</a>, and later changed to the Daimler-Benz MB 517 diesel engine. This drove an electrical generator, and their combined length occupied the central/rear two-thirds of the Maus' hull, cutting off the forward driver's compartment in the hull from direct access to the turret from within the tank. Each 1.1 metre-wide track, which used the same basic "contact shoe" and "connector link" design format as the Henschel-built <a href="/wiki/Tiger_II" title="Tiger II">Tiger II</a>, was driven by its own electric motor mounted within the upper rear area of each hull side. Each set of tracks had a suspension design containing a total of 24 road wheels per side, in six bogie sets, staggered to be spread over the entire width of the track.</p>
<p>Due to the return "run" of the uniquely wide tracks used being completely enclosed within the fixed outer side armor panels that defined its overall hull width, with the inner vertical lengthwise walls of the hull used to mount the suspension components, a narrow lengthwise "tub" remained between the hull's inner armored walls, under and to the rear of the turret to house the engine and generator of the tank's powertrain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The armour was substantial: the hull front was 220 millimetres (8.7 in) thick, the sides and rear of the hull were up to 190 millimetres (7.5 in). The turret armour was even thicker, the turret front was up to 240 millimetres (9.4 in)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyc._German_WW2.2C_Maus_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Encyc._German_WW2.2C_Maus-5">[5]</a></sup> and the sides and rear 200 millimetres (7.9 in). The <a href="/wiki/Gun_mantlet" title="Gun mantlet">gun mantlet</a> was 250 millimetres (9.8 in), and combined with the turret armour behind, the protection level at that section was even higher.</p>
<p>The initial plan for the <i>Maus</i> was for the prototype to have been completed by mid-1943, with monthly production scheduled to run at ten vehicles per month after delivery of the prototype. The work on the <i>Maus</i> would be divided between <a href="/wiki/Krupp" title="Krupp">Krupp</a>, responsible for the chassis, armament and turret and <a href="/wiki/Alkett" title="Alkett">Alkett</a>, who would be responsible for final assembly.</p>
<p>The <i>Maus</i> tank was originally designed to weigh approximately 100 tons and be armed with a 128 mm main gun and a 75 mm co-axial secondary gun. Additional armament options were studied including various versions of 128 mm, 150 mm, and 170 mm guns. In January 1943 Hitler himself insisted that the armament be a 128 mm main gun with a coaxial 75 mm gun. The 128 mm PaK 44 anti-tank field artillery piece of 1943 that Krupp adapted for arming the <i>Maus</i> as the <i>Kampfwagenkanone (KwK) 44</i> retained, in parallel to the Porsche project, its original anti-tank <i>Panzerabwehrkanone</i> family designation of PaK 44 when mounted in the <a href="/wiki/Casemate" title="Casemate">casemate</a>-style <a href="/wiki/Jagdtiger" title="Jagdtiger">Jagdtiger</a> tank destroyer.</p>
<p>By May 1943, a wooden mockup of the final <i>Maus</i> configuration was ready and presented to Hitler, who approved it for mass production, ordering a first series of 150. At this point, the estimated weight of the <i>Maus</i> was 188 tons. However, there is a story that concerns the main armament of the <i>Maus</i> being changed by Hitler who said that the 128 mm gun looked like a 'toy gun' when compared to the tank, causing the 128 mm to be replaced by a 150 mm gun.</p>
<p>In his book <a href="/wiki/Panzer_Leader_(book)" title="Panzer Leader (book)"><i>Panzer Leader</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/Heinz_Guderian" title="Heinz Guderian">Heinz Guderian</a> wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>On 1 May a wooden model of the "Maus", a tank project of <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Porsche" title="Ferdinand Porsche">Porsche</a> and <a href="/wiki/Krupp" title="Krupp">Krupp</a>, was shown to Hitler. It was intended to mount a 150 mm gun. The total weight of the tank was supposed to reach 175 tons. It should be considered that after the design changes on Hitler's instructions the tank will weigh 200 tons. The model didn't have a single machine gun for close combat, and for this reason I had to reject it. It had the same design flaw that made the <a href="/wiki/Elefant" title="Elefant">Elefant</a> unsuitable for close combat. In the end, the tank will inevitably have to wage a close combat since it operates in cooperation with the infantry. An intense debate started, and except for me, all of the present found the "Maus" magnificent. It was promising to be exactly that, a "giant".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>This lack of close combat armament was later addressed with the addition of a <a href="/wiki/Nahverteidigungswaffe" title="Nahverteidigungswaffe">nahverteidigungswaffe</a> mounted in the turret roof, a 7.92 mm (0.31 in) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MG_34_machine_gun" title="MG 34 machine gun">MG 34 machine gun</a> with 1,000 rounds mounted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coaxial_weapon" title="Coaxial weapon">coaxially</a> with the main weapons in the turret, and three pistol ports for submachine guns in the sides and rear of the turret. Future planned modifications included provisions for a <a href="/wiki/MG_151_cannon#History_.28MG_151.2F20.29" title="MG 151 cannon">MG 151/20 cannon</a> for anti-aircraft defense mounted in the turret roof.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Panzer_Tracts_6-3-23_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Panzer_Tracts_6-3-23-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="V1">V1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panzer_VIII_Maus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: V1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first, turretless prototype (V1) was assembled by Alkett in December 1943. Tests started the same month, with a mockup turret fitted of the same weight as the real turret.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyc._German_WW2.2C_Maus_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Encyc._German_WW2.2C_Maus-5">[5]</a></sup> In June 1944 the production turret, with armament, was used for tests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyc._German_WW2.2C_Maus_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Encyc._German_WW2.2C_Maus-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Maus</i> was too heavy to cross bridges. As a result, an alternative system was developed, where the <i>Maus</i> would instead ford the rivers it needed to cross. Due to its size it could ford relatively deep streams, but for deeper ones it was to submerge and drive across the river bottom. The solution required tanks to be paired up. One <i>Maus</i> would supply electrical power to the crossing vehicle via a cable until it reached the other side. The crew would receive air through a large <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_snorkel" title="Vehicle snorkel">snorkel</a>, which was long enough for the tank to go 7.9 m (26 ft) under water.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="V2">V2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panzer_VIII_Maus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: V2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In March<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> 1944 the second prototype, the V2, was delivered. It differed in many details from the V1 prototype. In mid-1944, the V2 prototype was fitted with a powerplant and the first produced <i>Maus</i> turret. This turret was fitted with a 128 mm KwK 44 L/55 gun, a coaxial 75 mm KwK 44 L/36.5 gun and a coaxial 7.92 mm MG 34. The V1 prototype was supposed to be fitted with the second produced turret, but this never happened.</p>
<p>By July 1944, Krupp was in the process of producing four more <i>Maus</i> hulls, but they were ordered to halt production and scrap these. Krupp stopped all work on it in August 1944. Meanwhile, the V2 prototype started tests in September 1944, fitted with a Daimler-Benz MB 517 diesel engine,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyc._German_WW2.2C_Maus_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Encyc._German_WW2.2C_Maus-5">[5]</a></sup> new electric steering system and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skoda_Works" title="Skoda Works">Skoda Works</a> designed running gear and tracks.</p>
<p>There was also a special railroad carriage made for transporting the <i>Maus</i> prototypes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Capture">Capture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panzer_VIII_Maus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Capture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The working <i>Maus</i> prototypes remained at <a href="/wiki/Kummersdorf" title="Kummersdorf">Kummersdorf</a> after being tested at <a href="/wiki/B%C3%B6blingen" title="Böblingen">Böblingen</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Maus V2 was ordered to Wünsdorf to protect the OKH, probably V1 was ordered there, too, as support for the V2 if it drove into mud or to help with diving through rivers (where it would have served as generator unit for V2). V2 ended at the Hindenburgplatz, in front of the bunker Maybach I, where it was destroyed by placing charges in the engine and fighting compartments. Because it had ammunition stowed under the turret, it was damaged more extensively than V1, with the turret being more or less intact. Maus V1 did not reach this area.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>After the war, the Soviet Commander of Armored and Mechanized troops ordered the hull of V1 to be mated with the turret of V2. The Soviets used six German FAMO-built <a href="/wiki/Sd.Kfz._9" title="Sd.Kfz. 9">18t German half-tracks</a>, the largest half-track vehicles that Germany built in the war years, to pull the 55 ton turret off the destroyed hull. The combined V1 hull/V2 turret vehicle was completed in Germany and sent back to the USSR for further testing.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It arrived there on 4 May 1946. When further testing was completed the vehicle was taken over by the <a href="/wiki/Kubinka_Tank_Museum" title="Kubinka Tank Museum">Kubinka Tank Museum</a> for storage where it is now on display.</p>
