<p><b>Wilfrid Voynich</b>, born <b>Michał Wojnicz</b> (<a href="/wiki/Tel%C5%A1iai" title="Telšiai">Telsze</a>, 12 November [<a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">O.S.</a> 31 October] 1865<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enc_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Enc-1">[1]</a></sup> – <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>, 19 March 1930), was a <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Polish</a> revolutionary, antiquarian and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bibliophile" title="Bibliophile">bibliophile</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Eponym" title="Eponym">eponym</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Voynich_manuscript" title="Voynich manuscript">Voynich manuscript</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wilfrid_Voynich&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wilfrid Michał Habdank-Wojnicz was born in Telsze (since 1918 <a href="/wiki/Tel%C5%A1iai" title="Telšiai">Telšiai</a>—a town in then <a href="/wiki/Kovno_Governorate" title="Kovno Governorate">Kovno Governorate</a>, which was part of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a> now it's <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a>)—into a <a href="/wiki/Lithuanians" title="Lithuanians">Lithuanian</a> - <a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">Polish</a> noble family. The "<a href="/wiki/Abdank_coat_of_arms" title="Abdank coat of arms">Habdank</a>" part of his surname is the name of a Polish heraldic clan.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> He was the son of a Polish petty official (<a href="/wiki/Table_of_Ranks" title="Table of Ranks">titular counsellor</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enc_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Enc-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>He attended a <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(school)" title="Gymnasium (school)"><i>gimnazjum</i></a> in <a href="/wiki/Suwa%C5%82ki" title="Suwałki">Suwałki</a> (a town in northeastern Poland), then studied at the universities of Warsaw, St. Petersburg, and Moscow. He graduated from Moscow University in chemistry and became a licensed pharmacist.</p>
<p>In 1885, in <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a>, Wojnicz joined <a href="/wiki/Ludwik_Wary%C5%84ski" title="Ludwik Waryński">Ludwik Waryński</a>'s revolutionary organization, <i><a href="/wiki/Proletariat_(party)#First_Proletariat" title="Proletariat (party)">Proletariat</a></i>. In 1886, after a failed attempt to free fellow-conspirators Piotr Bardowski (1846-1886) and Stanisław Kunicki (1861-1886), who had both been sentenced to death, from the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Citadel" title="Warsaw Citadel">Warsaw Citadel</a>, he was arrested by the Russian police. In 1887, he was sent to <a href="/wiki/Katorga" title="Katorga">penal servitude</a> at <a href="/wiki/Tunka" title="Tunka">Tunka</a>.</p>
<p>In 1890 he escaped from <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> and got to <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a> and, returning to Europe, eventually went from <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a> to <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>.</p>
<p>Under the assumed name of Ivan Kel'chevskii, he helped <a href="/wiki/Sergey_Stepnyak-Kravchinsky" title="Sergey Stepnyak-Kravchinsky">Stepniak</a>, a fellow revolutionary, to found the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Friends_of_Russian_Freedom" title="Society of Friends of Russian Freedom">Society of Friends of Russian Freedom</a> in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After Stepniak's death in a railway crossing accident in 1895, Voynich ceased revolutionary activity. In 1898 he opened a bookshop in London. In 1902 he married a fellow former revolutionary, <a href="/wiki/Ethel_Lilian_Voynich" title="Ethel Lilian Voynich">Ethel Lilian Boole</a>, daughter of the British mathematician, <a href="/wiki/George_Boole" title="George Boole">George Boole</a>. Voynich was naturalised a British subject on 25 April 1904, taking the legal name Wilfrid Michael Voynich.</p>
<p>Voynich opened another bookshop in 1914 in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>. He became deeply involved in the antiquarian book trade, and wrote a number of catalogues and other texts on the subject.</p>
<p>Voynich died in New York in 1930.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Voynich_manuscript">Voynich manuscript</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wilfrid_Voynich&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Voynich manuscript">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Voynich_manuscript" title="Voynich manuscript">Voynich manuscript</a></div>
<p>The most famous of Voynich's possessions was a mysterious <a href="/wiki/Manuscript" title="Manuscript">manuscript</a> he said he acquired in 1912 at the <a href="/wiki/Villa_Mondragone" title="Villa Mondragone">Villa Mondragone</a> in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, but first presented in public in 1915. He owned the manuscript until his death. It is written in an unknown script.</p>
