<p><b>Bing</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" title="Web search engine">web search engine</a> owned and operated by <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>. The service has its origins in Microsoft's previous search engines: <b>MSN Search</b>, <b>Windows Live Search</b> and later <b>Live Search</b>. Bing provides a variety of search services, including web, video, image and map search products. It uses the <a href="/wiki/ASP.NET" title="ASP.NET">ASP.NET</a> <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming language</a> and follows the design principles of Microsoft's <a href="/wiki/Metro_(design_language)" title="Metro (design language)">"Metro"</a> <a href="/wiki/Design_language" title="Design language">design language</a>.</p>
<p>Bing, Microsoft's replacement for Live Search, was unveiled by Microsoft CEO <a href="/wiki/Steve_Ballmer" title="Steve Ballmer">Steve Ballmer</a> on May 28, 2009, at the <i><a href="/wiki/All_Things_Digital" title="All Things Digital">All Things Digital</a></i> conference in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a>, California, for release on June 1, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Notable new features at the time included the listing of search suggestions while queries are entered and a list of related searches (called "Explore pane") based on <a href="/wiki/Semantic_technology" title="Semantic technology">semantic technology</a> from <a href="/wiki/Powerset_(company)" title="Powerset (company)">Powerset</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> which Microsoft had acquired in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2009, Microsoft and <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a> announced a deal in which Bing would power <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Search" title="Yahoo! Search">Yahoo! Search</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> All Yahoo! Search global customers and partners made the transition by early 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YahooHelp_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-YahooHelp-7">[7]</a></sup> The deal was altered in 2015, meaning Yahoo! was only required to use Bing for a "majority" of searches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2011, Microsoft stated that they were working on new back-end search infrastructure with the goal of delivering faster and slightly more relevant search results for users. Known as "Tiger", the new index-serving technology had been incorporated into Bing globally since August that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In May 2012, Microsoft announced another redesign of its search engine that includes "Sidebar", a social feature that searches users' social networks for information relevant to the search query.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldman_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goldman-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of November 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bing_(search_engine)&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Bing is the second largest search engine in the US with a query volume of 20.9%, behind <a href="/wiki/Google_Search" title="Google Search">Google</a> on 63.9%. <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Search" title="Yahoo! Search">Yahoo! Search</a>, which Bing largely powers, has 12.5%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>Microsoft originally launched <a href="/wiki/MSN" title="MSN">MSN</a> Search in the third quarter of 1998, using search results from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inktomi_(company)" title="Inktomi (company)">Inktomi</a>. It consisted of a search engine, index, and <a href="/wiki/Web_crawler" title="Web crawler">web crawler</a>. In early 1999, MSN Search launched a version which displayed listings from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Looksmart" title="Looksmart">Looksmart</a> blended with results from Inktomi except for a short time in 1999 when results from <a href="/wiki/AltaVista" title="AltaVista">AltaVista</a> were used instead. Since then Microsoft upgraded MSN Search to provide its own self-built search engine results, the index of which was updated weekly and sometimes daily. The upgrade started as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_testing" title="Beta testing">beta</a> program in November 2004, and came out of beta in February 2005. Image search was powered by a third party, <a href="/wiki/Picsearch" title="Picsearch">Picsearch</a>. The service also started providing its search results to other search engine portals in an effort to better compete in the market.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Windows_Live_Search">Windows Live Search</span></h3>

<div class="thumbcaption">

Windows Live Search homepage</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first public beta of Windows Live Search was unveiled on March 8, 2006, with the final release on September 11, 2006 replacing MSN Search. The new search engine used search tabs that include Web, news, images, music, desktop, local, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microsoft_Encarta" title="Microsoft Encarta">Microsoft Encarta</a>.</p>
<p>In the roll-over from MSN Search to Windows Live Search, Microsoft stopped using Picsearch as their image search provider and started performing their own image search, fueled by their own internal image search algorithms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Live_Search">Live Search</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Live_Search_New.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="398" data-file-width="680" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/23/Live_Search_New.png/220px-Live_Search_New.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/23/Live_Search_New.png/330px-Live_Search_New.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/23/Live_Search_New.png/440px-Live_Search_New.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Live_Search_New.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Live Search homepage, which would help to create the Bing homepage later on</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On March 21, 2007, Microsoft announced that it would separate its search developments from the <a href="/wiki/Windows_Live" title="Windows Live">Windows Live</a> services family, rebranding the service as Live Search. Live Search was integrated into the <i>Live Search and Ad Platform</i> headed by <a href="/wiki/Satya_Nadella" title="Satya Nadella">Satya Nadella</a>, part of Microsoft's Platform and Systems division. As part of this change, Live Search was merged with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microsoft_adCenter" title="Microsoft adCenter">Microsoft adCenter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>A series of reorganisations and consolidations of Microsoft's search offerings were made under the Live Search branding. On May 23, 2008, Microsoft announced the discontinuation of <a href="/wiki/Live_Search_Books" title="Live Search Books">Live Search Books</a> and <a href="/wiki/Live_Search_Academic" title="Live Search Academic">Live Search Academic</a> and integrated all academic and book search results into regular search, and as a result this also included the closure of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live_Search_Books_Publisher_Program" title="Live Search Books Publisher Program">Live Search Books Publisher Program</a>. Soon after, <a href="/wiki/Windows_Live_Expo" title="Windows Live Expo">Windows Live Expo</a> was discontinued on July 31, 2008. Live Search Macros, a service for users to create their own custom search engines or use macros created by other users, was also discontinued shortly after. On May 15, 2009, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live_Product_Upload" title="Live Product Upload">Live Product Upload</a>, a service which allowed merchants to upload products information onto <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live_Search_Products" title="Live Search Products">Live Search Products</a>, was discontinued. The final reorganisation came as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live_Search_QnA" title="Live Search QnA">Live Search QnA</a> was rebranded as <a href="/wiki/MSN_QnA" title="MSN QnA">MSN QnA</a> on February 18, 2009, however, it was subsequently discontinued on May 21, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>Microsoft recognised that there would be a problem with branding as long as the word "Live" remained in the name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> As an effort to create a new identity for Microsoft's search services, Live Search was officially replaced by Bing on June 3, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Bing name was chosen through focus groups, and Microsoft decided that the name was memorable, short, easy to spell, and that it would function well as a URL around the world. The word would remind people of the sound made during "the moment of discovery and decision making."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Microsoft was assisted by branding consultancy <a href="/wiki/Interbrand" title="Interbrand">Interbrand</a> in their search for the best name for the new search engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The name also has strong similarity to the word '<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bingo" title="wikt:bingo">bingo</a>', which is used to mean that something sought has been found or realized, as is interjected when winning the game <a href="/wiki/Bingo_(U.S.)" title="Bingo (U.S.)">Bingo</a>. Microsoft advertising strategist David Webster originally proposed the name "Bang" for the same reasons the name Bing was ultimately chosen (easy to spell, one syllable, and easy to remember). He noted, "It's there, it's an exclamation point [...] It's the opposite of a question mark." This name was ultimately not chosen because it could not be properly used as a verb in the context of an internet search; Webster commented "Oh, 'I banged it' is very different than 'I binged it'".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the <i>Guardian</i> "[Microsoft] hasn't confirmed that it stands recursively for Bing Is Not Google, but that's the sort of joke software engineers enjoy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Qi Lu, president of Microsoft Online Services, also announced that Bing's official Chinese name is <i>bì yìng</i> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans">必应</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant">必應</span>), which literally means "very certain to respond" or "very certain to answer" in Chinese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>While being tested internally by Microsoft employees, Bing's codename was <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/en:kumo#Japanese" title="wikt:en:kumo">Kumo</a></i> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/en:%E3%81%8F%E3%82%82#Japanese" title="wikt:en:くも">くも</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> which came from the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> word for <i><a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spider</a></i> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/en:%E8%9C%98%E8%9B%9B#Japanese" title="wikt:en:蜘蛛">蜘蛛</a>; <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/en:%E3%81%8F%E3%82%82#Japanese" title="wikt:en:くも">くも</a>, <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/en:kumo#Japanese" title="wikt:en:kumo">kumo</a></i>) as well as <i><a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a></i> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/en:%E9%9B%B2#Japanese" title="wikt:en:雲">雲</a>; <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/en:%E3%81%8F%E3%82%82#Japanese" title="wikt:en:くも">くも</a>, <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/en:kumo#Japanese" title="wikt:en:kumo">kumo</a></i>), referring to the manner in which search engines "<a href="/wiki/Web_crawler" title="Web crawler">spider</a>" Internet resources to add them to their database, as well as <a href="/wiki/Cloud_computing" title="Cloud computing">cloud computing</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_challenges">Legal challenges</span></h3>
<p>On July 31, 2009, The Laptop Company, Inc. stated in a press release that it would challenge Bing's trademark application, alleging that Bing may cause confusion in the marketplace as Bing and their product BongoBing both do online product search.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Software company TeraByte Unlimited, which has a product called BootIt Next Generation (abbreviated to BING), also contended the trademark application on similar grounds, as did a Missouri-based design company called Bing! Information Design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Microsoft contended that claims challenging its trademark were without merit because these companies filed for U.S. federal trademark applications only after Microsoft filed for the Bing trademark in March 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yahoo.21_search_deal">Yahoo! search deal</span></h3>
<p>On July 29, 2009, Microsoft and Yahoo! announced that they had made a ten-year deal in which the Yahoo! search engine would be replaced by Bing, retaining the Yahoo! <a href="/wiki/User_interface" title="User interface">user interface</a>. Yahoo! will get to keep 88% of the revenue from all search ad sales on its site for the first five years of the deal, and have the right to sell advertising on some Microsoft sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> All Yahoo! Search global customers and partners made the transition by early 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YahooHelp_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-YahooHelp-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<li>Spell check – Will change frequently misspelled search terms to the more commonly spelled alternative.</li>
<li>Best match (plus similar sites)</li>
<li>Product shopping and "Bing cashback"</li>
<li>Health information</li>
<li>Flight tracking</li>
<li>Translate – Auto translation of certain search phrases, often with phrases including "translate" or "in English". <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Third-party_integration">Third-party integration</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> users have the option to share their searches with their Facebook friends using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Facebook_Connect" title="Facebook Connect">Facebook Connect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liedtke_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Liedtke-32">[32]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Integration_with_Windows_8">Integration with Windows 8</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Windows_8.1" title="Windows 8.1">Windows 8.1</a> includes Bing "Smart Search" integration, which processes all queries submitted through the Windows Start Screen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The Bing integration captures a variety of features, one of the most prominent and advertised: Hero Search. This feature allows users to browse for popular and well-known places, objects or people. Searching France, for example, will show popular search items, such as population, calling code and date founded. The current weather and location are also directly accessible using Bing Weather and Bing maps. The "Hero" result will go further to provide attractions using Bing Images and popular websites relating to France, such as France Wikipedia and France's official website. Searching an artist will display similar results with the option to play music using the Windows 8-integrated <a href="/wiki/Groove_Music" title="Groove Music">Groove Music</a> application.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Translator">Translator</span></h3>
<p>Bing Translator is a user facing translation portal provided by Microsoft to translate texts or entire web pages into different languages. All translation pairs are powered by the <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Translator" title="Microsoft Translator">Microsoft Translator</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Statistical_machine_translation" title="Statistical machine translation">statistical machine translation</a> platform and web service, developed by <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Research" title="Microsoft Research">Microsoft Research</a>, as its backend translation software. Two transliteration pairs (between <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese (Simplified)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese (Traditional)</a>) are provided by Microsoft's Windows International team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> As of January 2017, Bing Translator offers translations in 60 different language systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bing Translator can translate phrases entered by the user or acquire a link to a web page and translate its entirely. When translating an entire web page, or when the user selects "Translate this page" in Bing search results, the Bilingual Viewer is shown, which allows users to browse the original web page text and translation in parallel, supported by synchronized highlights, scrolling, and navigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-About_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-About-37">[37]</a></sup> Four Bilingual Viewer layouts are available: side by side, top and bottom, original with hover translation and translation with hover original.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mobile_services">Mobile services</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bing_Mobile" title="Bing Mobile">Bing Mobile</a> allow users to conduct search queries on their mobile devices, either via the mobile browser or a downloadable mobile application.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Developer_services">Developer services</span></h3>
<p>Bing Application Programming Interface enables developers to programmatically submit queries and retrieve results from the Bing Engine. <a class="external free" href="http://www.bing.com/developers" rel="nofollow">http://www.bing.com/developers</a></p>
<p>To use the Bing API developers have to obtain an Application ID, <a class="external free" href="http://www.bing.com/developers/createapp.aspx" rel="nofollow">http://www.bing.com/developers/createapp.aspx</a></p>
<p>Bing API can be used with following protocols:</p>
<ul>
<li>XML</li>
<li>JSON</li>
<li>SOAP</li>
</ul>
<p>Query examples:</p>
<ul>
<li>http://api.bing.net/xml.aspx?AppId=YOUR_APPID&amp;Version=2.2&amp;Market=en-US&amp;Query=YOUR_QUERY&amp;Sources=web+spell&amp;Web.Count=1</li>
<li>http://api.bing.net/json.aspx?AppId=YOUR_APPID&amp;Version=2.2&amp;Market=en-US&amp;Query=YOUR_QUERY&amp;Sources=web+spell&amp;Web.Count=1</li>
<li>http://api.bing.net/search.wsdl?AppID=YourAppId&amp;Version=2.2</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Software">Software</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toolbars">Toolbars</span></h3>
<p>The Bing Bar, a <a href="/wiki/Browser_extension" title="Browser extension">browser extension</a> <a href="/wiki/Toolbar" title="Toolbar">toolbar</a> that replaced the MSN Toolbar, provides users with links to Bing and MSN content from within their web browser without needing to navigate away from a web page they are already on. The user can customize the theme and color scheme of the Bing Bar as well as choose which MSN content buttons to present within the user interface. Bing Bar also displays the current local weather forecast and stock market positions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Bing Bar features integration with Microsoft Bing search engine. In addition to the traditional web search functions, Bing Bar also allows search on other Bing services such as Images, Video, News and Maps. When users perform a search on another search engine, the Bing Bar's search box will automatically populate itself, allowing the user to view the results from Bing, should it be desired.</p>
<p>Bing Bar also links to <a href="/wiki/Outlook.com" title="Outlook.com">Outlook.com</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skype" title="Skype">Skype</a> and <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Market_share">Market share</span></h2>
<p>Before the launch of Bing, the marketshare of Microsoft web search pages (MSN and Live search) had been small. By January 2011, Experian Hitwise show that Bing's market share had increased to 12.8% at the expense of Yahoo! and <a href="/wiki/Google_Search" title="Google Search">Google</a>. Bing powered searches also continued to have a higher "success rate" compared to Google, with more users clicking on the resulting links.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="the link to hitwise is not valid (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the same period, comScore’s "2010 U.S. Digital Year in Review" report showed that "Bing was the big gainer in year-over-year search activity, picking up 29% more searches in 2010 than it did in 2009".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" title="Wall Street Journal">Wall Street Journal</a></i> notes the 1% jump in share "appeared to come at the expense of rival Google Inc".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> In February 2011, Bing beat Yahoo! for the first time with 4.37% search share while Yahoo! received 3.93%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-post_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-post-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Counting core searches only, i.e., those where the user has an intent to interact with the search result, Bing had a market share of 14.54% in the second quarter of 2011 in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liedtke_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Liedtke-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>The combined "Bing Powered" U.S. searches have declined from 26.5% in 2011 to 25.9% in April 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> By November 2015, its market share had declined further to 20.9%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing_and_advertisements">Marketing and advertisements</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Live_Search_2">Live Search</span></h3>
<p>Since 2006, Microsoft had conducted a number of tie-ins and promotions for promoting Microsoft's search offerings. These include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon</a>'s <a href="/wiki/A9.com" title="A9.com">A9</a> search service and the experimental <a href="/wiki/Ms._Dewey" title="Ms. Dewey">Ms. Dewey</a> interactive search site syndicated all search results from Microsoft's then search engine, Live Search. This tie-in started on May 1, 2006.</li>
<li>Search and Give - a promotional website launched on January 17, 2007 where all searches done from a special portal site would lead to a donation to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNHCR" title="UNHCR">UNHCR</a>'s organization for refugee children, ninemillion.org. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reuters_AlertNet" title="Reuters AlertNet">Reuters AlertNet</a> reported in 2007 that the amount to be donated would be $0.01 per search, with a minimum of $100,000 and a maximum of $250,000 (equivalent to 25 million searches).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> According to the website the service was decommissioned on June 1, 2009, having donated over $500,000 to charity and schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Club_Bing" title="Club Bing">Club Bing</a> - a promotional website where users can win prizes by playing word games that generate search queries on Microsoft's then search service Live Search. This website began in April 2007 as Live Search Club.</li>
<li>Big Snap Search - a promotional website similar to Live Search Club. This website began in February 2008, but was discontinued shortly after.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></li>
<li>Live Search SearchPerks! - a promotional website which allowed users to redeem tickets for prizes while using Microsoft's search engine. This website began on October 1, 2008 and was decommissioned on April 15, 2009.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Debut">Debut</span></h3>
<p>Bing's debut featured an $80 to $100 million online, TV, print, and radio advertising campaign in the US. The advertisements do not mention other search engine competitors, such as Google and Yahoo!, directly by name; rather, they attempt to convince users to switch to Bing by focusing on Bing's search features and functionality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> The ads claim that Bing does a better job countering "search overload".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Decision_engine.22">"Decision engine"</span></h3>
<p>Bing has been heavily advertised as a "decision engine",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> though thought by columnist David Berkowitz to be more closely related to a <a href="/wiki/Web_portal" title="Web portal">web portal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bing_Rewards">Bing Rewards</span></h3>
<p>Currently known as "<b>Microsoft Rewards</b>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> launched by Microsoft in September 2010, it provides credits to users through regular Bing searches and special promotions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> These credits are then redeemed for various products including electronics, gift cards, sweepstakes, and charitable donations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Users are given either Member, Silver or Gold status at one given time based on how frequent they use the program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Initially, participants were required to download and use the Bing Bar for <a href="/wiki/Internet_Explorer" title="Internet Explorer">Internet Explorer</a> in order to earn credits; however, this is no longer the case, and the service now works with all desktop browsers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> The Bing Rewards program is similar to two earlier services, SearchPerks! and <a href="/wiki/Bing_Shopping#Cashback_program" title="Bing Shopping">Bing Cashback</a>, which have now been discontinued.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Colbert_Report">The Colbert Report</span></h3>
<p>During the episode of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Colbert_Report" title="The Colbert Report">The Colbert Report</a></i> that aired on June 8, 2010, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Colbert" title="Stephen Colbert">Stephen Colbert</a> stated that Microsoft would donate $2,500 to help clean up the <a href="/wiki/Deepwater_Horizon_oil_spill" title="Deepwater Horizon oil spill">Gulf oil spill</a> each time he mentioned the word "Bing" on air. Colbert mostly mentioned Bing in out-of-context situations, such as <a href="/wiki/Bing_Crosby" title="Bing Crosby">Bing Crosby</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bing_cherry" title="Bing cherry">Bing cherries</a>. By the end of the show, Colbert had said the word 40 times, for a total donation of $100,000. Colbert poked fun at their rivalry with Google, stating "Bing is a great website for doing Internet searches. I know that, because I Googled it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Search_deals">Search deals</span></h3>
<p>Bing was added into the list of search engines available in <a href="/wiki/Opera_(web_browser)" title="Opera (web browser)">Opera browser</a> from v10.6, but <a href="/wiki/Google_Search" title="Google Search">Google</a> remained the default search engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Firefox" title="Firefox">Mozilla Firefox</a> made a deal with Microsoft to jointly release <a class="external text" href="http://www.firefoxwithbing.com/" rel="nofollow">"Firefox with Bing"</a>, an edition of Firefox where Bing has replaced Google as the default search engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> The standard edition of Firefox has Google as its default search engine, but has included Bing in its list of search providers since Firefox version 4.0.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, Microsoft paid <a href="/wiki/Verizon_Wireless" title="Verizon Wireless">Verizon Wireless</a> $550 million USD<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> to use Bing as the default search provider on Verizon's BlackBerry and have Verizon "turn off" (via BlackBerry service books) the other search providers available. Users could still access other search engines via the mobile browser.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bing_It_On">Bing It On</span></h3>
<p>In 2012, a Bing marketing campaign asked the public which search engine they believed was better when its results were presented without branding, similar to the <a href="/wiki/Pepsi_Challenge" title="Pepsi Challenge">Pepsi Challenge</a> in the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> This poll was nicknamed "Bing It On".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> Microsoft presented a study of almost 1,000 people<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> which showed that 57% of participants in such a test preferred Bing's results, with only 30% preferring Google.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adult_content">Adult content</span></h2>
<p>Bing censors results for "adult" search terms for some regions, including India, People's Republic of China, Germany and Arab countries<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> where required by local laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> However, Bing allows users to change their country or region preference to somewhere without restrictions, such as the United States, United Kingdom or Republic of Ireland.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Censorship">Censorship</span></h3>
<p>Microsoft has been criticized for censoring Bing search results to queries made in <a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese characters</a>, used in <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">mainland China</a>. This is done to comply with the censorship requirements of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Government of the People's Republic of China">government in China</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> Microsoft has not indicated a willingness to stop censoring search results in simplified Chinese characters in the wake of Google's decision to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> All simplified Chinese searches in Bing are censored regardless of the user's country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Performance_issues">Performance issues</span></h3>
<p>Bing has been criticized for being slower to index websites than Google. It has also been criticized for not indexing some websites at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Copying_Google.27s_results">Copying Google's results</span></h3>
<p>Bing has been criticized by competitor Google for utilizing user input via Internet Explorer, the Bing Toolbar, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suggested_Sites" title="Suggested Sites">Suggested Sites</a>, to add results to Bing. After discovering in October 2010 that Bing appeared to be imitating Google's auto-correct results for a misspelling, despite not actually fixing the spelling of the term, Google set up a <a href="/wiki/Honeypot_(computing)" title="Honeypot (computing)">honeypot</a>, configuring the Google search engine to return specific unrelated results for 100 nonsensical queries such as <i>hiybbprqag</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> Over the next couple of weeks, Google engineers entered the search term into Google, while using Microsoft Internet Explorer, with the Bing Toolbar installed and the optional Suggested Sites enabled. In 9 out of the 100 queries, Bing later started returning the same results as Google, despite the only apparent connection between the result and search term being that Google's results connected the two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-googleblogbingcopying_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-googleblogbingcopying-95">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bingcopying_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-bingcopying-96">[96]</a></sup></p>
<p>Microsoft's response to this issue, coming from a company's spokesperson, was: "We do not copy Google's results." Bing's Vice President, Harry Shum, later reiterated that the search result data Google claimed that Bing copied had in fact come from Bing's very own users. Shum further wrote that "we use over 1,000 different signals and features in our ranking algorithm. A small piece of that is clickstream data we get from some of our customers, who opt into sharing anonymous data as they navigate the web in order to help us improve the experience for all users." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-googlebingsearchresults_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-googlebingsearchresults-97">[97]</a></sup> Microsoft commented that clickstream data from customers who had opted in was collected, but said that it was just a small piece of over 1000 signals used in their ranking algorithm, and that their intention was to learn from their collective customers. They stated that Bing was not intended to be a duplicate of any existing search engines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bingsearchquality_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-bingsearchquality-98">[98]</a></sup></p>
