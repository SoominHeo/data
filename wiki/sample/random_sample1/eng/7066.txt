<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Iceberg</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>An <b>iceberg</b> or <b>ice mountain</b> is a large piece of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> that has broken off a <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glacier</a> or an <a href="/wiki/Ice_shelf" title="Ice shelf">ice shelf</a> and is floating freely in open water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It may subsequently become frozen into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pack_ice" title="Pack ice">pack ice</a> (one form of <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">sea ice</a>). As it drifts into shallower waters, it may come into contact with the seabed, a process referred to as <a href="/wiki/Seabed_gouging_by_ice" title="Seabed gouging by ice">seabed gouging by ice</a>. Almost 91% of an iceberg is below the surface of the water.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Urban myth (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iceberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word "iceberg" is a partial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loan_translation" title="Loan translation">loan translation</a> from <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i>Eisberg</i>, literally meaning <i>ice mountain</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> cognate to <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a> <i>isbjerg</i>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> <i>ijsberg</i>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Low_Saxon" title="Northern Low Saxon">Low Saxon</a> <i><span dir="ltr" lang="nds-nl">Iesbarg</span></i> <span dir="ltr" lang="nds-nl">and <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a> <i><span dir="ltr" lang="sv">isberg</span>.</i></span></p>
<h2><span dir="ltr" lang="nds-nl"><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iceberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grotto_in_an_iceberg.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5845" data-file-width="4389" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Grotto_in_an_iceberg.jpg/220px-Grotto_in_an_iceberg.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Grotto_in_an_iceberg.jpg/330px-Grotto_in_an_iceberg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Grotto_in_an_iceberg.jpg/440px-Grotto_in_an_iceberg.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Grotto in an iceberg, photographed during the <a href="/wiki/Terra_Nova_Expedition" title="Terra Nova Expedition">British Antarctic Expedition</a> of 1911–1913, 5 Jan 1911. Photographer: Herbert Ponting, Alexander Turnbull Library</div>

<p>Because the <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> of pure ice is about 920 <a href="/wiki/Kilogram_per_cubic_metre" title="Kilogram per cubic metre">kg/m³</a>, and that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_water" title="Sea water">seawater</a> about 1025 kg/m³, typically only one-tenth of the volume of an iceberg is above water (due to <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">Archimedes's Principle</a>). The shape of the underwater portion can be difficult to judge by looking at the portion above the surface. This has led to the expression "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tip_of_the_iceberg" title="wikt:tip of the iceberg">tip of the iceberg</a>", for a problem or difficulty that is only a small manifestation of a larger problem.</p>
<p>Icebergs generally range from 1 to 75 metres (3.3 to 246.1 ft) above sea level and weigh 100,000 to 200,000 metric tons (110,000 to 220,000 short tons). The largest known iceberg in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a> was 168 metres (551 ft) above sea level, reported by the USCG icebreaker <i>East Wind</i> in 1958, making it the height of a 55-story building. These icebergs originate from the glaciers of western <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and may have an interior temperature of −15 to −20 °C (5 to −4 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cgfoi_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-cgfoi-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Icebergs are usually confined by winds and currents to move close to the coast. The largest icebergs recorded have been <a href="/wiki/Ice_calving" title="Ice calving">calved</a>, or broken off, from the <a href="/wiki/Ross_Ice_Shelf" title="Ross Ice Shelf">Ross Ice Shelf</a> of <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>. <a href="/wiki/Iceberg_B-15" title="Iceberg B-15">Iceberg B-15</a>, photographed by satellite in 2000, measured 295 by 37 kilometres (183 by 23 mi), with a surface area of 11,000 square kilometres (4,200 sq mi). The largest iceberg on record was an Antarctic tabular iceberg of over 31,000 square kilometres (12,000 sq mi) [335 by 97 kilometres (208 by 60 mi)] sighted 150 miles (240 km) west of <a href="/wiki/Scott_Island" title="Scott Island">Scott Island</a>, in the South Pacific Ocean, by the USS <i>Glacier</i> on November 12, 1956. This iceberg was larger than <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>When a piece of iceberg ice melts, it makes a fizzing sound called "Bergie <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seltzer_water" title="Seltzer water">Seltzer</a>". This sound is made when the water-ice interface reaches compressed air bubbles trapped in the ice. As this happens, each bubble bursts, making a 'popping' sound. The bubbles contain air trapped in snow layers very early in the history of the ice, that eventually got buried to a given depth (up to several kilometers) and pressurized as it transformed into <a href="/wiki/Firn" title="Firn">firn</a> then to glacial ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cgfoi_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-cgfoi-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

</div>
<p>In addition to size classification, icebergs can be classified on the basis of their shape. The two basic types of iceberg forms are <i>tabular</i> and <i>non-tabular</i>. Tabular icebergs have steep sides and a flat top, much like a <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">plateau</a>, with a length-to-height ratio of more than 5:1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iipssc_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-iipssc-12">[12]</a></sup> This type of iceberg, also known as an <i>ice island</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> can be quite large, as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Pobeda_Ice_Island" title="Pobeda Ice Island">Pobeda Ice Island</a>. <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a> icebergs formed by breaking off from an <a href="/wiki/Ice_shelf" title="Ice shelf">ice shelf</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Ross_Ice_Shelf" title="Ross Ice Shelf">Ross Ice Shelf</a> or <a href="/wiki/Filchner-Ronne_Ice_Shelf" title="Filchner-Ronne Ice Shelf">Filchner-Ronne Ice Shelf</a>, are typically tabular. The largest icebergs in the world are formed this way.</p>
<p>Non-tabular icebergs have different shapes and include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Types_of_Icebergs.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="384" data-file-width="512" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Types_of_Icebergs.svg/220px-Types_of_Icebergs.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Types_of_Icebergs.svg/330px-Types_of_Icebergs.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Types_of_Icebergs.svg/440px-Types_of_Icebergs.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Types_of_Icebergs.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Different shapes of icebergs. 1: Tabular; 2: Wedge; 3: Dome; 4: Drydock; 5: Pinnacled; 6: Blocky.</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li><i>Dome</i>: An iceberg with a rounded top.</li>
<li><i>Pinnacle</i>: An iceberg with one or more <a href="/wiki/Spire" title="Spire">spires</a>.</li>
<li><i>Wedge</i>: An iceberg with a steep edge on one side and a slope on the opposite side.</li>
<li><i>Dry-Dock</i>: An iceberg that has <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">eroded</a> to form a slot or <a href="/wiki/Channel_(geography)" title="Channel (geography)">channel</a>.</li>
<li><i>Blocky</i>: An iceberg with steep, vertical sides and a flat top. It differs from tabular icebergs in that its shape is more like a block than a flat sheet.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Monitoring">Monitoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iceberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Monitoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
<div class="mediaContainer" style="width:220px"><audio class="kskin" controls="" data-durationhint="280.61231292517" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons" data-mwtitle="Singing_iceberg.oga" data-startoffset="0" id="mwe_player_0" preload="none" style="width:220px">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Singing_iceberg.oga" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Acoustic monitoring of an iceberg.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Icebergs are monitored worldwide by the U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Ice_Center" title="National Ice Center">National Ice Center</a> (NIC), established in 1995, which produces analyses and forecasts of <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">Antarctic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a> ice conditions. More than 95% of the data used in its sea ice analyses are derived from the remote sensors on polar-orbiting satellites that survey these remote regions of the Earth.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Iceberg_A22A,_South_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1553" data-file-width="2345" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Iceberg_A22A%2C_South_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg/220px-Iceberg_A22A%2C_South_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Iceberg_A22A%2C_South_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg/330px-Iceberg_A22A%2C_South_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Iceberg_A22A%2C_South_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg/440px-Iceberg_A22A%2C_South_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Iceberg_A22A,_South_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Iceberg A22A in the South <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The NIC is the only organization that names and tracks all Antarctic Icebergs. It assigns each iceberg larger than 10 nautical miles (19 km) along at least one axis a name composed of a letter indicating its point of origin and a running number. The letters used are as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd><b>A</b> – <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> 0° to 90° W (<a href="/wiki/Bellingshausen_Sea" title="Bellingshausen Sea">Bellingshausen Sea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weddell_Sea" title="Weddell Sea">Weddell Sea</a>)</dd>
<dd><b>B</b> – longitude 90° W to 180° (<a href="/wiki/Amundsen_Sea" title="Amundsen Sea">Amundsen Sea</a>, Eastern <a href="/wiki/Ross_Sea" title="Ross Sea">Ross Sea</a>)</dd>
<dd><b>C</b> – longitude 90° E to 180° (Western Ross Sea, <a href="/wiki/Wilkes_Land" title="Wilkes Land">Wilkes Land</a>)</dd>
<dd><b>D</b> – longitude 0° to 90° E (<a href="/wiki/Amery_Ice_Shelf" title="Amery Ice Shelf">Amery Ice Shelf</a>, Eastern Weddell Sea)</dd>
</dl>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iceberg_B15" title="Iceberg B15">Iceberg B15</a> calved from the <a href="/wiki/Ross_Ice_Shelf" title="Ross Ice Shelf">Ross Ice Shelf</a> in 2000 and initially had an area of 11,000 square kilometres (4,200 sq mi). It broke apart in November 2002. The largest remaining piece of it, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iceberg_B-15A" title="Iceberg B-15A">Iceberg B-15A</a>, with an area of 3,000 square kilometres (1,200 sq mi), was still the largest iceberg on Earth until it ran aground and split into several pieces October 27, 2005, an event that was observed by seismographs both on the iceberg and across Antarctica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It has been hypothesized that this breakup may also have been abetted by ocean swell generated by an <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaskan</a> storm 6 days earlier and 13,500 kilometres (8,400 mi) away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iceberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Titanic_iceberg.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="740" data-file-width="1308" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Titanic_iceberg.jpg/220px-Titanic_iceberg.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Titanic_iceberg.jpg/330px-Titanic_iceberg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Titanic_iceberg.jpg/440px-Titanic_iceberg.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Titanic_iceberg.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The iceberg suspected of sinking the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">RMS Titanic</a>; a smudge of red paint much like the Titanic's red hull stripe was seen near its base at the waterline.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the 20th century, several scientific bodies were established to study and monitor the icebergs. The <a href="/wiki/International_Ice_Patrol" title="International Ice Patrol">International Ice Patrol</a>, formed in 1914 in response to the April 1912 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinking_of_the_Titanic" title="Sinking of the Titanic">sinking</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">Titanic</a></i>, which killed 1,517 of its 2,223 passengers, monitors iceberg dangers near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Banks" title="Grand Banks">Grand Banks</a> of Newfoundland and provides the "limits of all known ice" in that vicinity to the maritime community.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology_history">Technology history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iceberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Technology history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before the early 1910s there was no system in place to track icebergs to guard ships against collisions, most likely because they weren't considered a serious threat back then, ships have managed to survive even direct crashes. In 1907 <i><a href="/wiki/SS_Kronprinz_Wilhelm" title="SS Kronprinz Wilhelm">SS Kronprinz Wilhelm</a></i>, a German liner, had rammed an iceberg and suffered a crushed bow, but was still able to complete her voyage. The April 1912 sinking of the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic"><i>Titanic</i></a> however changed all that, and created the demand for a system to observe icebergs. For the remainder of the ice season of that year, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> patrolled the waters and monitored ice flow. In November 1913, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Conference_on_the_Safety_of_Life_at_Sea" title="International Conference on the Safety of Life at Sea">International Conference on the Safety of Life at Sea</a> met in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> to devise a more permanent system of observing icebergs. Within three months the participating maritime nations had formed the <a href="/wiki/International_Ice_Patrol" title="International Ice Patrol">International Ice Patrol</a> (IIP). The goal of the IIP was to collect data on <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanography</a> to measure currents, ice-flow, ocean temperature, and salinity levels. They published their first records in 1921, which allowed for a year-by-year comparison of iceberg movement.</p>
<p>New technologies monitor icebergs. Aerial surveillance of the seas in the early 1930s allowed for the development of charter systems that could accurately detail the ocean currents and iceberg locations. In 1945, experiments tested the effectiveness of <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> in detecting icebergs. A decade later, oceanographic monitoring outposts were established for the purpose of collecting data; these outposts continue to serve in environmental study. A computer was first installed on a ship for the purpose of oceanographic monitoring in 1964, which allowed for a faster evaluation of data. By the 1970s, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Icebreaking" title="Icebreaking">icebreaking</a> ships were equipped with automatic transmissions of <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a> photographs of ice in <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>. Systems for optical satellites had been developed but were still limited by weather conditions. In the 1980s, drifting <a href="/wiki/Buoy" title="Buoy">buoys</a> were used in Antarctic waters for oceanographic and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_research" title="Climate research">climate research</a>. They are equipped with sensors that measure ocean temperature and currents.</p>

An iceberg being pushed by three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a> ships in <a href="/wiki/McMurdo_Sound" title="McMurdo Sound">McMurdo Sound</a>, Antarctica</div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Side_looking_airborne_radar" title="Side looking airborne radar">Side looking airborne radar</a> (SLAR) made it possible to acquire images regardless of weather conditions. On November 4, 1995, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> launched <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RADARSAT-1" title="RADARSAT-1">RADARSAT-1</a>. Developed by the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Space_Agency" title="Canadian Space Agency">Canadian Space Agency</a>, it provides images of Earth for scientific and commercial purposes. This system was the first to use <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_aperture_radar" title="Synthetic aperture radar">synthetic aperture radar</a> (SAR), which sends <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> energy to the ocean surface and records the reflections to track icebergs. The <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a> launched <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ENVISAT" title="ENVISAT">ENVISAT</a> (an observation satellite that orbits the Earth's poles)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> on March 1, 2002. ENVISAT employs advanced synthetic aperture radar (ASAR) technology, which can detect changes in surface height accurately. The Canadian Space Agency launched <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RADARSAT-2" title="RADARSAT-2">RADARSAT-2</a> in December 2007, which uses SAR and multi-polarization modes and follows the same <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> path as RADARSAT-1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
