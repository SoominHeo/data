<p><b>Ice crystals</b> are solid <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> exhibiting atomic ordering on various length scales and include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hexagonal_crystal_system" title="Hexagonal crystal system">hexagonal</a> columns, hexagonal plates, <a href="/wiki/Dendrite_(crystal)" title="Dendrite (crystal)">dendritic crystals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Diamond_dust" title="Diamond dust">diamond dust</a>. The hugely symmetric shapes are due to <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(chemistry)" title="Deposition (chemistry)">depositional</a> <a href="/wiki/Crystal_growth" title="Crystal growth">growth</a>, namely, direct deposition of water vapour onto the ice crystal. Depending on environmental temperature and humidity, ice crystals can develop from the initial hexagonal prism into numerous symmetric shapes. Possible shapes for ice crystals are columns, <a href="/wiki/Needle_ice" title="Needle ice">needles</a>, plates and dendrites. If the crystal migrates into regions with different environmental conditions, the growth pattern may change, and the final crystal may show mixed patterns. An example are capped columns. Ice crystals tend to fall with their major axis aligned along the horizontal, and are thus visible in polarimetric weather radar signatures with enhanced (positive) differential reflectivity values. Electrification of ice crystals can induce alignments different from the horizontal. Electrified ice crystals are also well detectable by polarimetric weather radars.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">Temperature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a> determine many different crystalline forms. Ice crystals are responsible for various <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheric</a> optics displays.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ice_cloud" title="Ice cloud">Ice clouds</a> are composed of ice crystals, the most notable being <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_cloud" title="Cirrus cloud">cirrus clouds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ice_fog" title="Ice fog">ice fog</a>. The slight whitening of a clear blue sky caused by ice crystals high in the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> can be a sign that a <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">weather front</a> (and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rain_front" title="Rain front">rain</a>) is approaching, as moist air is carried to high levels and freezes to ice crystals.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geometry">Geometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ice_crystals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At ambient temperature and pressure, water molecules have a V shape. The two hydrogen atoms bond to the oxygen atom at a 105° angle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hexagonal.svg"><img alt="Hexagonal.svg" data-file-height="339" data-file-width="295" height="339" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Hexagonal.svg/295px-Hexagonal.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Hexagonal.svg/443px-Hexagonal.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Hexagonal.svg/590px-Hexagonal.svg.png 2x" width="295"/></a><br/>
Common ice <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystal_symmetry" title="Crystal symmetry">crystals are symmetrical</a> and have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hexagonal_crystal_system" title="Hexagonal crystal system">hexagonal pattern</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Square_crystals">Square crystals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ice_crystals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Square crystals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Square ice crystals form at room temperature when squeezed between two layers of <a href="/wiki/Graphene" title="Graphene">graphene</a>. The material is a new crystalline phase of ice, joining 17 others. The research derived from the earlier discovery that <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> and liquid water could pass through laminated sheets of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graphene_oxide" title="Graphene oxide">graphene oxide</a>, unlike smaller molecules such as <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>. The effect is thought to be driven by the <a href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_force" title="Van der Waals force">van der Waals force</a>, which may involve more than 10,000 <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atmospheres</a> of pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
