<p>"<b>Little Red Riding Hood</b>", or "<b>Little Red Ridinghood</b>", also known as "<b>Little Red Cap</b>" or simply "<b>Red Riding Hood</b>", is a European <a href="/wiki/Fairy_tale" title="Fairy tale">fairy tale</a> about a young girl and a <a href="/wiki/Big_Bad_Wolf" title="Big Bad Wolf">Big Bad Wolf</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacques_Berlioz_2007_p63_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jacques_Berlioz_2007_p63-1">[1]</a></sup> Its origins can be traced back to the 10th century by several European <a href="/wiki/Fable" title="Fable">folk tales</a>, including one from <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> called <i>The False Grandmother</i> (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <span lang="it"><i>La finta nonna</i></span>), later written among others by <a href="/wiki/Italo_Calvino" title="Italo Calvino">Italo Calvino</a> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Italian_Folktales" title="Italian Folktales">Italian Folktales</a></i> collection; the best known versions were written by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Perrault" title="Charles Perrault">Charles Perrault</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brothers_Grimm" title="Brothers Grimm">Brothers Grimm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The story has been changed considerably in various retellings and subjected to numerous modern adaptations and readings. It is number 333 in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aarne-Thompson_classification_system" title="Aarne-Thompson classification system">Aarne-Thompson classification system</a> for folktales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Variations of the story have developed, incorporating various cultural beliefs and regional dialects into the story. An example of this is "<b>Kawoni's Journey Across the Mountain: A Cherokee Little Red Riding Hood</b>", which introduces <a href="/wiki/Cherokee" title="Cherokee">Cherokee</a> myths and language into the traditional story. Another such example is "<b>Petite Rouge Riding Hood</b>", which approaches the story from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cajun" title="Cajun">Cajun</a> perspective.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tale">Tale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Red_Riding_Hood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Tale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Little_Red_Riding_Hood_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_19993.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="890" data-file-width="610" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Little_Red_Riding_Hood_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_19993.jpg/170px-Little_Red_Riding_Hood_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_19993.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Little_Red_Riding_Hood_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_19993.jpg/255px-Little_Red_Riding_Hood_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_19993.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Little_Red_Riding_Hood_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_19993.jpg/340px-Little_Red_Riding_Hood_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_19993.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Little_Red_Riding_Hood_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_19993.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Little Red Riding Hood</i>, illustrated in a 1927 story anthology</div>
</div>

<p>The story revolves around a girl called Little Red Riding Hood. In <a href="/wiki/Grimms%27_Fairy_Tales" title="Grimms' Fairy Tales">Grimms'</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Perrault" title="Charles Perrault">Perrault</a>'s versions of the tale, she is named after the red <a href="/wiki/Hood_(headgear)" title="Hood (headgear)">hooded</a> <a href="/wiki/Cape" title="Cape">cape</a>/<a href="/wiki/Cloak" title="Cloak">cloak</a> that she wears. The girl walks through the woods to deliver food to her sickly grandmother (wine and cake depending on the translation). In the Grimms' version, her mother had ordered her to stay strictly on the path.</p>
<p>A Big Bad Wolf wants to eat the girl and the food in the basket. He secretly stalks her behind trees, bushes, shrubs, and patches of little and tall grass. He approaches Little Red Riding Hood, and she naïvely tells him where she is going. He suggests that the girl pick some flowers, which she does. In the meantime, he goes to the grandmother's house and gains entry by pretending to be the girl. He swallows the grandmother whole (in some stories, he locks her in the closet) and waits for the girl, disguised as the grandma.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GustaveDore_She_was_astonished_to_see_how_her_grandmother_looked.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="672" data-file-width="962" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/GustaveDore_She_was_astonished_to_see_how_her_grandmother_looked.jpg/220px-GustaveDore_She_was_astonished_to_see_how_her_grandmother_looked.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/GustaveDore_She_was_astonished_to_see_how_her_grandmother_looked.jpg/330px-GustaveDore_She_was_astonished_to_see_how_her_grandmother_looked.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/GustaveDore_She_was_astonished_to_see_how_her_grandmother_looked.jpg/440px-GustaveDore_She_was_astonished_to_see_how_her_grandmother_looked.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GustaveDore_She_was_astonished_to_see_how_her_grandmother_looked.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Gustave_Dor%C3%A9" title="Gustave Doré">Gustave Doré</a>'s engraving of the scene: "She was astonished to see how her grandmother looked"</div>
</div>

<p>When the girl arrives, she notices that her grandmother looks very strange. Little Red then says, "What a deep voice you have!" ("The better to greet you with", responds the wolf), "Goodness, what big eyes you have!" ("The better to see you with", responds the wolf), "And what big hands you have!" ("The better to hug/grab you with", responds the wolf), and lastly, "What a big mouth you have" ("The better to eat you with!", responds the wolf), at which point the wolf jumps out of bed and eats her up too. Then he falls asleep. In Charles Perrault's version of the story (the first version to be published), the tale ends here. However, in later versions the story continues generally as follows:</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Lumberjack" title="Lumberjack">woodcutter</a> in the French version, but a <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunter</a> in the Brothers Grimm and traditional German versions, comes to the rescue and with his axe cuts open the sleeping wolf. Little Red Riding Hood and her grandmother emerge unharmed. They then fill the wolf's body with heavy stones. The wolf awakens and tries to flee, but the stones cause him to collapse and die. Sanitized versions of the story have the grandmother locked in the closet instead of eaten and some have Little Red Riding Hood saved by the lumberjack as the wolf advances on her rather than after she is eaten where the woodcutter kills the wolf with his axe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>The tale makes the clearest contrast between the safe world of the village and the dangers of the <a href="/wiki/Enchanted_forest" title="Enchanted forest">forest</a>, conventional <a href="/wiki/Antithesis" title="Antithesis">antitheses</a> that are essentially medieval, though no written versions are as old as that.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It also warns about the dangers of not obeying one's mother (at least in the Grimms' version).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_other_tales">Relationship to other tales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Red_Riding_Hood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Relationship to other tales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A very similar story also belongs to the North African tradition, namely in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kabylia" title="Kabylia">Kabylia</a>, where a number of versions are attested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The theme of the little girl who visits her (grand-)dad in his cabin and is recognized by the sound of her bracelets constitutes the refrain of a well-known song by the modern singer <a href="/wiki/Idir" title="Idir">Idir</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Vava_Inouva" title="A Vava Inouva">A Vava Inouva</a></i>:</p>
<table>
<tr>
<td>
<dl>
<dd>‘I beseech you, open the door for me, father.</dd>
<dd>Jingle your bracelets, oh my daughter Ghriba.</dd>
<dd>I’m afraid of the monster in the forest, father.</dd>
<dd>I, too, am afraid, oh my daughter Ghriba.’<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>The theme of the ravening wolf and of the creature released unharmed from its <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">belly</a> is also reflected in the Russian tale <i><a href="/wiki/Peter_and_the_Wolf" title="Peter and the Wolf">Peter and the Wolf</a></i><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and another Grimm tale <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wolf_and_the_Seven_Young_Kids" title="The Wolf and the Seven Young Kids">The Wolf and the Seven Young Kids</a></i>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> but its general theme of restoration is at least as old as the biblical story, <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Jonah" title="Book of Jonah">Jonah and the Whale</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The theme also appears in the story of the life of <a href="/wiki/Margaret_the_Virgin" title="Margaret the Virgin">Saint Margaret</a>, wherein the saint emerges unharmed from the belly of a <a href="/wiki/Dragon" title="Dragon">dragon</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and in the epic "The Red Path" by <a href="/wiki/Jim_C._Hines" title="Jim C. Hines">Jim C. Hines</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>There is even a connection to <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>: <a href="/wiki/Cronus" title="Cronus">Cronus</a>, the leader of the <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" title="Titan (mythology)">Titans</a>, knew he was destined to be overcome by his own sons and, as a result, he devoured his children <a href="/wiki/Demeter" title="Demeter">Demeter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hestia" title="Hestia">Hestia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hera" title="Hera">Hera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hades" title="Hades">Hades</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Poseidon" title="Poseidon">Poseidon</a> as soon as they were born. They were eventually set free, unharmed, by <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a>, when he gave Cronus an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emetic" title="Emetic">emetic</a> (or, in some versions, cut his stomach open).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The dialogue between the Big Bad Wolf and Little Red Riding Hood has its analogies to the Norse <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%9Erymskvi%C3%B0a" title="Þrymskviða">Þrymskviða</a></i> from the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elder_Edda" title="Elder Edda">Elder Edda</a></i>; the giant <a href="/wiki/%C3%9Erymr" title="Þrymr">Þrymr</a> had stolen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mj%C3%B6lner" title="Mjölner">Mjölner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thor" title="Thor">Thor</a>'s hammer, and demanded <a href="/wiki/Freyja" title="Freyja">Freyja</a> as his bride for its return. Instead, the gods dressed Thor as a bride and sent him. When the giants note Thor's unladylike eyes, eating, and drinking, <a href="/wiki/Loki" title="Loki">Loki</a> explains them as Freyja's not having slept, or eaten, or drunk, out of longing for the wedding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earliest_versions">Earliest versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Red_Riding_Hood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Earliest versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The origins of the Little Red Riding Hood story can be traced to versions from various European countries and more than likely preceding the 17th century, of which several exist, some significantly different from the currently known, Grimms-inspired version. It was told by <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> peasants in the 10th century<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacques_Berlioz_2007_p63_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jacques_Berlioz_2007_p63-1">[1]</a></sup> and recorded by the cathedral schoolmaster Egbert of Liege.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, the Little Red Riding Hood was told by peasants in the fourteenth century, where a number of versions exist, including <i>La finta nonna</i> (The False Grandmother), written among others by <a href="/wiki/Italo_Calvino" title="Italo Calvino">Italo Calvino</a> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Italian_Folktales" title="Italian Folktales">Italian Folktales</a></i> collection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It has also been called "The Story of Grandmother". It is also possible that this early tale has roots in very similar Oriental tales (e.g. "Grandaunt Tiger").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>These early variations of the tale differ from the currently known version in several ways. The antagonist is not always a wolf, but sometimes an <a href="/wiki/Ogre" title="Ogre">ogre</a> or a 'bzou' (<a href="/wiki/Werewolf" title="Werewolf">werewolf</a>), making these tales relevant to the werewolf-trials (similar to witch trials) of the time (e.g. the trial of <a href="/wiki/Peter_Stumpp" title="Peter Stumpp">Peter Stumpp</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The wolf usually leaves the grandmother's blood and meat for the girl to eat, who then unwittingly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cannibal" title="Cannibal">cannibalizes</a> her own grandmother. Furthermore, the wolf was also known to ask her to remove her clothing and toss it into the fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In some versions, the wolf eats the girl after she gets into bed with him, and the story ends there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-14">[14]</a></sup> In others, she sees through his disguise and tries to escape, complaining to her "grandmother" that she needs to defecate and would not wish to do so in the bed. The wolf reluctantly lets her go, tied to a piece of string so she does not get away. However, the girl slips the string over something else and runs off. In these stories she escapes with no help from any male or older female figure, instead using her own cunning. Sometimes, though more rarely, the red hood is even non-existent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In other tellings of the story, the wolf chases after Little Red Riding Hood. She escapes with the help of some laundresses, who spread a sheet taut over a river so she may escape. When the wolf follows Red over the bridge of cloth, the sheet is released and the wolf drowns in the river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, it was revealed from scientific research that the tale originated in the 1st century in the Middle East and not, as previously assumed, in China. The scientists analysed the storylines and characters from 58 versions of the tale from different areas. By means of a computer model they then determined how the different versions of Little Red Riding Hood are related.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Charles_Perrault">Charles Perrault</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Red_Riding_Hood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Charles Perrault">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The earliest known printed version<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> was known as <i>Le Petit <a href="/wiki/Chaperon_(headgear)" title="Chaperon (headgear)">Chaperon</a> Rouge</i> and may have had its origins in 17th-century French <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a>. It was included in the collection <i><a href="/wiki/Histoires_ou_contes_du_temps_pass%C3%A9" title="Histoires ou contes du temps passé">Tales and Stories of the Past with Morals. Tales of Mother Goose</a></i> (<i>Histoires et contes du temps passé, avec des moralités. Contes de ma mère l'Oye</i>), in 1697, by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Perrault" title="Charles Perrault">Charles Perrault</a>. As the title implies, this version<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> is both more sinister and more overtly moralized than the later ones. The redness of the hood, which has been given symbolic significance in many interpretations of the tale, was a detail introduced by Perrault.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Little_Red_Riding_Hood.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1137" data-file-width="1677" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Little_Red_Riding_Hood.jpg/220px-Little_Red_Riding_Hood.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Little_Red_Riding_Hood.jpg/330px-Little_Red_Riding_Hood.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Little_Red_Riding_Hood.jpg/440px-Little_Red_Riding_Hood.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Little_Red_Riding_Hood.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
French images, like this 19th-century painting, show the much shorter red <a href="/wiki/Chaperon_(headgear)" title="Chaperon (headgear)">chaperon</a> being worn</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The story had as its subject an "attractive, well-bred young lady", a village girl of the country being deceived into giving a wolf she encountered the information he needed to find her grandmother's house successfully and eat the old woman while at the same time avoiding being noticed by woodcutters working in the nearby forest. Then he proceeded to lay a trap for the Red Riding Hood. Little Red Riding Hood ends up being asked to climb into the bed before being eaten by the wolf, where the story ends. The wolf emerges the victor of the encounter and there is no happy ending.</p>
<p>Charles Perrault explained the 'moral' at the end of the tale:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> so that no doubt is left to his intended meaning:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>From this story one learns that children, especially young lasses, pretty, courteous and well-bred, do very wrong to listen to strangers, And it is not an unheard thing if the Wolf is thereby provided with his dinner. I say Wolf, for all wolves are not of the same sort; there is <a href="/wiki/Rake_(character)" title="Rake (character)">one kind</a> with an amenable disposition – neither noisy, nor hateful, nor angry, but tame, obliging and gentle, following the young maids in the streets, even into their homes. Alas! Who does not know that these gentle wolves are of all such creatures the most dangerous!</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This, the presumed original, version of the tale was written for late seventeenth-century French court of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Louis_XIV" title="King Louis XIV">King Louis XIV</a>. This audience, whom the King entertained with extravagant parties, presumably would take from the story the intended meaning.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grimm_Brothers">Grimm Brothers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Red_Riding_Hood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Grimm Brothers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grimm.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="553" height="239" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Grimm.jpg/220px-Grimm.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Grimm.jpg/330px-Grimm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Grimm.jpg/440px-Grimm.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grimm.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Wilhelm (left) and Jacob Grimm (right) from an 1855 painting by Elisabeth Jerichau-Baumann.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the 19th century two separate German versions were retold to <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Grimm" title="Jacob Grimm">Jacob Grimm</a> and his younger brother <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Grimm" title="Wilhelm Grimm">Wilhelm Grimm</a>, known as the <a href="/wiki/Brothers_Grimm" title="Brothers Grimm">Brothers Grimm</a>, the first by Jeanette Hassenpflug (1791–1860) and the second by Marie Hassenpflug (1788–1856). The brothers turned the first version to the main body of the story and the second into a sequel of it. The story as <i>Rotkäppchen</i> was included in the first edition of their collection <i><a href="/wiki/Grimms%27_Fairy_Tales" title="Grimms' Fairy Tales">Kinder- und Hausmärchen</a></i> (Children's and Household Tales (1812)).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The earlier parts of the tale agree so closely with Perrault's variant that it is almost certainly the source of the tale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> However, they modified the ending; this version had the little girl and her grandmother saved by a huntsman who was after the wolf's skin; this ending is identical to that in the tale <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wolf_and_the_Seven_Young_Kids" title="The Wolf and the Seven Young Kids">The Wolf and the Seven Young Kids</a></i>, which appears to be the source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The second part featured the girl and her grandmother trapping and killing another wolf, this time anticipating his moves based on their experience with the previous one. The girl did not leave the path when the wolf spoke to her, her grandmother locked the door to keep it out, and when the wolf lurked, the grandmother had Little Red Riding Hood put a trough under the chimney and fill it with water that sausages had been cooked in; the smell lured the wolf down, and it drowned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Brothers further revised the story in later editions and it reached the above-mentioned final and better-known version in the 1857 edition of their work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> It is notably tamer than the older stories which contained darker themes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_Grimms">After the Grimms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Red_Riding_Hood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: After the Grimms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:New-33.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="457" data-file-width="473" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/10/New-33.jpg/220px-New-33.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/10/New-33.jpg/330px-New-33.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/10/New-33.jpg/440px-New-33.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:New-33.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An engraving from the <i>Cyclopedia of Wit and Humor</i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Numerous authors have rewritten or adapted this tale.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Andrew_Lang" title="Andrew Lang">Andrew Lang</a> included a variant called "The True History of Little Goldenhood"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Red_Fairy_Book" title="The Red Fairy Book">The Red Fairy Book</a></i> (1890). He derived it from the works of Charles Marelles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> in <i>Contes of Charles Marelles</i>. This version explicitly states that the story had been mistold earlier. The girl is saved, but not by the huntsman; when the wolf tries to eat her, its mouth is burned by the golden hood she wears, which is enchanted.</p>
<p>James N. Barker wrote a variation of Little Red Riding Hood in 1827 as an approximately 1000-word story. It was later reprinted in 1858 in a book of collected stories edited by William E Burton, called the <i>Cyclopedia of Wit and Humor</i>. The reprint also features a wood engraving of a clothed wolf on bended knee holding Little Red Riding Hood's hand.</p>
<p>In the 20th century, the popularity of the tale appeared to snowball, with many new versions being written and produced, especially in the wake of Freudian analysis, <a href="/wiki/Deconstruction" title="Deconstruction">deconstruction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feminist_literary_criticism" title="Feminist literary criticism">feminist critical theory</a>. (See <a href="#Modern_uses_and_adaptations">"Modern uses and adaptations" below</a>.) This trend has also led to a number of academic texts being written that focus on Little Red Riding Hood, including works by <a href="/wiki/Alan_Dundes" title="Alan Dundes">Alan Dundes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Zipes" title="Jack Zipes">Jack Zipes</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretations">Interpretations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Red_Riding_Hood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Interpretations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dore_ridinghood.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="961" data-file-width="747" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Dore_ridinghood.jpg/170px-Dore_ridinghood.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Dore_ridinghood.jpg/255px-Dore_ridinghood.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Dore_ridinghood.jpg/340px-Dore_ridinghood.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

</div>
<p>Besides the overt warning about talking to strangers, there are many interpretations of the classic fairy tale, many of them sexual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Some are listed below.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_cycles">Natural cycles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Red_Riding_Hood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Natural cycles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Folkloristics" title="Folkloristics">Folklorists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cultural_anthropology" title="Cultural anthropology">cultural anthropologists</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P._Saintyves" title="P. Saintyves">P. Saintyves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Burnett_Tylor" title="Edward Burnett Tylor">Edward Burnett Tylor</a>, saw "Little Red Riding Hood" in terms of solar myths and other naturally occurring cycles. Her red hood could represent the bright sun which is ultimately swallowed by the terrible night (the wolf), and the variations in which she is cut out of the wolf's belly represent the dawn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In this interpretation, there is a connection between the wolf of this tale and <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%B6ll" title="Sköll">Sköll</a>, the wolf in Norse mythology that will swallow <a href="/wiki/S%C3%B3l_(sun)" title="Sól (sun)">the personified Sun</a> at <a href="/wiki/Ragnar%C3%B6k" title="Ragnarök">Ragnarök</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Fenrir" title="Fenrir">Fenrir</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Alternatively, the tale could be about the season of spring or the month of May, escaping the winter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rite">Rite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Red_Riding_Hood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Rite">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The tale has been interpreted as a <a href="/wiki/Puberty" title="Puberty">puberty</a> rite, stemming from a prehistoric origin (sometimes an origin stemming from a previous matriarchal era).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The girl, leaving home, enters a <a href="/wiki/Liminality" title="Liminality">liminal</a> state and by going through the acts of the tale, is transformed into an adult woman by the act of coming out of the wolf's belly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rebirth">Rebirth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Red_Riding_Hood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Rebirth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bruno_Bettelheim" title="Bruno Bettelheim">Bruno Bettelheim</a>, in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Uses_of_Enchantment" title="The Uses of Enchantment">The Uses of Enchantment</a>: The Meaning and Importance of Fairy Tales</i> (1976), recast the Little Red Riding Hood motif in terms of classic <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">Freudian</a> analysis, that shows how fairy tales educate, support, and liberate children's emotions. The motif of the huntsman cutting open the wolf he interpreted as a "rebirth"; the girl who foolishly listened to the wolf has been reborn as a new person.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norse_myth">Norse myth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Red_Riding_Hood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Norse myth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The poem "<a href="/wiki/%C3%9Erymskvi%C3%B0a" title="Þrymskviða">Þrymskviða</a>" from the <i><a href="/wiki/Poetic_Edda" title="Poetic Edda">Poetic Edda</a></i> mirrors some elements of Red Riding Hood. <a href="/wiki/Loki" title="Loki">Loki</a>'s explanations for the strange behavior of "<a href="/wiki/Freyja" title="Freyja">Freyja</a>" (actually <a href="/wiki/Thor" title="Thor">Thor</a> disguised as Freya) mirror the wolf's explanations for his strange appearance. The red hood has often been given great importance in many interpretations, with a significance from the dawn to blood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Erotic.2C_romantic.2C_or_rape_connotations">Erotic, romantic, or rape connotations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Red_Riding_Hood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Erotic, romantic, or rape connotations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A sexual analysis of the tale may also include negative connotations in terms of rape or abduction. In <i>Against Our Will</i>, <a href="/wiki/Susan_Brownmiller" title="Susan Brownmiller">Susan Brownmiller</a> describes the fairy tale as a description of rape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> However, many revisionist retellings choose to focus on empowerment, and depict Little Red Riding Hood or the grandmother successfully defending herself against the wolf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Such tellings bear some similarity to the "animal bridegroom" tales, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Beauty_and_the_Beast" title="Beauty and the Beast">Beauty and the Beast</a></i> or <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Frog_Prince_(story)" title="The Frog Prince (story)">The Frog Prince</a></i>, but where the heroines of those tales transform the hero into a prince, these tellings of <i>Little Red Riding Hood</i> reveal to the heroine that she has a wild nature like the hero's.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> These interpretations refuse to characterize Little Red Riding Hood as a victim; these are tales of female empowerment.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Animations.2C_films.2C_and_TV_shows">Animations, films, and TV shows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Red_Riding_Hood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Animations, films, and TV shows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Tex_Avery" title="Tex Avery">Tex Avery</a>'s short <a href="/wiki/Animated_cartoon" title="Animated cartoon">animated cartoon</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Hot_Riding_Hood" title="Red Hot Riding Hood">Red Hot Riding Hood</a></i> (1943), the story is recast in an adult-oriented urban setting, with the suave, sharp-dressed Wolf howling after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nightclub_singer" title="Nightclub singer">nightclub singer</a> Red. Avery used the same cast and themes in a subsequent series of cartoons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Neil_Jordan" title="Neil Jordan">Neil Jordan</a>'s film version of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Company_of_Wolves" title="The Company of Wolves">The Company of Wolves</a></i> (1984) is about the journey of a young girl into womanhood, which can be interpreted as her menstruating for the first time (at the start of the tale, she is in bed with a stomach ache, according to her sister), but also of sexual awakening (in it, she wears red lipstick in bed). The wolf in this version of the tale is in fact a werewolf, which comes to the newly menstruating Red Riding Hood in the forest, in the form of a charming hunter. He turns into a wolf and eats her grandmother, and is about to devour Red Riding Hood as well, but she is equally seductive and ends up lying with the wolf man.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lipstick">Lipstick</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Red_Riding_Hood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Lipstick">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>Allusions to the tale can be more or less overtly sexual, as when the color of a lipstick is advertised as "Riding Hood Red".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Red_Riding_Hood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>Charles Perrault's "Le Petit Chaperon rouge" ("Little Red Riding Hood") is centered on an erotic metaphor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li>Little Red Riding Hood appears in <a href="/wiki/Angela_Carter" title="Angela Carter">Angela Carter</a>'s short story "The Company of Wolves", published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bloody_Chamber" title="The Bloody Chamber">The Bloody Chamber</a></i> (1979), her collection of "dark, feminist fables" filled with "bestial and ferocious" heroines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In her revision of the classic, Carter examines female lust, which according to author Catherine Orenstein is "healthy, but also challenging and sometimes disturbing, unbridled and feral lust that delivers up contradictions." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orenstein.2C_Catherine_167_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Orenstein.2C_Catherine_167-44">[44]</a></sup> As Orenstein points out, Carter does this by unravelling the original tale's "underlying sexual currents" and by impregnating the new Little Red Riding Hood (Rosaleen played by <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Patterson" title="Sarah Patterson">Sarah Patterson</a>) with "animal instincts" that lead to her transformation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orenstein.2C_Catherine_167_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Orenstein.2C_Catherine_167-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li>In Michelle Augello-Page's story "Wolf Moon",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Little Red is an adult who has been irrevocably changed by the events in her childhood, and it is the hunter who saves her "once upon a time, and again" in this tale of sexual awakening, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bdsm" title="Bdsm">bdsm</a>, relationships, female rites and rebirth.</li>
</ul>
