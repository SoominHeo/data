<p>A <b>drinking straw</b> is a tube for transferring a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beverage" title="Beverage">beverage</a> from its container to the mouth of the drinker. A thin tube of <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">polypropylene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">polystyrene</a>) or other material, straight or with an angle-adjustable <a href="/wiki/Bellows" title="Bellows">bellows</a> segment, it is used by being held with one end in the mouth and another end in the drink. Muscular action (a combination of the muscles of the <a href="/wiki/Tongue" title="Tongue">Tongue</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cheek" title="Cheek">Cheeks</a>) reduces air <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> in the mouth and above the liquid in the straw, whereupon <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> forces the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beverage" title="Beverage">beverage</a> through the straw.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_straw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first known straws were made by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sumerians" title="Sumerians">Sumerians</a>, and were used for drinking <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thompson2011_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-thompson2011-1">[1]</a></sup> probably to avoid the solid byproducts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(food)" title="Fermentation (food)">fermentation</a> that sink to the bottom.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The oldest drinking straw in existence, found in a Sumerian tomb dated 3,000 B.C.E., was a gold tube inlaid with the precious blue stone <a href="/wiki/Lapis_lazuli" title="Lapis lazuli">lapis lazuli</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thompson2011_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-thompson2011-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentines</a> and their neighbors used a similar metallic device called a <a href="/wiki/Bombilla" title="Bombilla">bombilla</a>, that acts as both a straw and sieve for drinking <a href="/wiki/Yerba_mate" title="Yerba mate">mate</a> tea for hundreds of years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thompson2011_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-thompson2011-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1800s, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rye_grass" title="Rye grass">rye grass</a> straw came into fashion because it was cheap and soft, but it had an unfortunate tendency to turn to mush in liquid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thompson2011_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-thompson2011-1">[1]</a></sup> To address these shortcomings, <a href="/wiki/Marvin_Stone" title="Marvin Stone">Marvin C. Stone</a> patented the modern drinking straw, made of paper, in 1888.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He came upon the idea while drinking a <a href="/wiki/Mint_julep" title="Mint julep">mint julep</a> on a hot day in Washington, D.C.; the taste of the rye was mixing with the drink and giving it a grassy taste, which he found unsatisfactory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thompson2011_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-thompson2011-1">[1]</a></sup> He wound paper around a pencil to make a thin tube, slid out the pencil from one end, and applied glue between the strips.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thompson2011_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-thompson2011-1">[1]</a></sup> He later refined it by building a machine that would coat the outside of the paper with wax to hold it together, so the glue wouldn't dissolve in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bourbon_(whiskey)" title="Bourbon (whiskey)">bourbon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thompson2011_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-thompson2011-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early paper straws had a narrow <a href="/wiki/Lumen_(anatomy)" title="Lumen (anatomy)">bore</a> similar to that of the grass stems then in common use. It was common to use two of them, to reduce the effort needed to take each sip. (The cocktail straw, which is sometimes used in pairs, may be derived from such early straws.) Modern plastic straws are made with a larger bore, and only one is needed for ease of drinking.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_and_environment">Health and environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_straw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Health and environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Drinking straws are a form of plastic consumption when made, and become a <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfill</a> item when discarded. Made from polypropylene, they are strong and can be reused rather than recycled into other products. Waste straws in Uganda are collected from beer and soft drink depots, cleaned, and woven into mats for picnics and prayers or joined to form bags.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_straw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Straw-O.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Straw-O.jpg/220px-Straw-O.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Straw-O.jpg/330px-Straw-O.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Straw-O.jpg/440px-Straw-O.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Straw-O.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A depiction of the various widths of drinking straws. Size in <a href="/wiki/Millimetre" title="Millimetre">mm</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>A <b>basic drinking straw</b> is straight for the full length.</li>
<li>A <b>bendable straw</b> or "bendy straw" (known in the industry as an "articulated straw") has a <a href="/wiki/Concertina" title="Concertina">concertina</a>-type hinge near the top for convenience. This variation was invented by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Friedman" title="Joseph Friedman">Joseph Friedman</a> in 1937.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thompson2011_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-thompson2011-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Candy straws</b>, such as <a href="/wiki/Liquorice" title="Liquorice">licorice</a> straws (or lico-straws), are made from some type of chewy candy.</li>
<li>A recent addition to the straw family is the <b>cereal straw</b>, such as the ones made by <a href="/wiki/Kellogg%27s" title="Kellogg's">Kellogg's</a>.</li>
<li>Special "<b>color-changer</b>" straws change color when cold (or hot) liquid passes through them. At some restaurants, frozen beverages like <a href="/wiki/Slush_(beverage)" title="Slush (beverage)">slush</a> or <a href="/wiki/Frapp%C3%A9_coffee" title="Frappé coffee">frappes</a> are served with color changing straws. Many of these are larger in diameter than typical straws to aid in drinking the thicker beverages.</li>
<li>A "<b>crazy straw</b>" is hard, <a href="/wiki/Transparency_and_translucency" title="Transparency and translucency">transparent or translucent</a> plastic and has a number of twists and turns at the top. When liquid is sucked through the straw, it quickly flows through the winding path, creating a mildly amusing spectacle, popular with children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The crazy straw also has some occupational therapy benefit for autism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Extendo-straws</b> come in small plastic wrappings like miniature straws, but can extend to reach the bottom of the carton.</li>
<li><b>Flavor straws</b> are a form of drinking straw with a flavoring included, designed to make drinking milk more pleasant for children. They first marketed in the United States in 1956 as <a href="/wiki/Flav-R-Straws" title="Flav-R-Straws">Flav-R-Straws</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In recent years, newer variations of the original idea have been resurrected in forms such as <a href="/wiki/Sipahh" title="Sipahh">Sipahhs</a>, and Magic Milk Straws that contain hundreds of flavored pellets encased within a stiff plastic straw.</li>
<li>A <b>miniature straw</b> is often attached to a <a href="/wiki/Juicebox_(container)" title="Juicebox (container)">drink box</a>.</li>
</ul>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:220px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vogue_SM.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="285" data-file-width="218" height="285" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bb/Vogue_SM.jpg" width="218"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vogue_SM.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cover of <i><a href="/wiki/Vogue_(British_magazine)" title="Vogue (British magazine)">Vogue</a></i> magazine depicting <a href="/wiki/Kate_Moss" title="Kate Moss">Kate Moss</a> drinking through a straw</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">Polypropylene</a></b> is becoming favored over polystyrene for manufacturing plastic drinking straws as polystyrene is brittle and tends to crack easily. Polystyrene is also denser than water, causing straws to sink when placed into beverages. Polypropylene straws, by contrast, are much more durable and do not sink.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Silicone" title="Silicone">Silicone</a></b> is a newer material used in drinking straws. Silicone straws are marketed for their freezability, invulnerability to cracking or peeling, and insulation for hot and cold drinks.</li>
<li><b>Metal</b> straws, very popular among campers, are made from <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">aluminum</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>.</li>
<li><b>"Sanitary" straws</b> are individually wrapped to avoid contamination. Straws were originally marketed as a means for people to reduce the risk of contracting an illness from improperly washed containers, glasses, or cups.</li>
<li>A <b>spoon straw</b> features a cut-away shape at one end that functions as a miniature spoon. It is intended for <a href="/wiki/Slush_(beverage)" title="Slush (beverage)">slush drinks</a> and milkshakes. Their original purpose was to avoid ice clogging up the submerged end of the straw.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:CIRCULAR" title="Wikipedia:CIRCULAR"><span title="This claim cites another Wikipedia article. Articles need references to reliable third-party sources. (June 2016)">circular reference</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>A <b>wide straw</b> is used for sipping <a href="/wiki/Bubble_tea" title="Bubble tea">bubble tea</a>. The larger diameter is necessary to accommodate the drink's characteristic <a href="/wiki/Tapioca" title="Tapioca">tapioca pearls</a>, and can also be used for stirring. The tip of these straws are sometimes cut at an angle creating a point. This allows the straw to puncture the plastic cover of the cup.</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nicholson_Baker" title="Nicholson Baker">Nicholson Baker</a>'s novel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mezzanine" title="The Mezzanine">The Mezzanine</a></i> (1988), includes a detailed discussion of various types of drinking straws experienced by the narrator and their relative merits.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_straw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">
