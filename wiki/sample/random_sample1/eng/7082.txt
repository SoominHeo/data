<div class="hatnote" role="note">This article uses anatomical terminology; for an overview, see <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terminology" title="Anatomical terminology">Anatomical terminology</a>.</div>
<p>The <b>epiphyseal plate</b> (or <b>epiphysial plate</b>, <b>physis</b>, or <b>growth plate</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Hyaline_cartilage" title="Hyaline cartilage">hyaline cartilage</a> plate in the <a href="/wiki/Metaphysis" title="Metaphysis">metaphysis</a> at each end of a <a href="/wiki/Long_bone" title="Long bone">long bone</a>.</p>
<p>The plate is found in children and adolescents; in adults, who have stopped growing, the plate is replaced by an <b>epiphyseal line</b>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Epiphyseal_plate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Epiphyseal_plate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Endochondral_ossification" title="Endochondral ossification">Endochondral ossification</a> is responsible for the initial bone development from cartilage <a href="/wiki/In_utero" title="In utero">in utero</a> and infants and the longitudinal growth of long bones in the epiphyseal plate. The plate's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chondrocytes" title="Chondrocytes">chondrocytes</a> are under constant division by <a href="/wiki/Mitosis" title="Mitosis">mitosis</a>. These <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daughter_cell" title="Daughter cell">daughter cells</a> stack facing the <a href="/wiki/Epiphysis" title="Epiphysis">epiphysis</a> while the older cells are pushed towards the <a href="/wiki/Diaphysis" title="Diaphysis">diaphysis</a>. As the older chondrocytes degenerate, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osteoblasts" title="Osteoblasts">osteoblasts</a> ossify the remains to form new bone. In puberty increasing levels of estrogen, in both females and males, leads to increased <a href="/wiki/Apoptosis" title="Apoptosis">apoptosis</a> of chondrocytes in the epiphyseal plate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid21068162_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid21068162-1">[1]</a></sup> Depletion of chondrocytes due to apoptosis leads to less <a href="/wiki/Ossification" title="Ossification">ossification</a> and growth slows down and later stops when the entire cartilage have become replaced by bone, leaving only a thin epiphyseal scar which later disappears.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seerbonedev_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-seerbonedev-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Histology">Histology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Epiphyseal_plate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Histology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The growth plate has a very specific morphology in having a zonal arrangement.</p>
<table class="wikitable">
<tr>
<th>Epiphyseal plate zone (from epiphysis to diaphysis</th>
<th>Description</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Zone of reserve</td>
<td>Quiescent chondrocytes are found at the epiphyseal end</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Zone of proliferation</td>
<td>Chondrocytes undergo rapid mitosis under influence of <a href="/wiki/Growth_hormone" title="Growth hormone">growth hormone</a></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Zone of maturation and hypertrophy</td>
<td>Chondrocytes stop mitosis, and begin to <a href="/wiki/Hypertrophy" title="Hypertrophy">hypertrophy</a> by accumulating glycogen, lipids, and <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_phosphatase" title="Alkaline phosphatase">alkaline phosphatase</a></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Zone of calcification</td>
<td>Chondrocytes undergo <a href="/wiki/Apoptosis" title="Apoptosis">apoptosis</a>. Cartilagenous matrix begins to calcify.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Zone of ossification</td>
<td>Osteoclasts and osteoblasts from the diaphyseal side break down the calcified cartilage and replace with mineralized bone tissue.</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A mnemonic for remembering the names of the epiphyseal plate growth zones is "Real People Have Career Options," standing for: Resting zone, Proliferative zone, Hypertrophic cartilage zone, Calcified cartilage zone, Ossification zone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The growth plate is clinically relevant in that it is often the primary site for infection, metastasis, fractures and the effects of endocrine bone disorders.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Epiphyseal_plate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Clinical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Defects in the development and continued division of epiphyseal plates can lead to growth disorders. The most common defect is <a href="/wiki/Achondroplasia" title="Achondroplasia">achondroplasia</a>, where there is a defect in cartilage formation. Achondroplasia is the most common cause of <a href="/wiki/Dwarfism" title="Dwarfism">dwarfism</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Salter%E2%80%93Harris_fracture" title="Salter–Harris fracture">Salter–Harris fractures</a> are fractures involving epiphyseal plates and hence tend to interfere with growth and height.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osgood-Schlatter_disease" title="Osgood-Schlatter disease">Osgood-Schlatter Disease</a> results from stress on the epiphyseal plate in the <a href="/wiki/Tibia" title="Tibia">tibia</a>, leading to excess bone growth and a painful lump at the knee.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_animals">Other animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Epiphyseal_plate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Hunter_(surgeon)" title="John Hunter (surgeon)">John Hunter</a> studied growing chickens. He observed bones grew at the ends and thus demonstrated the existence of the epiphyseal plates. Hunter is considered the "father of the growth plate".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
