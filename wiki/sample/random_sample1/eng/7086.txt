<p><b>Pridi Banomyong</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pridi_Phanomyong.2C_adapted_by_Chris_Baker_and_Pasuk_Phongpaichit_2000_13.E2.80.93The_Banomyong_Family_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pridi_Phanomyong.2C_adapted_by_Chris_Baker_and_Pasuk_Phongpaichit_2000_13.E2.80.93The_Banomyong_Family-1">[1]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th" xml:lang="th">ปรีดี พนมยงค์</span>; <a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_General_System_of_Transcription" title="Royal Thai General System of Transcription"><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">rtgs</span></a>: <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Thai transliteration"><b>Pridi Phanomyong</b></span>; <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Thai_and_Lao" title="Help:IPA for Thai and Lao">[priː.diː pʰa.nom.joŋ]</a></span>; 11 May 1900 – 2 May 1983) was a <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thai</a> politician. He was a <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Thailand" title="Prime Minister of Thailand">Prime Minister</a> and senior statesman of <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, and was named one of the world's great personalities of the 20th century by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_background">Family background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pridi_Banomyong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Family background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pridi wrote that his great-great-great grandfather, Heng, was a native of Etang Village in the <a href="/wiki/Chenghai_District" title="Chenghai District">Chenghai County</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guangdong_Province" title="Guangdong Province">Guangdong Province</a>, southern China, who came to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siam" title="Siam">Siam</a> during the reign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boromaracha_V" title="Boromaracha V">Boromaracha V</a> (r. 1758–1767), leaving behind his wife, who was pregnant with their son, Seng. Heng lived in Siam among the Chinese relatives of King <a href="/wiki/Taksin" title="Taksin">Taksin</a>, who recruited some of the local Chinese, including Heng, to fight against the Burmese invaders in 1767. Heng died in the service of the half-Chinese king. Taksin compensated Heng's family, after they sent a letter inquiring about him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Seng chose to live his life in China as a rice farmer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, Seng's son, Tan Nai Kok (陳盛于/陈盛于; Chen Chengyu; Tan Sêng-u),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E6.B3.B0.E5.9B.BD_.E6.B4.AA.E6.9E.97.2C_.E9.BB.8E.E9.81.93.E7.BA.B2.E4.B8.BB.E7.BC.96_2006_17_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E6.B3.B0.E5.9B.BD_.E6.B4.AA.E6.9E.97.2C_.E9.BB.8E.E9.81.93.E7.BA.B2.E4.B8.BB.E7.BC.96_2006_17-5">[5]</a></sup> emigrated to Siam in 1814, during the reign of King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddha_Loetla_Nabhalai" title="Buddha Loetla Nabhalai">Rama II</a>. Nai Kok settled in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ayutthaya_(city)" title="Ayutthaya (city)">Ayutthaya</a> and made his living by selling Chinese and Thai sweets; it is said he had made innovations by combining <a href="/wiki/Chinese_cuisine" title="Chinese cuisine">Chinese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thai_cuisine" title="Thai cuisine">Thai culinary</a> skills. A devout <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhist</a>, Nai Kok married a Thai woman named Pin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Pin's sister, Boonma, would become an ancestor of Pridi's wife Poonsuk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pridi_Phanomyong.2C_adapted_by_Chris_Baker_and_Pasuk_Phongpaichit_2000_19.E2.80.93The_Banomyong_Family_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pridi_Phanomyong.2C_adapted_by_Chris_Baker_and_Pasuk_Phongpaichit_2000_19.E2.80.93The_Banomyong_Family-7">[7]</a></sup> Their son, Nai Koet, married Khum, daughter of a wealthy Chinese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entrepreneur" title="Entrepreneur">entrepreneur</a>. When Nai Koet died, his wife directed that his remains were to be <a href="/wiki/Cremation" title="Cremation">cremated</a> and interred at the shrine at Phanomyong hill, which is the origin of their Thai surname.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Their son, Nai Siang, who became a wealthy rice merchant, married Lukchan; they were the parents of Pridi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pridi_Phanomyong.2C_adapted_by_Chris_Baker_and_Pasuk_Phongpaichit_2000_13.E2.80.93The_Banomyong_Family_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pridi_Phanomyong.2C_adapted_by_Chris_Baker_and_Pasuk_Phongpaichit_2000_13.E2.80.93The_Banomyong_Family-1">[1]</a></sup> Nai Siang adopted the surname Phanomyong in 1866.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pridi_Phanomyong.2C_adapted_by_Chris_Baker_and_Pasuk_Phongpaichit_2000_19.E2.80.93The_Banomyong_Family_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pridi_Phanomyong.2C_adapted_by_Chris_Baker_and_Pasuk_Phongpaichit_2000_19.E2.80.93The_Banomyong_Family-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pridi_Banomyong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pridi_Banomyong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pridi Phanomyong was born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ayutthaya_Province" title="Ayutthaya Province">Ayutthaya Province</a>, the second of five children. He had two half-siblings from his father's minor wife. In 1915, following the royal decree issued by King <a href="/wiki/Vajiravudh" title="Vajiravudh">Vajiravudh</a>, Pridi and his family dropped the "Nai" from their names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pridi_Phanomyong.2C_adapted_by_Chris_Baker_and_Pasuk_Phongpaichit_2000_13.E2.80.93The_Banomyong_Family_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pridi_Phanomyong.2C_adapted_by_Chris_Baker_and_Pasuk_Phongpaichit_2000_13.E2.80.93The_Banomyong_Family-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>After having graduated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LLB" title="LLB">LLB</a> from <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thai_Royal_College_of_Law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thai Royal College of Law (page does not exist)">Thai Royal College of Law</a>, he received a government scholarship to study <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a> and political economy to get <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">MA</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PhD" title="PhD">PhD</a> degrees at the <a href="/wiki/Sciences_Po" title="Sciences Po">Sciences Po</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, and earned his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PhD" title="PhD">PhD</a> in 1927 and returned to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siam" title="Siam">Siam</a> that year to work for the Ministry of Justice. He quickly rose in rank, and was granted the royal title <b><a href="/wiki/Thai_royal_and_noble_titles#Luang" title="Thai royal and noble titles">Luang</a> Praditmanutham</b> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th" xml:lang="th">หลวงประดิษฐ์มนูธรรม</span>). However, he also began assembling a group of fifty civil servants who wanted to replace to the absolute monarchy with a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="People.27s_Party">People's Party</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pridi_Banomyong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: People's Party">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siamese_Revolution_of_1932" title="Siamese Revolution of 1932">Siamese Revolution of 1932</a></div>
<p>On 24 June 1932, "Khana Ratsadon", the tiny <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Party_(Thailand)" title="People's Party (Thailand)">People's Party</a>, with Pridi as the leader of the civilian faction, carried out a lightning coup that abruptly ended 150 years of absolute monarchy under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chakri_Dynasty" title="Chakri Dynasty">Chakri Dynasty</a>.</p>
<p>In 1933, Pridi went into voluntary exile when his radical economic plans, which called for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nationalisation" title="Nationalisation">nationalisation</a> of all land and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_(economics)" title="Labor (economics)">labour</a>, were violently rejected by many.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Statesman">Statesman</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pridi_Banomyong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Statesman">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He returned in 1934 to found <a href="/wiki/Thammasat_University" title="Thammasat University">Thammasat University</a> as an open university, before assuming the posts of Minister of the Interior that year, Minister of Foreign Affairs in 1935, and Minister of Finance in 1938.</p>
<p>As Minister of Foreign Affairs from 1935 to 1937, Pridi signed treaties revoking the <a href="/wiki/Extraterritoriality" title="Extraterritoriality">extraterritorial rights</a> of 12 countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> With these treaties, Thailand was able to regain complete independence with regard to legal <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdiction</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxation" title="Taxation">taxation</a> for the first time since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unequal_treaties" title="Unequal treaties">unequal treaties</a> were signed under duress during the reign of King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rama_IV" title="Rama IV">Rama IV</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although he had been friends with Field Marshal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plaek_Pibulsonggram" title="Plaek Pibulsonggram">Phibunsongkhram</a> during the early days of the People's Party, the two fell out in the following years. Pridi was engaged in a double-dealing actions during the World War II as well as a violent left-leaning, and therefore opposed many of Phibun's militaristic policies which tended to be more conciliatory toward the Japanese. The antipathy between the two characters would define how Thailand fared by World War II when Japan was quickly consuming territory in Asia.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Free_Thai_movement">Free Thai movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pridi_Banomyong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Free Thai movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Free_Thai_Movement" title="Free Thai Movement">Free Thai Movement</a></div>
<p>On 8 December 1941, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_Japan" title="Imperial Japan">Imperial Japan</a> launched its attacks on <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied</a> possessions in the region, which resulted in the full development of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_War" title="Pacific War">Pacific War</a>. This included numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Invasion_of_Thailand" title="Japanese Invasion of Thailand">amphibious landings</a> in Thailand and an invasion across the border from <a href="/wiki/French_Indochina" title="French Indochina">French Indochina</a>. After initially resisting, the Thai government reluctantly agreed to let the Japanese pass through the country and use its military bases to strike other Allied possessions in the region, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Malaya" title="Battle of Malaya">Battle of Malaya</a>.</p>
<p>When Field Marshal <a href="/wiki/Plaek_Phibunsongkhram" title="Plaek Phibunsongkhram">Plaek Phibunsongkhram</a> issued a <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_war" title="Declaration of war">declaration of war</a> against <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> in January 1942, however, Pridi refused to sign it. As a result, he was effectively demoted by Phibun to the figurehead role of <a href="/wiki/Regent_of_Thailand" title="Regent of Thailand">Regent</a> for the young monarch, who was studying in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>. In this capacity, Pridi built up the anti-Japanese underground Free Thai Movement ("Seri Thai") network in Thailand. Codenamed "Ruth", he established contact with the Allies and the parallel Thai resistance organisations based in Britain and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. As the war progressed and the fortunes of the Japanese turned, public dissatisfaction grew and Phibun was forced to resign as prime minister in 1944. Eventually, in August 1944, the parliament chose Pridi to be the 'regent' as the king had left the country for doing his study in Switzerland.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khuang_Abhaiwongse" title="Khuang Abhaiwongse">Khuang Abhaiwongse</a>, a liberal lawyer and member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seri_Thai" title="Seri Thai">Seri Thai</a>, was chosen to be prime minister because of "his ability to dissemble with the Japanese" to shield the growing Seri Thai movement while at the same time improving surface relations with the Japanese occupiers.</p>
<p>When Japan's surrender ended the war, the Seri Thai-dominated government immediately acted to "restore the pre-war status quo". As regent, Pridi termed "the declaration of war illegal and null, and void" as improperly made, and repudiated all agreements made with Japan by Phibun.</p>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Louis_Mountbatten,_1st_Earl_Mountbatten_of_Burma" title="Louis Mountbatten, 1st Earl Mountbatten of Burma">Lord Louis Mountbatten</a>, the Supreme Commander, South East Asia, visited <a href="/wiki/Bangkok" title="Bangkok">Bangkok</a> in late 1945, he recorded a tribute to Pridi in which he said that there had existed a unique situation wherein "the Supreme Allied Commander was exchanging vital military plans with the Head of a State technically at war with us".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war_years">Post-war years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pridi_Banomyong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Post-war years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In December 1945 upon the return of King <a href="/wiki/Ananda_Mahidol" title="Ananda Mahidol">Ananda Mahidol</a> (King Rama VIII), Pridi was formally named a Senior Statesman (Ratthaburut Awuso), and served as a respected advisor to the post-war civilian governments of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tawee_Boonyaket" title="Tawee Boonyaket">Tawee Boonyaket</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seni_Pramoj" title="Seni Pramoj">Seni Pramoj</a>. Pridi was reluctant to accept the position because as a result of being appointed as a Senoir Stateman, he was automatically discharged from the regency of the king. The conflict between Pridi and the King was then aggravated.</p>
<p>In March 1946, Khuang, who had been elected Prime Minister in January, resigned. Pridi took the premiership instantly, the political situation was then spiralling out of control. It was during the first months of the Pridi government that the war crimes trial against Phibun was dismissed on a legal technicality.</p>
<p>After King Rama VIII refused to sign in the permanent Constitution drafted by Pridi, on the morning of 9 June 1946, the young king was found dead in his bed. The monarch's death resulted from a gunshot to the head, while in his bedroom in the Baromphiman Mansion in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Palace,_Bangkok" title="Grand Palace, Bangkok">Grand Palace</a>. After 4 months, in October 1946, a Commission of Inquiry reported the King's death could not have been accidental, but that neither suicide nor murder was satisfactorily proved.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.khunnamob.info/board/note.php?ref=m1ebrtzP" rel="nofollow">[1]</a></p>
<p>After a general election, Pridi resigned as prime minister, resumed his status of Senior Statesman, and left on a world tour, visiting Generalissimo <a href="/wiki/Chiang_Kai-shek" title="Chiang Kai-shek">Chiang Kai-shek</a> and United States President <a href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman" title="Harry S. Truman">Harry S. Truman</a> along the way.</p>
<p>On 8 November 1947, army troops seized various government installations in Bangkok. The coup, led by Lieutenant General <a href="/wiki/Phin_Choonhavan" title="Phin Choonhavan">Phin Choonhavan</a> and Colonel Kat Katsongkhram, ousted <a href="/wiki/Thawan_Thamrongnawasawat" title="Thawan Thamrongnawasawat">Thamrong</a>'s government. It marked the return to power of Phibun. At the same time, armoured cars arrived in front of Pridi's riverside residence. However, when the troops entered, they found that Pridi had already left. Pridi spent a week hiding with the Royal Thai Navy at Admiral Sindhu Songkhramchai's headquarters. On 20 November, the statesman was spirited out of the country by British and American agents to <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>.</p>
<p>Phibun arrested King Ananda's secretary, Senator Chaleo Patoomros, and two of his pages under charges of conspiracy to kill the King. Due to the fact that all of the suspects arrested were appointed to their positions by Pridi, rumours were then spread among the public that Pridi was part of a conspiracy involved in the alleged <a href="/wiki/Regicide" title="Regicide">regicide</a>, and that he had plans to turn Thailand into a republic. After a farcial trial, during which the entire defence counsel resigned and two members of a subsequent counsel were arrested under charges of treason, the judges ruled that none of the accused could have fired the fatal shot. However, it did convict one of pages, Chit Singhaseni, of being a party to the crime. Chit appealed his conviction. The Appeal Court later dismissed Chit's appeal and, undeterred by the legal doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Double_jeopardy" title="Double jeopardy">double jeopardy</a>, found the other page, But Pathamasarin, also guilty. The Supreme Court upheld the convictions, convicting Chaleo as well. All three were executed several years later.</p>
<p>According to biographer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=William_Stevenson_(biographer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="William Stevenson (biographer) (page does not exist)">William Stevenson</a>, King <a href="/wiki/Bhumibol_Adulyadej" title="Bhumibol Adulyadej">Bhumibol Adulyadej</a> has said he does not know whether Pridi was involved in his brother's death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> However, due to his testimonial in the court, he did not give any comment on the rumour of the conflict between Pridi and his faction, with Rama VIII.</p>
<p>Pridi also supported the Vietnamese independent movement of <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh" title="Ho Chi Minh">Ho Chi Minh</a>. As the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold_war" title="Cold war">cold war</a> shadowed the post-war arena of <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, Thailand apparently became a focus point of the world powers; USA &amp; Soviet Union. In 1949 when China became under the communists, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vietminh" title="Vietminh">Vietminh</a> ran the anti-French war, many, including USA, doubted that Pridi would lead Thailand to support the communist movement in the region. The policy of Pridi became controversial, leading to the coup which ousted him from power by his former ally, the wartime leader, <a href="/wiki/Plaek_Phibunsongkhram" title="Plaek Phibunsongkhram">Plaek Phibunsongkhram</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Permanent_exile">Permanent exile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pridi_Banomyong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Permanent exile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pridi secretly returned in 1949 in order to stage a coup d'état against Phibun's government. On 26 February 1949, Pridi and his disciples used Thammasat University to upload and store offensive weapons and armament. On the same day Pridi ordered his accomplice to cut down all communication used by the government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Pridi led the junto to seize the Grand Palace. The seize was done swiftly by force and with the help of his disciples within the courtier. However, the coup led by Pridi was a failure due to the late arrival of Pridi's associates in the Navy. When it failed, Pridi left for China, never to return to Thailand. From China, he travelled to <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, where he spent the remainder of his life. Pridi died on 2 May 1983, at his home in the suburbs of <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pridi_Banomyong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Pridi remains a controversial figure in Thai modern history. As one of the leaders in 1932 Pro-Democracy Coup, he has been viewed in many ways. The first declaration of the "revolution", which harshly attacked the king and his government, was written by Pridi himself. Nevertheless, Pridi held the position of regent when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rama_VIII" title="Rama VIII">Rama VIII</a> ascended to the throne.</p>
<p>During the period of military rule, Pridi was portrayed as a communist owing to the fact that several of his books and articles contained ideology and means similar to Marxism, Socialism, and Communism ideologies.</p>
<p>With obvious conflict between Pridi and the King Rama VIII, the young king's tragic death came to be blamed on Pridi. Pridi was accused of being the leader of a plot to assassinate the popular young monarch. This culminated in the military coup in 1957.</p>
<p>In his later years <a href="/wiki/Seni_Pramoj" title="Seni Pramoj">Seni Pramoj</a>, also promoted the idea that he had saved Thailand from a post-war British colonial rule that Pridi had been willing to accept.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Nigel Brailey treats the Free Thai movement as largely a sham and casts doubt on Pridi’s part, arguing “it appears questionable whether Pridi committed himself personally to the Allied cause much prior to August 1942, if even then,” suggesting that “his eventual anti-Japanese stance was a consequence primarily of his hostility to Phibun.” <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is no doubt that Pridi wanted to remove Phibun from power, and the war offered an opportunity to do so. However, there is no question that Pridi recognised well before the war that Thailand’s alignment with the Axis powers would work to Phibun’s advantage and enable him to strengthen his <a href="/wiki/Dictatorship" title="Dictatorship">dictatorship</a>. Even the Japanese recognised Pridi’s hostility, which is why he was forced out of the cabinet in December 1941. It was the reason every knowledgeable person on the Allied side, from Seni Pramoj and Prince Suphasawat, a chief organiser of the movement in Great Britain, to former British ambassador <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Josiah_Crosby&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Josiah Crosby (page does not exist)">Josiah Crosby</a>, anticipated that Pridi would emerge as the head of a domestic resistance movement.</p>
<p>One time conservative monarchist <a href="/wiki/Sulak_Sivaraksa" title="Sulak Sivaraksa">Sulak Sivaraksa</a> has emerged as Pridi’s most ardent champion. A prolific critic of the Thai status quo, Sulak, in addition to praising the achievements of the Free Thai in saving Thailand’s sovereignty, has criticised Seni and his Democrat Party for alleged complicity in the military’s return to power in 1947.</p>
<p>Sulak led efforts to rehabilitate Pridi which achieved significant results. Four Bangkok streets now are named for him: three as Pridi Banomyong Road and one called Praditmanutham Road (his royally granted title). His birthday, 11 May, is now celebrated as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pridi_Banomyong_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pridi Banomyong Day (page does not exist)">Pridi Banomyong Day</a>. In 1997 the Thai government dedicated a park in eastern Bangkok to the Free Thai resistance movement. On 16 August 2003, a library/museum, built as a replica of Pridi’s wartime residence, opened at the park.</p>
<p>There are two Pridi Banomyong Memorials, one in Pridi's hometown, the other on the campus of Thammasat University, which he had founded. Thammasat is home to the Pridi Banomyong Library and the Pridi Banomyong International College. The law faculty at <a href="/wiki/Dhurakij_Pundit_University" title="Dhurakij Pundit University">Dhurakij Pundit University</a> is called the Pridi Banomyong Faculty of Law. The Pridi (<i>Chloropsis aurifrons pridii</i>), a species of <a href="/wiki/Leafbird" title="Leafbird">leafbird</a>, and Pridi Banomyong Institute, a non-profit academic organisation, are also named in his honour. The Pridi Banomyong Institute holds an annual Pridi Banomyong Lecture, initially on Pridi Banomyong Day, but moved in recent years to 24 June, in honour of his role in the 1932 coup.</p>
<p>The <a class="extiw" href="https://th.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E0%B8%96%E0%B8%99%E0%B8%99%E0%B8%AA%E0%B8%B8%E0%B8%82%E0%B8%B8%E0%B8%A1%E0%B8%A7%E0%B8%B4%E0%B8%97_71_(%E0%B8%9B%E0%B8%A3%E0%B8%B5%E0%B8%94%E0%B8%B5_%E0%B8%9E%E0%B8%99%E0%B8%A1%E0%B8%A2%E0%B8%87%E0%B8%84%E0%B9%8C)" title="th:ถนนสุขุมวิท 71 (ปรีดี พนมยงค์)">Sukhumvit Soi 71</a> road in Bangkok is named in his honour.</p>
