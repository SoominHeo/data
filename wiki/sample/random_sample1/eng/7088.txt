<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ci (poetry)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><i><b>Ci</b></i> (<i><b>tzu</b></i>; <a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">词</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">詞</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">cí</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="Wade–Giles">Wade–Giles</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-wadegile" xml:lang="zh-Latn-wadegile">w=tzʻŭ<sup>2</sup></span></i>, and, interchangeably –– to a point –– with 辭/辞) are a poetic form, a type of <a href="/wiki/Lyric_poetry" title="Lyric poetry">lyric poetry</a>, done in the tradition of <a href="/wiki/Classical_Chinese_poetry" title="Classical Chinese poetry">Classical Chinese poetry</a>. <i>Ci</i> use a set of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poetic_meter" title="Poetic meter">poetic meters</a> derived from a base set of certain patterns, in fixed-rhythm, fixed-tone, and variable line-length formal types, or model examples: the rhythmic and tonal pattern of the <i>ci</i> are based upon certain, definitive musical song tunes. They are also known as <i>Changduanju</i> (長短句/长短句, "lines of irregular lengths") and <i>Shiyu</i> (詩餘/诗馀, "that which is beside poetry").</p>
<p>Typically the number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_character" title="Chinese character">characters</a> in each line and the arrangement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tonal_language" title="Tonal language">tones</a> were determined by one of around 800 set patterns, each associated with a particular title, called <i>cípái</i> 詞牌. Originally they were written to be sung to a tune of that title, with set rhythm, <a href="/wiki/Rhyme" title="Rhyme">rhyme</a>, and tempo. Therefore, the title may have nothing to do with its contents, and it is common for several <i>ci</i> to share the same title, having little or nothing to do with the topics of those poems, but rather refers to their shared rhythmic and tonal patterns. Some <i>ci</i> would have a "subtitle" (or a commentary, sometimes as long as a paragraph) indicating the contents. Sometimes, for the sake of clarity, a <i>ci</i> is listed under its title plus its first line.</p>
<p><i>Ci</i> most often express feelings of desire, often in an adopted <a href="/wiki/Persona" title="Persona">persona</a>, but the greatest exponents of the form (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Li_Houzhu" title="Li Houzhu">Li Houzhu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Su_Shi" title="Su Shi">Su Shi</a>) used it to address a wide range of topics.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ci_(poetry)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the oldest surviving textual examples of surviving <i>ci</i> are from 8th century CE <a href="/wiki/Dunhuang_manuscripts" title="Dunhuang manuscripts">Dunhuang manuscripts</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> beginning in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Six_dynasties_poetry" title="Six dynasties poetry">poetry</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liang_Dynasty" title="Liang Dynasty">Liang Dynasty</a>, the <i>ci</i> followed the tradition of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shi_Jing" title="Shi Jing">Shi Jing</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Yuefu" title="Yuefu">yuefu</a></i>: they were lyrics which developed from anonymous popular songs into a sophisticated literary genre; although in the case of the <i>ci</i> form some of its fixed-rhythm patterns have an origin in <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>. The form was further developed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tang_Dynasty" title="Tang Dynasty">Tang Dynasty</a>. Although the contributions of <a href="/wiki/Li_Bai" title="Li Bai">Li Bo</a> (also known as Li Po, 701 – 762) are fraught with historical doubt, certainly the Tang poet <a href="/wiki/Wen_Tingyun" title="Wen Tingyun">Wen Tingyun</a> (812-870) was a great master of the <i>ci</i>, writing it in its distinct and mature form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> One of the more notable practitioners and developers of this form was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Li_Houzhu" title="Li Houzhu">Li Yu</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Tang" title="Southern Tang">Southern Tang</a> Dynasty during the <a href="/wiki/Five_Dynasties_and_Ten_Kingdoms_period" title="Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period">Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period</a>. However, the <i>ci</i> form of Classical Chinese poetry is especially associated with the <a href="/wiki/Song_poetry" title="Song poetry">poetry of the Song Dynasty</a>, during which it was indeed a popular poetic form. A revival of the <i>ci</i> poetry form occurred during the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a> and the beginning of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qing_Dynasty" title="Qing Dynasty">Qing Dynasty</a> which was characterized by an exploration of the emotions connected with romantic love together with its valorization, often in a context of a brief poetic story narrative within a <i>ci</i> poem or a linked group of <i>ci</i> poems in an application of the <i><a href="/wiki/Chuanqi" title="Chuanqi">chuanqi</a></i> form of short story tales to poetry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ci_(poetry)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two main categories of <i>ci</i> employed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" title="Song Dynasty">Song Dynasty</a> were <i>xiǎolìng</i> 小令 (the original form since Pre-Song) and <i>màncí</i> 慢詞 (starting after <a href="/wiki/Liu_Yong_(Song_dynasty)" title="Liu Yong (Song dynasty)">Liu Yong</a>), depending on the song being either short and in fast tempo or long and in slow tempo. Later in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qing_Dynasty" title="Qing Dynasty">Qing Dyansties</a>, the <i>ci</i>, or rather the <i>cipai</i>, are classified for the number of characters it dictates. It's called <i>xiǎolìng</i> 小令 if it's no more than 58 characters, <i>zhōngdiào</i> 中調 for 59-90, and <i>chángdiào</i> 長調 for over 90. If the <i>ci</i> appears in one stanza, it's called <i>dāndiào</i> 單調, mostly <i>xiǎolìng</i> written in Pre-Song era. The largest majority is <i>shuāngdiào</i> 雙調 with two stanzas or <i>qüè</i> 闋 in identical or nearly identical patterns. There also are rare cases of <i>sāndié</i> 三疊 and <i>sìdié</i> 四疊, for three and four <i>qüè</i>, respectively. In terms of style, <i>ci</i> can also be classified as either <i>wǎnyuē</i> 婉約 (grace) or <i>háofàng</i> 豪放 (bold).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cipai">Cipai</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ci_(poetry)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cipai">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most <i>cipai</i> consist of three characters. The literal meaning of a <i>cipai</i> can be rather obscure, making it difficult to translate. Some are taken straight from earlier poems, and some are clearly of Non-Han origin — mostly songs introduced from Central Asia. Some <i>cipai</i> have alternative names, usually taken from a famous piece of that very <i>cipai</i>. There also are variants of certain <i>cipai</i>, indicated by a prefix or a suffix.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Example">Example</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ci_(poetry)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Example">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>To the Tune of Riverside City - For ten years here I wander and there you lie</b></p>
<ul>
<li><span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">「十年生死兩茫茫。不思量，自難忘。千里孤墳，無處話淒涼。縱使相逢應不識，塵滿面、鬢如霜。 夜來幽夢忽還鄉。小軒窗，正梳妝。相顧無言，惟有淚千行。料得年年腸斷處：明月夜，短松岡。」</span>
<dl>
<dd>“For ten years here I wander and there you lie./ I don't think about you often,/ yet how can I forget you!/ With your grave a thousand miles away,/ where can I confide my loneliness?/ Even if we met, could you recognize me,/ with dust all over my face/ and hair like frost?/ Last night I had a dream in which I returned home./ By the window,/ you were combing your hair./ We looked at each other silently,/ with tears streaming down our cheeks./ There's a place which every year will be my misery：/ the moonlit night,/ the hill of short pines. 「Su Shi, 《江城子·十年生死兩茫茫》，蘇軾」</dd>
</dl>
</li>
</ul>
<p>In the title of this <i>ci</i>, "the Tune of Riverside City" is the <i>cipai</i>, followed by the first sentence of the poem. Su Shi got married when he was 19, his wife 16. His wife died when she was only 27. Because of his government duties, Su Shi had moved to many different places in China, all far away from his hometown. One night in early 1075, about 10 years after her death, Su Shi dreamed of his wife, then composed this famous <i>ci</i>.</p>
