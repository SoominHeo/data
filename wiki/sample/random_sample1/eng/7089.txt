<p><b>Certiorari</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɜːr/ 'ur' in 'fur'">ɜːr</span><span title="/ʃ/ 'sh' in 'shy'">ʃ</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɛər/ 'are' in 'bare'">ɛər</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/-/ affix">-</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɛər/ 'are' in 'bare'">ɛər</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>, or <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/-/ affix">-</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxf_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-oxf-2">[2]</a></sup>), often abbreviated as <b>cert.</b> in the United States, is a <a href="/wiki/Writ" title="Writ">writ</a> seeking <a href="/wiki/Judicial_review" title="Judicial review">judicial review</a>. It is issued by a <a href="/wiki/Superior_court" title="Superior court">superior court</a>, directing an inferior court, tribunal, or other public authority to send the record of a proceeding for review.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Certiorari&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term comes from the words used at the beginning of these writs when they were written in Latin: <i>certiorārī (volumus)</i> "(we wish) to be informed". <i>Certiorari</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Present_tense" title="Present tense">present</a> <a href="/wiki/Passive_voice" title="Passive voice">passive</a> <a href="/wiki/Infinitive" title="Infinitive">infinitive</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> verb <i>certioro, certiorare</i> ("to inform, apprise, show").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxf_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-oxf-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_and_modern_jurisdictions">Historical and modern jurisdictions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Certiorari&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical and modern jurisdictions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historical usage dates back to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Law" title="Roman Law">Roman Law</a>. In modern law, certiorari is recognized in many jurisdictions, including <a href="/wiki/Law_of_the_United_States" title="Law of the United States">the United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/English_law" title="English law">the United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Canada" title="Law of Canada">Canada</a>, India and <a href="/wiki/Philippine_legal_codes" title="Philippine legal codes">the Philippines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Rome">Ancient Rome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Certiorari&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ancient Rome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In Roman law, certiorari was suggested in terms of reviewing a case—much as the term is applied today—although the term was also used in writing to indicate the need or duty to inform other parties of a court's ruling. It was a highly technical term appearing only in jurisprudential Latin, most frequently in the works of <a href="/wiki/Ulpian" title="Ulpian">Ulpian</a>.</p>
<p>The term <i>certiorari</i> is often found in Roman literature on law but applied in a philosophical rather than tangible manner when concerning the action of review of a case or aspects of a case. Essentially, it states that the case will be heard.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_law_and_Commonwealth">Common law and Commonwealth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Certiorari&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Common law and Commonwealth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a>, certiorari was a supervisory writ, serving to keep "all inferior jurisdictions within the bounds of their authority ... [protecting] the liberty of the subject, by speedy and summary interposition".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In England and Wales, and separately in Northern Ireland, the Court of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King%27s_Bench" title="King's Bench">King's Bench</a> was tasked with the duty of supervising all lower courts, and had power to issue all writs necessary for the discharge of that duty; the justices of that Court appeared to have no discretion as to whether it was heard, as long as an application for a bill of certiorari met established criteria, as it arose from their duty of supervision.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The underlying policy is that all inferior courts and authorities have only limited jurisdiction or powers and must be kept within their legal bounds. This is the concern of the Crown, for the sake of orderly administration of justice, but it is a private complaint which sets the Crown in motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>As Associate Justice <a href="/wiki/James_Wilson" title="James Wilson">James Wilson</a>, the person primarily responsible for the drafting of <a href="/wiki/Article_Three_of_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Article Three of the United States Constitution">Article Three of the United States Constitution</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> explains:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In every judicial department, well arranged and well organized, there should be a regular, progressive, gradation of jurisdiction; and one supreme tribunal should superintend and govern all the others.</p>
<p>An arrangement in this manner is proper for two reasons:</p>
<ol>
<li>The supreme tribunal produces and preserves a uniformity of decision through the whole judicial system.</li>
<li>It confines and supports every inferior court within the limits of its just jurisdiction.</li>
</ol>
<p>If no superintending tribunal of this nature were established, different courts might adopt different and even contradictory rules of decision; and the distractions, springing from these different and contradictory rules, would be without remedy and without end. Opposite determinations of the same question, in different courts, would be equally final and irreversible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Consistent with Justice Wilson's explanation, the power to issue writs of certiorari is invested in the highest court of every Commonwealth jurisdiction, in some way, shape, or manner. While some incorporate this remedy into their Constitutions, e.g., India,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> others treat it as an implied power of <a href="/wiki/Superior_court" title="Superior court">superior courts</a>, e.g., Australia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> but in all Commonwealth jurisdictions—as distinguished from their American counterpart—it has evolved into a general remedy for the correction of plain error, to bring decisions of an inferior court or tribunal or public authority before the superior court for review so that the court can determine whether to quash such decisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p><span id="United_States_law"></span></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Certiorari&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Federal_courts">Federal courts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Certiorari&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Federal courts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_the_United_States" title="Law of the United States">United States</a>, certiorari is most often seen as the writ that the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court of the United States</a> issues to a lower court to review the lower court's judgment for legal error (reversible error) and review where no <a href="/wiki/Appeal" title="Appeal">appeal</a> is available as a matter of right. Before the <a href="/wiki/Judiciary_Act_of_1891" title="Judiciary Act of 1891">Judiciary Act of 1891</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> the cases that could reach the Supreme Court were heard as a matter of right, meaning that the Court was required to issue a decision in each of those cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> That is, the Court had to review all properly presented appeals on the merits, hear oral argument, and issue decisions. As the United States expanded in the nineteenth century, the federal judicial system became increasingly strained, and the Supreme Court, which routinely took three months off every year, not surprisingly had a backlog of cases several years long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The Act solved these problems by transferring most of the court's direct appeals to the newly created Circuit Courts of Appeals, whose decisions in those cases would normally be final.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The Supreme Court did not completely give up its judiciary authority, however, because it gained the ability to review the decisions of the courts of appeals at its discretion through writ of certiorari.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the <a href="/wiki/Judiciary_Act_of_1925" title="Judiciary Act of 1925">Judiciary Act of 1925</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_Case_Selections_Act" title="Supreme Court Case Selections Act">Supreme Court Case Selections Act</a> of 1988,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> most cases cannot be appealed to the Supreme Court of the United States as a matter of right. A party who wants the Supreme Court to review a decision of a federal or state court files a "petition for writ of certiorari" in the Supreme Court. A "petition" is printed in booklet format and 40 copies are filed with the Court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> If the Court <a href="/wiki/Procedures_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States#Selection_of_cases" title="Procedures of the Supreme Court of the United States">grants</a> the petition, the case is scheduled for the filing of briefs and for oral argument. A minimum of four of the nine Justices is required to grant a writ of certiorari, referred to as the "<a href="/wiki/Rule_of_four" title="Rule of four">rule of four</a>". The court denies the vast majority of petitions and thus leaves the decision of the lower court to stand without review; it takes roughly 80 to 150 cases each term. In the term that concluded in June 2009, for example, 8,241 petitions were filed, with a grant rate of approximately 1.1 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Cases on the paid certiorari docket are substantially more likely to be granted than those on the <i><a href="/wiki/In_forma_pauperis" title="In forma pauperis">in forma pauperis</a></i> docket.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The Supreme Court is generally careful to choose only cases over which the Court has <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdiction</a> and which the Court considers sufficiently important, such as cases involving deep constitutional questions, to merit the use of its limited resources. See also <a href="/wiki/Cert_pool" title="Cert pool">Cert pool</a>. While both appeals of right and cert petitions often present several alleged errors of the lower courts for appellate review, the Court normally grants review only of one or two questions presented in a certiorari petition.</p>
<p>The Supreme Court sometimes grants a writ of certiorari to resolve a "<a href="/wiki/Circuit_split" title="Circuit split">circuit split</a>", when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_court_of_appeals" title="United States court of appeals">federal appeals courts</a> in two (or more) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_federal_judicial_circuit" title="United States federal judicial circuit">federal judicial circuits</a> have ruled differently in similar situations. These are often called "percolating issues."</p>
<p>Certiorari is sometimes informally referred to as <i>cert.</i>, and cases warranting the Supreme Court's attention as "cert. worthy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The granting of a writ does not necessarily mean that the Supreme Court disagrees with the decision of the lower court. Granting a writ of certiorari means merely that at least four of the justices have determined that the circumstances described in the petition are sufficient to warrant review by the Court.</p>
<p>Conversely, the Supreme Court's denial of a petition for a writ of certiorari is sometimes misunderstood as implying that the Supreme Court approves the decision of the lower court. However, as the Court explained in <i><a href="/wiki/Missouri_v._Jenkins" title="Missouri v. Jenkins">Missouri v. Jenkins</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> such a denial "imports no expression of opinion upon the merits of the case[.]" In particular, a denial of a writ of certiorari means that no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binding_precedent" title="Binding precedent">binding precedent</a> is created by the denial itself, and the lower court's decision is treated as mandatory authority only within the geographical (or in the case of the Federal Circuit, subject-specific) jurisdiction of that court. The reasons for why a denial of certiorari cannot be treated as implicit approval were set forth in <i><a href="/wiki/Maryland_v._Baltimore_Radio_Show,_Inc." title="Maryland v. Baltimore Radio Show, Inc.">Maryland v. Baltimore Radio Show, Inc.</a></i> (1950), in which the Court explained the many rationales which could underlie the denial of a writ which have nothing to do with the merits of the case.</p>
<p>Since the vast majority of petitions for certiorari are routinely denied without comment, it is normally unnecessary to indicate that fact in citations to decisions of lower federal courts, unless it happened within the last two years or was otherwise particularly relevant (e.g., to support an inference that a particular appellant is a <a href="/wiki/Vexatious_litigation" title="Vexatious litigation">vexatious litigant</a>).</p>
<p>In legal citations, "cert. granted <i>sub nom</i>" is an abbreviation of the legal phrase "certiorari granted <i>sub nomine</i>", meaning "judicial review granted, under name", indicating that a petition for certiorari of a case has been granted, but that the court granting certiorari is hearing the case under a different name than the name under which the subordinate courts heard the case. For example, the case of <i><a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia_v._Heller" title="District of Columbia v. Heller">District of Columbia v. Heller</a></i> was known as <i>Parker v. District of Columbia</i> in the court below.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="State_courts">State courts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Certiorari&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: State courts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">United States state</a> court systems use the same terminology, but in others, <i>writ of review</i>, <i>leave to appeal</i>, or <i>certification for appeal</i> is used in place of <i>writ of certiorari</i> as the name for <a href="/wiki/Discretionary_review" title="Discretionary review">discretionary review</a> of a lower court's judgment. A handful of states lack intermediate appellate courts; in most of these, their supreme courts operate under a mandatory review regime, in which the supreme court must take all appeals in order to preserve the loser's traditional right to one appeal (except in criminal cases where the defendant was acquitted). Virginia has an intermediate appeals court, but operates under discretionary review except in family law and administrative cases. Mandatory review remains in place in all states where the <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment_in_the_United_States" title="Capital punishment in the United States">death penalty</a> exists; in those states, a sentence of death is automatically appealed to the state's highest court.</p>
<p>In two states without an intermediate appeals court—New Hampshire and West Virginia—the supreme court used to operate under discretionary review in all cases, whether civil or criminal. This meant that there was no right of appeal in either state, with the only exception being death penalty cases in New Hampshire. (West Virginia abolished its death penalty in 1965.) However, New Hampshire transitioned to mandatory review for the vast majority of cases beginning in 2004,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> while West Virginia transitioned to mandatory review for all cases beginning in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Texas is an unusual exception to the rule that denial of certiorari by the state supreme court normally does not imply approval or disapproval of the merits of the lower court's decision. In March 1927, the Texas Legislature enacted a law directing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Texas_Supreme_Court" title="Texas Supreme Court">Texas Supreme Court</a> to summarily <i>refuse</i> to hear applications for writs of error when it believed the Court of Appeals opinion correctly stated the law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steiner_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Steiner-26">[26]</a></sup> Thus, since June 1927, over 4,100 decisions of the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Courts_of_Appeals" title="Texas Courts of Appeals">Texas Courts of Appeals</a> have become valid binding precedent of the Texas Supreme Court itself because the high court refused applications for writ of error rather than denying them and thereby signaled that it approved of their holdings as the law of the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steiner_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Steiner-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Texas' unique practice saved the state supreme court from having to hear relatively minor cases just to create uniform statewide precedents on those issues, it also makes for lengthy citations to the opinions of the Courts of Appeals, since the subsequent writ history of the case must always be noted (e.g., no writ, writ refused, writ denied, etc.) in order for the reader to determine at a glance whether the cited opinion is binding precedent only in the district of the Court of Appeals in which it was decided, or binding precedent for the entire state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steiner_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-Steiner-26">[26]</a></sup> In contrast, California<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and New York<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> solved the problem of creating uniform precedent by simply holding that the first intermediate appellate court to reach a novel question of law always sets binding precedent for the entire state, unless and until another intermediate appellate court expressly disagrees with the first one.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Administrative_law">Administrative law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Certiorari&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Administrative law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Administrative_law" title="Administrative law">administrative law</a> context, the <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common-law</a> writ of certiorari was historically used by lower courts in the United States for <a href="/wiki/Judicial_review" title="Judicial review">judicial review</a> of decisions made by an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Administrative_agency" title="Administrative agency">administrative agency</a> after an adversarial hearing. Some states have retained this use of the writ of certiorari in state courts, while others have replaced it with statutory procedures. In the federal courts, this use of certiorari has been abolished and replaced by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_action" title="Civil action">civil action</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Administrative_Procedure_Act_(United_States)" title="Administrative Procedure Act (United States)">Administrative Procedure Act</a> in a <a href="/wiki/United_States_district_court" title="United States district court">United States district court</a> or in some circumstances a petition for review in a United States court of appeals.</p>
