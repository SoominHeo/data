<p><b>93 Minerva</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ɜːr/ 'ur' in 'fur'">ɜːr</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i>mi-<b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">NUR</span></span></b>-və</i></a></span>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Minor-planet_moon" title="Minor-planet moon">trinary</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Main-belt" title="Main-belt">main-belt</a> <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/C-type_asteroid" title="C-type asteroid">C-type asteroid</a>, meaning that it has a dark surface and possibly a primitive <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonaceous</a> composition. It was discovered by <a href="/wiki/James_Craig_Watson" title="James Craig Watson">J. C. Watson</a> on August 24, 1867, and named after <a href="/wiki/Minerva" title="Minerva">Minerva</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Roman_mythology" title="Roman mythology">Roman</a> equivalent of <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">Athena</a>, goddess of wisdom. An <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">occultation</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a> by Minerva was observed in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> on November 22, 1982. An occultation diameter of ~170 km was measured from the observations. Since then two more occultations have been observed, which give an estimated mean diameter of ~150 km for diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Satellites">Satellites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=93_Minerva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Satellites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On August 16, 2009, at 13:36 <a href="/wiki/Universal_Time" title="Universal Time">UT</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keck_Observatory" title="Keck Observatory">Keck Observatory</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_optics" title="Adaptive optics">adaptive optics</a> system revealed that the asteroid 93 Minerva possesses 2 small moons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marchis2009_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marchis2009-7">[7]</a></sup> They are 4 and 3 km in diameter and the projected separations from Minerva correspond to 630 km (8.8 x Rprimary) and 380 km (5.2 x Rprimary) respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marchis2009_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Marchis2009-7">[7]</a></sup> They have been named <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aegis_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aegis (moon) (page does not exist)">Aegis</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gorgoneion_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gorgoneion (moon) (page does not exist)">Gorgoneion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
