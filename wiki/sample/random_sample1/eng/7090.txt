<p><i><b>A Man for All Seasons</b></i> is a 1966 British <a href="/wiki/Biographical_film" title="Biographical film">biographical</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drama_film" title="Drama film">drama film</a> in <a href="/wiki/Technicolor" title="Technicolor">Technicolor</a> based on <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bolt" title="Robert Bolt">Robert Bolt</a>'s <a href="/wiki/A_Man_for_All_Seasons" title="A Man for All Seasons">play of the same name</a> and adapted for the big screen by Bolt himself. It was released on 12 December 1966. It was directed by <a href="/wiki/Fred_Zinnemann" title="Fred Zinnemann">Fred Zinnemann</a>, who had previously directed the films <i><a href="/wiki/High_Noon" title="High Noon">High Noon</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/From_Here_to_Eternity" title="From Here to Eternity">From Here to Eternity</a></i>. The film won six <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Oscars</a> at the <a href="/wiki/39th_Academy_Awards" title="39th Academy Awards">39th Academy Awards</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Picture" title="Academy Award for Best Picture">Best Picture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Director" title="Academy Award for Best Director">Best Director</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actor" title="Academy Award for Best Actor">Best Actor</a>.</p>
<p>The film and play both depict the final years of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Thomas_More" title="Sir Thomas More">Sir Thomas More</a>, the 16th-century <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor" title="Lord Chancellor">Lord Chancellor</a> of England who refused to sign a letter asking <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_VII" title="Pope Clement VII">Pope Clement VII</a> to annul King <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII of England</a>'s marriage to <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Aragon" title="Catherine of Aragon">Catherine of Aragon</a> and resigned rather than take an <a href="/wiki/Oath_of_Supremacy" title="Oath of Supremacy">Oath of Supremacy</a> declaring Henry VIII <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Head_of_the_Church_of_England" title="Supreme Head of the Church of England">Supreme Head of the Church of England</a>. Both the play and the film portray More as a <a href="/wiki/Tragic_hero" title="Tragic hero">tragic hero</a>, motivated by his devout Roman Catholic faith and envied by rivals, such as <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Cromwell" title="Thomas Cromwell">Thomas Cromwell</a>. He is also deeply loved by his family and respected by the common people. The film's story is set between 1529 and 1535, at the high point of the reign of Henry VIII of England. <a href="/wiki/Paul_Scofield" title="Paul Scofield">Paul Scofield</a>, who had played More in the West End stage premiere, also took the role in the film, and is joined by <a href="/wiki/Wendy_Hiller" title="Wendy Hiller">Wendy Hiller</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Shaw_(British_actor)" title="Robert Shaw (British actor)">Robert Shaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orson_Welles" title="Orson Welles">Orson Welles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Susannah_York" title="Susannah York">Susannah York</a>. Also appearing are <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Davenport" title="Nigel Davenport">Nigel Davenport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leo_McKern" title="Leo McKern">Leo McKern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corin_Redgrave" title="Corin Redgrave">Corin Redgrave</a> and, in one of his earliest screen roles, <a href="/wiki/John_Hurt" title="John Hurt">John Hurt</a>.</p>
<p><i>A Man for All Seasons</i> was a critical and box office success. The film ranked number 43 on the <a href="/wiki/British_Film_Institute" title="British Film Institute">British Film Institute</a>'s list of the <a href="/wiki/BFI_Top_100_British_films" title="BFI Top 100 British films">top 100 British films</a>. In 1995, on the occasion of the 100th anniversary of cinema, the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Vatican</a> listed it among the <a href="/wiki/Vatican%27s_list_of_films#Religion" title="Vatican's list of films">greatest movies of all time</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Title">Title</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Man_for_All_Seasons_(1966_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Title">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The title reflects playwright Bolt's portrayal of More as the ultimate man of <a href="/wiki/Conscience" title="Conscience">conscience</a> and as remaining true to his principles and religion under all circumstances and at all times. Bolt borrowed the title from <a href="/wiki/Robert_Whittington" title="Robert Whittington">Robert Whittington</a>, a contemporary of More, who in 1520 wrote of him:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>More is a man of an angel's wit and singular learning. I know not his fellow. For where is the man of that gentleness, lowliness and affability? And, as time requireth, a man of marvelous mirth and pastimes, and sometime of as sad gravity. A man for all seasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Man_for_All_Seasons_(1966_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_Wolsey" title="Cardinal Wolsey">Cardinal Wolsey</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor" title="Lord Chancellor">Lord Chancellor</a> of England, summons Sir Thomas More to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hampton_Court" title="Hampton Court">Hampton Court</a>, one of the principal royal palaces in the vicinity of London, for a private, late-night meeting. As both men know, King Henry VIII wishes to divorce his wife <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Aragon" title="Catherine of Aragon">Catherine</a> and marry <a href="/wiki/Anne_Boleyn" title="Anne Boleyn">Anne Boleyn</a>. Saying that England needs a male heir to prevent another period of dynastic wars like the <a href="/wiki/Wars_of_the_Roses" title="Wars of the Roses">Wars of the Roses</a> and that Henry's present queen cannot provide one, Wolsey chastises More for being the only member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Privy_Council" title="Privy Council">Privy Council</a> to oppose Wolsey's policies to obtain a divorce from the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Vatican</a>. More states that he can never go along with Wolsey's suggestion that they apply "pressure" on Church property and revenue in England to force the issue. Unknown to More, the conversation is overheard by Wolsey's aide, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Cromwell" title="Thomas Cromwell">Thomas Cromwell</a>.</p>
<p>Returning to his home at <a href="/wiki/Chelsea,_London" title="Chelsea, London">Chelsea</a> at dawn, More finds <a href="/wiki/Richard_Rich,_1st_Baron_Rich" title="Richard Rich, 1st Baron Rich">Richard Rich</a>, waiting by the dock for his return, lobbying for a position at Court. More recommends against that, citing many corruptions, and instead recommends Rich find a job as a teacher. Rich declines More's advice, saying that has little prestige.</p>
<p>More enters his house to find his daughter <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Roper" title="Margaret Roper">Meg</a> chatting with a brilliant young lawyer named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Roper_(biographer)" title="William Roper (biographer)">William Roper</a>, who announces his desire to marry her. The devoutly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> More states that he cannot give his blessing as long as Roper remains a <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheran</a>.</p>
<p>Some time later, Wolsey dies of a heart attack. King Henry appoints More as <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor" title="Lord Chancellor">Lord Chancellor</a> of England to succeed Wolsey.</p>
<p>The King makes an "impromptu" visit to the More estate to inquire about his divorce. More remains unmoved as Henry alternates between threats, tantrums, and promises of unbounded Royal favour. After the King leaves, Cromwell promises Rich a position at Court in return for damaging information about More.</p>
<p>Roper, learning of More's quarrel with the King, reveals that his religious opinions have altered considerably: He declares that by attacking the Church, the King has become "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Devil" title="The Devil">the Devil</a>'s minister." More admonishes him to be more guarded as Rich arrives, pleading again for a position at Court. When More again refuses, Rich denounces More's steward as a spy for Cromwell. An unmoved More responds, "Of course, that's one of my servants."</p>
<p>Humiliated, Rich joins Cromwell in attempting to bring down More. Meanwhile, the King has Parliament declare him "Supreme Head of the Church of England." He demands that both the bishops and Parliament renounce all allegiance to the Pope. More quietly resigns as Lord Chancellor rather than accept the new order. As he does so, his close friend, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Howard,_3rd_Duke_of_Norfolk" title="Thomas Howard, 3rd Duke of Norfolk">Thomas Howard, 3rd Duke of Norfolk</a>, attempts to draw his opinions out as part of a friendly chat with no witnesses present. More, however, knows that the time for speaking openly of such matters is over.</p>
<p>Cromwell, in a meeting with Norfolk, implies that More's troubles would be over were he to attend the King's wedding to Anne Boleyn. When More declines the invitation, he is summoned again to Hampton Court, now occupied by Cromwell. More is interrogated, but refuses to answer. Infuriated, Cromwell declares that the King views him as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traitor" title="Traitor">traitor</a>, but allows him to return home.</p>
<p>Upon returning home, Meg informs her father that a <a href="/wiki/Oath_of_Supremacy" title="Oath of Supremacy">new oath</a> is being circulated and that all must take it or face charges of <a href="/wiki/High_treason" title="High treason">high treason</a>. Initially, More says he would be willing to take the oath, provided it refers only to the King's marriage to Boleyn. Upon learning that it names the King as Supreme Head of the Church, More refuses to take it and is subsequently imprisoned in the <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_London" title="Tower of London">Tower of London</a>.</p>
<p>More remains steadfast in his refusal to take the Oath and refuses to explain his objections, knowing that he cannot be convicted without a stated reason. A request for new books to read backfires, resulting in confiscation of the books he has, and Rich removes them from More's cell.</p>
<p>More is finally brought to trial, but refuses to speak about the marriage or why he will not take the Oath, and cites his silence in defense. Rich then testifies that when he came to take away More's books, More told him he would not take the Oath because the King could not be Head of the Church, thus committing treason by contradicting the Act of Supremacy. More is convicted of treason on the <a href="/wiki/Perjury" title="Perjury">perjured</a> testimony of Rich, who has been made Attorney General for <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> as a reward.</p>
<p>With nothing left to lose, More denounces the King's actions as illegal. As grounds, he cites the Biblical basis for the authority of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papacy" title="Papacy">Papacy</a> over <a href="/wiki/Christendom" title="Christendom">Christendom</a>. He further declares that the Church's immunity to State interference is guaranteed both in <a href="/wiki/Magna_Carta" title="Magna Carta">Magna Carta</a> and in the King's own Coronation Oath. As the audience screams in protest, More is condemned to death by beheading. Before his execution on <a href="/wiki/Tower_Hill" title="Tower Hill">Tower Hill</a>, More pardons the executioner, and says, "I die His Majesty's good servant, but God's first."</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptation">Adaptation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Man_for_All_Seasons_(1966_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Adaptation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Bolt" title="Robert Bolt">Robert Bolt</a> adapted the screenplay himself. The running commentary of The Common Man was deleted and the character was divided into the roles of the Thames boatman, More's steward, an innkeeper, the jailer from the Tower, the jury foreman and the executioner. The subplot involving the imperial ambassador, <a href="/wiki/Eustace_Chapuys" title="Eustace Chapuys">Eustace Chapuys</a>, was also excised. A few minor scenes were added to the play, for instance Wolsey's death, More's investiture as Chancellor, and the King's wedding to <a href="/wiki/Anne_Boleyn" title="Anne Boleyn">Anne Boleyn</a>, to cover narrative gaps left by the exclusion of the Common Man.</p>
<p>For obvious reasons, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brecht" title="Brecht">Brechtian</a> staging of the final courtroom scene (which depicted the Jury as consisting of the Common Man and several sticks bearing the hats of the various characters he has played) is changed to a more realistic setting. Also, while <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Howard,_3rd_Duke_of_Norfolk" title="Thomas Howard, 3rd Duke of Norfolk">the Duke of Norfolk</a> was the judge both historically and in the play's depiction of the trial, the character of the <a href="/wiki/Chief_Justice" title="Chief Justice">Chief Justice</a> (<a href="/wiki/Jack_Gwillim" title="Jack Gwillim">Jack Gwillim</a>) was created for the film. Norfolk is still present, but plays little role in the proceedings.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Man_for_All_Seasons_(1966_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The producers initially feared that Scofield was not a big enough name to draw in audiences, so the producers approached <a href="/wiki/Richard_Burton" title="Richard Burton">Richard Burton</a>, who turned down the part. <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Olivier" title="Laurence Olivier">Laurence Olivier</a> was also considered, but director Zinnemann demanded that Scofield be cast. He played More both in London's West End and on Broadway; the latter appearance led to a Tony Award.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alec_Guinness" title="Alec Guinness">Alec Guinness</a> was the studio's first choice to play Cardinal Wolsey, and <a href="/wiki/Peter_O%27Toole" title="Peter O'Toole">Peter O'Toole</a> was the first choice to play Henry VIII. <a href="/wiki/Richard_Harris" title="Richard Harris">Richard Harris</a> was also considered. Bolt wanted film director <a href="/wiki/John_Huston" title="John Huston">John Huston</a> to play Norfolk, but he refused. <a href="/wiki/Vanessa_Redgrave" title="Vanessa Redgrave">Vanessa Redgrave</a> was originally to have played Margaret, but she had a theatre commitment. She agreed to a cameo as <a href="/wiki/Anne_Boleyn" title="Anne Boleyn">Anne Boleyn</a> on the condition that she not be billed in the part or mentioned in the previews.</p>
<p>To keep the budget at under $2 million, the actors all took salary cuts. Only Scofield, York, and Welles were paid salaries exceeding £10,000. For playing Rich, his first major film role, <a href="/wiki/John_Hurt" title="John Hurt">John Hurt</a> was paid £3,000. <a href="/wiki/Vanessa_Redgrave" title="Vanessa Redgrave">Vanessa Redgrave</a> appeared simply for the fun of it and refused to accept any money.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Leo_McKern" title="Leo McKern">Leo McKern</a> had played the Common Man in the original West End production of the show, but had been shifted to Cromwell for the Broadway production. He and Scofield are the only members of the cast to appear in both the stage and screen versions of the story. Vanessa Redgrave did appear as Lady Alice in a <a href="/wiki/A_Man_for_All_Seasons_(1988_film)" title="A Man for All Seasons (1988 film)">1988 remake</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Man_for_All_Seasons_(1966_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Man_for_All_Seasons_(1966_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film was a box office success, making $28,350,000 in the US alone,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-numbers_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-numbers-2">[2]</a></sup> making it the <a href="/wiki/1966_in_film" title="1966 in film">fifth highest-grossing film of 1966</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Man_for_All_Seasons_(1966_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It has received positive reviews from film critics, with an 86% approval rating in review aggregator <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A. D. Murphy of <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> wrote: "Producer-director Fred Zinnemann has blended all filmmaking elements into an excellent, handsome and stirring film version of <i>A Man for All Seasons</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Paul Scofield's performance was particularly praised. Kate Cameron of the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Daily_News" title="New York Daily News">New York Daily News</a></i> said: "over all these fine performances, including Robert Shaw's opulent, bluff and forceful representation of the king, it is Scofield who dominates the screen with his genteel voice and steadfast refusal to kowtow to the king, even at the expense of his head."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Pauline_Kael" title="Pauline Kael">Pauline Kael</a> gave the film a more critical review, writing: "There's more than a little of the school pageant in the rhythm of the movie: Though it's neater than our school drama coaches could make it, the figures group and say their assigned lines and move on."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>It's also ranked number 43 on the <a href="/wiki/British_Film_Institute" title="British Film Institute">British Film Institute</a>'s list of the <a href="/wiki/BFI_Top_100_British_films" title="BFI Top 100 British films">top 100 British films</a>.</p>
