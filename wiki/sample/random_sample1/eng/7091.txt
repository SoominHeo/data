<p><b>Bowman's capsule</b> (or the <b>Bowman capsule</b>, <b>capsula glomeruli</b>, or <b>glomerular capsule</b>) is a cup-like sack at the beginning of the tubular component of a <a href="/wiki/Nephron" title="Nephron">nephron</a> in the mammalian <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidney</a> that performs the first step in the filtration of blood to form urine. A <a href="/wiki/Glomerulus_(kidney)" title="Glomerulus (kidney)">glomerulus</a> is enclosed in the sac. Fluids from blood in the glomerulus are collected in the Bowman's capsule (i.e., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glomerular_filtrate" title="Glomerular filtrate">glomerular filtrate</a>) and further processed along the nephron to form <a href="/wiki/Urine" title="Urine">urine</a>. This process is known as <a href="/wiki/Ultrafiltration_(renal)" title="Ultrafiltration (renal)">ultrafiltration</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Bowman's capsule is named after <a href="/wiki/Sir_William_Bowman,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir William Bowman, 1st Baronet">Sir William Bowman</a>, who identified it in 1842.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anatomy">Anatomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowman%27s_capsule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Anatomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Outside the capsule, there are two "poles":</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Vascular_pole" title="Vascular pole">vascular pole</a> is the side with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afferent_arteriole" title="Afferent arteriole">afferent arteriole</a> and <a href="/wiki/Efferent_arteriole" title="Efferent arteriole">efferent arteriole</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Urinary_pole" title="Urinary pole">urinary pole</a> is the side with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proximal_convoluted_tubule" title="Proximal convoluted tubule">proximal convoluted tubule</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Inside the capsule, the layers are as follows, from outside to inside:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>Parietal layer</i>—A single layer of <a href="/wiki/Simple_squamous_epithelium" title="Simple squamous epithelium">simple squamous epithelium</a>. Does not function in filtration.</li>
<li><i>Bowman's space (or "urinary space", or "capsular space")</i>—Between the visceral and parietal layers, into which the filtrate enters after passing through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filtration_slits" title="Filtration slits">filtration slits</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Visceral layer</i>—Lies just above the thickened <a href="/wiki/Glomerular_basement_membrane" title="Glomerular basement membrane">glomerular basement membrane</a> and is made of <a href="/wiki/Podocyte" title="Podocyte">podocytes</a>. Beneath the visceral layer lie the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glomerular_capillaries" title="Glomerular capillaries">glomerular capillaries</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><i>Filtration barrier</i>—The filtration barrier is composed of the fenestrated endothelium of the glomerular capillaries, the fused basal lamina of the endothelial cells and podocytes, and the filtration slits of the podocytes. The barrier permits the passage of water, ions, and small molecules from the bloodstream into the Bowman's space. The barrier prevents the passage of large and/or negatively charged proteins (such as albumin). The basal lamina of the filtration barrier is composed of three layers. The first layer is the lamina rara externa, adjacent to the podocyte processes. The second layer is the lamina rara interna, adjacent to the endothelial cells. The final layer is the lamina densa which is a darker central zone of the basal lamina. It consists of the meshwork of type IV collagen and laminin which act as a selective macromolecular filter.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physiology">Physiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowman%27s_capsule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The process of filtration of the blood in the Bowman's capsule is <a href="/wiki/Ultrafiltration_(renal)" title="Ultrafiltration (renal)">ultrafiltration</a> (or glomerular filtration), and the normal rate of filtration is 125 ml/min, equivalent to 80 times the daily blood volume.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Any proteins under roughly 30 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dalton_(unit)" title="Dalton (unit)">kilodaltons</a> can pass freely through the membrane, although there is some extra hindrance for negatively charged molecules due to the negative charge of the basement membrane and the podocytes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Any small molecules such as <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> (NaCl), <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a> pass freely into Bowman's space, but <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platelets" title="Platelets">platelets</a> and large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteins" title="Proteins">proteins</a> do not.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>As a result, the filtrate leaving the Bowman's capsule is very similar to <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">blood plasma</a> (filtrate or glomerular filtrate is composed of blood plasma minus plasma protein i.e. it contains all the components of blood plasma except the proteins) in composition as it passes into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proximal_convoluted_tubule" title="Proximal convoluted tubule">proximal convoluted tubule</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowman%27s_capsule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Clinical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Measuring the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glomerular_filtration_rate" title="Glomerular filtration rate">glomerular filtration rate</a> (GFR) is a <a href="/wiki/Renal_physiology#Tests" title="Renal physiology">diagnostic test of kidney function</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A decreased GFR may be a sign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renal_failure" title="Renal failure">renal failure</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A number of diseases can result in various problems within the glomerulus. Examples include acute proliferative (endocapillary) glomerulonephritis, mesangioproliferative glomerulonephritis, mesangiocapillary (membranoproliferative) glomerulonephritis, acute crescentic glomerulonephritis, focal segmental glomerulonephritis, and diabetic glomerulosclerosis.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eponym">Eponym</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bowman%27s_capsule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Eponym">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bowman's capsule is named after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_William_Bowman" title="Sir William Bowman">Sir William Bowman</a> (1816-1892), a British <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgeon</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anatomist" title="Anatomist">anatomist</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, thorough microscopical anatomy of kidney including the nephronic capsule was described by the Ukrainian surgeon and anatomist from the Russian Empire, Prof. <a class="extiw" href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%A8%D1%83%D0%BC%D0%BB%D1%8F%D0%BD%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B8%D0%B9,_%D0%90%D0%BB%D0%B5%D0%BA%D1%81%D0%B0%D0%BD%D0%B4%D1%80_%D0%9C%D0%B8%D1%85%D0%B0%D0%B9%D0%BB%D0%BE%D0%B2%D0%B8%D1%87" title="ru:Шумлянский, Александр Михайлович">Alexander Shumliansky</a> (1748-1795), in his 1788 doctoral thesis "De structura renum: Tractatus physiologico-anatomicus" ("About Kidney Structure: an Physiological-Anatomical Treatise," in Latin); thus, much prior to Bowman. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Together with the glomerulus it is known as a <a href="/wiki/Renal_corpuscle" title="Renal corpuscle">renal corpuscle</a>, or a Malpighian corpuscle, <a href="/wiki/Eponym" title="Eponym">named after</a> <a href="/wiki/Marcello_Malpighi" title="Marcello Malpighi">Marcello Malpighi</a> (1628-1694), an Italian <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physician</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biologist" title="Biologist">biologist</a>. This name is not used widely anymore, probably to avoid confusion with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Splenic_lymphoid_nodules" title="Splenic lymphoid nodules">Malpighian bodies of the spleen</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
