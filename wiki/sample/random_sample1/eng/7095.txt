<p><b>Canes Venatici I</b> or <b>CVn I</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_spheroidal_galaxy" title="Dwarf spheroidal galaxy">dwarf spheroidal galaxy</a> situated in the <a href="/wiki/Canes_Venatici" title="Canes Venatici">Canes Venatici</a> <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> and discovered in 2006 in the data obtained by <a href="/wiki/Sloan_Digital_Sky_Survey" title="Sloan Digital Sky Survey">Sloan Digital Sky Survey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zucker2006_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zucker2006-4">[4]</a></sup> It is one of the most distant known satellites of the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> as of 2011 together with <a href="/wiki/Leo_I_(dwarf_galaxy)" title="Leo I (dwarf galaxy)">Leo I</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leo_II_(dwarf_galaxy)" title="Leo II (dwarf galaxy)">Leo II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zucker2006_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Zucker2006-4">[4]</a></sup> The galaxy is located at a distance of about 220 <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">kpc</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> and is moving away from the Sun at a velocity of about 31 km/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simon2007_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Simon2007-6">[6]</a></sup> It is classified as a <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_spheroidal_galaxy" title="Dwarf spheroidal galaxy">dwarf spheroidal galaxy</a> (dSph) meaning that it has an elliptical (ratio of axes ~ 2.5:1) shape with the half-light radius of about 550 pc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zucker2006_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Zucker2006-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin2007_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Martin2007-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>CVn I is a relatively faint satellite of the Milky Way—its integrated luminosity is about 230,000 times that of the Sun (absolute <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_magnitude" title="Visible magnitude">visible magnitude</a> of about −8.6).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin2007_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Martin2007-5">[5]</a></sup> However, its mass is about 27 million <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">solar masses</a>, which means that the galaxy's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_to_light_ratio" title="Mass to light ratio">mass to light ratio</a> is around 220. A high mass to light ratio implies that CVn I is dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simon2007_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Simon2007-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The stellar population of CVn I consists mainly of old stars formed more than 10 billion years ago. The metallicity of these old stars is also very low at <span class="nowrap">[Fe/H] ≈ −2.08 ± 0.02</span>, which means that they contain 110 times less heavy elements than the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kirby2008_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kirby2008-7">[7]</a></sup> There are also about 60 <a href="/wiki/RR_Lyrae" title="RR Lyrae">RR Lyrae</a> stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuehn2008_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kuehn2008-3">[3]</a></sup> The galaxy also contains a small fraction of younger (1–2 billion years old) more metal-rich (<span class="nowrap">[Fe/H] ≈ −1.5</span>) stars, which account for about 5% of its mass and 10% of its light. These younger stars are concentrated in the center of the galaxy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin2008_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Martin2008-2">[2]</a></sup> There is currently no star formation in CVn I and the measurements have so far failed to detect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutral_hydrogen" title="Neutral hydrogen">neutral hydrogen</a> in it—the upper limit is 30,000 solar masses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grcevich2009_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grcevich2009-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
