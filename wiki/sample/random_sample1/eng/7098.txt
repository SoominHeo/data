<p>An <b>ancestral shrine</b>, <b>hall</b> or <b>temple</b> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%A5%A0" title="wikt:祠">祠</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A0%82" title="wikt:堂">堂</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/C%C3%AD" title="wikt:Cí">Cí</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/t%C3%A1ng" title="wikt:táng">táng</a></span></i> or <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%AE%97" title="wikt:宗">宗</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%A5%A0" title="wikt:祠">祠</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Z%C5%8Dng" title="wikt:Zōng">Zōng</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/C%C3%AD" title="wikt:Cí">Cí</a></span></i>), also called <b>lineage temple</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Chinese_temple" title="Chinese temple">Chinese temple</a> dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Ancestor_veneration_in_China" title="Ancestor veneration in China">deified</a> <a href="/wiki/Ancestor" title="Ancestor">ancestors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Progenitor" title="Progenitor">progenitors</a> of surname lineages or families in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_traditional_religion" title="Chinese traditional religion">Chinese traditional religion</a>. Ancestral temples are closely linked to <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucian</a> culture and the emphasis that it places on <a href="/wiki/Filial_piety" title="Filial piety">filial piety</a>.</p>
<p>A common central feature of the ancestral temples are the <a href="/wiki/Spirit_tablet" title="Spirit tablet">ancestral tablets</a> that embody the ancestral spirits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eccc_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-eccc-1">[1]</a></sup> The ancestral tablets are typically arranged by seniority of the ancestors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eccc_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-eccc-1">[1]</a></sup> Altars and other ritual objects such as incense burners are also common fixtures. Ancestors and gods can also be represented by statues.</p>
<p>The temples are used for collective rituals and festivals in honor of the ancestors<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eccc_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-eccc-1">[1]</a></sup> but also for other family- and community-related functions such as weddings and funerals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eccc_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-eccc-1">[1]</a></sup> Sometimes, they serve wider community functions such as meetings and local elections.</p>
<p>In traditional weddings, the ancestral temple serves a major symbolic function, completing the transfer of a woman to her husband's family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lwx_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-lwx-2">[2]</a></sup> During the wedding rites, the bride and groom worship at the groom's ancestral shrine, bowing as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lwx_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-lwx-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>first bow - Heaven and Earth</li>
<li>second bow - ancestors</li>
<li>third bow - parents</li>
<li>fourth bow - spouse</li>
</ol>
<p>Three months after the marriage, the wife undertakes worship at the husband's ancestral shrine, in a rite known as <i>miaojian</i> (廟見).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lwx_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-lwx-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ancestral temples have often been secularized to serve as village schools or granaries during the land reform of the 1950s and the <a href="/wiki/Cultural_Revolution" title="Cultural Revolution">Cultural Revolution</a>. They have experienced a revival since the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_economic_reform" title="Chinese economic reform">economic liberalization</a> of the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eccc_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-eccc-1">[1]</a></sup> The revival of the ancestral temples has been particularly strong in southern China where lineage organization had stronger roots in the local culture and local communities are more likely to have members living overseas who can support rebuilding of the shrines through donations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eccc_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-eccc-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hong_Kong">Hong Kong</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancestral_shrine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hong Kong">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Notable ancestral temples in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Tang Ancestral Hall and Yu Kiu Ancestral Hall, along the <a href="/wiki/Ping_Shan_Heritage_Trail" title="Ping Shan Heritage Trail">Ping Shan Heritage Trail</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/King_Law_Ka_Shuk" title="King Law Ka Shuk">King Law Ka Shuk</a></li>
