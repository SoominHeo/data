<p>The <b>Four Great Classical Novels</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_literature" title="Chinese literature">Chinese literature</a> are the four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Novels" title="Novels">novels</a> commonly regarded by <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> literary criticism to be the greatest and most influential of pre-modern Chinese fiction. Dating from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qing_Dynasty" title="Qing Dynasty">Qing</a> dynasties, they are well-known to most Chinese either directly or through their many adaptations to <a href="/wiki/Chinese_opera" title="Chinese opera">opera</a> and various <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">popular culture</a> media.</p>
<p>They are among the world's longest and oldest novels<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-talk_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-talk-2">[2]</a></sup> and are considered to be the pinnacle of China's achievement in classical novels, influencing the creation of many stories, plays, movies, games, and other forms of entertainment throughout countries in <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Four_Great_Classical_Novels&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chinese fiction, rooted in narrative classics such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shishuo_Xinyu" title="Shishuo Xinyu">Shishuo Xinyu</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sou_Shen_Ji" title="Sou Shen Ji">Sou Shen Ji</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Wenyuan_Yinghua" title="Wenyuan Yinghua">Wenyuan Yinghua</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Da_Tang_Xiyu_Ji" title="Da Tang Xiyu Ji">Da Tang Xiyu Ji</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Youyang_Zazu" title="Youyang Zazu">Youyang Zazu</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Taiping_Guangji" title="Taiping Guangji">Taiping Guangji</a></i>, and official histories, developed into the <a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">novel</a> as early as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" title="Song Dynasty">Song Dynasty</a>. The novel as an extended prose narrative which realistically creates a believable world of its own evolved in China and in Europe from the 14th to 18th centuries, though a little earlier in China. Chinese audiences were more interested in history and were more historically minded. They appreciated relative optimism, moral humanism, and relative emphasis on collective behavior and the welfare of the society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERopp1990310-311_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERopp1990310-311-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rise of a money economy and urbanization beginning in the Song era led to a professionalization of entertainment which was further encouraged by the spread of printing, the rise of literacy, and education. In both China and Western Europe, the novel gradually became more autobiographical and serious in exploration of social, moral, and philosophical problems. Chinese fiction of the late <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a> and early <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing dynasty</a> was varied, self-conscious, and experimental. In China, however, there was no counterpart to the 19th-century European explosion of novels. The novels of the Ming and early Qing dynasties represented a pinnacle of classical Chinese fiction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERopp1990311_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERopp1990311-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The scholar and literary critic <a href="/wiki/Andrew_H._Plaks" title="Andrew H. Plaks">Andrew H. Plaks</a> argues that <i>Romance of the Three Kingdoms</i>, <i>Water Margin</i>, <i>Journey to the West</i>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Golden_Lotus" title="The Golden Lotus">The Golden Lotus</a></i> collectively constituted a technical breakthrough reflecting new cultural values and intellectual concerns. Their educated editors, authors, and commentators used the <a href="/wiki/Narrative" title="Narrative">narrative conventions</a> developed from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huaben_(Chinese_novella)" title="Huaben (Chinese novella)">earlier story-tellers</a>, such as the episodic structure, interspersed songs and folk sayings, or speaking directly to the reader, but they fashioned self-consciously ironic narratives whose seeming familiarity camouflaged a Neo-Confucian moral critique of late Ming decadence. Plaks explores the textual history of the novels (all published after their author's deaths, usually anonymously) and how the ironic and satiric devices of these novels paved the way for the great novels of the 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Plaks further shows these Ming novels share formal characteristics. They are almost all over 100 chapters in length; divided into ten chapter narrative blocks which are broken into two to three chapter episodes; arranged into first and second halves which are symmetrical; and arrange their events in patterns which follow seasons and geography. They manipulated the conventions of popular story telling in an ironic way in order to go against the surface meanings of the story. <i>Three Kingdoms</i>, he argues, presents a contrast between the ideal, that is, dynastic order, and the reality of political collapse and near anarchy; <i>Water Margin</i> likewise presents heroic stories from the popular tradition in a way that exposes the heroism as brutal and selfish; <i>Journey to the West</i> is an outwardly serious spiritual quest undercut by comic and sometimes bawdy tone. <i>Jin Ping Mei</i> is the clearest and most sophisticated example; the action is sometimes grossly sexual, but in the end emphasizes conventional morality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Four_Great_Classical_Novels&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The four novels were highly influential in the development of vernacular works in <a href="/wiki/Chinese_literature" title="Chinese literature">Chinese literary history</a>. Traditionally, <a href="/wiki/Fiction" title="Fiction">fiction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drama" title="Drama">drama</a> were not held in "high regard" in the Chinese or East Asian literary hierarchy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lit_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-lit-8">[7]</a></sup> and they were generally not seen as true "literature" by scholars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-talk_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-talk-2">[2]</a></sup> Writers in these forms would not have the same level of prestige as <a href="/wiki/Chinese_poetry" title="Chinese poetry">poets</a> or scholars of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_classics" title="Chinese classics">Chinese classics</a> would have had.</p>
<p>All four of the novels were written in a style that is a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Classical_Chinese" title="Classical Chinese">Classical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Written_vernacular_Chinese" title="Written vernacular Chinese">vernacular</a> Chinese,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-talk_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-talk-2">[2]</a></sup> with some that are more completely vernacular than the others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> For instance, <i>Romance of the Three Kingdoms</i> is known for its mix of classical prose with folklore and popular narratives,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> while the <i>Dream of the Red Chamber</i> is known for the use of poetry within its mostly vernacular style. These four novels are thought to have popularized, and more importantly "legitimatized" the role of vernacular literature among the literary circles of China.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_great_classic_novels">Other great classic novels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Four_Great_Classical_Novels&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other great classic novels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "classic novels," writes <a href="/wiki/Andrew_H._Plaks" title="Andrew H. Plaks">Andrew H. Plaks</a>, is a “neologism of twentieth century scholarship" which seems to have come into common use under the influence of C. T. Hsia’s <i>Classic Chinese Novel</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPlaks19874_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPlaks19874-11">[10]</a></sup> Paul Ropp, following Hsia's selection,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHsia1968_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHsia1968-12">[11]</a></sup> notes that “an almost universal consensus affirms six works as truly great,” including, in addition to those above <i><a href="/wiki/Jin_Ping_Mei" title="Jin Ping Mei">The Plum in the Golden Vase</a></i> by Lanling Xiaoxiaosheng and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scholars_(novel)" title="The Scholars (novel)">The Scholars</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Wu_Jingzi" title="Wu Jingzi">Wu Jingzi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERopp1990317_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERopp1990317-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because of its explicit descriptions of sex, <i>The Plum in the Golden Vase</i> has been banned for most of its existence. Despite this, many if not most scholars and writers, including <a href="/wiki/Lu_Xun" title="Lu Xun">Lu Xun</a>, place it among the top Chinese novels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
