<p><b>Simon the Zealot</b> (<span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Acts+1%3A13&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Acts 1:13</a></span>), <b>Simon, who was called the Zealot</b> (<span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Luke+6%3A15&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Luke 6:15</a></span>), <b>Simon Kananaios</b> (<span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matthew+10%3A4&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Matthew 10:4</a></span>) or <b>Simon Cananeus</b> (<span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Mark+3%3A18&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Mark 3:18</a></span>) was one of the most obscure among the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apostle_(Christian)" title="Apostle (Christian)">apostles of Jesus</a>. A few <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudepigraph" title="Pseudepigraph">pseudepigraphical</a> writings were connected to him, and the theologian and <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_the_Church" title="Doctor of the Church">Doctor of the Church</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Jerome" title="Saint Jerome">Saint Jerome</a>, does not include him in <a href="/wiki/De_Viris_Illustribus_(Jerome)" title="De Viris Illustribus (Jerome)"><i>De viris illustribus</i></a> written between 392–393 AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identity">Identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simon_the_Zealot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SimonTheZealotWithSaw.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1091" data-file-width="358" height="609" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/SimonTheZealotWithSaw.JPG/200px-SimonTheZealotWithSaw.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/SimonTheZealotWithSaw.JPG/300px-SimonTheZealotWithSaw.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a1/SimonTheZealotWithSaw.JPG 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SimonTheZealotWithSaw.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Saint Simon the Zealot with his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_symbology" title="Saint symbology">attribute</a> of a saw</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The name Simon occurs in all of the <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_Gospels" title="Synoptic Gospels">Synoptic Gospels</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Book_of_Acts" title="Book of Acts">Book of Acts</a> each time there is a list of apostles, without further details:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Simon, (whom he also named Peter,) and Andrew his brother, James and John, Philip and Bartholomew, Matthew and Thomas, James the son of Alphaeus, and Simon called Zelotes, And Judas the brother of James, and Judas Iscariot, which also was the traitor.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Luke+6%3A14%E2%80%9316&amp;version=KJV" rel="nofollow">Luke 6:14–16</a></span></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>To distinguish him from <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Simon Peter</a> he is called <i>Kananaios</i> (<span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matthew+10%3A4&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Matthew 10:4</a></span>) or <i>Kananites</i> (<span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Mark+3%3A18&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Mark 3:18</a></span>), and in the list of apostles in Luke 6:15, repeated in Acts 1:13, <i>Zelotes,</i> the "<a href="/wiki/Zealots_(Judea)" title="Zealots (Judea)">Zealot</a>". Both titles derive from the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> word <i>qana</i>, meaning <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/zealous" title="wikt:zealous">zealous</a></i>, although <a href="/wiki/Jerome" title="Jerome">Jerome</a> and others mistook the word to signify the apostle was from the town of <a href="/wiki/Cana" title="Cana">Cana</a>, in which case his <a href="/wiki/Epithet" title="Epithet">epithet</a> would have been "Kanaios", or even from the region of <a href="/wiki/Canaan" title="Canaan">Canaan</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As such, the translation of the word as "the Cananite" or "the Canaanite" is traditional and without contemporary extra-canonic parallel.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brooklyn_Museum_-_Saint_Simon_-_James_Tissot_-_overall.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="473" height="325" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Brooklyn_Museum_-_Saint_Simon_-_James_Tissot_-_overall.jpg/200px-Brooklyn_Museum_-_Saint_Simon_-_James_Tissot_-_overall.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Brooklyn_Museum_-_Saint_Simon_-_James_Tissot_-_overall.jpg/300px-Brooklyn_Museum_-_Saint_Simon_-_James_Tissot_-_overall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Brooklyn_Museum_-_Saint_Simon_-_James_Tissot_-_overall.jpg/400px-Brooklyn_Museum_-_Saint_Simon_-_James_Tissot_-_overall.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brooklyn_Museum_-_Saint_Simon_-_James_Tissot_-_overall.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/James_Tissot" title="James Tissot">James Tissot</a> - Saint Simon - <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Museum" title="Brooklyn Museum">Brooklyn Museum</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Eisenman" title="Robert Eisenman">Robert Eisenman</a> has pointed out contemporary <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">talmudic</a> references to Zealots as <i>kanna'im</i> "but not really as a group — rather as avenging priests in the Temple".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Eisenman's broader conclusions, that the zealot element in the original apostle group was disguised and overwritten to make it support the assimilative <a href="/wiki/Pauline_Christianity" title="Pauline Christianity">Pauline Christianity</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Gentile" title="Gentile">Gentiles</a>, are more controversial. <a href="/wiki/John_P._Meier" title="John P. Meier">John P. Meier</a> points out that the term "Zealot" is a mistranslation and in the context of the Gospels means "zealous" or "jealous" (in this case, for keeping the Law of Moses), as the Zealot movement did not exist until 30 to 40 years after the events of the Gospels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> However, neither Brandon,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brandon_1967_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brandon_1967-6">[6]</a></sup> nor Hengel <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hengel_1989_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hengel_1989-7">[7]</a></sup> support this view, both independently concluding that the revolt by <a href="/wiki/Judas_of_Galilee" title="Judas of Galilee">Judas of Galilee</a>, arising from the <a href="/wiki/Census_of_Quirinius" title="Census of Quirinius">census of Quirinius</a> in 6 AD, was the ultimate origin of the Jewish freedom movement, which developed via the "<a href="/wiki/Zealots_(Judea)" title="Zealots (Judea)">Fourth Philosophy</a>" group into the Zealots, even by the time of Jesus. Both of these researchers suggest that "Simon Zelotes" was indeed a Zealot belonging to this movement, and perhaps that other disciples were also. However, Hengel (in particular) concluded that Jesus himself was not a zealot, as much of his teaching was actually contrary to Fourth Philosophy views.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:L%27ap%C3%B4tre_saint_Simon.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="1836" height="391" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/L%27ap%C3%B4tre_saint_Simon.jpg/220px-L%27ap%C3%B4tre_saint_Simon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/L%27ap%C3%B4tre_saint_Simon.jpg/330px-L%27ap%C3%B4tre_saint_Simon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/L%27ap%C3%B4tre_saint_Simon.jpg/440px-L%27ap%C3%B4tre_saint_Simon.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:L%27ap%C3%B4tre_saint_Simon.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Statue of St. Simon in the <a href="/wiki/Archbasilica_of_St._John_Lateran" title="Archbasilica of St. John Lateran">Archbasilica of St. John Lateran</a> by Francesco Moratti.</div>

<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gospels" title="Gospels">Gospels</a>, Simon the Zealot is never identified with <a href="/wiki/Simon,_brother_of_Jesus" title="Simon, brother of Jesus">Simon the brother of Jesus</a> mentioned in <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Mark+6%3A3&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Mark 6:3</a></span>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>3 Is not this the carpenter, the son of Mary and brother of James and Joses and Judas and Simon, and are not his sisters here with us?” And they took offense at him.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Encyclopedia" title="Catholic Encyclopedia">Catholic Encyclopedia</a> suggests that Simon the Zealot may be the same person as <a href="/wiki/Simeon_of_Jerusalem" title="Simeon of Jerusalem">Simeon of Jerusalem</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simon_(brother_of_Jesus)" title="Simon (brother of Jesus)">Simon the brother of Jesus</a>. He could perhaps be the cousin of Jesus or a son of Joseph from a previous marriage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another tradition holds that this is the Simeon of Jerusalem who became the second bishop of Jerusalem, although he was born in Galilee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_tradition">Later tradition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simon_the_Zealot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Later tradition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Isidore_of_Seville" title="Isidore of Seville">St. Isidore of Seville</a> drew together the accumulated anecdotes of St. Simon in <i>De Vita et Morte</i>; the fully developed legend is presented in the <i><a href="/wiki/Golden_Legend" title="Golden Legend">Legenda Aurea</a></i> (<i>ca</i> 1260).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In later tradition, Simon is often associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Jude" title="Saint Jude">St. Jude</a> as an evangelizing team; in Western Christianity, they share their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feast_day" title="Feast day">feast day</a> on 28 October. The most widespread tradition is that after evangelizing in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, Simon joined Jude in <a href="/wiki/Parthian_Empire" title="Parthian Empire">Persia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Armenia_(antiquity)" title="Kingdom of Armenia (antiquity)">Armenia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Beirut" title="Beirut">Beirut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, where both were martyred in 65 AD. This version is the one found in the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Legend" title="Golden Legend">Golden Legend</a>. He may have suffered crucifixion as the Bishop of Jerusalem.</p>
<p>One tradition states that he traveled in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. Christian <a href="/wiki/People_of_Ethiopia" title="People of Ethiopia">Ethiopians</a> claim that he was crucified in <a href="/wiki/Samaria" title="Samaria">Samaria</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Justus_Lipsius" title="Justus Lipsius">Justus Lipsius</a> writes that he was sawn in half at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Suanir&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Suanir (page does not exist)">Suanir</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">Persia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saint_Simon_the_Apostle_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Saint_Simon_the_Apostle-2">[2]</a></sup> However, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moses_of_Chorene" title="Moses of Chorene">Moses of Chorene</a> writes that he was martyred at Weriosphora in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caucasian_Iberia" title="Caucasian Iberia">Caucasian Iberia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saint_Simon_the_Apostle_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Saint_Simon_the_Apostle-2">[2]</a></sup> Tradition also claims he died peacefully at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edessa,_Mesopotamia" title="Edessa, Mesopotamia">Edessa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Another tradition says he visited <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Britain</a>—possibly <a href="/wiki/Glastonbury" title="Glastonbury">Glastonbury</a>—and was martyred in <a href="/wiki/Caistor" title="Caistor">Caistor</a>, modern-day <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>. Another, doubtless inspired by his title "the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zealot" title="Zealot">Zealot</a>", states that he was a member of the group involved in the Jewish revolt against the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Romans</a>, which was brutally suppressed. [See the paragraph above citing the work of Brandon <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brandon_1967_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brandon_1967-6">[6]</a></sup> and Hengel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hengel_1989_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hengel_1989-7">[7]</a></sup>]</p>
<p>The 2nd century <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epistle_of_the_Apostles" title="Epistle of the Apostles">Epistle of the Apostles</a></i> (<i>Epistula Apostolorum</i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> a polemic against <a href="/wiki/Gnosticism" title="Gnosticism">gnostics</a>, lists him among the apostles purported to be writing the letter (who include Thomas) as <b>Judas Zelotes</b> and certain <a href="/wiki/Vetus_Latina" title="Vetus Latina">Old Latin translations</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">Gospel of Matthew</a> substitute "Judas the Zealot" for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Jude" title="Saint Jude">Thaddeus/Lebbaeus</a> in Matthew 10:3. To some readers, this suggests that he may be identical with the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Jude" title="Saint Jude">Judas not Iscariot</a>" mentioned in <a class="external text" href="http://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=john%2014:22&amp;version=KJV" rel="nofollow">John 14:22</a>: "Judas saith unto him, not Iscariot, Our Lord, how is it that thou wilt manifest thyself unto us, and not unto the world?" As it has been suggested that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Jude" title="Saint Jude">Jude</a> is identical with the apostle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_(Apostle)" title="Thomas (Apostle)">Thomas</a> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jude_Thomas" title="Jude Thomas">Jude Thomas</a>), an identification of "Simon Zelotes" with Thomas is also possible. <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Thiering" title="Barbara Thiering">Barbara Thiering</a> identified Simon Zelotes with <a href="/wiki/Simon_Magus" title="Simon Magus">Simon Magus</a>, however this view has received no serious acceptance. The <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> records nothing more of Simon, aside from this multitude of possible but unlikely pseudonyms. He is buried in the same tomb as <a href="/wiki/Jude_the_Apostle" title="Jude the Apostle">St. Jude Thaddeus</a>, in the left transept of the St. Peter's Basilica in Rome, under the altar of St. Joseph.</p>
<p>In art, Simon has the identifying attribute of a <a href="/wiki/Saw" title="Saw">saw</a> because he was traditionally martyred by being sawn in half.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sainthood">Sainthood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simon_the_Zealot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sainthood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Simon, like the other Apostles, is regarded as a saint by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Churches" title="Eastern Orthodox Churches">Eastern Orthodox Churches</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodox_Churches" title="Oriental Orthodox Churches">Oriental Orthodox Churches</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Churches" title="Eastern Catholic Churches">Eastern Catholic Churches</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglican_Church" title="Anglican Church">Anglican Church</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutheran_Church" title="Lutheran Church">Lutheran Church</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Islam">Islam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simon_the_Zealot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Islam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslims</a> accept <a href="/wiki/Jesus_in_Islam" title="Jesus in Islam">Jesus</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Prophet" title="Prophet">prophet</a> of <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">Qur'an</a> also speaks of Jesus' disciples but does not mention their names, instead referring to them as "helpers to the work of <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Muslim exegesis and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">Qur'an</a> commentary, however, names them and includes Simon amongst the disciples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hadith" title="Hadith">Muslim tradition</a> says that Simon was sent to preach the faith of <a href="/wiki/God_in_Islam" title="God in Islam">God</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_people" title="Berber people">Berbers</a>, outside <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Barnabas" title="Gospel of Barnabas">Gospel of Barnabas</a>, an Islamic book dated to the late 16th century that recounts a life story of Jesus from an Islamic perspective, a list of the twelve apostles is registered. In this list the only apostle that does not match with one of the traditional apostles of Christianity is Simon the Zealot, naming in his place a person who identifies himself as Barnabas, who appears as author of the book.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simon_the_Zealot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Rock_opera" title="Rock opera">rock opera</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ_Superstar" title="Jesus Christ Superstar">Jesus Christ Superstar</a></i>, Simon Zealotes tries to persuade Jesus to stir hatred among the masses against the Roman occupiers.</li>
<li>In the play <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Days_of_Judas_Iscariot" title="The Last Days of Judas Iscariot">The Last Days of Judas Iscariot</a></i>, Simon the Zealot is a witness for the defense at the trial of <a href="/wiki/Judas_Iscariot" title="Judas Iscariot">Judas Iscariot</a> in <a href="/wiki/Purgatory" title="Purgatory">Purgatory</a>.</li>
<li>In the miniseries <i><a href="/wiki/Jesus_of_Nazareth_(miniseries)" title="Jesus of Nazareth (miniseries)">Jesus of Nazareth</a></i>, before joining the Apostles he is portrayed first as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Zealotry_in_Jewish_history" title="Zealotry in Jewish history">Zealots</a>, hence his name.</li>
<li>Simon the Zealot is a blacksmith in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bronze_Bow" title="The Bronze Bow">The Bronze Bow</a></i>.</li>
</ul>
