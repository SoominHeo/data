<p><b>Thomas the Apostle</b> (called <b>Didymus</b> which means "the twin") was one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twelve_Apostles" title="Twelve Apostles">Twelve Apostles</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" title="Jesus Christ">Jesus Christ</a>, according to the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>. He is informally called <i><a href="/wiki/Doubting_Thomas" title="Doubting Thomas">doubting Thomas</a></i> because he doubted Jesus' resurrection when first told (in the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_John" title="Gospel of John">Gospel of John</a> account only), followed later by his confession of faith, "My Lord and my God", on seeing Jesus' wounded body.</p>
<p>Traditional legend written centuries later claims he travelled outside the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> to preach the Gospel, travelling as far as <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">Tamil Nadu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a> in present-day <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erwin_Fahlbusch_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Erwin_Fahlbusch-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orpa_Slapak_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Orpa_Slapak-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> According to undocumented traditions, the Apostle reached <a href="/wiki/Muziris" title="Muziris">Muziris</a>, (modern-day North Paravur and Kodungalloor in the state of <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>) in AD 52 and baptized several people, founding what today are known as <a href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas_Christians" title="Saint Thomas Christians">Saint Thomas Christians</a> or Nasranis. After his death, the reputed relics of Saint Thomas the Apostle were enshrined as far as <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> in the 3rd century, and later moved to various places.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1258, some of the relics were brought to <a href="/wiki/Abruzzo" title="Abruzzo">Abruzzo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ortona" title="Ortona">Ortona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, where they have been held in the Church of Saint Thomas the Apostle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He is often regarded as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patron_Saint" title="Patron Saint">Patron Saint</a> of India,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and the name <i>Thoma</i> remains quite popular among Saint Thomas Christians of India.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gospel_of_John">Gospel of John</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_the_Apostle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gospel of John">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thomas first speaks in the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_John" title="Gospel of John">Gospel of John</a>. In <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=John+11%3A16&amp;version=NIV" rel="nofollow">John 11:16</a></span>, when <a href="/wiki/Lazarus_of_Bethany" title="Lazarus of Bethany">Lazarus</a> had recently died, the apostles do not wish to go back to <a href="/wiki/Judea" title="Judea">Judea</a>, where some Jews had attempted to stone Jesus. Thomas says: "Let us also go, that we may die with him" (<a href="/wiki/New_International_Version" title="New International Version">NIV</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>He speaks again in <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=John+14%3A5&amp;version=NIV" rel="nofollow">John 14:5</a></span>. There, Jesus had just explained that he was going away to prepare a heavenly home for his followers, and that one day they would join him there. Thomas reacted by saying, "Lord, we don’t know where you are going, so how can we know the way?" (NIV)</p>
<p><span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=John+20%3A24-29&amp;version=KJV" rel="nofollow">John 20:24-29</a></span> tells how <a href="/wiki/Doubting_Thomas" title="Doubting Thomas">doubting Thomas</a> was skeptical at first when he heard that Jesus had risen from the dead and appeared to the other apostles, saying, "Except I shall see in his hands the print of the nails, and put my finger into the print of the nails, and thrust my hand into his side, I will not believe." (v.25) But when Jesus appeared later and invited Thomas to touch his wounds and behold him, Thomas showed his belief by saying, "My Lord and my God". (v.28) Jesus then said, "Thomas, because thou hast seen me, thou hast believed: blessed <i>[are]</i> they that have not seen, and <i>[yet]</i> have believed." (v.29)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_and_etymologies">Names and etymologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_the_Apostle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Names and etymologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name <i>Thomas</i> (<a href="/wiki/Koine_Greek" title="Koine Greek">Koine Greek</a>: Θωμᾶς) given for the apostle in the New Testament is derived from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aramaic" title="Aramaic">Aramaic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="syc" xml:lang="syc">ܬܐܘܡܐ</span>‎ <i>Toma</i>, equivalently from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew" title="Hebrew">Hebrew</a> <i>Teom</i>, meaning "twin". The equivalent term for twin in Greek, which is also used in the New Testament, is Δίδυμος <i>Didymus</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_names">Other names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_the_Apostle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nag_Hammadi_library" title="Nag Hammadi library">Nag Hammadi</a> copy of the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Thomas" title="Gospel of Thomas">Gospel of Thomas</a> begins: "These are the secret sayings that the living Jesus spoke and Didymos Judas Thomas recorded." Early Syrian traditions also relate the apostle's full name as Judas Thomas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Some have seen in the <i><a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Thomas" title="Acts of Thomas">Acts of Thomas</a></i> (written in east Syria in the early 3rd century, or perhaps as early as the first half of the 2nd century) an identification of Saint Thomas with the apostle Judas, brother of James, better known in English as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Jude" title="Saint Jude">Jude</a>. However, the first sentence of the Acts follows the Gospels and the Acts of the Apostles in distinguishing the apostle Thomas and the apostle Judas son of James. Few texts identify Thomas' twin. In the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Thomas_the_Contender" title="Book of Thomas the Contender">Book of Thomas the Contender</a>, part of the Nag Hammadi, it is said to be Jesus himself: "Now, since it has been said that you are my twin and true companion, examine yourself…"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-turner_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-turner-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Doubting_Thomas" title="Doubting Thomas">Doubting Thomas</a> is a skeptic who refuses to believe without direct personal experience—a reference to the Apostle Thomas, due to his refusal to believe the resurrected <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> had appeared to the ten other apostles, until he could see and feel the wounds received by Jesus on the cross.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feast_days">Feast days</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_the_Apostle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Feast days">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When the feast of Saint Thomas was inserted in the Roman calendar in the 9th century, it was assigned to 21 December. The <i>Martyrology of St. Jerome</i> mentioned the apostle on 3 July, the date to which the Roman celebration was transferred in 1969, so that it would no longer interfere with the major ferial days of <a href="/wiki/Advent" title="Advent">Advent</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> 3 July was the day on which his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relics" title="Relics">relics</a> were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Translation_(relics)" title="Translation (relics)">translated</a> from <a href="/wiki/Mylapore" title="Mylapore">Mylapore</a>, a place along the coast of the <a href="/wiki/Marina_Beach" title="Marina Beach">Marina Beach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">Chennai</a> (Madras) in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, to the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edessa,_Mesopotamia" title="Edessa, Mesopotamia">Edessa</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>. Traditionalist Roman Catholics (who follow the <a href="/wiki/General_Roman_Calendar_of_1960" title="General Roman Calendar of 1960">General Roman Calendar of 1960</a> or earlier) and many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglicans" title="Anglicans">Anglicans</a> (including members of the <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_(United_States)" title="Episcopal Church (United States)">Episcopal Church</a> as well as members of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutheran_Church" title="Lutheran Church">Lutheran Church</a>, who worship according to the 1662 edition of the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Book of Common Prayer</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-commonprayer.org_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-commonprayer.org-18">[18]</a></sup> still celebrate his feast day on 21 December. However, most modern liturgical calendars (including the <a href="/wiki/Common_Worship" title="Common Worship">Common Worship</a> calendar of the Church of England) prefer 3 July.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox" title="Eastern Orthodox">Eastern Orthodox</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine_Catholic" title="Byzantine Catholic">Byzantine Catholic</a> churches celebrate his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feast_day" title="Feast day">feast day</a> on 6 October<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> (for those churches which follow the traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julian_Calendar" title="Julian Calendar">Julian Calendar</a>, 6 October currently falls on 19 October of the modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gregorian_Calendar" title="Gregorian Calendar">Gregorian Calendar</a>). In addition, the next Sunday of the Easter (Pascha) is celebrated as the Sunday of Thomas, in commemoration of Thomas' question to Jesus, which led him to proclaim, according to Orthodox teaching, two natures of Jesus, both human and divine. Thomas is commemorated in common with all of the other apostles on 30 June (13 July), in a feast called the <a href="/wiki/Synaxis" title="Synaxis">Synaxis</a> of the Holy Apostles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> He is also associated with the "Arabian" (or "Arapet") <a href="/wiki/Icon" title="Icon">icon</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Theotokos" title="Theotokos">Theotokos</a> (Mother of God), which is commemorated on 6 September (19 September).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The Malankara Orthodox church celebrates his feast on three days, 3 July <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>(in memory of the relic translation to Edessa), 18 December (the Day he was lanced),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and 21 December (when he died).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_history_and_traditions">Later history and traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_the_Apostle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later history and traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The Passing of Mary</i>, adjudged heretical by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gelasius_I" title="Pope Gelasius I">Pope Gelasius I</a> in 494, was attributed to <a href="/wiki/Joseph_of_Arimathea" title="Joseph of Arimathea">Joseph of Arimathea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The document states that Thomas was the only witness of the <a href="/wiki/Assumption_of_Mary" title="Assumption of Mary">Assumption of Mary</a> into heaven. The other apostles were miraculously transported to Jerusalem to witness her death. Thomas was left in India, but after her first <a href="/wiki/Burial" title="Burial">burial</a>, he was transported to her <a href="/wiki/Tomb" title="Tomb">tomb</a>, where he witnessed her bodily assumption into <a href="/wiki/Heaven" title="Heaven">heaven</a>, from which she dropped her <a href="/wiki/Girdle" title="Girdle">girdle</a>. In an inversion of the story of Thomas' doubts, the other apostles are skeptical of Thomas' story until they see the empty tomb and the girdle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ccel_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-ccel-27">[27]</a></sup> Thomas' receipt of the girdle is commonly depicted in medieval and pre-<a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trent" title="Council of Trent">Tridentine</a> Renaissance art,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> the apostle's infamous doubting reduced to a metaphorical knot in the Bavarian baroque <a href="/wiki/Mary_Untier_of_Knots" title="Mary Untier of Knots">Mary Untier of Knots</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_in_India">Mission in India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_the_Apostle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mission in India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas_Christians" title="Saint Thomas Christians">Saint Thomas Christians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_India" title="Christianity in India">Christianity in India</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_Kerala" title="Christianity in Kerala">Christianity in Kerala</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tomb_of_St._Thomas_in_India.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1756" data-file-width="2394" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Tomb_of_St._Thomas_in_India.JPG/220px-Tomb_of_St._Thomas_in_India.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Tomb_of_St._Thomas_in_India.JPG/330px-Tomb_of_St._Thomas_in_India.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Tomb_of_St._Thomas_in_India.JPG/440px-Tomb_of_St._Thomas_in_India.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tomb_of_St._Thomas_in_India.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The tomb of St. Thomas the Apostle in <a href="/wiki/Mylapore" title="Mylapore">Mylapore</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Thomas is traditionally believed to have sailed to India in AD 52 to spread the Christian faith, and is believed to have landed at the port of <a href="/wiki/Muziris" title="Muziris">Muziris</a>, (modern-day <a href="/wiki/North_Paravur" title="North Paravur">North Paravur</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kodungalloor" title="Kodungalloor">Kodungalloor</a> in modern-day <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a> state) where there was a <a href="/wiki/Cochin_Jews" title="Cochin Jews">Jewish community</a> at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erwin_Fahlbusch_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Erwin_Fahlbusch-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orpa_Slapak_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Orpa_Slapak-4">[4]</a></sup> The port was destroyed in 1341 by a massive flood that realigned the coasts. He is believed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Thomas_Christian" title="St Thomas Christian">St Thomas Christian</a> tradition to have established <i>Ezharappallikal</i> or <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seven_and_Half_Churches" title="Seven and Half Churches">Seven and Half Churches</a></i> in Kerala. These churches are at Kodungallur, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Thomas_Syro-Malabar_Catholic_Church,_Palayoor" title="St. Thomas Syro-Malabar Catholic Church, Palayoor">Palayoor</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kottakkavu_Mar_Thoma_Syro-Malabar_Pilgrim_Church,_North_Paravur" title="Kottakkavu Mar Thoma Syro-Malabar Pilgrim Church, North Paravur">Kottakkavu</a> (Paravur), <a href="/wiki/Kokkamangalam" title="Kokkamangalam">Kokkamangalam</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niranam_Church" title="Niranam Church">Niranam</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nilackal_St._Thomas_Church" title="Nilackal St. Thomas Church">Nilackal (Chayal)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kollam" title="Kollam">Kollam</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thiruvithamcode_Arappally" title="Thiruvithamcode Arappally">Thiruvithamcode</a> (half church).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ppn_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-ppn-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>It was to a land of dark people he was sent, to clothe them by Baptism in white robes. His grateful dawn dispelled India's painful darkness. It was his mission to espouse India to the One-Begotten. The merchant is blessed for having so great a treasure. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edessa,_Mesopotamia" title="Edessa, Mesopotamia">Edessa</a> thus became the blessed city by possessing the greatest pearl India could yield. Thomas works miracles in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, and at Edessa Thomas is destined to baptize peoples perverse and steeped in darkness, and that in the land of India.</p>
<dl>
<dd>—Hymns of <a href="/wiki/Ephrem_the_Syrian" title="Ephrem the Syrian">St. Ephrem</a>, edited by Lamy (Ephr. Hymni et Sermones, IV).</dd>
</dl>
</blockquote>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eusebius_of_Caesarea" title="Eusebius of Caesarea">Eusebius of Caesarea</a> quotes <a href="/wiki/Origen" title="Origen">Origen</a> (died mid-3rd century) as having stated that Thomas was the apostle to the <a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthians</a>, but Thomas is better known as the missionary to <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> through the <i><a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Thomas" title="Acts of Thomas">Acts of Thomas</a></i>, perhaps written as late as c. 200. In Edessa, where his remains were venerated, the poet St. Ephrem (died 373) wrote a hymn in which the Devil cries,</p>
<blockquote>
<dl>
<dd>... Into what land shall I fly from the just?</dd>
</dl>
<p>I stirred up Death the Apostles to slay, that by their death I might escape their blows.<br/>
But harder still am I now stricken: the Apostle I slew in India has overtaken me in Edessa; here and there he is all himself.<br/>
There went I, and there was he: here and there to my grief I find him.</p>
<dl>
<dd>—quoted in Medlycott 1905, ch. ii.</dd>
</dl>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ephrem_the_Syrian" title="Ephrem the Syrian">St. Ephrem</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_the_Church" title="Doctor of the Church">doctor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Christianity" title="Syriac Christianity">Syriac Christianity</a>, writes in the forty-second of his "Carmina Nisibina" that the Apostle was put to death in India, and that his remains were subsequently buried in Edessa, brought there by an unnamed merchant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MEDLYCOTT_1905_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-MEDLYCOTT_1905-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>A Syrian ecclesiastical calendar of an early date confirms the above and gives the merchant a name. The entry reads: "3 July, St. Thomas who was pierced with a lance in 'India'. His body is in Urhai (Edessa) having been brought there by the merchant Khabin. A great festival."</p>
<p>A long public tradition in Edessa honoring Thomas as the "Apostle of India" resulted in several surviving <a href="/wiki/Hymn" title="Hymn">hymns</a>, that are attributed to Ephrem, copied in codices of the 8th and 9th centuries. References in the hymns preserve the tradition that Thomas' bones were brought from India to Edessa by a merchant, and that the relics worked miracles both in India and Edessa. A pontiff assigned his feast day and a king and a queen erected his shrine. The Thomas traditions became embodied in <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a> liturgy, thus they were universally credited by the Christian community there. There is a legend that Thomas had met the <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Magi" title="Biblical Magi">biblical Magi</a> on his way to India.</p>
<p>According to Eusebius' record, Thomas and Bartholomew were assigned to Parthia and India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFM_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-AFM-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Didascalia" title="Didascalia">Didascalia</a></i> (dating from the end of the 3rd century) states, “India and all countries condering it, even to the farthest seas... received the apostolic ordinances from Judas Thomas, who was a guide and ruler in the church which he built.” Moreover, there is a wealth of confirmatory information in the Syriac writings, <a href="/wiki/Liturgical_book" title="Liturgical book">liturgical books</a>, and calendars of the Church of the East, not to mention the writings of the Fathers, the calendars, the sacramentaries, and the martyrologies of the Roman, Greek and Ethiopian churches.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_AFM"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_the_Apostle#endnote_AFM">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>An early 3rd-century Syriac work known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Thomas" title="Acts of Thomas">Acts of Thomas</a></i><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_AFM"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_the_Apostle#endnote_AFM">[2]</a></sup> connects the apostle's Indian ministry with two kings, one in the north and the other in the south. According to one of the legends in the <i>Acts</i>, Thomas was at first reluctant to accept this mission, but the Lord appeared to him in a night vision and said,</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“Fear not, Thomas. Go away to India and proclaim the Word, for my grace shall be with you.” But the Apostle still demurred, so the Lord overruled the stubborn disciple by ordering circumstances so compelling that he was forced to accompany an 'Indian' merchant, Abbanes, as a slave to his native place in northwest 'India', where he found himself in the service of the Indo-Parthian king, <a href="/wiki/Gondophares" title="Gondophares">Gondophares</a>. According to the Acts of Thomas, the apostle's ministry resulted in many conversions throughout the kingdom, including the king and his brother.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_AFM"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_the_Apostle#endnote_AFM">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Remains of some of his buildings, influenced by Greek architecture, indicate that he was a great builder. According to the legend, Thomas was a skilled carpenter and was bidden to build a palace for the king. However, the Apostle decided to teach the king a lesson by devoting the royal grant to acts of charity and thereby laying up treasure for the heavenly abode. Although little is known of the immediate growth of the church, Bar-Daisan (154–223) reports that in his time there were Christian tribes in India which claimed to have been converted by Thomas and to have books and relics to prove it.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_AFM"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_the_Apostle#endnote_AFM">[4]</a></sup> But at least by the year of the establishment of the Second Persian Empire (226), there were bishops of the Church of the East in northwest India (<a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balochistan_(region)" title="Balochistan (region)">Baluchistan</a>), with laymen and clergy alike engaging in missionary activity.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_AFM"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_the_Apostle#endnote_AFM">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is most significant that, aside from a small remnant of the Church of the East in <a href="/wiki/Kurdistan" title="Kurdistan">Kurdistan</a>, the only other church to maintain a distinctive identity is the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas_Christians" title="Saint Thomas Christians">Saint Thomas Christian</a> congregations along the <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a> in southwest India. According to the most ancient tradition of this church, Thomas evangelized this area and then crossed to the <a href="/wiki/Coromandel_Coast" title="Coromandel Coast">Coromandel Coast</a> of southeast India, where, after carrying out a second mission, he died at <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">Chennai</a>. Throughout the period under review, the church in India was under the jurisdiction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edessa,_Mesopotamia" title="Edessa, Mesopotamia">Edessa</a>, which was then under the Mesopotamian patriarchate at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seleucia-Ctesiphon" title="Seleucia-Ctesiphon">Seleucia-Ctesiphon</a> and later at Baghdad and Mosul. Historian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vincent_A._Smith" title="Vincent A. Smith">Vincent A. Smith</a> says, “It must be admitted that a personal visit of the Apostle Thomas to South India was easily feasible in the traditional belief that he came by way of <a href="/wiki/Socotra" title="Socotra">Socotra</a>, where an ancient Christian settlement undoubtedly existed. I am now satisfied that the Christian church of South India is extremely ancient...”.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_AFM"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_the_Apostle#endnote_AFM">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thomas is believed to have left northwest India when invasion threatened and traveled by vessel to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malabar_coast" title="Malabar coast">Malabar coast</a>, possibly visiting southeast Arabia and Socotra en route, and landing at the former flourishing port of <a href="/wiki/Muziris" title="Muziris">Muziris</a> (modern-day <a href="/wiki/North_Paravur" title="North Paravur">North Paravur</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kodungalloor" title="Kodungalloor">Kodungalloor</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ppn_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-ppn-30">[30]</a></sup> (c. 51–52 AD) in the company of a Jewish merchant Abbanes (Hebban). From there he is said to have preached the gospel throughout the Malabar coast. The various churches he founded were located mainly on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periyar_River" title="Periyar River">Periyar River</a> and its tributaries and along the coast, where there were Jewish colonies.In accordance with apostolic custom, Thomas ordained teachers and leaders or elders, who were reported to be the earliest ministry of the Malabar Church.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_the_Apostle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to tradition, St. Thomas was killed at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St.Thomas_Mount" title="St.Thomas Mount">St.Thomas Mount</a>, near Chennai, in 72 and his body was interred in <a href="/wiki/Mylapore" title="Mylapore">Mylapore</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ephrem_the_Syrian" title="Ephrem the Syrian">Ephrem the Syrian</a> states that the Apostle was martyred in India, and that his relics were taken then to Edessa. This is the earliest known record of his martyrdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since at least the 16th century, the <a href="/wiki/St._Thomas_Mount" title="St. Thomas Mount">St. Thomas Mount</a> has been a common site revered by Hindus, Muslims and Christians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWH_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-WWH-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:31</sup> The records of <a href="/wiki/Duarte_Barbosa" title="Duarte Barbosa">Barbosa</a> from early 16th century inform that the tomb was then maintained by a Muslim who kept a lamp burning there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWH_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-WWH-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:237</sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Thome_Basilica" title="San Thome Basilica">San Thome Basilica</a> Mylapore, <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">Chennai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">Tamil Nadu</a>, India presently located at the tomb was first built in the 16th century and rebuilt in the 19th.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mylapore.2C_Chennai.2C_Tamil_Nadu.2C_India">Mylapore, Chennai, Tamil Nadu, India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_the_Apostle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mylapore, Chennai, Tamil Nadu, India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>St. Thomas arrived in Southern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> - in the present day Indian state of <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a> and a few relics are still kept at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Thome_Basilica" title="San Thome Basilica">San Thome Basilica</a> in Chennai, Mylapore, India. <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">Marco Polo</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Venetian</a> traveller and author of <i>Description of the World,</i> popularly known as <i>Il Milione,</i> is reputed to have visited Southern India in 1288 and 1292. The first date has been rejected as he was in China at the time, but the second date is accepted by many historians.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title='"need'>citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> He also stopped at Quilon (<a href="/wiki/Kollam" title="Kollam">Kollam</a>) on the western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malabar_coast" title="Malabar coast">Malabar coast</a> of India, where he met <a href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas_Christians" title="Saint Thomas Christians">Syrian Christians</a> and recorded their tradition of St. Thomas and his tomb on the eastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coromandel_coast" title="Coromandel coast">Coromandel coast</a> of the country. <i>Il Milione,</i> the book he dictated on his return to Europe, was on its publication condemned by the Church as a collection of impious and improbable traveller's tales. It became very popular reading in medieval Europe and inspired Spanish and Portuguese sailors to seek out the fabulous (and possibly Christian) India described in it.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Edessa">Edessa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_the_Apostle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Edessa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>According to tradition, in 232 AD, the greater portion of relics of the Apostle Thomas are said to have been sent by an Indian king and brought from Mylapore to the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edessa,_Mesopotamia" title="Edessa, Mesopotamia">Edessa, Mesopotamia</a>, on which occasion his Syriac <i>Acts</i> were written.</p>
<p>The Indian king is named as "Mazdai" in Syriac sources, "Misdeos" and "Misdeus" in Greek and Latin sources respectively, which has been connected to the "Bazdeo" on the Kushan coinage of <a href="/wiki/Vasudeva_I" title="Vasudeva I">Vasudeva I</a>, the transition between "M" and "B" being a current one in Classical sources for Indian names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mario_Bussagli_p255_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mario_Bussagli_p255-38">[38]</a></sup> The martyrologist Rabban Sliba dedicated a special day to both the Indian king, his family, and St Thomas:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>Coronatio Thomae apostoli et Misdeus rex Indiae, Johannes eus filius huisque mater Tertia</i> (Coronation of Thomas the Apostle, and Misdeus king of India, together with his son Johannes (thought to be a latinization of <i>Vizan</i>) and his mother Tertia) Rabban Sliba<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mario_Bussagli_p255_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mario_Bussagli_p255-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In the 4th century, the martyrium erected over his burial place brought pilgrims to Edessa. In the 380s, <a href="/wiki/Egeria_(pilgrim)" title="Egeria (pilgrim)">Egeria</a> described her visit in a letter she sent to her community of nuns at home (<i>Itineraria Egeriae</i>):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>We arrived at Edessa in the Name of Christ our God, and, on our arrival, we straightway repaired to the church and memorial of saint Thomas. There, according to custom, prayers were made and the other things that were customary in the holy places were done; we read also some things concerning saint Thomas himself. The church there is very great, very beautiful and of new construction, well worthy to be the house of God, and as there was much that I desired to see, it was necessary for me to make a three days' stay there.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In 441, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magister_militum_per_Orientem" title="Magister militum per Orientem">Magister militum per Orientem</a></i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anatolius_(consul_440)" title="Anatolius (consul 440)">Anatolius</a> donated a silver coffin to hold the relics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edessa_2005.2C_pp._174_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Edessa_2005.2C_pp._174-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 522 AD, <a href="/wiki/Cosmas_Indicopleustes" title="Cosmas Indicopleustes">Cosmas Indicopleustes</a> (called the Alexandrian) visited the Malabar Coast. He is the first traveller who mentions Syrian Christians in Malabar, in his book <i>Christian Topography.</i> He mentions that in the town of "Kalliana" (Quilon or Kollam) there was a bishop who had been consecrated in Persia.</p>
<p>In 1144, the city was conquered by the <a href="/wiki/Zengid_dynasty" title="Zengid dynasty">Zengids</a> and the shrine destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edessa_2005.2C_pp._174_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Edessa_2005.2C_pp._174-40">[40]</a></sup></p>

<p>After a short stay on the Greek island of <a href="/wiki/Chios" title="Chios">Chios</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="describe, if you like, how they got from wherever to Chios (June 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> on 6 September 1258, the relics were transported to the West, and now rest in the Cathedral of St. Thomas the Apostle in <a href="/wiki/Ortona" title="Ortona">Ortona</a>, Italy. However, the skull of Thomas is said to be at <a href="/wiki/Monastery_of_Saint_John_the_Theologian" title="Monastery of Saint John the Theologian">Monastery of Saint John the Theologian</a> on the Greek island of <a href="/wiki/Patmos" title="Patmos">Patmos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ortona's three galleys reached the island of <a href="/wiki/Chios" title="Chios">Chios</a> in 1258, led by General Leone Acciaiuoli. In fact Chios was considered the island where St. Thomas, after the martyrdom in India, had been buried. A portion fought around the Peloponnese and the Aegean islands, the other in the sea lapping at the then Syrian coast. The three galleys of Ortona moved on the second front of the war and reached the island of Chios.</p>
<p>The tale is provided by Giambattista De Lectis, physician and writer of the 16th century of Ortona. After the looting, the navarca Ortona Leone went to pray in the main church of the island of Chios and was drawn to a chapel adorned and resplendent with lights. An elderly priest, through an interpreter informed him that in that oratory was venerated the Body of St. Thomas the Apostle. Lion, filled with an unusual sweetness, gathered in deep prayer. At that moment a light hand twice invited him to come closer. The navarca Leone reached out and took a bone from the largest hole of the tombstone, on which were carved the Greek letters and a halo depicted a bishop from the waist up. He was the confirmation of what he had said the old priest and that you are indeed in the presence of the Apostle's body. He went back on the galley and planned the theft for the next night, along with fellow Ruggiero Grogno. They lifted the heavy gravestone and watched the underlying relics. The wrapped in snow-white cloths them laid in a wooden box (stored at Ortona to the looting of 1566) and brought them aboard the galley. Lion, then, along with other comrades, he returned again in the church, took the tombstone and took her away. Just the Chinardo admiral was aware of the precious cargo moved all the sailors of the Muslim faith on other ships and ordered him to take the route to Ortona.</p>

According to the story of De Lectis, he was informed the abbot Jacopo responsible for Ortona Church, which predispose full provision for hospitality felt and shared by all the people. Since then the body of the apostle and the gravestone are preserved in the crypt of the Basilica. In 1259 a parchment written in Bari by the court under John Peacock contracts, the presence of five witnesses, preserved in Ortona at the Diocesan Library, confirming the veracity of that event, reported, as mentioned, by Giambattista De Lectis, physician and writer Ortona of the 16th century.</p>
<p>The relics resisted both the Saracen looting of 1566, that the destruction of the Nazis in the famous battle of Ortona, fought in late December 1943. The basilica was blown up because his civic eras tower lookout point considered by the allies, who were coming by sea from San Vito Chietino. <p>Tombstone of Thomas, brought to Ortona from Chios along with the relics of the Apostle, is currently preserved in the crypt of St Thomas Basilica, behind the altar. The urn containing the bones instead is placed under the altar. It is the cover of a fake coffin, fairly widespread burial form in the early Christian world, as the top of a tomb of less expensive material. The plaque has an inscription and a bas-relief that refer, in many respects, to the Syro-Mesopotamian. Tombstone Thomas the Apostle on inclusion can be read, in Greek characters uncial, the expression 'osios thomas, that St. Thomas. It can be dated from the point of view palaeographic and lexical to III-V century, a time when the term osios is still used as a synonym of aghios in that holy is he that is in the grace of God and is inserted in the Church: the two vocabulary, therefore, indicate the Christians. In the particular case of St. Thomas' plaque, then, the word osios can easily be the translation of the word Syriac <i>mar</i> (Lord), attributed in the ancient world, but also to the present day, is a saint to be a bishop.</p>

<p>A number of early Christian writings written during centuries immediately following the first Ecumenical Council of 325 mention Thomas' mission.</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/New_Testament_apocrypha" title="New Testament apocrypha">apocryphal</a> <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Thomas" title="Acts of Thomas">Acts of Thomas</a>, sometimes called by its full name <i>The Acts of Judas Thomas</i>: 2nd/3rd century (c. 180–230)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Apocryphal_Acts_of_the_Apostles_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Apocryphal_Acts_of_the_Apostles-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Gist of the testimony: The Apostles cast lots as to where they should go, and to Thomas fell India. Habban took Thomas to king <a href="/wiki/Gondophares" title="Gondophares">Gondophares</a>, the ruler of <a href="/wiki/Indo-Parthian_Kingdom" title="Indo-Parthian Kingdom">Indo-Parthian Kingdom</a>, as an architect and carpenter. The journey to <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> is described in detail. After a long residence in the court at <a href="/wiki/Taxila" title="Taxila">Taxila</a>, he ordained leaders for the Church and left in a chariot for the kingdom of Mazdei. According to the Acts of St. Thomas, the Kingdom of Mazdai, in the Southern India, was ruled by <a href="/wiki/Vasudeva_I#Saint_Thomas_Christian_connection" title="Vasudeva I">King Misdeus</a>. Some <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek</a> Satraps, the descendents of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>, were vassals to the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Parthian_Kingdom" title="Indo-Parthian Kingdom">Indo-Parthian Kingdom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The king <i>Misdeus</i> was infuriated when St. Thomas converted his <i>Queen Tertia</i>, son <i>Juzanes</i>, sister-in-law princess <a href="/wiki/Mygdonia" title="Mygdonia">Mygdonia</a> (a province of <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>), and her friend <i>Markia</i>. Misdeus led St. Thomas outside the city and ordered four soldiers to take him to the nearby hill, where the soldiers speared St. Thomas and killed him. <i>Syphorus</i> was elected the first presbyter by the brethren, after Thomas' death, while Juzanes the prince became the deacon. The names Misdeus, Tertia, Juzanes, Syphorus, Markia, and Mygdonia suggest Greek descent or Hellenised Persian descent<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> There, after performing many miracles, he dies a martyr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-early_christian_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-early_christian-47">[47]</a></sup> During the rule of <a href="/wiki/Vasudeva_I" title="Vasudeva I">Vasudeva I</a>, the Kushan emperor, the bones of St. Thomas were transferred from the <a href="/wiki/Mylapore" title="Mylapore">Mylapore</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edessa,_Mesopotamia" title="Edessa, Mesopotamia">Edessa</a>. These are generally rejected by various Christian religions as either apocryphal or heretical. The two centuries that lapsed between the life of the apostle and the recording of this work cast doubt on their authenticity.</li>
<li>Doctrine of the Apostles, 3rd century; Church represented: Syrian <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cardinalmai_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-cardinalmai-48">[48]</a></sup> “After the death of the Apostles there were Guides and Rulers in the Churches… They again at their deaths also committed and delivered to their disciples after them everything which they had received from the Apostles; … (also what) Judas Thomas (had written) from India”.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="is this simply one more quote from the Acts of Thomas? </ul>

<p>“India and all its own countries, and those bordering on it, even to the farther sea, received the Apostle’s hand of Priesthood from Judas Thomas, who was Guide and Ruler in the Church which he built and ministered there”. In what follows “the whole Persia of the Assyrians and Medes, and of the countries round about Babylon… even to the borders of the Indians and even to the country of <a href="/wiki/Gog_and_Magog" title="Gog and Magog">Gog and Magog</a>” are said to have received the Apostles’ Hand of Priesthood from Aggaeus the disciple of <a href="/wiki/Thaddeus_of_Edessa" title="Thaddeus of Edessa">Addaeus</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cureton32_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-cureton32-49">[49]</a></sup></p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Gregory_of_Nazianzus" title="Gregory of Nazianzus">Gregory of Nazianzus</a>: 4th century (died 389); Church Represented: Alexandrian. Biographical Note: Gregory of Nazianzus was born AD 330, <a href="/wiki/Consecration" title="Consecration">consecrated</a> a bishop by his friend <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Basil" title="St. Basil">St. Basil</a>; in 372 his father, the Bishop of Nazianzus, induced him to share his charge. In 379 the people of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> called him to be their bishop. By the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthodox_Church" title="Orthodox Church">Orthodox Church</a> he is emphatically called "the Theologian".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-contra_arianos_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-contra_arianos-56">[56]</a></sup> “What? were not the Apostles strangers amidst the many nations and countries over which they spread themselves? … Peter indeed may have belonged to Judea; but what had Paul in common with the gentiles, Luke with Achaia, Andrew with Epirus, John with Ephesus, Thomas with India, Mark with Italy?” <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20c.42.43_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-20c.42.43-57">[57]</a></sup></li>
<li>Ambrose of Milan: 4th century (died 397); Church Represented: Western. Biographical Note: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Ambrose" title="St. Ambrose">St. Ambrose</a> was thoroughly acquainted with the Greek and Latin Classics, and had a good deal of information on India and Indians. He speaks of the Gymnosophists of India, the Indian Ocean, the river Ganges etc., a number of times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-migne_140_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-migne_140-58">[58]</a></sup> “This admitted of the Apostles being sent without delay according to the saying of our Lord Jesus… Even those Kingdoms which were shut out by rugged mountains became accessible to them, as India to Thomas, Persia to Matthew...” <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-various_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-various-59">[59]</a></sup></li>
<li>St. Gregory of Tours (died 594) <a href="/wiki/Gregory_of_Tours" title="Gregory of Tours">St. Gregory's</a> testimony: “Thomas the Apostle, according to the narrative of his martyrdom is stated to have suffered in India. His holy remains (corpus), after a long interval of time, were removed to the city of Edessa in Syria and there interred. In that part of India where they first rested, stand a monastery and a church of striking dimensions, elaborately adorned and designed. This Theodore, who had been to the place, narrated to us.'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writings">Writings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_the_Apostle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Writings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<blockquote>
<p>Let none read the gospel according to Thomas, for it is the work, not of one of the twelve apostles, but of one of <a href="/wiki/Mani_(prophet)" title="Mani (prophet)">Mani's</a> three wicked disciples.</p>
<dl>
<dd>—<a href="/wiki/Cyril_of_Jerusalem" title="Cyril of Jerusalem">Cyril of Jerusalem</a>, <i>Cathechesis</i> V (4th century)</dd>
</dl>
</blockquote>
<p>In the first two centuries of the Christian era, a number of writings were circulated. It is unclear now why Thomas was seen as an authority for doctrine, although this belief is documented in Gnostic groups as early as the <i><a href="/wiki/Pistis_Sophia" title="Pistis Sophia">Pistis Sophia</a></i>. In that Gnostic work, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Magdalene" title="Mary Magdalene">Mary Magdalene</a> (one of the disciples) says:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Now at this time, my Lord, hear, so that I speak openly, for thou hast said to us "He who has ears to hear, let him hear:" Concerning the word which thou didst say to Philip: "Thou and Thomas and Matthew are the three to whom it has been given… to write every word of the Kingdom of the Light, and to bear witness to them"; hear now that I give the interpretation of these words. It is this which thy light-power once prophesied through Moses: "Through two and three witnesses everything will be established. The three witnesses are Philip and Thomas and Matthew"</p>
<dl>
<dd>—<i>Pistis Sophia</i> 1:43</dd>
</dl>
</blockquote>
<p>An early, non-Gnostic tradition may lie behind this statement, which also emphasizes the primacy of the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">Gospel of Matthew</a> in its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aramaic" title="Aramaic">Aramaic</a> form, over the other canonical three.</p>
<p>Besides the <i><a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Thomas" title="Acts of Thomas">Acts of Thomas</a></i> there was a widely circulated <i><a href="/wiki/Infancy_Gospel_of_Thomas" title="Infancy Gospel of Thomas">Infancy Gospel of Thomas</a></i> probably written in the later 2nd century, and probably also in <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, which relates the miraculous events and prodigies of Jesus' boyhood. This is the document which tells for the first time the familiar legend of the twelve sparrows which Jesus, at the age of five, fashioned from clay on the Sabbath day, which took wing and flew away. The earliest manuscript of this work is a 6th-century one in <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a>. This gospel was first referred to by <a href="/wiki/Irenaeus" title="Irenaeus">Irenaeus</a>; Ron Cameron notes: "In his citation, Irenaeus first quotes a non-canonical story that circulated about the childhood of Jesus and then goes directly on to quote a passage from the infancy narrative of the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke" title="Gospel of Luke">Gospel of Luke</a> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Luke#2:49" title="s:Bible (King James)/Luke">Luke 2:49</a>). Since the Infancy Gospel of Thomas records both of these stories, in relative close proximity to one another, it is possible that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apocryphal" title="Apocryphal">apocryphal</a> writing cited by <a href="/wiki/Irenaeus" title="Irenaeus">Irenaeus</a> is, in fact, what is now known as the Infancy Gospel of Thomas. Because of the complexities of the manuscript tradition, however, there is no certainty as to when the stories of the Infancy Gospel of Thomas began to be written down."</p>
<p>The best known in modern times of these documents is the "sayings" document that is being called the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Thomas" title="Gospel of Thomas">Gospel of Thomas</a>, a noncanonical work whose date is disputed. The opening line claims it is the work of "Didymos Judas Thomas" – whose identity is unknown. This work was discovered in a <a href="/wiki/Coptic_language" title="Coptic language">Coptic</a> translation in 1945 at the Egyptian village of <a href="/wiki/Nag_Hammadi" title="Nag Hammadi">Nag Hammadi</a>, near the site of the monastery of Chenoboskion. Once the Coptic text was published, scholars recognized that an earlier Greek translation had been published from fragments of <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a> found at <a href="/wiki/Oxyrhynchus" title="Oxyrhynchus">Oxyrhynchus</a> in the 1890s.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Saint_Thomas_Cross">Saint Thomas Cross</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_the_Apostle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Saint Thomas Cross">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nasrani_cross.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1473" data-file-width="1117" height="290" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Nasrani_cross.jpg/220px-Nasrani_cross.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Nasrani_cross.jpg/330px-Nasrani_cross.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Nasrani_cross.jpg/440px-Nasrani_cross.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nasrani_cross.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Saint Thomas Christian cross</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the 16th-century work <i>Jornada</i>, Antonio Gouvea writes of ornate crosses known as <i>Saint Thomas Crosses</i>. It is also known as Nasrani Menorah<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> or Mar Thoma Sleeba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> These crosses date from the 6th century and are found in a number of churches in Kerala, Mylapore and Goa. <i>Jornada</i> is the oldest known written document to refer to this type of cross as a St. Thomas Cross. Gouvea also writes about the veneration of the Cross at Cranganore, referring to the cross as "Cross of Christians". It is widely perceived as the symbol of Saint Thomas Christians.</p>
<p>There are several interpretations of the Nasrani symbol. The interpretation based on Christian Jewish tradition assumes that its design was based on Jewish <a href="/wiki/Menorah_(Temple)" title="Menorah (Temple)">menorah</a>, an ancient symbol of the Hebrews, which consists of seven branched lamp stand (candelabra).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The interpretation based on local culture states that the Cross without the figure of Jesus and with flowery arms symbolizing "joyfulness" points to the resurrection theology of St. Paul; the Holy Spirit on the top represents the role of Holy Spirit in the resurrection of Jesus Christ. The lotus symbolizing Buddhism and the Cross over it shows that Christianity was established in the land of Buddha. The 3 steps indicate Calvary and the rivulets, channels of grace flowing from the Cross.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
