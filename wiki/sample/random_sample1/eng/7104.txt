<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Apostolic see</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a>, an <b>apostolic see</b> is any <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_see" title="Episcopal see">episcopal see</a> whose foundation is attributed to one or more of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twelve_Apostles" title="Twelve Apostles">apostles</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a>.</p>
<p>The fourth canon of the <a href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Nicaea" title="First Council of Nicaea">First Council of Nicaea</a> of 325 attributed to the bishop of the capital (metropolis) of each <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">Roman province</a> (the "<a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_bishop" title="Metropolitan bishop">metropolitan bishop</a>") a position of authority among the bishops of the province, without reference to the founding figure of that bishop's see.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicaea_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nicaea-1">[1]</a></sup> Its sixth canon recognized the wider authority, extending beyond a single province, traditionally held by <a href="/wiki/Early_centers_of_Christianity#Rome" title="Early centers of Christianity">Rome</a> and <a href="/wiki/Early_centers_of_Christianity#Alexandria" title="Early centers of Christianity">Alexandria</a>, and the prerogatives of the churches in <a href="/wiki/Early_centers_of_Christianity#Antioch" title="Early centers of Christianity">Antioch</a> and the other provinces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicaea_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nicaea-1">[1]</a></sup> Of <a href="/wiki/Aelia_Capitolina" title="Aelia Capitolina">Aelia</a>, the Roman city built on the site of the destroyed city of <a href="/wiki/Early_centers_of_Christianity#Jerusalem" title="Early centers of Christianity">Jerusalem</a>, the council's seventh canon reads: "Since custom and ancient tradition have prevailed that the Bishop of Aelia should be honoured, let him, saving its due dignity to the Metropolis, have the next place of honour."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicaea_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nicaea-1">[1]</a></sup> The metropolis in question is generally taken to be <a href="/wiki/Early_centers_of_Christianity#Caesarea" title="Early centers of Christianity">Caesarea Maritima</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> though in the late 19th century <a href="/wiki/Philip_Schaff" title="Philip Schaff">Philip Schaff</a> also mentioned other views.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pentarchy">Pentarchy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apostolic_see&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pentarchy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pentarchy" title="Pentarchy">Pentarchy</a></div>
<p>This Council of Nicaea, being held in 325, of course made no mention of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, a city which was only officially founded five years later, at which point it became the capital of the Empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> But the <a href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Constantinople" title="First Council of Constantinople">First Council of Constantinople</a> (381) decreed in a canon of disputed validity: "The Bishop of Constantinople, however, shall have the prerogative of honour after the Bishop of Rome; because Constantinople is New Rome."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> A century after the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Chalcedon" title="Council of Chalcedon">Council of Chalcedon</a> (451) and the ensuing schism between those who accepted it and those who rejected it, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Christianity" title="Eastern Orthodox Christianity">Eastern Orthodox Christianity</a> wove these two sources together to develop the theory of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentarchy_(Christianity)" title="Pentarchy (Christianity)">Pentarchy</a>: "[F]ormulated in the legislation of the emperor <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Justinian I</a> (527–565), especially in his <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Juris_Civilis#Novellae" title="Corpus Juris Civilis">Novella</a> 131, the theory received formal ecclesiastical sanction at the <a href="/wiki/Quinisext_Council" title="Quinisext Council">Council in Trullo</a> (692), which ranked the five sees as Rome, Constantinople, Alexandria, Antioch, and Jerusalem."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Earlier, the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Ephesus" title="Council of Ephesus">Council of Ephesus</a> decreed in 431 that the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Cyprus" title="Church of Cyprus">Church of Cyprus</a> should be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autocephalous" title="Autocephalous">autocephalous</a>, against the claims of Antioch, the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_diocese" title="Roman diocese">Roman diocese</a> of the East, of which Cyprus was part.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Listing_of_the_sees">Listing of the sees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apostolic_see&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Listing of the sees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarchs" title="Patriarchs">patriarchs</a> of these five sees consider themselves to be successors of those given special status in these canons:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Rome</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> (<a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Saint Peter</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Paul" title="Saint Paul">Saint Paul</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ecumenical_Patriarchate_of_Constantinople" title="Ecumenical Patriarchate of Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, now <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a> in present-day Turkey (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Andrew" title="Saint Andrew">Saint Andrew</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Patriarch_of_Alexandria" title="Patriarch of Alexandria">Alexandria</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Mark" title="Saint Mark">Saint Mark the Evangelist</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Patriarch_of_Antioch" title="Patriarch of Antioch">Antioch</a>, in present-day <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> (<a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Saint Peter</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Church_of_Jerusalem" title="Greek Orthodox Church of Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Land" title="Holy Land">Holy Land</a> (<a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Saint Peter</a> and <a href="/wiki/James,_brother_of_Jesus" title="James, brother of Jesus">Saint James</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Other sees who claim to be founded by an apostle and thus can claim to be <i>apostolic sees</i> include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Cyprus" title="Church of Cyprus">Church of Cyprus</a>, based at New Justiniana (<a href="/wiki/Erdek" title="Erdek">Erdek</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Paul" title="Saint Paul">Saint Paul</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Barnabas" title="Saint Barnabas">Saint Barnabas</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/See_of_Athens" title="See of Athens">See of Athens</a>, Greece (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Paul" title="Saint Paul">Saint Paul</a>)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/See_of_Ephesus" title="See of Ephesus">See of Ephesus</a>, in present-day Turkey (<a href="/wiki/John_the_Apostle" title="John the Apostle">John the Apostle</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Compostela" title="Santiago de Compostela">Santiago de Compostela</a>, in present-day Spain (<a href="/wiki/James,_son_of_Zebedee" title="James, son of Zebedee">James, son of Zebedee</a>), also known as James the Apostle</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_of_Babylon" title="Patriarch of Babylon">Babylon</a> or <a href="/wiki/List_of_Patriarchs_of_the_Church_of_the_East" title="List of Patriarchs of the Church of the East">Seleucia-Ctesiphon</a>, in present-day Iraq (<a href="/wiki/Thomas_the_Apostle" title="Thomas the Apostle">Thomas the Apostle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bartholomew_the_Apostle" title="Bartholomew the Apostle">Bartholomew the Apostle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thaddeus_of_Edessa" title="Thaddeus of Edessa">Thaddeus of Edessa</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_bishops_and_patriarchs_of_Aquileia" title="List of bishops and patriarchs of Aquileia">Aquileia</a>, in northeastern Italy (<a href="/wiki/Mark_the_Evangelist" title="Mark the Evangelist">Mark the Evangelist</a> as one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seventy_Apostles" title="Seventy Apostles">Seventy Apostles</a>)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/See_of_Milan" title="See of Milan">See of Milan</a>, in northwestern Italy (<a href="/wiki/Barnabas" title="Barnabas">Barnabas</a> the Apostle)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/See_of_Syracuse" title="See of Syracuse">See of Syracuse</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> (Peter)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Philippi#The_early_Christian_era" title="Philippi">Philippi</a>, in Greece (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Paul" title="Saint Paul">Saint Paul</a>)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/See_of_Thessaloniki" title="See of Thessaloniki">See of Thessaloniki</a>, in Greece (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Paul" title="Saint Paul">Saint Paul</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/See_of_Corinth" title="See of Corinth">See of Corinth</a>, in Greece (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Paul" title="Saint Paul">Saint Paul</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_of_Malta" title="Church of Malta">Church of Malta</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Paul" title="Saint Paul">Saint Paul</a>)</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church" title="Russian Orthodox Church">Russian Orthodox Church</a> claims a connection with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Andrew" title="Saint Andrew">Saint Andrew</a>, who is said to have visited the area where the city of <a href="/wiki/Kiev" title="Kiev">Kiev</a> later arose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Armenian_Apostolic_Church" title="Armenian Apostolic Church">Armenian Apostolic Church</a> (Thaddaeus (<a href="/wiki/Jude_the_Apostle" title="Jude the Apostle">Jude the Apostle</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Bartholomew_the_Apostle" title="Bartholomew the Apostle">Bartholomew the Apostle</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Orthodox_Tewahedo_Church" title="Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church">Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church</a> maintains that Christianity was originally introduced to Ethiopia via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Philip_the_Evangelist" title="Saint Philip the Evangelist">Saint Philip the Evangelist</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Georgian_Orthodox_Church" title="Georgian Orthodox Church">Orthodox Church of Georgia</a> claims Saint Andrew and <a href="/wiki/Simon_the_Zealot" title="Simon the Zealot">Simon the Canaanite</a> as its founders.</li>
<li>The Malankara Orthodox Syrian Church in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> (Thomas the Apostle)</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specific_reference_to_Rome">Specific reference to Rome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apostolic_see&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Specific reference to Rome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Papal_supremacy" title="Papal supremacy">Papal supremacy</a></div>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic</a> usage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> "<b><i>the</i> Apostolic See</b>" is used in the singular and capitalized to refer specifically to the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">See of Rome</a>, with reference to the <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a>'s status as <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_succession" title="Apostolic succession">successor</a> of the Apostle Peter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> This usage existed already at the time of the third <a href="/wiki/Ecumenical_council" title="Ecumenical council">ecumenical council</a>, held at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Ephesus" title="First Council of Ephesus">Ephesus</a> in 431, at which the phrase "our most holy and blessed pope <a href="/wiki/Pope_Celestine_I" title="Pope Celestine I">Cœlestine</a>, bishop of the Apostolic See" was used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Canon_law_(Catholic_Church)" title="Canon law (Catholic Church)">Catholic canon law</a>, the term is applied also to the various departments of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Curia" title="Roman Curia">Roman Curia</a>. The Code of Canon Law states: "In this Code the terms Apostolic See or Holy See mean not only the Roman Pontiff, but also, unless the contrary is clear from the nature of things or from the context, the Secretariat of State, the Council for the public affairs of the Church, and the other Institutes of the Roman Curia."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The bodies in question are seen as speaking on behalf of the See of Rome.</p>
