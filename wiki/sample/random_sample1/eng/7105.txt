<p><i><b>Acta Apostolicae Sedis</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "Register of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apostolic_See" title="Apostolic See">Apostolic See</a>"), often cited as <b>AAS</b>, is the official <a href="/wiki/Gazette" title="Gazette">gazette</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a>, appearing about twelve times a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODCC_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ODCC-1">[1]</a></sup> It was established by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_X" title="Pope Pius X">Pope Pius X</a> on 29 September 1908 with the decree <i>Promulgandi Pontificias Constitutiones</i>, and publication began in January 1909.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODCC_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ODCC-1">[1]</a></sup> It contains all the principal decrees, encyclical letters, decisions of <a href="/wiki/Congregation_(Roman_Curia)" title="Congregation (Roman Curia)">Roman congregations</a>, and notices of ecclesiastical appointments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hardon_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hardon-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Canon_law_(Catholic_Church)" title="Canon law (Catholic Church)">laws</a> contained in it are to be considered promulgated when published, and effective three months from date of issue, unless a shorter or longer time is specified in the law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hardon_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hardon-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1909_Acta_Apostolicae_Sedis.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="960" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/1909_Acta_Apostolicae_Sedis.jpg/220px-1909_Acta_Apostolicae_Sedis.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/1909_Acta_Apostolicae_Sedis.jpg/330px-1909_Acta_Apostolicae_Sedis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/1909_Acta_Apostolicae_Sedis.jpg/440px-1909_Acta_Apostolicae_Sedis.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1909_Acta_Apostolicae_Sedis.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cover page and leaf of Vol. 1, No. 1 of the <i>Acta Apostolicae Sedis</i> (1909)</div>

<p>It replaced a similar publication that had existed since 1865, under the title of <i><a href="/wiki/Acta_Sanctae_Sedis" title="Acta Sanctae Sedis">Acta Sanctae Sedis</a></i>. Though not designated as the official means of promulgating laws of the Holy See, this was on 23 May 1904 declared an organ of the Holy See to the extent that all documents printed in it were considered "authentic and official".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> As indicated above, the <i>Acta Sanctae Sedis</i> ceased publication four years later.</p>
<p><i>Acta Apostolicae Sedis</i> is published in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>.</p>
<p>Since 1929, <i>Acta Apostolicae Sedis</i> carries a supplement in <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>, called <i>Supplemento per le leggi e disposizioni dello Stato della Città del Vaticano</i>, containing laws and regulations of <a href="/wiki/Vatican_City" title="Vatican City">Vatican City</a>, the city-state founded in that year. In accordance with paragraph 2 of the <i>Legge sulle fonti del diritto</i> of 7 June 1929,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> the laws of the state are promulgated by being included in this supplement.</p>
