<p>The <b>coup of <a href="/wiki/Cao_Mao" title="Cao Mao">Cao Mao</a></b> was a <a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d'état">coup d'état</a> that occurred in 260 in <a href="/wiki/Luoyang" title="Luoyang">Luoyang</a>, the capital of the state of <a href="/wiki/Cao_Wei" title="Cao Wei">Cao Wei</a>, during the <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms" title="Three Kingdoms">Three Kingdoms</a> period. <a href="/wiki/Cao_Mao" title="Cao Mao">Cao Mao</a>, the nominal emperor of Wei, attempted to launch a coup to oust the regent <a href="/wiki/Sima_Zhao" title="Sima Zhao">Sima Zhao</a>, who effectively controlled the Wei government. However, the coup concluded with Cao Mao's death and Sima Zhao retaining his status. Contrary to its intention, the coup actually increased the Sima clan's power and influence in Wei, thus providing a foundation for the eventual usurpation of the Wei throne in 265 by Sima Zhao's son <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wu_of_Jin" title="Emperor Wu of Jin">Sima Yan</a>, who founded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jin_Dynasty_(265%E2%80%93420)" title="Jin Dynasty (265–420)">Western Jin Dynasty</a>.</p>
<p>The coup is also mentioned in the historical novel <i><a href="/wiki/Romance_of_the_Three_Kingdoms" title="Romance of the Three Kingdoms">Romance of the Three Kingdoms</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Luo_Guanzhong" title="Luo Guanzhong">Luo Guanzhong</a>, which dramatises the history of the late <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Han_Dynasty" title="Eastern Han Dynasty">Eastern Han Dynasty</a> and the Three Kingdoms period. The events of the coup described in the novel are largely similar to that described in historical sources.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coup_of_Cao_Mao&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The state of Cao Wei was established in 220 by <a href="/wiki/Cao_Pi" title="Cao Pi">Cao Pi</a>, which marked the start of the <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms" title="Three Kingdoms">Three Kingdoms</a> period in China. The authority of the Wei imperial family had been weakening since the death of the second Wei emperor, <a href="/wiki/Cao_Rui" title="Cao Rui">Cao Rui</a>, and reached a nadir after the <a href="/wiki/Incident_at_Gaoping_Tombs" title="Incident at Gaoping Tombs">Incident at Gaoping Tombs</a> in 249, when the Wei general <a href="/wiki/Sima_Yi" title="Sima Yi">Sima Yi</a> seized power from the regent <a href="/wiki/Cao_Shuang" title="Cao Shuang">Cao Shuang</a>. Sima Yi died in 251 and was succeeded by his sons <a href="/wiki/Sima_Shi" title="Sima Shi">Sima Shi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sima_Zhao" title="Sima Zhao">Sima Zhao</a>, who effectively controlled the Wei government.</p>
<p>In 254, Sima Shi deposed the third Wei ruler <a href="/wiki/Cao_Fang" title="Cao Fang">Cao Fang</a> and installed a 13-year-old <a href="/wiki/Cao_Mao" title="Cao Mao">Cao Mao</a> on the throne. From the start of his reign, Cao Mao acted in defiance of the Simas, refusing to accept the imperial seal directly from Sima Shi, and, after Sima Shi's death in 255, he attempted to order Sima Zhao to remain in <a href="/wiki/Xuchang" title="Xuchang">Xuchang</a> to keep watch over Shouchun (present-day <a href="/wiki/Shou_County" title="Shou County">Shou County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anhui" title="Anhui">Anhui</a>), where a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_Rebellion_in_Shouchun" title="Second Rebellion in Shouchun">rebellion</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Guanqiu_Jian" title="Guanqiu Jian">Guanqiu Jian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wen_Qin" title="Wen Qin">Wen Qin</a> had just been suppressed. However, Sima Zhao ignored Cao Mao's edict and returned to the capital <a href="/wiki/Luoyang" title="Luoyang">Luoyang</a>. While Sima Zhao administered state affairs, Cao Mao attempted to gain the favour of the literati in the court through unremarkable meetings to discuss literature with some officials—Sima Zhao's cousin <a href="/wiki/Sima_Wang" title="Sima Wang">Sima Wang</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wang_Shen_(Three_Kingdoms)" title="Wang Shen (Three Kingdoms)">Wang Shen</a> (王沈), <a href="/wiki/Pei_Xiu" title="Pei Xiu">Pei Xiu</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zhong_Hui" title="Zhong Hui">Zhong Hui</a>. Cao Mao also provided Sima Wang with a chariot and five imperial guardsmen as escorts because the latter lived further away from the palace than the others.</p>
<p>In 258, Sima Zhao was offered and declined title as Duke of Jin and the <a href="/wiki/Nine_bestowments" title="Nine bestowments">nine bestowments</a> over nine times. Discussion and debate over the matter allowed him to judge his level of support.</p>
<p>In 259 a year later, Cao Mao received sightings of yellow dragons in two wells, and he wrote <a href="/wiki/Cao_Mao#Reign" title="Cao Mao">a poem</a> identifying the dragons as being trapped inside wells and likened himself to the dragons. Sima Zhao saw the poem and was angry.</p>
<p>In 260 Sima Zhao was again offered title as Duke of Jin and the <a href="/wiki/Nine_bestowments" title="Nine bestowments">nine bestowments</a> and did not immediately decline.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_coup">The coup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coup_of_Cao_Mao&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The coup">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 260, when he was 19, Cao Mao gathered his associates <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wang_Shen_(Three_Kingdoms)" title="Wang Shen (Three Kingdoms)">Wang Shen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wang_Jing_(Three_Kingdoms)" title="Wang Jing (Three Kingdoms)">Wang Jing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wang_Ye_(Cao_Wei)" title="Wang Ye (Cao Wei)">Wang Ye</a> declaring his intention to make a last-ditch attempt to overthrow Sima Zhao, even at the cost of his life.</p>
<p>Wang Jing advised the emperor against such action, but Cao Mao ignored Wang Jing's advice and informed <a href="/wiki/Empress_Guo_(Cao_Rui%27s_wife)" title="Empress Guo (Cao Rui's wife)">Empress Dowager Guo</a> of his plan. In his absence, Wang Shen and Wang Ye secretly deserted Cao Mao and notified Sima Zhao about the plot.</p>
<p>Cao Mao then led his forces from the palace, personally wielding sword. Sima Zhao's younger brother <a href="/wiki/Sima_Zhou" title="Sima Zhou">Sima Zhou</a> attempted to put up resistance at one of the gates, but was defeated and fled. Sima Zhao's aide <a href="/wiki/Jia_Chong" title="Jia Chong">Jia Chong</a> led another defense at the South Watchtower. When Cheng Ji (成濟), a military officer under Jia Chong, asked Jia what to do, Jia told him to defend the Sima clan regardless of the consequences. Cheng Ji then approached Cao Mao and killed the emperor with his spear.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coup_of_Cao_Mao&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After Cao Mao's death, the public called for <a href="/wiki/Jia_Chong" title="Jia Chong">Jia Chong</a>'s execution on the grounds that he had committed <a href="/wiki/Regicide" title="Regicide">regicide</a>. Sima Zhao forced <a href="/wiki/Empress_Guo_(Cao_Rui%27s_wife)" title="Empress Guo (Cao Rui's wife)">Empress Dowager Guo</a> to posthumously demote Cao Mao to the status of a commoner, and then ordered <a href="/wiki/Wang_Jing_(Three_Kingdoms)" title="Wang Jing (Three Kingdoms)">Wang Jing</a> and his clan to be executed. The following day, after pleas from his uncle <a href="/wiki/Sima_Fu" title="Sima Fu">Sima Fu</a>, Sima Zhao asked Empress Dowager Guo to posthumously instate Cao Mao as the "Duke of Gaogui" (高貴鄉公) and bury Cao Mao with the ceremonies befitting that of a prince, though this was not actually done. Cao Huang (later renamed to <a href="/wiki/Cao_Huan" title="Cao Huan">Cao Huan</a>), the Duke of Changdao (常道鄉公), was chosen to succeed Cao Mao and was installed on the throne. 19 days later, Sima Zhao had Cheng Ji and his family executed to appease public anger but Jia Chong was spared.</p>
<p>Sima Zhao successfully retained his power during the coup and eliminated his opponents in the Wei court, leaving only the young Cao Huan as a puppet emperor under his control. He did not take the Wei throne, even until his death, but was granted the title of a vassal king—"King of Jin" (晉王)—and the <a href="/wiki/Nine_bestowments" title="Nine bestowments">nine bestowments</a> by Cao Huan in 264. Sima Zhao died in 265 and was succeeded by his son <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wu_of_Jin" title="Emperor Wu of Jin">Sima Yan</a>, who forced Cao Huan to abdicate in his favour and established the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jin_Dynasty_(265-420)" title="Jin Dynasty (265-420)">Western Jin Dynasty</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coup_of_Cao_Mao&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Dynasty_Warriors_7" title="Dynasty Warriors 7">seventh instalment</a> of <a href="/wiki/Koei" title="Koei">Koei</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Dynasty_Warriors" title="Dynasty Warriors">Dynasty Warriors</a></i> video game series, a stage is dedicated to the initial coup d'état against Sima Zhao, where it is called "Wei's Emperor's Last Stand"; the other two are mentioned in the narration.</p>
