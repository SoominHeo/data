<p>The <b>crab louse</b> (<i>Pthirus pubis</i>, also <b>pubic louse</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insect</a> that is an <a href="/wiki/Obligate_parasite" title="Obligate parasite">obligate</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">ectoparasite</a> of <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>, feeding exclusively on <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bolognia_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bolognia-1">[1]</a></sup> The crab louse usually is found in the person's <a href="/wiki/Pubic_hair" title="Pubic hair">pubic hair</a>. Although the louse cannot jump, it can also live in other areas of the body that are covered with coarse hair, such as the <a href="/wiki/Eyelash" title="Eyelash">eyelashes</a>. Humans are the only known hosts of the crab louse, although a closely related species, <i><a href="/wiki/Pthirus_gorillae" title="Pthirus gorillae">Pthirus gorillae</a></i>, infects <a href="/wiki/Gorilla" title="Gorilla">gorilla</a> populations. The human parasite diverged from <i>Pthirus gorillae</i> approximately 3.3 million years ago,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and is more distantly related to the genus <i>Pediculus</i>, which contains the human <a href="/wiki/Head_louse" title="Head louse">head</a> and <a href="/wiki/Body_louse" title="Body louse">body lice</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crab_louse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An adult crab louse is about 1.3–2 mm long (slightly smaller than the <a href="/wiki/Body_louse" title="Body louse">body louse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Head_louse" title="Head louse">head louse</a>), and can be distinguished from those other species by its almost round body. Another distinguishing feature is that the rearmost two pairs of legs of a crab louse are much thicker than the front legs and have large claws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-service_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-service-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle">Life cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crab_louse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Life cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The eggs of the crab louse are laid usually on the coarse hairs of the genital and perianal regions of the human body. Crab lice may also be found on other areas of the body that have coarse and relatively sparse coverings of hair, such as the beard, moustache, eyelashes, underneath the arms. They do not generally occur on the finer hair of the scalp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-service_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-service-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1a_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-1a-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The female lays about three eggs a day. The eggs take 6–8 days to hatch, and there are three <a href="/wiki/Nymph_(biology)" title="Nymph (biology)">nymphal</a> stages which together take 10–17 days before the adult develops, making a total life cycle from egg to adult of 16–25 days. Adults live for up to 30 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-service_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-service-3">[3]</a></sup> Crab lice feed exclusively on blood, and take a blood meal 4–5 times daily.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Infestation_of_humans">Infestation of humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crab_louse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Infestation of humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pediculosis_pubis" title="Pediculosis pubis">Pediculosis pubis</a></div>

<p>Infestations of crab lice are known as <a href="/wiki/Pediculosis_pubis" title="Pediculosis pubis">pediculosis pubis</a> or phthiriasis pubis (which, unlike the generic name of the louse, is spelled with a <i>phth</i>). Infestation of the eyelashes is referred to as pediculosis ciliaris or phthiriasis palpebrarum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The main symptom of infestation with crab lice is <a href="/wiki/Itch" title="Itch">itching</a>, usually in the pubic-hair area, resulting from <a href="/wiki/Hypersensitivity" title="Hypersensitivity">hypersensitivity</a> to louse saliva, which can become stronger over two or more weeks following initial infestation. In some infestations, a characteristic grey-blue or slate coloration appears (<i>maculae caeruleae</i>) at the feeding site, which may last for several days.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crab_egg.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="476" data-file-width="636" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Crab_egg.jpg/175px-Crab_egg.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Crab_egg.jpg/263px-Crab_egg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Crab_egg.jpg/350px-Crab_egg.jpg 2x" width="175"/></a>

Crab louse egg on human body hair</div>

<p>Current worldwide prevalence has been estimated at 2% of the human population, but accurate numbers are difficult to gauge because crab lice infestations are not considered a reportable condition by many governments, and many cases are self-treated or treated discreetly by primary physicians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Crab lice usually infect a new host only by close contact between individuals, usually through <a href="/wiki/Sexual_intercourse" title="Sexual intercourse">sexual intercourse</a>. Adults are more frequently infested than children. Non-sexual transmissions may occur among family and roommates through the use of shared towels, clothing, beds or closets. They can only survive a short time away from the warmth and humidity of the human body.</p>
<p>It has been suggested that an increasing percentage of humans <a href="/wiki/Body_grooming" title="Body grooming">removing</a> their <a href="/wiki/Pubic_hair" title="Pubic hair">pubic hair</a> has led to reduced crab louse populations in some parts of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Because the World Health Organization and other authorities do not record statistics for pubic louse infestation, there is virtually no hard data to support this supposition.</p>
