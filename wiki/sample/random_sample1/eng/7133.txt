<p>The <b>serpentine subgroup</b> (part of the kaolinite-serpentine group) are greenish, brownish, or spotted minerals commonly found in <a href="/wiki/Serpentinite" title="Serpentinite">serpentinite</a> rocks. They are used as a source of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asbestos" title="Asbestos">asbestos</a>, and as a decorative stone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The name is thought to come from the greenish color being that of a serpent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The serpentine group describes a group of common <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a>-forming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">hydrous</a> <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> <a href="/wiki/Silicate_minerals#Phyllosilicates" title="Silicate minerals">phyllosilicate</a> ((<a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">Mg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">Fe</a>)<sub>3</sub><a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">Si</a><sub>2</sub><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sub>5</sub>(<a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">OH</a>)<sub>4</sub>) <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>; they may contain minor amounts of other elements including <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Mineralogy" title="Mineralogy">mineralogy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gemology" title="Gemology">gemology</a>, serpentine may refer to any of 20 varieties belonging to the serpentine group. Owing to admixture, these varieties are not always easy to individualize, and distinctions are not usually made. There are three important mineral <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphs</a> of serpentine: antigorite, <a href="/wiki/Chrysotile" title="Chrysotile">chrysotile</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lizardite" title="Lizardite">lizardite</a>.</p>
<p>The serpentine group of minerals are <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphous</a>, meaning that they have the same <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_formulae" title="Chemical formulae">chemical formulae</a>, but the atoms are arranged into different structures, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystal_lattices" title="Crystal lattices">crystal lattices</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MN_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-MN-3">[3]</a></sup> Chrysotile, which has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiberous_habit" title="Fiberous habit">fiberous habit</a>, is one polymorph of serpentine and is an important component of <a href="/wiki/Asbestos" title="Asbestos">asbestos</a>. Other polymorphs in the serpentine group may have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platy_habit" title="Platy habit">platy habit</a>. Antigorite and lizardite are the polymorphs with platy habit.</p>
<p>Many types of serpentine have been used for <a href="/wiki/Jewellery" title="Jewellery">jewellery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hardstone_carving" title="Hardstone carving">hardstone carving</a>, sometimes under the name <i>false jade</i> or <i>Teton jade</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HUN_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-HUN-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serpentine_subgroup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Their olive green colour and smooth or scaly appearance is the basis of the name from the Latin <i>serpentinus</i>, meaning "serpent rock," according to Best (2003). They have their origins in <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic</a> alterations of <a href="/wiki/Peridotite" title="Peridotite">peridotite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pyroxene" title="Pyroxene">pyroxene</a>. Serpentines may also <a href="/wiki/Pseudomorph" title="Pseudomorph">pseudomorphously</a> replace other magnesium silicates. Alterations may be incomplete, causing physical properties of serpentines to vary widely. Where they form a significant part of the land surface, the <a href="/wiki/Serpentine_soil" title="Serpentine soil">soil</a> is unusually high in <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a>.</p>
<p>Antigorite is the <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorph</a> of serpentine that most commonly forms during metamorphism of wet ultramafic rocks and is stable at the highest temperatures—to over 600 °C (1,112 °F) at depths of 60 km (37 mi) or so. In contrast, lizardite and chrysotile typically form near the Earth's surface and break down at relatively low temperatures, probably well below 400 °C (752 °F). It has been suggested that chrysotile is never stable relative to either of the other two serpentine polymorphs.</p>
<p>Samples of the oceanic crust and uppermost mantle from ocean basins document that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultramafic" title="Ultramafic">ultramafic</a> rocks there commonly contain abundant serpentine. Antigorite contains water in its structure, about 13 percent by weight. Hence, antigorite may play an important role in the transport of water into the earth in <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction</a> zones and in the subsequent release of water to create magmas in <a href="/wiki/Island_arc" title="Island arc">island arcs</a>, and some of the water may be carried to yet greater depths.</p>
<p>Soils derived from serpentine are toxic to many <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, because of high levels of <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a>; growth of many plants is also inhibited by low levels of <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a> and a low ratio of <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>/<a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flora_(plants)" title="Flora (plants)">flora</a> is generally very distinctive, with specialised, slow-growing species. Areas of <a href="/wiki/Serpentine_soil" title="Serpentine soil">serpentine-derived soil</a> will show as strips of <a href="/wiki/Shrubland" title="Shrubland">shrubland</a> and open, scattered small <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">trees</a> (often <a href="/wiki/Pinophyta" title="Pinophyta">conifers</a>) within otherwise <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forested</a> areas; these areas are called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serpentine_barrens" title="Serpentine barrens">serpentine barrens</a>.</p>
<p>Most serpentines are opaque to translucent, light (<a href="/wiki/Specific_gravity" title="Specific gravity">specific gravity</a> between 2.2–2.9), soft (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohs_hardness_scale" title="Mohs hardness scale">hardness</a> 2.5–4), infusible and susceptible to <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a>. All are <a href="/wiki/Microcrystalline" title="Microcrystalline">microcrystalline</a> and massive in <a href="/wiki/Crystal_habit" title="Crystal habit">habit</a>, never being found as single <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystals</a>. <a href="/wiki/Lustre_(mineralogy)" title="Lustre (mineralogy)">Lustre</a> may be vitreous, greasy or silky. Colours range from white to grey, yellow to green, and brown to black, and are often splotchy or veined. Many are intergrown with other minerals, such as <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a>. Occurrence is worldwide; <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> (<a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US" title="US">US</a> (northern <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>), <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> (<a href="/wiki/Thessaly" title="Thessaly">Thessaly</a>), <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Ural Mountains</a> (<a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>), <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> (<a href="/wiki/Styria" title="Styria">Styria</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carinthia_(state)" title="Carinthia (state)">Carinthia</a>), <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> (<a href="/wiki/Assam" title="Assam">Assam</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Manipur" title="Manipur">Manipur</a>), <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">Burma</a>), <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> are notable localities.</p>
<p>Serpentines find use in industry for a number of purposes, such as railway ballasts, building materials, and the asbestiform types find use as thermal and electrical insulation (chrysotile <a href="/wiki/Asbestos" title="Asbestos">asbestos</a>). The asbestos content can be released to the air when serpentine is excavated and if it is used as a road surface, forming a long term health hazard by breathing. Asbestos from serpentine can also appear at low levels in water supplies through normal weathering processes, but there is as yet no identified health hazard associated with use or ingestion. In its natural state, some forms of serpentine react with carbon dioxide and re-release oxygen into the atmosphere.</p>
<p>The more attractive and durable varieties (all of antigorite) are termed "noble" or "precious" serpentine and are used extensively as <a href="/wiki/Gemstone" title="Gemstone">gems</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Hardstone_carving" title="Hardstone carving">ornamental carvings</a>. The town of <a href="/wiki/Bhera" title="Bhera">Bhera</a> in the historic <a href="/wiki/Punjab,_India" title="Punjab, India">Punjab province</a> of the <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian subcontinent</a> was known for centuries for finishing a relatively pure form of green serpentine obtained from quarries in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> into <a href="/wiki/Lapidary" title="Lapidary">lapidary</a> work, cups, ornamental sword hilts, and dagger handles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HUN_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-HUN-5">[5]</a></sup> This high-grade serpentine ore was known as <i>sang-i-yashm</i> or to the English, <i>false jade</i>, and was used for generations by Indian craftsmen for lapidary work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HUN_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-HUN-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAT_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-WAT-7">[7]</a></sup> It is easily carved, taking a good polish, and is said to have a pleasingly greasy feel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STO_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-STO-8">[8]</a></sup> Less valuable serpentine ores of varying hardness and clarity are also sometimes dyed to imitate <a href="/wiki/Jade" title="Jade">jade</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STO_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-STO-8">[8]</a></sup> Misleading synonyms for this material include "Suzhou jade", "Styrian jade", and "New jade".</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Serpentine-bowenite.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="212" data-file-width="300" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c2/Serpentine-bowenite.jpg" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">Polished slab of bowenite serpentine, a variety of antigorite. Typical cloudy patches and veining are apparent.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>New Caledonian serpentine is particularly rich in <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>. The <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Māori</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> once carved beautiful objects from local serpentine, which they called <i>tangiwai</i>, meaning "tears".</p>
<p>The <i>lapis atracius</i> of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a>, now known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verde_antique" title="Verde antique">verde antique</a>, or verde antico, is a serpentinite <a href="/wiki/Breccia" title="Breccia">breccia</a> popular as a decorative facing stone. In classical times it was mined at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Casambala&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Casambala (page does not exist)">Casambala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thessaly" title="Thessaly">Thessaly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>. Serpentinite <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marbles</a> are also widely used: Green <i>Connemara marble</i> (or <i>Irish green marble</i>) from <a href="/wiki/Connemara" title="Connemara">Connemara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> (and many other sources), and red <i>Rosso di Levanto marble</i> from Italy. Use is limited to indoor settings as serpentinites do not <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weather</a> well.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Antigorite">Antigorite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serpentine_subgroup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Antigorite">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lamellated antigorite occurs in tough, pleated masses. It is usually dark green in colour, but may also be yellowish, gray, brown or black. It has a hardness of 3.5–4 and its lustre is greasy. The monoclinic crystals show micaceous <a href="/wiki/Cleavage_(crystal)" title="Cleavage (crystal)">cleavage</a> and fuse with difficulty. Antigorite is named after its type locality, the Geisspfad serpentinite, <a href="/wiki/Domodossola" title="Domodossola">Valle Antigorio</a> in border region <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>/ <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bowenite" title="Bowenite">Bowenite</a> is an especially hard serpentine (5.5) of a light to dark apple green colour, often mottled with cloudy white patches and darker veining. It is the serpentine most frequently encountered in carving and jewellery. The name <i>retinalite</i> is sometimes applied to yellow bowenite. The New Zealand material is called <i>tangiwai</i>.</p>
<p>Although not an official species, bowenite is the state mineral of <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>: this is also the variety's type locality. A bowenite <a href="/wiki/Cabochon" title="Cabochon">cabochon</a> featured as part of the "Our Mineral Heritage Brooch", was presented to First Lady Mrs. <a href="/wiki/Lady_Bird_Johnson" title="Lady Bird Johnson">Lady Bird Johnson</a> in 1967.</p>
<p>Williamsite is a local varietal name for antigorite that is oil-green with black crystals of <a href="/wiki/Chromite" title="Chromite">chromite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a> often included. Somewhat resembling fine jade, williamsite is cut into cabochons and beads. It is found mainly in <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US" title="US">US</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gymnite is an amorphous form of antigorite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It was originally found in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bare_Hills,_Maryland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bare Hills, Maryland (page does not exist)">Bare Hills, Maryland</a>, and is named from the Greek, gymnos meaning bare or naked.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="State_emblem">State emblem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serpentine_subgroup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: State emblem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
