<p>The <b>ground pangolin</b> (<i>Smutsia temminckii</i>), also known as <b>Temminck's pangolin</b> or the <b>Cape pangolin</b>, is one of four species of <a href="/wiki/Pangolin" title="Pangolin">pangolins</a> which can be found in Africa, and the only one in southern and eastern Africa. The animal was named for the Dutch zoologist <a href="/wiki/Coenraad_Jacob_Temminck" title="Coenraad Jacob Temminck">Coenraad Jacob Temminck</a>. As a group, pangolins are among the most critically endangered animals in the world.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_description">Physical description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ground_pangolin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physical description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although their outward appearance is <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptilian</a>, pangolins are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammals" title="Mammals">mammals</a>. Pangolins are almost completely covered in overlapping, protective scales,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIAP_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-WIAP-4">[4]</a></sup> which makes up about 20% of their body weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-awf_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-awf-5">[5]</a></sup> The scales are composed of <a href="/wiki/Keratin" title="Keratin">keratin</a>, the same material that forms human hair and fingernails,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-awf_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-awf-5">[5]</a></sup> and give pangolins an appearance similar to a pinecone or artichoke.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnn-6">[6]</a></sup> The underside of a pangolin is not covered with scales, but sparse fur, instead. When threatened, it usually rolls up into a ball, thus protecting its vulnerable belly. Depending on the species, pangolin weight varies from 3.5 to 7.3 lb (1.6 to 3.3 kg) and their length ranges from 12 to 39 in. Mature adults are light brown, olive, and dark brown in color, while young are pale brown or pink in color.</p>
<p>Pangolins walk on all four of their stout limbs, which are adapted for digging. Their limbs have five toes each with the fore feet having three long, curved claws, which are designed to demolish termite nests and to dig <a href="/wiki/Burrow" title="Burrow">burrows</a>. Because of these claws, pangolins must balance on the outer edges of their fore feet and tuck in the claws to prevent damage. Pangolins have long, broad tails and small, conical heads with jaws that lack teeth. To replace the act of chewing, the pangolin stomach is muscular, with keratinous spines that project into the interior and contains small stones to mash and grind prey, similarly to a bird’s <a href="/wiki/Gizzard" title="Gizzard">gizzard</a>. Pangolins also have long, muscular tongues to reach and lap up ants and termites in cavities. Their tongues stretch so far, they are actually longer than their bodies. The tongue is attached in the lower cavity, near the pelvis and the last pair of ribs, and is able to retract and rest in the chest cavity. Pangolins have no external ears, so have poor hearing, as well as poor vision, although they do have a strong sense of smell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIAP_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-WIAP-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Range_and_distribution">Range and distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ground_pangolin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Range and distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The African pangolin species are native to 15 African countries dispersed throughout southern, central, and east Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-awf_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-awf-5">[5]</a></sup> <i>M. temminckii</i> is the only species found in southern and eastern Africa. It prefers savannah woodland with moderate amounts of scrub at low elevations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior_and_social_organization">Behavior and social organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ground_pangolin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behavior and social organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Little is known about the pangolin, as it is difficult to study in the wild. Pangolins are solitary animals and only interact for mating. They dig and live in deep burrows made of semispherical chambers. These burrows are large enough for humans to crawl into and stand up. Although it is capable of digging its own burrow, the ground pangolin prefers to occupy those abandoned by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warthogs" title="Warthogs">warthogs</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aardvarks" title="Aardvarks">aardvarks</a> or to lie in dense vegetation, making it even more difficult to observe. African pangolins such as the ground pangolin prefer burrows, while Asian pangolins sleep in hollows and forks of trees and logs. They are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a> animals. They mark their territory with urine, secretions, and by scattering their feces. When threatened, their defense mechanism is to curl into a ball with their scales outward, hiss and puff, and lash out with their sharp-edged tails.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIAP_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-WIAP-4">[4]</a></sup> The scales on the tails are capable of a cutting action to inflict serious wounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-awf_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-awf-5">[5]</a></sup> Pangolins are also capable of emitting noxious acid from glands near the anus, similar to a skunk, to ward off predators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guy_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-guy-7">[7]</a></sup> The ground pangolin's main predators are leopards, hyenas, and humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-awf_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-awf-5">[5]</a></sup> Young pangolins ride on the base of their mothers' tails and slip under the mother when she curls up for protection.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ground_pangolin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ground pangolin is wholly <a href="/wiki/Myrmecophagy" title="Myrmecophagy">myrmecophagous</a>, meaning that they only feed on <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termites</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup> In fact, they demonstrate prey selectivity, only eating specific <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termite</a> species rather than foraging on the most abundant species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup> They have been observed exposing entire <a href="/wiki/Subterranean_fauna" title="Subterranean fauna">subterranean</a> nests of a certain species of <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termites</a> without eating any, preferring to find their species of choice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Their determination of suitable prey does not seem to be based on the size of the species alone, but likely also depends on the chemical and mechanical defenses of each species. Even in <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> environments, ground pangolins remain selective in their dietary habits with regard to prey species and only prey on a small subset of available <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termite</a> species.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction_and_lifecycle">Reproduction and lifecycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ground_pangolin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reproduction and lifecycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lifespan of the pangolin is unknown, but the observed lifespan in captivity is 20 years. They are <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexually dimorphic</a>, with the males being 10-50% heavier than females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIAP_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-WIAP-4">[4]</a></sup> No defined mating season is known, but pangolins tend to mate during the summer and autumn. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gestation_period" title="Gestation period">gestation period</a> ranges up to 139 days for ground pangolins and other African species. African species females usually birth only one offspring, but litters of three have been observed in Asian species. When born, a pangolin has soft, pale scales, which begin to harden by the second day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIAP_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-WIAP-4">[4]</a></sup> The young are usually about 6 in (15 cm) long and about 12 oz (340.19 g) at birth. They are nursed by their mothers for 3 to 4 months, but begin eating termites after only one month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-awf_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-awf-5">[5]</a></sup> Pangolins reach sexual maturity at 2 years old, when they leave their mothers and begin living alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIAP_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-WIAP-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status_and_threats">Conservation status and threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ground_pangolin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Conservation status and threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ground pangolin is listed as <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">vulnerable</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a>. The assessors state, “there is an inferred past/ongoing and projected future population reduction of 30-40% over a 27-year period (nine years past, 18 years future; generation length estimated at nine years) based primarily on ongoing exploitation for traditional medicine and bushmeat throughout the species' range and evidence of increased intercontinental trade to Asia.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup> All eight extant pangolin species are now considered to be threatened with extinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>The two main threats encountered by ground pangolin populations are <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat loss</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_trade" title="Wildlife trade">illegal trafficking</a>. Due to human cultivation of land, the pangolin faces <a href="/wiki/Habitat_fragmentation" title="Habitat fragmentation">habitat fragmentation</a> and corresponding reduction in numbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIAP_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-WIAP-4">[4]</a></sup> Meanwhile, illegal trade has an even stronger impact, as pangolins are reported to be the most trafficked animal in the world (with elephants a close second). The scales alone account for 20% of the <a href="/wiki/Black_market" title="Black market">black market</a> in protected animal parts;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guy_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-guy-7">[7]</a></sup> they are boiled off the body and used for traditional medicines. Pangolin meat is sold as a high-end delicacy in China and Vietnam, the blood is believed to be a healing tonic, and pangolin fetuses have alleged health benefits and <a href="/wiki/Aphrodisiac" title="Aphrodisiac">aphrodisiac</a> qualities. A conservative estimate of pangolins trafficked illegally each year is 10,000, while actual numbers for a two-year period may be in excess of 200,000. How many are left in the wild is unknown. Pangolins are generally poorly known to the public and their endangered status has so far received much less publicity than in the case of more iconic species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-cnn-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
