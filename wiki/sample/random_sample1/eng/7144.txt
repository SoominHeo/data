<p>A <b>quarter note</b> (American) or <b>crotchet</b> (British, from the sense 'hook') is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Note_(music)" title="Note (music)">note</a> played for one quarter of the duration of a <a href="/wiki/Whole_note" title="Whole note">whole note</a> (or <i>semibreve</i>). Often, musicians will say that a crotchet is one <a href="/wiki/Beat_(music)" title="Beat (music)">beat</a>, but this is not always correct, as the beat is indicated by the <a href="/wiki/Time_signature" title="Time signature">time signature</a> of the music; a quarter note may or may not be the beat. Quarter notes are notated with a filled-in oval <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Note_head" title="Note head">note head</a> and a straight, flagless <a href="/wiki/Stem_(music)" title="Stem (music)">stem</a>. The stem usually points upwards if it is below the middle line of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musical_staff" title="Musical staff">stave</a> or downwards if it is on or above the middle line. However, the stem direction may differentiate more than one <a href="/wiki/Part_(music)" title="Part (music)">part</a>. The head of the note also reverses its orientation in relation to the stem. (See image.)</p>

<p>A related value is the <b>quarter <a href="/wiki/Rest_(music)" title="Rest (music)">rest</a></b> (or <b>crotchet rest</b>). It denotes a silence of the same duration as a quarter note. It typically appears as the symbol <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crotchet_rest_alt_plain-svg.svg"><img alt="Crotchet rest alt plain-svg.svg" data-file-height="75" data-file-width="26" height="20" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Crotchet_rest_alt_plain-svg.svg/7px-Crotchet_rest_alt_plain-svg.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Crotchet_rest_alt_plain-svg.svg/10px-Crotchet_rest_alt_plain-svg.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Crotchet_rest_alt_plain-svg.svg/14px-Crotchet_rest_alt_plain-svg.svg.png 2x" width="7"/></a>, plus, occasionally, as the older symbol <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crotchet_rest_plain-svg.svg"><img alt="Crotchet rest plain-svg.svg" data-file-height="46" data-file-width="27" height="14" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Crotchet_rest_plain-svg.svg/8px-Crotchet_rest_plain-svg.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Crotchet_rest_plain-svg.svg/12px-Crotchet_rest_plain-svg.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Crotchet_rest_plain-svg.svg/16px-Crotchet_rest_plain-svg.svg.png 2x" width="8"/></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The note derives from the <i>semiminima</i> of <a href="/wiki/Mensural_notation" title="Mensural notation">mensural notation</a>. The word <i>crotchet</i> comes from <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Crochet" title="Crochet">crochet</a></i>, meaning 'little hook', diminutive of <i>croc</i>, 'hook', because of the hook used on the note in black notation. However, because the hook appeared on the <a href="/wiki/Eighth_note" title="Eighth note">eighth note</a> (or <i>quaver</i>) in the later white notation, the modern French term <i>croche</i> refers to an eighth note.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The quarter note is played for half the length of a half note and twice that of an eighth note. It is one beat in a bar of <span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span style="font-family: Georgia, 'DejaVu Serif', serif;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:0.85em;font-size:80%;text-align:center"><b>4</b><br/>
<b>4</b></span></span></span>. The term <i>quarter note</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">calque</a> (loan translation) of the German term <i>Viertelnote</i>. The names of this note (and rest) in many other languages are <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">calqued</a> from the same source; <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance languages</a> usually use a term derived from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a> <i>negra</i> meaning ‘black’:</p>

<p>The Galician, Catalan, French, and Spanish names for the note (all of them meaning "black") derive from the fact that the <i>semiminima</i> was the longest note to be colored in mensural white notation, which is true as well of the modern form.</p>
<p>The Bulgarian, Chinese, Croatian, Czech, Japanese, Korean, Polish, Russian, Serbian and Slovak names mean "quarter" (for the note) and "quarter's pause" (for the rest).</p>
