<p><b>Sabratha</b>, <b>Sabratah</b> or <b>Siburata</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">صبراتة</span>‎‎), in the <a href="/wiki/Zawiya_District" title="Zawiya District">Zawiya District</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shabyat-2007_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-shabyat-2007-2">[2]</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>, was the westernmost of the ancient "three cities" of Roman <a href="/wiki/Tripolis_(region_of_Africa)" title="Tripolis (region of Africa)">Tripolis</a>. From 2001 to 2007 it was the capital of the former <a href="/wiki/Sabratha_wa_Sorman_District" title="Sabratha wa Sorman District">Sabratha wa Sorman District</a>. It lies on the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a> coast about 66 km (41 mi) west of modern <a href="/wiki/Tripoli" title="Tripoli">Tripoli</a>. The extant archaeological site was inscribed as a UNESCO <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> in 1982.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Sabratha">Ancient Sabratha</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabratha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ancient Sabratha">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sabratha's port was established, perhaps about 500 BC, as a <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenician</a> trading-post that served as a coastal outlet for the products of the African hinterland. The Phoenicians gave it the <a href="/wiki/Berber_languages" title="Berber languages">Lybico-Berber</a> name 'Sbrt'n',<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CB_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-CB-3">[3]</a></sup> which suggests that there may have been a native settlement built there prior to the Phoenicians' arrival. Sabratha became part of the short-lived <a href="/wiki/Numidia" title="Numidia">Numidian</a> Kingdom of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Massinissa" title="Massinissa">Massinissa</a> before being Romanized and rebuilt in the 2nd and 3rd centuries AD. The Emperor <a href="/wiki/Septimius_Severus" title="Septimius Severus">Septimius Severus</a> was born nearby in <a href="/wiki/Leptis_Magna" title="Leptis Magna">Leptis Magna</a>, and Sabratha reached its monumental peak during the rule of the Severans. The city was badly damaged by earthquakes during the 4th century, particularly <a href="/wiki/365_Crete_earthquake" title="365 Crete earthquake">the quake of AD 365</a>. It was rebuilt on a more modest scale by <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> governors. The town was site of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishopric" title="Bishopric">bishopric</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Within a hundred years of the Arab conquest of the <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">Maghreb</a>, trade had shifted to other ports and Sabratha dwindled to a village.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_ruins_in_danger_of_erosion_damage_.5B_April_2016_report_.5D">Ancient ruins in danger of erosion damage [ April 2016 report ]</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabratha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient ruins in danger of erosion damage [ April 2016 report ]">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to soft soil composition and the nature of the Coast of Sabratha being mostly made up of soft rock and sand, the Ruins of Sabratha are undergoing dangerous periods of coastal erosion. The public baths, Olive Press building and 'Harbor' can be observed as being most damaged as the buildings have crumbled due to storms and unsettled seas.</p>
<p>This Erosion of the coast of Ancient Sabratha can be seen yearly with significant differences in beach layout and recent crumbled buildings. Breakwaters set in the vicinity of the Harbor and Olive press are inadequate and too small to efficiently protect the Ancient City of Sabratha.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_archaeological_site">The archaeological site</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabratha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The archaeological site">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LY-Sabratha.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="700" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/LY-Sabratha.png/220px-LY-Sabratha.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/LY-Sabratha.png/330px-LY-Sabratha.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/LY-Sabratha.png/440px-LY-Sabratha.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LY-Sabratha.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of Sabratha</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Besides its <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Theatre_at_Sabratha&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Theatre at Sabratha (page does not exist)">Theatre at Sabratha</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Th%C3%A9%C3%A2tre_antique_de_Sabratha" title="fr:Théâtre antique de Sabratha">fr</a>)</span> that retains its three-storey architectural backdrop, Sabratha has temples dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Liber" title="Liber">Liber Pater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serapis" title="Serapis">Serapis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a>. There is a Christian <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a> of the time of <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Justinian</a> and also remnants of some of the <a href="/wiki/Mosaic" title="Mosaic">mosaic</a> floors that enriched elite dwellings of Roman North Africa (for example, at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Villa_Sileen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Villa Sileen (page does not exist)">Villa Sileen</a>, near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khoms,_Libya" title="Khoms, Libya">Khoms</a>). However, these are most clearly preserved in the coloured patterns of the seaward (or Forum) baths, directly overlooking the shore, and in the black and white floors of the Theatre baths.</p>
<p>There is an adjacent museum containing some treasures from Sabratha, but others can be seen in the national museum in <a href="/wiki/Tripoli" title="Tripoli">Tripoli</a>.</p>
<p>In 1943, during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, archaeologist <a href="/wiki/Max_Mallowan" title="Max Mallowan">Max Mallowan</a>, husband of novelist <a href="/wiki/Agatha_Christie" title="Agatha Christie">Agatha Christie</a>, was based at Sabratha as an assistant to the Senior Civil Affairs Officer of the Western Province of <a href="/wiki/Tripolitania" title="Tripolitania">Tripolitania</a>. His main task was to oversee the allocation of grain rations, but it was, in the words of Christie's biographer, a "glorious attachment", during which Mallowan lived in an Italian villa with a patio overlooking the sea and dined on fresh <a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">tunny</a> fish and olives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Sabratha">Modern Sabratha</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabratha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern Sabratha">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wefaq_Sabratha" title="Wefaq Sabratha">Wefaq Sabratha</a> is the football club, playing at <a href="/wiki/Sabratha_Stadium" title="Sabratha Stadium">Sabratha Stadium</a>.</p>
<p>In 2011, the town became involved in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Libyan_Civil_War" title="Libyan Civil War">Libyan Civil War</a>. At first seeming to have rebelled against the government, with sword-wielding townspeople fighting against soldiers with guns,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> as of 2 March it had been retaken by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pro-Gaddafi_forces" title="Pro-Gaddafi forces">pro-Gaddafi forces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> However, the town was recaptured by the rebels in August.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Report: In Jan 2014, The bodies of two foreign nationals were discovered on Tallil Seyahi Beach on the outskirts of the coastal city of Sabratha, according to the Sabratha Media Center. An initial police investigation shows the two were having a picnic in a somewhat remote area near a partially built resort. A photo posted on the Sabratha Media Center Facebook page purports to show the man and woman, who are lying face down in the sand. Near the bodies is a blanket that is spread out, with food and drinks strewn about. A backpack sat nearby. The identities and the nationalities of the two, described only as a man and a woman, have not been released.</p>
