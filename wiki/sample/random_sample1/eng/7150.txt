<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Tetraoxygen</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>The <b>tetraoxygen</b> molecule (<b>O<sub>4</sub></b>), also called <b>oxozone</b>, was first predicted in 1924 by <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_N._Lewis" title="Gilbert N. Lewis">Gilbert N. Lewis</a>, who proposed it as an explanation for the failure of <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> to obey <a href="/wiki/Curie%27s_law" title="Curie's law">Curie's law</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Today it seems Lewis was off, but not by much: computer simulations indicate that although there are no stable O<sub>4</sub> molecules in liquid oxygen, O<sub>2</sub> molecules do tend to associate in pairs with antiparallel <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spins</a>, forming transient O<sub>4</sub> units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1999, researchers thought that <a href="/wiki/Solid_oxygen" title="Solid oxygen">solid oxygen</a> existed in its ε-phase (at pressures above 10 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigapascal" title="Gigapascal">GPa</a>) as O<sub>4</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gorelli_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gorelli-3">[3]</a></sup> However, in 2006, it was shown by <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">X-ray crystallography</a> that this stable <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phase</a> known as <b>ε oxygen</b> or <b>red oxygen</b> is in fact <a href="/wiki/Solid_oxygen#Red_oxygen" title="Solid oxygen"><span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
8</span></span></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Nevertheless, tetraoxygen has been detected as a short-lived <a href="/wiki/Chemical_species" title="Chemical species">chemical species</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry" title="Mass spectrometry">mass spectrometry</a> experiments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cacace_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cacace-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Absorption bands of the O<sub>4</sub> molecule e.g. at 360, 477 and 577 nm are frequently used to do aerosol inversions in atmospheric optical <a href="/wiki/Absorption_spectroscopy" title="Absorption spectroscopy">absorption spectroscopy</a>. Due to the known distribution of O<sub>2</sub> and therefore also O<sub>4</sub>, O<sub>4</sub> slant column densities can be used to retrieve aerosol profiles which can then be used again in radiative transfer models to model light paths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frie.C3.9F_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frie.C3.9F-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Free_molecule">Free molecule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tetraoxygen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Free molecule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Theoretical calculations have predicted the existence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metastability_in_molecules" title="Metastability in molecules">metastable</a> O<sub>4</sub> molecules with two different shapes: a "puckered" square like <a href="/wiki/Cyclobutane" title="Cyclobutane">cyclobutane</a> or <a href="/wiki/Allotropes_of_sulfur#S4.2Ctetrasulfur" title="Allotropes of sulfur">S<sub>4</sub></a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and a "pinwheel" with three oxygen atoms surrounding a central one in a trigonal planar formation similar to <a href="/wiki/Boron_trifluoride" title="Boron trifluoride">boron trifluoride</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It was previously pointed out that the "pinwheel" O<sub>4</sub> molecule should be the natural continuation of the isoelectronic series <a href="/wiki/Borate" title="Borate"><span class="chemf nowrap">BO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">3−<br/>
3</span></span></a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate"><span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">2−<br/>
3</span></span></a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate"><span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/>
3</span></span></a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and analogous to <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_trioxide" title="Sulfur trioxide">SO<sub>3</sub></a>; that observation served as the basis for the mentioned theoretical calculations.</p>

<p>In 2001, a team at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Rome_La_Sapienza" title="University of Rome La Sapienza">University of Rome La Sapienza</a> conducted a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Neutralization-reionization_mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Neutralization-reionization mass spectrometry (page does not exist)">neutralization-reionization mass spectrometry</a> experiment to investigate the structure of free O<sub>4</sub> molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cacace_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cacace-5">[5]</a></sup> Their results did not agree with either of the two proposed molecular structures, but they did agree with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complex_(chemistry)" title="Complex (chemistry)">complex</a> between two O<sub>2</sub> molecules, one in the <a href="/wiki/Ground_state" title="Ground state">ground state</a> and the other in a specific <a href="/wiki/Excited_state" title="Excited state">excited state</a>.</p>
