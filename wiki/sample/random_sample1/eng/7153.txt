<p><b>Strabismus</b>, also called <b>crossed eyes</b>, is a condition in which the eyes do not properly align with each other when looking at an object.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Which eye is focused on the object in question can switch. It may also be present occasionally or constantly. If present during a large part of childhood <a href="/wiki/Amblyopia" title="Amblyopia">amblyopia</a> may result and <a href="/wiki/Depth_perception" title="Depth perception">depth perception</a> may be lost. Adults may have <a href="/wiki/Diplopia" title="Diplopia">double vision</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gun2015_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gun2015-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Strabismus can occur due to muscle dysfunction, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farsightedness" title="Farsightedness">farsightedness</a>, problems in the brain, trauma, or infections. Risk factors include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Premature_birth" title="Premature birth">premature birth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_palsy" title="Cerebral palsy">cerebral palsy</a>, and a family history of the condition. Types include <a href="/wiki/Esotropia" title="Esotropia">esotropia</a> where the eyes are crossed; <a href="/wiki/Exotropia" title="Exotropia">exotropia</a> where the eyes diverge; and <a href="/wiki/Hypertropia" title="Hypertropia">hypertropia</a> where they are vertically misaligned. They can also be classified by whether the problem is present in all directions a person looks (comitant) or varies by direction (incomitant). Diagnosis may be made by observing the light reflecting from the person's eyes and finding that it is not centered on the <a href="/wiki/Pupil" title="Pupil">pupil</a>. Another condition that produces similar symptoms is a <a href="/wiki/Cranial_nerve_disease" title="Cranial nerve disease">cranial nerve disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gun2015_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gun2015-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Treatment depends on the type of strabismus and the underlying cause. This may include the use of <a href="/wiki/Glasses" title="Glasses">glasses</a> and possibly surgery. Some types benefit from early surgery. Strabismus occurs in about 2% of children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gun2015_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gun2015-2">[2]</a></sup> The term is from the Greek <i>strabismós</i> meaning "to squint".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Other terms for the condition include "squint", "wall-eye", and "cast of the eye".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bro1993_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bro1993-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>When observing a person with strabismus, the misalignment of the eyes may be quite apparent. A patient with a constant eye turn of significant magnitude is very easy to notice. However, a small magnitude or intermittent strabismus can easily be missed upon casual observation. In any case, an <a href="/wiki/Eye_care_professional" title="Eye care professional">eye care professional</a> can conduct various tests, such as cover testing, to determine the full extent of the strabismus.</p>
<p>Symptoms of strabismus include <a href="/wiki/Diplopia" title="Diplopia">double vision</a> and/or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asthenopia" title="Asthenopia">eye strain</a>. To avoid double vision, the brain may adapt by <a href="/wiki/Suppression_(eye)" title="Suppression (eye)">ignoring one eye</a>. In this case, often no noticeable symptoms are seen other than a minor loss of depth perception. This deficit may not be noticeable in someone who has had strabismus since birth or early childhood, as they have likely learned to judge depth and distances using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monocular_depth_cues" title="Monocular depth cues">monocular cues</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. However, a constant unilateral strabismus causing constant suppression is a risk for <a href="/wiki/Amblyopia" title="Amblyopia">amblyopia</a> in children. Small-angle and intermittent strabismus are more likely to cause disruptive visual symptoms. In addition to headaches and eye strain, symptoms may include an inability to read comfortably, fatigue when reading, and unstable or "jittery" vision.</p>

<p>People of all ages who have noticeable strabismus may experience psychosocial difficulties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-satterfield-et-al-1993_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-satterfield-et-al-1993-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olitsky-et-al-1999_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-olitsky-et-al-1999-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uretmen-et-al-2003_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-uretmen-et-al-2003-8">[8]</a></sup> Attention has also been drawn to potential socioeconomic impact resulting from cases of detectable strabismus. A socioeconomic consideration exists as well in the context of decisions regarding strabismus treatment,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-satterfield-et-al-1993_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-satterfield-et-al-1993-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olitsky-et-al-1999_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-olitsky-et-al-1999-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uretmen-et-al-2003_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-uretmen-et-al-2003-8">[8]</a></sup> including efforts to re-establish binocular vision and the possibility of <a href="/wiki/Stereopsis_recovery" title="Stereopsis recovery">stereopsis recovery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>One study has shown that strabismic children commonly exhibit behaviors marked by higher degrees of inhibition, anxiety, and emotional distress, often leading to outright emotional disorders. These disorders are often related to a negative perception of the child by peers. This is due not only to an altered aesthetic appearance, but also because of the inherent symbolic nature of the <a href="/wiki/Eye" title="Eye">eye</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gaze" title="Gaze">gaze</a>, and the vitally important role they play in an individual's life as social components. For some, these issues improved dramatically following <a href="/wiki/Strabismus_surgery" title="Strabismus surgery">strabismus surgery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Notably, strabismus interferes with normal <a href="/wiki/Eye_contact" title="Eye contact">eye contact</a>, often causing <a href="/wiki/Embarrassment" title="Embarrassment">embarrassment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anger" title="Anger">anger</a>, and feelings of awkwardness, thereby affecting social communication in a fundamental way, with a possible negative effect on <a href="/wiki/Self-esteem" title="Self-esteem">self-esteem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are indications that children with strabismus, particularly those with <a href="/wiki/Exotropia" title="Exotropia">exotropia</a> (an outward turn), are more likely to develop a mental health disorder than normal-sighted children. Researchers have theorized that esotropia (an inward turn) was not found to be linked to a higher propensity for mental illness due to the age range of the participants, as well as the shorter follow-up time period; esotropic children were monitored to a mean age of 15.8 years, compared with 20.3 years for the exotropic group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A subsequent study with participants from the same area monitored <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congenital_esotropia" title="Congenital esotropia">congenital esotropia</a> patients for a longer time period; results indicated that esotropic patients <i>were</i> also more likely to develop mental illness of some sort upon reaching early adulthood, similar to those with constant exotropia, intermittent exotropia, or <a href="/wiki/Convergence_insufficiency" title="Convergence insufficiency">convergence insufficiency</a>. The likelihood was 2.6 times that of controls. No apparent association with premature birth was observed, and no evidence was found linking later onset of mental illness to psychosocial stressors frequently encountered by those with strabismus.</p>
<p>Investigations have highlighted the impact that strabismus may typically have on quality of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Studies centered around images of strabismic and non-strabismic persons showed a strong negative bias towards those visibly displaying the condition, clearly demonstrating the potential for future <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socioeconomic" title="Socioeconomic">socioeconomic</a> implications with regard to <a href="/wiki/Employability" title="Employability">employability</a>, as well as other <a href="/wiki/Strabismus#Psychosocial_effects" title="Strabismus">psychosocial effects</a> related to an individual's overall happiness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Adult and child observers perceived a right heterotropia as more disturbing than a left heterotropia, and child observers perceived an esotropia as "worse" than an exotropia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Successful surgical correction of strabismus—for adult patients as well as children—has been shown to have a significantly positive effect on psychological well-being.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Very little research exists regarding <a href="/wiki/Coping_(psychology)" title="Coping (psychology)">coping</a> strategies employed by adult strabismics. One study categorized coping methods into three sub-categories: avoidance (refraining from participation an activity), distraction (deflecting attention from the condition), and adjustment (approaching an activity differently). The authors of the study suggested that individuals with strabismus may benefit from psychosocial support such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interpersonal_skills" title="Interpersonal skills">interpersonal skills</a> training.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>No studies have evaluated whether psychosocial interventions have had any benefits on individuals undergoing strabismus surgery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacKenzie_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-MacKenzie-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cause">Cause</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strabismus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cause">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Strabismus can be seen in <a href="/wiki/Down_syndrome" title="Down syndrome">Down syndrome</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loeys-Dietz_syndrome" title="Loeys-Dietz syndrome">Loeys-Dietz syndrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_palsy" title="Cerebral palsy">cerebral palsy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Edwards_syndrome" title="Edwards syndrome">Edwards syndrome</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pathophysiology">Pathophysiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strabismus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pathophysiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Extraocular_muscles" title="Extraocular muscles">extraocular muscles</a> control the position of the eyes. Thus, a problem with the muscles or the nerves controlling them can cause paralytic strabismus. The extraocular muscles are controlled by cranial nerves <a href="/wiki/Oculomotor_nerve" title="Oculomotor nerve">III</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trochlear_nerve" title="Trochlear nerve">IV</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Abducens_nerve" title="Abducens nerve">VI</a>. An <a href="/wiki/Oculomotor_nerve_palsy" title="Oculomotor nerve palsy">impairment of cranial nerve III</a> causes the associated eye to deviate down and out and may or may not affect the size of the pupil. <a href="/wiki/Fourth_nerve_palsy" title="Fourth nerve palsy">Impairment of cranial nerve IV</a>, which can be <a href="/wiki/Congenital_fourth_nerve_palsy" title="Congenital fourth nerve palsy">congenital</a>, causes the eye to drift up and perhaps slightly inward. <a href="/wiki/Sixth_nerve_palsy" title="Sixth nerve palsy">Sixth nerve palsy</a> causes the eyes to deviate inward and has many causes due to the relatively long path of the nerve. <a href="/wiki/Intracranial_pressure" title="Intracranial pressure">Increased cranial pressure</a> can compress the nerve as it runs between the <a href="/wiki/Clivus_(anatomy)" title="Clivus (anatomy)">clivus</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brain_stem" title="Brain stem">brain stem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vaughan_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-vaughan-23">[23]</a></sup> Also, if the <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">doctor</a> is not careful, twisting of the baby's neck during <a href="/wiki/Forceps_in_childbirth" title="Forceps in childbirth">forceps delivery</a> can damage cranial nerve VI.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Evidence indicates a cause for strabismus may lie with the input provided to the <a href="/wiki/Visual_cortex" title="Visual cortex">visual cortex</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> This allows for strabismus to occur without the direct impairment of any cranial nerves or extraocular muscles.</p>
<p>Strabismus may cause <a href="/wiki/Amblyopia" title="Amblyopia">amblyopia</a> due to the brain ignoring one eye. Amblyopia is the failure of one or both eyes to achieve normal visual acuity despite normal structural health. During the first seven to eight years of life, the brain learns how to interpret the signals that come from an eye through a process called visual development. Development may be interrupted by strabismus if the child always fixates with one eye and rarely or never fixates with the other. To avoid double vision, the signal from the deviated eye is <a href="/wiki/Suppression_(eye)" title="Suppression (eye)">suppressed</a>, and the constant suppression of one eye causes a failure of the visual development in that eye.</p>
<p>Also, amblyopia may cause strabismus. If a great difference in clarity occurs between the images from the right and left eyes, input may be insufficient to correctly reposition the eyes. Other causes of a visual difference between right and left eyes, such as asymmetrical cataracts, refractive error, or other eye disease, can also cause or worsen strabismus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vaughan_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-vaughan-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accommodative_esotropia" title="Accommodative esotropia">Accommodative esotropia</a> is a form of strabismus caused by <a href="/wiki/Refractive_error" title="Refractive error">refractive error</a> in one or both eyes. Due to the <a href="/wiki/Accommodation_reflex" title="Accommodation reflex">near triad</a>, when a patient engages <a href="/wiki/Accommodation_(eye)" title="Accommodation (eye)">accommodation</a> to focus on a near object, an increase in the signal sent by cranial nerve III to the medial rectus muscles results, drawing the eyes inward; this is called the <a href="/wiki/Accommodation_reflex" title="Accommodation reflex">accommodation reflex</a>. If the accommodation needed is more than the usual amount, such as with people with significant hyperopia, the extra convergence can cause the eyes to cross.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">Diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strabismus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During an <a href="/wiki/Eye_examination" title="Eye examination">eye examination</a>, a test such as <a href="/wiki/Cover_test" title="Cover test">cover testing</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Hirschberg_test" title="Hirschberg test">Hirschberg test</a> is used in the diagnosis and measurement of strabismus and its impact on vision. <a href="/wiki/Retinal_birefringence_scanning" title="Retinal birefringence scanning">Retinal birefringence scanning</a> can be used for <a href="/wiki/Screening_(medicine)" title="Screening (medicine)">screening</a> of young children for eye misaligments.</p>
<p>Several classifications are made when diagnosing strabismus.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latency">Latency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strabismus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Latency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Strabismus can be manifest (<i>-tropia</i>) or latent (<i>-phoria</i>). A manifest deviation, or heterotropia (which may be <i>eso-</i>, <i>exo-</i>, <i>hyper-</i>, <i>hypo-</i>, <i>cyclotropia</i> or a combination of these), is present while the patient views a target binocularly, with no occlusion of either eye. The patient is unable to align the gaze of each eye to achieve fusion. A latent deviation, or <a href="/wiki/Heterophoria" title="Heterophoria">heterophoria</a> (<i>eso-</i>, <i>exo-</i>, <i>hyper-</i>, <i>hypo-</i>, <i>cyclophoria</i> or a combination of these), is only present after binocular vision has been interrupted, typically by covering one eye. This type of patient can typically maintain fusion despite the misalignment that occurs when the positioning system is relaxed. Intermittent strabismus is a combination of both of these types, where the patient can achieve fusion, but occasionally or frequently falters to the point of a manifest deviation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Onset">Onset</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strabismus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Onset">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Strabismus may also be classified based on time of onset, either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congenital" title="Congenital">congenital</a>, acquired, or secondary to another pathological process. Many infants are born with their eyes slightly misaligned, and this is typically outgrown by six to 12 months of age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nield_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-nield-25">[25]</a></sup> Acquired and secondary strabismus develop later. The onset of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accommodative_esotropia" title="Accommodative esotropia">accommodative esotropia</a>, an overconvergence of the eyes due to the effort of <a href="/wiki/Accommodation_(eye)" title="Accommodation (eye)">accommodation</a>, is mostly in early childhood. Acquired non-accommodative strabismus and secondary strabismus are developed after normal binocular vision has developed. In adults with previously normal alignment, the onset of strabismus usually results in <a href="/wiki/Diplopia" title="Diplopia">double vision</a>.</p>
<p>Any disease that causes vision loss may also cause strabismus,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> but it can also result from any severe and/or traumatic injury to the affected eye. Sensory strabismus is strabismus due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vision_loss" title="Vision loss">vision loss</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vision_impairment" title="Vision impairment">impairment</a>, leading to horizontal, vertical or torsional misalignment or to a combination thereof, with the eye with poorer vision drifting slightly over time. Most often, the outcome is horizontal misalignment. Its direction depends on the patient age at which the damage occurs: patients whose vision is lost or impaired at birth are more likely to develop esotropia, whereas patients with acquired vision loss or impairment mostly develop exotropia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In the extreme, complete <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blindness" title="Blindness">blindness</a> in one eye generally leads to the blind eye reverting to an anatomical position of rest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although many possible causes of strabismus are known, among them severe and/or traumatic injuries to the afflicted eye, in many cases no specific cause can be identified. This last is typically the case when strabismus is present since <a href="/wiki/Infantile_esotropia" title="Infantile esotropia">early childhood</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Results of a U.S. cohort study indicate that the incidence of adult-onset strabismus increases with age, especially after the sixth decade of life, and peaks in the eighth decade of life, and that the lifetime risk of being diagnosed with adult-onset strabismus is approximately 4%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laterality">Laterality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strabismus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Laterality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Strabismus may be classified as unilateral if the one eye consistently deviates, or alternating if either of the eyes can be seen to deviate. Alternation of the strabismus may occur spontaneously, with or without subjective awareness of the alternation. Alternation may also be triggered by various tests during an eye exam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meei_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-meei-33">[33]</a></sup> Unilateral strabismus has been observed to result from a severe and/or traumatic injury to the affected eye.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Direction">Direction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strabismus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Direction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Horizontal deviations are classified into two varieties. <i>Eso</i> describes inward or convergent deviations towards the midline. <i>Exo</i> describes outward or divergent misalignment. Vertical deviations are also classified into two varieties. <i>Hyper</i> is the term for an eye whose gaze is directed higher than the fellow eye while <i>hypo</i> refers to an eye whose gaze is directed lower. <i>Cyclo</i> refers to torsional strabismus, which occurs when the eyes rotate around the anterior-posterior axis to become misaligned and is quite rare.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">Naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strabismus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The directional prefixes are combined with -tropia and -phoria to describe various types of strabismus. For example, a constant left hypertropia exists when a patient's left eye is always aimed higher than the right. A patient with an intermittent right esotropia has a right eye that occasionally drifts toward the patient's nose, but at other times is able to align with the gaze of the left eye. A patient with a mild exophoria can maintain fusion during normal circumstances, but when the system is disrupted, the relaxed posture of the eyes is slightly divergent.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_considerations">Other considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strabismus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Strabismus can be further classified as follows</b>:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Paretic strabismus is due to paralysis of one or several <a href="/wiki/Extraocular_muscles" title="Extraocular muscles">extraocular muscles</a>.</li>
<li>Nonparetic strabismus is not due to paralysis of extraocular muscles.</li>
<li>Comitant (or <i>concomitant</i>) strabismus is a deviation that is the same magnitude regardless of gaze position.</li>
<li>Noncomitant (or <i>incomitant</i>) strabismus has a magnitude that varies as the patient shifts his or her gaze up, down, or to the sides.</li>
</ul>
<p>Nonparetic strabismus is generally concomitant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Most types of infant and childhood strabismus are comitant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wright-etal-2003-p155_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wright-etal-2003-p155-35">[35]</a></sup> Paretic strabismus can be either comitant or noncomitant. Incomitant strabismus is almost always caused by a limitation of ocular rotations that is due to a restriction of extraocular eye movement (ocular restriction) or due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extraocular_muscle_paresis" title="Extraocular muscle paresis">extraocular muscle paresis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wright-etal-2003-p155_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wright-etal-2003-p155-35">[35]</a></sup> Incomitant strabismus cannot be fully corrected by <a href="/wiki/Prism" title="Prism">prism</a> glasses, because the eyes would require different degrees of prismatic correction dependent on the direction of the gaze.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Incomitant strabismus of the eso- or exo-type are classified as "alphabet patterns": they are denoted as A- or V- or more rarely <a href="/wiki/Lambda" title="Lambda">λ</a>-, Y- or X-pattern depending on the extent of convergence or divergence when the gaze moves upward or downward. These letters of the alphabet denote ocular motility pattern that have a similarity to the respective letter: in the A-pattern there is (relatively speaking) more convergence when the gaze is directed upwards and more divergence when it is directed downwards, in the V-pattern it is the contrary, in the λ-, Y- and X-patterns there is little or no strabismus in the middle position but relatively more divergence in one or both of the upward and downward positions, depending on the "shape" of the letter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Types of incomitant strabismus include: <a href="/wiki/Duane_syndrome" title="Duane syndrome">Duane syndrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_gaze_palsy" title="Horizontal gaze palsy">horizontal gaze palsy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Congenital_fibrosis_of_the_extraocular_muscles" title="Congenital fibrosis of the extraocular muscles">congenital fibrosis of the extraocular muscles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>When the misalignment of the eyes is large and obvious, the strabismus is called large-angle, referring to the angle of deviation between the lines of sight of the eyes. Less severe eye turns are called small-angle strabismus. The degree of strabismus can vary based on whether the patient is viewing a distant or near target.</p>
<p>Strabismus that sets in after eye alignment had been surgically corrected is called <i>consecutive strabismus</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Differential_diagnosis">Differential diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strabismus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Differential diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pseudostrabismus" title="Pseudostrabismus">Pseudostrabismus</a> is the false appearance of strabismus. It generally occurs in infants and toddlers whose bridge of the nose is wide and flat, causing the appearance of esotropia due to less <a href="/wiki/Sclera" title="Sclera">sclera</a> being visible nasally. With age, the bridge of the child's nose narrows and the <a href="/wiki/Epicanthic_fold" title="Epicanthic fold">folds in the corner of the eyes</a> become less prominent.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Retinoblastoma" title="Retinoblastoma">Retinoblastoma</a> may also result in abnormal light reflection from the eye.</p>

</div>
<p>As with other binocular vision disorders, the primary goal is comfortable, single, clear, normal binocular vision at all distances and directions of gaze.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Strabismus is usually treated with a combination of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eyeglasses" title="Eyeglasses">eyeglasses</a>, vision therapy, and <a href="/wiki/Strabismus_surgery" title="Strabismus surgery">surgery</a>, depending on the underlying reason for the misalignment.</p>
<p>Whereas <a href="/wiki/Amblyopia" title="Amblyopia">amblyopia</a> (lazy eye), if minor and detected early, can often be corrected with use of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eye_patch" title="Eye patch">eye patch</a> on the dominant eye and/or <a href="/wiki/Vision_therapy" title="Vision therapy">vision therapy</a>, the use of eye patches is unlikely to change the angle of strabismus.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glasses">Glasses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strabismus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Glasses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In cases of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accommodative_esotropia" title="Accommodative esotropia">accommodative esotropia</a>, the eyes turn inward due to the effort of focusing far-sighted eyes, and the treatment of this type of strabismus necessarily involves refractive correction, which is usually done via corrective glasses or contact lenses, and in these cases surgical alignment is considered only if such correction does not resolve the eye turn.</p>
<p>In case of strong <a href="/wiki/Anisometropia" title="Anisometropia">anisometropia</a>, contact lenses may be preferable to spectacles because they avoid the problem of visual disparities due to size differences (<a href="/wiki/Aniseikonia" title="Aniseikonia">aniseikonia</a>) which is otherwise caused by spectacles in which the refractive power is very different for the two eyes. In a few cases of strabismic children with anisometropic amblyopia, a balancing of the refractive error eyes via <a href="/wiki/Refractive_surgery#Children" title="Refractive surgery">refractive surgery</a> has been performed before strabismus surgery was undertaken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early treatment of strabismus when the person is a baby may reduce the chance of developing amblyopia and depth perception problems. However, a review of randomized controlled trials concluded that the use of corrective glasses to prevent strabismus is not supported by existing research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-41">[41]</a></sup> Most children eventually recover from amblyopia if they have had the benefit of patches and corrective glasses.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Amblyopia has long been considered to remain permanent if not treated within a critical period, namely before the age of about seven years;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nield_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-nield-25">[25]</a></sup> however, recent discoveries give reason to challenge this view and to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Susan_Barry#Challenging_earlier_views" title="Susan Barry">adapt the earlier notion of a critical period</a> to account for <a href="/wiki/Stereopsis_recovery" title="Stereopsis recovery">stereopsis recovery</a> in adults.</p>
<p>Eyes that remain misaligned can still develop visual problems. Although not a cure for strabismus, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prism_(optics)" title="Prism (optics)">prism</a> lenses can also be used to provide some temporary comfort and to prevent double vision from occurring.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surgery">Surgery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strabismus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Surgery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Strabismus surgery does not remove the need for a child to wear glasses. Currently it is unknown whether there are any differences for completing strabismus surgery before or after amblyopia therapy in children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Korah_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Korah-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Strabismus surgery attempts to align the eyes by shortening, lengthening, or changing the position of one or more of the extraocular eye muscles. The procedure can typically be performed in about an hour, and requires about six to eight weeks for recovery. Adjustable sutures may be used to permit refinement of the eye alignment in the early postoperative period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leffler_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-leffler-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Double vision can rarely result, especially immediately after the surgery,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and vision loss is very rare. Glasses affect the position by changing the person's reaction to focusing. Prisms change the way light, and therefore images, strike the eye, simulating a change in the eye position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-medlineplus_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-medlineplus-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medication">Medication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strabismus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Medication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Medication used for strabismus in certain circumstances. In 1989, the US <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">FDA</a> approved <a href="/wiki/Botulinum_toxin_therapy_of_strabismus" title="Botulinum toxin therapy of strabismus">Botulinum toxin therapy</a> for strabismus in patients over 12 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Most commonly used in adults, the technique is also used for treating children, in particular children affected by infantile esotropia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The toxin is injected in the stronger muscle, causing temporary and partial paralysis. The treatment may need to be repeated three to four months later once the paralysis wears off. Common side effects are double vision, droopy eyelid, overcorrection, and no effect. The side effects typically resolve also within three to four months.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prognosis">Prognosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strabismus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Prognosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When strabismus is congenital or develops in infancy, it can cause amblyopia, in which the brain ignores input from the deviated eye. Even with therapy for amblyopia, <a href="/wiki/Stereoblindness" title="Stereoblindness">stereoblindness</a> may occur. The appearance of strabismus may also be a <a href="/wiki/Beauty" title="Beauty">cosmetic</a> problem. One study reported 85% of adult strabismus patients "reported that they had problems with work, school, and sports because of their strabismus." The same study also reported 70% said strabismus "had a negative effect on their <a href="/wiki/Self-image" title="Self-image">self-image</a>." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> A second operation is sometimes required to straighten the eyes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vaughan_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-vaughan-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
