<p>A <b>business model</b> is an "abstract representation of an organization, be it conceptual, textual, and/or graphical, of all core interrelated architectural, co-operational, and financial arrangements designed and developed by an organization presently and in the future, as well as all core products and/or services the organization offers, or will offer, based on these arrangements that are needed to achieve its strategic goals and objectives."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Al-Debei.2C_M._M._2008_pp._1-11_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Al-Debei.2C_M._M._2008_pp._1-11-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strategy_Markup_Language_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strategy_Markup_Language-2">[2]</a></sup> This definition by Al-Debei, El-Haddadeh and Avison (2008) indicates that <a href="/wiki/Value_proposition" title="Value proposition">value proposition</a>, value architecture (the organizational infrastructure and technological architecture that allows the movement of products, services, and information), value finance (modeling information related to total cost of ownership, pricing methods, and revenue structure), and <a href="/wiki/Value_network" title="Value network">value network</a> articulate the primary constructs or dimensions of business models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Al-Debei.2C_M._M._2010_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Al-Debei.2C_M._M._2010-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A business model describes the <a href="/wiki/Explanation" title="Explanation">rationale</a> of how an <a href="/wiki/Organization" title="Organization">organization</a> creates, delivers, and captures value,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osterwalder2010_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Osterwalder2010-4">[4]</a></sup> in economic, social, cultural or other contexts. The process of business model construction is part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Business_strategy" title="Business strategy">business strategy</a>.</p>
<p>In theory and practice, the term <i>business model</i> is used for a broad range of informal and formal descriptions to represent core aspects of a <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">business</a>, including purpose, <a href="/wiki/Business_process" title="Business process">business process</a>, target customers, offerings, strategies, infrastructure, organizational structures, sourcing, trading practices, and operational processes and policies including culture. The literature has provided very diverse interpretations and definitions of a business model. A systematic review and analysis of manager responses to a survey defines business models as the design of organizational structures to enact a commercial opportunity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-define_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-define-5">[5]</a></sup> Further extensions to this design logic emphasize the use of narrative or coherence in business model descriptions as mechanisms by which entrepreneurs create extraordinarily successful <a href="/wiki/Startup_company" title="Startup company">growth firms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-narrative_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-narrative-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Business models are used to describe and classify businesses, especially in an entrepreneurial setting, but they are also used by managers inside companies to explore possibilities for future development. Well-known business models can operate as "recipes" for creative managers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Business models are also referred to in some instances within the context of accounting for purposes of public reporting.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Over the years, business models have become much more sophisticated. The <i>bait and hook</i> business model (also referred to as the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Razor_and_blades_business_model" title="Razor and blades business model">razor and blades business model</a>" or the "tied products business model") was introduced in the early 20th century. This involves offering a basic product at a very low cost, often at a loss (the "bait"), then charging compensatory recurring amounts for refills or associated products or services (the "hook"). Examples include: razor (bait) and blades (hook); cell phones (bait) and air time (hook); computer printers (bait) and ink cartridge refills (hook); and cameras (bait) and prints (hook). A variant of this model is <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Systems" title="Adobe Systems">Adobe</a>, a software developer that gives away its document reader free of charge but charges several hundred dollars for its document writer.</p>
<p>In the 1950s, new business models came from <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald's">McDonald's</a> Restaurants and <a href="/wiki/Toyota" title="Toyota">Toyota</a>. In the 1960s, the innovators were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wal-Mart" title="Wal-Mart">Wal-Mart</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypermarkets" title="Hypermarkets">Hypermarkets</a>. The 1970s saw new business models from <a href="/wiki/FedEx" title="FedEx">FedEx</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toys_R_Us" title="Toys R Us">Toys R Us</a>; the 1980s from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blockbuster_(movie_rental_store)" title="Blockbuster (movie rental store)">Blockbuster</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Home_Depot" title="Home Depot">Home Depot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dell_Computer" title="Dell Computer">Dell Computer</a>; the 1990s from <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a>, <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Starbucks" title="Starbucks">Starbucks</a>.</p>
<p>Today, the type of business models might depend on how technology is used. For example, entrepreneurs on the internet have also created entirely new models that depend entirely on existing or emergent technology. Using technology, businesses can reach a large number of customers with minimal costs. In addition, the rise of outsourcing and globalization has meant that business models must also account for strategic sourcing, complex supply chains and moves to collaborative, relational contracting structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theoretical_and_empirical_insights">Theoretical and empirical insights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Theoretical and empirical insights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_logic_and_narrative_coherence">Design logic and narrative coherence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Design logic and narrative coherence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Design logic views the business model as an outcome of creating new organizational structures or changing existing structures to pursue a new opportunity. <a href="/wiki/Gerry_George" title="Gerry George">Gerry George</a> and Adam Bock (2011) conducted a comprehensive literature review and surveyed managers to understand how they perceived the components of a business model. In that analysis these authors show that there is a design logic behind how entrepreneurs and managers perceive and explain their business model. In further extensions to the design logic, George and Bock (2012) use case studies and the IBM survey data on business models in large companies, to describe how CEOs and entrepreneurs create narratives or stories in a coherent manner to move the business from one opportunity to another. They also show that when the narrative is incoherent or the components of the story are misaligned, that these businesses tend to fail. They recommend ways in which the entrepreneur or CEO can create strong narratives for change.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Complementarities_of_between_partnering_firms">Complementarities of between partnering firms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Complementarities of between partnering firms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Studying collaborative research and the accessing of external sources of technology, Hummel et al. (2010) found that in deciding on business partners, it is important to make sure that both parties' business models are complementary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> For example, they found that it was important to identify the value drivers of potential partners by analyzing their business models, and that it is beneficial to find partner firms that understand key aspects of our own firm's business model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The University of Tennessee conducted research into highly collaborative business relationships. Researchers codified their research into a sourcing business model known as Vested (also referred to as <a href="/wiki/Vested_outsourcing" title="Vested outsourcing">Vested Outsourcing</a>). Vested is a hybrid sourcing business model in which buyers and suppliers in an outsourcing or business relationship focus on shared values and goals to create an arrangement that is highly collaborative and mutually beneficial to each.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Categorization">Categorization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Categorization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From about 2012, some research and experimentation has theorized about a so-called "liquid business model".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="V4_BM_framework">V<sup>4</sup> BM framework</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: V4 BM framework">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Al-Debei and Avison (2010) <b>V<sup>4</sup> BM Framework</b> - four main dimensions encapsulating sixteen elements: Value Proposition, Value Architecture, Value Network, and Value Finance<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Al-Debei.2C_M._M._2010_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Al-Debei.2C_M._M._2010-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Value Proposition:</b> This dimension implies that a BM should include a description of the products/services a digital organization offers, or will offer, along with their related information. Furthermore, the BM needs also to describe the value elements incorporated within the offering, as well as the nature of targeted market segment(s) along with their preferences.</li>
<li><b>Value Architecture:</b> portrays the concept as a holistic structural design of an organization, including its technological architecture, organizational infrastructure, and their configurations.</li>
<li><b>Value Network:</b> depicts the cross-company or inter-organization perspective towards the concept and has gained much attention in the BM literature.</li>
<li><b>Value Finance:</b> depicts information related to costing, pricing methods, and revenue structure</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shift_from_pipes_to_platforms">Shift from pipes to platforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Shift from pipes to platforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sangeet_Paul_Choudary" title="Sangeet Paul Choudary">Sangeet Paul Choudary</a> (2013) distinguishes between two broad families of business models in an article in <a href="/wiki/Wired_(magazine)" title="Wired (magazine)"><i>Wired</i> magazine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Choudary contrasts pipes (linear business models) with platforms (networked business models). In the case of pipes, firms create goods and services, push them out and sell them to customers. Value is produced upstream and consumed downstream. There is a linear flow, much like water flowing through a pipe. Unlike pipes, platforms do not just create and push stuff out. They allow users to create and consume value.</p>
<p>In an op-ed on MarketWatch,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Choudary, <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Van_Alstyne" title="Marshall Van Alstyne">Van Alstyne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_G_Parker" title="Geoffrey G Parker">Parker</a> further explain how business models are moving from pipes to platforms, leading to disruption of entire industries.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Platform">Platform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Platform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are three elements to a successful platform business model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The <i>Toolbox</i> creates connection by making it easy for others to plug into the platform. This infrastructure enables interactions between participants. The <i>Magnet</i> creates pull that attracts participants to the platform. For transaction platforms, both producers and consumers must be present to achieve critical mass. The <i>Matchmaker</i> fosters the flow of value by making connections between producers and consumers. Data is at the heart of successful matchmaking, and distinguishes platforms from other business models.</p>
<p>Chen (2009) stated that the business model has to take into account the capabilities of <a href="/wiki/Web_2.0" title="Web 2.0">Web 2.0</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Collective_intelligence" title="Collective intelligence">collective intelligence</a>, network effects, <a href="/wiki/User-generated_content" title="User-generated content">user-generated content</a>, and the possibility of self-improving systems. He suggested that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Service_industry" title="Service industry">service industry</a> such as the airline, traffic, transportation, hotel, restaurant, information and communications technology and online gaming industries will be able to benefit in adopting business models that take into account the characteristics of Web 2.0. He also emphasized that Business Model 2.0 has to take into account not just the technology effect of Web 2.0 but also the networking effect. He gave the example of the success story of <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon</a> in making huge revenues each year by developing an open platform that supports a community of companies that re-use Amazon's on-demand commerce services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Quotation needed from source to verify. (February 2015)">need quotation to verify</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Thomas_W._Malone" title="Thomas W. Malone">Malone</a> et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> found that some business models, as defined by them, indeed performed better than others in a dataset consisting of the largest U.S. firms, in the period 1998 through 2002, while they did not prove whether the existence of a business model mattered.</p>
<p>In the context of the Software-Cluster, which is funded by the German Federal Ministry of Education and Research, a business model wizard<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> for software companies has been developed. It supports the design and analysis of software business models. The tool's underlying concept and data were published in various<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> scientific publications.</p>
<p>The concept of a business model has been incorporated into certain <a href="/wiki/Accounting" title="Accounting">accounting</a> standards. For example, the <a href="/wiki/International_Accounting_Standards_Board" title="International Accounting Standards Board">International Accounting Standards Board</a> (IASB) utilizes an "entity's business model for managing the financial assets" as a criterion for determining whether such assets should be measured at <a href="/wiki/Amortization_(business)" title="Amortization (business)">amortized cost</a> or at <a href="/wiki/Fair_value" title="Fair value">fair value</a> in its <a href="/wiki/Financial_instrument" title="Financial instrument">financial instruments</a> accounting standard, <a href="/wiki/IFRS_9" title="IFRS 9">IFRS 9</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In their 2013 proposal for accounting for financial instruments, the <a href="/wiki/Financial_Accounting_Standards_Board" title="Financial Accounting Standards Board">Financial Accounting Standards Board</a> also proposed a similar use of business model for classifying financial instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The concept of business model has also been introduced into the accounting of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deferred_taxes" title="Deferred taxes">deferred taxes</a> under <a href="/wiki/International_Financial_Reporting_Standards" title="International Financial Reporting Standards">International Financial Reporting Standards</a> with 2010 amendments to IAS 12 addressing deferred taxes related to investment property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both IASB and FASB have proposed using the concept of business model in the context of reporting a lessor's <a href="/wiki/Lease" title="Lease">lease</a> income and lease expense within their joint project on accounting for leases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In its 2016 lease accounting model, <a href="/wiki/IFRS_16" title="IFRS 16">IFRS 16</a>, the IASB chose not to include a criterion of "stand alone utility" in its lease definition because "entities might reach different conclusions for contracts that contain the same rights of use, depending on differences between customers' resources or suppliers' business models."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The concept has also been proposed as an approach for determining the measurement and classification when accounting for <a href="/wiki/Insurance_policy" title="Insurance policy">insurance contracts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> As a result of the increasing prominence the concept of business model has received in the context of financial reporting, the <a href="/wiki/European_Financial_Reporting_Advisory_Group" title="European Financial Reporting Advisory Group">European Financial Reporting Advisory Group</a> (EFRAG), which advises the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> on endorsement of financial reporting standards, commenced a project on the "Role of the Business Model in Financial Reporting" in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Business model design</b> generally refers to the activity of designing a company's business model. It is part of the <a href="/wiki/Business_development" title="Business development">business development</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Business_strategy" title="Business strategy">business strategy</a> process and involves <a href="/wiki/Design_methods" title="Design methods">design methods</a>. Massa and Tucci (2014) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>highlighted the difference between crafting a new business models when none is in place, as it is often the case with academic spinoffs and high technology entrepreneurship, and changing an existing business model, such as when the tooling company Hilti shifted from selling its tools to a leasing model. They suggested that the differences are so profound (for example, lack of resource in the former case and inertia and conflicts with existing configurations and organisational structures in the latter) that it could be worthwhile to adopt different terms for the two. They suggest business model design to refer to the process of crafting a business model when none is in place and business model reconfiguration for process of changing an existing business model, also highlighting that the two process are not mutually exclusive, meaning reconfiguration may involve steps which parallel those of designing a business model.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Al-Debei and Avison (2010) define a business model as an abstract representation of an organization. This may be conceptual, textual, and/or graphical, of all core interrelated architectural, co-operational, and financial arrangements designed and developed by an organization presently and in the future, as well all core products and/or services the organization offers, or will offer, based on these arrangements that are needed to achieve its strategic goals and objectives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Al-Debei.2C_M._M._2008_pp._1-11_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Al-Debei.2C_M._M._2008_pp._1-11-1">[1]</a></sup> This definition indicates that value proposition, value architecture, value finance, and value network articulate the primary constructs or dimensions of business models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Al-Debei.2C_M._M._2010_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Al-Debei.2C_M._M._2010-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_consideration">Economic consideration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Economic consideration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Al-Debei and Avison (2010) consider value finance as one of the main dimensions of BM which depicts information related to costing, pricing methods, and revenue structure. Stewart and Zhao (2000) defined the business model as <i>a statement of how a firm will make money and sustain its profit stream over time.</i> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stewart_and_Zhao_.282000.29_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stewart_and_Zhao_.282000.29-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Component_consideration">Component consideration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Component consideration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Osterwalder et al. (2005) consider the Business Model as the blueprint of how a company does business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osterwalder_et_al._.282005.29_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Osterwalder_et_al._.282005.29-39">[39]</a></sup> Slywotzky (1996) regards the business model as <i>the totality of how a company selects its customers, defines and differentiates it offerings, defines the tasks it will perform itself and those it will outsource, configures its resources, goes to market, creates utility for customers and captures profits.</i> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slywotzky_.281996.29_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Slywotzky_.281996.29-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Strategic_outcome">Strategic outcome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Strategic outcome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Mayo and Brown (1999) considered the business model as <i>the design of key interdependent systems that create and sustain a competitive business.</i> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mayo_and_Brown_.281999.29_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mayo_and_Brown_.281999.29-41">[41]</a></sup> Casadesus-Masanell and Ricart (2011) explain a business model as a set of <i>choices (policy, assets and governance)</i> and <i>consequences (flexible and rigid)</i> and underline the importance of considering <i>how it interacts with models of other players in the industry</i> instead of thinking of it in isolation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions_of_design_or_development">Definitions of design or development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Definitions of design or development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Zott and Amit (2009) consider business model design from the perspectives of design themes and design content. Design themes refer to the system's dominant value creation drivers and design content examines in greater detail the activities to be performed, the linking and sequencing of the activities and who will perform the activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zott_and_Amit_.282009.29_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zott_and_Amit_.282009.29-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_themes_emphasis">Design themes emphasis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Design themes emphasis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Environment-Strategy-Structure-Operations_(ESSO)_Business_Model_as_designed_by_Dr_Michael_Lim_2010.pdf"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1240" data-file-width="1754" height="247" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Environment-Strategy-Structure-Operations_%28ESSO%29_Business_Model_as_designed_by_Dr_Michael_Lim_2010.pdf/page1-350px-Environment-Strategy-Structure-Operations_%28ESSO%29_Business_Model_as_designed_by_Dr_Michael_Lim_2010.pdf.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Environment-Strategy-Structure-Operations_%28ESSO%29_Business_Model_as_designed_by_Dr_Michael_Lim_2010.pdf/page1-525px-Environment-Strategy-Structure-Operations_%28ESSO%29_Business_Model_as_designed_by_Dr_Michael_Lim_2010.pdf.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Environment-Strategy-Structure-Operations_%28ESSO%29_Business_Model_as_designed_by_Dr_Michael_Lim_2010.pdf/page1-700px-Environment-Strategy-Structure-Operations_%28ESSO%29_Business_Model_as_designed_by_Dr_Michael_Lim_2010.pdf.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Environment-Strategy-Structure-Operations_(ESSO)_Business_Model_as_designed_by_Dr_Michael_Lim_2010.pdf" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<b>Environment-Strategy-Structure-Operations (ESSO) Business Model Development</b></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Developing a Framework for Business Model Development with an emphasis on Design Themes, Lim (2010) proposed the Environment-Strategy-Structure-Operations (ESSO) Business Model Development which takes into consideration the alignment of the organization's strategy with the organization's structure, operations, and the environmental factors in achieving <a href="/wiki/Competitive_advantage" title="Competitive advantage">competitive advantage</a> in varying combination of cost, quality, time, flexibility, innovation and affective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lim_.282010.29_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lim_.282010.29-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_content_emphasis">Design content emphasis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Design content emphasis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Business model design includes the modeling and description of a company's:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Value_proposition" title="Value proposition">value propositions</a></li>
<li>target customer segments</li>
<li>distribution channels</li>
<li>customer relationships</li>
<li>value configurations</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Core_competency" title="Core competency">core capabilities</a></li>
<li>commercial network</li>
<li>partner network</li>
<li>cost structure</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Revenue_model" title="Revenue model">revenue model</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Business model design is distinct from <a href="/wiki/Business_process_modeling" title="Business process modeling">business modeling</a>. The former refers to defining the business logic of a company at the strategic level, whereas the latter refers to business process design at the operational level.</p>
<p>A business model design template can facilitate the process of designing and describing a company's business model.</p>
<p>Daas et al. (2012) developed a <a href="/wiki/Decision_support_system" title="Decision support system">decision support system (DSS)</a> for business model design. In their study a decision support system (DSS) is developed to help <a href="/wiki/Software_as_a_service" title="Software as a service">SaaS</a> in this process, based on a design approach consisting of a design process that is guided by various design methods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daas_et_al._.282012.29_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Daas_et_al._.282012.29-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early history of business models it was very typical to define business model types such as bricks-and-mortar or e-broker. However, these types usually describe only one aspect of the business (most often the revenue model). Therefore, more recent literature on business models concentrate on describing a business model as a whole, instead of only the most visible aspects.</p>
<p>The following examples provide an overview for various business model types that have been in discussion since the invention of term <i>business model</i>:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bricks_and_clicks" title="Bricks and clicks">Bricks and clicks business model</a></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Business model by which a company integrates both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/On-line_and_off-line" title="On-line and off-line">offline (<i>bricks</i>) and online (<i>clicks</i>)</a> presences. One example of the bricks-and-clicks model is when a chain of stores allows the user to order products online, but lets them pick up their order at a local store.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Collective_business_system" title="Collective business system">Collective business models</a></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Business system, organization or association typically composed of relatively large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">businesses</a>, tradespersons or <a href="/wiki/Professional" title="Professional">professionals</a> in the same or related fields of endeavor, which pools <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resources" title="Resources">resources</a>, shares information or provides other benefits for their members. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Science_park" title="Science park">science park</a> or high-tech campus provides shared resources (e.g. cleanrooms and other lab facilities) to the firms located on its premises, and in addition seeks to create an innovation community among these firms and their employees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Disintermediation" title="Disintermediation">Cutting out the middleman</a> model</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>The removal of <a href="/wiki/Intermediary" title="Intermediary">intermediaries</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Supply_chain" title="Supply chain">supply chain</a>: "cutting out the middleman". Instead of going through traditional distribution channels, which had some type of intermediate (such as a <a href="/wiki/Distribution_(business)" title="Distribution (business)">distributor</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wholesaler" title="Wholesaler">wholesaler</a>, broker, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agency_(law)" title="Agency (law)">agent</a>), companies may now deal with every customer directly, for example via the Internet.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Direct_selling" title="Direct selling">Direct sales model</a></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Direct selling is <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">marketing</a> and selling products to consumers directly, away from a fixed retail location. Sales are typically made through <a href="/wiki/Party_plan" title="Party plan">party plan</a>, one-to-one demonstrations, and other personal contact arrangements. A text book definition is: "The direct personal presentation, demonstration, and sale of products and services to consumers, usually in their homes or at their jobs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Distribution business models, various</li>
<li>Fee in, free out</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Business model which works by charging the first client a fee for a service, while offering that service free of charge to subsequent clients.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Franchising" title="Franchising">Franchise</a></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Franchising is the practice of using another firm's successful business model. For the franchisor, the franchise is an alternative to building 'chain stores' to <i>distribute</i> goods and avoid investment and liability over a chain. The franchisor's success is the success of the franchisees. The franchisee is said to have a greater incentive than a direct employee because he or she has a direct stake in the business.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Sourcing business model</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>A sourcing business model is a type of business model that is applied specifically to business relationships where more than one party needs to work with another party to be successful. It is the combination of two concepts: the contractual relationship framework a company uses with its supplier (transactional, relational, investment based), and the economic model used (transactional, output or outcome-based).</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Freemium" title="Freemium">Freemium business model</a></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Business model that works by offering basic Web services, or a basic downloadable digital product, for free, while charging a premium for advanced or special features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pay_what_you_can" title="Pay what you can">Pay what you can</a> (PWYC)</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-profit" title="Non-profit">non-profit</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/For-profit" title="For-profit">for-profit</a> business model which does not depend on set prices for its goods, but instead asks customers to pay what they feel the product or service is worth to them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washtimes_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-washtimes-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> It is often used as a promotional tactic,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> but can also be the regular method of doing business. It is a variation on the <a href="/wiki/Gift_economy" title="Gift economy">gift economy</a> and cross-subsidization, in that it depends on <a href="/wiki/Reciprocity_(social_and_political_philosophy)" title="Reciprocity (social and political philosophy)">reciprocity</a> and trust to succeed.</dd>
<dd>"<a href="/wiki/Pay_what_you_want" title="Pay what you want">Pay what you want</a>" (PWYW) is sometimes used synonymously, but "pay what you can" is often more oriented to charity or socially oriented uses, based more on <i>ability</i> to pay, while "pay what you want" is often more broadly oriented to perceived value in combination with willingness and ability to pay.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Frameworks">Frameworks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Frameworks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Technology centric communities have defined "frameworks" for business modeling. These frameworks attempt to define a rigorous approach to defining business value streams. It is not clear, however, to what extent such frameworks are actually important for business planning. Business model frameworks represent the core aspect of any company; they involve "the totality of how a company selects its customers defines and differentiates its offerings, defines the tasks it will perform itself and those it will outsource, configures its resource, goes to market, creates utility for customers, and captures profits".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> A business framework involves internal factors (market analysis; products/services promotion; development of trust; social influence and knowledge sharing) and external factors (competitors and technological aspects).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>A review on business model frameworks can be found in Krumeich et al. (2012).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krumeich2012_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Krumeich2012-55">[55]</a></sup> In the following some frameworks are introduced.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Business_reference_model" title="Business reference model">Business reference model</a></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Business reference model is a reference model, concentrating on the <a href="/wiki/Business_architecture" title="Business architecture">architectural</a> aspects of the core business of an enterprise, service organization or government agency.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Component_business_model" title="Component business model">Component business model</a></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Technique developed by <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> to model and analyze an enterprise. It is a logical representation or map of business components or "building blocks" and can be depicted on a single page. It can be used to analyze the alignment of enterprise strategy with the organization's capabilities and <a href="/wiki/Investment" title="Investment">investments</a>, identify redundant or overlapping business capabilities, etc.</dd>
</dl>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_concepts">Related concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Related concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The process of business model design is part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Business_strategy" title="Business strategy">business strategy</a>. Business model design and innovation refer to the way a firm (or a network of firms) defines its business logic at the strategic level.</p>
<p>In contrast, firms implement their business model at the operational level, through their <a href="/wiki/Business_operations" title="Business operations">business operations</a>. This refers to their process-level activities, capabilities, functions and infrastructure (for example, their business processes and business process modeling), their organisational structures (e.g. organigrams, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Workflows" title="Workflows">workflows</a>, human resources) and systems (e.g. information technology architecture, production lines).</p>
<p>Consequently, an operationally viable and feasible business model requires lateral alignment with the underlining business operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solaimani_2014_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Solaimani_2014-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Brand" title="Brand">brand</a> is a consequence of the business model and has a symbiotic relationship with it, because the business model determines the brand promise, and the brand equity becomes a feature of the model. Managing this is a task of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integrated_marketing" title="Integrated marketing">integrated marketing</a>.</p>
<p>The standard terminology and examples of business models do not apply to most <a href="/wiki/Nonprofit_organization" title="Nonprofit organization">nonprofit organizations</a>, since their sources of income are generally not the same as the beneficiaries. The term 'funding model' is generally used instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>The model is defined by the organization's vision, mission, and values, as well as sets of boundaries for the organization—what products or services it will deliver, what customers or markets it will target, and what supply and delivery channels it will use. While the business model includes high-level strategies and tactical direction for how the organization will implement the model, it also includes the annual goals that set the specific steps the organization intends to undertake in the next year and the measures for their expected accomplishment. Each of these is likely to be part of internal documentation that is available to the internal auditor.</p>
