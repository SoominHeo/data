<p>A <b>working dog</b> is a canine <a href="/wiki/Working_animal" title="Working animal">working animal</a>, i.e., a <a href="/wiki/Dog_type" title="Dog type">type</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dog</a> that is not merely a <a href="/wiki/Pet" title="Pet">pet</a> but learns and performs tasks to assist and/or entertain its human companions, or a breed of such origin. In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, a <b>working dog</b> is one which has been trained to work <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a>, irrespective of its breeding.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Komondor_delvin.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="713" data-file-width="672" height="276" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Komondor_delvin.jpg/260px-Komondor_delvin.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Komondor_delvin.jpg/390px-Komondor_delvin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Komondor_delvin.jpg/520px-Komondor_delvin.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Komondor_delvin.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
This <a href="/wiki/Komondor" title="Komondor">Komondor</a> is guarding <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Within this general description, however, there are several ways in which the phrase is used.</p>
<ul>
<li>To identify any dog that performs actions on a regular basis to assist people. In this context, a dog that helps a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rancher" title="Rancher">rancher</a> manage <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a> or that performs stunts for a trainer who receives pay for its acts is a working dog, as is a <a href="/wiki/Service_dog" title="Service dog">service dog</a> or an <a href="/wiki/Assistance_dog" title="Assistance dog">assistance dog</a>. This might be in comparison to a <a href="/wiki/Companion_dog" title="Companion dog">companion dog</a>, whose purpose is primarily as a <a href="/wiki/Pet" title="Pet">pet</a>.</li>
<li>To distinguish between dogs that are bred for <i>appearance</i> primarily to win <a href="/wiki/Conformation_show" title="Conformation show">conformation shows</a> and <b>working dogs</b> that are bred primarily for their <i>ability</i> to perform a task. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Border_Collie" title="Border Collie">Border Collie</a> that is a conformation champion is not necessarily a good <a href="/wiki/Herding_dog" title="Herding dog">sheepdog</a> and a Border Collie that is a champion at <a href="/wiki/Sheepdog_trial" title="Sheepdog trial">sheepdog trials</a> might not succeed in show rings for its nonstandard appearance. It is also possible that a specimen may excel in both appearance and performance. In many <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Cynologique_Internationale" title="Fédération Cynologique Internationale">FCI</a> countries it is impossible for some working breeds' dogs to become conformation champions without having passed adequate breed specific tests measuring their working abilities.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>For some breeds, there are separate <a href="/wiki/Breed_registry" title="Breed registry">registries</a> for tracking the ancestry of working and show dogs. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, there are separate registries for working and show <a href="/wiki/Australian_Kelpie" title="Australian Kelpie">Australian Kelpies</a>; the working registry encourages the breeding of any Kelpies with a strong instinct to herd, no matter their appearance or coat color; the show registry encourages breeding only among Kelpies whose ancestors were registered as show dogs and who have only solid-colored coats. Other breeds have just a working dog register, independent of the showing registers; such a breed is the <a href="/wiki/Boerboel" title="Boerboel">Boerboel</a> — the breeders of this dog consider entry into the AKC for example would damage the dog's genetic working base if it were ever to be bred for the showing.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>As a catch-all for dog breeds whose original purpose was to perform tasks that do not fit into a more specific category of work. For example, until 1983 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herding_dogs" title="Herding dogs">herding dogs</a> were part of the Working Group. The Herding Group was created when the Working Group became too large.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hartnagle-Taylor_and_Ty_Taylor_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hartnagle-Taylor_and_Ty_Taylor-2">[2]</a></sup> Today, the <a href="/wiki/American_Kennel_Club" title="American Kennel Club">American Kennel Club</a> uses <i>Working Dogs</i> to describe breeds who were originally bred for jobs other than herding or hunting. Such jobs might include pulling carts, guarding and so on. However, in practice these 'show' dogs under the AKC are not proper working dogs, and would indeed not be suitable for use as such. Many true working breeds are still very valuable as working dogs, and without them local economies would suffer. An example of such a breed is the <a href="/wiki/Anatolian_Shepherd" title="Anatolian Shepherd">Anatolian Shepherd</a>, without which many sheep would be destroyed for example by wolves, feral domestic dogs, and in Namibia, the cheetah.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Jobs_performed_by_dogs">Jobs performed by dogs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Working_dog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Jobs performed by dogs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Although most modern dogs are kept as <a href="/wiki/Pet" title="Pet">pets</a>, there are still a tremendous number of ways in which dogs can and do assist humans, and more uses are found for them every year. The following list provides an idea of the versatility of dogs:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turnspit_dog" title="Turnspit dog">Turnspit dogs</a> were used as a source of power, they turned a treadmill connected to a roasting spit. Similar arrangements were used for household duties such as churning butter.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dogcart_(dog-drawn)" title="Dogcart (dog-drawn)">Dogs were used as draught animals</a> to pull small carts for farms, peddlers, or travellers (milk, fish, rags &amp; bones, meat, bread, and other products), to deliver mail, and to pull carts carrying people for transportation or entertainment. They were used in World War I to pull small field guns. Dogs in harness sometimes had guard dogs to protect them from stray dogs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-photos_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-photos-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1839, a ban on draught dogs in London and a later ban on all draught dogs and a tax on other working dogs caused the deaths of over 150,000 dogs, who were replaced in their work by children and adults.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-messy_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-messy-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Service_dog" title="Service dog">Service</a> or assistance dogs help people with various disabilities in every day tasks. Some examples include <a href="/wiki/Mobility_assistance_dog" title="Mobility assistance dog">mobility assistance dogs</a> for the physically handicapped, <a href="/wiki/Guide_dog" title="Guide dog">guide dogs</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Visual_impairment" title="Visual impairment">visually impaired</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hearing_dog" title="Hearing dog">hearing dogs</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearing_impairment" title="Hearing impairment">hearing impaired</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Therapy_dog" title="Therapy dog">Therapy dogs</a> visit people who are incapacitated or prevented in some way from having freedom of movement; these dogs provide cheer and entertainment for the elderly in retirement facilities, the ill and injured in hospitals, and so on. The very act of training dogs can also act as a therapy for human handlers, as in a prisoner rehabilitation project.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Search_and_rescue_dog" title="Search and rescue dog">Rescue dogs</a> assist people who are in difficult situations, such as in the water after a boat disaster, lost in the wilderness, escaped from nursing homes, covered in snow avalanches, buried under collapsed buildings, etc.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Herding_dog" title="Herding dog">Herding dogs</a> are still invaluable to sheep and cattle handlers (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/stockman" title="wikt:stockman">stockmen</a>) around the world for <a href="/wiki/Muster_(livestock)" title="Muster (livestock)">mustering</a>; different breeds are used for the different jobs involved in stock work and for guarding the flocks and herds. Modern herding dogs help to control cattle and wild geese in parks or goats used for weed control. A well trained dog can adapt to control any sort of domestic and many wild animals.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sled_dog" title="Sled dog">Sled dogs</a>, although today primarily used in sporting events, still can assist in transporting people and supplies in rugged, snowy terrain.</li>
<li>Performing dogs such as Circus dogs and dog actors are trained to perform acts that are not intrinsically useful, but instead provide entertainment to their audience or enable human artistic performances.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hunting_dog" title="Hunting dog">Hunting dogs</a> assist hunters in finding, tracking, and retrieving game, or in routing vermin. For larger game animals such as wild boar or bears, dogs may be trained to either hold the prey at bay until the hunter arrives, or to directly attack and hold them.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Guard_dog" title="Guard dog">Guard dogs</a> and <b>watch dogs</b> help to protect private or public property, either in living or used for patrols, as in the military and with security firms.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tracking_(dog)" title="Tracking (dog)">Tracking</a> dogs help find lost people and animals or track down possible criminals.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cadaver_dog_or_Human_Remains_Detection_Dogs" title="Cadaver dog or Human Remains Detection Dogs">Cadaver dog or Human Remains Detection Dogs</a> use their scenting ability to discover bodies or human remains at the scenes of disasters, crimes, accidents, or suicides.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Detection_dog" title="Detection dog">Detection dogs</a> of a wide variety help to detect termites and bedbugs in homes, illegal substances in luggage, bombs, chemicals, and many other substances.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dogs_in_warfare" title="Dogs in warfare">War Dogs</a> or K9 Corps are used by armed forces in many of the same roles as civilian working dogs, but in a military context. In addition, specialized military tasks such as mine detection or wire laying have been assigned to dogs. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_Working_Dog" title="Military Working Dog">Military Working Dog</a> is the more formal, current term for dogs trained for use in military tasks.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Police_dog" title="Police dog">Police dogs</a>, also sometimes called K9 Units, are usually trained to track or immobilize possible criminals while assisting officers in making arrests or investigating the scene of a crime. Some are even specially trained for anti-terrorist units, as in Austria.</li>
<li>Dogs are sometimes used in programs to assist children in learning how to read. The Reading With Rover program in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" title="Washington (U.S. state)">Washington</a> pairs trained dogs with children who read aloud to the dog. This process builds confidence and reduces stress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Seattle_Times_2007-06-16_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Seattle_Times_2007-06-16-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Canine_cancer_detection" title="Canine cancer detection">Canine cancer detection</a></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Search_and_rescue_dogs">Search and rescue dogs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Working_dog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Search and rescue dogs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Search_and_rescue_dog" title="Search and rescue dog">Search and rescue dog</a></div>
<p>Dogs are commonly used as <a href="/wiki/Search_and_rescue" title="Search and rescue">search and rescue</a> workers in cases of lost people and disasters. The <a href="/wiki/St._Bernard_(dog)" title="St. Bernard (dog)">St. Bernard</a> was historically used in Europe in the case of <a href="/wiki/Avalanche" title="Avalanche">avalanches</a> and lost travelers. Search dogs are used in lost person searches each year saving human lives. Several breeds of dogs were used during World War I to locate wounded soldiers in the field. Several cities in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> are experimenting with working dogs as rescue swimmers. In this situation, a strong and well-trained dog is equipped with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flotation_device" title="Flotation device">flotation devices</a> and dropped in the water near a floundering swimmer. The swimmer then grabs onto the dog, and the animal tows the swimmer to shore. The <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(dog)" title="Newfoundland (dog)">Newfoundland</a> has long been used for water rescue, not only on shore, but from fishing boats as well.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pets"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pets" title="Pets">Pets</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Working_dog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Selective_breeding" title="Selective breeding">breeding</a> of working dogs originated from selecting highly intelligent<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, hardy, alert <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mixed-breed_dogs" title="Mixed-breed dogs">mixed-breed dogs</a>. Working dogs resulted when dogs with similar desirable characteristics, such as loyalty and good temperament, were bred. As a result, many working breeds are sought after as family pets. For search and rescue work, typical breeds seen in the field include <a href="/wiki/Labrador_Retriever" title="Labrador Retriever">Labrador Retrievers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Border_Collie" title="Border Collie">Border Collies</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Shepherd_Dog" title="German Shepherd Dog">German Shepherd Dogs</a> and certain members of the hound group. These dogs should have a good prey drive, desire to please the handler, ability to work on and off lead, and be sociable in public settings.</p>
<p>Working dogs make excellent pets as long as potential owners realize that these dogs must be given 'work' to do. Dogs that are not to be used for their original purpose must be trained from a young age and are best suited to active persons and families. <a href="/wiki/Obedience_training" title="Obedience training">Obedience training</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dog_sports" title="Dog sports">dog sports</a>, informal or novelty shows, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sheepdog_trials" title="Sheepdog trials">trial</a> work are all excellent channels for these breeds' energy. At the very least they must have daily walks or other exercise at an appropriate level for the breed, given toys, played with, and provided with human company.</p>
<p>Working dogs that are left alone or ignored become bored, vocal, and even <a href="/wiki/Neuroticism" title="Neuroticism">neurotic</a>; they may exhibit malaise, lethargy, destructive behavior or attempt to escape. Working dogs inappropriately chosen as pets are often surrendered to shelters for these reasons.</p>
