<p><b>Vicarious liability</b> is a form of a <a href="/wiki/Strict_liability" title="Strict liability">strict</a>, <a href="/wiki/Secondary_liability" title="Secondary liability">secondary liability</a> that arises under the <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> doctrine of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agency_(law)" title="Agency (law)">agency</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Respondeat_superior" title="Respondeat superior">respondeat superior</a></i>, the responsibility of the superior for the acts of their subordinate or, in a broader sense, the responsibility of any third party that had the "right, ability or duty to control" the activities of a violator. It can be distinguished from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contributory_liability" title="Contributory liability">contributory liability</a>, another form of secondary liability, which is rooted in the <a href="/wiki/Tort" title="Tort">tort</a> theory of <a href="/wiki/Enterprise_liability" title="Enterprise liability">enterprise liability</a> because, unlike contributory infringement, knowledge is not an element of vicarious liability. <a class="external text" href="https://www.law.cornell.edu/ecourse/cases/netcom.htm" rel="nofollow">907 F. Supp. 1361, **36, citing 3 NIMMER ON COPYRIGHT § 12.04{A}{1}, at 12-70</a> (1995) The law has developed the view that some relationships by their nature require the person who engages others to accept responsibility for the wrongdoing of those others. The most important such relationship for practical purposes is that of employer and employee <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Employers.27_liability">Employers' liability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vicarious_liability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Employers' liability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Employer" title="Employer">Employers</a> are <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vicarious" title="wikt:vicarious">vicariously</a> liable, under the <i><a href="/wiki/Respondeat_superior" title="Respondeat superior">respondeat superior</a></i> doctrine, for negligent acts or omissions by their employees in the course of <a href="/wiki/Employment" title="Employment">employment</a> (sometimes referred to as 'scope of employment').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> To determine whether the employer is liable, the difference between an independent contractor and an employee is to be drawn. Since <i>Hollis v Vabu</i> [2001] 207 CLR 21, the requisite 'employer-employee' relationship is now established through the 'sufficient relationship' test which entails the balancing of several factors such as skill levels required in the job, pay schemes, and degree of control granted to the worker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> For an act to be considered within the course of employment, it must either be authorized or be so connected with an authorized act that it can be considered a mode, though an improper mode, of performing it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Courts sometimes distinguish between an employee's "detour" vs. "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frolic_(law)" title="Frolic (law)">a frolic of their own</a>". For instance, an employer will be held liable if it is shown that the employee had gone on a mere detour in carrying out their duties, whereas an employee acting in his or her own right rather than on the employer's business is undertaking a "frolic" and will not subject the employer to liability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The employer will be held liable if an employee does an authorized act in an unauthorized way.</p>
<p>Employers are also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liable" title="Liable">liable</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> principle represented in the Latin phrase, <i>qui facit per alium facit per se</i> (one who acts through another acts in one's own interests). That is a parallel concept to vicarious liability and strict liability, in which one person is held liable in <a href="/wiki/Criminal_law" title="Criminal law">criminal law</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tort" title="Tort">tort</a> for the acts or omissions of another.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principals.27_liability">Principals' liability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vicarious_liability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Principals' liability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The owner of an automobile can be held vicariously liable for negligence committed by a person to whom the car has been lent, as if the owner was a principal and the driver his or her agent, <i>if</i> the driver is using the car primarily for the purpose of performing a task for the owner. Courts have been reluctant to extend this liability to the owners of other kinds of chattel. For example, the owner of a plane will not be vicariously liable for the actions of a pilot to whom he or she has lent it to perform the owner's purpose. In the United States, vicarious liability for automobiles has since been abolished with respect to car leasing and rental in all 50 states.</p>
<p>One example is in the case of a bank, finance company or other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lienholder" title="Lienholder">lienholder</a> performing a <a href="/wiki/Repossession" title="Repossession">repossession</a> of an automobile from the <a href="/wiki/Registered_owner" title="Registered owner">registered owner</a> for non-payment, the lienholder has a non-delegable duty not to cause a breach of the peace in performing the repossession, or it will be liable for damages even if the repossession is performed by an agent. This requirement means that whether a repossession is performed by the lienholder or by an agent, the repossessor must not cause a breach of the peace or the lienholder will be held responsible.</p>
<p>This requirement not to breach the peace is held upon the lienholder even if the breach is caused by, say, the debtor's objecting to the repossession or resisting the repossession. In the court case of <i>MBank El Paso v. Sanchez</i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Court_citation" title="Court citation">836 S.W.2d 151</a>, where a hired repossessor towed away a car even after the registered owner locked herself in it, the court decided that this was an unlawful breach of the peace and declared the repossession invalid. The debtor was also awarded $1,200,000 in damages from the bank.</p>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the question of <a href="/wiki/Parental_responsibility_(criminal)" title="Parental responsibility (criminal)">parental responsibility</a> generally and the issue of parental vicarious liability for the torts of their children is evolving. What is clear is that parents can be held liable for their own negligent acts, such as failure to supervise a child, or failure to keep a dangerous instrument such as a <a href="/wiki/Handgun" title="Handgun">handgun</a> outside the reach of their children.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Liability_of_corporations_in_tort">Liability of corporations in tort</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vicarious_liability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Liability of corporations in tort">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/English_law" title="English law">English law</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporation</a> can only act through its employees and agents so it is necessary to decide in which circumstances the law of agency or vicarious liability will apply to hold the corporation liable in tort for the <a href="/wiki/Fraud" title="Fraud">frauds</a> of its directors or senior officers.</p>
<p>If liability for the particular tort requires a state of mind, then to be liable, the director or senior officer must have that state of mind and it must be attributed to the company. In <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Meridian_Global_Funds_Management_Asia_Limited_v._Securities_Commission&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Meridian Global Funds Management Asia Limited v. Securities Commission (page does not exist)">Meridian Global Funds Management Asia Limited v. Securities Commission</a></i> [1995] 2 AC 500, two employees of the company, acting within the scope of their authority but unknown to the directors, used company funds to acquire some shares. The question was whether the company knew, or ought to have known, that it had acquired those shares.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Privy_Council" title="Privy Council">Privy Council</a> held that it did. Whether by virtue of their actual or ostensible authority as agents acting within their authority (see <i>Lloyd v Grace, Smith &amp; Co.</i> [1912] AC 716) or as employees acting in the course of their employment (see <i>Armagas Limited v Mundogas S.A.</i> [1986] 1 AC 717), their acts and omissions and their knowledge could be attributed to the company, and this could give rise to liability as joint tortfeasors where the directors have assumed responsibility on their own behalf and not just on behalf of the company.</p>
<p>So if a director or officer is expressly authorised to make representations of a particular class on behalf of the company, and fraudulently makes a representation of that class to a third party causing loss, the company will be liable even though the particular representation was an improper way of doing what he was authorised to do. The extent of authority is a question of fact and is significantly more than the fact of an employment which gave the employee the opportunity to carry out the fraud.</p>
<p>In <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panorama_Developments_Ltd_v_Fidelis_Furnishing_Fabrics_Ltd" title="Panorama Developments Ltd v Fidelis Furnishing Fabrics Ltd">Panorama Developments (Guildford) Limited v Fidelis Furnishing Fabrics Limited</a></i> [1971] 2 QB 711, a company secretary fraudulently hired cars for his own use without the knowledge of the managing director. A company secretary routinely enters into contracts in the company's name and has administrative responsibilities that would give apparent authority to hire cars. Hence, the company was liable.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Employees.27_continued_liability_and_indemnity">Employees' continued liability and indemnity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vicarious_liability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Employees' continued liability and indemnity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A common misconception involves the liability of the employee for tortious acts committed within the scope and authority of their employment. Although the employer is liable under respondeat superior for the employee's conduct, the employee, too, remains jointly liable for the harm caused. As the American Law Institute's Restatement of the Law, Third, Agency § 7.01 states,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>An agent is subject to liability to a third party harmed by the agent’s tortious conduct. Unless an applicable statute provides otherwise, an actor remains subject to liability although the actor acts as an agent or an employee, with actual or apparent authority, or within the scope of employment.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Every American state follows this same rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The question of indemnification arises when either solely the employee or solely the employer is sued. If only the employee is sued, then that employee may seek indemnification from the employer if the conduct was within the course and scope of their employment. If only the employer is sued, then the employer can attempt to avoid liability by claiming the employee's conduct was outside of the scope of the employee's authority, but the employer generally cannot sue the employee to recover indemnification for the employee's torts. For an example of a court confirming an employer's right to sue an employee for indemnification, see the case of <i><a class="external text" href="http://www.safetyphoto.co.uk/subsite/case%20i%20j%20k%20l/lister_v_romford_ice__cold_storage.htm" rel="nofollow">Lister v Romford Ice Cold Storage</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecclesiastical_corporations">Ecclesiastical corporations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vicarious_liability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ecclesiastical corporations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 2003 decision <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doe_v._Bennett" title="Doe v. Bennett">Doe v. Bennett</a>, the Supreme Court of Canada ruled that in cases of abuse scandals involving Catholic priests, liability derives from the power and authority over parishioners that the Church gave to its clergymen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
