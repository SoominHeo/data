<p>The <b>9K38 <i>Igla</i></b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Игла́</span>, "needle") is a Russian/<a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> man-portable <a href="/wiki/Infrared_homing" title="Infrared homing">infrared homing</a> <a href="/wiki/Surface-to-air_missile" title="Surface-to-air missile">surface-to-air missile</a> (SAM). "9K38" is the Russian <a href="/wiki/GRAU" title="GRAU">GRAU</a> designation of the system. Its <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">US DoD</a> designation is <b>SA-18</b> and its <a href="/wiki/NATO_reporting_name" title="NATO reporting name">NATO reporting name</a> is <b><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/grouse" title="wiktionary:grouse">Grouse</a></b>; a simplified, earlier version is known as the <b>9K310 Igla-1</b>, or <b>SA-16 <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gimlet" title="wiktionary:gimlet">Gimlet</a></b>. The latest variant is the <b>9K338 Igla-S</b>, with NATO reporting name <b>SA-24 Grinch</b>. It has been fielded by the Russian Army since 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is also a two-barrel 9K38 missile launcher called Djigit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=9K38_Igla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The development of the Igla short-range man-portable air defense system (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MANPADS" title="MANPADS">MANPADS</a>) began in the <a href="/wiki/Kolomna" title="Kolomna">Kolomna</a> <a href="/wiki/OKB" title="OKB">OKB</a> in 1972. Contrary to what is commonly reported, the Igla is not an improved version of the earlier <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Strela_(disambiguation)" title="Strela (disambiguation)">Strela</a> family (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SA-7_Grail" title="SA-7 Grail">Strela-2</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SA-14_Gremlin" title="SA-14 Gremlin">Strela-3</a>), but an all new project.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The main goals were to create a missile with better resistance to countermeasures and wider engagement envelope than the earlier Strela series MANPADS systems.</p>
<p>Technical difficulties in the development quickly made it obvious that the development would take far longer than anticipated, however, and in 1978 the program split in two: while the development of the full-capability Igla would continue, a simplified version (Igla-1) with a simpler IR seeker based on that of the earlier Strela-3 would be developed to enter service earlier than the full-capability version could be finished.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Igla-1">Igla-1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=9K38_Igla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Igla-1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 9K310 Igla-1 system and its 9M313 missile were accepted into service in the Soviet army on 11 March 1981. The main differences from the Strela-3 included an optional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Identification_Friend_or_Foe" title="Identification Friend or Foe">Identification Friend or Foe</a> system to prevent firing on friendly aircraft, an automatic lead and super elevation to simplify shooting and reduce minimum firing range, a slightly larger rocket, reduced drag and better guidance system extend maximum range and improve performance against fast and maneuverable targets, an improved lethality on target achieved by a combination of delayed impact <a href="/wiki/Fuse_(explosives)" title="Fuse (explosives)">fuzing</a>, terminal maneuver to hit the fuselage rather than jet nozzle, an additional charge to set off the remaining rocket fuel (if any) on impact, an improved resistance to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_countermeasures" title="Infrared countermeasures">infrared countermeasures</a> (both decoy flares and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ALQ-144" title="ALQ-144">ALQ-144</a> series jamming emitters), and slightly improved seeker sensitivity.</p>
<p>The seeker has two detectors – a cooled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MWIR" title="MWIR">MWIR</a> <a href="/wiki/Indium_antimonide" title="Indium antimonide">InSb</a> detector for detection of the target and uncooled <a href="/wiki/Lead(II)_sulfide" title="Lead(II) sulfide">PbS</a> SWIR detector for detection of IR decoys (flares). The built-in logic determines whether the detected object is a target or a decoy. The latest version (Igla-S) is reported to have additional detectors around the main seeker to provide further resistance against pulsed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IRCM" title="IRCM">IRCM</a> devices commonly used on helicopters.</p>
<p>The 9M313 missile features an aerospike mounted on a tripod (Igla's 9M39 missile has aerospike attached directly to the seeker dome), which reduces a <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock wave</a>, thus providing less dome heating and greater range. The name Igla is derived from these devices.</p>
<p>Like many other MANPADS, Igla-1 and Igla feature so-called rolling airframe missiles. These missiles roll in flight (900 – 1200 rpm) so steering the missile requires just a single pair of control surfaces, unlike roll-stabilized missiles, which require separate control surfaces for pitch and yaw. Both 9M313 and 9M39 missiles contain a <a href="/wiki/Gas_generator" title="Gas generator">gas generator</a>, which drives a small <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbine</a> to provide electrical power, and the pistons, which move the <a href="/wiki/Canard_(aeronautics)" title="Canard (aeronautics)">canards</a> used to steer the missile in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bang-bang_control" title="Bang-bang control">bang-bang</a> mode. In addition to that, two exhaust tubes of the gas generator are placed perpendicular to the steering canards to provide maneuverability immediately after launch when the missile airspeed is too low for canards to be effective. Later versions of Igla are reported to use <a href="/wiki/Proportional_control" title="Proportional control">proportional control</a> to drive the canards, which enables greater precision and less oscillation of the flightpath.</p>
<p>According to the manufacturer, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South African</a> tests have shown<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the Igla's superiority over the contemporary (1982 service entry) but smaller and lighter American FIM-92A <a href="/wiki/FIM-92_Stinger" title="FIM-92 Stinger">Stinger</a> missile. However, other tests in <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a> did not support<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> any clear superiority, but effectively equal seeker performance and only marginally shorter time of flight and longer range for the Igla.</p>
<p>According to Kolomna OKB,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the Igla-1 has a P<sub>k</sub> (<a href="/wiki/Probability_of_kill" title="Probability of kill">probability of kill</a>) of 0.30 to 0.48 against unprotected targets which is reduced to 0.24 in the presence of decoy flares and jamming. In another report, the manufacturer claimed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> a P<sub>k</sub> of 0.59 against an approaching and 0.44 against receding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-4_Phantom_II" title="F-4 Phantom II">F-4 Phantom II</a> fighter not employing <a href="/wiki/Infrared_countermeasure" title="Infrared countermeasure">infrared countermeasures</a> or evasive maneuvers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Igla">Igla</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=9K38_Igla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Igla">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The full-capability 9K38 Igla with its 9M39 missile was finally accepted into service in the Soviet Army in 1983. The main improvements over the Igla-1 included much improved resistance against flares and jamming, a more sensitive seeker, expanding forward-hemisphere engagement capability to include straight-approaching fighters (<a href="/wiki/All-aspect" title="All-aspect">all-aspect</a> capability) under favourable circumstances, a slightly longer range, a higher-impulse, shorter-burning rocket with higher peak velocity (but approximately same time of flight to maximum range).</p>
<p>The naval variant of 9K38 Igla has the NATO reporting name SA-N-10 Grouse.</p>
<p>The Igla–1M missile consists of a Ground Power Supply Source (GPSS), Launching Tube, Launching Mechanism &amp; Missile (9M 313–1).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Replacement">Replacement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=9K38_Igla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Replacement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Igla is being replaced in Russian service by the <a href="/wiki/9K333_Verba" title="9K333 Verba">9K333 Verba</a> (Willow) MANPADS. The Verba's primary feature is its multispectral optical seeker, using three sensors as opposed to the Igla-S' two. Cross-checking sensors against one another better discriminates between relevant targets and decoys, and decreases the chance of disruption from countermeasures, including lasers that attempt to blind missiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iraq">Iraq</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=9K38_Igla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Iraq">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Desert_Storm_.281991.29">Desert Storm (1991)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=9K38_Igla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Desert Storm (1991)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_combat_losses_of_United_States_military_aircraft_since_the_Vietnam_War#1991_.28Operation_Desert_Shield.2FDesert_Storm.29" title="List of combat losses of United States military aircraft since the Vietnam War">US aircraft losses in Desert Storm</a></div>
<p>The first combat use of the Igla-1E was during the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a>. On 17 January 1991, a <a href="/wiki/Panavia_Tornado" title="Panavia Tornado">Panavia Tornado</a> bomber of the British Royal Air Force was shot down by an Iraqi MANPADS that may have been an Igla-1E (or Strela-3) after an unsuccessful bombing mission. The crew, <a href="/wiki/Flight_lieutenant" title="Flight lieutenant">Flt Lts</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Peters_(RAF_officer)" title="John Peters (RAF officer)">J G Peters</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Nichol_(RAF_officer)" title="John Nichol (RAF officer)">A J Nichol</a>, were both captured and held as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prisoners_of_war" title="Prisoners of war">prisoners of war</a> (POWs) until the cessation of hostilities. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, an Igla-1E shot down an American <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_F-16_Fighting_Falcon" title="General Dynamics F-16 Fighting Falcon">F-16</a> on 27 February 1991. The pilot was captured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is uncertain if an <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_AC-130" title="Lockheed AC-130">AC-130H</a> lost was hit by a 'Strela' missile or a more recent Igla since Iraq had SA-7, SA-14 and SA-16 missiles at the time, according to the SIPRI database.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="From_2003">From 2003</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=9K38_Igla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: From 2003">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_aviation_shootdowns_and_accidents_during_the_Iraq_War" title="List of aviation shootdowns and accidents during the Iraq War">List of aviation shootdowns and accidents during the Iraq War</a></div>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>, American and coalition forces suffered a relevant number of helicopter losses. Around a third of them, around 40 aircraft were due to hostile fire, including losses to small arms fire, <a href="/wiki/Anti-aircraft_warfare" title="Anti-aircraft warfare">Anti Aircraft guns</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket-propelled_grenades" title="Rocket-propelled grenades">Rocket-propelled grenades</a> and MANPADS; any kind of combat helicopter was shot down from small observation helicopters to armoured <a href="/wiki/Boeing_AH-64_Apache" title="Boeing AH-64 Apache">Apache</a> gunships. Among the losses to MANPADS, some were reported as losses to older <a href="/wiki/9K32_Strela-2" title="9K32 Strela-2">Strela-2 (SA-7)</a> or <a href="/wiki/9K34_Strela-3" title="9K34 Strela-3">Strela-3 (SA-14)</a> while others were due to more modern Igla-1E (SA-16) missiles.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rwanda">Rwanda</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=9K38_Igla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Rwanda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_Juv%C3%A9nal_Habyarimana_and_Cyprien_Ntaryamira" title="Assassination of Juvénal Habyarimana and Cyprien Ntaryamira">Assassination of Juvénal Habyarimana and Cyprien Ntaryamira</a></div>
<p>Private intelligence company <a href="/wiki/Stratfor" title="Stratfor">Stratfor</a> asserts that Igla-1E missiles were used in the 1994 shoot down of a Rwandan government flight, killing the presidents of Rwanda and Burundi and sparking the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rwandan_Genocide" title="Rwandan Genocide">Rwandan Genocide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cenepa_War">Cenepa War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=9K38_Igla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cenepa War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Cenepa_War" title="Cenepa War">Cenepa War</a> between Ecuador and Peru, both the <a href="/wiki/Ecuadorian_Army" title="Ecuadorian Army">Ecuadorian Army</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Peruvian_Army" title="Peruvian Army">Peruvian Army</a> (which had 90 functioning firing units) utilized Igla-1E missiles against aircraft and helicopters.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Peruvian_Air_Force" title="Peruvian Air Force">Peruvian Air Force</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mi-25" title="Mi-25">Mi-25</a> attack helicopter was shot down on 7 February 1995 around Base del Sur, killing the 3 crewmen, while an <a href="/wiki/Ecuadorian_Air_Force" title="Ecuadorian Air Force">Ecuadorian Air Force</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-37_Dragonfly" title="A-37 Dragonfly">A-37 Dragonfly</a> was hit but managed to land on 11 February. Hits on additional Ecuadorian aircraft were claimed but could not be confirmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bosnia">Bosnia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=9K38_Igla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Bosnia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/Operation_Deliberate_Force" title="Operation Deliberate Force">Operation Deliberate Force</a>, on 30 August 1995; a French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mirage_2000D" title="Mirage 2000D">Mirage 2000D</a> was shot down over <a href="/wiki/Pale,_Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Pale, Bosnia and Herzegovina">Pale</a> by an Igla fired by air defence units of the <a href="/wiki/Army_of_Republika_Srpska" title="Army of Republika Srpska">Army of Republika Srpska</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The pilots, Lt. Jose-Manuel Souvignet (pilot) and Capt. Frederic Chiffot (back-seater), were captured and freed in December 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chechnya">Chechnya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=9K38_Igla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Chechnya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Russian_aircraft_losses_in_the_Second_Chechen_War" title="List of Russian aircraft losses in the Second Chechen War">List of Russian aircraft losses in the Second Chechen War</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/2002_Khankala_Mi-26_crash" title="2002 Khankala Mi-26 crash">2002 Khankala Mi-26 crash</a> occurred on 19 August 2002 when a team of <a href="/wiki/Chechnya" title="Chechnya">Chechen</a> separatists brought down a Russian <a href="/wiki/Mil_Mi-26" title="Mil Mi-26">Mil Mi-26</a> helicopter in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minefield" title="Minefield">minefield</a> with an Igla; this resulted in the death of 127 Russian soldiers in the greatest loss of life in the history of helicopter aviation. It was also the most deadly aviation disaster ever suffered by the Russian armed forces,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-life_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-life-14">[14]</a></sup> as well as their worst loss of life in a single day since 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Egypt">Egypt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=9K38_Igla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Egypt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 26 January 2014, the militant group <a href="/wiki/Ansar_Bait_al-Maqdis" title="Ansar Bait al-Maqdis">Ansar Bait al-Maqdis</a> shot down an Egyptian Mi-17 over the <a href="/wiki/Sinai_insurgency" title="Sinai insurgency">northern Sinai peninsula</a> using a suspected Igla-1E or Igla. How the group came to obtain the weapon is currently unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Libya">Libya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=9K38_Igla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Libya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/2011_military_intervention_in_Libya" title="2011 military intervention in Libya">2011 military intervention in Libya</a>, Libyan loyalist forces engaged coalition aircraft with a certain number of Igla-S. Three Igla-S were fired against British <a href="/wiki/AgustaWestland_Apache" title="AgustaWestland Apache">Apache</a> attack helicopters of the <a href="/wiki/No._656_Squadron_AAC" title="No. 656 Squadron AAC">656 Squadron Army Air Corps</a> operating from the <a href="/wiki/HMS_Ocean_(L12)" title="HMS Ocean (L12)">HMS Ocean</a> <a href="/wiki/Amphibious_assault_ship" title="Amphibious assault ship">amphibious assault ship</a>. According to the squadron commander at the time, they were all dodged by insistent use of <a href="/wiki/Flare_(countermeasure)" title="Flare (countermeasure)">decoy flares</a> by the gunships who in exchange successfully engaged the shooters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 23 March 2015, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Libya_Dawn" title="Libya Dawn">Libya Dawn</a>-operated MiG-23UB was shot down with an Igla-S (reportedly a truck-mounted Strelets variant) while bombing Al Watiya airbase (near <a href="/wiki/Zintan" title="Zintan">Zintan</a>), controlled by forces from the internationally recognized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_of_Deputies" title="Council of Deputies">Council of Deputies</a>. Both pilots were killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Syria">Syria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=9K38_Igla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Syria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_aviation_shootdowns_and_accidents_during_the_Syrian_Civil_War" title="List of aviation shootdowns and accidents during the Syrian Civil War">List of aviation shootdowns and accidents during the Syrian Civil War</a></div>
<p>Video has surfaced showing rebels using an Igla-1E on a Syrian government helicopter. Such weapons were believed to have been looted from a Syrian army base in Aleppo in February 2013. In 2014, a member of the rebel group Harakat Hazm was filmed aiming an Igla-1E into the air on the same day that the group was filmed operating <a href="/wiki/BGM-71_TOW" title="BGM-71 TOW">BGM-71 TOW</a> missiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Whether these weapons were raided from regime stockpiles or supplied via overseas is unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ukraine">Ukraine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=9K38_Igla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Ukraine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Ukrainian_aircraft_losses_during_the_Ukrainian_crisis" title="List of Ukrainian aircraft losses during the Ukrainian crisis">List of Ukrainian aircraft losses during the Ukrainian crisis</a></div>
<p>On 14 June 2014, rebel forces near <a href="/wiki/Luhansk_International_Airport" title="Luhansk International Airport">Luhansk International Airport</a> in Eastern Ukraine <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_Air_Force_Ilyushin_Il-76_shoot-down" title="Ukrainian Air Force Ilyushin Il-76 shoot-down">shot down an IL-76 of the Ukrainian Airforce</a> probably using an Igla MANPADS, killing all 49 Ukrainian service personnel on board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nagorno_Karabakh">Nagorno Karabakh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=9K38_Igla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Nagorno Karabakh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2014_Armenian_Mil_Mi-24_shootdown" title="2014 Armenian Mil Mi-24 shootdown">2014 Armenian Mil Mi-24 shootdown</a></div>
<p>On 12 November 2014, Azerbaijani forces shot down an <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Army" title="Armenian Army">Armenian Army</a> Mi-24 of a formation of two which were flying near the Azerbaijani border. All three on board died when the helicopter crashed while flying at low altitude and was hit by an Igla-S MANPADS fired by Azerbaijani soldiers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Turkey">Turkey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=9K38_Igla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Turkey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 13 May 2016, <a href="/wiki/Kurdistan_Workers%27_Party" title="Kurdistan Workers' Party">PKK</a> militants shot down a Turkish Army <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bell_AH-1W_SuperCobra" title="Bell AH-1W SuperCobra">Bell AH-1W SuperCobra</a> attack helicopter using 9K38 Igla (SA-18 Grouse) version of this missile system. The missile severed the tail section from the rest of the helicopter, causing it to fragment in midair and crash, killing the two pilots on board. The Turkish government first claimed that it fell due to technical failure before it became clear that it was shot down. The PKK later released video footage of the rocket being fired and striking the helicopter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<p>Several variants of the Igla were developed for specific applications:</p>
<dl>
<dt>Igla-1E</dt>
<dd>Export version.</dd>
<dt>Igla-1M</dt>
<dd>Improved version of 9K38 Igla. Entered service in Soviet Military during the late 1980s.</dd>
<dt>Igla-1D</dt>
<dd>A version for paratroopers and special forces.</dd>
<dt>Igla-1V</dt>
<dd>Air-launched version, mainly for combat helicopters.</dd>
<dt>Igla-1N</dt>
<dd>A version with heavier warhead at the cost of a slight reduction in range and speed.</dd>
<dt>Igla-1A</dt>
<dd>Export version?</dd>
<dt><a class="external text" href="http://www.armyrecognition.com/russia_russian_army_light_heavy_weapons_uk/sa-24_grinch_9k338_igla-s_portable_air_defense_missile_system_technical_data_sheet_specifications_uk.html" rel="nofollow">Igla-S (SA-24 Grinch)</a> </dt>
<dd>The newest variant, which is a substantially improved variant with longer range, more sensitive seeker, improved resistance to latest countermeasures, and a heavier warhead. Manufacturer reports hit probability of 0.8–0.9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> State tests were completed in December 2001 and the system entered service in 2002. Series produced by the Degtryaev plant since 1 December 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9.D0.9A338_.D0.98.D0.B3.D0.BB.D0.B0-.D0.A1_-_SA-24_GRINCH_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-9.D0.9A338_.D0.98.D0.B3.D0.BB.D0.B0-.D0.A1_-_SA-24_GRINCH-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></dd>
<dt><a class="external text" href="http://www.armyrecognition.com/russia_russian_missile_system_vehicle_uk/strelets_igla_igla-s_sa-24_sa-18_automatic_remote_firing_control_launcher_unit_data_sheet_uk.html" rel="nofollow">Strelets Igla-S / Igla</a></dt>
<dd>The Strelets is designed for remote automated firing of the Igla and Igla-S surface-to-air missile by single shot, ripple or in salvo.</dd>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_plot_against_Air_Force_One">Use in plot against Air Force One</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=9K38_Igla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Use in plot against Air Force One">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 12 August 2003, as a result of a <a href="/wiki/Sting_operation" title="Sting operation">sting operation</a> arranged as a result of cooperation between the American, British and Russian intelligence agencies, <a href="/wiki/Hemant_Lakhani" title="Hemant Lakhani">Hemant Lakhani</a>, a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> national, was intercepted attempting to bring what he had thought was an older-generation Igla into the United States. He is said to have intended the missile to be used in an attack on <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_One" title="Air Force One">Air Force One</a>, the American presidential plane, or on a commercial US airliner, and is understood to have planned to buy 50 more of these weapons.</p>
<p>After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federalnaya_Sluzhba_Bezopasnosti" title="Federalnaya Sluzhba Bezopasnosti">Federalnaya Sluzhba Bezopasnosti</a> (FSB) detected the dealer in Russia, he was approached by US undercover agents posing as terrorists wanting to shoot down a commercial plane. He was then provided with an inert Igla by undercover Russian agents, and arrested in <a href="/wiki/Newark,_New_Jersey" title="Newark, New Jersey">Newark, New Jersey</a>, when making the delivery to the undercover US agent. An Indian citizen residing in <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Moinuddeen_Ahmed_Hameed&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Moinuddeen Ahmed Hameed (page does not exist)">Moinuddeen Ahmed Hameed</a> and an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yehuda_Abraham&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yehuda Abraham (page does not exist)">Yehuda Abraham</a> who allegedly provided money to buy the missile were also arrested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Yehuda Abraham is President and CEO of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ambuy_Gem_Corp&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ambuy Gem Corp (page does not exist)">Ambuy Gem Corp</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Lakhani was convicted by jury in April 2005, and was sentenced to 47 years in prison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>

<p>Igla and Igla-1 SAMs have been exported from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Former_Soviet_Union" title="Former Soviet Union">former Soviet Union</a> to over 30 countries, including <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Bosnia and Herzegovina">Bosnia and Herzegovina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a> (former producer), <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eritrea" title="Eritrea">Eritrea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, Hungary, India, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Macedonia" title="Republic of Macedonia">Republic of Macedonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, Mexico, <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>. Several guerrilla and terrorist organizations are also known to have Iglas. Alleged Operatives of the <a href="/wiki/Liberation_Tigers_of_Tamil_Eelam" title="Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam">Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam</a> a rebel organization fighting for a homeland for Tamils in the island of Sri Lanka were arrested in August 2006 by undercover agents of the FBI posing as arms dealers, while trying to purchase the Igla. In 2003 the unit cost was approximately US$60,000–80,000.</p>
<p>Large numbers have been sold to the government of <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, raising United States concerns that they may end up in the hands of Colombian guerillas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
