<p><i><b>Saurophaganax</b></i> ("lizard-eater") is a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allosaurid" title="Allosaurid">allosaurid</a> <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaur</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Morrison_Formation" title="Morrison Formation">Morrison Formation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Late_Jurassic" title="Late Jurassic">Late Jurassic</a> <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a> (latest <a href="/wiki/Kimmeridgian" title="Kimmeridgian">Kimmeridgian</a> age, about 151 million years ago), USA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-turner1999_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-turner1999-1">[1]</a></sup> Some paleontologists consider it to be a species of <i><a href="/wiki/Allosaurus" title="Allosaurus">Allosaurus</a></i> (<i>A. maximus</i>). <i>Saurophaganax</i> represents a very large Morrison allosaurid characterized by horizontal laminae at the bases of the dorsal neural spines above the transverse processes, and "meat-chopper" <a href="/wiki/Chevron_(anatomy)" title="Chevron (anatomy)">chevrons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DFG97_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-DFG97-2">[2]</a></sup> The maximum size of <i>S. maximus</i> has been estimated at anywhere from 10.5 metres (34 ft)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G.S.Paul2010_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-G.S.Paul2010-3">[3]</a></sup> to 13 m (43 ft) in length,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holtz2008_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holtz2008-4">[4]</a></sup> and around 3 tonnes (3.0 long tons; 3.3 short tons) in weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G.S.Paul2010_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-G.S.Paul2010-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_naming">Discovery and naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saurophaganax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery and naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saurophaganax_NT.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="302" data-file-width="600" height="111" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Saurophaganax_NT.jpg/220px-Saurophaganax_NT.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Saurophaganax_NT.jpg/330px-Saurophaganax_NT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Saurophaganax_NT.jpg/440px-Saurophaganax_NT.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Saurophaganax_NT.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Life restoration</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1931 and 1932 <a href="/wiki/J._Willis_Stovall" title="J. Willis Stovall">John Willis Stovall</a> uncovered remains of a large theropod near <a href="/wiki/Kenton" title="Kenton">Kenton</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cimarron_County" title="Cimarron County">Cimarron County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a> in layers of the late <a href="/wiki/Kimmeridgian" title="Kimmeridgian">Kimmeridgian</a>. In 1941 these were named <i>Saurophagus maximus</i> by Stovall in an article by journalist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Grace_Ernestine_Ray&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Grace Ernestine Ray (page does not exist)">Grace Ernestine Ray</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The generic name is derived from Greek σαυρος, <i>sauros</i>, "lizard" and φάγειν, <i>phagein</i>, "to eat", with the compound meaning of "eater of saurians". The <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">specific epithet</a> <i>maximus</i> means "the largest" in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>. Because the naming article did not contain a description, the name remained a <i><a href="/wiki/Nomen_nudum" title="Nomen nudum">nomen nudum</a></i>. In 1987 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spencer_George_Lucas" title="Spencer George Lucas">Spencer George Lucas</a> erroneously made OMNH 4666, a <a href="/wiki/Tibia" title="Tibia">tibia</a>, the lectotype, unaware that "Saurophagus" was a <i>nomen nudum</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later it was discovered that the name <i>Saurophagus</i> was preoccupied: it had already in 1831 been given by <a href="/wiki/William_John_Swainson" title="William John Swainson">William Swainson</a> to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tyrant-flycatcher" title="Tyrant-flycatcher">tyrant-flycatcher</a>, an extant eater of taxonomically true lizards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1995 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Chure&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Daniel Chure (page does not exist)">Daniel Chure</a> named a new genus: <i>Saurophaganax</i>, adding Greek suffix -άναξ, <i>anax</i>, meaning "ruler", to the earlier name. Chure also found OMNH 4666 undiagnostic in relation to <i>Allosaurus</i>, so he chose OMNH 01123, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neural_arch" title="Neural arch">neural arch</a>, as the holotype for <i>Saurophaganax</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> — and <i>Saurophaganax</i> is not a renaming of "Saurophagus".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DJC95_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-DJC95-9">[9]</a></sup> Much of the material informally named "Saurophagus maximus", namely those diagnostic elements that could be distinguished from <i>Allosaurus</i>, were by Chure referred to <i>Saurophaganax maximus</i>. They contain disarticulated bones of at least four individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DJC95_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-DJC95-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Saurophaganax</i> is the official <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_fossil" title="State fossil">state fossil</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and a large skeleton of <i>Saurophaganax</i> can be seen in the Jurassic hall in the <a href="/wiki/Sam_Noble_Oklahoma_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="Sam Noble Oklahoma Museum of Natural History">Sam Noble Oklahoma Museum of Natural History</a>. Although the best known <i>Saurophaganax</i> material was found in the panhandle of Oklahoma, possible <i>Saurophaganax</i> material, NMMNH P-26083, a partial skeleton including a femur, several tail vertebrae, and a hip bone, has been found in northern <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_Allosaurus">Relationship with <i>Allosaurus</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saurophaganax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Relationship with Allosaurus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The identification of <i>Saurophaganax</i> is a matter of dispute. It has been described as its own genus,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DJC95_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-DJC95-9">[9]</a></sup> or as a species of <i>Allosaurus</i>: <i>Allosaurus maximus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DKS98_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-DKS98-11">[11]</a></sup> The most recent review of basal tetanurans accepted <i>Saurophaganax</i> as a distinct genus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HMC04_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-HMC04-12">[12]</a></sup> New possible <i>Saurophaganax</i> material from New Mexico may clear up the status of the genus.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saurophaganax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><i>Saurophaganax</i> was one of the largest carnivores of Late Jurassic North America. Chure even gave an estimate of 14 m (46 ft),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DJC95_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-DJC95-9">[9]</a></sup> though other estimations have been lower. The fossils known of <i>Saurophaganax</i> (both the possible New Mexican material and the Oklahoma material) are known from the Brushy Basin Member, which is the latest part of the <a href="/wiki/Morrison_Formation" title="Morrison Formation">Morrison Formation</a>, suggesting that this genus was either always uncommon or that it first appeared rather late in the Jurassic. <i>Saurophaganax</i> was large for an allosaurid, and bigger than both its contemporaries <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torvosaurus_tanneri" title="Torvosaurus tanneri">Torvosaurus tanneri</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allosaurus_fragilis" title="Allosaurus fragilis">Allosaurus fragilis</a></i>. Being much rarer than its contemporaries, making up one percent or less of the Morrison theropod fauna, not much about its behavior is known.</p>
<p>The Morrison Formation is a sequence of shallow marine and alluvial sediments which, according to <a href="/wiki/Radiometric_dating" title="Radiometric dating">radiometric dating</a>, ranges between 156.3 million years old (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mega-annum" title="Mega-annum">Ma</a>) at its base,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR06_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCR06-13">[13]</a></sup> to 146.8 million years old at the top,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAB98_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-SAB98-14">[14]</a></sup> which places it in the late <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxfordian_stage" title="Oxfordian stage">Oxfordian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kimmeridgian" title="Kimmeridgian">Kimmeridgian</a>, and early <a href="/wiki/Tithonian" title="Tithonian">Tithonian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faunal_stages" title="Faunal stages">stages</a> of the Late Jurassic period. This formation is interpreted as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semiarid" title="Semiarid">semiarid</a> environment with distinct <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">wet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">dry seasons</a>. The Morrison Basin where dinosaurs lived, stretched from New Mexico to Alberta and Saskatchewan, and was formed when the precursors to the <a href="/wiki/Front_Range" title="Front Range">Front Range</a> of the Rocky Mountains started pushing up to the west. The deposits from their east-facing <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basins</a> were carried by streams and <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a> and deposited in <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swampy</a> lowlands, lakes, river channels and <a href="/wiki/Floodplain" title="Floodplain">floodplains</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAR89_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-DAR89-15">[15]</a></sup> This formation is similar in age to the <a href="/wiki/Solnhofen_limestone" title="Solnhofen limestone">Solnhofen Limestone</a> Formation in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tendaguru" title="Tendaguru">Tendaguru Formation</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>. In 1877 this formation became the center of the <a href="/wiki/Bone_Wars" title="Bone Wars">Bone Wars</a>, a fossil-collecting rivalry between early paleontologists <a href="/wiki/Othniel_Charles_Marsh" title="Othniel Charles Marsh">Othniel Charles Marsh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Drinker_Cope" title="Edward Drinker Cope">Edward Drinker Cope</a>.</p>
<p>The Morrison Formation records an environment and time dominated by gigantic sauropod dinosaurs such as <i><a href="/wiki/Barosaurus" title="Barosaurus">Barosaurus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Brontosaurus" title="Brontosaurus">Brontosaurus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Camarasaurus" title="Camarasaurus">Camarasaurus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Diplodocus" title="Diplodocus">Diplodocus</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Brachiosaurus" title="Brachiosaurus">Brachiosaurus</a></i>. Dinosaurs that lived alongside <i>Saurophaganax</i>, and may have served as prey, included the herbivorous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ornithischians" title="Ornithischians">ornithischians</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Camptosaurus" title="Camptosaurus">Camptosaurus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Dryosaurus" title="Dryosaurus">Dryosaurus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Stegosaurus" title="Stegosaurus">Stegosaurus</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Othnielosaurus" title="Othnielosaurus">Othnielosaurus</a></i>. Predators in this paleoenvironment included the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theropods" title="Theropods">theropods</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Torvosaurus" title="Torvosaurus">Torvosaurus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ceratosaurus" title="Ceratosaurus">Ceratosaurus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Marshosaurus" title="Marshosaurus">Marshosaurus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Stokesosaurus" title="Stokesosaurus">Stokesosaurus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ornitholestes" title="Ornitholestes">Ornitholestes</a></i>, and<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Allosaurus" title="Allosaurus">Allosaurus</a></i>, which accounted for 70 to 75% of theropod specimens and was at the top <a href="/wiki/Trophic_level" title="Trophic level">trophic level</a> of the Morrison <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food web</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JRF03a_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-JRF03a-17">[17]</a></sup> Other vertebrates that shared this paleoenvironment included <a href="/wiki/Actinopterygii" title="Actinopterygii">ray-finned fishes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Eobatrachus" title="Eobatrachus">Eobatrachus</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Salamander" title="Salamander">salamanders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turtle" title="Turtle">turtles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sphenodontia" title="Sphenodontia">sphenodonts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a>, terrestrial and aquatic <a href="/wiki/Crocodylomorpha" title="Crocodylomorpha">crocodylomorphans</a> like <i><a href="/wiki/Goniopholis" title="Goniopholis">Goniopholis</a></i>, and several species of <a href="/wiki/Pterosaur" title="Pterosaur">pterosaur</a> like <i><a href="/wiki/Kepodactylus" title="Kepodactylus">Kepodactylus</a></i>. Early mammals were present in this region, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Fruitafossor" title="Fruitafossor">Fruitafossor</a></i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Docodonts" title="Docodonts">docodonts</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multituberculates" title="Multituberculates">multituberculates</a>, symmetrodonts, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triconodonts" title="Triconodonts">triconodonts</a>. The flora of the period has been revealed by fossils of <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyta" title="Chlorophyta">green algae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">mosses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Equisetum" title="Equisetum">horsetails</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cycad" title="Cycad">cycads</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ginkgo" title="Ginkgo">ginkgoes</a>, and several families of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer">conifers</a>. Vegetation varied from <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a>-lining forests of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_fern" title="Tree fern">tree ferns</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">ferns</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gallery_forest" title="Gallery forest">gallery forests</a>), to fern <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savannas</a> with occasional trees such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Araucaria" title="Araucaria">Araucaria</a></i>-like conifer <i><a href="/wiki/Brachyphyllum" title="Brachyphyllum">Brachyphyllum</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KC06_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-KC06-18">[18]</a></sup> In Oklahoma, Stovall unearthed a considerable number of <i><a href="/wiki/Apatosaurus" title="Apatosaurus">Apatosaurus</a></i> specimens, which may have represented possible prey for a large theropod like <i>Saurophaganax</i>.</p>
