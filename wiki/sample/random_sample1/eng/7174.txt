<p><i><b>Saurolophus</b></i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɔː/ 'au' in 'fraud'">ɔː</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>; meaning "lizard crest") is a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of large <a href="/wiki/Saurolophinae" title="Saurolophinae">saurolophine</a> <a href="/wiki/Hadrosaurid" title="Hadrosaurid">hadrosaurid</a> <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a> that lived about 70.0–68.5 million years ago,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABS09_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABS09-1">[1]</a></sup> in the <a href="/wiki/Late_Cretaceous" title="Late Cretaceous">Late Cretaceous</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>; it is one of the few genera of <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a> known from multiple continents. It is distinguished by a spike-like crest which projects up and back from the skull. <i>Saurolophus</i> was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbivorous" title="Herbivorous">herbivorous</a> dinosaur which could move about either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biped" title="Biped">bipedally</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quadruped" title="Quadruped">quadrupedally</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a>, <i>S. osborni</i>, was described by <a href="/wiki/Barnum_Brown" title="Barnum Brown">Barnum Brown</a> in 1912 from <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> fossils. A second valid species, <i>S. angustirostris</i>, is represented by numerous specimens from Mongolia, and was described by <a href="/wiki/Anatoly_Konstantinovich_Rozhdestvensky" title="Anatoly Konstantinovich Rozhdestvensky">Anatoly Konstantinovich Rozhdestvensky</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saurolophus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saurolophus_scalation.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="990" data-file-width="2103" height="104" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Saurolophus_scalation.png/220px-Saurolophus_scalation.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Saurolophus_scalation.png/330px-Saurolophus_scalation.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Saurolophus_scalation.png/440px-Saurolophus_scalation.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Saurolophus_scalation.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Restoration of <i>S. osborni</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Saurolophus</i> is known from material including nearly complete skeletons, giving researchers a clear picture of its bony anatomy. <i>S. osborni</i>, the rarer <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Albertan</a> species, was around 9.8 m (32 ft) long, with its skull 1.0 m (3.3 ft) long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LW42a_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-LW42a-2">[2]</a></sup> Its weight is estimated at 1.9 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> (2.1 tons).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DFG97_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-DFG97-3">[3]</a></sup> <i>S. angustirostris</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolian</a> species, was larger; the <a href="/wiki/Holotype" title="Holotype">type</a> skeleton is roughly 12 m (39 ft) long, and larger remains are reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DFG97_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-DFG97-3">[3]</a></sup> Aside from size, the two species are virtually identical, with differentiation hindered by lack of study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NS00_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NS00-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most distinctive feature of <i>Saurolophus</i> is its cranial crest, which is present in young individuals, but is smaller. It is long and spike-like and projects upward and backward at about a 45° angle, starting from over the eyes. This crest is often described as solid, but appears to be solid only at the point, with internal chambers that may have had a <a href="/wiki/Respiration_(physiology)" title="Respiration (physiology)">respiratory</a> and/or heat-regulation function.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MO81_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-MO81-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The holotype of <i>S. angustirostris</i> is a skull and postcrania, so the cranium of the species is well-described. Bell <i>et al.</i> re-evaluated the entire species in a 2011 publication with <i><a href="/wiki/Acta_Palaeontologica_Polonica" title="Acta Palaeontologica Polonica">Acta Palaeontologica Polonica</a></i>. Their description found the skull to be generalized among hadrosaurines, and are much larger than any skulls of <i>S. osborni</i>. The most unusual feature for a hadrosaurine is the long, protruding, solid crest that extends upwards diagonally from the back of the skull roof. Unlike lambeosaurines, the crests are made up completely of the <a href="/wiki/Nasal_bone" title="Nasal bone">nasal bone</a>. The premaxilla bones make up almost 50% of the entire skull length, and both sides are filled with small holes. Only in adult individuals has the front of the premaxillary contact been fused. Longer than the premaxilla, the nasal bones are the longest in the skull. They make up the entire length of the crest, and are never preserved as fused.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bell2011_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-bell2011-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_history">Discovery and history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saurolophus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery and history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saurolophus_excavation.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1751" data-file-width="2551" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Saurolophus_excavation.jpg/220px-Saurolophus_excavation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Saurolophus_excavation.jpg/330px-Saurolophus_excavation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Saurolophus_excavation.jpg/440px-Saurolophus_excavation.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Saurolophus_excavation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Photo from the excavation of <i>S. osborni</i> in 1911</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Barnum Brown recovered the first described remains of <i>Saurolophus</i> in 1911, including a nearly complete skeleton (<a href="/wiki/American_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="American Museum of Natural History">AMNH 5220</a>). Now on display in the American Museum of Natural History, this skeleton was the first nearly complete dinosaur skeleton from Canada. It was found in rocks of <a href="/wiki/Maastrichtian" title="Maastrichtian">early Maastrichtian</a> age, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_Cretaceous" title="Upper Cretaceous">Upper Cretaceous</a> <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe_Canyon_Formation" title="Horseshoe Canyon Formation">Horseshoe Canyon Formation</a> (then known as the Edmonton Formation) near Tolman Ferry on the <a href="/wiki/Red_Deer_River" title="Red Deer River">Red Deer River</a> in Alberta. Brown wasted little time in describing his material,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BB12_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-BB12-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BB13_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-BB13-8">[8]</a></sup> giving it its own subfamily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BB14_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-BB14-9">[9]</a></sup> <i>Saurolophus</i> was an important early reference for other hadrosaurs, as seen in the names of <i>Prosaurolophus</i> ("before <i>Saurolophus</i>") and <i><a href="/wiki/Parasaurolophus" title="Parasaurolophus">Parasaurolophus</a></i> ("near <i>Saurolophus</i>"). However, little additional material has been recovered and described.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saurolophus_skeleton.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="809" data-file-width="2874" height="62" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Saurolophus_skeleton.jpg/220px-Saurolophus_skeleton.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Saurolophus_skeleton.jpg/330px-Saurolophus_skeleton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Saurolophus_skeleton.jpg/440px-Saurolophus_skeleton.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Saurolophus_skeleton.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A photograph of the panel mount of the <a href="/wiki/Holotype" title="Holotype">holotype</a> of <i>S. osborni</i>, from Barnum Brown, 1913</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Instead, more abundant remains from Asia have provided more data. Initial remains were not promising; a partial fragmentary <a href="/wiki/Ischium" title="Ischium">ischium</a> from <a href="/wiki/Heilongjiang" title="Heilongjiang">Heilongjiang</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">China</a>, that Riabinin named <i>S. kryschtofovici</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ANR30_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ANR30-10">[10]</a></sup> Much better remains were soon recovered, though, but from <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>'s early Maastrichtian-age <a href="/wiki/Nemegt_Formation" title="Nemegt Formation">Nemegt Formation</a>. The 1946–1949 <a href="/wiki/Russians" title="Russians">Russian</a>-<a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongolian</a> paleontological expeditions recovered the large skeleton that became <i>S. angustirostris</i> as described by <a href="/wiki/Anatoly_Konstantinovich_Rozhdestvensky" title="Anatoly Konstantinovich Rozhdestvensky">Anatoly Rozhdestvensky</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AKR52_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-AKR52-11">[11]</a></sup> Other skeletons from a variety of growth stages have also been discovered, and <i>S. angustirostris</i> is now the most abundant Asian hadrosaurid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DFG97_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-DFG97-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1939–40, two partial skeletons were found in the late Maastrichtian age <a href="/wiki/Moreno_Formation" title="Moreno Formation">Moreno Formation</a> of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. These specimens were referred to cf. <i>Saurolophus</i> sp. In 2010, one of the skulls was instead assigned to <i><a href="/wiki/Edmontosaurus" title="Edmontosaurus">Edmontosaurus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BE10_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-BE10-12">[12]</a></sup> A 2013 placed the two specimens in a new species, <i>S. morrisi</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-P-RW11_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-P-RW11-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2014, the species was reassigned to a new genus, <i><a href="/wiki/Augustynolophus" title="Augustynolophus">Augustynolophus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Species">Species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saurolophus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saurolophus_angustirostris.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1023" data-file-width="2067" height="109" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Saurolophus_angustirostris.png/220px-Saurolophus_angustirostris.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Saurolophus_angustirostris.png/330px-Saurolophus_angustirostris.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Saurolophus_angustirostris.png/440px-Saurolophus_angustirostris.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Saurolophus_angustirostris.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Restoration of <i>S. angustirostris</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Two species are regarded as valid today: the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a> <i>S. osborni</i>, and <i>S. angustirostris</i>. <i>S. osborni</i> (Brown, 1912) is known from a skull and skeleton, two other complete skulls, and skull fragments. <i>S. angustirostris</i> (Rozhdestvensky, 1952) is known from at least 15 specimens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HWF04_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-HWF04-15">[15]</a></sup> It differs from <i>S. osborni</i> by some details of the skull, as well as in the pattern of scales found in skin impressions. The Mongolian species had a longer skull (by 20%) and the front of the snout (the <a href="/wiki/Premaxilla" title="Premaxilla">premaxillary</a> bones) were more upwardly directed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBinpress_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-PBinpress-16">[16]</a></sup> <i>S. angustirostris</i> also had a distinctive row of square scales along the back and tail, which was smooth in <i>S. osborni</i>. In <i>S. angustirostris</i>, the scales on the tail flank were arranged in vertical patterns, which may have corresponded to striped coloration in life. This area was covered in radial scale patterns in <i>S. osborni</i>, possibly indicating a more mottled or spotted coloration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bell2012_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-bell2012-17">[17]</a></sup> <i>S. kryschtofovici</i> (Riabinin, 1930) is not considered valid; either it is regarded as a <a href="/wiki/Nomen_dubium" title="Nomen dubium">dubious</a> name,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NS00_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-NS00-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HWF04_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-HWF04-15">[15]</a></sup> or as a synonym of <i>S. angustirostris</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DFG97_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-DFG97-3">[3]</a></sup> (although the name antedates <i>S. angustirostris</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-P-RW11_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-P-RW11-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Until a 2011 reevaluation of the species by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Phil_R._Bell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Phil R. Bell (page does not exist)">Phil R. Bell</a>, <i>S. angustoristris</i> was not well-described. No <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autapomorphies" title="Autapomorphies">autapomorphies</a>, unique derived traits, had been established distinguishing it from <i>S. osborni</i>. Bell found in a publication earlier in the year that the two previous studies of <i>S. angustirostris</i>, by Rozhdestvensky in 1952, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teresa_Maryanska" title="Teresa Maryanska">Maryanska</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halszka_Osmolska" title="Halszka Osmolska">Osmolska</a> in 1981, do not provide a comprehensive enough description to compare the species with <i>S. osborni</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bell2011_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-bell2011-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saurolophus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saurolophus_skull.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1927" data-file-width="4737" height="89" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Saurolophus_skull.jpg/220px-Saurolophus_skull.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Saurolophus_skull.jpg/330px-Saurolophus_skull.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Saurolophus_skull.jpg/440px-Saurolophus_skull.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<i>S. angustirostris</i> skin impressions</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Barnum Brown, who described the first specimens, put it in its own <a href="/wiki/Subfamily" title="Subfamily">subfamily</a> in "Trachodontidae" (=Hadrosauridae), the Saurolophinae. At the time, this also included <i><a href="/wiki/Corythosaurus" title="Corythosaurus">Corythosaurus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Hypacrosaurus" title="Hypacrosaurus">Hypacrosaurus</a></i>, the only well-known examples of what would become the <a href="/wiki/Lambeosaurinae" title="Lambeosaurinae">Lambeosaurinae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BB14_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-BB14-9">[9]</a></sup> Brown thought that <i>Saurolophus</i> had an expanded tip to the <a href="/wiki/Ischium" title="Ischium">ischium</a> bone in the hip, as dinosaurs now recognized as lambeosaurines had, but this appears to have been based on a mistakenly associated lambeosaurine ischium. Additionally, he misinterpreted the crests of <i>Saurolophus</i> and lambeosaurines as being made of the same bones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CMS54_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-CMS54-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most publications before 2010 classified <i>Saurolophus</i> as a member of Hadrosaurinae, often known colloquially as the "flat-headed hadrosaurs". In 2010, the subfamily Saurolophinae was brought back into use because <i><a href="/wiki/Hadrosaurus" title="Hadrosaurus">Hadrosaurus</a></i> appears to have branched off prior to the "hadrosaurine"–lambeosaurine split. As a result, Hadrosaurinae by definition cannot include the traditional "hadrosaurines". Saurolophinae is the oldest available name for the former "hadrosaurine" <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PM2010_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-PM2010-19">[19]</a></sup> <i>Saurolophus</i>, as the name suggests, is a saurolophine, as it has a saurolophine <a href="/wiki/Pelvis" title="Pelvis">pelvis</a> and a (largely) solid crest.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleobiology">Paleobiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saurolophus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Paleobiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saurolophus_debivort.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="922" data-file-width="1800" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Saurolophus_debivort.png/220px-Saurolophus_debivort.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Saurolophus_debivort.png/330px-Saurolophus_debivort.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Saurolophus_debivort.png/440px-Saurolophus_debivort.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Saurolophus_debivort.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Saurolophus</i> restoration</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As a <a href="/wiki/Hadrosaurid" title="Hadrosaurid">hadrosaurid</a>, <i>Saurolophus</i> would have been a <a href="/wiki/Bipedalism" title="Bipedalism">bipedal</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quadruped" title="Quadruped">quadrupedal</a> <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivore</a>, eating a variety of <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>. Its skull permitted a grinding motion analogous to <a href="/wiki/Mastication" title="Mastication">chewing</a>, and its <a href="/wiki/Tooth" title="Tooth">teeth</a> were continually replacing and packed into dental batteries that contained hundreds of teeth, only a relative handful of which were in use at any time. Plant material would have been cropped by its broad beak, and held in the jaws by a <a href="/wiki/Cheek" title="Cheek">cheek</a>-like organ. Its feeding range would have extended from the ground to about 4 m (13 ft) above.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HWF04_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-HWF04-15">[15]</a></sup> Common <i>S. angustirostris</i> would have been an important large herbivore in the Nemegt Formation, but <i>S. osborni</i> was rare in the Horseshoe Canyon Formation and faced competition from other duckbills (genus <i><a href="/wiki/Hypacrosaurus" title="Hypacrosaurus">Hypacrosaurus</a></i>). Comparisons between the <a href="/wiki/Sclerotic_ring" title="Sclerotic ring">scleral rings</a> of <i>Saurolophus</i> and modern birds and reptiles suggest that it may have been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathemeral" title="Cathemeral">cathemeral</a>, active throughout the day at short intervals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crest">Crest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saurolophus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Crest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crane_de_Saurolophus.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4500" data-file-width="6500" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Crane_de_Saurolophus.jpg/220px-Crane_de_Saurolophus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Crane_de_Saurolophus.jpg/330px-Crane_de_Saurolophus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Crane_de_Saurolophus.jpg/440px-Crane_de_Saurolophus.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Crane_de_Saurolophus.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Skull of <i>S. osborni</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The distinctive spike-like crest of <i>Saurolophus</i> has been interpreted in multiple ways, and could have had multiple functions. Brown compared it to the crest of a <a href="/wiki/Chameleon" title="Chameleon">chameleon</a>, and suggested it could provide an area for muscle attachment and a connection point for a nonbody back frill like that seen in the <a href="/wiki/Basiliscus_(genus)" title="Basiliscus (genus)">basilisk lizard</a>. <a href="/wiki/Peter_Dodson" title="Peter Dodson">Peter Dodson</a> interpreted similar features in other duckbills as having use in sexual identification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PD75_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-PD75-22">[22]</a></sup> Maryańska and Osmólska, noting the hollow base, suggested that the crest increased the surface area of the respiratory cavity, and helped in thermoregulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MO81_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-MO81-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/James_Hopson" title="James Hopson">James Hopson</a> supported a function as a visual signal, and further mentioned the possibility that the inflatable skin flaps over the nostrils could have acted as resonators and additional visual signals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JAH75_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-JAH75-23">[23]</a></sup> This idea has been picked up by authors of popular dinosaur works, such as <a href="/wiki/David_B._Norman" title="David B. Norman">David B. Norman</a>, who discussed hadrosaurid display at length and included a life restoration of such an adaptation in action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DBN85_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-DBN85-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The unique crest of <i>Saurolophus</i> is made up almost completely by the nasal bones, and in <i>S. angustirostris</i> it is solid. In adult specimens the crests are a rounded triangular shape in cross section. The crest protrudes past the edge of the skull backwards. Thin processes from the <a href="/wiki/Frontal_bone" title="Frontal bone">frontals</a> and prefrontals extend along the underside of the crest, probably to strengthen it. At the end of the crest is a swelling of the nasal, which is often termed differently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bell2011_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-bell2011-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ontogeny">Ontogeny</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saurolophus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ontogeny">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Though the growth rates of Saurolophus are poorly understood, a group of perinatal Saurolophus was recently discovered in an area of the Gobi Desert known as "The Dragon's Tomb".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The animals uncovered had skull lengths less than five percent of the length of the skulls of the adults, indicating they were in the earliest developmental stage at the time of their deaths The discovery of Saurolophus neonates also indicates the distinct crest found in adults was poorly developed in infancy. It remains unknown if the animals were still within their eggs or if they had hatched before they died. The specimens were described in the journal <a href="/wiki/PLOS_ONE" title="PLOS ONE">PLOS ONE</a> on October 14, 2015 by Leonard Dewaele et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleoecology">Paleoecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saurolophus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Paleoecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tarbosaurus_and_Saurolophus_by_durbed.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2550" data-file-width="3509" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Tarbosaurus_and_Saurolophus_by_durbed.jpg/220px-Tarbosaurus_and_Saurolophus_by_durbed.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Tarbosaurus_and_Saurolophus_by_durbed.jpg/330px-Tarbosaurus_and_Saurolophus_by_durbed.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Tarbosaurus_and_Saurolophus_by_durbed.jpg/440px-Tarbosaurus_and_Saurolophus_by_durbed.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tarbosaurus_and_Saurolophus_by_durbed.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Restoration of <i><a href="/wiki/Tarbosaurus" title="Tarbosaurus">Tarbosaurus</a></i> pursuing <i>S. angustirostris</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>S. osborni</i> is known only from the upper part (unit 4) of the Horseshoe Canyon Formation. It lived alongside other dinosaur species including the ornithopods <i>Hypacrosaurus altispinus</i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parksosaurus_warreni" title="Parksosaurus warreni">Parksosaurus warreni</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Ankylosauridae" title="Ankylosauridae">ankylosaurid</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anodontosaurus_lambei" title="Anodontosaurus lambei">Anodontosaurus lambei</a></i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pachycephalosaurid" title="Pachycephalosaurid">pachycephalosaurid</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sphaerotholus_edmontonense" title="Sphaerotholus edmontonense">Sphaerotholus edmontonense</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Ornithomimidae" title="Ornithomimidae">ornithomimids</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ornithomimus_brevitertius" title="Ornithomimus brevitertius">Ornithomimus brevitertius</a></i> and an unnamed species of <i><a href="/wiki/Struthiomimus" title="Struthiomimus">Struthiomimus</a></i>, small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theropod" title="Theropod">theropods</a> including <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atrociraptor_marshalli" title="Atrociraptor marshalli">Atrociraptor marshalli</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albertonykus_borealis" title="Albertonykus borealis">Albertonykus borealis</a></i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Tyrannosauridae" title="Tyrannosauridae">tyrannosauroid</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albertosaurus_sarcophagus" title="Albertosaurus sarcophagus">Albertosaurus sarcophagus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DBWetal04_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-DBWetal04-27">[27]</a></sup> The dinosaurs from this formation form part of the <a href="/wiki/Edmontonian" title="Edmontonian">Edmontonian</a> land vertebrate age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PD96_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-PD96-28">[28]</a></sup> The Horseshoe Canyon Formation is interpreted as having a significant <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">marine</a> influence, due to an encroaching <a href="/wiki/Western_Interior_Seaway" title="Western Interior Seaway">Western Interior Seaway</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epeiric_Sea" title="Epeiric Sea">shallow sea</a> that covered the midsection of North America through much of the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PD96_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-PD96-28">[28]</a></sup> <i>S. osborni</i> may have preferred to stay inland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HWF04_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-HWF04-15">[15]</a></sup> A 2001 study suggested that <i>Saurolophus osborni</i> was part of a distinct inland fauna characterized by an association between <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anchiceratops_ornatus" title="Anchiceratops ornatus">Anchiceratops ornatus</a></i> and it, while the contemporary coastal fauna was characterized by the association of <i>Pachyrhinosaurus canadensis</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Edmontosaurus_regalis" title="Edmontosaurus regalis">Edmontosaurus regalis</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pachy-province_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-pachy-province-29">[29]</a></sup> However, the association between <i>S. osborni</i> and <i>Anchiceratops</i> was later noted to be in error, <i>Anchiceratops</i> only occurs lower in the Horseshoe Canyon Formation, before the major transgression of the Western Interior Seaway represented by the Drumheller Marine Tongue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sullivanlucas2006_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-sullivanlucas2006-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>S. angustirostris</i> was one of the largest herbivores of the Nemegt Formation, which lacked large horned dinosaurs, but had <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sauropod" title="Sauropod">sauropods</a> and a more diverse theropod fauna. It coexisted with the rare hadrosaurid <i><a href="/wiki/Barsboldia" title="Barsboldia">Barsboldia</a></i>, flat-headed pachycephalosaurian <i><a href="/wiki/Homalocephale" title="Homalocephale">Homalocephale</a></i> and domed <i><a href="/wiki/Prenocephale" title="Prenocephale">Prenocephale</a></i>, the large ankylosaurid <i><a href="/wiki/Saichania" title="Saichania">Saichania</a></i>, rare <a href="/wiki/Saltasauridae" title="Saltasauridae">saltasaurid</a> sauropods <i><a href="/wiki/Nemegtosaurus" title="Nemegtosaurus">Nemegtosaurus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Opisthocoelicaudia" title="Opisthocoelicaudia">Opisthocoelicaudia</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Alvarezsauridae" title="Alvarezsauridae">alvarezsaurid</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Mononykus" title="Mononykus">Mononykus</a></i>, three types of troodontids including <i><a href="/wiki/Zanabazar_(dinosaur)" title="Zanabazar (dinosaur)">Zanabazar</a></i>, several <a href="/wiki/Oviraptorosauria" title="Oviraptorosauria">oviraptorosaurians</a> including <i><a href="/wiki/Rinchenia" title="Rinchenia">Rinchenia</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Nomingia" title="Nomingia">Nomingia</a></i>, the ostrich-mimics <i><a href="/wiki/Gallimimus" title="Gallimimus">Gallimimus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Deinocheirus" title="Deinocheirus">Deinocheirus</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Therizinosaur" title="Therizinosaur">therizinosaurid</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Therizinosaurus" title="Therizinosaurus">Therizinosaurus</a></i>, tyrannosaurid relative <i><a href="/wiki/Bagaraatan" title="Bagaraatan">Bagaraatan</a></i>, and the tyrannosaurid <i><a href="/wiki/Tarbosaurus" title="Tarbosaurus">Tarbosaurus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DBWetal04_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-DBWetal04-27">[27]</a></sup> Unlike other Mongolian formations like the well-known <a href="/wiki/Djadochta_Formation" title="Djadochta Formation">Djadochta Formation</a> that includes <i><a href="/wiki/Velociraptor" title="Velociraptor">Velociraptor</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Protoceratops" title="Protoceratops">Protoceratops</a></i>, the Nemegt is interpreted as being well-watered, like the <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur_Park_Formation" title="Dinosaur Park Formation">Dinosaur Park Formation</a> in Alberta.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PD96_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-PD96-28">[28]</a></sup> When examined, the rock <a href="/wiki/Facies" title="Facies">facies</a> of the Nemegt formation suggest the presence of stream and river channels, mudflats, and shallow lakes. Sediments also indicate that a rich habitat existed, offering diverse food in abundant amounts that could sustain Cretaceous dinosaurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
