<p><b>South Pasadena</b> is a <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">city</a> in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_County,_California" title="Los Angeles County, California">Los Angeles County</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. As of the 2010 census, it had a population of 25,619, up from 24,292 at the 2000 census. It is located in the West <a href="/wiki/San_Gabriel_Valley" title="San Gabriel Valley">San Gabriel Valley</a>. It is 3.42 square miles in area and lies between the much larger City of <a href="/wiki/Pasadena,_California" title="Pasadena, California">Pasadena</a>, of which it was once a part, and the metropolis of <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> South Pasadena is the oldest self-builder of floats in the historic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tournament_of_Roses_Parade" title="Tournament of Roses Parade">Tournament of Roses Parade</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pasadena,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The original inhabitants of South Pasadena and surrounding areas were members of the Native American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hahamog-na" title="Hahamog-na">Hahamog-na</a> tribe, a branch of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tongva_people" title="Tongva people">Tongva</a> Nation (part of the <a href="/wiki/Shoshone" title="Shoshone">Shoshone</a> language group) that occupied the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Basin" title="Los Angeles Basin">Los Angeles Basin</a>. The Tongva name for the area that covers modern day South Pasadena and much of Alhambra was Vaytsuung'xuilhoor pronounced /ʋaitsyŋ sʐuilχøɛr/. Tongva dwellings lined the <a href="/wiki/Arroyo_Seco_(Los_Angeles_County)" title="Arroyo Seco (Los Angeles County)">Arroyo Seco (Los Angeles County)</a> in South Pasadena and south to where it joins the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_River" title="Los Angeles River">Los Angeles River</a> and along other natural waterways in the city. They lived in thatched, dome-shape lodges characteristic for their use of carved wood decorations in the <a href="/wiki/Nahua_peoples" title="Nahua peoples">Nava</a> style. For food, they lived on a diet of corn meal, seeds and herbs, venison, berries, fruits and other small animals. They traded for ocean fish with the coastal Tongva on a daily basis. They made cooking vessels from steatite <a href="/wiki/Soapstone" title="Soapstone">soapstone</a> from <a href="/wiki/Santa_Catalina_Island,_California" title="Santa Catalina Island, California">Catalina Island</a>. South Pasadena also has a strong claim to having the oldest and most historic sites in the San Gabriel Valley. For many centuries, its adjacency to a natural fording place along the Arroyo Seco had served as a gateway to travel and commerce for aboriginal peoples here and along the coast. It was here that Hahamognas greeted Portola and the missionaries who later established the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Gabriel_Mission" title="San Gabriel Mission">San Gabriel Mission</a> a few miles to the east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The initial buildings on the <a href="/wiki/Rancho_San_Pascual" title="Rancho San Pascual">Rancho San Pascual</a> were built on the land which eventually became the cities of Pasadena, South Pasadena and Altadena. The first of these adobe structures became headquarters for General Flores and his staff in 1847 where they agreed to surrender to American forces, ending <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mexican_Colonial_rule&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mexican Colonial rule (page does not exist)">Mexican Colonial rule</a> in California. In 1875 the landowners of the area encompassing present-day <a href="/wiki/Pasadena,_California" title="Pasadena, California">Pasadena</a> and South Pasadena voted to rename their association, Pasadena.</p>
<p>South Pasadena's first mayor was Donald McIntyre Graham.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In February 1888, members of the southern portion of Pasadena attempted to gain more control over their own property and a vote for incorporation was made. In 1888, South Pasadena incorporated the southern portion of the Indiana Colony and land south and eastward to the Los Angeles border. Few Tongva had received any land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tong_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-tong-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> On 2 March 1888, the city of South Pasadena was incorporated with a population slightly over 500 residents, becoming the sixth municipality in Los Angeles County. It was chartered with roughly the same area as the current South Pasadena, about 3.42 square miles (8.91 square kilometers). With completion of the Pacific Electric Short Line, putting the entire city within easy walking distance of the “red car” stations, South Pasadena also became one of the first suburbs of Los Angeles.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>South Pasadena's history is associated with that of the <a href="/wiki/Cawston_Ostrich_Farm" title="Cawston Ostrich Farm">Cawston Ostrich Farm</a> and the Fair Oaks Pharmacy and Soda Fountain, which played vital roles in the history of the city.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_South_Pasadena">Modern South Pasadena</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pasadena,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modern South Pasadena">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>South Pasadena's streets are lined with numerous species of native California trees. These include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redwood" title="Redwood">redwood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sequoia_sempervirens" title="Sequoia sempervirens">sequoia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fraxinus" title="Fraxinus">ash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walnut" title="Walnut">walnut</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sycamore" title="Sycamore">sycamore</a>. Some non-native trees, such as <a href="/wiki/Liquidambar" title="Liquidambar">sweetgum</a>, are also seen. Because there are very few <a href="/wiki/Stucco" title="Stucco">stucco</a>-clad <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Colonial_Revival_Style_architecture" title="Spanish Colonial Revival Style architecture">Spanish Colonial</a> houses and virtually no palm trees in some parts of the city, South Pasadena is a popular stand-in for Midwestern and Northeastern towns in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motion_picture" title="Motion picture">motion picture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> productions. South Pasadena sits less than 10 miles (16 km) from <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Los_Angeles" title="Downtown Los Angeles">Downtown Los Angeles</a>; substantial numbers of residents work either in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bunker_Hill,_Los_Angeles,_California" title="Bunker Hill, Los Angeles, California">Bunker Hill</a> or as professors and staff at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">University of Southern California</a>.</p>
<p>"Mom and Pop" merchants populate the business district, and the Mission West area is a part of the original <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_66" title="U.S. Route 66">U.S. Route 66</a>. Of historical relevance is The Fair Oaks Pharmacy and Soda Fountain; also the <a href="/wiki/Rialto_Theatre_(South_Pasadena,_California)" title="Rialto Theatre (South Pasadena, California)">Rialto Theater</a> in downtown South Pasadena is a unique blend of Spanish Baroque and Egyptian stylings and was built in 1925. It is one of the last remaining single screen cinemas in the country. The Rialto was added to the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> in 1978, having narrowly missed being torn down that year. It went out of business on August 19, 2007 because of low profits. It has been featured in many films and commercials, most notably Robert Altman's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Player_(film)" title="The Player (film)">The Player</a></i> and more recently in <i><a href="/wiki/Scream_2" title="Scream 2">Scream 2</a></i>.</p>
<p>The Farmer's Market has become a tradition in the historic Mission-West District of South Pasadena on every Thursday from 4pm to 8pm. On the first Saturday of December every year, South Pasadena Booster Club hosts an annual 5K/10K run around South Pasadena known as the "Tiger Run", after the SPHS mascot. Racers from kindergarten to age 80 are invited to participate, including a wheelchair event. The 5K is run on flat sidewalks and roads around town, but the 10K (6.2 miles) includes some difficult hills. There is also a 300-meter children's run for kids 10 and under.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>South Pasadena can often be seen in motion picture productions with its beautiful tree-lined streets and "anywhere in America" feel. Such movies as <i><a href="/wiki/Freaky_Friday_(1976_film)" title="Freaky Friday (1976 film)">Freaky Friday</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Terminator" title="The Terminator">The Terminator</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Gone_with_the_Wind_(film)" title="Gone with the Wind (film)">Gone with the Wind</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Halloween_(1978_film)" title="Halloween (1978 film)">Halloween</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pee-Wee%27s_Big_Adventure" title="Pee-Wee's Big Adventure">Pee-Wee's Big Adventure</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/American_Pie_(film)" title="American Pie (film)">American Pie</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Girl_Next_Door_(2004_film)" title="The Girl Next Door (2004 film)">The Girl Next Door</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Legally_Blonde" title="Legally Blonde">Legally Blonde</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/13_Going_on_30" title="13 Going on 30">13 Going on 30</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Back_to_the_Future" title="Back to the Future">Back to the Future</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._Deeds" title="Mr. Deeds">Mr. Deeds</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bruce_Almighty" title="Bruce Almighty">Bruce Almighty</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Old_School_(film)" title="Old School (film)">Old School</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Ugly_Truth_(film)" title="The Ugly Truth (film)">The Ugly Truth</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/License_to_Wed" title="License to Wed">License to Wed</a></i> are just a few of the notable films shot on location in South Pasadena. Notable television series that have been filmed there include <i><a href="/wiki/Parenthood_(2010_TV_series)" title="Parenthood (2010 TV series)">Parenthood</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Family_(1976_TV_series)" title="Family (1976 TV series)">Family</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Boston_Public" title="Boston Public">Boston Public</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Nip/Tuck" title="Nip/Tuck">Nip/Tuck</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Desperate_Housewives" title="Desperate Housewives">Desperate Housewives</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold_Case_(TV_series)" title="Cold Case (TV series)">Cold Case</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bones_(TV_series)" title="Bones (TV series)">Bones</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Modern_Family" title="Modern Family">Modern Family</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/No_Ordinary_Family" title="No Ordinary Family">No Ordinary Family</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Big_Love" title="Big Love">Big Love</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/CSI:_Crime_Scene_Investigation" title="CSI: Crime Scene Investigation">CSI: Crime Scene Investigation</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interstate_710_extension_controversy">Interstate 710 extension controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pasadena,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Interstate 710 extension controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_710_(California)#The_South_Pasadena_Gap" title="Interstate 710 (California)">Interstate 710 (California): The South Pasadena Gap</a></div>
<p>South Pasadena, together with a broad coalition of national, state and local organizations, has opposed the 710 Freeway <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_Beach_Freeway" title="Long Beach Freeway">Long Beach Freeway</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_710_(California)" title="Interstate 710 (California)">I-710</a>) extension from Alhambra's Valley Blvd. to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foothill_Freeway" title="Foothill Freeway">Foothill Freeway</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_210_(California)" title="Interstate 210 (California)">I-210</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Pasadena,_California" title="Pasadena, California">Pasadena</a> at California Blvd. The proposed 1960's route would have sliced through the middle of the city, as well as through neighborhoods in El Sereno and Pasadena having an impact on nearly 1,000 homes in its path. However, this incompletion cuts off a north-south route from the San Gabriels Mountains in the north to Long Beach in the south, as well as connecting the 710 to the 110, 134, and 210 freeways.</p>
<p>On July 19, 1999, United States District Court Judge Pregerson issued a preliminary injunction prohibiting defendants Caltrans, et al., from proceeding with the 710 Freeway Project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The financial support for the fight against a major highway project through the city has come mostly from the residents themselves, who pay legal bills incurred by the city in the freeway fight from their general fund (no special taxes are used), making the fight an ongoing local election issue. South Pasadena has been cited five times on the <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Historic_Preservation" title="National Trust for Historic Preservation">National Trust for Historic Preservation's</a> list of "Most Endangered Places."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Litigation over the 710 extension has run for almost 5 decades. The City of South Pasadena has filed a federal lawsuit citing failure to protect clean air, the environment and historic properties, and until the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans) completes a comprehensive new environmental study, this will bring additional delays to the 40-year-old project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caltrans" title="Caltrans">Caltrans</a> (the California Department of Transportation) proposed a compromise route of boring a tunnel beneath the city. Having purchased hundreds of properties along the proposed right-of-way in the 1960s, Caltrans proposed selling these in order to partially finance the tunnel. The Southern California real estate boom of the early 2000s caused those properties in South Pasadena alone to appreciate to a combined value of over $300 million.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Carol_Liu" title="Carol Liu">State Senator Carol Liu</a> sponsored a bill to compel Caltrans to sell the properties no longer needed for the project. SB-416 also prohibits funds from the sale of surplus properties in the SR 10 corridor from being used to advance or construct any proposed North State Route 710 tunnel. <a href="/wiki/Chris_Holden" title="Chris Holden">State Assemblyman Chris Holden</a> co-sponsored the bill and remarked after it was signed into law, "…the surface route of the 710 Freeway is not going to happen and everyone knows that and so these properties should then be put back into the community".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Caltrans, however, maintains that the freeway extension/connections are needed for the overall benefit of the Southern California public and continues to fight for its completion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pasadena,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>South Pasadena is located at the western end of the <a href="/wiki/San_Gabriel_Valley" title="San Gabriel Valley">San Gabriel Valley</a>, north of the <a href="/wiki/San_Rafael_Hills" title="San Rafael Hills">San Rafael Hills</a>, east of the <a href="/wiki/Arroyo_Seco_(Los_Angeles_County)" title="Arroyo Seco (Los Angeles County)">Arroyo Seco</a> seasonal river, and south of the separate city of <a href="/wiki/Pasadena,_California" title="Pasadena, California">Pasadena, California</a>. Adjacent cities are Los Angeles to the west and south, Pasadena to the north, <a href="/wiki/San_Marino,_California" title="San Marino, California">San Marino</a> to the east, and <a href="/wiki/Alhambra,_California" title="Alhambra, California">Alhambra</a> to the southeast. According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the city's total area of 3.42 square miles (8.9 km<sup>2</sup>), is virtually all land.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010">2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pasadena,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2010">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 United States Census</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> reported that South Pasadena had a population of 25,619. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 7,496.4 people per square mile (2,894.4/km²). The racial makeup of South Pasadena was 13,922 (54.3%) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a> (43.6% Non-Hispanic White),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quif_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-quif-7">[7]</a></sup> 771 (3.0%) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 107 (0.4%) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 7,973 (31.1%) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 9 (0.0%) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 1,422 (5.6%) from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 1,415 (5.5%) from two or more races. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 4,767 persons (18.6%).</p>
<p>The Census reported that 25,456 people (99.4% of the population) lived in households, 8 (0%) lived in non-institutionalized group quarters, and 155 (0.6%) were institutionalized.</p>
<p>There were 10,467 households, out of which 3,621 (34.6%) had children under the age of 18 living in them, 4,904 (46.9%) were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">opposite-sex married couples</a> living together, 1,264 (12.1%) had a female householder with no husband present, 451 (4.3%) had a male householder with no wife present. There were 501 (4.8%) <a href="/wiki/POSSLQ" title="POSSLQ">unmarried opposite-sex partnerships</a>, and 107 (1.0%) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Same-sex_partnerships" title="Same-sex partnerships">same-sex married couples or partnerships</a>. 3,073 households (29.4%) were made up of individuals and 802 (7.7%) had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.43. There were 6,619 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Family_(U.S._Census)" title="Family (U.S. Census)">families</a> (63.2% of all households); the average family size was 3.06.</p>
<p>The population was spread out with 5,998 people (23.4%) under the age of 18, 1,576 people (6.2%) aged 18 to 24, 7,431 people (29.0%) aged 25 to 44, 7,510 people (29.3%) aged 45 to 64, and 3,104 people (12.1%) who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 40.1 years. For every 100 females there were 90.3 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 85.5 males.</p>
<p>There were 11,118 housing units at an average density of 3,253.2 per square mile (1,256.1/km²), of which 4,787 (45.7%) were owner-occupied, and 5,680 (54.3%) were occupied by renters. The homeowner vacancy rate was 1.1%; the rental vacancy rate was 6.1%. 13,185 people (51.5% of the population) lived in owner-occupied housing units and 12,271 people (47.9%) lived in rental housing units.</p>
<p>According to the 2010 United States Census, South Pasadena had a median household income of $85,058, with 6.7% of the population living below the federal poverty line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quif_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-quif-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parks_and_recreation">Parks and recreation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pasadena,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Parks and recreation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Arroyo_Seco_(Los_Angeles_County)" title="Arroyo Seco (Los Angeles County)">Arroyo Seco</a> (canyon, stream, and cultural landscape) offers a diverse range of experiences for walkers and more experienced hikers.</p>
<p>The Arroyo Seco stream begins at Red Box near Mount Wilson in the <a href="/wiki/San_Gabriel_Mountains" title="San Gabriel Mountains">San Gabriel Mountains</a> and proceeds through steep mountain canyons for eleven miles (18 km) until it enters the urban plain of Southern California at the <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">Jet Propulsion Laboratory</a>. The stream, largely channelized south of Devil's Gate Dam, proceeds in the Arroyo Seco canyon for eleven miles (18 km) more: through Pasadena, South Pasadena and Northeast Los Angeles to the confluence with the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_River" title="Los Angeles River">Los Angeles River</a> near Elysian Park, Chinatown and downtown Los Angeles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pasadena,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/California_State_Legislature" title="California State Legislature">California State Legislature</a>, South Pasadena is in <a href="/wiki/California%27s_25th_State_Senate_district" title="California's 25th State Senate district">the 25th Senate District</a>, represented by <a href="/wiki/California_Democratic_Party" title="California Democratic Party">Democrat</a> <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Portantino" title="Anthony Portantino">Anthony Portantino</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/California%27s_41st_State_Assembly_district" title="California's 41st State Assembly district">the 41st Assembly District</a>, represented by <a href="/wiki/California_Democratic_Party" title="California Democratic Party">Democrat</a> <a href="/wiki/Chris_Holden" title="Chris Holden">Chris Holden</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a>, South Pasadena is in <a href="/wiki/California%27s_27th_congressional_district" title="California's 27th congressional district">California's 27th congressional district</a>, represented by <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democrat</a> <a href="/wiki/Judy_Chu" title="Judy Chu">Judy Chu</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/South_Pasadena_Unified_School_District" title="South Pasadena Unified School District">South Pasadena Unified School District</a>(SPUSD) includes five schools: three elementary schools (Monterey Hills, Marengo and Arroyo Vista), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Pasadena_Middle_School" title="South Pasadena Middle School">South Pasadena Middle School</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Pasadena_High_School" title="South Pasadena High School">South Pasadena High School</a>. Former elementary schools now closed or renamed are Lincoln (now Arroyo Vista), El Centro (now the school district headquarters), Las Flores (limited grades, near <a class="external text" href="http://www.laokay.com/halac/FloresAdobe.htm" rel="nofollow">Flores Adobe</a>, historic landmark), and Oneonta (later a private Montessori school).</p>
<p>South Pasadena and the neighboring city of <a href="/wiki/San_Marino,_California" title="San Marino, California">San Marino</a> have had a long-standing rivalry. Until the 1955, the two cities shared the same high school, which was adjacent to the South Pasadena Public Library. Every year, the schools' football teams compete for a victor's plaque. As of 2014, South Pasadena team had won 28 and San Marino 29. There have been three ties. Many SPHS team have won <a class="external text" href="http://www.cifstate.org/" rel="nofollow">CIF</a> titles over the years.</p>
<p>SPEF (<a class="external text" href="http://www.spef4kids.com/" rel="nofollow">South Pasadena Educational Foundation</a>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/501(c)" title="501(c)">501(c)</a>(3) charity designated by the SPUSD as the official private fund-raising organization for the support of the district's educational programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pasadena,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fair_Oaks_Avenue_(Pasadena)" title="Fair Oaks Avenue (Pasadena)">Fair Oaks Avenue</a>, Huntington Drive, and Mission Street are the main thoroughfares through South Pasadena.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pasadena_Freeway" title="Pasadena Freeway">Pasadena Freeway</a> has two exits in South Pasadena--<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Orange_Grove_Avenue&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Orange Grove Avenue (page does not exist)">Orange Grove Avenue</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fair_Oaks_Avenue_(Pasadena)" title="Fair Oaks Avenue (Pasadena)">Fair Oaks Avenue</a>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LACMTA" title="LACMTA">LACMTA</a> operates six bus lines (79, 176, 256, 258, 260, 762) through South Pasadena. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Pasadena_(Los_Angeles_Metro_station)" title="South Pasadena (Los Angeles Metro station)">South Pasadena Station</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Gold_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)" title="Gold Line (Los Angeles Metro)">Metro Gold Line</a> is in the heart of South Pasadena, located at the corner of Mission and Meridian.</p>
<p>South Pasadena operates its own public transportation system. Since 2003, South Pasadena has been operating the City of South Pasadena Community Transit to connect with the Mission Gold Line Station, whose schedule is linked to the Gold Line schedule. The system was originally called "South Pasadena Gold Link."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Additionally South Pasadena has a transit shuttle that operates around the city. As of 2007, many outdated traffic signals have been replaced throughout South Pasadena.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_County_Department_of_Health_Services" title="Los Angeles County Department of Health Services">Los Angeles County Department of Health Services</a> operates the Monrovia Health Center in <a href="/wiki/Monrovia,_California" title="Monrovia, California">Monrovia</a>, serving South Pasadena.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
