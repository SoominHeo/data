<p><i><b>Son of Saul</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a>: <span lang="hu"><i><b>Saul fia</b></i></span>) is a 2015 Hungarian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drama_film" title="Drama film">drama film</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A1szl%C3%B3_Nemes" title="László Nemes">László Nemes</a> and co-written by Nemes and <a href="/wiki/Clara_Royer" title="Clara Royer">Clara Royer</a>. It is set in the <a href="/wiki/Auschwitz_concentration_camp" title="Auschwitz concentration camp">Auschwitz concentration camp</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, and follows a day-and-a-half in the life of Saul Ausländer (played by <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9za_R%C3%B6hrig" title="Géza Röhrig">Géza Röhrig</a>), a Hungarian member of the <a href="/wiki/Sonderkommando" title="Sonderkommando">Sonderkommando</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film premiered at the <a href="/wiki/2015_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2015 Cannes Film Festival">2015 Cannes Film Festival</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2015Cannes_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-2015Cannes-5">[5]</a></sup> where it won the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Prix_(Cannes_Film_Festival)" title="Grand Prix (Cannes Film Festival)">Grand Prix</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barnes_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-barnes-6">[6]</a></sup> It was also shown in the Special Presentations section of the <a href="/wiki/2015_Toronto_International_Film_Festival" title="2015 Toronto International Film Festival">2015 Toronto International Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScreenDaily_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ScreenDaily-7">[7]</a></sup> The film won the award for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Foreign_Language_Film" title="Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film">Best Foreign Language Film</a> at the <a href="/wiki/88th_Academy_Awards" title="88th Academy Awards">88th Academy Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88nom_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-88nom-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WinnerHWR_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-WinnerHWR-9">[9]</a></sup> It is the ninth Hungarian film to be nominated for the award, and the first one since <a href="/wiki/Istv%C3%A1n_Szab%C3%B3" title="István Szabó">István Szabó</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Hanussen_(1988_film)" title="Hanussen (1988 film)">Hanussen</a></i> in 1988. It is the second Hungarian film to win the award, the first one being Szabó's <i><a href="/wiki/Mephisto_(1981_film)" title="Mephisto (1981 film)">Mephisto</a></i> in 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It also won the <a href="/wiki/73rd_Golden_Globe_Awards" title="73rd Golden Globe Awards">Golden Globe</a> for <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Foreign_Language_Film" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Foreign Language Film">Best Foreign Language Film</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> becoming the first Hungarian film to win the award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Son_of_Saul&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In October 1944, Saul Ausländer (<a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9za_R%C3%B6hrig" title="Géza Röhrig">Géza Röhrig</a>), a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hungarian-Jewish" title="Hungarian-Jewish">Jewish–Hungarian</a> prisoner in the Nazi extermination camp <a href="/wiki/Auschwitz_concentration_camp" title="Auschwitz concentration camp">Auschwitz</a>, works as a <i><a href="/wiki/Sonderkommando" title="Sonderkommando">Sonderkommando</a></i> member. Amongst other duties, he must salvage valuables from the clothing of the dead, drag their bodies from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_chambers" title="Gas chambers">gas chambers</a> and scrub the floors immediately afterwards. He performs these daily tasks with a stoic, impassive expression, seemingly having been numbed by the daily horrors. One day, following the gassing, he comes upon the body of a boy whom he recognizes. The boy is somehow still breathing after having been gassed. From a distance, Saul witnesses a Nazi physician methodically suffocating the boy to ensure his death and ordering for an autopsy. Saul impulsively comes forth and insists on carrying the body himself to the Hungarian prison doctor, Miklós (<a href="/wiki/S%C3%A1ndor_Zs%C3%B3t%C3%A9r" title="Sándor Zsótér">Sándor Zsótér</a>). He is able to convince Miklós to not perform the autopsy as he intends to give the boy a proper Jewish burial, for which he needs a rabbi. He goes to Rabbi Frankel (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jerzy_Walczak&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jerzy Walczak (page does not exist)">Jerzy Walczak</a>) who refuses and suggests that Saul perform the burial himself.</p>
<p>Another member of the <i>Sonderkommando</i>, Abraham (<a href="/wiki/Levente_Moln%C3%A1r" title="Levente Molnár">Levente Molnár</a>), talks about a rebellion against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS" title="SS">SS</a>-guards with <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oberkapo" title="Oberkapo">Oberkapo</a></i> Biedermann (<a href="/wiki/Urs_Rechn" title="Urs Rechn">Urs Rechn</a>). While Abraham advocates an uprising, Biedermann prefers a plan to photograph the camp's atrocities and to smuggle the pictures outside to attract attention and help. Saul asks for another rabbi to help him bury the body and Abraham tells him of the Renegade who is Greek and supposedly a former rabbi. Saul in return offers his assistance in their plan and is instructed to go with a prisoner (Katz) to repair a shack and is given a piece of jewellery for use as a bribe in case he's caught. When Saul and Katz arrive at the shack, Saul pretends to fix the front door's lock, while Katz takes out a camera from inside the shack and starts to take pictures of the cremation. Saul hears the guards and hides the camera outside in a drain. The guards search the shack, only to find nothing.</p>
<p>Saul then sneaks onto a truck for another <i>Sonderkommando</i> unit, heading to a nearby riverbank, where the ashes from the crematoria are dumped into the river. Saul finds the Renegade, but the Renegade refuses to help him. Saul then threatens to alert the <i>Oberkapo</i> of the unit, Mietek (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kamil_Dobrowolski&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kamil Dobrowolski (page does not exist)">Kamil Dobrowolski</a>), that the Renegade is a rabbi by reciting the <i><a href="/wiki/Kiddush" title="Kiddush">Kiddush</a></i>. When the Renegade refuses yet again Saul throws the man's shovel into the water. The rabbi jumps into the river in a half-hearted attempt to retrieve the shovel or drown himself. Saul manages to bring the Renegade back to the riverbank and both are then taken to the SS-commandant of the unit (<a href="/wiki/Christian_Harting" title="Christian Harting">Christian Harting</a>). After an interrogation, the Renegade is executed and Saul is allowed to go back to the unit.</p>
<p>Saul is then confronted by Mietek, who realizes that he is from another unit. To mollify Mietek, Saul gives him the piece of jewellery. Back at the camp, following roll call, Saul heads to Miklós' office but is caught by a group of Nazi physicians. One of the physicians pushes Saul around like a puppet and makes a mockery of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_dances" title="Jewish dances">Jewish dances</a>, finally forcing him out of the room. After searching in vain for the boy's body, Saul confronts Miklós, who assures him that he has hidden it from the other doctors for safety. Saul sneaks into the autopsy room and takes the body back to his own barrack in a sack.</p>
<p>That night Saul is summoned to clean the dinner-tables by SS-commandant Voss (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Uwe_Lauer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Uwe Lauer (page does not exist)">Uwe Lauer</a>). Biedermann walks in and is ordered to write up a list of seventy names. This leads Biedermann to believe that his unit will soon be gassed. Biedermann discloses the information to Abraham, who instructs Saul to head to the women's camp, where he will pick up a smuggled package of gunpowder from a prisoner named Ella. After collecting the package, Saul deliberately falls into a line of newly arrived Hungarian Jews, who are being led into the woods for execution. Saul again looks for a rabbi among the arrivals. A Frenchman named Braun (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Todd_Charmont&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Todd Charmont (page does not exist)">Todd Charmont</a>) approaches him and convinces Saul that he is a rabbi. Saul disguises Braun as a member of the <i>Sonderkommando</i> and sneaks him into the camp. When Saul arrives at the camp he is confronted by Abraham and realizes that during the turmoil in the woods he has lost the package. On further questioning, he reveals that the boy is his illegitimate son.</p>
<p>The next morning during roll call, Miklós finds Saul and tells him that he needs a replacement body, similar to the one Saul has taken. The prisoners are then summoned into the crematorium to get back to work, where they discover that Biedermann has been killed by the SS. Abraham starts a riot with the other prisoners and they attack the SS-guards, starting the rebellion. After managing to escape from the crematorium, Saul retrieves his son's body and escapes to the woods with Braun and few other prisoners. When they reach a river, Saul finds a chance to bury the body, only to discover that Braun is a fraud when he can't recite the <i><a href="/wiki/Kaddish" title="Kaddish">Kaddish</a></i>. When he hears the guards approaching, Saul tries to carry the body across the river. Unable to manage the current with the added weight, he loses his grasp on the sack and is pulled out of the river by Rabbi Frankel as the body floats away. When the prisoners arrive at a shed in the forest, they start to discuss a plan to join the <a href="/wiki/Home_Army" title="Home Army">Polish resistance</a>. Saul notices a young peasant boy peeking into the shed and smiles at him. As the boy runs away, guards run past in the direction of the shed. Gunfire echoes as the camera follows the boy into the woods.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Son_of_Saul&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Son_of_Saul&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nemes conceived of the film from the book <i>The Scrolls of Auschwitz</i>, a collection of testimonies by Sonderkommando members,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicholas_Chare_and_Dominic_Williams_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nicholas_Chare_and_Dominic_Williams-13">[13]</a></sup> after discovering it during the production of <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_Tarr" title="Béla Tarr">Béla Tarr</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Man_from_London" title="The Man from London">The Man from London</a></i> in 2005 when he was working as Tarr's assistant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kilday_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-kilday-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-presskit_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-presskit-14">[14]</a></sup> Nemes started working on the screenplay with Royer in 2010 and completed the first draft in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kilday_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-kilday-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chan_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-chan-15">[15]</a></sup> The writers spent several years on research, while historians such as <a href="/wiki/Gideon_Greif" title="Gideon Greif">Gideon Greif</a>, Philippe Mesnard and Zoltán Vági provided support for the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-presskit_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-presskit-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chan_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-chan-15">[15]</a></sup> Prof. Gideon Greif's book <i>We Wept without Tears</i> inspired director <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A1szl%C3%B3_Nemes" title="László Nemes">László Nemes</a> to create a film dedicated to the Sonderkommando, the most delicate, complicated and sensitive topic within the Holocaust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The project struggled to find its financiers due to the film's unconventional approach and Nemes's lack of experience in directing a feature film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kilday_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-kilday-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-macnab_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-macnab-17">[17]</a></sup> Originally intended to be a French production with a French protagonist, the film was produced entirely in Hungary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kilday_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-kilday-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-macnab_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-macnab-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> After potential co-production partners in France, Israel, Germany and Austria turned down the project, the €1.5 million budget was ultimately covered by the Hungarian National Film Fund, Hungarian tax credits and the <a href="/wiki/Claims_Conference" title="Claims Conference">Claims Conference</a>, accounting respectively for 70%, 25% and 5%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kilday_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-kilday-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-macnab_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-macnab-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Casting">Casting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Son_of_Saul&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Casting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nemes insisted on casting actors who spoke their characters' own languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-macnab_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-macnab-17">[17]</a></sup> New York City-based Hungarian poet <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9za_R%C3%B6hrig" title="Géza Röhrig">Géza Röhrig</a>, who had not acted in film since the 1980s, was cast as the main character, Saul, after being considered originally for a supporting role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kilday_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-kilday-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-macnab_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-macnab-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Son_of_Saul&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film was shot on <a href="/wiki/35_mm_film" title="35 mm film">35 mm film</a> in 28 days in <a href="/wiki/Budafok" title="Budafok">Budafok</a>, <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kilday_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-kilday-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-presskit_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-presskit-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> A 40 mm lens and the <a href="/wiki/Academy_ratio" title="Academy ratio">Academy aspect ratio of 1.375:1</a> were adopted to realise shallow focus and a portrait-like narrow field of vision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-egan_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-egan-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Architect and liberal activist <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A1szl%C3%B3_Rajk_Jr." title="László Rajk Jr.">László Rajk</a>, who also worked on the permanent Hungarian exhibition at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auschwitz-Birkenau_State_Museum" title="Auschwitz-Birkenau State Museum">Auschwitz-Birkenau State Museum</a>, designed the re-creation of the crematoria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kilday_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-kilday-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-production">Post-production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Son_of_Saul&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Post-production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film took five months of sound design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-egan_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-egan-21">[21]</a></sup> Human voices in eight languages were recorded and attached to the original recording of the production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kilday_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-kilday-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-egan_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-egan-21">[21]</a></sup> Sound designer Tamás Zányi described the sound in the film "as a sort of acoustic counterpoint to the intentionally narrowed imagery".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kilday_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-kilday-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Son_of_Saul&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film premiered in competition at the <a href="/wiki/2015_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2015 Cannes Film Festival">68th Cannes Film Festival</a> on 15 May 2015,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2015Cannes_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-2015Cannes-5">[5]</a></sup> where it won the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Prix_(Cannes_Film_Festival)" title="Grand Prix (Cannes Film Festival)">Grand Prix</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barnes_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-barnes-6">[6]</a></sup> The filmmakers initially tried to premiere the film at the <a href="/wiki/65th_Berlin_International_Film_Festival" title="65th Berlin International Film Festival">65th Berlin International Film Festival</a>, but after the festival offered them a spot only in the Panorama section, not in the main competition, they decided to refuse the proposal and instead aim for the Cannes competition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-macnab_17-5"><a href="#cite_note-macnab-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Hungary, the film was released on 11 June 2015 and sold more than 142,000 tickets, placing it as the highest-grossing domestic film released since the slapstick comedy <i>Üvegtigris 3</i> in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Son_of_Saul&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Upon its release at Cannes, the film was met with critical acclaim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> On the review aggregator website <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, the film holds a 96% approval rating based on reviews from 197 critics, with an average rating of 8.9 out of 10. The site's critical consensus reads, "Grimly intense yet thoroughly rewarding, <i>Son of Saul</i> offers an unforgettable viewing experience – and establishes director László Nemes as a talent to watch".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, the film has received a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weighted_average" title="Weighted average">weighted average</a> score of 89 out of 100, based on 39 critics, indicating "universal acclaim".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his review for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Bradshaw" title="Peter Bradshaw">Peter Bradshaw</a> rated the film five out of five stars, calling it an "astonishing debut film" and "a horror movie of extraordinary focus and courage". He ended his review writing: "Nemes's film has found a way to create a fictional drama with a gaunt, fierce kind of courage...."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Writing for <i><a href="/wiki/Time_Out_(magazine)" title="Time Out (magazine)">Time Out</a></i>, Dave Calhoun also gave the film five out of five stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indiewire" title="Indiewire">Indiewire</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Eric Kohn awarded the film an A- rating, calling it "a remarkable refashioning of the Holocaust drama that reignites the setting with extraordinary immediacy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In his review written for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i>, Boyd van Hoeij praised the cinematography and the soundwork of the film. He writes: "Shot (and shown in Cannes) on 35mm, often in sickly greens and yellows and with deep shadows, Erdely's cinematography is one of the film's major assets, but it wouldn't be half as effective without the soundwork, which plays a major role in suggesting what is happening around Saul, with audiences often forced to rely on the sound to imagine the whole, horrible picture".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Writing for <i>The Film Stage</i>, Giovanni Marchini Camia gave the film an A rating, and called it "a towering landmark for filmic fictionalizations of the Holocaust".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> A.A. Dowd of <i><a href="/wiki/The_A.V._Club" title="The A.V. Club">The A.V. Club</a></i> gave the film an A- rating, and praised the movie's unique perspective: "<i>Son of Saul</i> is the rare Holocaust drama that finds actual drama, and not just despair, in the living hell of a concentration camp". [...] "<i>Son of Saul</i> sees humanity in effort, identity in action; it watches someone with nothing, a man reduced to a statistic, get a piece of himself back, mostly by finding some meaning in a place of meaningless evil".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Claude_Lanzmann" title="Claude Lanzmann">Claude Lanzmann</a>, director of the documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Shoah_(film)" title="Shoah (film)">Shoah</a></i>, gave the film high praise,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> stating that "it's a very new film, very original, very unusual. It's a film that gives a very real sense of what it was like to be in the Sonderkommando. It's not at all melodramatic. It's done with a very great modesty".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-34">[34]</a></sup> Philosopher <a href="/wiki/Georges_Didi-Huberman" title="Georges Didi-Huberman">Georges Didi-Huberman</a> also praised the film, and he wrote a 25-page open letter to Nemes, which opened with "Your film, ‘Son of Saul,' is a monster. A necessary, coherent, beneficial, innocent monster".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nemes was questioned about the similarities between <i>Son of Saul</i> and the 2001 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Grey_Zone" title="The Grey Zone">The Grey Zone</a></i>, with Nemes replying "But mine is an Anti-<i>Grey Zone</i>!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greyzone_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-greyzone-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Son_of_Saul&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the Cannes Film Festival the film won the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Prix_(Cannes_Film_Festival)" title="Grand Prix (Cannes Film Festival)">Grand Prix</a> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Federation_of_Film_Critics" title="International Federation of Film Critics">FIPRESCI Prize</a> in the main competition section.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto1_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-37">[37]</a></sup> The film also won the <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Chalais_Prize" title="François Chalais Prize">François Chalais Prize</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_Award" title="Vulcan Award">Vulcan Award</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto2_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto2-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto3_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto3-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto4_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto4-40">[40]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/88th_Academy_Awards" title="88th Academy Awards">88th Academy Awards</a>, <i>Son of Saul</i> won the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Foreign_Language_Film" title="Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film">Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WinnerHWR_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-WinnerHWR-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
