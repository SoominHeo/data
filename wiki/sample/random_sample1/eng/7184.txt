<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Private property</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Private_property_(disambiguation)" title="Private property (disambiguation)">Private property (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Private property</b> is a legal designation for the ownership of <a href="/wiki/Property" title="Property">property</a> by non-governmental <a href="/wiki/Legal_personality" title="Legal personality">legal entities</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Private property is distinguishable from <a href="/wiki/Public_property" title="Public property">public property</a>, which is owned by a state entity; and from <a href="/wiki/Collective_ownership" title="Collective ownership">collective</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Cooperative" title="Cooperative">cooperative</a>) property, which is owned by a group of <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">non-governmental entities</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Private property is further distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Personal_property" title="Personal property">personal property</a>, which refers to property for personal use and consumption. Private property is a legal concept defined and enforced by a country's political system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Private_property&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PrivatePropertySign.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="4928" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/PrivatePropertySign.jpg/220px-PrivatePropertySign.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/PrivatePropertySign.jpg/330px-PrivatePropertySign.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/PrivatePropertySign.jpg/440px-PrivatePropertySign.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PrivatePropertySign.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Gate with a private property sign.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Prior to the 18th century, English-speakers generally used the word "property" in reference to <a href="/wiki/Landed_property" title="Landed property">land ownership</a>. In England, "property" did not have a legal definition until the 17th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Private property as commercial property was invented with the great European trading companies of the 17th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The issue of the <a href="/wiki/Enclosure" title="Enclosure">enclosure</a> of agricultural land in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, especially as debated in the 17th and 18th centuries, accompanied efforts in philosophy and political thought—by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes" title="Thomas Hobbes">Thomas Hobbes</a> (1588–1679), <a href="/wiki/James_Harrington_(author)" title="James Harrington (author)">James Harrington</a> (1611–1677), and <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a> (1632–1704), for example—to address the phenomenon of property <a href="/wiki/Ownership" title="Ownership">ownership</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>John Locke, in arguing against supporters of absolute monarchy, conceptualized property as a "natural right" that God had not bestowed exclusively on the monarchy. Influenced by the rise of <a href="/wiki/Mercantilism" title="Mercantilism">mercantilism</a>, Locke argued that private property was antecedent to, and thus independent of, government. Locke distinguished between "common property", by which he meant open-access property; and property in consumer goods and producer-goods, the latter of which referred to land. His chief argument for property in land was improved land management and cultivation over common (open-access) land. Locke developed a normative theory of property rights based on labor, which stated that property is a natural result of labor improving upon nature; and thus by virtue of labor expenditure, the laborer becomes entitled to its produce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Property_Rights_in_the_History_of_Economic_Thought:_From_Locke_to_J.S._Mill_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Property_Rights_in_the_History_of_Economic_Thought:_From_Locke_to_J.S._Mill-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 18th century during the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, the moral philosopher and economist <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a> (1723–1790), in contrast to Locke, drew a distinction between the "right to property" as an acquired right and natural rights. Smith confined natural rights to "liberty and life". Smith also drew attention to the relationship between employee and employer and identified that property and civil government were dependent upon each other, recognizing that "the state of property must always vary with the form of government". Smith further argued that civil government could not exist without property, as its main function was to safeguard property ownership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Property_Rights_in_the_History_of_Economic_Thought:_From_Locke_to_J.S._Mill_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Property_Rights_in_the_History_of_Economic_Thought:_From_Locke_to_J.S._Mill-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 19th century the economist and philosopher <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> (1818–1883) provided an influential analysis on the history and development of private property. Drawing a distinction between pre-capitalist property formations and property in the context of capitalism, Marx's definition of private property referred to ownership of productive (value-producing) assets—termed the "means of production"—by a small class of owners who appropriate, and thus live off of, the <a href="/wiki/Surplus_value" title="Surplus value">value</a> produced by mental and material labor operating the means of production. In a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capitalist" title="Capitalist">capitalist</a> economy, the surplus value takes the form of <a href="/wiki/Profit_(economics)" title="Profit (economics)">profit</a>, enabling a small class to subsist on the resulting <a href="/wiki/Property_income" title="Property income">property income</a> in an exploitative relationship with non-property owners who produce society's wealth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Tied to his analysis of <a href="/wiki/Capital_accumulation" title="Capital accumulation">capital accumulation</a> and crisis, Marx believed that private property in the means of production would ultimately be replaced by <a href="/wiki/Social_ownership" title="Social ownership">social ownership</a>. When Marx called for the abolition of private property, he was not referring to "personal property" such as clothing and furniture that was not used to produce the "social wealth," but to productive property that requires collective effort to operate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This conception of private property has proven influential for many subsequent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist" title="Socialist">socialist</a> political movements and economic theories, and led to the widespread association of "private property" with capitalism.</p>

<p>Although contemporary <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_economics" title="Neoclassical economics">neoclassical economics</a>, currently the dominant school of economics, rejects some of the assumptions of early philosophers that underpinned classical economics, it has been argued that neoclassical economics continues to be influenced by the legacy of natural moral theory and the concept of "natural rights", which has led to the presentation of private market exchange and private property rights as "natural rights" inherent in nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Economic_liberalism" title="Economic liberalism">Economic liberals</a> (defined as those who support a private sector-driven market economy) consider private property to be essential for the construction of a prosperous society. They believe private ownership of land ensures the land will be put to productive use and its <a href="/wiki/Value_(economics)" title="Value (economics)">value</a> protected by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landowner" title="Landowner">landowner</a>. If the owners must pay <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Property_taxes" title="Property taxes">property taxes</a>, this forces the owners to maintain a productive output from the land to keep taxes current. Private property also attaches a monetary value to land, which can be used to <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">trade</a> or as <a href="/wiki/Collateral_(finance)" title="Collateral (finance)">collateral</a>. Private property thus is an important part of capitalization within the <a href="/wiki/Economic_system" title="Economic system">economy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist" title="Socialist">Socialist</a> economists are critical of private property as socialism aims to substitute private property in the means of production for <a href="/wiki/Social_ownership" title="Social ownership">social ownership</a> or <a href="/wiki/Public_property" title="Public property">public property</a>. Socialists generally argue that private property relations limit the potential of the <a href="/wiki/Productive_forces" title="Productive forces">productive forces</a> in the economy when productive activity becomes a collective activity, where the role of the capitalist becomes redundant (as a passive owner). Socialists generally favor social ownership either to eliminate the class distinctions between owners and workers, and as a component of the development of a post-capitalist economic system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In response to the socialist critique, the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_School" title="Austrian School">Austrian School</a> economist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ludwig_Von_Mises" title="Ludwig Von Mises">Ludwig Von Mises</a> argued that private property rights are a requisite for what he called "rational" economic calculation and that the prices of goods and services cannot be determined accurately enough to make efficient economic calculation without having clearly defined private-property rights. Mises argued that a socialist system, which by definition would lack private property in the factors of production, would be unable to determine appropriate price valuations for the factors of production. According to Mises, this problem would make rational socialist calculation impossible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Marxian_economics" title="Marxian economics">Marxian economics</a> and socialist politics, there is distinction between "private property" and "<a href="/wiki/Personal_property" title="Personal property">personal property</a>". The former is defined as the means of production in reference to private ownership over an economic enterprise based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialization_(economics)" title="Socialization (economics)">socialized production</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wage_labor" title="Wage labor">wage labor</a>; the latter is defined as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consumer_goods" title="Consumer goods">consumer goods</a> or goods produced by an individual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Prior to the 18th century, private property usually referred to <a href="/wiki/Landed_property" title="Landed property">land ownership</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Private_property&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Social_ownership" title="Social ownership">Social ownership</a></div>
<p>Private property in the means of production is criticized by socialists. The socialist critique of private ownership is heavily influenced by the Marxian analysis of capitalist property forms as part of its broader critique of <a href="/wiki/Marx%27s_theory_of_alienation" title="Marx's theory of alienation">alienation</a> and exploitation in capitalism. Although there is considerable disagreement among socialists about the validity of certain aspects of Marxian analysis, the majority of socialists are sympathetic to Marx's views on exploitation and alienation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Socialists critique the private appropriation of property income on the grounds that, because such income does not correspond to a return on any productive activity and is generated by the working class, it represents exploitation. The property-owning (capitalist) class lives off passive property income produced by the working population by virtue of their claim to ownership in the form of stock, bonds or private equity. This exploitative arrangement is perpetuated due to the structure of capitalist society. From this perspective, capitalism is regarded as class system akin to historical class systems like slavery and feudalism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Private ownership has also been criticized on non-Marxist ethical grounds by advocates of <a href="/wiki/Market_socialism" title="Market socialism">market socialism</a>. According to the economist James Yunker, the ethical case for <a href="/wiki/Market_socialism" title="Market socialism">market socialism</a> is as follows: because passive property income requires no mental or physical exertion on the part of the recipient, and because its appropriation by a small group of private owners is the source of the vast inequalities in contemporary capitalism, social ownership in a market economy would resolve the major cause of social inequality and its accompanying social ills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
