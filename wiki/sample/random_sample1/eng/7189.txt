<p><b>Saimaa</b> is a lake in southeastern <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>. At approximately 4,400 square kilometres (1,700 sq mi), it is the largest <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a> in Finland, and the <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_lakes_of_Europe" title="List of largest lakes of Europe">fourth largest natural freshwater lake in Europe</a>. It was formed by <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glacial</a> melting at the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_Age" title="Ice Age">Ice Age</a>. Major <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">towns</a> on the lakeshore include <a href="/wiki/Lappeenranta" title="Lappeenranta">Lappeenranta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Imatra" title="Imatra">Imatra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Savonlinna" title="Savonlinna">Savonlinna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mikkeli" title="Mikkeli">Mikkeli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Varkaus" title="Varkaus">Varkaus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Joensuu" title="Joensuu">Joensuu</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Vuoksi_River" title="Vuoksi River">Vuoksi River</a> flows from Saimaa to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ladoga" title="Lake Ladoga">Lake Ladoga</a>. Most of the lake is spotted with islands, and narrow canals divide the lake in many parts, each having their own names (major basins include <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Suur-Saimaa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Suur-Saimaa (page does not exist)">Suur-Saimaa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Orivesi" title="Lake Orivesi">Orivesi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puruvesi" title="Puruvesi">Puruvesi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haukivesi" title="Haukivesi">Haukivesi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Y%C3%B6vesi" title="Yövesi">Yövesi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pihlajavesi_(Saimaa)" title="Pihlajavesi (Saimaa)">Pihlajavesi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Pyh%C3%A4selk%C3%A4" title="Lake Pyhäselkä">Pyhäselkä</a>). Thus, Saimaa exhibits all major types of lake in Finland at different levels of eutropification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>In places in the Saimaa basin (an area larger than the lake), "there is more shoreline here per unit of area than anywhere else in the world, the total length being nearly 15,000 kilometres (9,300 mi). The number of islands in the region, 14,000, also shows what a maze of detail the system is."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VF_Hamalainen_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-VF_Hamalainen-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Saimaa_Canal" title="Saimaa Canal">Saimaa Canal</a> from <a href="/wiki/Lappeenranta" title="Lappeenranta">Lappeenranta</a> to <a href="/wiki/Vyborg" title="Vyborg">Vyborg</a> connects Saimaa to the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Finland" title="Gulf of Finland">Gulf of Finland</a>. Other <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canals</a> connect Saimaa to smaller lakes in Eastern Finland and form a network of <a href="/wiki/Waterway" title="Waterway">waterways</a>. These waterways are mainly used to transport <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wood_pulp" title="Wood pulp">pulp</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Cargo" title="Cargo">cargo</a>, but also <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourists</a> use the waterways.</p>
<p>An endangered <a href="/wiki/Freshwater_seal" title="Freshwater seal">freshwater seal</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saimaa_Ringed_Seal" title="Saimaa Ringed Seal">Saimaa Ringed Seal</a>, lives only at Saimaa. Another of the lake's endangered species is the Saimaa <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-F.26S_salmon_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-F.26S_salmon-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>About 6000 year ago, ancient Lake Saimaa, estimated to cover nearly 9000 km2 at the time, was abruptly discharged through a new outlet. The event created thousands of square kilometres of new residual wetlands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Following this event, the region saw a population maximum in the decades following only to later return to an ecological development towards old boreal conifer forests which saw a decline in population.</p>
<p>Due to its rich, easily accessible asbestos deposits, the shores of the lake are the most probable origin of <a href="/wiki/Asbestos-ceramic" title="Asbestos-ceramic">asbestos-ceramic</a>, a type of pottery made between c. 1900 BC – 200 AD.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saimaa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Russian writer <a href="/wiki/Maxim_Gorky" title="Maxim Gorky">Maxim Gorky</a> went into exile near the shores of Lake Saimaa for a period of time after his apartment was raided by the <a href="/wiki/Black_Hundreds" title="Black Hundreds">Black Hundreds</a> in the aftermath of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moscow_Uprising_of_1905" title="Moscow Uprising of 1905">Moscow Uprising of 1905</a>. He wrote to his divorced wife Ekaterina, writing "it's beautiful here, like a fairy tale".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
