<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Cyberpunk</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

William Gibson's <a href="/wiki/Sprawl_trilogy" title="Sprawl trilogy"><i>Sprawl</i> trilogy</a> novels (published 1984–1988) are early examples of cyberpunk fiction.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Cyberpunk</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subgenre" title="Subgenre">subgenre</a> of <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Future" title="Future">future</a> setting that tends to focus on society as "<a href="/wiki/High_tech" title="High tech">high tech</a> low life"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> featuring advanced technological and scientific achievements, such as <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">information technology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cybernetics" title="Cybernetics">cybernetics</a>, juxtaposed with a degree of breakdown or radical change in the <a href="/wiki/Social_order" title="Social order">social order</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cyberpunk plots often center on conflict among <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligences</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hackers" title="Hackers">hackers</a>, and among <a href="/wiki/Megacorporation" title="Megacorporation">megacorporations</a>, and tend to be set in a near-future <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, rather than in the far-future settings or galactic vistas found in novels such as <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">Isaac Asimov</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Foundation_(Isaac_Asimov_novel)" title="Foundation (Isaac Asimov novel)">Foundation</a></i> or <a href="/wiki/Frank_Herbert" title="Frank Herbert">Frank Herbert</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Dune_(novel)" title="Dune (novel)">Dune</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham-3">[3]</a></sup> The settings are usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-industrial" title="Post-industrial">post-industrial</a> <a href="/wiki/Dystopia" title="Dystopia">dystopias</a> but tend to feature extraordinary cultural ferment and the use of technology in ways never anticipated by its original inventors ("the street finds its own uses for things").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gibson.2C_William_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gibson.2C_William-4">[4]</a></sup> Much of the genre's atmosphere echoes <a href="/wiki/Film_noir" title="Film noir">film noir</a>, and written works in the genre often use techniques from <a href="/wiki/Detective_fiction" title="Detective fiction">detective fiction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Classic cyberpunk characters were marginalized, alienated loners who lived on the edge of society in generally dystopic futures where daily life was impacted by rapid technological change, an ubiquitous datasphere of computerized information, and invasive modification of the human body.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lawrence_Person" title="Lawrence Person">Lawrence Person</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Person_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Person-6">[6]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style_and_ethos">Style and ethos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyberpunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Style and ethos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Primary exponents of the cyberpunk field include <a href="/wiki/William_Gibson" title="William Gibson">William Gibson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neal_Stephenson" title="Neal Stephenson">Neal Stephenson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Sterling" title="Bruce Sterling">Bruce Sterling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Bethke" title="Bruce Bethke">Bruce Bethke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pat_Cadigan" title="Pat Cadigan">Pat Cadigan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rudy_Rucker" title="Rudy Rucker">Rudy Rucker</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Shirley" title="John Shirley">John Shirley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philip_K._Dick" title="Philip K. Dick">Philip K. Dick</a> (author of <i><a href="/wiki/Do_Androids_Dream_of_Electric_Sheep%3F" title="Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?">Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?</a></i>, from which the film <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a></i> was adapted).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Blade Runner</i> can be seen as a quintessential example of the cyberpunk style and theme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Graham-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">Video games</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Board_games" title="Board games">board games</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tabletop_role-playing_game" title="Tabletop role-playing game">tabletop role-playing games</a>, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Cyberpunk_2020" title="Cyberpunk 2020">Cyberpunk 2020</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Shadowrun" title="Shadowrun">Shadowrun</a></i>, often feature storylines that are heavily influenced by cyberpunk writing and movies. Beginning in the early 1990s, some trends in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyberpunk_fashion" title="Cyberpunk fashion">fashion</a> and music were also labeled as cyberpunk. Cyberpunk is also featured prominently in <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CWCcyber_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-CWCcyber-8">[8]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Akira_(manga)" title="Akira (manga)">Akira</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gunnm" title="Gunnm">Gunnm</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ghost_in_the_Shell_(1995_film)" title="Ghost in the Shell (1995 film)">Ghost in the Shell</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Serial_Experiments_Lain" title="Serial Experiments Lain">Serial Experiments Lain</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dennou_Coil" title="Dennou Coil">Dennou Coil</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ergo_Proxy" title="Ergo Proxy">Ergo Proxy</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psycho_Pass" title="Psycho Pass">Psycho Pass</a></i> being among the most notable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CWCcyber_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-CWCcyber-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Setting">Setting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyberpunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Setting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Shibuya_Night_(HDR).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="683" data-file-width="1024" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Shibuya_Night_%28HDR%29.jpg/220px-Shibuya_Night_%28HDR%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Shibuya_Night_%28HDR%29.jpg/330px-Shibuya_Night_%28HDR%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Shibuya_Night_%28HDR%29.jpg/440px-Shibuya_Night_%28HDR%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Shibuya_Night_(HDR).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shibuya,_Tokyo" title="Shibuya, Tokyo">Shibuya, Tokyo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Of Japan's influence on the genre, William Gibson said, "modern Japan simply was cyberpunk."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cyberpunk1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-cyberpunk1-11">[11]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Cyberpunk writers tend to use elements from <a href="/wiki/Hardboiled" title="Hardboiled">hardboiled</a> <a href="/wiki/Detective_fiction" title="Detective fiction">detective fiction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Film_noir" title="Film noir">film noir</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">postmodernist</a> prose to describe the often <a href="/wiki/Nihilism" title="Nihilism">nihilistic</a> underground side of an electronic society. The genre's vision of a <a href="/wiki/Dystopia" title="Dystopia">troubled future</a> is often called the antithesis of the generally <a href="/wiki/Utopia" title="Utopia">utopian</a> visions of the future popular in the 1940s and 1950s. Gibson defined cyberpunk's antipathy towards utopian SF in his 1981 short story "<a href="/wiki/The_Gernsback_Continuum" title="The Gernsback Continuum">The Gernsback Continuum</a>," which pokes fun at and, to a certain extent, condemns utopian science fiction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In some cyberpunk writing, much of the action takes place <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Online" title="Online">online</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Cyberspace" title="Cyberspace">cyberspace</a>, blurring the border between actual and <a href="/wiki/Virtual_reality" title="Virtual reality">virtual reality</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> A typical <a href="/wiki/Trope_(literature)" title="Trope (literature)">trope</a> in such work is a direct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brain-computer_interface" title="Brain-computer interface">connection</a> between the human brain and computer systems. Cyberpunk settings are dystopias with corruption, computers and Internet connectivity. Giant, <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational corporations</a> have for the most part replaced governments as centers of political, economic, and even military power.</p>
<p>The economic and technological state of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, in the 80s influenced Cyberpunk literature at the time. Of Japan's influence on the genre, William Gibson said, "Modern Japan simply was cyberpunk."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cyberpunk1_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-cyberpunk1-11">[11]</a></sup> Cyberpunk is often set in urbanized, artificial landscapes, and "city lights, receding" was used by Gibson as one of the genre's first <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphors</a> for cyberspace and virtual reality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The cityscapes of <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ags_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ags-17">[17]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> have had major influences in the urban backgrounds, ambiance and settings in many cyberpunk works such as <a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shadowrun" title="Shadowrun">Shadowrun</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ridley_Scott" title="Ridley Scott">Ridley Scott</a> envisioned the landscape of cyberpunk <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> in Blade Runner to be "Hong Kong on a very bad day".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The streetscapes of <a href="/wiki/Ghost_in_the_Shell" title="Ghost in the Shell">Ghost in the Shell</a> were based on Hong Kong. <a href="/wiki/Mamoru_Oshii" title="Mamoru Oshii">Mamoru Oshii</a> felt that Hong Kong's strange and chaotic streets where old and new exist in confusing relationships, fit the theme of the film well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ags_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-ags-17">[17]</a></sup> Hong Kong's <a href="/wiki/Kowloon_Walled_City" title="Kowloon Walled City">Kowloon Walled City</a> is particularly notable for its disorganized hyper-urbanization and breakdown in traditional urban planning to be an inspiration to cyberpunk landscapes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protagonists">Protagonists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyberpunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Protagonists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the cyberpunk genre's prototype characters is Case, from Gibson's <i><a href="/wiki/Neuromancer" title="Neuromancer">Neuromancer</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Case is a "console cowboy," a brilliant hacker who has betrayed his organized criminal partners. Robbed of his talent through a crippling injury inflicted by the vengeful partners, Case unexpectedly receives a once-in-a-lifetime opportunity to be healed by expert medical care but only if he participates in another criminal enterprise with a new crew.</p>
<p>Like Case, many cyberpunk protagonists are manipulated, placed in situations where they have little or no choice, and although they might see things through, they do not necessarily come out any further ahead than they previously were. These <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-hero" title="Anti-hero">anti-heroes</a>—"criminals, outcasts, visionaries, dissenters and misfits"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>—call to mind the private eye of detective fiction. This emphasis on the misfits and the malcontents is the "<a href="/wiki/Punk_subculture" title="Punk subculture">punk</a>" component of cyberpunk.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Society_and_government">Society and government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyberpunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Society and government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cyberpunk can be intended to disquiet readers and call them to action. It often expresses a sense of rebellion, suggesting that one could describe it as a type of culture revolution in science fiction. In the words of author and critic <a href="/wiki/David_Brin" title="David Brin">David Brin</a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>...a closer look [at cyberpunk authors] reveals that they nearly always portray future societies in which governments have become wimpy and pathetic ...Popular science fiction tales by Gibson, Williams, Cadigan and others <i>do</i> depict <a href="/wiki/Orwellian" title="Orwellian">Orwellian</a> accumulations of power in the next century, but nearly always clutched in the secretive hands of a wealthy or corporate <a href="/wiki/Elite" title="Elite">elite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Cyberpunk stories have also been seen as fictional forecasts of the evolution of the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>. The earliest descriptions of a global communications network came long before the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> entered popular awareness, though not before traditional science-fiction writers such as <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">Arthur C. Clarke</a> and some social commentators such as <a href="/wiki/James_Burke_(science_historian)" title="James Burke (science historian)">James Burke</a> began predicting that such networks would eventually form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyberpunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_cyberpunk_works#Print_media" title="List of cyberpunk works">List of cyberpunk works § Print media</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cyborgs_in_fiction" title="Cyborgs in fiction">Cyborgs in fiction</a></div>
<p>The science-fiction editor <a href="/wiki/Gardner_Dozois" title="Gardner Dozois">Gardner Dozois</a> is generally acknowledged as the person who popularized the use of the term "cyberpunk" as a kind of <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a> <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2015)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup>, although Minnesota writer <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Bethke" title="Bruce Bethke">Bruce Bethke</a> coined the term in 1980 for his short story "Cyberpunk," which was published in the November 1983 issue of <i><a href="/wiki/Amazing_Stories" title="Amazing Stories">Amazing Science Fiction Stories</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The term was quickly appropriated as a label to be applied to the works of <a href="/wiki/William_Gibson" title="William Gibson">William Gibson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Sterling" title="Bruce Sterling">Bruce Sterling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pat_Cadigan" title="Pat Cadigan">Pat Cadigan</a> and others. Of these, Sterling became the movement's chief ideologue, thanks to his <a href="/wiki/Fanzine" title="Fanzine">fanzine</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Cheap_Truth" title="Cheap Truth">Cheap Truth</a>.</i> John Shirley wrote articles on Sterling and Rucker's significance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Brunner_(novelist)" title="John Brunner (novelist)">John Brunner</a>'s 1975 novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shockwave_Rider" title="The Shockwave Rider">The Shockwave Rider</a></i> is considered by many<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2015)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> to be the first cyberpunk novel with many of the <a href="/wiki/Trope_(literature)" title="Trope (literature)">tropes</a> commonly associated with the genre, some five years before the term was popularized by Dozois.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/William_Gibson" title="William Gibson">William Gibson</a> with his novel <i><a href="/wiki/Neuromancer" title="Neuromancer">Neuromancer</a></i> (1984) is likely<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2015)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> the most famous writer connected with the term cyberpunk. He emphasized style, a fascination with surfaces, and atmosphere over traditional science-fiction tropes. Regarded as ground-breaking and sometimes as "the archetypal cyberpunk work,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Person_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Person-6">[6]</a></sup> <i>Neuromancer</i> was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Award" title="Hugo Award">Hugo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nebula_Award" title="Nebula Award">Nebula</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Philip_K._Dick" title="Philip K. Dick">Philip K. Dick</a> Awards. <i><a href="/wiki/Count_Zero" title="Count Zero">Count Zero</a></i> (1986) and <i><a href="/wiki/Mona_Lisa_Overdrive" title="Mona Lisa Overdrive">Mona Lisa Overdrive</a></i> (1988) followed after Gibson's popular debut novel. According to the <a href="/wiki/Jargon_File" title="Jargon File">Jargon File</a>, "Gibson's near-total ignorance of computers and the present-day hacker culture enabled him to speculate about the role of computers and hackers in the future in ways hackers have since found both irritatingly naïve and tremendously stimulating."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early on, cyberpunk was hailed as a radical departure from science-fiction standards and a new manifestation of vitality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brians_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brians-28">[28]</a></sup> Shortly thereafter, however, some critics arose to challenge its status as a revolutionary movement. These critics said that the SF <a href="/wiki/New_Wave_science_fiction" title="New Wave science fiction">New Wave</a> of the 1960s was much more innovative as far as narrative techniques and styles were concerned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Furthermore, while <i>Neuromancer</i>'s narrator may have had an unusual "voice" for science fiction, much older examples can be found: Gibson's narrative voice, for example, resembles that of an updated <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Chandler" title="Raymond Chandler">Raymond Chandler</a>, as in his novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Sleep" title="The Big Sleep">The Big Sleep</a></i> (1939).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brians_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brians-28">[28]</a></sup> Others noted that almost all traits claimed to be uniquely cyberpunk could in fact be found in older writers' works—often citing <a href="/wiki/J._G._Ballard" title="J. G. Ballard">J. G. Ballard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philip_K._Dick" title="Philip K. Dick">Philip K. Dick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harlan_Ellison" title="Harlan Ellison">Harlan Ellison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stanis%C5%82aw_Lem" title="Stanisław Lem">Stanisław Lem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_R._Delany" title="Samuel R. Delany">Samuel R. Delany</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/William_S._Burroughs" title="William S. Burroughs">William S. Burroughs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brians_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-Brians-28">[28]</a></sup> For example, Philip K. Dick's works contain recurring themes of social decay, artificial intelligence, paranoia, and blurred lines between objective and subjective realities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The influential cyberpunk movie <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a></i> (1982) is based on his book, <i><a href="/wiki/Do_Androids_Dream_of_Electric_Sheep%3F" title="Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?">Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Humans linked to machines are found in Pohl and Kornbluth's <i><a href="/wiki/Wolfbane_(novel)" title="Wolfbane (novel)">Wolfbane</a></i> (1959) and <a href="/wiki/Roger_Zelazny" title="Roger Zelazny">Roger Zelazny</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Creatures_of_Light_and_Darkness" title="Creatures of Light and Darkness">Creatures of Light and Darkness</a></i> (1968).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1994, scholar Brian Stonehill suggested that <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Pynchon" title="Thomas Pynchon">Thomas Pynchon</a>'s 1973 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Gravity%27s_Rainbow" title="Gravity's Rainbow">Gravity's Rainbow</a></i> "not only curses but precurses what we now glibly dub cyberspace."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Other important<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2015)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> predecessors include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfred_Bester_(author)" title="Alfred Bester (author)">Alfred Bester</a>'s two most celebrated novels, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Demolished_Man" title="The Demolished Man">The Demolished Man</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Stars_My_Destination" title="The Stars My Destination">The Stars My Destination</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> as well as <a href="/wiki/Vernor_Vinge" title="Vernor Vinge">Vernor Vinge</a>'s novella <i><a href="/wiki/True_Names" title="True Names">True Names</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception_and_impact">Reception and impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyberpunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reception and impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Science-fiction writer <a href="/wiki/David_Brin" title="David Brin">David Brin</a> describes cyberpunk as "the finest free promotion campaign ever waged on behalf of science fiction." It may not have attracted the "real punks," but it did ensnare many new readers, and it provided the sort of movement that postmodern literary critics found alluring. Cyberpunk made science fiction more attractive to academics, argues Brin; in addition, it made science fiction more profitable to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hollywood,_Los_Angeles,_California" title="Hollywood, Los Angeles, California">Hollywood</a> and to the visual arts generally. Although the "self-important rhetoric and whines of persecution" on the part of cyberpunk fans were irritating at worst and humorous at best, Brin declares that the "rebels did shake things up. We owe them a debt."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fredric_Jameson" title="Fredric Jameson">Fredric Jameson</a> considers cyberpunk the "supreme literary expression if not of <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">postmodernism</a>, then of <a href="/wiki/Late_capitalism" title="Late capitalism">late capitalism</a> itself".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cyberpunk further inspired many professional writers who were not among the "original" cyberpunks to incorporate cyberpunk ideas into their own works,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> such as <a href="/wiki/George_Alec_Effinger" title="George Alec Effinger">George Alec Effinger</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/When_Gravity_Fails" title="When Gravity Fails">When Gravity Fails</a></i>. <i><a href="/wiki/Wired_(magazine)" title="Wired (magazine)">Wired</a></i> magazine, created by Louis Rossetto and Jane Metcalfe, mixes new technology, art, literature, and current topics in order to interest today's cyberpunk fans, which Paula Yoo claims "proves that hardcore hackers, multimedia junkies, cyberpunks and cellular freaks are poised to take over the world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_and_television">Film and television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyberpunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Film and television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_cyberpunk_works#Films" title="List of cyberpunk works">List of cyberpunk works § Films</a>, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_cyberpunk_works#Television_and_Web_Series" title="List of cyberpunk works">List of cyberpunk works § Television and Web Series</a></div>
<p>The film <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a></i> (1982)—adapted from Philip K. Dick's <i><a href="/wiki/Do_Androids_Dream_of_Electric_Sheep%3F" title="Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?">Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?</a></i>—is set in 2019 in a dystopian future in which manufactured beings called <a href="/wiki/Replicant" title="Replicant">replicants</a> are slaves used on space colonies and are legal prey on Earth to various bounty hunters who "retire" (kill) them. Although <i>Blade Runner</i> was largely unsuccessful in its first theatrical release, it found a viewership in the home video market and became a <a href="/wiki/Cult_film" title="Cult film">cult film</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Since the movie omits the religious and mythical elements of Dick's original novel (e.g. empathy boxes and Wilbur Mercer), it falls more strictly within the cyberpunk genre than the novel does. William Gibson would later reveal that upon first viewing the film, he was surprised at how the look of this film matched his vision when he was working on <i><a href="/wiki/Neuromancer" title="Neuromancer">Neuromancer</a></i>. The film's tone has since been the staple of many cyberpunk movies, such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Matrix" title="The Matrix">The Matrix</a></i> (1999), which uses a wide variety of cyberpunk elements.</p>
<p>The number of films in the genre or at least using a few genre elements has grown steadily since <i>Blade Runner</i>. Several of Philip K. Dick's works have been adapted to the silver screen. The films <i><a href="/wiki/Johnny_Mnemonic_(film)" title="Johnny Mnemonic (film)">Johnny Mnemonic</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/New_Rose_Hotel_(film)" title="New Rose Hotel (film)">New Rose Hotel</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> both based upon short stories by William Gibson, flopped commercially and critically.</p>
<p>In addition, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tech-noir" title="Tech-noir">tech-noir</a>" film as a hybrid genre, means a work of combining <a href="/wiki/Neo-noir" title="Neo-noir">neo-noir</a> and science fiction or cyberpunk. It includes many cyberpunk films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Burst_City" title="Burst City">Burst City</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burst_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-burst-42">[42]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Terminator" title="The Terminator">The Terminator</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/RoboCop_(franchise)" title="RoboCop (franchise)">Robocop</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/12_Monkeys_(film)" title="12 Monkeys (film)">12 Monkeys</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lawnmower_Man_(film)" title="The Lawnmower Man (film)">The Lawnmower Man</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hackers_(film)" title="Hackers (film)">Hackers</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hardware_(film)" title="Hardware (film)">Hardware</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Strange_Days_(film)" title="Strange Days (film)">Strange Days</a>.</i></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anime_and_manga">Anime and manga</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyberpunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Anime and manga">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_cyberpunk_works#Animation" title="List of cyberpunk works">List of cyberpunk works § Animation</a></div>
<p>Cyberpunk themes are widely visible in <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Cosplay" title="Cosplay">cosplay</a> is popular and not only teenagers display such fashion styles, cyberpunk has been accepted and its influence is widespread. William Gibson's <i>Neuromancer,</i> whose influence dominated the early cyberpunk movement, was also set in <a href="/wiki/Chiba_Prefecture" title="Chiba Prefecture">Chiba</a>, one of Japan's largest industrial areas, although at the time of writing the novel Gibson did not know the location of Chiba and had no idea how perfectly it fit his vision in some ways. The exposure to cyberpunk ideas and fiction in the mid 1980s has allowed it to seep into the Japanese culture.</p>
<p>Cyberpunk anime and manga draw upon a futuristic vision which has elements in common with western science fiction and therefore have received wide international acceptance outside Japan. "The conceptualization involved in cyberpunk is more of forging ahead, looking at the new global culture. It is a culture that does not exist right now, so the Japanese concept of a cyberpunk future, seems just as valid as a Western one, especially as Western cyberpunk often incorporates many Japanese elements."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> William Gibson is now a frequent visitor to Japan, and he came to see that many of his visions of Japan have become a reality:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Modern Japan simply was cyberpunk. The <a href="/wiki/Japanese_people" title="Japanese people">Japanese</a> themselves knew it and delighted in it. I remember my first glimpse of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shibuya,_Tokyo" title="Shibuya, Tokyo">Shibuya</a>, when one of the young <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> journalists who had taken me there, his face drenched with the light of a thousand media-suns—all that towering, animated crawl of commercial information—said, "You see? You see? It is <i>Blade Runner</i> town." And it was. It so evidently was.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cyberpunk1_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-cyberpunk1-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Cyberpunk has influenced many anime and manga including the ground-breaking <i><a href="/wiki/Akira_(manga)" title="Akira (manga)">Akira</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ghost_in_the_Shell" title="Ghost in the Shell">Ghost in the Shell</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ergo_Proxy" title="Ergo Proxy">Ergo Proxy</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Battle_Angel_Alita" title="Battle Angel Alita">Battle Angel Alita</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Megazone_23" title="Megazone 23">Megazone 23</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Neo_Tokyo_(film)" title="Neo Tokyo (film)">Neo Tokyo</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Goku_Midnight_Eye" title="Goku Midnight Eye">Goku Midnight Eye</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cyber_City_Oedo_808" title="Cyber City Oedo 808">Cyber City Oedo 808</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bubblegum_Crisis" title="Bubblegum Crisis">Bubblegum Crisis</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A.D._Police:_Dead_End_City" title="A.D. Police: Dead End City">A.D. Police: Dead End City</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Angel_Cop" title="Angel Cop">Angel Cop</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/K%C5%8Dji_Morimoto" title="Kōji Morimoto">Extra</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Blame!" title="Blame!">Blame!</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Armitage_III" title="Armitage III">Armitage III</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Texhnolyze" title="Texhnolyze">Texhnolyze</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Serial_Experiments_Lain" title="Serial Experiments Lain">Serial Experiments Lain</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Neon_Genesis_Evangelion" title="Neon Genesis Evangelion">Neon Genesis Evangelion</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Psycho-Pass" title="Psycho-Pass">Psycho-Pass</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Games">Games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyberpunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_cyberpunk_works#Video_games" title="List of cyberpunk works">List of cyberpunk works § Video games</a>, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_cyberpunk_works#Role-playing_games" title="List of cyberpunk works">List of cyberpunk works § Role-playing games</a></div>
<p>There are <a href="/wiki/Category:Cyberpunk_video_games" title="Category:Cyberpunk video games">many</a> cyberpunk <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video games</a>. Popular series include the <i><a href="/wiki/Metal_Gear" title="Metal Gear">Metal Gear</a></i> series, <i><a href="/wiki/Megami_Tensei" title="Megami Tensei">Megami Tensei</a></i> series, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deus_Ex_(series)" title="Deus Ex (series)">Deus Ex</a></i> series, <i><a href="/wiki/Syndicate_(series)" title="Syndicate (series)">Syndicate</a></i> series, and <i><a href="/wiki/System_Shock" title="System Shock">System Shock</a></i> and its <a href="/wiki/System_Shock_2" title="System Shock 2">sequel</a>. Other games, like <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner_(1997_video_game)" title="Blade Runner (1997 video game)">Blade Runner</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ghost_in_the_Shell_(video_game)" title="Ghost in the Shell (video game)">Ghost in the Shell</a></i>, and the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Matrix_(franchise)#Video_games" title="The Matrix (franchise)">Matrix</a></i> series, are based upon genre movies, or <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_game" title="Role-playing game">role-playing games</a> (for instance the various <i><a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Shadowrun_(disambiguation)" title="Shadowrun (disambiguation)">Shadowrun</a></i> games). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CD_Projekt_RED" title="CD Projekt RED">CD Projekt RED</a> are currently developing a cyberpunk game, <i><a href="/wiki/Cyberpunk_2077" title="Cyberpunk 2077">Cyberpunk 2077</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Role-playing_games" title="Role-playing games">role-playing games</a> (RPGs) called <i>Cyberpunk</i> exist: <i>Cyberpunk</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cyberpunk_2020" title="Cyberpunk 2020">Cyberpunk 2020</a></i> and <i>Cyberpunk v3</i>, by <a href="/wiki/R._Talsorian_Games" title="R. Talsorian Games">R. Talsorian Games</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/GURPS_Cyberpunk" title="GURPS Cyberpunk">GURPS Cyberpunk</a></i>, published by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Jackson_Games" title="Steve Jackson Games">Steve Jackson Games</a> as a module of the <a href="/wiki/GURPS" title="GURPS">GURPS</a> family of RPGs. <i>Cyberpunk 2020</i> was designed with the settings of William Gibson's writings in mind, and to some extent with his approval<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, unlike the approach taken by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FASA_Corporation" title="FASA Corporation">FASA</a> in producing the transgenre <i><a href="/wiki/Shadowrun" title="Shadowrun">Shadowrun</a></i> game. Both are set in the near future, in a world where <a href="/wiki/Cyberware" title="Cyberware">cybernetics</a> are prominent. In addition, <a href="/wiki/Iron_Crown_Enterprises" title="Iron Crown Enterprises">Iron Crown Enterprises</a> released an RPG named <i><a href="/wiki/Cyberspace_(role-playing_game)" title="Cyberspace (role-playing game)">Cyberspace</a></i>, which was out of print for several years until recently being re-released in online PDF form.</p>
<p>In 1990, in a convergence of cyberpunk art and reality, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secret_Service" title="United States Secret Service">United States Secret Service</a> raided Steve Jackson Games's headquarters and confiscated all their computers. This was allegedly because the <i><a href="/wiki/GURPS_Cyberpunk" title="GURPS Cyberpunk">GURPS Cyberpunk</a></i> sourcebook could be used to perpetrate computer crime. That was, in fact, not the main reason for the raid, but after the event it was too late to correct the public's impression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Steve Jackson Games later won a lawsuit against the Secret Service, aided by the new <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Frontier_Foundation" title="Electronic Frontier Foundation">Electronic Frontier Foundation</a>. This event has achieved a sort of notoriety, which has extended to the book itself as well. All published editions of <i>GURPS Cyberpunk</i> have a tagline on the front cover, which reads "The book that was seized by the U.S. Secret Service!" Inside, the book provides a summary of the raid and its aftermath.</p>
<p>Cyberpunk has also inspired several <a href="/wiki/Tabletop_game" title="Tabletop game">tabletop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miniature_wargaming" title="Miniature wargaming">miniature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Board_game" title="Board game">board games</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Necromunda" title="Necromunda">Necromunda</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Games_Workshop" title="Games Workshop">Games Workshop</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Netrunner" title="Netrunner">Netrunner</a></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Collectible_card_game" title="Collectible card game">collectible card game</a> introduced in 1996, based on the <i><a href="/wiki/Cyberpunk_2020" title="Cyberpunk 2020">Cyberpunk 2020</a></i> role-playing game. <i><a href="/wiki/Tokyo_NOVA" title="Tokyo NOVA">Tokyo NOVA</a></i>, debuting in 1993, is a cyberpunk role-playing game that uses <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Playing_cards" title="Playing cards">playing cards</a> instead of dice.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyberpunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_cyberpunk_works#Music" title="List of cyberpunk works">List of cyberpunk works § Music</a></div>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"Much of the industrial/dance heavy 'Cyberpunk'—recorded in <a href="/wiki/Billy_Idol" title="Billy Idol">Billy Idol</a>'s Macintosh-run studio—revolves around Idol's theme of the common man rising up to fight against a faceless, soulless, corporate world."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">—Julie Romandetta<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Romandetta_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Romandetta-46">[46]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Some musicians and acts have been classified as cyberpunk due to their aesthetic style and musical content. Often dealing with dystopian visions of the future or biomechanical themes, some fit more squarely in the category than others. Bands whose music has been classified as cyberpunk include <a href="/wiki/Psydoll" title="Psydoll">Psydoll</a>, <a href="/wiki/Front_Line_Assembly" title="Front Line Assembly">Front Line Assembly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clock_DVA" title="Clock DVA">Clock DVA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sigue_Sigue_Sputnik" title="Sigue Sigue Sputnik">Sigue Sigue Sputnik</a>. Some musicians not normally associated with cyberpunk have at times been inspired to create concept albums exploring such themes. Albums such as <a href="/wiki/Gary_Numan" title="Gary Numan">Gary Numan</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Replicas_(album)" title="Replicas (album)">Replicas</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Pleasure_Principle_(Gary_Numan_album)" title="The Pleasure Principle (Gary Numan album)">The Pleasure Principle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Telekon" title="Telekon">Telekon</a> were heavily inspired by the works of Philip K. Dick. <a href="/wiki/Kraftwerk" title="Kraftwerk">Kraftwerk</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Man-Machine" title="The Man-Machine">The Man-Machine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer_World" title="Computer World">Computer World</a> albums both explored the theme of humanity becoming dependent on technology. <a href="/wiki/Nine_Inch_Nails" title="Nine Inch Nails">Nine Inch Nails</a>' concept album <a href="/wiki/Year_Zero_(album)" title="Year Zero (album)">Year Zero</a> also fits into this category. <a href="/wiki/Billy_Idol" title="Billy Idol">Billy Idol</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Cyberpunk_(album)" title="Cyberpunk (album)">Cyberpunk</a></i> drew heavily from cyberpunk literature and the <a href="/wiki/Cyberdelic" title="Cyberdelic">cyberdelic</a> counter culture in its creation. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1._Outside" title="1. Outside">1. Outside</a></i>, a cyberpunk narrative fueled concept album by <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">David Bowie</a>, was warmly met by critics upon its release in 1995. Many musicians have also taken inspiration from specific cyberpunk works or authors, including <a href="/wiki/Sonic_Youth" title="Sonic Youth">Sonic Youth</a>, whose albums <a href="/wiki/Sister_(Sonic_Youth_album)" title="Sister (Sonic Youth album)">Sister</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daydream_Nation" title="Daydream Nation">Daydream Nation</a> take influence from the works of Phillip K. Dick and William Gibson respectively.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vaporwave" title="Vaporwave">Vaporwave</a> and <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Synthwave" title="Synthwave">Synthwave</a> are also influenced by cyberpunk. The former has been interpreted as a dystopian<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-47">[47]</a></sup> critique of <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stylus1_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-stylus1-48">[48]</a></sup> in the vein of cyberpunk and the latter as a nostalgic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrofuturistic" title="Retrofuturistic">retrofuturistic</a> revival of aspects of cyberpunk's origins.</p>
<p>Furthermore, many <a href="/wiki/Dubstep" title="Dubstep">dubstep</a> producers, such as <a href="/wiki/Machine_Man" title="Machine Man">Machine Man</a> and Ghosthack, have found inspiration in cyberpunk themes for their works.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_impact">Social impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyberpunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Social impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_and_architecture">Art and architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyberpunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Art and architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo-Futurism" title="Neo-Futurism">Neo-Futurism</a> artworks and cityscapes have been influenced by cyberpunk, such as <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cyberpunk1_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-cyberpunk1-11">[11]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Sony_Center" title="Sony Center">Sony Center</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Potsdamer_Platz" title="Potsdamer Platz">Potsdamer Platz</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_square" title="Public square">public square</a> of <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Society_and_counterculture">Society and counterculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyberpunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Society and counterculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several subcultures have been inspired by cyberpunk fiction. These include the <a href="/wiki/Cyberdelic" title="Cyberdelic">cyberdelic</a> counter culture of the late 1980s and early 90s. Cyberdelic, whose adherents referred to themselves as "cyberpunks", attempted to blend the <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_era" title="Psychedelic era">psychedelic art and drug movement</a> with the technology of <a href="/wiki/Cyberculture" title="Cyberculture">cyberculture</a>. Early adherents included <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Leary" title="Timothy Leary">Timothy Leary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Frauenfelder" title="Mark Frauenfelder">Mark Frauenfelder</a> and <a href="/wiki/R._U._Sirius" title="R. U. Sirius">R. U. Sirius</a>. The movement largely faded following the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">dot-com bubble</a> implosion of 2000.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cybergoth" title="Cybergoth">Cybergoth</a> is a fashion and dance subculture which draws its inspiration from cyberpunk fiction, as well as <a href="/wiki/Rave" title="Rave">rave</a> and <a href="/wiki/Goth_subculture" title="Goth subculture">Gothic</a> subcultures. In addition, a distinct cyberpunk fashion of its own has emerged in recent years<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2015)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> which rejects the raver and goth influences of <a href="/wiki/Cybergoth" title="Cybergoth">cybergoth</a>, and draws inspiration from urban street fashion, "post apocalypse", functional clothing, high tech sports wear, tactical uniform and multifunction. This fashion goes by names like "tech wear", "goth ninja" or "tech ninja". Important designers in this type of fashion<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2015)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> are ACRONYM, Demobaza, <a href="/wiki/Boris_Bidjan_Saberi" title="Boris Bidjan Saberi">Boris Bidjan Saberi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rick_Owens" title="Rick Owens">Rick Owens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Wang_(designer)" title="Alexander Wang (designer)">Alexander Wang</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Kowloon_Walled_City" title="Kowloon Walled City">Kowloon Walled City</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> (demolished in 1994) is often referenced as the model cyberpunk/dystopian slum as, given its poor living conditions at the time coupled by the city's political, physical, and economic isolation has caused many in academia to be fascinated by the ingenuity of its spawning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_genres">Related genres</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyberpunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Related genres">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Cyberpunk_derivatives" title="Cyberpunk derivatives">Cyberpunk derivatives</a></div>
<p>As a wider variety of writers began to work with cyberpunk concepts, new subgenres of science fiction emerged, some of which could be considered as playing off the cyberpunk label, others which could be considered as legitimate explorations into newer territory. These focused on technology and its social effects in different ways. One prominent subgenre is "<a href="/wiki/Steampunk" title="Steampunk">steampunk</a>," which is set in an <a href="/wiki/Alternate_history" title="Alternate history">alternate history</a> <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a> that combines anachronistic technology with cyberpunk's bleak <a href="/wiki/Film_noir" title="Film noir">film noir</a> world view. The term was originally coined around 1987 as a joke to describe some of the novels of <a href="/wiki/Tim_Powers" title="Tim Powers">Tim Powers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_P._Blaylock" title="James P. Blaylock">James P. Blaylock</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K.W._Jeter" title="K.W. Jeter">K.W. Jeter</a>, but by the time Gibson and Sterling entered the subgenre with their <a href="/wiki/Collaborative_fiction" title="Collaborative fiction">collaborative novel</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Difference_Engine" title="The Difference Engine">The Difference Engine</a></i> the term was being used earnestly as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another subgenre is "<a href="/wiki/Biopunk" title="Biopunk">biopunk</a>" (cyberpunk themes dominated by <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a>) from the early 1990s, a derivative style building on biotechnology rather than informational technology. In these stories, people are changed in some way not by mechanical means, but by <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetic manipulation</a>. <a href="/wiki/Paul_Di_Filippo" title="Paul Di Filippo">Paul Di Filippo</a> is seen as the most prominent biopunk writer, including his half-serious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribofunk" title="Ribofunk">ribofunk</a>. <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Sterling" title="Bruce Sterling">Bruce Sterling</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shaper/Mechanist" title="Shaper/Mechanist">Shaper/Mechanist</a> cycle is also seen as a major influence. In addition, some people consider works such as <a href="/wiki/Neal_Stephenson" title="Neal Stephenson">Neal Stephenson</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Diamond_Age" title="The Diamond Age">The Diamond Age</a></i> to be <a href="/wiki/Cyberpunk_derivatives#Postcyberpunk" title="Cyberpunk derivatives">postcyberpunk</a>.</p>
<p>Cyberpunk works have been described as well-situated within <a href="/wiki/Postmodern_literature" title="Postmodern literature">postmodern literature</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
