<p><br/>
The <b>Saionji family</b> (西園寺家, <i>-ke</i>) was a Japanese <i><a href="/wiki/Kuge" title="Kuge">kuge</a></i> (court aristocrat) family related to the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Fujiwara" title="Northern Fujiwara">Northern Fujiwara</a> branch of the <a href="/wiki/Fujiwara_clan" title="Fujiwara clan">Fujiwara clan</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Imadegawa_clan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Imadegawa clan (page does not exist)">Imadegawa clan</a>.</p>
<p>The family's name was taken from that of the family's formal residence in <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a>, and its <i><a href="/wiki/Mon_(emblem)" title="Mon (emblem)">kamon</a></i> (crest) was a <i><a href="/wiki/Tomoe" title="Tomoe">tomoe</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saionji_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The family was descended from <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Saionji_Michisue&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Saionji Michisue (page does not exist)">Saionji Michisue</a> (1090-1128), son of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fujiwara_no_Kinzane&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fujiwara no Kinzane (page does not exist)">Fujiwara no Kinzane</a>. In the time of Michisue's great-grandson <a href="/wiki/Saionji_Kintsune" title="Saionji Kintsune">Saionji Kintsune</a> (1171-1244), <a href="/wiki/Minamoto_no_Yoritomo" title="Minamoto no Yoritomo">Minamoto no Yoritomo</a>'s niece was married into the Saionji family, thus giving the <a href="/wiki/Kamakura_shogunate" title="Kamakura shogunate">Kamakura shoguns</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Minamoto_clan" title="Minamoto clan">Minamoto clan</a> some influence in, and protection from, the Imperial Court. Members of the Saionji family began to be appointed <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kant%C5%8D_M%C5%8Dshitsugi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kantō Mōshitsugi (page does not exist)">Kantō Mōshitsugi</a></i>, acting alongside the <i><a href="/wiki/Rokuhara_Tandai" title="Rokuhara Tandai">Rokuhara Tandai</a></i> to manage communications and relations between the shogunate and the Court. This began the family's rise to important Court positions, including posts as high as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daj%C5%8D_Daijin" title="Dajō Daijin">Dajō Daijin</a></i> (Chancellor of the Realm). Ever since Kintsune's time, the family, with the support of the Kamakura shogunate, could exert influence over even the Imperial regents, the <a href="/wiki/Sessh%C5%8D_and_Kampaku" title="Sesshō and Kampaku">Sesshō and Kampaku</a>.</p>
<p>The family made its formal residence in the Kitayama (northern mountains) area of Kyoto; the residence was likewise called Saionji, meaning "Western Garden Temple." Thus the family came to be sometimes known as the Lords of Kitayama; when <a href="/wiki/Ashikaga_Yoshimitsu" title="Ashikaga Yoshimitsu">Ashikaga Yoshimitsu</a> became <a href="/wiki/Shogun" title="Shogun">shogun</a> in 1368, he coopted the site for his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kinkakuji" title="Kinkakuji">Kinkakuji</a>, thus laying some claim to a connection to the Saionji and to their prestige as Lords of Kitayama.</p>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Saionji_Sanekane&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Saionji Sanekane (page does not exist)">Saionji Sanekane</a> joined the family to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daikakuji" title="Daikakuji">Daikakuji</a> line of the Imperial family, having become involved with the daughter of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Go-Daigo" title="Emperor Go-Daigo">Emperor Go-Daigo</a> or <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Kameyama" title="Emperor Kameyama">Emperor Kameyama</a> and siring a son, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Saionji_Kinhira&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Saionji Kinhira (page does not exist)">Saionji Kinhira</a>. Several decades later, in the time of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Saionji_Kinmune&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Saionji Kinmune (page does not exist)">Saionji Kinmune</a>, the Kamakura shogunate came to an end, and the Saionji were dismissed from their post as <i>Kantō Mōshitsugi</i>. Kinmune helped hide the persecuted <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=H%C5%8Dj%C5%8D_Yasuie&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hōjō Yasuie (page does not exist)">Hōjō Yasuie</a> and, in the wake of the death of Emperor Go-Daigo, helped plot to set <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Go-Fushimi" title="Emperor Go-Fushimi">Emperor Go-Fushimi</a> on the throne. His schemes revealed by his younger brother <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Saionji_Kinshige&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Saionji Kinshige (page does not exist)">Saionji Kinshige</a>, Kinmune was arrested and executed. During the <a href="/wiki/Nanboku-ch%C5%8D_period" title="Nanboku-chō period">Nanboku-chō period</a> (1336-1392) which followed, in which the two Imperial lines jousted for power, Kinmune's son <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Saionji_Sanetoshi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Saionji Sanetoshi (page does not exist)">Saionji Sanetoshi</a> served the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Court" title="Northern Court">Northern Court</a> as Minister of the Right (<i><a href="/wiki/Udaijin" title="Udaijin">Udaijin</a></i>), restoring the prestige of the family's name.</p>
<p>A Saionji family is known to have existed in <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a> (1600-1868) Kyoto, as producers of <i><a href="/wiki/Biwa" title="Biwa">biwa</a></i>. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Saionji_Saneharu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Saionji Saneharu (page does not exist)">Saionji Saneharu</a> was made Minister of the Left (<i><a href="/wiki/Sadaijin" title="Sadaijin">Sadaijin</a></i>), and gained influence and some financial support through connections to the <a href="/wiki/Hosokawa_clan" title="Hosokawa clan">Hosokawa</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nagaoka_clan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nagaoka clan (page does not exist)">Nagaoka clans</a>. Towards the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Tokugawa_shogunate" title="Late Tokugawa shogunate">end of the Edo period</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saionji_Kinmochi" title="Saionji Kinmochi">Saionji Kinmochi</a> was adopted into the family from the closely related <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tokudaiji_family&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tokudaiji family (page does not exist)">Tokudaiji</a> branch of the Fujiwara clan. Kinmochi lived through the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a>, becoming one of the <i><a href="/wiki/Genr%C5%8D" title="Genrō">genrō</a></i>, or elder statesmen who were a part of the original Meiji government at its beginning. He subsequently held a number of Cabinet posts, becoming <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Japan" title="Prime Minister of Japan">Prime Minister of Japan</a> in 1906.</p>
<p>As members of the <i><a href="/wiki/Kazoku" title="Kazoku">kazoku</a></i> (Western-style system of <a href="/wiki/Peerage" title="Peerage">peerage</a>), the Saionji maintained a considerable degree of prestige, and continued to be close to the world of politics, through the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, when the <i>kazoku</i> were dissolved. The family continues today, and Saionji remains now an uncommon Japanese surname.</p>
