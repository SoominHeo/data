<p><b>Cycling</b>, also called <b>bicycling</b> or <b>biking</b>, is the use of <a href="/wiki/Bicycle" title="Bicycle">bicycles</a> for <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Recreation" title="Recreation">recreation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physical_exercise" title="Physical exercise">exercise</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sport" title="Sport">sport</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Persons engaged in cycling are referred to as "cyclists",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> "bikers",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> or less commonly, as "bicyclists".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Apart from two-wheeled bicycles, "cycling" also includes the riding of <a href="/wiki/Unicycle" title="Unicycle">unicycles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tricycle" title="Tricycle">tricycles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quadracycle" title="Quadracycle">quadracycles</a>, recumbent and similar <a href="/wiki/Human-powered_transport" title="Human-powered transport">human-powered vehicles</a> (HPVs).</p>
<p>Bicycles were introduced in the 19th century and now number approximately one billion worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> They are the principal means of transportation in many parts of the world.</p>
<p>Cycling is widely regarded as a very effective and efficient mode of transportation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> optimal for short to moderate distances.</p>
<p>Bicycles provide numerous benefits in comparison with motor vehicles, including the sustained physical exercise involved in cycling, easier parking, increased maneuverability, and access to roads, <a href="/wiki/Bike_path" title="Bike path">bike paths</a> and rural trails. Cycling also offers a reduced consumption of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>, less <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air</a> or <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">noise pollution</a>, and much reduced <a href="/wiki/Traffic_congestion" title="Traffic congestion">traffic congestion</a>. These lead to less financial cost to the user as well as to society at large (negligible damage to roads, less road area required).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> By fitting bicycle racks on the front of buses, transit agencies can significantly increase the areas they can serve.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among the disadvantages of cycling are the requirement of bicycles (excepting tricycles or quadracycles) to be balanced by the rider in order to remain upright, the reduced protection in crashes in comparison to motor vehicles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> longer travel time (except in densely populated areas), vulnerability to weather conditions, difficulty in transporting passengers, and the fact that a basic level of fitness is required for cycling moderate to long distances.</p>

<p>In many countries, the most commonly used vehicle for road transport is a <a href="/wiki/Utility_bicycle" title="Utility bicycle">utility bicycle</a>. These have frames with relaxed geometry, protecting the rider from shocks of the road and easing steering at low speeds. Utility bicycles tend to be equipped with accessories such as mudguards, pannier racks and lights, which extends their usefulness on a daily basis. As the bicycle is so effective as a means of transportation various companies have developed methods of carrying anything from the weekly shop to children on bicycles. Certain countries rely heavily on bicycles and their culture has developed around the bicycle as a primary form of transport. In Europe, Denmark and the Netherlands have the most bicycles per capita and most often use bicycles for everyday transport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Road bikes tend to have a more upright shape and a shorter wheelbase, which make the bike more mobile but harder to ride slowly. The design, coupled with low or dropped handlebars, requires the rider to bend forward more, making use of stronger muscles (particularly the <a href="/wiki/Gluteus_maximus_muscle" title="Gluteus maximus muscle">gluteus maximus</a>) and reducing air resistance at high speed.</p>
<p>The price of a new bicycle can range from US$50 to more than US$20,000 (the highest priced bike in the world is the custom Madone by <a href="/wiki/Damien_Hirst" title="Damien Hirst">Damien Hirst</a>, sold at $500,000 USD<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> depending on quality, type and weight (the most exotic road bicycles can weigh as little as 3.2 kg (7 lb)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>). However, <a class="external text" href="http://www.uci.ch/Templates/UCI/UCI5/layout.asp?MenuID=MTYxNw&amp;LangId=1" rel="nofollow">UCI</a> regulations stipulate a legal <a href="/wiki/Racing_bicycle" title="Racing bicycle">race bike</a> cannot weigh less than 6.8 kg (14.99 lbs). Being measured for a bike and taking it for a test ride are recommended before buying.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bicycle#Drivetrain_and_gearing" title="Bicycle">drivetrain</a> components of the bike should also be considered. A middle grade <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D%C3%A9railleur" title="Dérailleur">dérailleur</a> is sufficient for a beginner, although many utility bikes are equipped with <a href="/wiki/Hub_gear" title="Hub gear">hub gears</a>. If the rider plans a significant amount of <a href="/wiki/Hillclimbing_(cycling)" title="Hillclimbing (cycling)">hillclimbing</a> a triple-chainrings <a href="/wiki/Crankset" title="Crankset">crankset</a> <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_gearing" title="Bicycle gearing">gear system</a> may be preferred. Otherwise, the relatively lighter and less expensive double chainring may be better. Much simpler fixed wheel bikes are also available.</p>
<p>Many road bikes, along with mountain bikes, include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clipless_pedal" title="Clipless pedal">clipless pedals</a> to which special shoes attach, via a <a href="/wiki/Cleat_(shoe)" title="Cleat (shoe)">cleat</a>, enabling the rider to pull on the pedals as well as push. Other possible accessories for the bicycle include front and rear lights, <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_bell" title="Bicycle bell">bells</a> or horns, child carrying seats, cycling computers with GPS, locks, bar tape, fenders (mud-guards), baggage racks, baggage carriers and pannier bags, water bottles and bottle cages.</p>
<p>For basic maintenance and repairs cyclists can carry a <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_pump" title="Bicycle pump">pump</a> (or a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub> cartridge</a>), a puncture repair kit, a spare inner tube, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tire_levers" title="Tire levers">tire levers</a> and a set of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allen_keys" title="Allen keys">allen keys</a>. Cycling can be more efficient and comfortable with special <a href="/wiki/Cycling_shoe" title="Cycling shoe">shoes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cycling_gloves" title="Cycling gloves">gloves</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cycling_shorts" title="Cycling shorts">shorts</a>. In wet weather, riding can be more tolerable with waterproof clothes, such as cape, jacket, trousers (pants) and overshoes and high-visibility clothing is advisable to reduce the risk from motor vehicle users.</p>
<p>Items legally required in some jurisdictions, or voluntarily adopted for safety reasons, include <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_helmet" title="Bicycle helmet">bicycle helmets</a>, generator or battery operated lights, <a href="/wiki/Retroreflector" title="Retroreflector">reflectors</a>, and audible signalling devices such as a bell or horn. Extras include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studded_tires" title="Studded tires">studded tires</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Cyclocomputer" title="Cyclocomputer">bicycle computer</a>.</p>
<p>Bikes can also be heavily customized, with different seat designs and handle bars, for example.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Skills">Skills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Skills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many schools and police departments run educational programs to instruct children in bicycle handling skills and introduce them to the <a href="/wiki/Traffic#Rules_of_the_road" title="Traffic">rules of the road</a> as they apply to cyclists. In different countries these may be known as <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_rodeo" title="Bicycle rodeo">bicycle rodeos</a> or operated as schemes such as <a href="/wiki/Bikeability" title="Bikeability">Bikeability</a>. Education for adult cyclists is available from organizations such as the <a href="/wiki/League_of_American_Bicyclists" title="League of American Bicyclists">League of American Bicyclists</a>.</p>
<p>Beyond simply riding, another skill is riding efficiently and safely in traffic. One popular approach to riding in <a href="/wiki/Motor_vehicle" title="Motor vehicle">motor vehicle</a> traffic is <a href="/wiki/Vehicular_cycling" title="Vehicular cycling">vehicular cycling</a>, occupying road space as car does. Alternately, in countries such as Denmark and the Netherlands, where cycling is popular, cyclists are sometimes segregated into bike lanes at the side of, or separate from, main highways and roads. Many primary schools participate in the national road test in which children individually complete a circuit on roads near the school while being observed by testers.</p>

<p>Cyclists, pedestrians and motorists make different demands on road design which may lead to conflicts. Some jurisdictions give priority to motorized traffic, for example setting up one-way street systems, free-right turns, high capacity roundabouts, and <a href="/wiki/Interchange_(road)" title="Interchange (road)">slip roads</a>. Others share priority with cyclists so as to encourage more cycling by applying varying combinations of <a href="/wiki/Traffic_calming" title="Traffic calming">traffic calming</a> measures to limit the impact of motorized transport, and by building <a href="/wiki/Bike_lane" title="Bike lane">bike lanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bike_path" title="Bike path">bike paths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cycle_track" title="Cycle track">cycle tracks</a>.</p>
<p>In jurisdictions where motor vehicles were given priority, cycling has tended to decline while in jurisdictions where cycling infrastructure was built, cycling rates have remained steady or increased. Occasionally, extreme measures against cycling may occur. In Shanghai, where bicycles were once the dominant <a href="/wiki/Mode_of_transport" title="Mode of transport">mode of transport</a>, bicycle travel on a few city roads was banned temporarily in December 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In areas in which cycling is popular and encouraged, cycle-parking facilities using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bicycle_stand" title="Bicycle stand">bicycle stands</a>, lockable mini-garages, and patrolled cycle parks are used in order to reduce theft. Local governments promote cycling by permitting bicycles to be carried on public transport or by providing external <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_carrier" title="Bicycle carrier">attachment devices</a> on public transport vehicles. Conversely, an absence of secure cycle-parking is a recurring complaint by cyclists from cities with low <a href="/wiki/Modal_share" title="Modal share">modal share</a> of cycling.</p>
<p>Extensive cycling infrastructure may be found in some cities. Such dedicated paths in some cities often have to be shared with in-line skaters, scooters, skateboarders, and pedestrians. Dedicated cycling infrastructure is treated differently in the <a href="/wiki/Bikeway_and_legislation" title="Bikeway and legislation">law</a> of every jurisdiction, including the question of liability of users in a collision. There is also some debate about the <a href="/wiki/Bikeway_safety" title="Bikeway safety">safety</a> of the various types of separated facilities.</p>
<p>Bicycles are considered a sustainable mode of transport, especially suited for urban use and relatively shorter distances when used for transport (compared to recreation). Case studies and good practices (from European cities and some worldwide examples) that promote and stimulate this kind of functional cycling in cities can be found at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eltis" title="Eltis">Eltis</a>, Europe's portal for local transport.</p>
<p>A number of cities, including Paris, London and <a href="/wiki/Bicing" title="Bicing">Barcelona</a>, now have successful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bicycle_sharing" title="Bicycle sharing">bike hire schemes</a> designed to help people cycle in the city. Typically these feature utilitarian city bikes which lock into docking stations, released on payment for set time periods. Costs vary from city to city. In London, initial hire access costs £2 per day. The first 30 minutes of each trip is free, with £2 for each additional 30 minutes until the bicycle is returned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p>Utility cycling refers both to cycling as a mode of daily <a href="/wiki/Commuting" title="Commuting">commuting</a> transport as well as the use of a bicycle in a commercial activity, mainly to transport goods, mostly accomplished in an <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban environment</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mail" title="Mail">postal services</a> of many countries have long relied on bicycles. The British <a href="/wiki/Royal_Mail" title="Royal Mail">Royal Mail</a> first started using bicycles in 1880; now bicycle delivery fleets include 37,000 in the UK, 25,700 in Germany, 10,500 in Hungary and 7000 in Sweden. In Australia, Australia Post has also reintroduced bicycle postal deliveries on some routes due to an inability to recruit sufficient licensed riders willing to use their uncomfortable motorbikes. The <a href="/wiki/London_Ambulance_Service" title="London Ambulance Service">London Ambulance Service</a> has recently introduced bicycling <a href="/wiki/Paramedic" title="Paramedic">paramedics</a>, who can often get to the scene of an incident in <a href="/wiki/Central_London" title="Central London">Central London</a> more quickly than a motorized ambulance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The use of <a href="/wiki/Police_bicycle" title="Police bicycle">bicycles by police</a> has been increasing, since they provide greater accessibility to bicycle and pedestrian zones and allow access when roads are congested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bicycles enjoy substantial use as general delivery vehicles in many countries. In the UK and North America, as their first jobs, generations of teenagers have worked at delivering newspapers by bicycle. London has many delivery companies that use bicycles with trailers. Most cities in the West, and many outside it, support a sizeable and visible industry of <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_messenger" title="Bicycle messenger">cycle couriers</a> who deliver documents and small packages. In India, many of Mumbai's <a href="/wiki/Dabbawala" title="Dabbawala">Dabbawalas</a> use bicycles to deliver home cooked lunches to the city’s workers. In <a href="/wiki/Bogot%C3%A1" title="Bogotá">Bogotá</a>, Colombia the city’s largest bakery recently replaced most of its delivery trucks with bicycles. Even the car industry uses bicycles. At the huge <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz" title="Mercedes-Benz">Mercedes-Benz</a> factory in <a href="/wiki/Sindelfingen" title="Sindelfingen">Sindelfingen</a>, Germany workers use bicycles, color-coded by department, to move around the factory.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>Bicycles are used for recreation at all ages. <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_touring" title="Bicycle touring">Bicycle touring</a>, also known as cyclotourism, involves touring and exploration or sightseeing by bicycle for leisure. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brevet_(cycling)" title="Brevet (cycling)">brevet</a> or randonnée is an organized long-distance ride.</p>
<p>One popular Dutch pleasure is the enjoyment of relaxed cycling in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rural" title="Rural">countryside</a> of the Netherlands. The land is very flat and full of public <a href="/wiki/Bike_path" title="Bike path">bicycle trails</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cycle_track" title="Cycle track">cycle tracks</a> where cyclists are not bothered by cars and other traffic, which makes it ideal for cycling recreation. Many <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch people</a> subscribe every year to an event called <i><a href="/wiki/Fietsvierdaagse" title="Fietsvierdaagse">fietsvierdaagse</a></i> — four days of organised cycling through the local environment. <a href="/wiki/Paris%E2%80%93Brest%E2%80%93Paris" title="Paris–Brest–Paris">Paris–Brest–Paris</a> (PBP), which began in 1891, is the oldest bicycling event still run on a regular basis on the open road, covers over 1,200 km (746 mi) and imposes a 90-hour time limit. Similar if smaller institutions exist in many countries.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Organized_rides">Organized rides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Organized rides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Cycling_club" title="Cycling club">cycling clubs</a> hold organized rides in which bicyclists of all levels participate. The typical organized ride starts with a large group of riders, called the mass, bunch or even <a href="/wiki/Peloton" title="Peloton">peloton</a>. This will thin out over the course of the ride. Many riders choose to ride together in groups of the same skill level to take advantage of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drafting_(racing)" title="Drafting (racing)">drafting</a>.</p>
<p>Most organized rides, for example <a href="/wiki/Cyclosportive" title="Cyclosportive">cyclosportives</a> (or gran fondos), <a href="/wiki/Challenge_riding" title="Challenge riding">Challenge Rides</a> or <a href="/wiki/Reliability_trial" title="Reliability trial">reliability trials</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hillclimbing_(cycling)" title="Hillclimbing (cycling)">hill climbs</a> include registration requirements and will provide information either through the mail or online concerning start times and other requirements. Rides usually consist of several different routes, sorted by mileage, and with a certain number of rest stops that usually include refreshments, first aid and maintenance tools. Routes can vary by as much as 100 miles (160 km).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mountain">Mountain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mountain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mountain_biking" title="Mountain biking">Mountain biking</a> began in the 1970s, originally as a downhill sport, practised on customized <a href="/wiki/Cruiser_bicycle" title="Cruiser bicycle">cruiser bicycles</a> around <a href="/wiki/Mount_Tamalpais" title="Mount Tamalpais">Mount Tamalpais</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Most mountain biking takes place on dirt roads, trails and in purpose-built parks. Downhill mountain biking has just evolved in the recent years and is performed at places such as Whistler Mountain Bike Park. Slopestyle, a form of downhill, is when riders do tricks such as tailwhips, 360s, backflips and front flips. There are several disciplines of mountain biking besides downhill. Cross country, often referred to as XC, all mountain, trail, free ride, and newly popular enduro.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Racing">Racing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Racing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bicycle_racing" title="Bicycle racing">Bicycle racing</a></div>
<p>Shortly after the introduction of bicycles, competitions developed independently in many parts of the world. Early races involving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boneshaker_(bicycle)" title="Boneshaker (bicycle)">boneshaker</a> style bicycles were predictably fraught with injuries. Large races became popular during the 1890s "Golden Age of Cycling", with events across Europe, and in the U.S. and Japan as well. At one point, almost every major city in the US had a <a href="/wiki/Velodrome" title="Velodrome">velodrome</a> or two for <a href="/wiki/Track_cycling" title="Track cycling">track racing</a> events, however since the middle of the 20th century cycling has become a minority sport in the US whilst in Continental Europe it continues to be a major sport, particularly in the United Kingdom, France, Belgium, Italy and Spain. The most famous of all bicycle races is the <a href="/wiki/Tour_de_France" title="Tour de France">Tour de France</a>. This began in 1903, and continues to capture the attention of the sporting world.</p>
<p>In 1899, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Minthorn_Murphy" title="Charles Minthorn Murphy">Charles Minthorn Murphy</a> became the first man to ride his bicycle a mile in under a minute (hence his nickname, Mile-a-Minute Murphy), which he did by drafting a <a href="/wiki/Locomotive" title="Locomotive">locomotive</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>.</p>
<p>As the bicycle evolved its various forms, different racing formats developed. Road races may involve both team and individual competition, and are contested in various ways. They range from the one-day road race, <a href="/wiki/Criterium" title="Criterium">criterium</a>, and time trial to multi-stage events like the Tour de France and its sister events which make up cycling's <a href="/wiki/Grand_Tour_(cycling)" title="Grand Tour (cycling)">Grand Tours</a>. <a href="/wiki/Recumbent_bicycle" title="Recumbent bicycle">Recumbent bicycles</a> were banned from bike races in 1934 after Marcel Berthet set a new <a href="/wiki/Hour_record" title="Hour record">hour record</a> in his <i>Velodyne streamliner</i> (49.992 km on November 18, 1933). <a href="/wiki/Track_bicycle" title="Track bicycle">Track bicycles</a> are used for <a href="/wiki/Track_cycling" title="Track cycling">track cycling</a> in <a href="/wiki/Velodrome" title="Velodrome">Velodromes</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Cyclo-cross" title="Cyclo-cross">cyclo-cross</a> races are held on outdoor terrain, including pavement, grass, and mud. Cyclocross races feature man-made features such as small barriers which riders either <a href="/wiki/Bunny_hop_(cycling)" title="Bunny hop (cycling)">bunny hop</a> over or dismount and walk over. <a href="/wiki/Individual_time_trial" title="Individual time trial">Time trial</a> races, another form of road racing require a rider to ride against the clock. Time trials can be performed as a team or as a single rider. Bikes are changed for time trial races, using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aero_bars" title="Aero bars">aero bars</a>. In the past decade, <a href="/wiki/Mountain_biking" title="Mountain biking">mountain bike racing</a> has also reached international popularity and is even an Olympic sport.</p>
<p>Professional racing organizations place limitations on the bicycles that can be used in the races that they sanction. For example, the Union Cycliste Internationale, the governing body of international cycle sport (which sanctions races such as the Tour de France), decided in the late 1990s to create additional rules which prohibit racing bicycles weighing less than 6.8 kilograms (14.96 pounds). The UCI rules also effectively ban some <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_frame" title="Bicycle frame">bicycle frame</a> innovations (such as the <a href="/wiki/Recumbent_bicycle" title="Recumbent bicycle">recumbent bicycle</a>) by requiring a double triangle structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="War">War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_infantry" title="Bicycle infantry">Bicycle infantry</a></div>
<p>The bicycle has been used as a method of reconnaissance as well as transporting soldiers and supplies to combat zones. In this it has taken over many of the functions of <a href="/wiki/Horses_in_warfare" title="Horses in warfare">horses in warfare</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Second_Boer_War" title="Second Boer War">Second Boer War</a>, both sides used bicycles for scouting. In World War I, France, Germany, Australia and New Zealand used bicycles to move troops. In its 1937 invasion of China, Japan employed some 50,000 bicycle troops, and similar forces were instrumental in Japan's <a href="/wiki/Malayan_Campaign" title="Malayan Campaign">march or "roll" through Malaya</a> in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Germany used bicycles again in World War II, while the British employed airborne "Cycle-commandos" with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folding_bike" title="Folding bike">folding bikes</a>.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, communist forces used bicycles extensively as cargo carriers along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh_Trail" title="Ho Chi Minh Trail">Ho Chi Minh Trail</a>.</p>
<p>The last country known to maintain a regiment of bicycle troops was Switzerland, which disbanded its last unit in 2003.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Activism">Activism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Activism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two broad and correlated themes run in bicycle activism: one is about advocating the bicycle as an alternative mode of transport, and the other is about the creation of conditions to permit and/or encourage bicycle use, both for utility and recreational cycling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Although the first, which emphasizes the potential for energy and resource conservation and health benefits gained from cycling versus automobile use, is relatively undisputed, the second is the subject of much debate.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Critical_Mass,_San_Francisco,_April_29,_2005.jpg"><img alt="Many cyclists on a road, all going in the same direction." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Critical_Mass%2C_San_Francisco%2C_April_29%2C_2005.jpg/220px-Critical_Mass%2C_San_Francisco%2C_April_29%2C_2005.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Critical_Mass%2C_San_Francisco%2C_April_29%2C_2005.jpg/330px-Critical_Mass%2C_San_Francisco%2C_April_29%2C_2005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Critical_Mass%2C_San_Francisco%2C_April_29%2C_2005.jpg/440px-Critical_Mass%2C_San_Francisco%2C_April_29%2C_2005.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Critical_Mass,_San_Francisco,_April_29,_2005.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
San Francisco <a href="/wiki/Critical_Mass_(cycling)" title="Critical Mass (cycling)">Critical Mass</a>, April 29, 2005.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It is generally agreed that improved local and <a href="/wiki/Inter-city_rail" title="Inter-city rail">inter-city rail</a> services and other methods of mass transportation (including greater provision for cycle carriage on such services) create conditions to encourage bicycle use. However, there are different opinions on the role of various types of <a href="/wiki/Cycling_infrastructure" title="Cycling infrastructure">cycling infrastructure</a> in building <a href="/wiki/Bicycle-friendly" title="Bicycle-friendly">bicycle-friendly</a> cities and roads.</p>
<p>Some bicycle activists (including some traffic management advisers) seek the construction of <a href="/wiki/Bike_path" title="Bike path">bike paths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cycle_track" title="Cycle track">cycle tracks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bike_lane" title="Bike lane">bike lanes</a> for journeys of all lengths and point to their success in promoting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bike_safety" title="Bike safety">safety</a> and encouraging more people to cycle. Some activists, especially those from the <a href="/wiki/Vehicular_cycling" title="Vehicular cycling">vehicular cycling</a> tradition, view the safety, practicality, and intent of such facilities with suspicion. They favor a more holistic approach based on <a href="/wiki/Traffic_psychology#Accident_prevention_and_improvement_of_traffic_safety" title="Traffic psychology">the 4 'E's</a>; <i>education</i> (of everyone involved), <i>encouragement</i> (to apply the education), <i>enforcement</i> (to protect the rights of others), and <i>engineering</i> (to facilitate travel while respecting every person's equal right to do so). Some groups offer <a href="/wiki/Vehicular_cycling#Education" title="Vehicular cycling">training courses</a> to help cyclists integrate themselves with other traffic.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Critical_Mass_(cycling)" title="Critical Mass (cycling)">Critical Mass</a> is an event typically held on the last Friday of every month in <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">cities</a> around the world where bicyclists take to the streets <i>en masse</i>. While the ride was originally founded with the idea of drawing attention to how unfriendly the city was to bicyclists, the leaderless structure of Critical Mass makes it impossible to assign it any one specific goal. In fact, the purpose of Critical Mass is not formalized beyond the <a href="/wiki/Direct_action" title="Direct action">direct action</a> of meeting at a set location and time and traveling as a group through city streets.</p>
<p>There is a long-running <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_helmet#Opinions_for_and_against_the_use_of_helmets" title="Bicycle helmet">cycle helmet debate</a> among activists. The most heated controversy surrounds the topic of <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_helmet#Legislation_and_culture" title="Bicycle helmet">compulsory helmet use</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Associations">Associations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Associations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:World_Cycling_Centre_-_Aigle_Switzerland.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/World_Cycling_Centre_-_Aigle_Switzerland.jpg/220px-World_Cycling_Centre_-_Aigle_Switzerland.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/World_Cycling_Centre_-_Aigle_Switzerland.jpg/330px-World_Cycling_Centre_-_Aigle_Switzerland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/World_Cycling_Centre_-_Aigle_Switzerland.jpg/440px-World_Cycling_Centre_-_Aigle_Switzerland.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:World_Cycling_Centre_-_Aigle_Switzerland.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Headquarters of the <a href="/wiki/Union_Cycliste_Internationale" title="Union Cycliste Internationale">Union Cycliste Internationale</a> in Switzerland</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Cyclists form associations, both for specific interests (trails development, road maintenance, bike maintenance, urban design, racing clubs, touring clubs, etc.) and for more global goals (<a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">energy conservation</a>, pollution reduction, promotion of fitness). Some bicycle clubs and national associations became prominent advocates for improvements to roads and highways. In the United States, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/League_of_American_Wheelmen" title="League of American Wheelmen">League of American Wheelmen</a> lobbied for the improvement of roads in the last part of the 19th century, founding and leading the national <a href="/wiki/Good_Roads_Movement" title="Good Roads Movement">Good Roads Movement</a>. Their model for political organization, as well as the paved roads for which they argued, facilitated the growth of the automobile.</p>
<p>As a sport, cycling is governed internationally by the <a href="/wiki/Union_Cycliste_Internationale" title="Union Cycliste Internationale">Union Cycliste Internationale</a> in Switzerland, <a href="/wiki/USA_Cycling" title="USA Cycling">USA Cycling</a> (merged with the United States Cycling Federation in 1995) in the United States, (for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upright_bicycle" title="Upright bicycle">upright bicycles</a>) and by the <a href="/wiki/International_Human_Powered_Vehicle_Association" title="International Human Powered Vehicle Association">International Human Powered Vehicle Association</a> (for other HPVs, or human-powered vehicles). Cycling for transport and touring is promoted on a European level by the <a href="/wiki/European_Cyclists%27_Federation" title="European Cyclists' Federation">European Cyclists' Federation</a>, with associated members from Great Britain, Japan and elsewhere. Regular conferences on cycling as transport are held under the auspices of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velo_City" title="Velo City">Velo City</a>; global conferences are coordinated by Velo Mondial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_effects">Health effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Health effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cycling_woman_wearing_miniskirt.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2353" data-file-width="2626" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Cycling_woman_wearing_miniskirt.jpg/200px-Cycling_woman_wearing_miniskirt.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Cycling_woman_wearing_miniskirt.jpg/300px-Cycling_woman_wearing_miniskirt.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Cycling_woman_wearing_miniskirt.jpg/400px-Cycling_woman_wearing_miniskirt.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cycling_woman_wearing_miniskirt.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Woman doing casual cycling in Canada</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The health benefits of cycling outweigh the risks, when cycling is compared to a sedentary lifestyle. A Dutch study found that cycling can extend lifespans by up to 14 months, but the risks equated to a reduced lifespan of 40 days or less.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cycling_in_the_Netherlands" title="Cycling in the Netherlands">Cycling in the Netherlands</a> is often safer than in other parts of the world, so the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Risk-benefit_ratio" title="Risk-benefit ratio">risk-benefit ratio</a> will be different in other regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Overall, benefits of cycling or walking have been shown to exceed risks by ratios of 9:1 to 96:1 when compared with no exercise at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> However these studies did not compare cycling to other forms of <a href="/wiki/Physical_exercise" title="Physical exercise">exercise</a> that can involve less risk.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exercise">Exercise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Exercise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cyclist-189.JPG"><img alt="A man with sports clothes and a white helmet on a bicycle on a road." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Cyclist-189.JPG/220px-Cyclist-189.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Cyclist-189.JPG/330px-Cyclist-189.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Cyclist-189.JPG/440px-Cyclist-189.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cyclist-189.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Heavily equipped London cyclist: specialist cycle clothing, pollution mask, dark glasses and helmet.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Physical_exercise" title="Physical exercise">physical exercise</a> gained from cycling is generally linked with increased health and well-being. According to the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a>, physical inactivity is second only to <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_smoking" title="Tobacco smoking">tobacco smoking</a> as a health risk in developed countries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO-27">[27]</a></sup> and this is associated with many tens of billions of dollars of healthcare costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The WHO's report<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-WHO-27">[27]</a></sup> suggests that increasing physical activity is a public health 'best buy', and that cycling is a 'highly suitable activity' for this purpose. The charity <a href="/wiki/Sustrans" title="Sustrans">Sustrans</a> reports that investment in cycling provision can give a 20:1 return from health and other benefits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> It has been estimated that, on average, approximately 20 life-years are gained from the health benefits of road bicycling for every life-year lost through injury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bicycles are often used by people seeking to improve their fitness and cardiovascular health. In this regard, cycling is especially helpful for those with <a href="/wiki/Arthritis" title="Arthritis">arthritis</a> of the lower limbs who are unable to pursue sports that cause impact to the knees and other joints. Since cycling can be used for the practical purpose of transportation, there can be less need for self-discipline to exercise.</p>
<p>Cycling while seated is a relatively non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weight_bearing" title="Weight bearing">weight bearing</a> exercise that, like <a href="/wiki/Human_swimming#As_recreation_and_exercise" title="Human swimming">swimming</a>, does little to promote bone density.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Cycling up and out of the saddle, on the other hand, does a better job by transferring more of the rider's body weight to the legs. However, excessive cycling while standing can cause knee damage<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (May 2011)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> It used to be thought that cycling while standing was less energy efficient, but recent research has proven this not to be true. Other than air resistance, there is no wasted energy from cycling while standing, if it is done correctly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cycling on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stationary_cycle" title="Stationary cycle">stationary cycle</a> is frequently advocated as a suitable exercise for rehabilitation, particularly for lower limb injury, owing to the low impact which it has on the joints. In particular, cycling is commonly used within knee rehabilitation programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bike_at_Prins_hendrikkade_Amsterdam_centraal.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="4928" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Bike_at_Prins_hendrikkade_Amsterdam_centraal.JPG/220px-Bike_at_Prins_hendrikkade_Amsterdam_centraal.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Bike_at_Prins_hendrikkade_Amsterdam_centraal.JPG/330px-Bike_at_Prins_hendrikkade_Amsterdam_centraal.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Bike_at_Prins_hendrikkade_Amsterdam_centraal.JPG/440px-Bike_at_Prins_hendrikkade_Amsterdam_centraal.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bike_at_Prins_hendrikkade_Amsterdam_centraal.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bike at Prins Hendrikkade <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As a response to the increased global sedentarity and consequent overweight and <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a>, one response that has been adopted by many organizations concerned with health and environment is the promotion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_travel" title="Active travel">Active travel</a>, which seeks to promote walking and cycling as safe and attractive alternatives to motorized transport. Given that many journeys are for relatively short distances, there is considerable scope to replace car use with walking or cycling, though in many settings this may require some infrastructure modification, particularly to attract the less experienced and confident.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bicycle_safety">Bicycle safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Bicycle safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Cycling suffers from a perception that it is unsafe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> This perception is not always backed by hard numbers, because of under reporting of accidents and lack of bicycle use data (amount of cycling, kilometers cycled) which make it hard to assess the <a href="/wiki/Risk" title="Risk">risk</a> and monitor changes in risks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vanparijs_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vanparijs-37">[37]</a></sup> In the UK, fatality rates per mile or kilometre are slightly less than those for walking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UK_fatalities_by_mode_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-UK_fatalities_by_mode-38">[38]</a></sup> In the US, bicycling fatality rates are less than 2/3 of those walking the same distance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> However, in the UK for example the fatality and serious injury rates <i>per hour of travel</i> are just over <i>double</i> for cycling than those for walking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UK_fatalities_by_mode_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-UK_fatalities_by_mode-38">[38]</a></sup> Thus if a person is, for example, about to undertake a ten kilometre journey to a given destination it may on average be safer to undertake this journey by bicycle than on foot. However, if a person is intending, for example, to undertake an hour's exercise it is likely to be considerably <i>more</i> dangerous to take that exercise by cycling rather than by walking.</p>
<p>Despite the risk factors associated with bicycling, cyclists have a lower overall mortality rate when compared to other groups. A Danish study in 2000 found that even after adjustment for other risk factors, including leisure time physical activity, those who did not cycle to work experienced a 39% higher mortality rate than those who did.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Injuries (to cyclists, from cycling) can be divided into two types:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_trauma" title="Physical trauma">Physical trauma</a> (extrinsic)</li>
<li>Overuse (intrinsic).</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_trauma">Physical trauma</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Physical trauma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acute_(medical)" title="Acute (medical)">Acute</a> physical trauma includes injuries to the head and extremities resulting from falls and collisions. Most cycle deaths result from a collision with a car or heavy goods vehicle, both motorist and cyclist having been found responsible for collisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> A third of collisions between motorists and cyclists are caused by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car_dooring" title="Car dooring">car dooring</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> However, around 16% of serious cyclist injuries reported to police in the UK in 2014 did not involve any other person or vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ROSPA_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-ROSPA-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although a majority of bicycle collisions occur during the day,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ROSPA_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-ROSPA-46">[46]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_lighting#Safety" title="Bicycle lighting">bicycle lighting</a> is recommended for <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_safety" title="Bicycle safety">safety when bicycling</a> at night to increase visibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHSTA_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHSTA-47">[47]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Overuse_injuries">Overuse injuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Overuse injuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Of a study of 518 cyclists, a large majority reported at least one overuse injury, with over one third requiring medical treatment. The most common injury sites were the neck (48.8%) and the knees (41.7%), as well as the groin/buttocks (36.1%), hands (31.1%), and back (30.3%). Women were more likely to suffer from neck and shoulder pain than men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many cyclists suffer from overuse injuries to the knees, affecting cyclists at all levels. These are caused by many factors:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Incorrect bicycle fit or adjustment, particularly the saddle.</li>
<li>Incorrect adjustment of clipless pedals.</li>
<li>Too many hills, or too many miles, too early in the training season.</li>
<li>Poor training preparation for long touring rides.</li>
<li>Selecting too high a gear. A lower gear for uphill climb protects the knees, even though muscles may be well able to handle a higher gear.</li>
</ul>
<p>Excessive saddle height can cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Posterior_(anatomy)" title="Posterior (anatomy)">posterior</a> knee pain, while setting the saddle too low can cause pain in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anterior" title="Anterior">anterior</a> of the knee. An incorrectly fitted saddle may eventually lead to muscle imbalance. A 25 to 35 degree knee angle is recommended to avoid an overuse injury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Overuse injuries, including chronic nerve damage at weight bearing locations, can occur as a result of repeatedly riding a bicycle for extended periods of time. Damage to the <a href="/wiki/Ulnar_nerve" title="Ulnar nerve">ulnar nerve</a> in the palm, <a href="/wiki/Carpal_tunnel_syndrome" title="Carpal tunnel syndrome">carpal tunnel</a> in the wrist, the genitourinary tract<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bicycle_seat_neuropathy" title="Bicycle seat neuropathy">bicycle seat neuropathy</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> may result from overuse. <a href="/wiki/Recumbent_bicycle" title="Recumbent bicycle">Recumbent bicycles</a> are designed on different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ergonomics" title="Ergonomics">ergonomic</a> principles and eliminate pressure from the saddle and handlebars, due to the relaxed riding position.</p>
<p>Note that overuse is a relative term, and capacity varies greatly between individuals. Someone starting out in cycling must be careful to increase length and frequency of cycling sessions slowly, starting for example at an hour or two per day, or a hundred miles or kilometers per week. Bilateral muscular pain is a normal by-product of the training process, whereas unilateral pain may reveal "exercise-induced arterial endofibrosis".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Joint pain and numbness are also early signs of overuse injury.</p>
<p>Cycling has been linked to sexual impotence due to pressure on the perineum from the seat, but fitting a proper sized seat prevents this effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bmj.com_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-bmj.com-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-no_nose_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-no_nose-57">[57]</a></sup> In extreme cases, <a href="/wiki/Pudendal_nerve_entrapment" title="Pudendal nerve entrapment">pudendal nerve entrapment</a> can be a source of intractable perineal pain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Some cyclists with induced pudendal nerve pressure neuropathy gained relief from improvements in saddle position and riding techniques.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) has investigated the potential health effects of prolonged bicycling in police bicycle patrol units, including the possibility that some bicycle saddles exert excessive pressure on the urogenital area of cyclists, restricting blood flow to the genitals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Their study found that using bicycle seats without protruding noses reduced pressure on the groin by at least 65% and significantly reduced the number of cases of urogenital paresthesia. A follow-up found that 90% of bicycle officers who tried the no-nose seat were using it six months later. NIOSH recommends that riders use a no-nose bicycle seat for workplace bicycling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-no_nose_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-no_nose-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-impact_niosh_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-impact_niosh-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>A Spanish study of top <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triathlete" title="Triathlete">triathletes</a> found those who cover more than 186 miles (300 km) a week on their bikes have less than 4% normal looking <a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a>, where normal adult males would be expected to have from 15% to 20%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bmj.com_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-bmj.com-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite rumors to the contrary, there is no <a href="/wiki/Scientific_evidence" title="Scientific evidence">scientific evidence</a> linking cycling with <a href="/wiki/Testicular_cancer" title="Testicular cancer">testicular cancer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Exposure_to_air_pollution">Exposure to air pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Exposure to air pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>One concern is that riding in traffic may expose the cyclist to higher levels of air pollution, especially if he or she travels on or along busy roads. Some authors have claimed this to be untrue, showing that the pollutant and irritant count within cars is consistently higher,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> presumably because of limited circulation of air within the car and due to the air intake being directly in the stream of other traffic. Other authors have found small or inconsistent differences in concentrations but claim that exposure of cyclists is higher due to increased minute <a href="/wiki/Ventilation_(physiology)" title="Ventilation (physiology)">ventilation</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> and is associated with minor biological changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> The significance of the associated health effect, if any, is unclear but probably much smaller than the health impacts associated with accidents and the health benefits derived from additional physical activity.</p>
