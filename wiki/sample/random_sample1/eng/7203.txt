<p><b>Cyclohexane</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Cycloalkane" title="Cycloalkane">cycloalkane</a> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_formula" title="Molecular formula">molecular formula</a> C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub> (abbreviated to <b>Cy</b>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Cyclohexane is mainly used for the industrial production of <a href="/wiki/Adipic_acid" title="Adipic acid">adipic acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caprolactam" title="Caprolactam">caprolactam</a>, which are precursors to <a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">nylon</a>. Cyclohexane is a colourless, flammable liquid with a distinctive detergent-like odor, reminiscent of cleaning products (in which it is sometimes used).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campbell2011_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell2011-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclohexane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_production">Modern production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclohexane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modern production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On an industrial scale, cyclohexane is produced by <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Producers of cyclohexane accounts for approximately 11.4% of global demand for benzene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The reaction is highly exothermic, with ΔH(500 K) = 216.37 kJ/mol). Dehydrogenation commenced noticeably above 300 °C, reflecting the favorable entropy for dehydrogenation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_methods">Historical methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclohexane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Historical methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unlike <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a>, cyclohexane is not easily obtained from natural resources such as coal. For this reason, early investigators synthesized their cyclohexane samples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_failures">Early failures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclohexane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early failures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>In 1867 <a href="/wiki/Marcellin_Berthelot" title="Marcellin Berthelot">Marcellin Berthelot</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_reduction" title="Organic reduction">reduced</a> <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a> with <a href="/wiki/Hydroiodic_acid" title="Hydroiodic acid">hydroiodic acid</a> at elevated temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 1870, <a href="/wiki/Adolf_von_Baeyer" title="Adolf von Baeyer">Adolf von Baeyer</a> repeated the reaction<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and pronounced the same reaction product "hexahydrobenzene"</li>
<li>in 1890 <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Markovnikov" title="Vladimir Markovnikov">Vladimir Markovnikov</a> believed he was able to distill the same compound from Caucasus <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>, calling his concoction "hexanaphtene".</li>
</ul>
<p>Surprisingly their cyclohexanes boiled higher by 10°C than either hexahydrobenzene or hexanaphtene but this riddle was solved in 1895 by Markovnikov, <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Kischner" title="Nikolai Kischner">N.M. Kishner</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nikolay_Zelinsky" title="Nikolay Zelinsky">Nikolay Zelinsky</a> when they reassigned "hexahydrobenzene" and "hexanaphtene" as <a href="/wiki/Alkyl_cycloalkane" title="Alkyl cycloalkane">methylcyclopentane</a>, the result of an unexpected <a href="/wiki/Rearrangement_reaction" title="Rearrangement reaction">rearrangement reaction</a>.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Success">Success</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclohexane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Success">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1894 Baeyer synthesized cyclohexane starting with a <a href="/wiki/Dieckmann_condensation" title="Dieckmann condensation">Dieckmann condensation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pimelic_acid" title="Pimelic acid">pimelic acid</a> followed by multiple reductions:</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclohexane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cyclohexane is rather unreactive, being a non-polar, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophobic" title="Hydrophobic">hydrophobic</a> hydrocarbon. It reacts with <a href="/wiki/Superacid" title="Superacid">superacids</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Fluoroantimonic_acid" title="Fluoroantimonic acid">HF + SbF<sub>5</sub></a>, which will lead to <a href="/wiki/Cracking_(chemistry)" title="Cracking (chemistry)">cracking</a>. Substituted cyclohexanes, however, may be reactive under a variety of conditions, many of which are important in organic chemistry.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclohexane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Commercially most of cyclohexane produced is converted into <a href="/wiki/Cyclohexanone" title="Cyclohexanone">cyclohexanone</a>–<a href="/wiki/Cyclohexanol" title="Cyclohexanol">cyclohexanol</a> mixture (or "<i>KA oil</i>") by catalytic oxidation. KA oil is then used as a raw material for <a href="/wiki/Adipic_acid" title="Adipic acid">adipic acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caprolactam" title="Caprolactam">caprolactam</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is used as a solvent in some brands of correction fluid and as a reactant in vacuum carburizing furnaces which are used in metal heat treating by equipment manufacturers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laboratory_uses">Laboratory uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclohexane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Laboratory uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cyclohexane is sometimes used as an organic solvent.</p>
<p>Cyclohexane is also used for calibration of <a href="/wiki/Differential_scanning_calorimetry" title="Differential scanning calorimetry">differential scanning calorimetry</a> (DSC) instruments, because of a convenient crystal-crystal transition at −87.1 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cyclohexane vapour is used in vacuum carburizing furnaces, in heat treating equipment manufacture.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conformation">Conformation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclohexane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Conformation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cyclohexane_conformation" title="Cyclohexane conformation">Cyclohexane conformation</a></div>
<p>The 6-vertex edge ring does not conform to the shape of a perfect <a href="/wiki/Hexagon" title="Hexagon">hexagon</a>. The conformation of a flat 2D planar hexagon has considerable angle strain because its bonds are not 109.5 degrees; the <a href="/wiki/Torsion_(mechanics)" title="Torsion (mechanics)">torsional</a> strain would also be considerable because all of the bonds would be <a href="/wiki/Eclipsed_conformation" title="Eclipsed conformation">eclipsed bonds</a>. Therefore, to reduce <a href="/wiki/Strain_(chemistry)#Torsional_strain" title="Strain (chemistry)">torsional strain</a>, cyclohexane adopts a three-dimensional structure known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chair_conformation" title="Chair conformation">chair conformation</a>. There are also two other intermediate conformers; half chair, which is the most unstable conformer, and twist boat, which is more stable than the boat conformer. This was first proposed as early as 1890 by Hermann Sachse, but only gained widespread acceptance much later. The new conformation puts the carbons at an angle of 109.5°. Half of the hydrogens are in the plane of the ring (<i>equatorial</i>) while the other half are perpendicular to the plane (<i>axial</i>). This conformation allows for the most stable structure of cyclohexane. Another conformation of cyclohexane exists, known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boat_conformation" title="Boat conformation">boat conformation</a>, but it interconverts to the slightly more stable chair formation. If cyclohexane is mono-substituted with a large <a href="/wiki/Substituent" title="Substituent">substituent</a>, then the substituent will most likely be found attached in an equatorial position, as this is the slightly more stable <a href="/wiki/Chemical_structure" title="Chemical structure">conformation</a>.</p>
<p>Cyclohexane has the lowest angle and torsional strain of all the cycloalkanes, as a result cyclohexane has been deemed a 0 in total ring strain.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solid_phases">Solid phases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclohexane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Solid phases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cyclohexane has two crystalline phases. The high-temperature phase I, stable between 186 K and the melting point 280 K, is a <a href="/wiki/Plastic_crystal" title="Plastic crystal">plastic crystal</a>, which means the molecules retain some rotational degree of freedom. The low-temperature (below 186 K) phase II is ordered. Two other low-temperature (metastable) phases III and IV have been obtained by application of moderate pressures above 30 MPa, where phase IV appears exclusively in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deuterated" title="Deuterated">deuterated</a> cyclohexane (note that application of pressure increases the values of all transition temperatures).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phase_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-phase-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<li><span class="reference-text">Bertholet (1868) "Méthode universelle pour réduire et saturer d'hydrogène les composés organiques" (Universal method for reducing and saturating organic compounds with hydrogen), <i>Bulletin de la Société chimique de Paris</i>, series 2, <b>9</b> : 8-31. <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=r1sSAAAAYAAJ&amp;vq=Bertholet&amp;pg=PA17#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">From page 17:</a> <i>"En effet, la benzine, chauffée à 280° pendant 24 heures avec 80 fois son poids d'une solution aqueuse saturée à froid d'acide iodhydrique, se change à peu près entièrement en hydrure d'hexylène, C<sub>12</sub>H<sub>14</sub>, en fixant 4 fois son volume d'hydrogène: C<sub>12</sub>H<sub>6</sub> + 4H<sub>2</sub> = C<sub>12</sub>H<sub>14</sub> … Le nouveau carbure formé par la benzine est un corps unique et défini: il bout à 69°, et offre toutes les propriétés et la composition de l'hydrure d'hexylène extrait des pétroles."</i> (In effect, benzene, heated to 280° for 24 hours with 80 times its weight of an aqueous solution of cold saturated hydroiodic acid, is changed almost entirely into hydride of hexylene, C<sub>12</sub>H<sub>14</sub>, [Note: this formula for hexane (C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>14</sub>) is wrong because chemists at that time used the incorrect atomic mass for carbon.] by fixing [i.e., combining with] 4 times its volume of hydrogen: C<sub>12</sub>H<sub>6</sub> + 4H<sub>2</sub> = C<sub>12</sub>H<sub>14</sub> … The new carbon compound formed by benzene is a unique and well-defined substance: it boils at 69° and presents all the properties and the composition of hydride of hexylene extracted from oil.)</span></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-11"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-11">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Adolf Baeyer (1870) <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=RJU8AAAAIAAJ&amp;pg=PA266#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">"Ueber die Reduction aromatischer Kohlenwasserstoffe durch Jodphosphonium"</a> (On the reduction of aromatic compound by phosphonium iodide [H<sub>4</sub>IP]), <i>Annalen der Chemie und Pharmacie</i>, <b>155</b> : 266-281. From page 279: <i>"Bei der Reduction mit Natriumamalgam oder Jodphosphonium addiren sich im höchsten Falle sechs Atome Wasserstoff, und es entstehen Abkömmlinge, die sich von einem Kohlenwasserstoff C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub> ableiten. Dieser Kohlenwasserstoff ist aller Wahrscheinlichkeit nach ein geschlossener Ring, da seine Derivate, das Hexahydromesitylen und Hexahydromellithsäure, mit Leichtigkeit wieder in Benzolabkömmlinge übergeführt werden können."</i> (During the reduction [of benzene] with sodium amalgam or phosphonium iodide, six atoms of hydrogen are added in the extreme case, and there arise derivatives, which derive from a hydrocarbon C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>. This hydrocarbon is in all probability a closed ring, since its derivatives — hexahydromesitylene [1,3,5 - trimethyl cyclohexane] and hexahydromellithic acid [cyclohexane-1,2,3,4,5,6-hexacarboxylic acid] — can be converted with ease again into benzene derivatives.)</span></li>
