<p>The <b>Battle of Saipan</b> was a battle of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_War" title="Pacific War">Pacific campaign</a> of World War II, fought on the island of <a href="/wiki/Saipan" title="Saipan">Saipan</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Islands" title="Mariana Islands">Mariana Islands</a> from 15 June to 9 July 1944. The Allied invasion fleet embarking the expeditionary forces left <a href="/wiki/Pearl_Harbor" title="Pearl Harbor">Pearl Harbor</a> on 5 June 1944, the day before <a href="/wiki/Operation_Overlord" title="Operation Overlord">Operation Overlord</a> in Europe was launched. The U.S. <a href="/wiki/2nd_Marine_Division_(United_States)" title="2nd Marine Division (United States)">2nd Marine Division</a>, <a href="/wiki/4th_Marine_Division_(United_States)" title="4th Marine Division (United States)">4th Marine Division</a>, and the Army's <a href="/wiki/27th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="27th Infantry Division (United States)">27th Infantry Division</a>, commanded by <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_general_(United_States)" title="Lieutenant general (United States)">Lieutenant General</a> <a href="/wiki/Holland_Smith" title="Holland Smith">Holland Smith</a>, defeated the <a href="/wiki/43rd_Division_(Imperial_Japanese_Army)" title="43rd Division (Imperial Japanese Army)">43rd Infantry Division</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Army" title="Imperial Japanese Army">Imperial Japanese Army</a>, commanded by Lieutenant General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yoshitsugu_Saito" title="Yoshitsugu Saito">Yoshitsugu Saito</a>.</p>

<p>In the campaigns of 1943 and the first half of 1944, the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a> had captured the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Islands" title="Gilbert Islands">Gilbert Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Islands" title="Marshall Islands">Marshall Islands</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Papuan_Peninsula" title="Papuan Peninsula">Papuan Peninsula</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>. This left the Japanese holding the Philippines, the <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Islands" title="Caroline Islands">Caroline Islands</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palau_Islands" title="Palau Islands">Palau Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Islands" title="Mariana Islands">Mariana Islands</a>.</p>
<p>It had always been the intention of the American planners to bypass the Carolines and Palauan islands and to seize the Marianas and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>. From these latter bases, communications between the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_archipelago" title="Japanese archipelago">Japanese archipelago</a> and Japanese forces to the south and west could be cut. From the Marianas, Japan would be well within the range of an air offensive relying on the new <a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-29_Superfortress" title="Boeing B-29 Superfortress">Boeing B-29 Superfortress</a> long-range <a href="/wiki/Bomber" title="Bomber">bomber</a> with its operational radius of 1,500 mi (2,400 km).</p>
<p>While not part of the original American plan, <a href="/wiki/Douglas_MacArthur" title="Douglas MacArthur">Douglas MacArthur</a>, commander of the <a href="/wiki/South_West_Pacific_Area_(command)" title="South West Pacific Area (command)">Southwest Pacific Area command</a>, obtained authorization to advance through New Guinea and <a href="/wiki/Morotai" title="Morotai">Morotai</a> toward the Philippines. This allowed MacArthur to keep his personal pledge to liberate the Philippines, made in his "I shall return" speech, and also allowed the active use of the large forces built up in the southwest Pacific theatre. The Japanese, expecting an attack somewhere on their perimeter, thought an attack on the Caroline Islands most likely. To reinforce and supply their garrisons, they needed naval and air superiority, so <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_A-Go" title="Operation A-Go">Operation A-Go</a>, a major <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">carrier</a> attack, was prepared for June 1944.</p>

<p>The bombardment of Saipan began on 13 June 1944. Fifteen <a href="/wiki/Battleship" title="Battleship">battleships</a> were involved, and 165,000 shells were fired. Seven modern fast battleships delivered 2,400 16 in (410 mm) shells, but to avoid potential minefields, fire was from a distance of 10,000 yd (9,100 m) or more, and crews were inexperienced in shore bombardment. The following day the eight <a href="/wiki/Dreadnought" title="Dreadnought">older</a> battleships and 11 cruisers under Admiral <a href="/wiki/Jesse_B._Oldendorf" title="Jesse B. Oldendorf">Jesse B. Oldendorf</a> replaced the fast battleships but were lacking in time and ammunition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The landings<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> began at 07:00 on 15 June 1944. More than 300 <a href="/wiki/Landing_Vehicle_Tracked" title="Landing Vehicle Tracked">LVTs</a> landed 8,000 Marines on the west coast of Saipan by about 09:00. Eleven fire support ships covered the Marine landings. The naval force consisted of the <a href="/wiki/Battleship" title="Battleship">battleships</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Tennessee_(BB-43)" title="USS Tennessee (BB-43)"><i>Tennessee</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_California_(BB-44)" title="USS California (BB-44)"><i>California</i></a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cruiser" title="Cruiser">cruisers</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Birmingham_(CL-62)" title="USS Birmingham (CL-62)"><i>Birmingham</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_Indianapolis_(CA-35)" title="USS Indianapolis (CA-35)"><i>Indianapolis</i></a>, the <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyers</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USS_Norman_Scott_(DD-690)" title="USS Norman Scott (DD-690)"><i>Norman Scott</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/USS_Monssen_(DD-798)" title="USS Monssen (DD-798)"><i>Monssen</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/USS_Coghlan_(DD-606)" title="USS Coghlan (DD-606)"><i>Coghlan</i></a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USS_Halsey_Powell_(DD-686)" title="USS Halsey Powell (DD-686)"><i>Halsey Powell</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/USS_Bailey_(DD-492)" title="USS Bailey (DD-492)"><i>Bailey</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/USS_Robinson_(DD-562)" title="USS Robinson (DD-562)"><i>Robinson</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_Albert_W._Grant_(DD-649)" title="USS Albert W. Grant (DD-649)"><i>Albert W. Grant</i></a>. Careful artillery preparation — placing flags in the lagoon to indicate the range — allowed the Japanese to destroy about 20 <a href="/wiki/Amphibious_vehicle#Tanks_and_Armored_Vehicles" title="Amphibious vehicle">amphibious tanks</a>, and they strategically placed barbed wire, artillery, machine gun emplacements and trenches to maximize the American casualties. However, by nightfall, the 2nd and 4th Marine Divisions had a <a href="/wiki/Beachhead" title="Beachhead">beachhead</a> about 6 mi (10 km) wide and 0.5 mi (1 km) deep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The Japanese counter-attacked at night but were repulsed with heavy losses. On 16 June, units of the U.S. Army's <a href="/wiki/27th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="27th Infantry Division (United States)">27th Infantry Division</a> landed and advanced on the airfield at Ås Lito (now the location of <a href="/wiki/Saipan_International_Airport" title="Saipan International Airport">Saipan International Airport</a>). Again the Japanese counter-attacked at night. On 18 June, Saito abandoned the airfield.</p>
<p>The invasion surprised the Japanese high command, which had been expecting an attack further south. Admiral <a href="/wiki/Soemu_Toyoda" title="Soemu Toyoda">Soemu Toyoda</a>, Commander-in-Chief of the Japanese Navy, saw an opportunity to use the <i>A-Go</i> force to attack the U.S. Navy forces around Saipan. On 15 June, he gave the order to attack. But the resulting <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Philippine_Sea" title="Battle of the Philippine Sea">battle of the Philippine Sea</a> was a disaster for the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Navy" title="Imperial Japanese Navy">Imperial Japanese Navy</a>, which lost three aircraft carriers and hundreds of planes. The garrisons of the Marianas would have no hope of resupply or reinforcement.</p>
<p>Without resupply, the battle on Saipan was hopeless for the defenders, but the Japanese were determined to fight to the last man. Saito organized his troops into a line anchored on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Tapotchau" title="Mount Tapotchau">Mount Tapotchau</a> in the defensible mountainous terrain of central Saipan. The nicknames given by the Americans to the features of the battle — "Hell's Pocket", "<a href="/wiki/Purple_Heart" title="Purple Heart">Purple Heart</a> Ridge" and "Death Valley" — indicate the severity of the fighting. The Japanese used the many caves in the volcanic landscape to delay the attackers, by hiding during the day and making sorties at night. The Americans gradually developed tactics for clearing the caves by using <a href="/wiki/Flamethrower" title="Flamethrower">flamethrower</a> teams supported by <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">artillery</a> and machine guns.</p>
<p>The operation was marred by <a href="/wiki/Interservice_rivalry" title="Interservice rivalry">inter-service controversy</a> when Marine General <a href="/wiki/Holland_Smith" title="Holland Smith">Holland Smith</a>, unsatisfied with the performance of the 27th Division, relieved its commander, Army Major General <a href="/wiki/Ralph_C._Smith" title="Ralph C. Smith">Ralph C. Smith</a>. However, General Holland Smith had not inspected the terrain over which the 27th was to advance. Essentially, it was a valley surrounded by hills and cliffs under Japanese control. The 27th took heavy casualties and eventually, under a plan developed by General Ralph Smith and implemented after his relief, had one battalion hold the area while two other battalions successfully flanked the Japanese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 7 July, the Japanese had nowhere to retreat. Saito made plans for a final suicidal <a href="/wiki/Banzai_charge" title="Banzai charge">banzai charge</a>. On the fate of the remaining civilians on the island, Saito said, "There is no longer any distinction between civilians and troops. It would be better for them to join in the attack with bamboo spears than be captured." At dawn, with a group of 12 men carrying a great red flag in the lead, the remaining able-bodied troops — about 3,000 men — charged forward in the final attack. Amazingly, behind them came the wounded, with bandaged heads, crutches, and barely armed. The Japanese surged over the American front lines, engaging both army and Marine units. The 1st and 2nd Battalions of the 105th Infantry Regiment were almost destroyed, losing 650 killed and wounded. However, the fierce resistance of these two battalions, as well as that of Headquarters Company, 105th Infantry, and of supply elements of 3rd Battalion, 10th Marine Artillery Regiment, resulted in over 4,300 Japanese killed. For their actions during the 15-hour Japanese attack, three men of the 105th Infantry were awarded the <a href="/wiki/Medal_of_Honor" title="Medal of Honor">Medal of Honor</a> — all posthumously. Numerous others fought the Japanese until they were overwhelmed by the largest Japanese Banzai attack in the Pacific War.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 16:15 on 9 July, Admiral Turner announced that Saipan was officially secured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Saito — along with commanders Hirakushi and Igeta — committed suicide in a cave. Vice-Admiral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chuichi_Nagumo" title="Chuichi Nagumo">Chuichi Nagumo</a>, the naval commander who led the Japanese carriers at <a href="/wiki/Attack_on_Pearl_Harbor" title="Attack on Pearl Harbor">Pearl Harbor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Midway" title="Battle of Midway">Midway</a>, also committed suicide in the closing stages of the battle. He had been in command of the Japanese naval air forces stationed on the island.</p>
<p>In the end, almost the entire garrison of troops on the island — at least 30,000 — died. For the Americans, the victory was the most costly to date in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_War" title="Pacific War">Pacific War</a>: out of 71,000 who landed, 2,949 were killed and 10,464 wounded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-worldatwar.com_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-worldatwar.com-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Future Hollywood actor <a href="/wiki/Lee_Marvin" title="Lee Marvin">Lee Marvin</a> was among the many Americans wounded. He was serving with "I" Company, <a href="/wiki/24th_Marine_Regiment_(United_States)" title="24th Marine Regiment (United States)">24th Marine Regiment</a>, when he was shot in the buttocks by Japanese machine gun fire during the assault on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Tapochau" title="Mount Tapochau">Mount Tapochau</a>. He was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Purple_Heart" title="Purple Heart">Purple Heart</a> and was given a <a href="/wiki/Military_discharge" title="Military discharge">medical discharge</a> with the rank of Private First Class in 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Civilian_casualties">Civilian casualties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Saipan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Civilian casualties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Being a former Spanish and then German territory, Saipan became a Mandate of Japan by the League of Nations after World War I, and thus a large number of Japanese civilians lived there — at least 25,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imperialconspiracy_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-imperialconspiracy-15">[15]</a></sup> The U.S. erected a civilian prisoner encampment on 23 June 1944 that soon had more than 1,000 inmates. Electric lights at the camp were conspicuously left on overnight to attract other civilians with the promise of three warm meals and no risk of accidentally being shot in combat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imperialconspiracy_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-imperialconspiracy-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Weapons and the tactics of close quarter fighting also resulted in high civilian casualties. Civilian shelters were located virtually everywhere on the island, with very little difference noticeable to attacking marines. The standard method of clearing suspected bunkers was with high-explosive and/or high-explosives augmented with petroleum (e.g., gelignite, napalm, diesel fuel). In such conditions, high civilian casualties were inevitable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historian Bergamini assumed that Emperor <a href="/wiki/Hirohito" title="Hirohito">Hirohito</a> personally found the threat of defection by Japanese civilians disturbing. Much of the community was of low caste, and there was a risk that live civilians would be surprised by generous U.S. treatment. Native Japanese sympathizers would hand the Americans a powerful propaganda weapon to subvert the "fighting spirit" of Japan in radio broadcasts. At the end of June, Hirohito sent out an imperial order encouraging the civilians of Saipan to commit suicide. As described by Bergamini, the order authorized the commander of Saipan to promise civilians who died there an equal spiritual status in the afterlife with those of soldiers perishing in combat. General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hideki_T%C5%8Dj%C5%8D" title="Hideki Tōjō">Hideki Tōjō</a> intercepted the order on 30 June and delayed its sending, but it went out anyway the next day. By the time the Marines advanced on the north tip of the island, from 8–12 July, most of the damage had been done.</p>
<p>1,000 Japanese civilians committed suicide in the last days of the battle by fears such as <a href="/wiki/American_mutilation_of_Japanese_war_dead" title="American mutilation of Japanese war dead">American mutilation of Japanese war dead</a>. Some Japanese people living beside the cliff jumped from places which were later named <a href="/wiki/Suicide_Cliff" title="Suicide Cliff">Suicide Cliff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Banzai_Cliff" title="Banzai Cliff">Banzai Cliff</a>. These would become part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark_District" title="National Historic Landmark District">National Historic Landmark District</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landing_Beaches;_Aslito/Isley_Field;_%26_Marpi_Point,_Saipan_Island" title="Landing Beaches; Aslito/Isley Field; &amp; Marpi Point, Saipan Island">Landing Beaches; Aslito/Isley Field; &amp; Marpi Point, Saipan Island</a>, designated in 1985. Today the sites are a memorial and Japanese people visit to console the victim's souls. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhl_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-nhl-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 16 June 1944, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gunnery_Sergeant" title="Gunnery Sergeant">Gunnery Sergeant</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_H._McCard" title="Robert H. McCard">Robert H. <p><b>Seth T. Garner</b></p>
<p>The President of the United States of America takes pleasure in presenting the Silver Star to Private First Class Seth T. Garner (MCSN: 416496), United States Marine Corps Reserve, for conspicuous gallantry and intrepidity while serving as a Group Leader of the First Battalion, Second Marines, SECOND Marine Division, in action against enemy Japanese forces on Saipan, Marianas Islands, 24 June 1944.</p>
<p>With his Battalion in attack, Private First Class Garner led a unit which made contact with a counterattacking force of thirty Japanese. With all save one of his men killed during the ensuing fire fight, he exerted tremendous effort and, although painfully wounded by an enemy grenade, tenaciously held the line at that point. Killing the Japanese officer leading the counterattack, he succeeded in disorganizing the group and in frustrating their attempts to break through out lines, thereby contributing materially to the effectiveness of his Battalion's attack.</p>
<p>Private First Class Garner's courage, initiative and devotion to duty in the face of strong opposition were in keeping with the highest traditions of the United States Naval Service.</p>

When American forces retook the position, they found the pistol, now empty, and eight dead Japanese soldiers around Baker's body. <p>PFC <a href="/wiki/Guy_Gabaldon" title="Guy Gabaldon">Guy Gabaldon</a>, of <a href="/wiki/Headquarters_and_Service_Company" title="Headquarters and Service Company">Headquarters and Service Company</a>, <a href="/wiki/2nd_Marine_Regiment_(United_States)" title="2nd Marine Regiment (United States)">2nd Marine Regiment</a>, <a href="/wiki/2nd_Marine_Division_(United_States)" title="2nd Marine Division (United States)">2nd Marine Division</a>, was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican-American" title="Mexican-American">Mexican-American</a> from Los Angeles. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Saipan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although major fighting had officially ceased on 9 July, pockets of Japanese resistance continued. In September 1944, U.S. Marines began patrols into the island interior in order to bring in civilians and soldiers still holding out in the jungles. A group led by <a href="/wiki/Captain_(armed_forces)" title="Captain (armed forces)">Captain</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sakae_Oba" title="Sakae Oba">Sakae Oba</a> managed to evade capture for more than 512 days until surrendering to American forces on 1 December 1945, three months after the official <a href="/wiki/Surrender_of_Japan" title="Surrender of Japan">surrender of Japan</a>. In February 2011, a film about Oba, <i><a href="/wiki/Oba:_The_Last_Samurai" title="Oba: The Last Samurai">Oba: The Last Samurai</a></i>, was released in Japan.</p>
<p>With the capture of Saipan, the American military was now only 1,300 mi (1,100 nmi; 2,100 km) away from the home islands of Japan. The victory would prove to be one of the most important strategic moments during the war in the Pacific Theater, as the Japanese mainland was now within striking distance of United States' <a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-29_Superfortress" title="Boeing B-29 Superfortress">B-29 bombers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> From this point on, Saipan would become the launch point for retaking other islands in the Mariana chain and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Leyte" title="Battle of Leyte">invasion of the Philippines</a> in October 1944. Four months after capture, more than 100 B-29s from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isely_Field#Role_in_World_War_II" title="Isely Field">Saipan's Isely Field</a> were regularly attacking the Philippines, the <a href="/wiki/Ryukyu_Islands" title="Ryukyu Islands">Ryukyu Islands</a> and the Japan mainland. In response, Japanese aircraft <a href="/wiki/Japanese_air_attacks_on_the_Mariana_Islands" title="Japanese air attacks on the Mariana Islands">attacked Saipan and Tinian</a> on several occasions between November 1944 and January 1945. The U.S. <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Iwo_Jima" title="Battle of Iwo Jima">capture of Iwo Jima</a> (19 February – 26 March 1945) ended further Japanese air attacks.</p>
<p>The loss of Saipan was a heavy blow to both the military and civilian administration of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Prime_Minister" title="Japanese Prime Minister">Japanese Prime Minister</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hideki_T%C5%8Dj%C5%8D" title="Hideki Tōjō">Hideki Tōjō</a>. According to one Japanese admiral: "Our war was lost with the loss of Saipan." U.S. Marine Corps General <a href="/wiki/Holland_Smith" title="Holland Smith">Holland Smith</a> said: "It was the decisive battle of the Pacific offensive [...] it opened the way to the Japanese home islands."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shaw_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shaw-27">[27]</a></sup> Shortly after Saipan was taken, a meeting at the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_General_Headquarters" title="Imperial General Headquarters">Imperial General Headquarters</a> was convened where it was decided that a symbolic change of leadership should be made: Tōjō would step aside and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Hirohito" title="Emperor Hirohito">Emperor Hirohito</a> would have less involvement in day-to-day military affairs, even though he was defined as both <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Generalissimo" title="Generalissimo">Generalissimo of the Imperial Japanese Armed Forces</a> according to the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Constitution" title="Meiji Constitution">Meiji Constitution</a> of 1889. The general staff believed it was now time to distance the <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Japan" title="Emperor of Japan">Imperial family</a> from blame as the tide of war turned against the Japanese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imperialconspiracy_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-imperialconspiracy-15">[15]</a></sup> Although Tōjō agreed to resign, Hirohito blocked his resignation because he considered Tōjō to be Japan's strongest war leader. But after Tōjō failed to shuffle his <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_Japan" title="Cabinet of Japan">Cabinet</a> due to excessive internal hostility, he conceded defeat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imperialconspiracy_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-imperialconspiracy-15">[15]</a></sup> On 18 July, Tōjō again submitted his resignation, this time unequivocally. His entire cabinet resigned with him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Former IJA General <a href="/wiki/Kuniaki_Koiso" title="Kuniaki Koiso">Kuniaki Koiso</a> became Prime Minister on 22 July. However, due to the legacy of Saipan, Koiso was nothing more than a <a href="/wiki/Titular_ruler" title="Titular ruler">titular Prime Minister</a>, and was prevented by the Imperial General Headquarters from participating in any military decisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Saipan also saw a change in the way <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_propaganda_during_World_War_II" title="Japanese propaganda during World War II">Japanese war reporting</a> was presented on the home front. Initially, as the battle started, Japanese accounts concentrated on the fighting spirit of the IJA and the heavy casualties it was inflicting on American forces. However, any reader familiar with Saipan's geography would have known from the chronology of engagements that the U.S. forces were relentlessly advancing northwards. No further mention of Saipan was made following the final battle on 7 July, which was not initially reported to the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> However, after Tōjō's resignation on 18 July, an accurate, almost day-by-day, account of the defeat on Saipan was published jointly by the Army and Navy. It mentioned the near total loss of all Japanese soldiers and civilians on the island and the use of "human bullets". The reports had a devastating effect on Japanese opinion; mass suicides were now seen as defeat, not evidence of an "Imperial Way".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hoyt352_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-hoyt352-31">[31]</a></sup> This was the first time Japanese forces had accurately been depicted in a battle since <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Midway" title="Battle of Midway">Midway</a>, which had been proclaimed a victory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hoyt352_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-hoyt352-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the war concluded, apologists for Hirohito asserted that the order encouraging the civilians of Saipan to commit suicide for benefits in the afterlife had in fact been forged, along with other incriminating orders. Historian <a href="/wiki/David_Bergamini" title="David Bergamini">David Bergamini</a> considers this unlikely, writing that "half the staff of the palace [...] would have felt obliged to <a href="/wiki/Seppuku" title="Seppuku">cut open their bellies</a> if the sacred seals of the Throne had ever been misapplied."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imperialconspiracy_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-imperialconspiracy-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Memorial">Memorial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Saipan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Memorial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Suicide_Cliff" title="Suicide Cliff">Suicide Cliff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Banzai_Cliff" title="Banzai Cliff">Banzai Cliff</a>, along with a number of surviving isolated Japanese fortifications, are recognized as historic sites on the U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>. The cliffs are also part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark_District" title="National Historic Landmark District">National Historic Landmark District</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landing_Beaches;_Aslito/Isley_Field;_%26_Marpi_Point,_Saipan_Island" title="Landing Beaches; Aslito/Isley Field; &amp; Marpi Point, Saipan Island">Landing Beaches; Aslito/Isley Field; &amp; Marpi Point, Saipan Island</a>, which also includes the American landing beaches, the B-29 runways of Isley Field, and the surviving Japanese infrastructure of the Aslito and Marpi Point airfields.</p>
