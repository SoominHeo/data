<p><b>Sakai</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">堺市</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Sakai-shi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Cities_of_Japan" title="Cities of Japan">city</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Osaka_Prefecture" title="Osaka Prefecture">Osaka Prefecture</a>, Japan on the edge of <a href="/wiki/Osaka_Bay" title="Osaka Bay">Osaka Bay</a> at the mouth of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yamato_River&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yamato River (page does not exist)">Yamato River</a>. It has been one of the largest and most important <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seaport" title="Seaport">seaports</a> of Japan since the Medieval era.</p>
<p>Following the February 2005 annexation of the town of <a href="/wiki/Mihara,_Osaka" title="Mihara, Osaka">Mihara</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Minamikawachi_District,_Osaka" title="Minamikawachi District, Osaka">Minamikawachi District</a>), the city has grown further and is now the fourteenth most populous city in Japan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> with 833,414 residents as of May 1, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The current city was legally founded on April 1, 1889 according to the laws of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_Japan" title="Imperial Japan">Imperial Japan</a>. Sakai became a <a href="/wiki/Cities_designated_by_government_ordinance_of_Japan" title="Cities designated by government ordinance of Japan">designated city</a> in April 2006<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> giving it a greater measure of self-determination in governmental affairs. It is divided into seven <a href="/wiki/Wards_of_Japan" title="Wards of Japan">districts</a>.</p>
<p>Sakai is known for its keyhole-shaped burial mounds, or <a href="/wiki/Kofun" title="Kofun">kofun</a>, which date from the 5th century. The largest of these, Daisen Kofun, is believed to be the grave of the <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Nintoku" title="Emperor Nintoku">Emperor Nintoku</a> and is the largest grave in the world by area. Once known for <a href="/wiki/Katana" title="Katana">samurai swords</a>, Sakai is now famous for the quality of its <a href="/wiki/Japanese_cutlery" title="Japanese cutlery">kitchen knives</a>; most high-quality Japanese cutlery originates in Sakai, and its production is a major industry in the city.</p>
<p>As of August 1, 2011, the city has an estimated population of 842,760 and a <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> of 5,600 persons per km<sup>2</sup>. The total area is 149.99 km<sup>2</sup>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sakai,_Osaka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The area that would later become known as Sakai has been inhabited since approximately 8,000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sakai.tcb.or.jp_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-sakai.tcb.or.jp-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the Yamato dynasty between 300 and 500 AD, the <a href="/wiki/Mozu_kofungun" title="Mozu kofungun">Mozu Tumulus Cluster</a> was built from over one hundred burial mounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sakai.tcb.or.jp_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-sakai.tcb.or.jp-5">[5]</a></sup> The name "Sakai" appears in Fujiwara Sadoyori's poetry by 1045.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-city.sakai_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-city.sakai-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tradition holds that 10,000 homes burned to the ground in 1399.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-city.sakai_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-city.sakai-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Medieval Sakai was an autonomous city run by merchant citizens. During the <a href="/wiki/Muromachi_period" title="Muromachi period">Muromachi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sengoku_period" title="Sengoku period">Sengoku periods</a> from about 1450 to 1600, Sakai became one of richest cities in Japan by using its location at the mouth of the Yamato River to connect foreign trade with inland trade to the <a href="/wiki/Yamato_Province" title="Yamato Province">Yamato Province</a>. It was a leading producer of textiles and ironwork.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_and_clark_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-oxford_and_clark-7">[7]</a></sup> In those days, it was said that the richest cities were <i>Umi no Sakai, Riku no Imai</i> (tr. "along the sea, Sakai; inlands, Imai"; the latter is now a part of <a href="/wiki/Kashihara,_Nara" title="Kashihara, Nara">Kashihara, Nara</a>). The famous <a href="/wiki/Zen" title="Zen">Zen</a> Buddhist priest <a href="/wiki/Ikky%C5%AB" title="Ikkyū">Ikkyū</a> chose to live in Sakai because of its free atmosphere.</p>
<p>The first reliable account of the city is dated to the 1480s and contains publicly issued legal notices, which suggests that the city had a governing council at that point. By the 1530s, the population was around 40,000 residents, almost all of which earned a living through commercial enterprises and some of which were the wealthiest people in Japan. At this time, Sakai was administered by an oligarchy of powerful merchants. The government had ten divisions <i>machi</i> that were subordinate to the representative council of wealthy townsmen known as the egoshu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_and_clark_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-oxford_and_clark-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-katsuro_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-katsuro-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sen_no_Riky%C5%AB" title="Sen no Rikyū">Sen no Rikyū</a>, known as the greatest master of the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_tea_ceremony" title="Japanese tea ceremony">tea ceremony</a>, was originally a merchant of Sakai. Because of the close relationship between the tea ceremony and Zen Buddhism and because of the prosperity of its citizens, Sakai was one of the main centers of the tea ceremony in Japan.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Sengoku_period" title="Sengoku period">Sengoku period</a>, some <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> priests, including <a href="/wiki/Francis_Xavier" title="Francis Xavier">Francis Xavier</a> in 1550, visited Sakai and documented its prosperity. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gaspar_Vilela&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gaspar Vilela (page does not exist)">Gaspar Vilela</a> described the town as the safest place in the area when he visited in 1561.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He also mentioned that the city was "governed by consuls like Venice in Italy", although he didn't elaborate on what he meant by "like consuls".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_and_clark_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-oxford_and_clark-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the coming of Europeans, Sakai became a manufacturing base of <a href="/wiki/Firearm" title="Firearm">firearms</a> and a <i><a href="/wiki/Daimy%C5%8D" title="Daimyō">daimyō</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Oda_Nobunaga" title="Oda Nobunaga">Oda Nobunaga</a>, was one of their important customers. During his ambitious attempt to unify Japan, Nobunaga attempted to take the autonomy privilege from Sakai. Sakai's citizens denied his order and pitched a desperate battle against his army. Most citizens fled and Sakai was burned and seized by Nobunaga. After the death of Nobunaga in 1582, one of his men, <a href="/wiki/Toyotomi_Hideyoshi" title="Toyotomi Hideyoshi">Toyotomi Hideyoshi</a>, seized power and abolished the autonomous system of Sakai and reportedly forced the local merchants to move to central Osaka near his stronghold to weaken the powerful business community there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xinhuanet_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-xinhuanet-11">[11]</a></sup> Sakai became a prosperous city again under his reign.</p>
<p>In 1615, Sakai burned to the ground in the Osaka summer battle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-city.sakai_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-city.sakai-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sakai was still an important trade center during the <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a> but was involved only in inland trade due to the <i><a href="/wiki/Sakoku" title="Sakoku">sakoku</a></i> policy of the Tokugawa government. At the end of this era, Westerners again landed in Sakai but it resulted in a tragic incident because the Japanese citizenry and the foreigners were ignorant of each other's ways. French sailors from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FS_Dupleix_(1861)" title="FS Dupleix (1861)"><i>Dupleix</i></a> and Sakai citizens clashed; some French were killed, and subsequently the Japanese responsible for these deaths were sentenced to death by <a href="/wiki/Seppuku" title="Seppuku">seppuku</a>. This incident is called the <a href="/wiki/Sakai_incident" title="Sakai incident">Sakai incident</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">堺事件</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Sakai-jiken</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>.</p>
<p>In modern times, Sakai is an industrial city with a large port. As such, its western area suffered widespread damage from bombing raids during the Second World War. It is now known for its knives and is the home of <a href="/wiki/Shimano" title="Shimano">Shimano</a> bicycle parts. With a population of over 800,000, it is the largest suburb of <a href="/wiki/Osaka" title="Osaka">Osaka City</a> and the fourteenth-largest city in Japan.</p>
