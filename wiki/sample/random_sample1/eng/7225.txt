<p><b>Sachiko Kamachi</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">蒲池 幸子</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Kamachi Sachiko</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, February 6, 1967 – May 27, 2007)</span>, known professionally as <b>Izumi Sakai</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">坂井 泉水</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Sakai Izumi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, was a Japanese <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a> singer, songwriter, and member of the group <a href="/wiki/Zard" title="Zard">Zard</a>. As Sakai was the only member who stayed in the group while others joined and left regularly, Zard and Sakai may be referred to interchangeably. She was the best-selling female recording artist of the 1990s and has sold over 37 million copies of sales, making her one of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_best-selling_music_artists_in_Japan" title="List of best-selling music artists in Japan">best-selling music artists in Japan</a> of all time.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Izumi_Sakai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Kurume,_Fukuoka" title="Kurume, Fukuoka">Kurume</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fukuoka_Prefecture" title="Fukuoka Prefecture">Fukuoka</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, Sakai grew up in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadano" title="Hadano">Hadano</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kanagawa" title="Kanagawa">Kanagawa</a>. Her father was a driving instructor, and she had a younger brother and younger sister. After her death, a neighbor recalled how popular and beautiful Sakai had been in elementary school. She was also athletic, joining the <a href="/wiki/Track_and_field" title="Track and field">track and field</a> team in junior high and playing tennis in high school. Graduating from Shoin Women's College (now <a href="/wiki/Shoin_University" title="Shoin University">Shoin University</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Atsugi,_Kanagawa" title="Atsugi, Kanagawa">Atsugi City</a>, Kanagawa, Sakai worked in a real estate company office for two years before being scouted by <a href="/wiki/Stardust_Promotion" title="Stardust Promotion">Stardust Promotion</a>.</p>
<p>Throughout her life, Sakai remained with her family, living modestly and mostly out of the public eye. Upon achieving career success, she helped pay for her parents home renovation. Acquaintances say that she commuted by subway every day, often wore T-shirts and minimal makeup. She did not wear any makeup in all seven of the television appearances she made in her lifetime. In promoting her third single, "Mō Sagasanai," and first album, <i><a href="/wiki/Good-bye_My_Loneliness_(album)" title="Good-bye My Loneliness (album)">Good-bye My Loneliness</a></i> on February 6, 1991, she wore glasses in the television interview, citing the fact she had not slept the night before. She also indicated that she often slept in the morning rather than the evening.</p>
<p>Sakai had a well-rounded personality. She began playing the piano at age four and aspired to be a musician at a very young age. She visited galleries, attended theater productions, made dry flowers, and painted in oil in her spare time. She also stated that one reason she did not like to travel was that she was not accustomed to eating <a href="/wiki/Sashimi" title="Sashimi">sashimi</a> and preferred cooked food. Because she was hardly ever seen in public, there were widespread conspiracy theories in Japan that works by Zard were not produced by the woman pictured (Sakai): She was referred to as an <a href="/wiki/Urban_legend" title="Urban legend">urban legend</a>.</p>
<p>Sakai appears to have been shy. In her first appearance on <i><a href="/wiki/Music_Station" title="Music Station">Music Station</a></i>, she was asked what took Zard so long to appear on camera. She replied that she wanted to make sure that the Zard project would in fact succeed first. In the other six interviews, Sakai expresses shyness on camera. In fact, a staff member revealed that when Sakai saw so many people lining up for her concert tour in 2004, she was taken aback and hid herself. After some effort, she was able to walk up to the crowd and thank them for coming. However, her shyness did not reflect an inability to work well with others. It has been noted that after she had gone home early one day she arranged for food to be sent to her staff at her office who were working late into the evening.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_career">Professional career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Izumi_Sakai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Professional career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For the next two years following her scouting, she was a <a href="/wiki/Toei_Company" title="Toei Company">Toei</a> "karaoke queen" and a <a href="/wiki/Promotional_model" title="Promotional model">promotional model</a> appearing in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_commercial" title="Television commercial">television commercials</a> for <a href="/wiki/Japan_Air_System" title="Japan Air System">Japan Air System</a>. The following year Sakai was a <a href="/wiki/Nissin_Foods" title="Nissin Foods">Nissin</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_queen" title="Race queen">race queen</a>. During her time as a model she also released <i>Nocturne</i>, a book of photos and <i>Sexy Shooting</i>, a video. In 1990, Taiko Nagato, music producer for Being Corporation, noted her potential as a singer/songwriter. Through this connection, she created a Being subsidiary called Sensui (same <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">Kanji</a> characters as Izumi) and started her career taking the name Izumi Sakai. In addition to taking a new name, Sakai revised her year of birth from 1967 to 1969. However, her earlier career as a model continued to haunt her. As her model books continued to sell in used markets as well as internet auctions, she quietly expressed regret about it. In a rare interview, Sakai said that a major inspiration to her lyrics was her "difficult past."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1991, Sakai joined the five-member pop group Zard as lead vocalist. The group name did not have any particular meaning except Sakai felt that word Zard sounded like a rock group. She also took the name as derived from words such as "blizzard" and "wizard." The group’s name very quickly became synonymous with Sakai herself, and Sakai wrote the <a href="/wiki/Lyrics" title="Lyrics">lyrics</a> to all of Zard's songs except <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Onna_de_Itai&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Onna de Itai (page does not exist)">Onna de Itai</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Koionna_no_Yuuutsu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Koionna no Yuuutsu (page does not exist)">Koionna no Yuuutsu</a>, both of which were written by Daria Kawashima. By 1993, the four male band members left the group but Sakai chose to keep the Zard name throughout her career. Izumi Sakai was Zard’s sole member at the time of the band’s debut, although between late 1991 and early 1993 four other members were introduced.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The melodies of early Zard hits were written by prominent Japanese <a href="/wiki/Composer" title="Composer">composers</a>, most notably Seiichirō Kuribayashi and <a href="/wiki/Tetsur%C5%8D_Oda" title="Tetsurō Oda">Tetsurō Oda</a>. Izumi Sakai wrote nearly all of the lyrics to Zard songs, totalling over one hundred fifty. A veteran recording producer described that while most artists communicate through the transparent glass in the recording studio, Sakai preferred covering the glass with a curtain.</p>
<p>Her 1991 first single, "Good-bye My Loneliness," sold very well, but her next two faltered. The <i>Good-bye My Loneliness</i> promotion video depicts a youthful and energetic Sakai. A decade after her debut, she listed this song as one of her most memorable pieces, especially because she had to sing it over a hundred times to get the recording right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Her fourth single, "Nemurenai Yoru o Daite" (Hold me through the sleepless night) was extremely successful, leading to four television appearances. And her best was still to come.</p>
<p>Izumi Sakai released "<a href="/wiki/Makenaide" title="Makenaide">Makenaide</a>" on January 27, 1993, her sixth single which appealed to the Japanese public. Released at a time that is now seen as the beginning of Japan's post <a href="/wiki/Japanese_asset_price_bubble" title="Japanese asset price bubble">economic bubble era</a> when the <a href="/wiki/Nikkei_225" title="Nikkei 225">Nikkei 225</a> Index had shrunk in value by a third in only three years, "Makenaide" (Don't Give Up) became known as the theme song of the country's <a href="/wiki/Lost_Decade_(Japan)" title="Lost Decade (Japan)">Lost Decade</a>." While Sakai commented on the television show <i>Music Station</i> that it would be a song to encourage men taking college and company employment examinations, many people said this song helped them cope with difficult issues such as school bullying. What is notable about "Makenaide" is that Zard fans’ favorite phrase, "Run through Until the End" was originally "Do Not Give Up until the End". "Makenaide" has been used as a theme song for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nippon_Television" title="Nippon Television">Nippon Television</a> program <i>24-hour TV</i>, an annual charity program hosted live by celebrities for a whole day. Sakai said that she was honored and looked forward to watching <i>24-hour TV.</i> Overall, "Makenaide" sold nearly 2 million copies. Later in 1993 she was ranked the top artist in CD sales and second as a lyricist.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Sakai produced 42 singles as well as 11 albums and 5 compilations in her lifetime. In addition to "Makenaide," she produced two other singles that sold over a million copies. Six of her albums as well as her first three compilations also surpassed the one-million mark, an record. In total CDs sold currently exceeds 30 million making Zard the eighth best-selling artist in Japan.</p>
<p>Since 2000, Sakai’s CD sales had declined but her death triggered an increase in CD sales. For example, her fifth compilation, <i>Golden Best: 15th Anniversary</i>, released in May 2006, had actually slipped to the top 300, but surged to No. 3 in the rankings and became the sixth highest-selling album<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (March 2009)">vague</span></a></i>]</sup> after her death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In the seven days to June 4, 2007, it sold 41,000 copies, a sixtyfold increase from the previous week. As of August 2007, Sakai is ranked fourth in all-time female album sales, just ahead of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hikaru_Utada" title="Hikaru Utada">Hikaru Utada</a>, at 19.2 million copies (see <a href="/wiki/Zard" title="Zard">Zard</a>).</p>
<p>Izumi Sakai sold more CDs than any female vocalist during the 1990s, surpassing even <a href="/wiki/Namie_Amuro" title="Namie Amuro">Namie Amuro</a>. Unlike Amuro, who won two consecutive Japan Record Awards in 1996 and 1997, an record until broken in 2004 by <a href="/wiki/Ayumi_Hamasaki" title="Ayumi Hamasaki">Ayumi Hamasaki</a>, Zard did not win a single award. Sakai may have refused award nominations on the grounds that she did not want to perform live on screen, a requirement to accept them<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>While many of her songs were intended to encourage other people<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, her 42nd and final single, "Heart ni Hi o Tsukete," was different. After Sakai died, a staff member revealed an email in which Sakai wrote that she felt that the song was written to encourage herself.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Significance">Significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Izumi_Sakai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/NHK" title="NHK">NHK</a> program Close Up Gendai reported on June 18, 2007, that the secret to Sakai’s success was that she hardly was seen in public, which created a mystic <a href="/wiki/Aura_(paranormal)" title="Aura (paranormal)">aura</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Izumi_Sakai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite her healthy lifestyle, which included abstaining from tobacco and alcohol, Sakai was seriously ill at times. According to the Kitto Wasurenai Official book, she had to stop her career temporarily due to various uterus-related illnesses in 2001, and did not begin working full-time until 2003. In June 2006, she was diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/Cervical_cancer" title="Cervical cancer">cervical cancer</a>, for which she immediately underwent treatment. She appeared to have healed, but discovered that her cancer had spread to her lungs, indicating a Stage 5 cancer. She began undergoing treatment at <a href="/wiki/Keio_University" title="Keio University">Keio University</a> Hospital in April 2007 but she never fully recovered.</p>
<p>However, Sakai was neither discouraged nor thought she was dying. After her death, the Japanese weekly magazine <i>Friday</i> ran an interview in which said Sakai thought that modern treatments would enable her to live long. Her mother said that she greeted her visitors cheerfully and did not seem to show the effects of her illness. A fellow patient later said that they walked together at times and Sakai sang "Makenaide" for her when she could not walk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Finally, Sakai sent an e-mail to her staff saying that she was anxious to go back to producing music and was looking forward to another concert in late 2007.</p>
<p>Sakai died on 27 May 2007. Police judged her death accidental, the result of a fall from the landing of an <a href="/wiki/Fire_escape" title="Fire escape">emergency-exit slope</a> at Keio University Hospital, where she was undergoing <a href="/wiki/Chemotherapy" title="Chemotherapy">chemotherapy</a>. The slope appeared to be very slippery due to rain the day before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> According to police, the fall took place during a walk on the morning of 26 May 2007, from a height of about 3 meters (about 9 feet and 10 inches). Sakai was discovered unconscious at around 5:40 a.m. by a passer-by and taken to the emergency room, where she died the following afternoon of <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_contusion" title="Cerebral contusion">head injuries</a>. Due to the unusual and unlikely nature of her death, police investigated for possibility of suicide, but concluded that it was indeed an accident. In the Friday article, her mother said that she took walks in rehabilitation and the location where she fell was her favorite place to meditate. Sakai had been planning to release a new album in fall 2007, as well as launch her first live tour in three years. She was 40.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Her family was at her side, but it was reported that she never regained consciousness.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epilogue:_After_Sakai.E2.80.99s_death">Epilogue: After Sakai’s death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Izumi_Sakai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Epilogue: After Sakai’s death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The sudden news of Sakai’s death caused an uproar in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_music" title="Japanese music">Japanese music</a> industry and began to dominate headlines and the "what’s new" spaces on many major music websites. <i><a href="/wiki/Music_Station" title="Music Station">Music Station</a></i>, a TV program, did a four-minute tribute to her during its 1 June 2007 broadcast. Due to viewer request, another tribute was aired a week later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Zard Official Book: Kitto Wasurenai</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">きっと忘れない―ZARD OFFICIAL BOOK</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> was released on August 15, 2007. This book contains tracks of 16 years by "Izumi Sakai's poetry" and "Comments of the staff who have helped ZARD".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The book records that she was informed two days before she died, and that Sakai was encouraged by the news that she was selected. Furthermore, the day before she died, Sakai told a producer who had been with her for 16 years that she was looking forward to have a recording machine at her home so she could start working upon discharge from hospital.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_and_private_memorial_services">Public and private memorial services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Izumi_Sakai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Public and private memorial services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A closed <a href="/wiki/Funeral#Memorial_services" title="Funeral">memorial service</a> was held on 26 June at a funeral hall in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aoyama,_Tokyo" title="Aoyama, Tokyo">Aoyama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> for members of the entertainment industry. This was attended by celebrities such as <a href="/wiki/Maki_Ohguro" title="Maki Ohguro">Maki Ohguro</a> (another female vocalist who, like Sakai, rarely appears in public and writes most of her own material). Almost as if to illustrate Sakai’s impact on the Japanese music scene and the depth of her presence, singers <a href="/wiki/Tak_Matsumoto" title="Tak Matsumoto">Tak Matsumoto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Koshi_Inaba" title="Koshi Inaba">Koshi Inaba</a>, members of the popular <a href="/wiki/B%27z" title="B'z">B'z</a> group, pop-singer <a href="/wiki/Mai_Kuraki" title="Mai Kuraki">Mai Kuraki</a>, and even baseball giant <a href="/wiki/Shigeo_Nagashima" title="Shigeo Nagashima">Shigeo Nagashima</a> all left moving messages of their encounters with Sakai.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Singers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hikaru_Utada" title="Hikaru Utada">Hikaru Utada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nanase_Aikawa" title="Nanase Aikawa">Nanase Aikawa</a>, though not personally acquainted with Sakai, also issued memorial statements on their official web pages, describing how Sakai’s death had shocked them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>A public memorial service for Sakai was held the next day and was attended by some 40,000 people from all over Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="What_a_Beautiful_Memory_concert_tours">What a Beautiful Memory concert tours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Izumi_Sakai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: What a Beautiful Memory concert tours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A series of memorial concerts were held at <a href="/wiki/Osaka" title="Osaka">Osaka</a>’s Festival on September 6 and 7, as well as September 14 in Tokyo’s <a href="/wiki/Nippon_Budokan" title="Nippon Budokan">Nippon Budokan</a>, called <i>What a Beautiful Memory</i>. Tickets sold out immediately and 15,000 people gathered for the Tokyo event. Sakai’s favorite microphone was placed center-stage, and a recording of Sakai's comments about her thoughts toward the lyrics from 2004 was played.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Over 20 members of Sakai’s band, who had come together again just for this occasion, began playing "<a href="/wiki/Yureru_Omoi" title="Yureru Omoi">Yureru Omoi</a>". During the intermezzo, video images of the dressing room were shown, showing how staff had set it up in the same way Sakai used it during her What a Beautiful Moment concert tour. The door was labeled "Ms. Sakai Izumi" and the room had a clipboard displaying the day’s schedule; lunch boxes were also prepared and laid out on a coffee table.</p>
<p>The band went on to perform 34 songs, ending with "Makenaide." When "Makenaide" ended, Sakai’s recorded voice was played back to the sold-out crowd: "Thank you for coming today. I look forward to seeing you all again!" A portion of the proceeds from the concerts were donated to help fund cervical cancer research.</p>
<p>On the stage, nine giant screens showed more previously unreleased off-screen footage of Sakai, excerpts from 10,000 <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">VHS</a> tape recordings of Sakai in off-screen footage that her staff discovered after her death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In the encore of the memorial concerts, the music staff displayed some 300 songs in notebooks hand-written by Sakai that were found after she died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Posthumous_single:_.22Glorious_Mind.22">Posthumous single: "Glorious Mind"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Izumi_Sakai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title='Edit section: Posthumous single: "Glorious Mind"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The audience was in awe on learning that Sakai had produced one unreleased song before she died, "<a href="/wiki/Glorious_Mind" title="Glorious Mind">Glorious Mind</a>." She has been able to record just the chorus in December 2006 despite being in cancer treatment at the time, and Sakai’s mother is reported<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (February 2009)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> to have accompanied her to the recording studio. PVTV<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (February 2009)">vague</span></a></i>]</sup>, in a three-minute report on Zard in November 2007, also reported that Sakai’s physical weakness had only allowed her to record her singing a handful of times, but successfully passed the trials<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (February 2009)">vague</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>On October 19, 2007, it was announced<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (December 2007)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> that "Glorious Mind" would be released as Sakai’s first posthumous CD single<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (February 2009)">vague</span></a></i>]</sup> on December 12, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The song will be used as the theme song of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detective_Conan" title="Detective Conan">Detective Conan</a></i>, a Japanese anime that was Sakai’s favorite. The song will be broadcast with the episode airing on October 15.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> This is the seventh time that a song by Sakai has been chosen as the theme for <i>Detective Conan</i>. The single will contain rearranged versions of "Ai o Shinjiteiru" and "Sagashi ni Ikou yo."</p>
<p>Such is Sakai’s popularity that, according to Oricon, "Glorious Mind" was the best-selling single of the day on the first six days of its release, falling to third place on the seventh day.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="What_a_Beautiful_Memory_Concert_Tour_2008">What a Beautiful Memory Concert Tour 2008</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Izumi_Sakai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: What a Beautiful Memory Concert Tour 2008">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sakai’s office announced that there will be a nationwide tour to follow the What a Beautiful Memory tour. Announced on November 16, 2007, through Zard’s official website, it will consist of 15 concerts at 13 locations in early 2008. The first concert will be at <a href="/wiki/Kobe" title="Kobe">Kobe</a>’s International Forum on January 19 and the final one will commemorate the first anniversary of Sakai’s death at the <a href="/wiki/Yoyogi_National_Gymnasium" title="Yoyogi National Gymnasium">Yoyogi National Gymnasium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Yoyogi" title="Yoyogi">Yoyogi</a> on May 27. None of the concerts will take place at the <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_International_Forum" title="Tokyo International Forum">Tokyo International Forum</a>, where the "What a Beautiful Moment" DVD was mainly recorded, or at <a href="/wiki/Nippon_Budokan" title="Nippon Budokan">Nippon Budokan</a>. Additional previously unreleased footage of Sakai will be shown throughout the tour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zard_after_Sakai.27s_death">Zard after Sakai's death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Izumi_Sakai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Zard after Sakai's death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (October 2007)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> speculate that an artist like Sakai might be chosen in the future to produce those songs. Months prior to the NHK's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C5%8Dhaku_Utagassen" title="Kōhaku Utagassen">Kōhaku Utagassen</a></i>, entertainment correspondents of conservative newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Yomiuri_Shimbun" title="Yomiuri Shimbun">Yomiuri</a></i> speculated that singer <a href="/wiki/Mai_Kuraki" title="Mai Kuraki">Mai Kuraki</a>, who was in the same management office, was likely to sing a Zard song.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
