<p><b>Zacatecas</b> (<small>Spanish pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Spanish" title="Help:IPA for Spanish">[sakaˈtekas]</a></span>), officially the <b>Free and Sovereign State of Zacatecas</b> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <span lang="es"><i>Estado Libre y Soberano de Zacatecas</i></span>), is one of the 31 states which, with the <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Federal District</a>, comprise the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_divisions_of_Mexico" title="Political divisions of Mexico">32 Federal Entities</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. It is divided in <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_of_Zacatecas" title="Municipalities of Zacatecas">58 municipalities</a> and its capital city is <a href="/wiki/Zacatecas_City" title="Zacatecas City">Zacatecas City</a>.</p>
<p>Zacatecas is located in <a href="/wiki/North-Central_Mexico" title="North-Central Mexico">North-Central Mexico</a>. It is bordered by the states of <a href="/wiki/Durango" title="Durango">Durango</a> to the northwest, <a href="/wiki/Coahuila" title="Coahuila">Coahuila</a> to the north, <a href="/wiki/Nayarit" title="Nayarit">Nayarit</a> to the west, <a href="/wiki/San_Luis_Potos%C3%AD" title="San Luis Potosí">San Luis Potosí</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuevo_Le%C3%B3n" title="Nuevo León">Nuevo León</a> to the east, and <a href="/wiki/Jalisco" title="Jalisco">Jalisco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guanajuato" title="Guanajuato">Guanajuato</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aguascalientes" title="Aguascalientes">Aguascalientes</a> to the south.</p>
<p>The state is best known for its rich deposits of <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minerals" title="Minerals">minerals</a>, its <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Colonial_architecture" title="Spanish Colonial architecture">colonial architecture</a> and its importance during the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Revolution" title="Mexican Revolution">Mexican Revolution</a>. Its main economic activities are <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_environment">Geography and environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zacatecas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography and environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Zacatecas is located in the <a href="/wiki/North-Central_Mexico" title="North-Central Mexico">center-north of Mexico</a>, and covers an area of 75,284km2, the tenth largest state in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-resumen_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-resumen-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-territorio_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-territorio-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aspectos_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-aspectos-11">[11]</a></sup> It borders the states of <a href="/wiki/Jalisco" title="Jalisco">Jalisco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aguascalientes" title="Aguascalientes">Aguascalientes</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Luis_Potos%C3%AD" title="San Luis Potosí">San Luis Potosí</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coahuila" title="Coahuila">Coahuila</a> and <a href="/wiki/Durango" title="Durango">Durango</a> and is divided into fifty eight municipalities and 4,882, towns, cities and other communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aspectos_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-aspectos-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>The state has an average altitude of 2230 meters above sea level, with the capital at 2,496 masl.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aspectos_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-aspectos-11">[11]</a></sup> The state has three main geographical regions, the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Madre_Occidental" title="Sierra Madre Occidental">Sierra Madre Occidental</a> in the west, the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Plateau" title="Mexican Plateau">Mexican Plateau</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Madre_Oriental" title="Sierra Madre Oriental">Sierra Madre Oriental</a>. Most of it is in the Sierra Madre Occidental with highly rugged with peaks of over 2,500 meters above sea level. The mountains of the southeast and northeast are lower but there large valleys such as the Juchipila and Tlaltenango. Most of the territory has only small mesas and other areas of flat land. In the center of the state, there is a small mountain chain called the Sierra de Fresnillo, from which much of the state’s mineral wealth comes from. In the extreme northwest, there is another important mountain chain called the Sierra de Sombrerete, marked by a mountain called Sombreretillo, which is an important source of mineral wealth. Near this chain is another called the Sierra de Órganos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arroyo_en_Sierra_de_Cardos.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2760" data-file-width="4912" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Arroyo_en_Sierra_de_Cardos.jpg/220px-Arroyo_en_Sierra_de_Cardos.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Arroyo_en_Sierra_de_Cardos.jpg/330px-Arroyo_en_Sierra_de_Cardos.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Arroyo_en_Sierra_de_Cardos.jpg/440px-Arroyo_en_Sierra_de_Cardos.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>No major rivers run through the state and most of the waterways run only during the rainy season. The state belongs to two basins. The south east of the state belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Lerma_River" title="Lerma River">Lerma River</a> basin, which eventually empties in the Pacific Ocean. Rivers belonging to this basin include the San Pedro, Juchipila, Jerez and Tlaltenango. The other basin is smaller and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endorheic" title="Endorheic">endorheic</a>, and does not empty into any ocean. The state has eighty dams with a total capacity of 595,337 million cubic meters. The largest of these are the Leobardo Reynoso in <a href="/wiki/Fresnillo" title="Fresnillo">Fresnillo</a>, Miguel Aleman in <a href="/wiki/Tlaltenango_de_S%C3%A1nchez_Rom%C3%A1n_Municipality" title="Tlaltenango de Sánchez Román Municipality">Tlaltenango</a> and El Chique in <a href="/wiki/Tabasco,_Zacatecas" title="Tabasco, Zacatecas">Tabasco</a>. Much of the state’s water is underground divided into twenty hydraulic zones. These are accesses with over 5,800 wells mostly for agricultural use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of the territory has a cool, dry climate, although areas in the south have more moisture, with most rain falling between June and September. The driest and coldest areas are in the northeast, known as the Salado because of its saltwater lakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aspectos_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-aspectos-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup> 75% of the state is arid or semi arid. 14% is arable and 79% is apt for the grazing of livestock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The average annual temperature is 16C with most of the state being temperate. The coldest months are from November to January, with frost not uncommon. The warmest month is June. The state gets an average rainfall of 400mm per year mostly in the summer, with the warmest and wettest part of the state is along the Sierra Madre Occidental.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aspectos_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-aspectos-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>Ecosystems vary depending on relief, soil and temperature, leading to a wide variety of vegetation, including forests, scrub and grasslands. Arid areas are dominated by various species of cactus. In the far south, there are deciduous trees that lose their leaves in winter and spring. Statewide the most common trees are mesquite, ironwood and palo verde (<a href="/wiki/Parkinsonia" title="Parkinsonia">Parkinsonia</a>). In the highest altitude, near the Jalisco border, there are mixed forests of pine and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holm_oak" title="Holm oak">holm oak</a>, with the latter dominating along the border with Durango and some along the border with San Luis Potosí.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-6"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup> One interesting tree that occurs in Zacatecas is the elephant tree (<i><a href="/wiki/Bursera_microphylla" title="Bursera microphylla">Bursera microphylla</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In the sierras there are many <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">wild boar</a>, <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_deer" title="White-tailed deer">white-tailed deer</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hares" title="Hares">hares</a>; in the valleys and plains it is common to find <a href="/wiki/Coyote" title="Coyote">coyote</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Badgers" title="Badgers">badgers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quails" title="Quails">quails</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ducks" title="Ducks">ducks</a>. The extreme northern part of the state is the southern fringe of the <a href="/wiki/Chihuahuan_Desert" title="Chihuahuan Desert">Chihuahuan Desert</a> and as such is rich and diverse in biology. This desert is home to a large amount of cacti and is one of the most ecologically diverse deserts on earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zacatecas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The state name derives from the name of its capital, Zacatecas. This word is derived from Nahuatl and means “where there is abundant zacate (grass)” .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-7"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pasado_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-pasado-15">[15]</a></sup> The state seal depicts the Cerro de la Bufa, a landmark of the capital, surrounded by the weapons of the original inhabitants. Above is the motto “Work conquers all.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-8"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hiscom_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-hiscom-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>Before the arrival of the Spanish, dominant ethnic groups included the <a href="/wiki/Caxcan" title="Caxcan">Caxcans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zacateco" title="Zacateco">Zacatecos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Guachichil" title="Guachichil">Guachichils</a>, with a probable rivalry between the Guchichils and the Caxcans. The history of these peoples is sketchy and it is not known when the first settlements were founding in the region. Between the fourth and tenth centuries in the Christian era, several large settlements developed such as Altavista, Chalchihuites and La Quemada, considered to be part of Greater <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</a> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hiscom_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-hiscom-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pasado_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-pasado-15">[15]</a></sup> Areas in the north of the state, without major settlements was part of what is called <a href="/wiki/Aridoamerica" title="Aridoamerica">Aridoamerica</a>, where inhabitants lived off hunting and gathering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-9"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup> The first of the major population centers emerged along the Suchil, Graceros and Guadiana Rivers. The archeological sites of today are all ceremonial centers and/or observatories in the center of metropolises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-10"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first settlement in the state’s current borders was in what is now Nochistlan in 1531, the original <a href="/wiki/Guadalajara" title="Guadalajara">Guadalajara</a>. This settlement was later moved to its current location in Jalisco because of water supply problems and indigenous attacks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-11"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup> The capital was founded by <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Tolosa" title="Juan de Tolosa">Juan de Tolosa</a> with the support of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cristobal_de_O%C3%B1ate" title="Cristobal de Oñate">Cristobal de Oñate</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_Almendez_Chirinos&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pedro Almendez Chirinos (page does not exist)">Pedro Almendez Chirinos</a> in 1546, after the discovery of one of the world’s richest silver veins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-12"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hiscom_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-hiscom-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bakewell_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bakewell-17">[17]</a></sup> However, shortly afterwards, the most Spanish attention turned back south because of indigenous uprisings. The area remained dangerous for Spanish settlement because of the fierce opposition of the native peoples. In 1541, an indigenous leader named <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Tenamaztle" title="Francisco Tenamaztle">Tenamextle, also known as Francisco Tenamaztle and Diego the Aztec</a>, rebelled, capturing and executing Spanish leader Miguel de Ibarra. The Spanish defeated the Caxcans during the <a href="/wiki/Mixt%C3%B3n_War" title="Mixtón War">Mixtón War</a> in the 1540s. Tenamextle escaped the battle and continued to organized rebellions against the Spanish. However, the Spanish continued to push into Zacatecas because of its silver wealth, making it a province of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Galicia" title="New Galicia">New Galicia</a>. Although able to establish mining towns, convoys transporting the metal were regularly attacked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hiscom_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-hiscom-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>Much of the state’s colonial history to the present has been related to its mineral production, especially of silver. The first boom was from the Conquest to the mid 17th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hiscom_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-hiscom-16">[16]</a></sup> The riches drew settlers from the south and in 1586, Phillip II gave the city the name of Noble and Loyal City of Nuestra Señora de los Zacatecas. In 1588, he authorized its coat of arms. Most of the state was evangelized by the Franciscans, who founded a hospice in the city in 1558 and by 1567 had built a large monastery. They officially took possession of its religious functions in 1603. Later other orders arrived, founding monasteries but they did not evangelize the indigenous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-13"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The next boom was in the early 18th century, with the state producing one-fifth of the world’s silver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hiscom_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-hiscom-16">[16]</a></sup> These riches supported the establishment of new settlements along with the building of elegant churches and mansions as the area became one of the most important of <a href="/wiki/New_Spain" title="New Spain">New Spain</a> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pasado_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-pasado-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_War_of_Independence" title="Mexican War of Independence">Mexican War of Independence</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miguel_Hidalgo" title="Miguel Hidalgo">Miguel Hidalgo</a>’s troops marched through Zacatecas twice, once when they were attacking royalist troops and later when fleeing them. The war ended in 1821 and Zacatecas formally became a state in 1824, with the city of Zacatecas as its capital, and this city continued to grow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-14"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hiscom_16-6"><a href="#cite_note-hiscom-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The state’s history during the most of the 19th century was tumultuous, along with the rest of the country. From Independence until the 1860s, Liberal and Conservative elements occupied the capital at one time or another, until Liberal leader Jesús González Ortega seized control of the state permanently in 1859. This leader’s decrees against Conservative sympathizers drove many Catholic priests out of the state. In 1861, French troops occupied Zacatecas but only for two years before being driven out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hiscom_16-7"><a href="#cite_note-hiscom-16">[16]</a></sup> For the rest of the century, the state was mostly controlled by local strongmen, such as González Ortega, Trinidad García de la Cadena and Genero Codina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-15"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup> The fighting depressed silver production until near the end of the century, but it recovered enough to account for sixty percent of the state’s export revenue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hiscom_16-8"><a href="#cite_note-hiscom-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>At the end of the century, technological innovations such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telegraph" title="Telegraph">telegraph</a>, telephone, electricity and rail lines connected the state with the rest of Mexico. Trains provided direct links to <a href="/wiki/Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez" title="Ciudad Juárez">Ciudad Juárez</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aguascalientes,_Aguascalientes" title="Aguascalientes, Aguascalientes">Aguascalientes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chihuahua,_Chihuahua" title="Chihuahua, Chihuahua">Chihuahua</a>, which led to emigration out of the state, primarily to the United States in the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-16"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hiscom_16-9"><a href="#cite_note-hiscom-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Zacatecas was again a battleground with the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Revolution" title="Mexican Revolution">Mexican Revolution</a> in the early 20th century. One of the largest and most decisive battle of this conflict took place outside the capital and is called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toma_de_Zacatecas" title="Toma de Zacatecas">Toma de Zacatecas</a> (Taking of Zacatecas). This battle pitted the troops of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francisco_Villa" title="Francisco Villa">Francisco Villa</a> against those of <a href="/wiki/Victoriano_Huerta" title="Victoriano Huerta">Victoriano Huerta</a>, resulting in the deaths of 7,000 soldiers and the wounding of 5,000. Civilian casualties were not recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hiscom_16-10"><a href="#cite_note-hiscom-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pasado_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-pasado-15">[15]</a></sup> The battle led to the naming of the city as a “Heróica Ciudad” (Heroic City).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-17"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1993, <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> named the historic center of Zacatecas as a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-18"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1998-2004, the state undertook a major project to expand the highway system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-19"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>As of 2010, the state had a population of 1,490,668. Forty one percent of the population lives in rural areas, with a population density of 18,13 per square kilometer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-20"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-densidad_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-densidad-20">[20]</a></sup> Fifty nine percent of the population lives in urban areas such as Fresnillo (pop. 213,139), <a href="/wiki/Guadalupe,_Zacatecas" title="Guadalupe, Zacatecas">Guadalupe</a> (159,991), Zacatecas, (138,176) along with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinos,_Zacatecas" title="Pinos, Zacatecas">Pinos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sombrerete,_Zacatecas" title="Sombrerete, Zacatecas">Sombrerete</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-resumen_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-resumen-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hiscom_16-11"><a href="#cite_note-hiscom-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-distri_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-distri-21">[21]</a></sup> Ninety four percent of the population is Roman Catholic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-diversidad_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-diversidad-22">[22]</a></sup> In the year 2008, Zacatecas had the smallest indigenous population percentage-wise in Mexico: 0.3%. Only the state of <a href="/wiki/Aguascalientes" title="Aguascalientes">Aguascalientes</a> has a smaller number of indigenous people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Only four in 1,000 speak an indigenous language, compared to the national average of 60/1000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-resumen_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-resumen-9">[9]</a></sup> Indigenous languages spoken in the state include <a href="/wiki/Huichol_language" title="Huichol language">Huichol</a> (1000 speakers), <a href="/wiki/Nahuatl" title="Nahuatl">Nahuatl</a> (500), <a href="/wiki/Tepehu%C3%A1n_language" title="Tepehuán language">Tepehuan</a> (just under 500) and <a href="/wiki/Tlapanec_language" title="Tlapanec language">Tlapanec</a> (about 400).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-diversidad_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-diversidad-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Zacatecas has more than tripled in a century, in 1900 its population was 462,190.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Contreras.2C_Joseph_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Contreras.2C_Joseph-25">[25]</a></sup> Since 1990, the state’s population has grown by at least 1.3% per year. Average life expectancy is slightly above the national average at 74.1 years for men and 78.5 for women. Principal causes of death are heart problems, malign tumors and diabetes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dinamica_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-dinamica-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The average number of years of schooling is 7.9 (second year of middle school), below the national average of 8.6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-resumen_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-resumen-9">[9]</a></sup> 5.9% have had no schooling at all and 66.8% have finished primary school. Only 12.3% have finished university level studies. 6% are illiterate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-educuenta_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-educuenta-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of those who leave the state permanently, most go to Aguascalientes, Jalisco and other northern states. Those who come to live in the state arrive from Jalisco, Aguacalientes and nearby northern states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-movimientos_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-movimientos-28">[28]</a></sup> It is estimated that half of the people from Zacatecas do not reside in the state. Mexico's National Population Council estimates that 600,000 natives of Zacatecas now live in the United States, a figure that is equivalent to 40 percent of the state's resident population of 1.5 million.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zacatecas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>As in the past, the state’s dominant sector is mining, accounting for 13% of the state’s GDP and .9% of the entire country’s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-resumen_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-resumen-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-econcuenta_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-econcuenta-29">[29]</a></sup> The state is rich in mineral wealth include <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> and copper with small quantities of gold and silver, along with non-metal mineral deposits such as <a href="/wiki/Kaolinite" title="Kaolinite">kaolinite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wollastonite" title="Wollastonite">wollastonite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fluorite" title="Fluorite">fluorite</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barite" title="Barite">barite</a>. The state has fifteen mining districts of which the most important are Fresnillo, Zacatecas, Concepción del Oro, Sombrerete and Chalchihuites, along with Nora de Angeles more recently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-21"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hiscom_16-12"><a href="#cite_note-hiscom-16">[16]</a></sup> Zacatecas accounts for 21% of the country’s gold production and 53.2% of its silver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brillan_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-brillan-30">[30]</a></sup> Two of the largest silver mines in the world currently are operated in Zacatecas, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fresnillo-Zacatecas_Silver_Mine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fresnillo-Zacatecas Silver Mine (page does not exist)">Fresnillo-Zacatecas Silver Mine</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pe%C3%B1asquito_Polymetallic_Mine" title="Peñasquito Polymetallic Mine">Peñasquito Polymetallic Mine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Zacatecas silver mostly accounts for Mexico’s status as the world’s largest producer of silver, accounting for 17% of the world’s output.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hiscom_16-13"><a href="#cite_note-hiscom-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Zacatecas’s economy used to be almost completely centered on mining but has since diversified into cattle raising, agriculture, communications, food processing, tourism and transportation. Zacatecas is Mexico’s main producer of beans, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chili_peppers" title="Chili peppers">chili peppers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guava" title="Guava">guavas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nopal" title="Nopal">nopal</a>, along with significant grain, sugar cane, grape and peach crops. It is also a major producer of rum, <a href="/wiki/Pulque" title="Pulque">pulque</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mezcal" title="Mezcal">mezcal</a> and even produces red wine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hiscom_16-14"><a href="#cite_note-hiscom-16">[16]</a></sup> These activities account for just over ten percent of the state’s GDP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-econcuenta_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-econcuenta-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Manufacturing accounts for over twelve percent of the state’s GDP and has attracted most of the state’s foreign investment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-econcuenta_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-econcuenta-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-econgob_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-econgob-32">[32]</a></sup> Traditional handcrafts include weaving in <a href="/wiki/Villa_Garcia" title="Villa Garcia">Villa Garcia</a>, saddles and jewelry in <a href="/wiki/Jerez_de_Garc%C3%ADa_Salinas" title="Jerez de García Salinas">Jerez</a> as well as furniture, leatherworking, miniatures, <a href="/wiki/Macram%C3%A9" title="Macramé">macramé</a>, ironwork and pottery in various locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-22"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tourism includes the capital along with the designation of “<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pueblos_M%C3%A1gicos" title="Pueblos Mágicos">Pueblos Mágicos</a>” such as Jerez, Teul de Gonzalez Ortega and Sombrerete, along with the shrine of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santo_Ni%C3%B1o_de_Atocha#Mexico" title="Santo Niño de Atocha">Santo Niño de Atocha</a>, which is visited by thousands every year. It also includes archeological sites such as <a href="/wiki/Altavista_(Zacatecas)" title="Altavista (Zacatecas)">Alta Vista</a> and <a href="/wiki/La_Quemada" title="La Quemada">La Quemada</a> along with thermal springs such as Paraíso Caxcan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-23"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Commerce and services accounts for over 53% of the GDP, mostly small scale operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-econcuenta_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-econcuenta-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture.2C_festivals.2C_and_traditions">Culture, festivals, and traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zacatecas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Culture, festivals, and traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Troupe_Mexique_07854.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="4912" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Troupe_Mexique_07854.JPG/220px-Troupe_Mexique_07854.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Troupe_Mexique_07854.JPG/330px-Troupe_Mexique_07854.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Troupe_Mexique_07854.JPG/440px-Troupe_Mexique_07854.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Troupe_Mexique_07854.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Zacatecan <i>Danza de <a href="/wiki/Matachines" title="Matachines">Matachines</a></i></div>

<p>Most of the state’s festivities are in honor of local patron saints and many of the secular festivals have links to religious ones. Such festivals often focus on recitals of traditional dances such as the Mexicapan. Many of these are derived from waltzes and polkas because of the state’s mining history. The Mexican Revolution took a toll on the state’s local musical traditions, but one that has survived is the Tamborazo, especially in Jerez.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-24"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the state capital, September 8 is dedicated to the Virgen del Patrocinio on the Cerro de la Bufa, with various cultural and artistic events such as bullfighting, concerts, horse racing and culinary demonstrations. The last week of August is dedicated to the Morismas de Bracho, a theatrical production of the struggle between Moors and Christians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-25"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>It hosts the International Folklore Festival in August, featuring dance and costumes from around the world. It is held during <a href="/wiki/Holy_Week_in_Mexico" title="Holy Week in Mexico">Holy Week</a>, and features music, food, street performances, dancing and parties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hiscom_16-15"><a href="#cite_note-hiscom-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other major festivals include the Festival Cultural Zacatecas, the Feria Nacional de Zacatecas and the Internacional Festival de Teatro de Calle, Feria de Primavera de Jerez, the Feria del Libro, and the Cabalgata Turistica Revolucionaria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ferias_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-ferias-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Traditional favorite foods include <a href="/wiki/Gordita" title="Gordita">gorditas</a> and panecillos, both made from corn and can be sweet or savory, depending on the filling. Wheat breads include panochas and semitas. Condoches are gorditas made with fresh corn cooked in corn husks. Gorditas de cuajada are representative of food on ranches. Meat is most typically prepared as part of a stew to which vegetables such as corn, chickpeas, squash, rice and more are added. One well-known meat preparation is asado de boda, which is pork in a sauce made with mild red chili peppers. Traditional beverages include <a href="/wiki/Pulque" title="Pulque">pulque</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aguamiel" title="Aguamiel">aguamiel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aguardiente" title="Aguardiente">aguardiente</a> and mezcal as well as a purely local beverage called <a href="/wiki/Colonche" title="Colonche">colonche</a>, made by fermenting a cactus fruit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-26"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archeological_sites">Archeological sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zacatecas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Archeological sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:La_Quemada,_Zacatecas.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/La_Quemada%2C_Zacatecas.jpg/220px-La_Quemada%2C_Zacatecas.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/La_Quemada%2C_Zacatecas.jpg/330px-La_Quemada%2C_Zacatecas.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/La_Quemada%2C_Zacatecas.jpg/440px-La_Quemada%2C_Zacatecas.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:La_Quemada,_Zacatecas.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
View of the Salón de las Columnas in La Quemada.</div>
</div>

<p>Altavista is located 229 km northwest of the city of Zacatecas. It was a ceremonial center, part of the Chalchihuite culture, active between 200 and 1000 CE. It is named after a local ranch, and was explored for the first time at the beginning of the 20th century by Manuel Gamio. Its main building is called the Labyrinth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-argob_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-argob-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>La Quemada is located fifty km south of the city of Zacatecas, the state’s largest pre Hispanic settlement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmuc_12-27"><a href="#cite_note-encmuc-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hiscom_16-16"><a href="#cite_note-hiscom-16">[16]</a></sup> It developed between 500 and 900 CE and covered an area of over 70,000m2 at its height. Its name, which means “the burnt” comes from evidence that the city was burned and abandoned. Who occupied the city is not known, with speculation relating to Teotihuacan, the Purépecha and the Toltecs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-argob_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-argob-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>El Teúl is on a large hill overlooking the modern town of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Te%C3%BAl_de_Gonz%C3%A1lez_Ortega&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Teúl de González Ortega (page does not exist)">Teúl de González Ortega</a>. The name comes from Nahuatl and means “of the gods.” It was a ceremonial center, with residences located north of it. The site is noted for its pit burials as well as the oldest copper smelting facility in Mesoamerica<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. It was inhabited from 200 CE to 1531, when the Spanish destroyed it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-argob_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-argob-34">[34]</a></sup> It is one of several religious and population centers created by the Caxcans, who were semi nomadic, along with others in Tlaltenango, Juchipila and Teocaltiche.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hiscom_16-17"><a href="#cite_note-hiscom-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
