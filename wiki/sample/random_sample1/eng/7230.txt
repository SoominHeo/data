<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Sakuradamon Incident (1860)</h1>

<p>The <b>Sakuradamon Incident</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">桜田門外の変</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Sakuradamon-gai no Hen</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, or 桜田門の変 <i>Sakuradamon no Hen</i>)</span> was the assassination of Japanese Chief Minister (<a href="/wiki/Tair%C5%8D" title="Tairō">Tairō</a>) <a href="/wiki/Ii_Naosuke" title="Ii Naosuke">Ii Naosuke</a> (1815–1860) on 24 March 1860 by <a href="/wiki/R%C5%8Dnin" title="Rōnin">rōnin</a> <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurai</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Mito_Domain" title="Mito Domain">Mito Domain</a>, outside the Sakurada Gate of <a href="/wiki/Edo_Castle" title="Edo Castle">Edo Castle</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Context">Context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sakuradamon_Incident_(1860)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bakumatsu_period" title="Bakumatsu period">Bakumatsu period</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ii_Naosuke.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4376" data-file-width="2062" height="467" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Ii_Naosuke.jpg/220px-Ii_Naosuke.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Ii_Naosuke.jpg/330px-Ii_Naosuke.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Ii_Naosuke.jpg/440px-Ii_Naosuke.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ii_Naosuke.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
In 1860, <a href="/wiki/Ii_Naosuke" title="Ii Naosuke">Ii Naosuke</a> was the most influential advisor to the shogunate.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Ii Naosuke, a leading figure of the <a href="/wiki/Bakumatsu" title="Bakumatsu">Bakumatsu</a> period and a proponent of the reopening of Japan after more than 200 years of <a href="/wiki/Sakoku" title="Sakoku">Seclusion</a>, was widely criticized for signing the 1858 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Amity_and_Commerce_(United_States-Japan)" title="Treaty of Amity and Commerce (United States-Japan)">Treaty of Amity and Commerce</a> with the United States Consul <a href="/wiki/Townsend_Harris" title="Townsend Harris">Townsend Harris</a> and, soon afterwards, similar treaties with other Western countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wata-39_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wata-39-1">[1]</a></sup> From 1859, the ports of <a href="/wiki/Nagasaki" title="Nagasaki">Nagasaki</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hakodate" title="Hakodate">Hakodate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yokohama" title="Yokohama">Yokohama</a> became open to foreign traders as a consequence of the Treaties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ii Naosuke was also criticized for reinforcing the authority of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shogunate" title="Shogunate">Shogunate</a> against regional Daimyos through the <a href="/wiki/Ansei_Purge" title="Ansei Purge">Ansei Purge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wata-39_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wata-39-1">[1]</a></sup> Naosuke made strong enemies in the dispute for the succession of Shogun <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_Iesada" title="Tokugawa Iesada">Tokugawa Iesada</a>, and because he forced retirement on his opponents, specifically the retainers of <a href="/wiki/Mito_Domain" title="Mito Domain">Mito</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hizen_Domain" title="Hizen Domain">Hizen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Owari_Domain" title="Owari Domain">Owari</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tosa_Domain" title="Tosa Domain">Tosa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Satsuma_Domain" title="Satsuma Domain">Satsuma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uwajima_Domain" title="Uwajima Domain">Uwajima</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>These policies generated strong sentiment against the Shogunate, especially among proponents of the <a href="/wiki/Mitogaku" title="Mitogaku">Mito school</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morishma-68_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morishma-68-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assassination">Assassination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sakuradamon_Incident_(1860)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Assassination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sakuradamon_incident_1860_silk.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1203" data-file-width="2964" height="101" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Sakuradamon_incident_1860_silk.jpg/250px-Sakuradamon_incident_1860_silk.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Sakuradamon_incident_1860_silk.jpg/375px-Sakuradamon_incident_1860_silk.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Sakuradamon_incident_1860_silk.jpg/500px-Sakuradamon_incident_1860_silk.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

<p>The assassination took place outside the Shogun's <a href="/wiki/Edo_Castle" title="Edo Castle">Edo Castle</a> in <a href="/wiki/Edo" title="Edo">Edo</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>), just as Ii Naosuke was reaching the premises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wata-39_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wata-39-1">[1]</a></sup> Ii Naosuke had been warned about his safety, and many encouraged him to retire from office, but he refused, replying that "My own safety is nothing when I see the danger threatening the future of the country".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>A total of 17 Mito rōnin ambushed Ii Naosuke together with Arimura Jisaemon (有村次左衛門), a samurai from <a href="/wiki/Satsuma_Domain" title="Satsuma Domain">Satsuma Domain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> While an attack at the front drew the attention of the guards, a lone assassin fired one shot into the palanquin containing Ii Naosuke, with a Japanese-made <a href="/wiki/Colt_1851_Navy_Revolver" title="Colt 1851 Navy Revolver">Colt 1851 Navy Revolver</a>, which had been copied from the firearms that Perry had given the Shogunate as gifts. Drawing the injured and likely paralyzed Ii Naosuke out, Arimura decapitated Ii Naosuke and then committed <a href="/wiki/Seppuku" title="Seppuku">seppuku</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arimura_Jisaemon.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1160" data-file-width="800" height="319" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Arimura_Jisaemon.jpg/220px-Arimura_Jisaemon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Arimura_Jisaemon.jpg/330px-Arimura_Jisaemon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Arimura_Jisaemon.jpg/440px-Arimura_Jisaemon.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arimura_Jisaemon.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Arimura Jisaemon, on the point of committing the assassination.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The conspirators carried a manifesto on themselves, outlining the reason for their act:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>While fully aware of the necessity for some change in policy since the coming of the Americans at <a href="/wiki/Uraga,_Kanagawa" title="Uraga, Kanagawa">Uraga</a>, it is entirely against the interest of the country and a stain on the national honour to open up commercial relations with foreigners, to admit foreigners into the Castle, to conclude treaties with them, to abolish the established practice of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fumie" title="Fumie">trampling on the picture of Christ</a>, to allow foreigners to build places of worship for the evil religion, and to allow the three Foreign Ministers to reside in the land (...) Therefore, we have consecrated ourselves to be the instruments of Heaven to punish this wicked man, and we have taken on ourselves the duty of ending a serious evil, by killing this atrocious autocrat.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Manifesto of the Sakuradamon conspirators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Accounts of the violent event were sent via ship across the Pacific to San Francisco and then sped by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pony_express" title="Pony express">pony express</a> across the American West. On June 12, 1860 the <i>New York Times</i> reported that <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Embassy_to_the_United_States_(1860)" title="Japanese Embassy to the United States (1860)">Japan's first diplomatic mission to the West</a> received the news about what happened in Edo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consequences">Consequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sakuradamon_Incident_(1860)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Consequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The popular upheaval against foreign encroachment and assassination of Ii Naosuke forced the Bakufu to soften its stance, and to adopt a compromise policy of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C5%8Dbu_Gattai" title="Kōbu Gattai">Kōbu Gattai</a></i> ("Union of the Emperor and the Shogun") suggested by <a href="/wiki/Satsuma_Domain" title="Satsuma Domain">Satsuma Domain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mito_Domain" title="Mito Domain">Mito Domain</a>, in which both parties vied for political supremacy in the years to follow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morishma-68_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morishma-68-4">[4]</a></sup> This soon amplified into the violent <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonn%C5%8D_J%C5%8Di" title="Sonnō Jōi">Sonnō Jōi</a></i> ("Revere the Emperor, Expel the Barbarians") movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>For the following years until the fall of Bakufu in 1868, Edo, and more generally the streets of Japan, would remain notably hazardous for Bakufu officials (see attack on <a href="/wiki/And%C5%8D_Nobumasa" title="Andō Nobumasa">Andō Nobumasa</a>) and foreigners alike (<a href="/wiki/Namamugi_Incident" title="Namamugi Incident">Richardson murder</a>), as the <i>Sonno Joi</i> movement continued to expand. According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Ernest_Satow" title="Sir Ernest Satow">Sir Ernest Satow</a>: "A bloody revenge was taken on the individual [Ii Naosuke], but the hostility to the system only increased with time, and in the end brought about its complete ruin".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The conflict reached its resolution with the military defeat of the Shogunate in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boshin_war" title="Boshin war">Boshin war</a>, and the installation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meiji_restoration" title="Meiji restoration">Meiji restoration</a> in 1868.</p>
