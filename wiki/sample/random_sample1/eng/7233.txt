<p><b>Tsutomu Sakuma</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">佐久間 勉</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Sakuma Tsutomu</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, September 13, 1879 – April 15, 1910)</span> was a career naval officer in the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Navy" title="Imperial Japanese Navy">Imperial Japanese Navy</a>, and a pioneer <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarine</a> commander, known primarily as the commanding officer during the sinking of Submarine <i>No.6</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tsutomu_Sakuma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Mikata_District,_Fukui" title="Mikata District, Fukui">Mikata District</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fukui_prefecture" title="Fukui prefecture">Fukui prefecture</a> (present-day city of <a href="/wiki/Wakasa,_Fukui" title="Wakasa, Fukui">Wakasa, Fukui</a>), to the family of a <a href="/wiki/Kannushi" title="Kannushi">Shinto priest</a>, Sakuma graduated from the 29th class of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Naval_Academy" title="Imperial Japanese Naval Academy">Imperial Japanese Naval Academy</a> in 1901. One of his classmates was the future <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_the_Navy_of_Japan" title="Ministry of the Navy of Japan">Navy Minister</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Japan" title="Prime Minister of Japan">Prime Minister of Japan</a> <a href="/wiki/Mitsumasa_Yonai" title="Mitsumasa Yonai">Mitsumasa Yonai</a>. He was assigned to the cruiser <a href="/wiki/Japanese_cruiser_Azuma" title="Japanese cruiser Azuma"><i>Azuma</i></a> shortly before the start of the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese War</a>. He subsequently served with the 15th Torpedo Boat Flotilla, and was on board the cruiser <a href="/wiki/Japanese_cruiser_Kasagi" title="Japanese cruiser Kasagi"><i>Kasagi</i></a> during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tsushima" title="Battle of Tsushima">Battle of Tsushima</a> on May 26, 1905.</p>
<p>After completing the <a href="/wiki/Torpedo" title="Torpedo">torpedo</a> warfare school, Sakuma served on the <a href="/wiki/Submarine_tender" title="Submarine tender">submarine tender</a> <a href="/wiki/Japanese_submarine_tender_Karasaki" title="Japanese submarine tender Karasaki"><i>Karasaki</i></a> and was later a squad leader on the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_cruiser_Tsushima" title="Japanese cruiser Tsushima">Japanese cruiser <i>Tsushima</i></a>. He started to get involved in the submarine service in 1906, first taking the command of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Navy_submarines#Origins" title="Imperial Japanese Navy submarines"><i>No.4 submarine</i></a>, and eventually the Japan-built <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Navy_submarines#Origins" title="Imperial Japanese Navy submarines">Holland-type <i>No.6</i></a> in 1908.</p>
<p>On April 15, 1910, while engaged in a practice dive in the <a href="/wiki/Seto_Inland_Sea" title="Seto Inland Sea">Inland Sea</a> off of the coast of <a href="/wiki/Yamaguchi_Prefecture" title="Yamaguchi Prefecture">Yamaguchi Prefecture</a>, the submarine suffered a malfunction resulting in the loss of the ship and crew. Two days later the submarine was recovered by Japanese authorities, and Sakuma's journal was found, with a detailed analysis of what may have caused the accident, ending with an apology to <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Meiji" title="Emperor Meiji">Emperor Meiji</a> for the loss of the submarine and 14 crew members before the ship's air ran out. The journal was posted in later press reports, and Sakuma became a posthumous national hero and an example for courage and steadfastness within the Imperial Japanese Navy.</p>
