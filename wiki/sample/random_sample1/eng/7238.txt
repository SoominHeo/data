<p><b>Naotake Satō</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">佐藤 尚武</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Satō Naotake</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, October 30, 1882 – December 18, 1971)</span> was a <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> diplomat and politician. He was born at <a href="/wiki/Osaka" title="Osaka">Osaka</a>. He graduated from the Tokyo Higher Commercial School (東京高等商業学校| <i>Tōkyō Kōtō Shōgyō Gakkō</i>, now <a href="/wiki/Hitotsubashi_University" title="Hitotsubashi University">Hitotsubashi University</a>) in 1904, attended the consul course of the same institute, and quit studying there in 1905.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naotake_Sat%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was born on October 30, 1882.</p>
<p>He was an active politician and diplomat. In 1905, he passed the Foreign Service exam and started to work at the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_(Japan)" title="Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Japan)">Ministry of Foreign Affairs</a>. After serving as Mukden Consul General and executive secretary of the <a href="/wiki/London_Naval_Treaty" title="London Naval Treaty">London Naval Treaty</a>, he served as Imperial Japan’s Ambassador to <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> in 1930 and to <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> in 1933. He became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister_of_Foreign_Affairs" title="Minister of Foreign Affairs">Minister of Foreign Affairs</a> (<a href="/wiki/Senj%C5%ABr%C5%8D_Hayashi" title="Senjūrō Hayashi">Senjūrō Hayashi</a> Cabinet) on March 1937, and resigned on June 1937, then was assigned as Diplomatic Adviser, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreign_Office" title="Foreign Office">Foreign Office</a>, under 1st <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fumimaro_Konoye" title="Fumimaro Konoye">Fumimaro Konoye</a> Cabinet and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hideki_T%C5%8Dj%C5%8D" title="Hideki Tōjō">Hideki Tōjō</a> Cabinet.</p>
<p>He had served as the last Imperial Japan’s Ambassador to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S.S.R." title="U.S.S.R.">U.S.S.R.</a> before the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_invasion_of_Manchuria" title="Soviet invasion of Manchuria">Soviet invasion of Manchuria</a> since 1942 upon the request of the Minister of Foreign Affairs, <a href="/wiki/Shigenori_T%C5%8Dg%C5%8D" title="Shigenori Tōgō">Shigenori Tōgō</a>. As Minister, he worked hard to avert war at the Imperial Diet. Allegedly, one of his missions as Japan’s Ambassador to the U.S.S.R. was to seek peace with the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a> through the assistance of the U.S.S.R. due to <a href="/wiki/Soviet%E2%80%93Japanese_Neutrality_Pact" title="Soviet–Japanese Neutrality Pact">Soviet–Japanese Neutrality Pact</a> under bad war conditions for Imperial Japan. However, Satō judged and reported to Tokyo that it was unlikely that the U.S.S.R. would assist Imperial Japan, because it was highly likely that Imperial Japan would lose the war, and urged it to end the war as early as possible. He was invited to the <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Kremlin" title="Moscow Kremlin">Kremlin</a> by the U.S.S.R. Foreign Minister, <a href="/wiki/Vyacheslav_Molotov" title="Vyacheslav Molotov">Vyacheslav Molotov</a>, on August 8, 1945, and received a declaration of war against Imperial Japan. After the war, he was elected to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Councillors" title="House of Councillors">House of Councillors</a> of the National Diet of Japan in 1947, and served as a president of House of Councillors from 1949 to 1953.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>He died on December 18, 1971.</p>
