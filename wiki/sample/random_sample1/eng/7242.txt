<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Þáttr</h1>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>The <i><b>þættir</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> singular <i>þáttr</i>, literally meaning a "strand" of rope or yarn<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O.27DONOGHUE226_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-O.27DONOGHUE226-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>) are short stories written mostly in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> during the 13th and 14th centuries.</p>
<p>The majority of þættir occur in two compendious manuscripts, <a href="/wiki/Morkinskinna" title="Morkinskinna">Morkinskinna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flateyjarb%C3%B3k" title="Flateyjarbók">Flateyjarbók</a>, and within them most are found as digressions within kings' sagas. Sverrir Tómasson regards those in Morkinskinna, at least, as <i><a href="/wiki/Exemplum" title="Exemplum">exempla</a></i> or illustrations inseparable from the narratives that contain them, filling out the picture of the kings' qualities, good and bad, as well as adding comic relief.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id=".C3.8Dslendinga_.C3.BE.C3.A6ttir">Íslendinga þættir</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%9E%C3%A1ttr&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Íslendinga þættir">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
