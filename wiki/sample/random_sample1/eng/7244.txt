<p><b>Saparmurat Atayevich Niyazov</b> (<a href="/wiki/Turkmen_language" title="Turkmen language">Turkmen</a>: <span lang="tk"><i>Saparmyrat Ataýewiç Nyýazow</i></span>, Сапармырат Атайевич Ныязов; 19 February 1940 – 21 December 2006) was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turkmen_people" title="Turkmen people">Turkmen</a> politician who served as the leader of <a href="/wiki/Turkmenistan" title="Turkmenistan">Turkmenistan</a> from 1985 until his death in 2006. He was First Secretary of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turkmen_Communist_Party" title="Turkmen Communist Party">Turkmen Communist Party</a> from 1985 until 1991 and continued to lead Turkmenistan for 15 years after independence from the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> in 1991.</p>
<p>Turkmen media referred to him using the title "<a href="/wiki/Excellency" title="Excellency">His Excellency</a> Saparmurat Türkmenbaşy, President of Turkmenistan and Chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_Ministers_(Turkmenistan)" title="Cabinet of Ministers (Turkmenistan)">Cabinet of Ministers</a>".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> His self-given title <b>Türkmenbaşy</b>, meaning <i>Head of the Turkmen</i>, referred to his position as the founder and president of the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Turkmens_of_the_World" title="Association of Turkmens of the World">Association of Turkmens of the World</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-excellency_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-excellency-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Foreign media criticized him for being one of the world's most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Totalitarian" title="Totalitarian">totalitarian</a> and repressive <a href="/wiki/Dictator" title="Dictator">dictators</a>, highlighting his reputation of imposing his personal <a href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(behavior)" title="Eccentricity (behavior)">eccentricities</a> upon the country, which extended to <a href="/wiki/Renaming_of_Turkmen_months_and_days_of_week,_2002" title="Renaming of Turkmen months and days of week, 2002">renaming months</a> for details of his own biography among other things.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Global_Witness" title="Global Witness">Global Witness</a>, a London-based human rights organization, reported that money under Niyazov's control and held overseas may be in excess of <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>3 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1000000000_(number)" title="1000000000 (number)">billion</a>, of which between $1.8–$2.6 billion was supposedly situated in the Foreign Exchange Reserve Fund at <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bank" title="Deutsche Bank">Deutsche Bank</a> in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saparmurat_Niyazov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Niyazov was born on 19 February 1940 in <a href="/wiki/Gypjak" title="Gypjak">Gypjak</a> (or Kipchak), just outside <a href="/wiki/Ashgabat" title="Ashgabat">Ashgabat</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turkmen_SSR" title="Turkmen SSR">Turkmen SSR</a>. He was a member of the influential <a href="/wiki/Turkmen_tribes" title="Turkmen tribes">Tekke tribe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESabol201010_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESabol201010-5">[5]</a></sup> According to the official version of his biography, his father died in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> fighting against <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>, while other sources contend that he dodged fighting and was therefore sentenced by a military court. The other members of his family were killed in <a href="/wiki/1948_Ashgabat_earthquake" title="1948 Ashgabat earthquake">a massive earthquake</a> that leveled <a href="/wiki/Ashgabat" title="Ashgabat">Ashgabat</a> in 1948. His mother <a href="/wiki/Gurbansoltan_Eje" title="Gurbansoltan Eje">Gurbansoltan Eje</a> was part of the <a href="/wiki/Cult_of_personality" title="Cult of personality">Cult of personality</a> later. He grew up in a <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> orphanage before the state put him in the custody of a distant relative.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>After finishing school in 1959, he worked as an instructor in the Turkmen trade-union exploratory committee. He then studied at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leningrad_Polytechnic_Institute" title="Leningrad Polytechnic Institute">Leningrad Polytechnic Institute</a>, where in 1967 he received a diploma as an electrical engineer. After graduating, he went to study in Russia, but was expelled a few years later for academic failure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KP_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-KP-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1962 Niyazov started his political career, becoming a member of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Communist Party</a>. He quickly rose through the ranks, becoming First Secretary of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Turkmen_SSR" title="Communist Party of the Turkmen SSR">Communist Party of the Turkmen SSR</a> in 1985. He gained this post after Soviet General Secretary <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a> had removed his predecessor, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muhammetnazar_Gapurov" title="Muhammetnazar Gapurov">Muhammetnazar Gapurov</a>, following a <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>-related scandal. Under Niyazov, the Turkmen Communist Party had a reputation as one of the most hardline and unreformed party organizations in the Soviet Union. On January 13, 1990, Niyazov became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Chairmen_of_the_Supreme_Soviet_of_the_Republic_of_Turkmenistan" title="List of Chairmen of the Supreme Soviet of the Republic of Turkmenistan">Chairman of the Supreme Soviet of the Turkmen SSR</a>, the supreme legislative body in the republic. The post was equivalent to that of president.</p>
<p>Niyazov supported the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_coup_attempt_of_1991" title="Soviet coup attempt of 1991">Soviet coup attempt of 1991</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> However, after the coup collapsed, he set about separating Turkmenistan from the dying Soviet Union. The Turkmen Supreme Soviet declared Turkmenistan independent and appointed Niyazov as the country's first president on October 27, 1991. On June 21, 1992 the <a href="/wiki/Turkmenistani_presidential_election,_1992" title="Turkmenistani presidential election, 1992">Turkmenistani presidential election of 1992</a> saw Niyazov - the sole candidate - chosen as the country's first popularly elected president. A year later he declared himself <i>Türkmenbaşy</i> - "Leader of all Turkmen".</p>
<p>In 1994 a plebiscite extended Niyazov's term to 2002 so he could oversee a 10-year development plan. The official results showed that 99.9% of voters approved this proposal. On December 28, 1999, Parliament declared Niyazov <a href="/wiki/President_for_Life" title="President for Life">President for Life</a>; parliamentary elections had been held a few weeks earlier for which the president had hand-picked all candidates.</p>
<p>Niyazov and his Russian-Jewish wife, Muza, had a son (Murat) and a daughter (Irina).</p>

<p>Niyazov became president at the transition of Turkmenistan from a Soviet republic to an independent state. His presidency was characterized by an initial crumbling of the centralized Soviet model that in many respects was unsuited to function as a separate entity; also, there were large amounts of foreign income from gas and petroleum reserves (approximately $2–4 billion as of 2005). There was outside concern about press freedom and to a lesser extent religious rights of minority religious groups. Niyazov made a personal attempt to create a cultural background for the new state of Turkmenistan by writing and promoting the <a href="/wiki/Ruhnama" title="Ruhnama">Ruhnama</a>, an autobiography meant to guide the people of Turkmenistan with his ideas and promote native culture (and by extension prohibiting foreign culture). He also took part in creating new holidays with a specific Turkmen nature and introduced a new Latin-based Turkmen alphabet to replace Russian Cyrillic. The Latin Turkmen alphabet consists of: Aa, Bb, Çç, Dd, Ee, Ää, Ff, Gg, Hh, Ii, Jj, Žž, Kk, Ll, Mm, Nn, Ňň, Oo, Öö, Pp, Rr, Ss, Şş, Tt, Uu, Üü, Ww, Yy, Ýý, Zz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Niyazov became a substitute for the vacuum left by the downfall of the communist system, with his image replacing those of Marx and Lenin. He renamed the town of Krasnovodsk "<a href="/wiki/T%C3%BCrkmenba%C5%9Fy,_Turkmenistan" title="Türkmenbaşy, Turkmenistan">Turkmenbashi</a>" after himself, and renamed schools, airports and even a <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">meteorite</a> after himself and members of his family. His many, sometimes erratic decrees, and the doting actions of the official Turkmen media gave rise to the clear appearance of a <a href="/wiki/Cult_of_personality" title="Cult of personality">cult of personality</a>. The eccentric nature of some of his decrees, and the vast number of images of the president led to the perception, especially in western countries, of a despotic leader, rich on oil wealth glorifying himself whilst the population gained no benefit.</p>
<p>Despite emphasizing a need to move from central planning to a market economy and to a full democracy during his reign, neither plan progressed. Yearly plans set forth by the government and a centralised economy gave little indication of moving away from state-dominated economics, and the dictatorial nature of many of his decrees and his declaring himself "President for Life" gave little hope as to much progress in these two areas.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saparmurat_Niyazov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil_and_gas">Oil and gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saparmurat_Niyazov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Oil and gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Turkmenistan has the second-largest oil reserves in the former Soviet Union, generating high revenue for the state. The government has used <a href="/wiki/Planned_economy" title="Planned economy">central planning</a>, such as state control of production and procurement, direct bank credits with low interest rates, exchange rate restrictions, and price controls, since it existed as a Republic within the U.S.S.R.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-badykova_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-badykova-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the years following independence, Turkmenistan invested heavily in plants and machinery in an attempt to convert it from being primarily a supplier of petroleum to a more advanced economy; such investments included oil refineries and a polyethylene plant. In an interview with <i>Rossiyskaya Gazeta</i> newspaper, Niyazov claimed that Turkmenistan was able to process 85% of its domestic output. Additionally, numerous petroleum transportation projects were completed such as a pipeline from the Korpedje field to Kort-Koi in Iran.</p>
<p>In 1991, Niyazov's government put forth a decree granting "the free use of water, gas and electricity and refined salt by the people of Turkmenistan for ten years";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-excellency_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-excellency-2">[2]</a></sup> when the decree expired, he extended it to 2020.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saparmurat_Niyazov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Turkmenistan's other primary resources are cotton and grain. Niyazov continued the old practice of demanding yearly quotas in agricultural output, and then blaming and/or sacking deputy ministers when quotas were not met.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Nevertheless, Turkmenistan had an emergent period during which there was heavy investment in plant and machinery so the country could change from a producer of raw cotton to a cotton processor. During Niyazov's presidency, a textile industry was founded in Turkmenistan.</p>
<p>Niyazov introduced the practice of "Melon Day," a harvest festival celebrated on the 2nd Sunday of August; unlike some of his other creations, the celebration of "Melon Day" has continued after his death.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saparmurat_Niyazov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Niyazov put the revival of Turkmen culture as one of the top priorities in Turkmenistan's development. He introduced a new <a href="/wiki/Turkmen_alphabet" title="Turkmen alphabet">Turkmen alphabet</a> based on the <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin alphabet</a> to replace <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">Cyrillic</a>. The <i>National Revival Movement</i>, an organisation to promote Turkmen culture (Turkmen: "Galkynish"), was also founded.</p>
<p>In many respects, Niyazov's cultural ideas and changes were most visible to external viewers. His <a href="/wiki/Renaming_of_Turkmen_months_and_days_of_week,_2002" title="Renaming of Turkmen months and days of week, 2002">renaming of months, as well as days of the week</a>, to Turkmen heroes, poets, historical events,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> family members and himself raised many eyebrows all over the world. For example, September was renamed <i><a href="/wiki/Ruhnama" title="Ruhnama">Ruhnama</a></i> in honour of the book written by Niyazov (which he finished writing on 19 September 2001).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Not all the changes promoted Niyazov; October was renamed <i>Garaşsyzlyk</i> (Independence) to mark the state's founding on 27 October 1991, and November <i>Sanjar</i> in honour of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ahmed_Sanjar" title="Ahmed Sanjar">Sultan Sanjar</a> who led the <a href="/wiki/Seljuq_dynasty" title="Seljuq dynasty">Seljuqs</a> to their last full flowering. The new names came into effect with the introduction of a new labour law which stated that "the dates of professional holidays are specified by decrees of the President of Turkmenistan".</p>
<p>These names were later abolished by his successor Berdymukhamedov in April 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_affairs">Internal affairs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saparmurat_Niyazov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Internal affairs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the earliest acts of the president was to abolish the death penalty. He also granted official human rights to the people, though they were not respected in practice with his government being criticized as one of the worst human rights violators in the world. Press freedom under Niyazov's leadership was much criticised as it was with other former Soviet central Asian states. Turkmenistan's media constantly doted on the president and helped build his <a href="/wiki/Cult_of_personality" title="Cult of personality">cult of personality</a>. In May 2000, the government revoked all Internet licenses except for the state-owned Turkmen Telecom and in June 2001 shut down all Internet cafés.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> By 2005 there were 36,000 Internet users in Turkmenistan, representing 0.7% of the population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2004, 15,000 public health workers were dismissed including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nurse" title="Nurse">nurses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Midwife" title="Midwife">midwives</a>, school health visitors and <a href="/wiki/Orderly" title="Orderly">orderlies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In February 2005 all hospitals outside <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A%C5%9Fgabat" title="Aşgabat">Aşgabat</a> were ordered shut, with the reasoning that the sick should come to the capital for treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> According to the paper <i>Neitralniy Turkmenistan</i> <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physicians</a> were ordered to swear an oath to the President, replacing the <a href="/wiki/Hippocratic_Oath" title="Hippocratic Oath">Hippocratic Oath</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> All libraries outside of the capital were also closed, as Niyazov believed that the only books that most Turkmen needed to read were the Qur'an and his Ruhnama.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2006 one-third of the country's elderly had their <a href="/wiki/Pension" title="Pension">pensions</a> discontinued, while another 200,000 had theirs reduced. Pensions received during the prior two years were ordered paid back to the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The Ministry of Foreign Affairs of Turkmenistan strongly denied allegations that the cut in pensions resulted in the deaths of many elderly Turkmens, accusing foreign media outlets of spreading "deliberately perverted" information on the issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> On March 19, 2007 Turkmenistan's new president <a href="/wiki/Gurbanguly_Berdimuhamedow" title="Gurbanguly Berdimuhamedow">Gurbanguly Berdimuhamedow</a> reversed Niyazov's decision by restoring pensions to more than 100,000 elderly citizens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2008, the new president also made changes to the <a href="/wiki/National_anthem_of_Turkmenistan" title="National anthem of Turkmenistan">national anthem</a>, the chorus of which referenced Niyazov.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Presidential_pardons">Presidential pardons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saparmurat_Niyazov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Presidential pardons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In keeping with the predominantly Islamic nature of Turkmen society, President Niyazov granted pardons each year on the <i><a href="/wiki/Laylat_al-Qadr" title="Laylat al-Qadr">Laylat al-Qadr</a></i> (<i>Night of Destiny</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Ramadan" title="Ramadan">Ramadan</a> month.</p>
<p>For example, in 2005, 8,145 convicts were pardoned including 229 foreign nationals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In 2006 Turkmenistan set free 10,056 prisoners, including 253 foreign nationals from 11 countries. Niyazov said:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Let this humane act on the part of the state serve strengthening truly moral values of the Turkmen society. Let the entire world know that there has never been a place for evil and violence on the blessed Turkmen soil."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Decrees_and_laws">Decrees and laws</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saparmurat_Niyazov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Decrees and laws">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>Niyazov banned the use of <a href="/wiki/Lip_sync" title="Lip sync">lip syncing</a> at public concerts in 2005 as well as sound recordings at "musical performances on state holidays, in broadcasts by Turkmen television channels, at all cultural events organized by the state... in places of mass assembly and at weddings and celebrations organized by the public," citing a negative effect on the development of musical arts incurred by the use of recorded music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hiro.2C_Dilip_2009._p227_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hiro.2C_Dilip_2009._p227-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li>Niyazov banished dogs from the capital <a href="/wiki/Ashgabat" title="Ashgabat">Ashgabat</a> because of their "unappealing odour." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hiro.2C_Dilip_2009._p227_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hiro.2C_Dilip_2009._p227-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li>According to the Ashgabat correspondent of Turkmenistan.ru, right-hand-drive imported cars converted to left-hand-drive were banned due to a perceived increased risk in accidents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li>Niyazov requested that a "<a href="/wiki/Ice_rink" title="Ice rink">palace of ice</a>", or indoor ice skating rink, be built near the capital, so that those living in the desert country could learn to skate. The rink was built in 2008 and is located near the new <a href="/wiki/Turkmen_State_Medical_University" title="Turkmen State Medical University">Turkmen State Medical University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li>After having to quit <a href="/wiki/Tobacco#Consumption" title="Tobacco">smoking</a> in 1997 due to his resultant heart surgery, he banned smoking in all public places and ordered all government employees to follow suit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IndependentObit_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-IndependentObit-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chewing_tobacco" title="Chewing tobacco">Chewing tobacco</a> on Turkmen soil was later banned as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li>He outlawed opera, ballet, and circuses in 2001 for being "decidedly unturkmen-like".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li>In February 2004 he decreed that men should no longer wear long hair or beards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li>He banned news reporters and anchors from wearing make-up on television, because he said he found it difficult to distinguish male anchors from female anchors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IndependentObit_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-IndependentObit-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gold_teeth" title="Gold teeth">Gold teeth</a> were discouraged in Turkmenistan after Niyazov suggested that the populace chew on bones to strengthen their teeth and lessen the rate at which they fall out. He said:
<blockquote>
<p>I watched young dogs when I was young. They were given bones to gnaw to strengthen their teeth. Those of you whose teeth have fallen out did not chew on bones. This is my advice...<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_policy">Foreign policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saparmurat_Niyazov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Foreign policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vladimir_Putin_with_Saparmurat_Niyazov-11.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="334" data-file-width="500" height="134" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Vladimir_Putin_with_Saparmurat_Niyazov-11.jpg/200px-Vladimir_Putin_with_Saparmurat_Niyazov-11.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Vladimir_Putin_with_Saparmurat_Niyazov-11.jpg/300px-Vladimir_Putin_with_Saparmurat_Niyazov-11.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Vladimir_Putin_with_Saparmurat_Niyazov-11.jpg/400px-Vladimir_Putin_with_Saparmurat_Niyazov-11.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vladimir_Putin_with_Saparmurat_Niyazov-11.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Niyazov with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_President" title="Russian President">Russian President</a> <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Putin" title="Vladimir Putin">Vladimir Putin</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Kremlin" title="Moscow Kremlin">Moscow Kremlin</a>, June 2000</div>

<p>Niyazov promoted a policy of strict <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutrality_(international_relations)" title="Neutrality (international relations)">neutrality</a> in foreign affairs, refraining from seeking membership in <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> or <a href="/wiki/GUAM_Organization_for_Democracy_and_Economic_Development" title="GUAM Organization for Democracy and Economic Development">GUAM</a> and almost ignoring the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collective_Security_Treaty_Organisation" title="Collective Security Treaty Organisation">CSTO</a>. Turkmenistan has not participated in any <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> peacekeeping missions. It has however become a member of <a href="/wiki/Interpol" title="Interpol">Interpol</a>.</p>
<p>The full independence of Turkmenistan was recognised by a UN General Assembly resolution "The permanent neutrality of Turkmenistan" of December 12, 1995. As a result, in 2005 Turkmenistan would downgrade its links with the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Independent_States" title="Commonwealth of Independent States">Commonwealth of Independent States</a> becoming only an associate member under article 8 of the CIS charter, as such it would not participate in any of the military structures of the CIS.</p>
<p>In 2006 the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> and the international trade committee of the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> voted to grant Turkmenistan "<a href="/wiki/Most_favoured_nation" title="Most favoured nation">most favoured nation</a>" trading status with the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, widely seen as motivated by interest in <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, after Niyazov announced he would enter a "human rights dialogue" with the EU.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition">Opposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saparmurat_Niyazov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Opposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After an alleged assassination attempt on November 25, 2002, the Turkmen government arrested thousands of suspected conspirators and members of their families. Critics claim the government staged the attempt in order to crack down on mounting domestic and foreign political opposition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The summer of 2004 saw a leaflet campaign in the capital, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A%C5%9Fgabat" title="Aşgabat">Aşgabat</a>, calling for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coup" title="Coup">overthrow</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trial_(law)" title="Trial (law)">trial</a> of Niyazov. The authorities were unable to stop the campaign and the President responded by firing his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interior_Minister" title="Interior Minister">Interior Minister</a> and director of the police academy on national television.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> He accused the minister of incompetence and declared: "I cannot say that you had any great merits or did much to combat crime."</p>
<p>Niyazov later announced that surveillance cameras were to be placed at all major streets and sites in Turkmenistan, an apparent precaution against future attempts.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saparmurat_Niyazov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On December 21, 2006, Turkmen state television announced that President Niyazov had died of a sudden <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">heart attack</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Niyazov had been taking medication for an unidentified cardiac condition. The <a href="/wiki/Embassy_of_Turkmenistan_in_Moscow" title="Embassy of Turkmenistan in Moscow">Turkmen Embassy</a> in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a> later confirmed this report.</p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Turkmenistan" title="Constitution of Turkmenistan">Constitution of Turkmenistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%96wezgeldi_Ata%C3%BDew" title="Öwezgeldi Ataýew">Öwezgeldi Ataýew</a>, Chairman of <a href="/wiki/Assembly_of_Turkmenistan" title="Assembly of Turkmenistan">the Assembly</a>, would assume the presidency. Deputy Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Gurbanguly_Berdimuhamedow" title="Gurbanguly Berdimuhamedow">Gurbanguly Berdimuhamedow</a> was named as head of the commission organizing the state funeral. Ataýew was arrested on 21 December 2006 and Berdimuhamedow was subsequently named acting president. Berdimuhamedow and the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Council_of_Turkmenistan" title="People's Council of Turkmenistan">Halk Maslahaty</a> announced on December 26, 2006 that the <a href="/wiki/Turkmenistani_presidential_election,_2007" title="Turkmenistani presidential election, 2007">next presidential elections</a> would be held on February 11, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The circumstances of Niyazov's death have been surrounded by some media speculation. Some Turkmen opposition sources also claim that Niyazov died several days before the officially announced date of December 21.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>News reports also claimed that Niyazov also suffered from <a href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus" title="Diabetes mellitus">diabetes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ischaemic_heart_disease" title="Ischaemic heart disease">ischemic heart disease</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renal_failure" title="Renal failure">kidney failure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KP_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-KP-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>Niyazov was buried in his ready prepared tomb in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kipchak_Mosque" title="Kipchak Mosque">Kipchak Mosque</a> on December 24 at his home village of <a href="/wiki/Gypjak" title="Gypjak">Gypjak</a>, approximately 7 kilometres west of <a href="/wiki/Ashgabat" title="Ashgabat">Ashgabat</a>. Prior to being moved to the village, Niyazov's body lay in state in an open coffin in the presidential palace. Many mourners, including foreign delegations, passed by the coffin in a three-hour period. Many of the ordinary citizens wept dramatically as they walked, some even clinging to the coffin and fainting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
