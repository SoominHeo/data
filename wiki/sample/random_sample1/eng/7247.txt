<p>The <b>Siberian musk deer</b> (<i>Moschus moschiferus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Musk_deer" title="Musk deer">musk deer</a> found in the mountain forests of <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Asia" title="Northeast Asia">Northeast Asia</a>. It is most common in the <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a> of southern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, but is also found in parts of <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inner_Mongolia" title="Inner Mongolia">Inner Mongolia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_peninsula" title="Korean peninsula">Korean peninsula</a>.</p>
<p>Their small shape allows them to hide from predators through tiny openings in the rocky terrain and also allow them to run exceptionally fast from their predators. Although bearing fangs, Siberian musk deer are actually herbivores with their main source of nutrients, <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichens</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:02-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to the severe amount of poaching for its musk gland, meat and deforestation, the deer population is continuing to decrease. It is expected that the population will be reduced to at least 30% over the next three generations. However, efforts from each sighted countries are beginning to reintroduce the musk deerâs population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyambayar2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nyambayar2-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Maturity_and_Mating">Maturity and Mating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siberian_musk_deer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Maturity and Mating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It takes approximately a year for the Siberian musk deer to reach maturity with an average deer to live at least 10 â 14 years.</p>
<p>During breeding season, male deer will grow fangs instead of antlers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:02-2">[2]</a></sup> These fangs are used to compete with other males and attract females. Fangs that are longer and stronger creates a more intimidating stance and becomes more attractive to females as the offspring of that male are likely to become healthier and fit.</p>
<p>Once the male and the female deer have procreated, the females will become pregnant lasting over 6 months and can give birth to 1-3 offspring, usually between the months of May through June.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qi2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Qi2-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviors">Behaviors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siberian_musk_deer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Musk will mark their territory warning trespassing deers not to cross the boundary. When marking their territories, musk deer gather fallen branches, tree trunks, as well as plant stems and place them in a circle. While placing the various branches around the circle, the deer will often do an <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_system" title="Olfactory system">olfactory</a> examination and turn the back of its body towards the marked territories. Other ways the Siberian Musk Deer will mark its territory is by defecating in already marked territories or unclaimed territories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maksimova_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Maksimova-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_Diet">Habitat and Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siberian_musk_deer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Habitat and Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most Siberian musk deer are generally nocturnal inhabiting the mountainous taiga and found in shrub-covered slopes where foods are abundant. The rocky location provides crevices and crags for the musk deer to hide from many predators, such as <a href="/wiki/Lynx" title="Lynx">lynx</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wolverine" title="Wolverine">wolverines</a>.</p>

<p>Musk deer have a preference for easily digestible nutritious foods that are both rich in protein and low in fiber. During periods of winter, musk deer can survive in even poorer food quality ranging in foods that are low in proteins but are high in energy and can be easily digested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The majority of their diet consists mostly of lichens, pine needles, leaves, and tree barks. During the winter, 99% of musk deerâs diet are lichens. Siberian Musk deer have a preference for easily digestible nutritious foods.</p>

<p>It is largely nocturnal, and migrates only over short distances. It prefers altitudes of more than 2600Â m. Adults are small, weighing 7â17Â kg.</p>
<p>The Siberian musk deer is classified as Vulnerable by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>. It is hunted for its <a href="/wiki/Musk" title="Musk">musk</a> gland, which fetches prices as high as $45,000 per kilogram. Only a few tens of grams can be extracted from an adult male. It is possible to remove the gland without killing the deer, but this is seldom done. In 2016, the Korean company Sooam Biotech was reported to be attempting to clone the Siberian musk deer to help conserve the species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewScientist500_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewScientist500-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most striking characteristics of the Siberian musk deer are its tusks and kangaroo-like face. Males grow the teeth for display instead of antlers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>A distinct subspecies roams the island of <a href="/wiki/Sakhalin" title="Sakhalin">Sakhalin</a>.</p>

<p>Siberian musk deer <a href="/wiki/Preputial_gland" title="Preputial gland">preputial gland</a> secretions are constituted of free fatty acids and phenols (10%), waxes (38%) and steroids. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cholestanol" title="Cholestanol">Cholestanol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cholesterol" title="Cholesterol">cholesterol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Androsterone" title="Androsterone">androsterone</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=%CE%944-3%CE%B1-hydroxy-17-ketoandrostene&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Î4-3Î±-hydroxy-17-ketoandrostene (page does not exist)">Î4-3Î±-hydroxy-17-ketoandrostene</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=5%CE%B2,3%CE%B1-hydroxy-17-ketoandrostane&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="5Î²,3Î±-hydroxy-17-ketoandrostane (page does not exist)">5Î²,3Î±-hydroxy-17-ketoandrostane</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=5%CE%B1,3%CE%B2,17%CE%B1-dihydroxyandrostane&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="5Î±,3Î²,17Î±-dihydroxyandrostane (page does not exist)">5Î±,3Î²,17Î±-dihydroxyandrostane</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=5%CE%B2,3%CE%B1,17%CE%B2-dihydroxyandrostane&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="5Î²,3Î±,17Î²-dihydroxyandrostane (page does not exist)">5Î²,3Î±,17Î²-dihydroxyandrostane</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=5%CE%B2,3%CE%B1,17%CE%B1-dihydroxyandrostane&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="5Î²,3Î±,17Î±-dihydroxyandrostane (page does not exist)">5Î²,3Î±,17Î±-dihydroxyandrostane</a> can be isolated from the steroid fraction. 3-Methylpentadecanone (<a href="/wiki/Muscone" title="Muscone">muscone</a>) was not identified among the secretion lipids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats">Threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siberian_musk_deer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The decline of the Siberian Musk Deerâs population began in China where most of the deer population was abundant. Most notably in the <a href="/wiki/Sichuan_Basin" title="Sichuan Basin">Sichuan plains</a>, the musk production was accounted for 80% of the domestic trade in the 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qi2_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Qi2-4">[4]</a></sup> New sightings of musk deer was later spotted in the upper northeast Asia and Russia; these spotted places soon opened their own musk market. After the 1980s, the production begins to steadily decline due to hunting for their musk glands. Then the cycle of over-harvesting the deerâs musk continued until the exploitation severely reduced the musk deerâs population.</p>
<p>Another threat comes from the habit loss by deforestation. For a long period, China cut more of its forest than they could replant. 200million cm<sup>3</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qi2_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Qi2-4">[4]</a></sup> of Chinaâs forest recourses were cut down in the past 25 years in order to harvest the timber stock in trade for commerce. <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">Deforestation</a> is a severe threat to the musk deerâs long term survival because the deer can only live in a few areas.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_Action">Conservation Action</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siberian_musk_deer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Conservation Action">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Siberian musk deer is considered vulnerable, but is slowly declining to endangerment. In Russia, the Siberian Musk Deer is protected as Very Rare under part <i>7.1 of the Law of the Mongolian Animal Kingdom (2000)</i> and also under the 1995 <i>Mongolian Hunting Law</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyambayar2_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nyambayar2-3">[3]</a></sup> The musk deers are also protected under the National Parks which accounts for approximately 13% of the Siberian Musk Deer population.</p>
<p>In China, at the international level, trading musk is controlled through <a href="/wiki/CITES" title="CITES">CITES</a>. All trades regarding musk is permitted to strict regulation to avoid exploitation of the survival of the deer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_efforts_include">Other efforts include</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siberian_musk_deer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other efforts include">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Breeding the musk deer in captivity farms in both Russia and China.</li>
<li>China has enacted many law and regulations to preserve rare animals and their habitats, many such as <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_Protection_Act,_1972" title="Wildlife Protection Act, 1972">Wildlife Protection Law</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Forestry_law" title="Forestry law">Forestry Law</a>.</li>
<li>Organization such as the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature" title="World Wide Fund for Nature">World Wildlife Fund</a> (WWF) is working through the American government to urge the efforts of protecting the musk deer and many other endangered speciesâ population.</li>
</ul>
