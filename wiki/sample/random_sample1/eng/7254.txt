<p><b>Sociobiology</b> is a field of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific" title="Scientific">scientific</a> study that is based on the hypothesis that <a href="/wiki/Social_behavior" title="Social behavior">social behavior</a> has resulted from <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> and attempts to examine and explain <a href="/wiki/Social_behavior" title="Social behavior">social behavior</a> within that context. It is a branch of <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> that deals with social behavior, and also draws from <a href="/wiki/Ethology" title="Ethology">ethology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Population_genetics" title="Population genetics">population genetics</a>, and other disciplines. Within the study of human <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">societies</a>, sociobiology is closely allied to <a href="/wiki/Darwinian_anthropology" title="Darwinian anthropology">Darwinian anthropology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_behavioral_ecology" title="Human behavioral ecology">human behavioral ecology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_psychology" title="Evolutionary psychology">evolutionary psychology</a>.</p>
<p>Sociobiology investigates social behaviors, such as mating patterns, territorial fights, pack hunting, and the hive society of social insects. It argues that just as selection pressure led to animals evolving useful ways of interacting with the natural environment, it led to the genetic evolution of advantageous social behavior.</p>
<p>While the term "sociobiology" can be traced to the 1940s, the concept did not gain major recognition until the publication of <a href="/wiki/E._O._Wilson" title="E. O. Wilson">Edward O. Wilson</a>'s book <i><a href="/wiki/Sociobiology:_The_New_Synthesis" title="Sociobiology: The New Synthesis">Sociobiology: The New Synthesis</a></i> in 1975. The new field quickly became the subject of <a href="/wiki/Scientific_controversy" title="Scientific controversy">controversy</a>. Criticism, most notably from <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lewontin" title="Richard Lewontin">Richard Lewontin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Jay_Gould" title="Stephen Jay Gould">Stephen Jay Gould</a>, centered on the contention that genes play a role in human behavior and that traits such as aggressiveness can be explained by biology rather than social environment. Sociobiologists responded to the criticism by pointing to the complex relationship between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nature_and_nurture" title="Nature and nurture">nature and nurture</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociobiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>E. O. Wilson defines sociobiology as : “The extension of population biology and evolutionary theory to social organization”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sociobiology is based on the premise that some behaviors (social and individual) are at least partly inherited and can be affected by <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a>. It begins with the idea that behaviors have evolved over time, similar to the way that physical traits are thought to have evolved. It predicts that animals will act in ways that have proven to be evolutionarily successful over time. This can, among other things, result in the formation of complex social processes conducive to evolutionary fitness.</p>
<p>The discipline seeks to explain behavior as a product of natural selection. Behavior is therefore seen as an effort to preserve one's genes in the population. Inherent in sociobiological reasoning is the idea that certain genes or gene combinations that influence particular behavioral traits can be <a href="/wiki/Heredity" title="Heredity">inherited</a> from generation to generation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introductory_example">Introductory example</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociobiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Introductory example">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For example, newly dominant male lions often will kill cubs in the pride that were not sired by them. This behavior is adaptive in evolutionary terms because killing the cubs eliminates competition for their own offspring and causes the nursing females to come into heat faster, thus allowing more of his genes to enter into the population. Sociobiologists would view this instinctual cub-killing behavior as being inherited through the genes of successfully reproducing male lions, whereas non-killing behavior may have "died out" as those lions were less successful in reproducing.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Support_for_premise">Support for premise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociobiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Support for premise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Genetic mouse mutants have now been harnessed to illustrate the power that genes exert on behaviour. For example, the transcription factor FEV (aka Pet1) has been shown, through its role in maintaining the serotonergic system in the brain, to be required for normal aggressive and anxiety-like behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Thus, when FEV is genetically deleted from the mouse genome, male mice will instantly attack other males, whereas their wild-type counterparts take significantly longer to initiate violent behaviour. In addition, FEV has been shown to be required for correct maternal behaviour in mice, such that their offspring do not survive unless cross-fostered to other wild-type female mice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>A genetic basis for instinctive behavioural traits among non-human species, such as in the above example, is commonly accepted among many biologists; however, attempting to use a genetic basis to explain complex behaviours in human societies has remained extremely controversial.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociobiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Plos_wilson.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1054" data-file-width="1240" height="204" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Plos_wilson.jpg/240px-Plos_wilson.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Plos_wilson.jpg/360px-Plos_wilson.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Plos_wilson.jpg/480px-Plos_wilson.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Plos_wilson.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/E._O._Wilson" title="E. O. Wilson">E. O. Wilson</a>, a central figure in the history of sociobiology.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">OED</a>, the ethologist John Paul Scott<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> coined the word "sociobiology" at a 1946 conference on genetics and social behaviour, and it became widely used after it was popularized by Edward O. Wilson in his 1975 book, <i>Sociobiology: The New Synthesis</i>. However, the influence of evolution on behavior has been of interest to biologists and philosophers since soon after the discovery of evolution itself. <a href="/wiki/Peter_Kropotkin" title="Peter Kropotkin">Peter Kropotkin</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Mutual_Aid:_A_Factor_of_Evolution" title="Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution">Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution</a></i>, written in the early 1890s, is a popular example. Antecedents of modern sociobiological thinking can be traced to the 1960s and the work of such biologists as <a href="/wiki/Richard_D._Alexander" title="Richard D. Alexander">Richard D. Alexander</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Trivers" title="Robert Trivers">Robert Trivers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_D._Hamilton" title="William D. Hamilton">William D. Hamilton</a>. The idea of the inheritance of behaviour arose from J B S Haldane's idea about how "altruistic behaviour" (see <a href="/wiki/Altruism" title="Altruism">Altruism</a>) could be passed from generation to generation <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Nonetheless, it was Wilson's book that pioneered and popularized the attempt to explain the evolutionary mechanics behind social behaviors such as <a href="/wiki/Altruism" title="Altruism">altruism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aggression" title="Aggression">aggression</a>, and nurturence, primarily in ants (Wilson's own research specialty) but also in other animals (bees, wasps and termites).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The final chapter of the book is devoted to sociobiological explanations of human behavior, and Wilson later wrote a <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a> winning book, <i><a href="/wiki/On_Human_Nature" title="On Human Nature">On Human Nature</a></i>, that addressed human behavior specifically.</p>
<p>Edward H. Hagen writes in <i>The Handbook of Evolutionary Psychology</i> that sociobiology is, despite the public controversy regarding the applications to humans, "one of the scientific triumphs of the twentieth century." "Sociobiology is now part of the core research and curriculum of virtually all biology departments, and it is a foundation of the work of almost all field biologists" Sociobiological research on nonhuman organisms has increased dramatically and continuously in the world's top scientific journals such as <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i>. The more general term <a href="/wiki/Behavioral_ecology" title="Behavioral ecology">behavioral ecology</a> is commonly used as substitute for the term sociobiology in order to avoid the public controversy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evolutionary_Psychology_2005_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Evolutionary_Psychology_2005-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociobiological_theory">Sociobiological theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociobiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sociobiological theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sociobiologists believe that <a href="/wiki/Human_nature" title="Human nature">human behavior</a>, as well as nonhuman animal behavior, can be partly explained as the outcome of natural selection. They contend that in order to fully understand behavior, it must be analyzed in terms of evolutionary considerations.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">Natural selection</a> is fundamental to evolutionary theory. Variants of hereditary traits which increase an organism's ability to survive and reproduce will be more greatly represented in subsequent generations, i.e., they will be "selected for". Thus, inherited behavioral mechanisms that allowed an <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organism</a> a greater chance of surviving and/or reproducing in the past are more likely to survive in present organisms. That inherited adaptive behaviors are present in nonhuman <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">animal species</a> has been multiply demonstrated by biologists, and it has become a foundation of <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_biology" title="Evolutionary biology">evolutionary biology</a>. However, there is continued resistance by some researchers over the application of evolutionary models to humans, particularly from within the social sciences, where culture has long been assumed to be the predominant driver of behavior.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nikolaas_Tinbergen_1978.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="559" data-file-width="470" height="214" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Nikolaas_Tinbergen_1978.jpg/180px-Nikolaas_Tinbergen_1978.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Nikolaas_Tinbergen_1978.jpg/270px-Nikolaas_Tinbergen_1978.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Nikolaas_Tinbergen_1978.jpg/360px-Nikolaas_Tinbergen_1978.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nikolaas_Tinbergen_1978.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Nikolaas_Tinbergen" title="Nikolaas Tinbergen">Nikolaas Tinbergen</a>, whose work influenced sociobiology.</div>

</div>
<p>Sociobiology is based upon two fundamental premises:</p>
<ul>
<li>Certain behavioral traits are inherited,</li>
<li>Inherited behavioral traits have been honed by natural selection. Therefore, these traits were probably "adaptive" in the species` evolutionarily evolved environment.</li>
</ul>
<p>Sociobiology uses <a href="/wiki/Nikolaas_Tinbergen" title="Nikolaas Tinbergen">Nikolaas Tinbergen</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Tinbergen%27s_four_questions" title="Tinbergen's four questions">four categories of questions</a> and explanations of animal behavior. Two categories are at the species level; two, at the individual level. The species-level categories (often called “ultimate explanations”) are</p>
<ul>
<li>the function (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adaptation</a>) that a behavior serves and</li>
<li>the evolutionary process (i.e., phylogeny) that resulted in this functionality.</li>
</ul>
<p>The individual-level categories (often called “proximate explanations”) are</p>
<ul>
<li>the development of the individual (i.e., ontogeny) and</li>
<li>the proximate mechanism (e.g., brain anatomy and hormones).</li>
</ul>
<p>Sociobiologists are interested in how behavior can be explained logically as a result of selective pressures in the history of a species. Thus, they are often interested in <a href="/wiki/Instinct" title="Instinct">instinctive</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intuition_(knowledge)" title="Intuition (knowledge)">intuitive</a> behavior, and in explaining the similarities, rather than the differences, between cultures. For example, mothers within many species of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammals" title="Mammals">mammals</a> – including humans – are very protective of their <a href="/wiki/Offspring" title="Offspring">offspring</a>. Sociobiologists reason that this protective behavior likely evolved over time because it helped those individuals which had the characteristic to survive and reproduce. Over time, individuals who exhibited such protective behaviours would have had more surviving offspring than those who did not display such behaviours, such that this parental protection would increase in frequency in the population. In this way, the social behavior is believed to have evolved in a fashion similar to other types of nonbehavioral <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adaptations</a>, such as (for example) fur or the sense of smell.</p>
<p>Individual genetic advantage often fails to explain certain social behaviors as a result of gene-centred selection, and evolution may also act upon <a href="/wiki/Social_group" title="Social group">groups</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The mechanisms responsible for <a href="/wiki/Group_selection" title="Group selection">group selection</a> employ <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paradigms" title="Paradigms">paradigms</a> and population statistics borrowed from <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_game_theory" title="Evolutionary game theory">evolutionary game theory</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E.O._Wilson" title="E.O. Wilson">E.O. Wilson</a> argued that altruistic individuals must reproduce their own altruistic genetic traits for altruism to survive. When altruists lavish their resources on non-altruists at the expense of their own kind, the altruists tend to die out and the others tend to grow. In other words, altruism is more likely to survive if altruists practice the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethic" title="Ethic">ethic</a> that "charity begins at home". Altruism is defined as "a concern for the welfare of others". An extreme example of altruism involves a soldier risking his life to help a fellow soldier. This example raises questions about how altruistic genes can be passed on if this soldier dies without having any children to exhibit the same altruistic traits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Within sociobiology, a social behavior is first explained as a sociobiological <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypothesis</a> by finding an <a href="/wiki/Evolutionarily_stable_strategy" title="Evolutionarily stable strategy">evolutionarily stable strategy</a> that matches the observed behavior. Stability of a strategy can be difficult to prove, but usually, a well-formed strategy will predict gene frequencies. The hypothesis can be supported by establishing a correlation between the gene frequencies predicted by the strategy, and those expressed in a population.</p>
<p>Altruism between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_insect" title="Social insect">social insects</a> and littermates has been explained in such a way. Altruistic behavior, behavior that increases the reproductive fitness of others at the apparent expense of the altruist,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> in some animals has been correlated to the degree of <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a> shared between altruistic individuals. A quantitative description of <a href="/wiki/Infanticide" title="Infanticide">infanticide</a> by male harem-mating animals when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha_male" title="Alpha male">alpha male</a> is displaced as well as <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodent</a> female infanticide and fetal resorption are active areas of study. In general, females with more bearing opportunities may value offspring less, and may also arrange bearing opportunities to maximize the <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> and protection from mates.</p>
<p>An important concept in sociobiology is that temperamental traits within a gene pool and between gene pools exist in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological" title="Ecological">ecological</a> balance. Just as an expansion of a <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a> population might encourage the expansion of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf">wolf</a> population, an expansion of altruistic traits within a gene pool may also encourage the expansion of individuals with dependent traits.</p>
<p>Sociobiology is sometimes associated with arguments over the "genetic" basis of intelligence. While sociobiology is predicated on the observation that genes do affect behavior, it is perfectly consistent to be a sociobiologist while arguing that measured IQ variations between individuals reflect mainly cultural or economic rather than genetic factors. However, many critics point out that the usefulness of sociobiology as an explanatory tool breaks down once a trait is so variable as to no longer be exposed to selective pressures. In order to explain aspects of human intelligence as the outcome of selective pressures, it must be demonstrated that those aspects are inherited, or genetic, but this does not necessarily imply differences among individuals: a common genetic inheritance could be shared by <i>all</i> humans, just as the genes responsible for number of limbs are shared by all individuals.</p>
<p>Studies of human behavior genetics have generally found behavioral traits such as creativity, extroversion, aggressiveness, and <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_quotient" title="Intelligence quotient">IQ</a> have high <a href="/wiki/Heritability" title="Heritability">heritability</a>. The researchers who carry out those studies are careful to point out that heritability does not constrain the influence that environmental or cultural factors may have on those traits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">Criminality</a> is actively under study, but extremely controversial. There are arguments that in some environments criminal behavior might be adaptive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The novelist <a href="/wiki/Elias_Canetti" title="Elias Canetti">Elias Canetti</a> also has noted applications of sociobiological theory to cultural practices such as slavery and autocracy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Differences_from_evolutionary_psychology">Differences from evolutionary psychology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociobiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Differences from evolutionary psychology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sociobiology differs in important ways from <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_psychology" title="Evolutionary psychology">evolutionary psychology</a>. Evolutionary psychology studies the animal nervous system from an evolutionary perspective, including aspects such as vision and navigation that are not necessarily related to social behavior. Sociobiology is restricted to the biology of social behavior but also studies organisms like plants. Evolutionary psychologists focus on the neural mechanisms that cause behavior whereas sociobiologists usually study only behavior. Evolutionary psychology emphasizes that, for humans, neural mechanisms evolved in an ancestral environment that differed from the current environment whereas animal sociobiologists look at animal adaptions to the current environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evolutionary_Psychology_2005_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Evolutionary_Psychology_2005-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociobiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eugenics_Quarterly_to_Social_Biology.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1026" data-file-width="1308" height="235" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Eugenics_Quarterly_to_Social_Biology.jpg/300px-Eugenics_Quarterly_to_Social_Biology.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Eugenics_Quarterly_to_Social_Biology.jpg/450px-Eugenics_Quarterly_to_Social_Biology.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Eugenics_Quarterly_to_Social_Biology.jpg/600px-Eugenics_Quarterly_to_Social_Biology.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eugenics_Quarterly_to_Social_Biology.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
In the decades after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the term "eugenics" had taken on a negative connotation and became increasingly unpopular within academic science. Many organizations and journals that had their origins in the eugenics movement began to distance themselves from the philosophy, as when <i>Eugenics Quarterly</i> became <i>Social Biology</i> in 1969.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Steven_Pinker" title="Steven Pinker">Steven Pinker</a> argues that critics have been overly swayed by politics and a fear of <a href="/wiki/Biological_determinism" title="Biological determinism">biological determinism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[a]</a></sup> accusing among others <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Jay_Gould" title="Stephen Jay Gould">Stephen Jay Gould</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lewontin" title="Richard Lewontin">Richard Lewontin</a> of being "radical scientists", whose stance on human nature is influenced by politics rather than science,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> while Lewontin, <a href="/wiki/Steven_Rose" title="Steven Rose">Steven Rose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leon_Kamin" title="Leon Kamin">Leon Kamin</a> who drew a distinction between the politics and history of an idea and its scientific validity<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lrk84_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-lrk84-17">[16]</a></sup> argue that sociobiology fails on scientific grounds, independent of their political critiques as has Gould.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/E._O._Wilson" title="E. O. Wilson">Wilson</a> and his supporters counter the intellectual link by denying that Wilson had a political agenda, still less a right-wing one. They pointed out that Wilson had personally adopted a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_liberalism" title="American liberalism">liberal</a> political stances and had attracted progressive sympathy for his outspoken <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalism</a>. They argued that as scientists they had a duty to uncover the truth whether that was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Politically_correct" title="Politically correct">politically correct</a> or not. They argued that sociobiology does not necessarily lead to any particular political <a href="/wiki/Ideology" title="Ideology">ideology</a>, as many critics implied. Many subsequent sociobiologists, including <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wright_(journalist)" title="Robert Wright (journalist)">Robert Wright</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anne_Campbell_(academic)" title="Anne Campbell (academic)">Anne Campbell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frans_de_Waal" title="Frans de Waal">Frans de Waal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Blaffer_Hrdy" title="Sarah Blaffer Hrdy">Sarah Blaffer Hrdy</a>, have used sociobiology to argue quite separate points.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">Noam Chomsky</a> has expressed views on sociobiology on several occasions. During a 1976 meeting of the <a href="/wiki/Sociobiology_Study_Group" title="Sociobiology Study Group">Sociobiology Study Group</a>, as reported by Ullica Segerstråle, Chomsky argued for the importance of a sociobiologically informed notion of human nature: "Chomsky even stated that he thought it was important for political radicals to postulate a relatively fixed human nature in order to be able to struggle for a better society. We need a clear view of human needs in order to know the kind of society we want, Chomsky proclaimed. Not surprisingly ... no Chomsky critique of sociobiology emerged."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> Chomsky's argument that human beings are biological organisms and ought to be studied as such, and his criticism of the "blank slate" doctrine in the social sciences (which would inspire a great deal of <a href="/wiki/Steven_Pinker" title="Steven Pinker">Steven Pinker</a>'s and others' work in evolutionary psychology), are revealed in <i>Reflections on Language</i>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The development of personality, behavior patterns, and cognitive structures in higher organisms has often been approached in a very different way. It is generally assumed that in these domains, social environment is the dominant factor. The structures of mind that develop over time are taken to be arbitrary and accidental; there is no “human nature” apart from what develops as a specific historical product. According to this view, typical of empiricist speculation, certain general principles of learning that are common in their essentials to all (or some large class of) organisms suffice to account for the cognitive structures attained by humans, structures which incorporate the principles by which human behavior is planned, organized, and controlled.</p>
<p>But human cognitive systems, when seriously investigated, prove to be no less marvelous and intricate than the physical structures that develop in the life of the organism. Why, then, should we not study the acquisition of a cognitive structure such as language more or less as we study some complex bodily organ?<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Chomsky has hinted at the possible reconciliation of his anarchist political views and sociobiology in a discussion of <a href="/wiki/Peter_Kropotkin" title="Peter Kropotkin">Peter Kropotkin</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Mutual_Aid:_A_Factor_of_Evolution" title="Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution">Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution</a></i>, which focused more on altruism than aggression, suggesting that anarchist societies were feasible because of an innate human tendency to cooperate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wilson's claims that he had never meant to imply what <i>ought</i> to be, only what <i>is</i> the case are supported by his writings, which are descriptive, not prescriptive. However, some critics have argued that the language of sociobiology sometimes slips from "is" to "ought".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lrk84_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-lrk84-17">[16]</a></sup> Views such as this, however, are often criticized as examples of the <a href="/wiki/Naturalistic_fallacy" title="Naturalistic fallacy">naturalistic fallacy</a>, when reasoning jumps from descriptions about what <i>is</i> to prescriptions about what <i>ought</i> to be. (A common example is the justification of <a href="/wiki/Militarism" title="Militarism">militarism</a> if scientific evidence showed warfare was part of human nature.) It has also been argued that opposition to stances considered anti-social, such as <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_nepotism" title="Ethnic nepotism">ethnic nepotism</a>, are based on <a href="/wiki/Morality" title="Morality">moral</a> assumptions, not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioscience" title="Bioscience">bioscientific</a> assumptions, meaning that it is not vulnerable to being disproved by bioscientific advances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pinker2002_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pinker2002-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:145</sup> The history of this debate, and others related to it, are covered in detail by Cronin (1992), Segerstråle (2000), and Alcock (2001).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
