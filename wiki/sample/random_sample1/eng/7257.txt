<p><b>Social constructivism</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Sociological_theory" title="Sociological theory">sociological theory</a> of <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a> according to which human development is socially situated and knowledge is constructed through interaction with others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKinley_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-McKinley-1">[1]</a></sup> The phrase was coined by <a href="/wiki/Peter_L._Berger" title="Peter L. Berger">Peter L. Berger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Luckmann" title="Thomas Luckmann">Thomas Luckmann</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Social_Construction_of_Reality" title="The Social Construction of Reality">The Social Construction of Reality</a></i>. Based on a combination of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfred_Schutz" title="Alfred Schutz">Alfred Schutz</a>' <a href="/wiki/Sociology_of_knowledge" title="Sociology of knowledge">Sociology of Knowledge</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Durkheim" title="Durkheim">Durkheim</a>'s concept of institutions, their theory aims to answer the question of how subjective meaning becomes a social fact.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_constructionism">Social constructionism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_constructivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Social constructionism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like <a href="/wiki/Social_constructionism" title="Social constructionism">social constructionism</a>, social constructivism states that people work together to construct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_artifact#Artifact" title="Social artifact">artifacts</a>. While social constructionism focuses on the artifacts that are created through the social interactions of a group, social constructivism focuses on an individual's learning that takes place because of his or her interactions in a group.</p>
<p>A very simple example is an object like a cup. The object can be used for many things, but its shape does suggest some 'knowledge' about carrying liquids (see also <a href="/wiki/Affordance" title="Affordance">Affordance</a>). A more complex example is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Online_course" title="Online course">online course</a>—not only do the 'shapes' of the software tools indicate certain things about the way online courses should work, but the activities and texts produced within the group as a whole will help shape how each person behaves within that group. A person's cognitive development will also be influenced by the culture that he or she is involved in, such as the language, history and social context.</p>
<p>For a philosophical account of one possible social-constructionist ontology, see the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Representative_realism#Criticism" title="Representative realism">'Criticism' section of <i>Representative realism</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_constructivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>'Strong' social constructivism as a philosophical approach tends to suggest that "the natural world has a small or non-existent role in the construction of scientific knowledge".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Maarten_Boudry" title="Maarten Boudry">Maarten Boudry</a> and Filip Buekens, <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalysis#Freudian_theory" title="Psychoanalysis">Freudian psychoanalysis</a> is a good example of this approach in action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boudry.2C_et_al._.282011.29_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boudry.2C_et_al._.282011.29-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Interestingly, however, Boudry and Buekens do not claim that 'bona fide' science is completely immune from all socialisation and <a href="/wiki/Paradigm_shift#Kuhnian_paradigm_shifts" title="Paradigm shift">paradigm shifts</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> merely that the 'strong' social constructivist claim that <i>all</i> scientific knowledge is constructed ignores the reality of scientific success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boudry.2C_et_al._.282011.29_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Boudry.2C_et_al._.282011.29-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>One characteristic of social constructivism is that it rejects the role of <a href="/wiki/Heroic_theory_of_invention_and_scientific_development" title="Heroic theory of invention and scientific development">superhuman necessity in either the invention/discovery of knowledge or its justification</a>. In the field of invention it looks to contingency as playing an important part in the origin of knowledge, with historical interests and resourcing swaying the direction of mathematical and scientific knowledge growth. In the area of justification while acknowledging the role of logic and reason in testing, it also accepts that the criteria for acceptance vary and change over time. Thus mathematical proofs follow different standards in the present and throughout different periods in the past, as Paul Ernest argues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychology_and_religion">Psychology and religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_constructivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Psychology and religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One branch of social constructivist philosophy is best represented in the works of the psychologist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rocco_Cottone" title="Robert Rocco Cottone">Robert Rocco Cottone</a>. Cottone has taken a radical philosophical position purporting a purest relational realism (an ontology where everything is viewed as relationship). Things, accordingly, only exist in relation to observers who are able to understand their perceptions through social interchange. Cottone merged the works of the cognitive biologist <a href="/wiki/Humberto_Maturana" title="Humberto Maturana">Humberto Maturana</a> with the works of the social psychologist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kenneth_Gergen" title="Kenneth Gergen">Kenneth Gergen</a> to produce a fully relational conception of the process of understanding experience. His most compelling concept is that of "Bracketed Absolute Truth" (also called a "consensuality"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> where a truth is held within a community as absolute, but outside the community it is held by observers as relative to other truths. All understanding of experience is thereby socially constructed, but different communities can construct different interpretations of their shared experience. Truths are never constructed outside of interaction—truth is social. There are as many truths on any one topic as there are communities to construct them. Some truths on one topic may be consistent and others may be contradictory, depending on the perceptual and social linguistic contexts of the groups making the interpretations. Cottone used the example of religion to make his point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cottone_.282011.29_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cottone_.282011.29-8">[8]</a></sup> Different communities may have different conceptions of a god, for example, even though historically they are speaking of the same godly origin (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>). Religion provides a compelling example of how people socially construct their understanding of experience by means of social-linguistic traditions. Each religion, therefore, represents a bracketed absolute truth. Cottone proposed that people operate in a matrix of multilayered consensualities and people progress through life by connecting with, disconnecting from, and continually negotiating through relationships that reflect communities of understanding (e.g., religions, professions, local communities, governments, etc.). He called this process "social trajectory".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cottone_.282011.29_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cottone_.282011.29-8">[8]</a></sup> This branch of social constructivist thought does not purport that individuals socially construct a reality, rather it purports that people construct understanding of experience together, not alone. In effect, there are communities of understanding.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_constructivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Social constructivism has been studied by many educational psychologists, who are concerned with its implications for teaching and learning. Social constructivism extends constructivism by incorporating the role of other actors and culture in development. In this sense it can also be contrasted with <a href="/wiki/Social_learning_theory" title="Social learning theory">social learning theory</a> by stressing interaction over observation. For more on the psychological dimensions of social constructivism, see the work of A. Sullivan Palincsar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Psychological tools are one of the key concepts in Vygotsky's sociocultural perspective.</p>
<p>An instructional strategy grounded in social constructivism that is an area of active research is <a href="/wiki/Computer-supported_collaborative_learning" title="Computer-supported collaborative learning">computer-supported collaborative learning</a> (CSCL). This strategy gives students opportunities to practice 21st-century skills in communication, knowledge sharing, critical thinking and use of relevant technologies found in the workplace.</p>
<p>Additionally, studies on increasing the use of student discussion in the classroom both support and are grounded in theories of social constructivism. There is a full range of advantages that results from the implementation of discussion in the classroom. Participating in group discussion allows students to generalize and transfer their knowledge of classroom learning and builds a strong foundation for communicating ideas orally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reznitskaya.2C_et_al._.282007.29_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reznitskaya.2C_et_al._.282007.29-10">[10]</a></sup> Many studies argue that discussion plays a vital role in increasing student ability to test their ideas, synthesize the ideas of others, and build deeper understanding of what they are learning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reznitskaya.2C_et_al._.282007.29_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reznitskaya.2C_et_al._.282007.29-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weber.2C_et_al._.282008.29_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weber.2C_et_al._.282008.29-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corden_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Corden-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nystrand_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nystrand-13">[13]</a></sup> Large and small group discussion also affords students opportunities to exercise self-regulation, self-determination, and a desire to persevere with tasks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corden_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Corden-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matsumara.2C_et_al_.282008.29_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Matsumara.2C_et_al_.282008.29-14">[14]</a></sup> Additionally, discussion increases student motivation, collaborative skills, and the ability to problem solve.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nystrand_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nystrand-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matsumara.2C_et_al_.282008.29_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Matsumara.2C_et_al_.282008.29-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyson_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dyson-15">[15]</a></sup> Increasing students’ opportunity to talk with one another and discuss their ideas increases their ability to support their thinking, develop reasoning skills, and to argue their opinions persuasively and respectfully.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reznitskaya.2C_et_al._.282007.29_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Reznitskaya.2C_et_al._.282007.29-10">[10]</a></sup> Furthermore, the feeling of community and collaboration in classrooms increases through offering more chances for students to talk together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weber.2C_et_al._.282008.29_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weber.2C_et_al._.282008.29-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Given the advantages that result from discussion, it is surprising that it is not used more often. Studies have found that students are not regularly accustomed to participating in academic discourse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corden_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Corden-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nystrand_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nystrand-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Martin_Nystrand" title="Martin Nystrand">Martin Nystrand</a> argues that teachers rarely choose classroom discussion as an instructional format. The results of Nystrand’s (1996) three-year study focusing on 2400 students in 60 different classrooms indicate that the typical classroom teacher spends under three minutes an hour allowing students to talk about ideas with one another and the teacher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nystrand_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nystrand-13">[13]</a></sup> Even within those three minutes of discussion, most talk is not true discussion because it depends upon teacher-directed questions with predetermined answers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corden_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Corden-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nystrand_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nystrand-13">[13]</a></sup> Multiple observations indicate that students in low socioeconomic schools and lower track classrooms are allowed even fewer opportunities for discussion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weber.2C_et_al._.282008.29_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Weber.2C_et_al._.282008.29-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corden_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Corden-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nystrand_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-Nystrand-13">[13]</a></sup> Teachers who teach as if they value what their students think create learners. Discussion and interactive discourse promote learning because they afford students the opportunity to use language as a demonstration of their independent thoughts. Discussion elicits sustained responses from students that encourage <a href="/wiki/Meaning-making" title="Meaning-making">meaning-making</a> through negotiating with the ideas of others. This type of learning “promotes retention and in-depth processing associated with the cognitive manipulation of information”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nystrand_13-6"><a href="#cite_note-Nystrand-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>One recent branch of work exploring social constructivist perspectives on learning focuses on the role of social technologies and social media in facilitating the generation of socially constructed knowledge and understanding in online environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_writing">Academic writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_constructivism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Academic writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a constructivist approach, the focus is on the sociocultural conventions of academic discourse such as citing evidence, hedging and boosting claims, interpreting the literature to back one's own claims, and addressing counter claims. These conventions are inherent to a constructivist approach as they place value on the communicative, interpersonal nature of academic writing with a strong focus on how the reader receives the message. The act of citing others’ work is more than accurate attribution; it is an important exercise in critical thinking in the construction of an authorial self.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKinley_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-McKinley-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyson_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dyson-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
