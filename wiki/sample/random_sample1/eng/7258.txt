<p><b>Socialist Alternative</b> (<b>SA</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Trotskyism" title="Trotskyism">Trotskyist</a> revolutionary political party active in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. It describes itself as "a national organization fighting in our workplaces, communities, and campuses against the exploitation and injustices people face every day." It describes itself as "a community of activists fighting against budget cuts in public services; fighting for <a href="/wiki/Living_wage" title="Living wage">living wage</a> jobs and militant, democratic unions; and people of all colors speaking out against racism and attacks on immigrants, students organizing against tuition hikes and war, women and men fighting sexism and homophobia."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SA_About_Page_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-SA_About_Page-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Socialist Alternative's highest profile public representative is Seattle City Councillor <a href="/wiki/Kshama_Sawant" title="Kshama Sawant">Kshama Sawant</a> who was elected in November 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is active in over 50 cities in the United States including <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oakland" title="Oakland">Oakland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madison,_Wisconsin" title="Madison, Wisconsin">Madison</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tampa" title="Tampa">Tampa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mobile,_Alabama" title="Mobile, Alabama">Mobile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austin" title="Austin">Austin</a> and more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SA_About_Page_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-SA_About_Page-2">[2]</a></sup> In September of 2013 it began publishing a monthly newspaper called "Socialist Alternative"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> along with various local newsletters and media outlets, including a radio show in the Boston area.</p>
<p>SA is a member of <a href="/wiki/Committee_for_a_Workers%27_International" title="Committee for a Workers' International">Committee for a Workers' International</a>, an international organization of Trotskyist parties.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist_Alternative_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Socialist Alternative was originally formed as Labor Militant in 1986 by members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Committee_for_a_Workers_International" title="Committee for a Workers International">Committee for a Workers International</a> who had moved to the United States. Labor Militant was a small group with its membership numbering mostly of trade union members. By the mid-1990s, Labor Militant became part of a campaign to form the US Labor Party where it was in the leadership of the New York Metro Chapter. The NY Metro Chapter, the largest in the country, saw Labor Militant and its allies run again for the leadership of the chapter under the "United Action" slate only to be defeated in an Executive Committee election. Labor Militant members and the United Action slate had argued that the Labor Party should vigorously run candidates against the Democrats, whereas the national leadership of the Labor Party refused to take such an approach. After the election, the New York Labor Party State Executive upheld the election results while suspending the NY Metro Chapter and several of its officers, eventually shutting down the chapter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the late 1990s, Labor Militant changed its name to Socialist Alternative to reflect what was classified as a change in the political period<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> From 1998 to 2002, the Socialist Alternative party was active in the <a href="/wiki/Anti-globalization_movement" title="Anti-globalization movement">anti-globalization movement</a>. It was present at many of the major protests during this time, including the <a href="/wiki/Anti-globalization_movement#Seattle.2FN30" title="Anti-globalization movement">N30 Protests in Seattle</a>. At these protests it argued that the movement should take up the key demands of "abolish the <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">IMF</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> and the <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">WTO</a>", "cancel the international debt", "papers for all undocumented immigrants" and "take the banks and financial institutions into public ownership".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Global_Capitalism_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Global_Capitalism-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004 Socialist Alternative party members initiated Youth Against War and Racism (YAWR) as a sustained campaign against the wars in <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%93present)" title="War in Afghanistan (2001–present)">Afghanistan</a>. YAWR worked mainly in high schools primarily in <a href="/wiki/Counter-recruitment" title="Counter-recruitment">counter-recruitment</a> activism in several cities. In Seattle in 2005 several hundred high school students walked out of class in order to march in protest of the war in Iraq causing conflict with parents and school officials who contended that the students should focus on school during the day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Following protests by members of YAWR and Socialist Alternative against military recruitment in schools, the Seattle School Board enacted some restrictions on military recruiters at Seattle High School's. The changes included limiting military recruiters to visiting twice a year to each school despite the demands by the YAWR protesters for a total ban on military recruitment at schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Socialist Alternative party supported the candidacy of <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Ralph Nader</a> during the 1996, 2000, 2004 and 2008 <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election" title="United States presidential election">presidential elections</a>. In 2012, they supported Green Party candidate Jill Stein in her run. Despite criticism from other socialist groups about supporting 'bourgeois candidates', Socialist Alternative argued that Stein supported a Green New Deal jobs program, ending wars, canceling student debt, a single-payer health care system, and other reforms supported by the party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the time leading up to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2008" title="United States presidential election, 2008">2008 presidential election</a> the Socialist Alternative party criticized <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-POTUS_Tracker_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-POTUS_Tracker-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Democrats_Hope_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Democrats_Hope-12">[12]</a></sup> pointing to his pro-free market stance on job creation, his record in congress of voting in favor of bills such as the <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Intelligence_Surveillance_Act" title="Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act">Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act</a>, his stance on healthcare reform, and on other issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beyond_Rhetoric_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beyond_Rhetoric-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, the Socialist Alternative party garnered attention when it successfully elected a member, <a href="/wiki/Kshama_Sawant" title="Kshama Sawant">Kshama Sawant</a>, to the Seattle City Council; Sawant is one of the few elected Socialists in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Calls_for_a_.22Mass_Workers.27_Party.22">Calls for a "Mass Workers' Party"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist_Alternative_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Calls for a &quot;Mass Workers' Party&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 2008, following Barack Obama's victory in the presidential election, Socialist Alternative called for "a mass workers' party" that would draw together workers, young people and activists from workplace, community, civil rights, environmental and antiwar campaigns, in order to provide a fighting, political alternative to what they called the pro-big business parties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SA_About_Page_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-SA_About_Page-2">[2]</a></sup> It paid specific attention to the role of unions in this push because according to them, unions and other social movement organizations needed to stop funding and supporting the Democratic and Republican Parties and instead organize independent left-wing, anti-corporate candidates and coalitions as a first step toward building a "workers' party."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SA_About_Page_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-SA_About_Page-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, the Socialist Alternative party proposed that the Occupy movement should run its own candidates as part of a challenge to what it called the "two-party corporate duopoly" in politics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The following year, the party would run its own candidates in Boston, Minneapolis and Seattle<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> resulting in the election of <a href="/wiki/Kshama_Sawant" title="Kshama Sawant">Kshama Sawant</a> in Seattle and the defeat of Ty Moore in Minneapolis.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trotskyism">Trotskyism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist_Alternative_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Trotskyism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Socialist Alternative party advocates socialist democracy as an alternative to <a href="/wiki/Bureaucratic_collectivism" title="Bureaucratic collectivism">bureaucratic socialism</a> of the former <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and the capitalist democratic model which it alleges is designed to only benefit the "ruling class and disenfranchise working people". The party proposes that a socialist society would change the relationship with "working people" running the economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The party holds that the former Soviet Union was not socialist, but instead a "tragic degeneration" of the <a href="/wiki/October_Revolution" title="October Revolution">Russian Revolution</a> and the socialist tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Socialism_FAQs_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Socialism_FAQs-18">[18]</a></sup> While it views the Russian Revolution positively as a mass democratic revolution of the working class in Russia, it opposes <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Great_Purge" title="Great Purge">reign of terror</a> following the death of Vladimir Lenin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Socialism_FAQs_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Socialism_FAQs-18">[18]</a></sup> Like other <a href="/wiki/Leninism" title="Leninism">Leninist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trotskyism" title="Trotskyism">Trotskyist</a> parties it upholds the principles of <a href="/wiki/Democratic_centralism" title="Democratic centralism">democratic centralism</a> in order to ensure bottom-up democracy among party members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Issues">Issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist_Alternative_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The party's ideology is based upon Marxism and Trotskyism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-socialistalternative.org_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-socialistalternative.org-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>The party advocates nationalizing the top 500 corporations and banks within the U.S. economy and running them under the "democratic management of elected representatives of the workers and the broader public."</li>
<li>The party advocates for creating "living-wage union jobs for all the unemployed through a massive public works program to develop mass transit, renewable energy, infrastructure, healthcare, education, and affordable housing."</li>
<li>The party wants to replace all health insurance companies with a publicly funded single-payer system.</li>
<li>The party wants the federal government to bail out states in order to prevent cuts and layoffs. It seeks a massive increase in taxes on the rich and corporations.</li>
<li>The party wants to raise the federal minimum wage to $15/hour, adjusted annually for cost of living increases and regional differences, as a step towards a living wage for all.</li>
<li>The party wants a minimum guaranteed weekly income of $600/week for the unemployed, disabled, stay-at-home parents, the elderly, and others unable to work.</li>
<li>The party wants to end home foreclosures and evictions, primarily through nationalization of major banks.</li>
<li>The party advocates nationalizing bankrupt and failing companies and reshaping them for the purpose of green production.</li>
<li>The party seeks to establish free, high quality public education for everyone from pre-school through college, full funding for schools to dramatically lower teacher-student ratios, and stopping the focus on high-stakes testing and stopping private education institutions by putting them under public ownership.</li>
<li>The party seeks to repeal all "anti-union" laws. It advocates for democratic unions run by union members in order to advocate for better pay, working conditions, social services. The party advocates for full-time union officials to be regularly elected and receive the average wage of those they represent.</li>
<li>The party wants to establish a guaranteed living wage pension.</li>
<li>The party wants to shorten the workweek with no loss in pay and benefits and to share out the work with the unemployed and create new jobs.</li>
</ul>
<p>The party takes a strong anti-war stance, and as such advocates:</p>
<ul>
<li>The party holds that the US needs to completely withdraw from Afghanistan and Iraq, bring all US troops stationed outside the US home and end all combat operations overseas.</li>
<li>The party wants to massively slash the military budget and divert funds into social programs instead.</li>
<li>The party seeks to abolish the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PATRIOT_Act" title="PATRIOT Act">PATRIOT Act</a> and any other legislation it believes "infringe on American's rights."</li>
</ul>
<p>The party takes strong stances on equal rights and as such advocates:</p>
<ul>
<li>The party seeks to fight discrimination on the grounds of race, nationality, gender, sexual orientation, religion, disability, age, and all other forms of prejudice. It seeks to establish an equal pay for equal work system.</li>
<li>The party wants to end what it calls the "police brutality and the institutional racism of the criminal justice system", which it wants to do by investing in rehabilitation, job-training, and living-wage jobs, instead of prisons.</li>
<li>The party seeks to abolish capital punishment.</li>
<li>The party wants full amnesty for all undocumented immigrants.</li>
<li>The party wants to fight sexual harassment, violence against women, and all forms of sexism.</li>
<li>The party seeks to support abortion rights. It wants free reproductive services, including all forms of birth control and safe, accessible abortions, comprehensive sex education, paid maternity and paternity leave, and fully subsidized, high-quality child care.</li>
<li>The party advocates for equal rights for lesbian, gay, bisexual, and transgender people, including same-sex marriage.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campaigns">Campaigns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist_Alternative_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Campaigns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Jobs_Not_Cuts.22">"Jobs Not Cuts"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist_Alternative_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title='Edit section: "Jobs Not Cuts"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Socialist Alternative initiated a national campaign called "Jobs Not Cuts" in the fall of 2011 in reaction to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_debt-ceiling_crisis_of_2011" title="United States debt-ceiling crisis of 2011">debt ceiling crisis</a> and subsequent <a href="/wiki/Budget_Control_Act_of_2011" title="Budget Control Act of 2011">Budget Control Act</a> passed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Congress" title="US Congress">US Congress</a> in August 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnnmoney_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnnmoney-20">[20]</a></sup> The bill called on the Federal Government to make $2.1 trillion in cuts the Federal budget, and issued the formation of a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress_Joint_Select_Committee_on_Deficit_Reduction" title="United States Congress Joint Select Committee on Deficit Reduction">"Supercommittee"</a> to decide how these cuts would be made. The campaign was endorsed by notable left-wing public figures such as <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">Noam Chomsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Hedges" title="Chris Hedges">Chris Hedges</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cindy_Sheehan" title="Cindy Sheehan">Cindy Sheehan</a>, and organizations such as the Massachusetts Nurses Association and <a href="/wiki/Veterans_for_Peace" title="Veterans for Peace">Veterans for Peace</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The goal of the campaign was to hold a national week of action from November 16 to 23 in protest against these cuts, and advocating for a mass public works project that could create jobs. Part of its demands were that the U.S. ended military involvement in wars Iraq and Afghanistan and dramatic tax increases on the wealthy in order to fund the project<a href="/wiki/American_upper_class" title="American upper class">.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Massive_Budget_Cuts_Coming_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Massive_Budget_Cuts_Coming-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Occupy_Movement">The Occupy Movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist_Alternative_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Occupy Movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occupy_Movement" title="Occupy Movement">Occupy Movement</a> began in the fall of 2011, Socialist Alternative became active within the movement in cities across the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-23">[23]</a></sup> The party issued a statement of solidarity to the movement on October 6, 2011. The party argued that the movement should develop concrete demands along working class lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-occupyws_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-occupyws-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Occupy_Homes_MN">Occupy Homes MN</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist_Alternative_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Occupy Homes MN">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>When the encampment at Occupy Minneapolis began to depreciate, Socialist Alternative worked to continue the Occupy Homes MN campaign and push the Occupy movement in Minneapolis into an anti-foreclosure campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Building_Foreclosure_Free_Neighborhoods_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Building_Foreclosure_Free_Neighborhoods-25">[25]</a></sup> The party assisted in outreach, planning, and organizing public meetings to help grow the campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-occupy_homes_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-occupy_homes-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="15_Now">15 Now</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist_Alternative_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 15 Now">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the election victory of Kshama Sawant and inspired by Proposition 1 in Sea-Tac, Socialist Alternative launched the 15 Now campaign. According campaign, their mission is to "empower working people and activate them into fighting movement" to win a $15 an hour minimum wage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Led by Socialist Alternative, 15 Now in Seattle built a local campaign based on neighborhood 'action groups' and won the endorsement of several major unions like SEIU, ATU, AFSCME, IBEW as well as community groups and national and local left-wing activists including Noam Chomsky, Tom Morello, and Glen Ford (Black Agenda Report).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> On April 26, 15 Now held an open conference for all of those participating in the movement from around the country to democratically decide the direction and tactics of 15 Now in Seattle and nationally, with over 500 people attending. The conference decided to launch a signature drive to push an amendment to the Seattle City Charter for a $15 an hour minimum wage because a winnable ballot initiative was considered the best tool in order to get the wage instituted. The decision to pursue a charter amendment saw the loss of support of many of 15 Now's labor allies due to what the party alleged was "pressure from the Democratic Party and fear of an open fight with big business."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> On May 1, 2014, Seattle Mayor Ed Murray announced his proposal for a $15 an hour minimum wage to be considered for adoption by the Seattle City Council. The Mayor's proposal was not the so-called "no exceptions" one that 15 Now had wanted and included what the party called many "corporate loopholes" including phase-ins for certain businesses and exceptions for tipped workers. Despite a fierce campaign to close the so-called "corporate loopholes", 15 Now eventually supported Kshama Sawant voting in favor of the Mayor's proposal, as they had lost the resources necessary to support a Charter Amendment campaign after many of the labor unions stopped supporting the movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> On June 2, the $15 an hour minimum wage was voted into law in the city, making Seattle the city with the highest minimum wage in the country at the time. Since 15 Now's work in Seattle, they initiated several different campaigns across the country, notably in Portland, Oregon, Minneapolis and Boston.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".23Movement4Bernie">#Movement4Bernie</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist_Alternative_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: #Movement4Bernie">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In January 2016 Socialist Alternative launched an initiative in support of Vermont Senator Bernie Sanders's campaign for the Democratic Party presidential nomination. This campaign was called #Movement4Bernie. According to the organization's website, "to achieve Sanders’ demands for $15 an hour, single payer healthcare, tuition-free college and the end of mass incarceration it will take an organized mass movement. To this end Socialist Alternative has launched #Movement4Bernie."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electoral_history">Electoral history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist_Alternative_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Electoral history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seattle_City_Council">Seattle City Council</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist_Alternative_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Seattle City Council">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2013, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seattle_Central_Community_College" title="Seattle Central Community College">Seattle Central Community College</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seattle_University" title="Seattle University">Seattle University</a> part-time economics professor <a href="/wiki/Kshama_Sawant" title="Kshama Sawant">Kshama Sawant</a> was elected to the Seattle City Council from Position 2 as a candidate for Socialist Alternative. She had previously won 35% of the vote in the August primary election and advanced into the general election against incumbent <a href="/wiki/Richard_Conlin" title="Richard Conlin">Richard Conlin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-st2013primary_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-st2013primary-35">[35]</a></sup> On November 15, 2013, Conlin conceded to Sawant after late returns showed him down by 1,640 votes or approximately 1% of the vote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-connellywin_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-connellywin-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> This made Sawant the first socialist to win a city-wide election in Seattle since the communist supporter <a href="/wiki/Anna_Louise_Strong" title="Anna Louise Strong">Anna Louise Strong</a> was elected to the School Board in 1916.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sawant had previously run for election as the Socialist Alternative candidate in the 43rd district of the <a href="/wiki/Washington_House_of_Representatives" title="Washington House of Representatives">Washington House of Representatives</a> against incumbent <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democrat</a> <a href="/wiki/Frank_Chopp" title="Frank Chopp">Frank Chopp</a>, in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Capitol_Hill_Online_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Capitol_Hill_Online-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ballot_Access_News_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ballot_Access_News-40">[40]</a></sup> Sawant advanced past the primaries for Position 2 while also advancing in Position 1 where she was on the ballot challenging Jamie Pedersen. The Sawant campaign won a subsequent court battle against the Secretary of State for the right to list her party preference on the ballot in the elections. Sawant was endorsed by the Local 587 of the <a href="/wiki/Amalgamated_Transit_Union" title="Amalgamated Transit Union">Amalgamated Transit Union</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Alternative_newspaper" title="Alternative newspaper">alternative</a> newspaper, <a href="/wiki/The_Stranger_(newspaper)" title="The Stranger (newspaper)">The Stranger</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> She received over 20,000 votes, or 28.62%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sawant's platform included a <a href="/wiki/Minimum_wage" title="Minimum wage">minimum wage</a> increase to <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>15/hour, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rent_control" title="Rent control">rent control</a>, and taxes on higher-income individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-st2013primary_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-st2013primary-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Washington_State_House">Washington State House</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist_Alternative_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Washington State House">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2014, the Socialist Alternative party chose Jess Spear, an Organizing Director for one of their campaigns, to run for Washington State Representative against Speaker of the House <a href="/wiki/Frank_Chopp" title="Frank Chopp">Frank Chopp</a>. Spear's platform included rent control, increasing education funding through increasing taxes on the wealthy, and stopping the use of all fossil fuels in Washington. During her campaign, Spear led several protests against oil and coal trains moving through Seattle and was arrested after trespassing at one of the protests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Spear garnered 17.7% of the vote or roughly 8,600 votes in the 2014 general election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boston_City_Council">Boston City Council</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist_Alternative_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Boston City Council">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2007, Matt Geary ran for City Council in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston, Massachusetts</a> as the Socialist Alternative candidate. He received 3,025 votes (2.41%) in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plurality-at-large" title="Plurality-at-large">plurality-at-large</a> election in which each voter could vote for up to four candidates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> In 2013, Socialist Alternative ran Registered Nurse and union activist Seamus Whelan for Boston City Council. In an unusually crowded municipal election including 19 candidates for City Councilor and 10 for Mayor, Whelan was eliminated in the preliminary election with over 3,000 votes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Whelan's main support was from working class areas in West Roxbury and Dorchester.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minneapolis_City_Council">Minneapolis City Council</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist_Alternative_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Minneapolis City Council">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2013, Ty Moore ran for City Council in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minneapolis,_Minnesota" title="Minneapolis, Minnesota">Minneapolis, Minnesota</a> as the Socialist Alternative Candidate. He received support from SEIU MN State Council, Occupy Homes, the Green Party of Minneapolis, some immigrant rights organizers, and some neighborhood leaders. Moore received 42% of the final vote and lost by a margin of 229 votes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
