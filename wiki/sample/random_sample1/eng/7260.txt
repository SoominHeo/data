<p>The <b>Socialist Revolutionary Party</b>, or <b>Party of Socialists Revolutionaries</b> (the <b>SRs</b>; <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Партия социалистов-революционеров (ПСР), эсеры</span>) was a major political party in early 20th century Russia and a key player in the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution" title="Russian Revolution">Russian Revolution</a>. Its general ideology was <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary_socialism" title="Revolutionary socialism">revolutionary socialism</a> of <a href="/wiki/Democratic_socialism" title="Democratic socialism">democratic socialist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agrarian_socialism" title="Agrarian socialism">agrarian socialist</a> forms. After the <a href="/wiki/February_Revolution" title="February Revolution">February Revolution</a> of 1917, it shared power with other liberal and democratic socialist forces within the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Provisional_Government" title="Russian Provisional Government">Russian Provisional Government</a>. In November 1917, it won a plurality of the national vote in <a href="/wiki/Russian_Constituent_Assembly_election,_1917" title="Russian Constituent Assembly election, 1917">Russia's first-ever democratic elections</a> (to the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Constituent_Assembly" title="Russian Constituent Assembly">Russian Constituent Assembly</a>), but the <a href="/wiki/October_Revolution" title="October Revolution">October Revolution</a> had changed the political landscape and the Bolsheviks disbanded the Constituent Assembly in January 1918.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The SRs soon split into pro-<a href="/wiki/Bolsheviks" title="Bolsheviks">Bolshevik</a> and anti-Bolshevik factions. The anti-Bolshevik faction of this party, known as the Right SRs, which remained loyal to the Provisional Government leader <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Kerensky" title="Alexander Kerensky">Alexander Kerensky</a> was defeated and destroyed by the <a href="/wiki/Bolsheviks" title="Bolsheviks">Bolsheviks</a> in the course of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Civil_War" title="Russian Civil War">Russian Civil War</a> and subsequent persecution.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist_Revolutionary_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prior_to_the_Russian_Revolution">Prior to the Russian Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist_Revolutionary_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prior to the Russian Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The party's ideology was built upon the philosophical foundation of Russia's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narodnik" title="Narodnik">narodnik</a>–<a href="/wiki/Populism" title="Populism">Populist</a> movement of the 1860s-70s and its worldview developed primarily by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Herzen" title="Alexander Herzen">Alexander Herzen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Lavrov" title="Pyotr Lavrov">Pyotr Lavrov</a>. After a period of decline and marginalization in the 1880s, the Populist/narodnik school of thought about social change in Russia was revived and substantially modified by a group of writers and activists known as "neonarodniki" (neo-Populists), particularly <a href="/wiki/Viktor_Chernov" title="Viktor Chernov">Viktor Chernov</a>. Their main innovation was a renewed dialogue with Marxism and integration of some of the key Marxist concepts into their thinking and practice. In this way, with the economic spurt and industrialization in Russia in the 1890s, they attempted to broaden their appeal in order to attract the rapidly growing urban workforce to their traditionally peasant-oriented programme. The intention was to widen the concept of the 'people' so that it encompassed all elements in society that opposed the <a href="/wiki/Tsar" title="Tsar">Tsarist</a> regime.</p>
<p>The Socialist Revolutionary Party was established in 1902 out of the Northern Union of Socialist Revolutionaries (founded in 1896), bringing together many local socialist-revolutionary groups established in the 1890s, notably <i>Workers' Party of Political Liberation of Russia</i> created by <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Breshkovsky" title="Catherine Breshkovsky">Catherine Breshkovsky</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grigory_Gershuni" title="Grigory Gershuni">Grigory Gershuni</a> in 1899. As primary party theorist emerged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victor_Chernov" title="Victor Chernov">Victor Chernov</a>, the editor of the first party organ, <i>Revolutsionnaya Rossiya</i> (<i>Revolutionary Russia</i>). Later party periodicals included <i>Znamia Truda</i> (<i>Labor's Banner</i>), <i>Delo Naroda</i> (<i>People's Cause</i>), and <i>Volia Naroda</i> (<i>People's Will</i>). Party leaders included Gershuni, Breshkovsky, <a href="/wiki/Andrei_Argunov" title="Andrei Argunov">AA Argunov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Avksentiev" title="Nikolai Avksentiev">ND Avksentiev</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gots" title="Mikhail Gots">MR Gots</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Natanson" title="Mark Natanson">Mark Natanson</a>, NI Rakitnikov (Maksimov), <a href="/wiki/Vadim_Rudnev" title="Vadim Rudnev">Vadim Rudnev</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nikolai_Rusanov" title="Nikolai Rusanov">NS Rusanov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ilya_Rubanovich" title="Ilya Rubanovich">IA Rubanovich</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Boris_Savinkov" title="Boris Savinkov">Boris Savinkov</a>.</p>
<p>The party's program was <a href="/wiki/Democratic_socialism" title="Democratic socialism">democratic socialist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agrarian_socialism" title="Agrarian socialism">agrarian socialist</a>; it garnered much support among Russia's rural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peasantry" title="Peasantry">peasantry</a>, who in particular supported their program of <i>land-socialization</i> as opposed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolshevik" title="Bolshevik">Bolshevik</a> programme of <i>land-nationalisation</i>—division of land to peasant tenants rather than collectivization in state management. The party's policy platform differed from that of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Social_Democratic_Labour_Party" title="Russian Social Democratic Labour Party">Russian Social Democratic Labour Parties</a> — both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolshevik" title="Bolshevik">Bolshevik</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Menshevik" title="Menshevik">Menshevik</a> — in that it was not officially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marxist" title="Marxist">Marxist</a> (though some of its ideologues considered themselves such); the SRs believed that the 'labouring peasantry', as well as the industrial proletariat, would be the revolutionary class in Russia. Whereas Russian SDs defined class membership in terms of ownership of the means of production, Chernov and other SR theorists defined class membership in terms of extraction of surplus value from labour. On the first definition, small-holding subsistence farmers who do not employ wage labour are, as owners of their land, members of the petty bourgeoisie; on the second definition, they can be grouped with all who provide, rather than purchase, labour-power, and hence with the proletariat as part of the 'labouring class'. Nevertheless, Chernov considered the proletariat as 'vanguard', and the peasantry as the 'main body' of the revolutionary army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

</div>
<p>The party played an active role in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Revolution_of_1905" title="Revolution of 1905">Revolution of 1905</a>, and in the <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Petersburg_Soviet" title="St. Petersburg Soviet">St. Petersburg Soviets</a>. Although the party officially boycotted the first <a href="/wiki/State_Duma" title="State Duma">State Duma</a> in 1906, 34 SRs were elected, while 37 were elected to the second Duma in 1907; the party boycotted both the third and fourth Dumas in 1907–1917. In this period, party membership drastically declined, and most of its leaders emigrated from Russia.</p>
<p>A distinctive feature of party tactics until about 1909 was its heavy reliance on assassinations of individual government officials. These tactics were inherited from SRs' predecessor in the Populist movement, People's Will, a conspiratorial organization of the 1880s. They were intended to embolden the "masses" and intimidate ("terrorize") the Tsarist government into <a href="/wiki/Concession_(politics)" title="Concession (politics)">political concessions</a>. The <a href="/wiki/SR_Combat_Organization" title="SR Combat Organization">SR Combat Organization</a>, responsible for assassinating government officials, was initially led by Gershuni and operated separately from the party so as not to jeopardize its political actions. SRCO agents assassinated two Ministers of the Interior, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dmitry_Sergeyevich_Sipyagin" title="Dmitry Sergeyevich Sipyagin">Dmitry Sipyagin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vyacheslav_von_Plehve" title="Vyacheslav von Plehve">V. K. von Plehve</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duke_Sergei_Alexandrovich_of_Russia" title="Grand Duke Sergei Alexandrovich of Russia">Grand Duke Sergei Aleksandrovich</a>, the Governor of Ufa N. M. Bogdanovich, and many other high-ranking officials.</p>
<p>In 1903, Gershuni was betrayed by his deputy, <a href="/wiki/Yevno_Azef" title="Yevno Azef">Yevno Azef</a>, an agent of the <a href="/wiki/Okhrana" title="Okhrana">Okhrana</a> secret police, arrested, convicted of terrorism and sentenced to life at hard labor, managing to escape, flee overseas and go into exile. Azef became the new leader of the SRCO, and continued working for both the SRCO and the Okhrana, simultaneously orchestrating terrorist acts and betraying his comrades. Boris Savinkov ran many of the actual operations, notably the assassination attempt on Admiral <a href="/wiki/Fyodor_Dubasov" title="Fyodor Dubasov">Fyodor Dubasov</a>.</p>
<p>Terrorism was controversial for the party from the beginning, however. At its Second Congress in <a href="/wiki/Imatra" title="Imatra">Imatra</a> in 1906, the controversy over terrorism was one of the main reasons for the defection of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist-Revolutionary_Maximalists_(Russia)" title="Socialist-Revolutionary Maximalists (Russia)">SR Maximalists</a> on the left and the <a href="/wiki/Popular_Socialists_(Russia)" title="Popular Socialists (Russia)">Popular Socialists</a> on the right. The Maximalists endorsed not only attacks on political and government targets but also 'economic terror' (i.e., attacks on landowners, factory owners etc.); the Popular Socialists rejected all terrorism. Other issues also divided the defectors from the PSR: Maximalists disagreed with the SRs' strategy of a 'two-stage' revolution as advocated by Chernov, the first stage being 'popular-democratic' and the second 'labour-socialist'. To Maximalists, this seemed like the Social-Democrats' distinction between 'bourgeois-democratic' and 'proletarian-socialist' stages of revolution. Maximalism stood for immediate socialist revolution. Meanwhile, the Popular Socialists disagreed with the party's proposal to 'socialise' the land (i.e., turn it over to collective peasant ownership) and instead wanted to 'nationalise' it (i.e., turn it over to the state; they also wanted landowners to be compensated, while the PSR rejected indemnities).</p>
<p>In late 1908, a Russian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narodnik" title="Narodnik">narodnik</a> and amateur spy hunter <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Burtsev" title="Vladimir Burtsev">Vladimir Burtsev</a> suggested that Azef might be a police spy. The party's Central Committee was outraged and set up a tribunal to try Burtsev for slander. At the trial, Azef was confronted with evidence and was caught lying, he fled and left the party in disarray. The party's Central Committee, most of whose members had close ties to Azef, felt obliged to resign. Many regional organizations, already weakened by the revolution's defeat in 1907, collapsed or became inactive. Savinkov's attempt to rebuild the SRCO failed and it was suspended in 1911. Ironically, Gershuni had defended Azef from exile in Zurich until his death there.</p>
<p>The Azef scandal contributed to a profound revision of SR tactics that was already underway. As a result, it renounced assassinations ("<a href="/wiki/Individual_terror" title="Individual terror">individual terror</a>") as a means of political protest.</p>
<p>With the start of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the party was divided on the issue of Russia's participation in the war. Most SR activists and leaders, particularly those remaining in Russia, chose to support the Tsarist government mobilization against Germany. Together with the like-minded members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Menshevik" title="Menshevik">Menshevik</a> party, they became known as <i>oborontsy</i> ("defensists"). Many younger defensists living in exile joined the French army as Russia's closest ally in the war. A smaller group, the internationalists, which included Chernov, favored the pursuit of peace through cooperation with socialist parties in both military blocs. This led them to participate in the <a href="/wiki/Zimmerwald_Conference" title="Zimmerwald Conference">Zimmerwald</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kienthal" title="Kienthal">Kienthal</a> conferences with Bolshevik emigres led by Lenin. This fact was later used against Chernov and his followers by their right-wing opponents as alleged evidence of their lack of patriotism and Bolshevik sympathies.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russian_Revolutions">Russian Revolutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist_Revolutionary_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Russian Revolutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution" title="Russian Revolution">Russian Revolution</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/February_Revolution" title="February Revolution">February Revolution</a> allowed the SRs to return to an active political role. Party leaders, including Chernov, returned to Russia. They played a major role in the formation and leadership of the Soviets, albeit in most cases playing second fiddle to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Menshevik" title="Menshevik">Mensheviks</a>. One member, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Kerensky" title="Alexander Kerensky">Alexander Kerensky</a>, joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Provisional_Government,_1917" title="Russian Provisional Government, 1917">Provisional Government</a> in March 1917 as Minister of Justice, eventually becoming the head of a coalition socialist-liberal government in July 1917, although his connection with the party was tenuous. (He had served in the <a href="/wiki/Duma" title="Duma">Duma</a> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trudovik" title="Trudovik">Trudoviks</a>, breakaway SRs that defied the party's refusal to participate in the Duma.)</p>
<p>After the fall of the first coalition in April–May 1917 and the reshuffling of the Provisional Government, the party played a larger role. Its key government official at the time was Chernov who joined the government as Minister of Agriculture. He also tried to play a larger role, particularly in foreign affairs, but soon found himself marginalized and his proposals of far-reaching agrarian reform blocked by more conservative members of the government. After the failed Bolshevik uprising of July 1917, Chernov found himself on the defensive as allegedly soft on the Bolsheviks and was excluded from the revamped coalition in August 1917. The party was now represented in the government by Nikolai Avksentyev, a right-wing defensist, as Minister of the Interior.</p>
<p>This weakening of the party's position intensified the growing divide within it between supporters of the coalition with the Mensheviks and those inclined toward more resolute, unilateral action. In August 1917, <a href="/wiki/Maria_Spiridonova" title="Maria Spiridonova">Maria Spiridonova</a>, leader of the Left SRs, advocated scuttling the coalition and forming an SR-only government, but was not supported by Chernov and his followers. This spurred the formation of the left-wing faction and its growing support for cooperation with the Bolsheviks. The Left SRs believed that Russia should withdraw immediately from <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, and they were frustrated that the Provisional Government wanted to postpone addressing the land question until after the convocation of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Constituent_Assembly" title="Russian Constituent Assembly">Russian Constituent Assembly</a> instead of immediately confiscating the land from the landowners and redistributing it to the peasants.</p>
<p>Left SRs and Bolsheviks referred to the mainstream SR party as the "<b>Right SR</b> party" whereas mainstream SRs referred to the party as just "SR" and reserved the term "Right SR" for the rightwing faction of the party led by <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Breshkovsky" title="Catherine Breshkovsky">Breshkovsky</a> and Avksentev.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The primary issues motivating the split were the war and the redistribution of land.</p>
<p>At the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_Congress_of_Soviets" title="Second Congress of Soviets">Second Congress of Soviets</a> on October 25, 1917, when the Bolsheviks proclaimed the deposition of the Provisional government, the split within the SR party became final. The Left SR stayed at the Congress and were elected to the permanent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VTsIK" title="VTsIK">VTsIK</a> executive (while initially refusing to join the Bolshevik government) while the mainstream SR and their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Menshevik" title="Menshevik">Menshevik</a> allies walked out of the Congress. In late November, the Left SR joined the Bolshevik government, obtaining three ministries.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_October_Revolution">After the October Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist_Revolutionary_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: After the October Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Constituent_Assembly_election,_1917" title="Russian Constituent Assembly election, 1917">election to the Russian Constituent Assembly</a> held two weeks after the Bolsheviks took power, the party still proved to be by far the most popular party across the country, gaining 40% of the popular vote as opposed to the Bolsheviks' 25%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> However, in January 1918 the Bolsheviks disbanded the Assembly and after that the SR became of less political significance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left_Socialist-Revolutionary_Party" title="Left Socialist-Revolutionary Party">Left SRs</a> became the coalition partner of the Bolsheviks in the Soviet government, although they resigned their positions after the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Brest-Litovsk" title="Treaty of Brest-Litovsk">Treaty of Brest-Litovsk</a> (the peace treaty with the Central Powers that ended Russia's participation in World War I). A few Left SRs like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yakov_Grigorevich_Blumkin" title="Yakov Grigorevich Blumkin">Yakov Grigorevich Blumkin</a> joined the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Communist Party</a>.</p>
<p>Dissatisfied with the Treaty of Brest-Litovsk, two <a href="/wiki/Cheka" title="Cheka">Chekists</a> who were left SRs assassinated the German ambassador to Russia, Count <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Mirbach" title="Wilhelm Mirbach">Wilhelm Mirbach</a> early in the afternoon on July 6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Felshtinsky_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Felshtinsky-6">[6]</a></sup> Following the assassination, on July 6-7, 1918, the left SRs attempted a "<a href="/wiki/Left_SR_uprising" title="Left SR uprising">Third Russian Revolution</a>" against the Bolsheviks, which failed, leading to the arrest, imprisonment, exile, and execution of party leaders and members. In response, some SRs turned again to violence. A former SR, <a href="/wiki/Fanny_Kaplan" title="Fanny Kaplan">Fanny Kaplan</a>, tried to assassinate Lenin on August 30, 1918. Many SRs fought for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_Army" title="White Army">Whites</a> or <a href="/wiki/Green_armies" title="Green armies">Greens</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Civil_War" title="Russian Civil War">Russian Civil War</a> alongside some Mensheviks and other banned moderate socialist elements. The <a href="/wiki/Tambov_Rebellion" title="Tambov Rebellion">Tambov Rebellion</a> against the Bolsheviks was led by an SR, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aleksandr_Antonov_(politician)" title="Aleksandr Antonov (politician)">Aleksandr Antonov</a>. However, after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aleksandr_Kolchak" title="Aleksandr Kolchak">Admiral Kolchak</a> was installed as "Supreme Leader," of the White Movement in November 1918, he expelled all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marxist" title="Marxist">Marxists</a> from the ranks. As a result, many SRs placed their organization behind White lines at the service of the Red Guards and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CHEKA" title="CHEKA">CHEKA</a>. Later, many Left SRs became Communists.</p>
<p>Following Lenin's instructions, a <a href="/wiki/1922_Moscow_Trial_of_Socialist_Revolutionaries" title="1922 Moscow Trial of Socialist Revolutionaries">trial of SRs was held in Moscow in 1922</a>, which led to protests by, among others, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugene_Debs" title="Eugene Debs">Eugene Debs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Kautsky" title="Karl Kautsky">Karl Kautsky</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>. Most of the defendants were found guilty, but did not plead guilty, unlike the defendants in the later show trials in the Soviet Union in the late 1920s and the 1930s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_exile">In exile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist_Revolutionary_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In exile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The party continued its activities in exile. A Foreign Delegation of the Central Committee was established, based in <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>. The party was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_and_Socialist_International" title="Labour and Socialist International">Labour and Socialist International</a> between 1923 and 1940.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
