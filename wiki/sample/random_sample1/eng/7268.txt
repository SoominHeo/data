<p><b>San Crisogono</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Churches_in_Rome" title="Churches in Rome">church in</a> <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> (rione <a href="/wiki/Trastevere" title="Trastevere">Trastevere</a>) dedicated to the martyr <a href="/wiki/Saint_Chrysogonus" title="Saint Chrysogonus">Saint Chrysogonus</a>. It was one of the <a href="/wiki/Titular_church" title="Titular church">tituli</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parish churches</a> of Rome, and was probably built in the 4th century under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pope_Silvester_I" title="Pope Silvester I">Pope Silvester I</a> (314–335), rebuilt in the 12th century by <a href="/wiki/John_of_Crema" title="John of Crema">John of Crema</a>, and again by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Soria" title="Giovanni Battista Soria">Giovanni Battista Soria</a>, funded by <a href="/wiki/Scipione_Borghese" title="Scipione Borghese">Scipione Borghese</a>, in the early 17th century.</p>
<p>The area beneath the sacristy was investigated by Fr. L. Manfredini and Fr. C. Piccolini in 1907. They found remains of the first church (see below). After they had made this discovery, the area was excavated and studied.</p>
<p>The church is served by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trinitarians" title="Trinitarians">Trinitarians</a>. Among the previous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_Priest" title="Cardinal Priest">Cardinal Priests</a> was Vincenzo Gioacchino Raffaele Luigi Pecci (1853–1878), elected <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_XIII" title="Pope Leo XIII">Pope Leo XIII</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_and_architecture">Art and architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Crisogono,_Rome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Art and architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:San_Crisogono_(Rome)_-_Interior_Diliff_edit.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5234" data-file-width="5282" height="297" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/San_Crisogono_%28Rome%29_-_Interior_Diliff_edit.jpg/300px-San_Crisogono_%28Rome%29_-_Interior_Diliff_edit.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/San_Crisogono_%28Rome%29_-_Interior_Diliff_edit.jpg/450px-San_Crisogono_%28Rome%29_-_Interior_Diliff_edit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/San_Crisogono_%28Rome%29_-_Interior_Diliff_edit.jpg/600px-San_Crisogono_%28Rome%29_-_Interior_Diliff_edit.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:San_Crisogono_(Rome)_-_Interior_Diliff_edit.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Interior</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bell_tower" title="Bell tower">bell tower</a> dates from the 12th century rebuilding. The interior of the church was rebuilt in the 1620s on the site of a 12th-century church. The 22 granite columns in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nave_(architecture)" title="Nave (architecture)">nave</a> are <a href="/wiki/Spolia" title="Spolia">reused antique columns</a>. The floor is <a href="/wiki/Cosmatesque" title="Cosmatesque">cosmatesque</a> <a href="/wiki/Mosaic" title="Mosaic">mosaic</a>, but most of it is hidden by the pews. The <a href="/wiki/Crypt" title="Crypt">confessio</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Sanctuary" title="Sanctuary">sanctuary</a> area is from the 8th century. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_altar" title="High altar">high altar</a> is from 1127, with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baldachino" title="Baldachino">baldachino</a> from (1627 or 1641) by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G.B_Soria" title="G.B Soria">G.B Soria</a>.</p>
<p>The painting in the middle of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baroque_style" title="Baroque style">Baroque</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coffered_ceiling" title="Coffered ceiling">coffered ceiling</a> is by <a href="/wiki/Guercino" title="Guercino">Guercino</a>, and depicts the <i>Glory of Saint Chrysogonus</i>. It is likely a copy of the original, which is thought to have been taken to London, but it is possible the one here is the original and the one in London is the copy.</p>
<p>On the left side of the nave is the shrine of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blessed_Anna_Maria_Taigi" title="Blessed Anna Maria Taigi">Blessed Anna Maria Taigi</a>, buried here in the habit of a <a href="/wiki/Third_order" title="Third order">tertiary</a> of the Trinitarians. Blessed Anna Maria Taigi (1769-1837) was a Christian mystic beatified in in 1920. Above the altar is a painting by Aronne Del Vecchio of the Trinitarian Saints in Glory. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Visitors can view some of her other belongings in the adjacent <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastery</a>, where they are <a href="/wiki/Veneration" title="Veneration">venerated</a> as <a href="/wiki/Relic" title="Relic">relics</a>.</p>
<p>The monument at the left of the entrance, dedicated to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_(Catholicism)" title="Cardinal (Catholicism)">Cardinal</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Giovanno_Jacopo_Millo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Giovanno Jacopo Millo (page does not exist)">Giovanno Jacopo Millo</a> was completed by <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Marchionni" title="Carlo Marchionni">Carlo Marchionni</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Bracci" title="Pietro Bracci">Pietro Bracci</a>. Along the right side of the nave are the remains of <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">frescoes</a>, including a <i>Santa Francesca Romana</i> and a <i>Crucifixion</i>, attributed to <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Guidotti" title="Paolo Guidotti">Paolo Guidotti</a> and transferred from the Church of Saints Barbara and Catherine. The nave also displays a painting of <i>Three Archangels</i> by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_da_San_Giovanni" title="Giovanni da San Giovanni">Giovanni da San Giovanni</a> and a <i>Trinity and Angels</i> by <a href="/wiki/Giacinto_Gimignani" title="Giacinto Gimignani">Giacinto Gimignani</a>, while the altar has a <i>Guardian Angel</i> by <a href="/wiki/Ludovico_Gimignani" title="Ludovico Gimignani">Ludovico Gimignani</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presbytery_(architecture)" title="Presbytery (architecture)">presbytery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ciborium_(architecture)" title="Ciborium (architecture)">ciborium</a> (or baldachin), created by Soria, are surrounded by four <a href="/wiki/Alabaster" title="Alabaster">alabaster</a> columns. The <a href="/wiki/Apse" title="Apse">apse</a> has frescoes of the <i>Life of Saint Crisogono</i> (16th century) above a <i>Madonna &amp; Child with Saints Crisogono &amp; James</i> by the 12th century school of <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Cavallini" title="Pietro Cavallini">Pietro Cavallini</a>. The presbytery <a href="/wiki/Vault_(architecture)" title="Vault (architecture)">vault</a> is frescoed with a Virgin by <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Cesari" title="Giuseppe Cesari">Giuseppe Cesari</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The inscriptions found in San Crisogono, a valuable source illustrating the history of the church, have been collected and published by Vincenzo Forcella.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Excavations">Excavations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Crisogono,_Rome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Excavations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Remains from the first church, possible from the reign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_I" title="Constantine I">Constantine I</a>, and earlier Roman houses can be seen in the lower parts, reached by a staircase in the sacristy. The ruins are confusing, but you can easily find the apse of the old church and you can see the remains of the martyr's shrine in middle of the apse wall. The church had an uncommon form; rather than the normal basilical plan with a central nave and two aisles on the sides, it has a single nave.</p>
<p>On either side of the apse are rooms known as <i>pastophoria</i>, service rooms of a type uncommon in the West but normal in Eastern churches. The one on the right-hand side is thought to have been used as a diaconium, with functions resembling those of the <a href="/wiki/Sacristy" title="Sacristy">sacristy</a> in later churches. The other would then probably have been a protesis, where holy relics were kept.</p>
<p>A number of basins were found here during the excavations, including one cut into the south wall. As the plan is so atypical of early Roman churches, some believe that the structure originally had a different function, and the presence of the basins could mean that it was a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fullonica" title="Fullonica">fullonica</a></i>, a laundry and dye-house. The area was a commercial district at the time, so this is quite likely. Others think that the basin in the south wall was made for <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptism</a> by immersion. As there were other basins too, it seems more likely that it was originally intended for a different use, but it may very well have been used as a baptismal font after the building had been consecrated as a church.</p>
<p>The paintings are from the 8th to the 11th century, and include <i><a href="/wiki/Pope_Sylvester_I" title="Pope Sylvester I">Pope Sylvester</a> Capturing the Dragon</i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Pantaleon" title="St Pantaleon">St Pantaleon</a> Healing the Blind Man</i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Benedict" title="St Benedict">St Benedict</a> Healing the Leper</i> and <i>The Rescue of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Placid" title="St Placid">St Placid</a></i>.</p>
<p>Several <a href="/wiki/Sarcophagus" title="Sarcophagus">sarcophagi</a> have been preserved here, some beautifully decorated.</p>
<p>Below the first church are remains of late Republican houses.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Liturgy">Liturgy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Crisogono,_Rome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Liturgy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The shrine was for many centuries the <a href="/wiki/National_churches_in_Rome" title="National churches in Rome">national church</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_people" title="Sardinian people">Sardinians</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corsican_people" title="Corsican people">Corsicans</a> resident in Rome. Starting with the 16th century, the Corsicans immigrated in the city settled in the <a href="/wiki/Tiber_Island" title="Tiber Island">Tiber Island</a> and in that part of Trastevere liying between the Port of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ripa_Grande" title="Ripa Grande">Ripa Grande</a> and the church. In the interior are buried several commanders of the <i><a href="/wiki/Corsican_Guard" title="Corsican Guard">Guardia corsa</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a> analogue to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swiss_guard" title="Swiss guard">Swiss guard</a>, which was active in Rome between the 15th and the 17th century.</p>
<p>The feast day of St Chrysogonus, 24 November, is also the dedication day of the church. Pilgrims and other faithful who attend Mass on this day receive a plenary indulgence.</p>
