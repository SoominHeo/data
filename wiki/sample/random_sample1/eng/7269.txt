<p>The <b>Papal Basilica of St. Paul outside the Walls</b> (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <span lang="it"><i>Basilica Papale di San Paolo fuori le Mura</i></span>), commonly known as <b>St. Paul's outside the Walls</b>, is one of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>'s four <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient</a>, <a href="/wiki/Major_basilica#Papal_Basilicas" title="Major basilica">Papal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Major_basilica" title="Major basilica">major basilicas</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[Notes 1]</a></sup> along with the Basilicas of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basilica_di_San_Giovanni_in_Laterano" title="Basilica di San Giovanni in Laterano">St. John in the Lateran</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St. Peter's Basilica">St. Peter's</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Basilica_di_Santa_Maria_Maggiore" title="Basilica di Santa Maria Maggiore">St. Mary Major</a>.</p>
<p>The Basilica is within Italian territory and not the territory of the <a href="/wiki/Vatican_City" title="Vatican City">Vatican City State</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[1]</a></sup> However, the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> fully owns the Basilica, and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> is legally obligated to recognize its full ownership thereof<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup> and to concede to it "the immunity granted by International Law to the headquarters of the diplomatic agents of foreign States".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/James_Michael_Harvey" title="James Michael Harvey">James Michael Harvey</a> was named Archpriest of the Basilica in 2012.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Basilica_of_Saint_Paul_Outside_the_Walls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Basilica was founded by the Roman <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_I_(emperor)" title="Constantine I (emperor)">Emperor Constantine I</a> over the burial place of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_of_Tarsus" title="Paul of Tarsus">St. Paul</a>, where it was said that, after the Apostle's execution, his followers erected a memorial, called a <i>cella memoriae</i>.</p>
<p>In 386, Emperor <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">Theodosius I</a> began erecting a much larger and more beautiful basilica with a nave and four aisles with a transept; the work including the mosaics was not completed until <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_I" title="Pope Leo I">Leo I</a>'s pontificate (440–461). In the 5th century it was larger than the <a href="/wiki/Old_St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="Old St. Peter's Basilica">Old St. Peter's Basilica</a>. The Christian poet <a href="/wiki/Prudentius" title="Prudentius">Prudentius</a>, who saw it at the time of emperor <a href="/wiki/Honorius_(emperor)" title="Honorius (emperor)">Honorius</a> (395–423), describes the splendours of the monument in a few expressive lines.</p>
<p>Under <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_I" title="Pope Gregory I">Pope St. Gregory the Great</a> (590–604) the Basilica was extensively modified. The pavement was raised to place the altar directly over St. Paul's tomb. A confession permitted access to the Apostle's sepulcher.</p>
<p>In that period there were two monasteries near the Basilica: St. Aristus's for men and St. Stefano's for women. Masses were celebrated by a special body of clerics instituted by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Simplicius" title="Pope Simplicius">Pope Simplicius</a>. Over time the monasteries and the Basilica's clergy declined; <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_II" title="Pope Gregory II">Pope St. Gregory II</a> restored the former and entrusted the monks with the Basilica's care.</p>
<p>As it lay outside the <a href="/wiki/Aurelian_Walls" title="Aurelian Walls">Aurelian Walls</a>, the Basilica was damaged in the 9th century during a <a href="/wiki/Arab_raid_against_Rome" title="Arab raid against Rome">Saracen raid</a>. Consequently, <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_VIII" title="Pope John VIII">Pope John VIII</a> (872–82) fortified the Basilica, the monastery, and the dwellings of the peasantry, forming the town of Joannispolis (Italian: <i>Giovannipoli</i>) which existed until 1348, when <a href="/wiki/1348_Friuli_earthquake" title="1348 Friuli earthquake">an earthquake</a> totally destroyed it.</p>
<p>In 937, when <a href="/wiki/Odo_of_Cluny" title="Odo of Cluny">Saint Odo of Cluny</a> came to Rome, <a href="/wiki/Alberic_II_of_Spoleto" title="Alberic II of Spoleto">Alberic II of Spoleto</a>, Patrician of Rome, entrusted the monastery and basilica to his congregation and Odo placed Balduino of <a href="/wiki/Monte_Cassino" title="Monte Cassino">Monte Cassino</a> in charge. <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_VII" title="Pope Gregory VII">Pope Gregory VII</a> was abbot of the monastery and in his time Pantaleone, a rich merchant of <a href="/wiki/Amalfi" title="Amalfi">Amalfi</a> who lived in <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, presented the bronze doors of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basilica_maior" title="Basilica maior">basilica maior</a>, which were executed by Constantinopolitan artists; the doors are inscribed with Pantaleone's prayer that the "doors of life" may be opened to him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pope_Martin_V" title="Pope Martin V">Pope Martin V</a> entrusted it to the monks of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congregation_of_Monte_Cassino" title="Congregation of Monte Cassino">Congregation of Monte Cassino</a>. It was then made an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbey_nullius" title="Abbey nullius">abbey nullius</a>. The abbot's jurisdiction extended over the districts of <a href="/wiki/Civitella_San_Paolo" title="Civitella San Paolo">Civitella San Paolo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leprignano" title="Leprignano">Leprignano</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nazzano" title="Nazzano">Nazzano</a>, all of which formed parishes. But the parish of San Paolo in Rome is under the jurisdiction of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_vicar" title="Cardinal vicar">cardinal vicar</a>.</p>

<p>From 1215 until 1964 it was the seat of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_Patriarch_of_Alexandria" title="Latin Patriarch of Alexandria">Latin Patriarch of Alexandria</a>.</p>

<p>On 15 July 1823, a negligent workman repairing the lead of the roof, started a fire that led to the near total destruction of this basilica, which, alone among all the churches of Rome, had preserved its primitive character for 1435 years.</p>
<p>Pope Leo XII issued a document Ad plurimas encouraging donations for reconstruction. It was re-opened in 1840, and reconsecrated in 1855 with the presence of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_IX" title="Pope Pius IX">Pope Pius IX</a> and fifty cardinals. The complete decoration and reconstruction, in charge of <a href="/wiki/Luigi_Poletti_(architect)" title="Luigi Poletti (architect)">Luigi Poletti</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> took longer, however, and many countries made their contributions. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viceroy_of_Egypt" title="Viceroy of Egypt">Viceroy of Egypt</a> sent pillars of <a href="/wiki/Alabaster" title="Alabaster">alabaster</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Russia" title="Emperor of Russia">Emperor of Russia</a> the precious <a href="/wiki/Malachite" title="Malachite">malachite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lapis_lazuli" title="Lapis lazuli">lapis lazuli</a> of the tabernacle. The work on the principal façade, looking toward the <a href="/wiki/Tiber" title="Tiber">Tiber</a>, was completed by the Italian Government, which declared the church a national monument. On 23 April 1891 the explosion of the gunpowder magazine at Forte Portuense destroyed the stained glass windows.</p>
<p>On 31 May 2005 <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">Pope Benedict XVI</a> ordered the Basilica to come under the control of an <a href="/wiki/Archpriest" title="Archpriest">Archpriest</a> and he named <a href="/wiki/Archbishop" title="Archbishop">Archbishop</a> <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Cordero_Lanza_di_Montezemolo" title="Andrea Cordero Lanza di Montezemolo">Andrea Cordero Lanza di Montezemolo</a> as its first archpriest.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture_and_Interior">Architecture and Interior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Basilica_of_Saint_Paul_Outside_the_Walls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Architecture and Interior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Colonnade_and_sky_of_Saint_Paul_(Rome).JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4000" data-file-width="6016" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Colonnade_and_sky_of_Saint_Paul_%28Rome%29.JPG/250px-Colonnade_and_sky_of_Saint_Paul_%28Rome%29.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Colonnade_and_sky_of_Saint_Paul_%28Rome%29.JPG/375px-Colonnade_and_sky_of_Saint_Paul_%28Rome%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Colonnade_and_sky_of_Saint_Paul_%28Rome%29.JPG/500px-Colonnade_and_sky_of_Saint_Paul_%28Rome%29.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Colonnade_and_sky_of_Saint_Paul_(Rome).JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Colonnades of and sky above Basilica of Saint Paul Outside the Walls</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The covered portico that precedes the façade is a Neo-classicist addition of the 19th-century reconstruction. The 20th-century door includes the remains of the leaves from the original portal, executed by Staurachius of Chios around 1070 in <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, with scenes from the New and Old Testament. On the right is the Holy Door, which is opened only during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jubilee_(Christian)" title="Jubilee (Christian)">Jubilees</a>.</p>
<p>The new basilica has maintained the original structure with one nave and four aisles. It is 131.66 metres (432.0 ft) long, 65 metres (213 ft)-wide, and 29.70 metres (97.4 ft)-high, the second largest in Rome.</p>
<p>The nave's 80 columns and its stucco-decorated ceiling are from the 19th century. All that remains of the ancient basilica are the interior portion of the <a href="/wiki/Apse" title="Apse">apse</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Triumphal_arch" title="Triumphal arch">triumphal arch</a>. The mosaics of the apse, work by <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Cavallini" title="Pietro Cavallini">Pietro Cavallini</a>, were mostly lost in the 1823 fire; only a few traces were incorporated in the reconstruction. The 5th-century mosaics of the triumphal arch are original: an inscription in the lower section attest they were done at the time of Leo I, paid by <a href="/wiki/Galla_Placidia" title="Galla Placidia">Galla Placidia</a>. The subject portrays the <a href="/wiki/Apocalypse" title="Apocalypse">Apocalypse</a> of John, with the bust of Christ in the middle flanked by the 24 doctors of the church, surmounted by the flying symbols of the four Evangelists. St. Peter and St. Paul are portrayed at the right and left of the arch, the latter pointing downwards (probably to his tomb).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Church_tabernacle" title="Church tabernacle">tabernacle</a> of the confession of <a href="/wiki/Arnolfo_di_Cambio" title="Arnolfo di Cambio">Arnolfo di Cambio</a> (1285) belongs to the 13th century.</p>
<p>In the old basilica each pope had his portrait in a <a href="/wiki/Frieze" title="Frieze">frieze</a> extending above the columns separating the four aisles and naves. A 19th-century version can be seen now. The nave's interior walls were also redecorated with scenes from Saint Paul's life in two mosaics.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sacristy" title="Sacristy">sacristy</a> contains a fine statue of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Boniface_IX" title="Pope Boniface IX">Pope Boniface IX</a>.</p>
<p>South of the transept is the cloister, considered "one of the most beautiful of the Middle Ages".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> Built by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vassalletto" title="Vassalletto">Vassalletto</a> in 1205-1241, it has double columns of different shapes. Some columns have inlays with golden and coloured-glass mosaics; the same decoration can be seen on the architrave and the inner frame of the cloister. Also visible are fragments from the destroyed basilica and ancient sarcophagi, one with scenes of the myth of Apollo.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Tomb_of_St._Paul">The Tomb of St. Paul</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Basilica_of_Saint_Paul_Outside_the_Walls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Tomb of St. Paul">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to tradition, St. Paul's body was buried two miles away from the place of his martyrdom, in the sepulchral area along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ostiense_Way" title="Ostiense Way">Ostiense Way</a>, which was owned by a Christian woman named Lucina. A <a href="/wiki/Tropaion" title="Tropaion">tropaeum</a> was erected on it and quickly became a place of veneration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[Notes 2]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_I" title="Constantine I">Constantine I</a> erected a basilica on the tropaeum's site, and the basilica was significantly extended by <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">Theodosius I</a> from 386, into what is now known as Saint Paul Outside the Walls. During the 4th century, Paul's remains, excluding the head, were moved into a sarcophagus. (According to church tradition the head rests at the <a href="/wiki/Lateran" title="Lateran">Lateran</a>.) Paul's tomb is below a marble tombstone in the Basilica's crypt, at 1.37 metres (4.5 ft) below the altar. The tombstone bears the Latin inscription <span lang="lat">PAULO APOSTOLO MART</span> ("to Paul the apostle and martyr"). The inscribed portion of the tombstone has three holes, two square and one circular. The circular hole is connected to the tomb by a pipeline, reflecting the Roman custom of pouring perfumes inside the sarcophagus, or to the practice of providing the bones of the dead with libations. The sarcophagus below the tombstone measures 2.55 metres (8.4 ft) long, 1.25 metres (4.1 ft) wide and 0.97 metres (3.2 ft) high.</p>

<p>The discovery of the sarcophagus is mentioned in the chronicle of the Benedictine monastery attached to the Basilica, in regard to the 19th century rebuilding. Unlike other sarcophagi found at that time, this was not mentioned in the excavation papers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 6 December 2006, it was announced that Vatican archaeologists had confirmed the presence of a white marble <a href="/wiki/Sarcophagus" title="Sarcophagus">sarcophagus</a> beneath the altar, perhaps containing the remains of the Apostle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[9]</a></sup> A press conference held on 11 December 2006<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup> gave more details of the work of excavation, which lasted from 2002 to 22 September 2006, and which had been initiated after pilgrims to the basilica expressed disappointment that the Apostle's tomb could not be visited or touched during the Jubilee year of 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[11]</a></sup> The sarcophagus was not extracted from its position, so that only one of its two narrow sides is visible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>A curved line of bricks indicating the outline of the apse of the Constantinian basilica was discovered immediately to the <i>west</i> of the sarcophagus, showing that the original basilica had its entrance to the east, like Saint Peter's Basilica in the Vatican. The larger 386 basilica that replaced it had the Via Ostiense (the road to Ostia) to the east and so was extended westward, towards the river Tiber, changing the orientation diametrically.</p>
