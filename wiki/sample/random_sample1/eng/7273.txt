<p><b>Sandomierz</b> (pronounced: <span class="IPA" style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Polish" title="Help:IPA for Polish">[sanˈdɔmʲɛʂ]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata"> (<a href="/wiki/File:Pl-Sandomierz.ogg" title="File:Pl-Sandomierz.ogg"><img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a> <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bf/Pl-Sandomierz.ogg" title="Pl-Sandomierz.ogg">listen</a>)</small>) is a town in south-eastern <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> with 25,714 inhabitants (2006), situated in the <a href="/wiki/%C5%9Awi%C4%99tokrzyskie_Voivodeship" title="Świętokrzyskie Voivodeship">Świętokrzyskie Voivodeship</a> (since 1999). It is the capital of <a href="/wiki/Sandomierz_County" title="Sandomierz County">Sandomierz County</a> (since 1999). Sandomierz is known for its Old Town, which is a major tourist attraction. In the past, Sandomierz used to be one of the most important urban centers not only of <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Poland" title="Lesser Poland">Lesser Poland</a>, but also of the whole country.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sandomierz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name of the city might have originated from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Polish_language" title="Old Polish language">Old Polish</a> <i><span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl">Sędomir</span></i>, composed of <i><span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl">Sędzi-</span></i> (from the verb <i><span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl">sądzić</span></i> "to judge") and <i><span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl">mir</span></i> ("peace"), or more likely from the antiquated <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> Sędzimir, once popular in several Slavic languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Sandomierz is known in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Sandomir</i></span>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Sandomiria</i></span>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yiddish_language" title="Yiddish language">Yiddish</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="yi" xml:lang="yi">צויזמיר</span>‎; <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a>: <span lang="hu" xml:lang="hu"><i>Szandomir</i></span>; <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">Ukrainian</a>: <span lang="uk" xml:lang="uk">Сандомир</span> or <span lang="uk" xml:lang="uk">Судомир</span>; <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Сандомир</span>; and <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a>: <span lang="cs" xml:lang="cs"><i>Sandoměř</i></span>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sandomierz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sandomierz is one of the oldest and historically most significant cities in Poland. Archeological finds around the city indicate that humans have inhabited the area since <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">neolithic</a> times. The city came into existence in the early <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, taking advantage of an excellent location at the junction of <a href="/wiki/Vistula" title="Vistula">Vistula</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_River" title="San River">San</a> rivers, and on the path of important trade routes. The first known historical mention of the city comes from the early 12th-century, when the chronicler <a href="/wiki/Gallus_Anonymus" title="Gallus Anonymus">Gallus Anonymus</a> ranked it together with <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w" title="Kraków">Kraków</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wroc%C5%82aw" title="Wrocław">Wrocław</a> as one of the main cities of Poland. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Testament_of_Boles%C5%82aw_III_Krzywousty" title="Testament of Bolesław III Krzywousty">testament</a> (ca 1115-1118) of <a href="/wiki/Boles%C5%82aw_III_Wrymouth" title="Bolesław III Wrymouth">Bolesław III Wrymouth</a>, in which he divided Poland among his sons, designated Sandomierz as the capital of one of the resulting principalities, the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Sandomierz" title="Duchy of Sandomierz">Duchy of Sandomierz</a>.</p>
<p>In the course of the 13th century the city suffered grievous damage during raids by <a href="/wiki/Tatars" title="Tatars">Tatars</a> in 1241, 1259 and 1287. The old wooden buildings of the town were completely destroyed. As a result, in 1286 the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Polish_monarchs" title="List of Polish monarchs">High Duke of Poland</a> <a href="/wiki/Leszek_II_the_Black" title="Leszek II the Black">Leszek II the Black</a>, effectively refounded the city under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magdeburg_Law" title="Magdeburg Law">Magdeburg Law</a>. The city archives preserve the founding document. (An important note: in 1260, as the Tartars invaded Christian Sandomir, a community of Dominicans was praying Matins while a novice read the martyrology for the next day: "the 49 martyrs of Sandomir". When the friars realized they were being warned of their death, they spent the remainder of the night and all the next day preparing to meet the Lord. At last, after the brethren had finished praying Compline, and as they processed singing the <i>Salve Regina</i> to Mary, the Tartars broke through the church door. While the Tartars intended to bring death to these Dominicans, they actually brought them great gifts - crowns of martyrdom. Ever since, at the death of every Dominican a song to his Beloved Mother is sung to usher him into her arms - the <i>Salve Regina</i> (or <i>Hail, Holy Queen</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Era">Modern Era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sandomierz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern Era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the re-unification of the Polish lands in the 14th century, the former principality became the <a href="/wiki/Sandomierz_Voivodeship" title="Sandomierz Voivodeship">Sandomierz Voivodeship</a>, incorporating large areas of southeastern Poland. Until 1474, it was one of two voivodeships of Lesser Poland, together with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Krakow_Voivodeship" title="Krakow Voivodeship">Krakow Voivodeship</a>. In 1474, <a href="/wiki/Lublin_Voivodeship" title="Lublin Voivodeship">Lublin Voivodeship</a> was created from eastern part of Sandomierz Voivodeship. At this time Sandomierz had about 3,000 inhabitants and was one of the largest Polish cities. In the middle of the 14th century the city was burned again during a raid by the <a href="/wiki/Lithuanians" title="Lithuanians">Lithuanians</a>. It was rebuilt during the rule of king <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casimir_III_of_Poland" title="Casimir III of Poland">Casimir III of Poland</a>. The layout of the city has survived practically unchanged since that time until the present day.</p>
<p>The following three hundred years, running until the middle of the 17th century, were quite prosperous for the city.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The most important historical buildings were built during this period. This golden age came to an end in 1655 when <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> forces captured the city in the course of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Deluge_(Polish_history)" title="The Deluge (Polish history)">Deluge</a>. After briefly holding out in the city, the withdrawing Swedes blew up the castle and caused heavy damage to other buildings. In the next 100 years the economy of Poland suffered a decline, which also affected the city. In 1570 an alliance of non-Catholic Polish Churches, the Lutherans, the Reformed, and the Bohemian Brethren, drew up what is known as the <i>Consensus of Sandomir</i>, effecting a confederation of the work in order to stave off defeat at the hands of the Roman Church. A great fire in 1757 and the <a href="/wiki/First_Partition_of_Poland" title="First Partition of Poland">First Partition of Poland</a> in 1772, which placed Sandomierz in <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria</a>, further reduced its status. As a result, Sandomierz lost its role as an administrative capital.</p>
<p>In 1809, during the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>, fighting between the forces of Austria and of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Warsaw" title="Duchy of Warsaw">Duchy of Warsaw</a> caused damage to the city. After 1815 it found itself in the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a> (<a href="/wiki/Congress_Poland" title="Congress Poland">Congress Poland</a>). At this point it had just 2640 inhabitants.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sandomierz_Cathedral_and_St_Paul.27s_Church_blood_libel_myth">Sandomierz Cathedral and St Paul's Church blood libel myth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sandomierz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sandomierz Cathedral and St Paul's Church blood libel myth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This cathedral contains a series of paintings built into the church's wooden panelling depicting the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martyrologium_Romanum" title="Martyrologium Romanum">Martyrologium Romanum</a></i>. The third painting shows the scene which, it is claimed:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> "...depicts ritual murders committed in Sandomierz by Jews on Christian children. The inscription above the painting reads <i>filius apothecary ab infidelibus judaeis sandomiriensibus occisus</i> (son of an apothecary, by infidel Sandomierz Jews killed) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The St Paul's Church contains a different series of paintings including one in the chancel, depicting the torment of Jerzy Krassowski who was allegedly strangled by the Jews. Discussion on these pictures has taken place with the participation of the Polish Jewish Community. "The Polish Council of Christians and Jews has offered to finance a plaque with explanations of the <a href="/wiki/Blood_libel" title="Blood libel">blood libel</a> and information about the official statements by various Popes rejecting the accusation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This plaque is now displayed in the St Paul's Church next to the picture in question.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_world_wars">The world wars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sandomierz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The world wars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city again suffered damage during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. In 1918 it again became part of independent Poland. In the 1930s, due to the massive public works project known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Industrial_Area" title="Central Industrial Area">Central Industrial Area</a>, Sandomierz began to grow quickly. It was projected to become capital of the <a href="/wiki/Sandomierz_Voivodeship_(1939)" title="Sandomierz Voivodeship (1939)">Sandomierz Voivodeship</a>, and local authorities planned fast development of the city. The <i>Greater Sandomierz</i> was to turn in the 1940s into a city of 120 000.</p>
<p>In September 1939, following the German invasion of Poland, the city was occupied by Germany and made part of the <a href="/wiki/General_Government" title="General Government">General Government</a>. The Jewish population of the city, consisting of about 2,500 people, perished during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holocaust" title="Holocaust">Holocaust</a>, mostly in the death camps of <a href="/wiki/Be%C5%82%C5%BCec_extermination_camp" title="Bełżec extermination camp">Bełżec</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treblinka" title="Treblinka">Treblinka</a>. The city was captured by the Soviet army in August, 1944.</p>
<p>No major industrial development took place in Sandomierz during the communist era, thus preserving its look of a charming, small city full of historical monuments among unspoiled landscape.</p>
