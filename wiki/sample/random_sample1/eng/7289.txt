<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arithmetic%E2%80%93geometric_mean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first algorithm based on this sequence pair appeared in the works of <a href="/wiki/Joseph-Louis_Lagrange" title="Joseph-Louis Lagrange">Lagrange</a>. Its properties were further analyzed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gauss" title="Gauss">Gauss</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BerggrenBorwein2004_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BerggrenBorwein2004-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arithmetic%E2%80%93geometric_mean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The geometric mean of two positive numbers is never bigger than the arithmetic mean (see <a href="/wiki/Inequality_of_arithmetic_and_geometric_means" title="Inequality of arithmetic and geometric means">inequality of arithmetic and geometric means</a>); as a consequence, <span class="texhtml">(<i>g<sub>n</sub></i>)</span> is an increasing sequence, <span class="texhtml">(<i>a<sub>n</sub></i>)</span> is a decreasing sequence, and <span class="texhtml"><i>g<sub>n</sub></i> ≤ <i>M</i>(<i>x</i>, <i>y</i>) ≤ <i>a<sub>n</sub></i></span>. These are strict inequalities if <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i> ≠ <i>y</i></span>.</p>
<p><span class="texhtml"><i>M</i>(<i>x</i>, <i>y</i>)</span> is thus a number between the geometric and arithmetic mean of <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>y</i></span>; it is also between <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>y</i></span>.</p>
<p>If <span class="texhtml"><i>r</i> ≥ 0</span>, then <span class="texhtml"><i>M</i>(<i>rx</i>,<i>ry</i>) = <i>r M</i>(<i>x</i>,<i>y</i>)</span>.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Geometric%E2%80%93harmonic_mean" title="Geometric–harmonic mean">geometric–harmonic mean</a> can be calculated by an analogous method, using sequences of geometric and <a href="/wiki/Harmonic_mean" title="Harmonic mean">harmonic</a> means. <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_applications">Other applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arithmetic%E2%80%93geometric_mean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Using this property of the AGM along with the ascending transformations of Landen,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Brent_(scientist)" title="Richard Brent (scientist)">Richard Brent</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> suggested the first AGM algorithms for the fast evaluation of elementary transcendental functions (<i>e</i><sup><i>x</i></sup>, cos <i>x</i>, sin <i>x</i>). Subsequently, many authors went on to study the use of the AGM algorithms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
