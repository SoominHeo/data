<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Postpartum period</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>A <b>postpartum period</b> or <b>postnatal period</b> is the period beginning immediately after the <a href="/wiki/Childbirth" title="Childbirth">birth</a> of a child and extending for about six weeks. Less frequently used are the terms <b>puerperium</b> or <b>puerperal period</b>. The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> (WHO) describes the postnatal period as the most critical and yet the most neglected phase in the lives of mothers and babies; most deaths occur during the postnatal period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World_Health_Organization_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-World_Health_Organization-1">[1]</a></sup> It is the time after birth, a time in which the mother's body, including <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormone</a> levels and <a href="/wiki/Uterus" title="Uterus">uterus</a> size, returns to a non-pregnant state. <a href="/wiki/Lochia" title="Lochia">Lochia</a> is postpartum vaginal discharge, containing blood, mucus, and uterine tissue.</p>
<p>In scientific literature, the term is commonly abbreviated to <b>P<i>x</i></b>, where <i><b>x</b></i> is a number; for example, "day P5" should be read as "the fifth day after birth". This is not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Gravidity_and_parity" title="Gravidity and parity">medical nomenclature that uses G P to stand for number of pregnancy and outcome of pregnancy</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Newborns">Newborns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postpartum_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Newborns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Infant" title="Infant">Infant</a></div>
<p>Upon its entry to the air-breathing world, without the nutrition and oxygenation from the umbilical cord, the newborn must begin to <a href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_extrauterine_life" title="Adaptation to extrauterine life">adjust to life outside the uterus</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Postpartum_period_in_mothers">Postpartum period in mothers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postpartum_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Postpartum period in mothers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A woman giving birth in a <a href="/wiki/Hospital" title="Hospital">hospital</a> may leave the hospital as soon as she is medically stable and chooses to leave, which can be as early as a few hours postpartum, though the average for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spontaneous_vaginal_delivery" title="Spontaneous vaginal delivery">a vaginal birth</a> is 1–2 days, and the average <a href="/wiki/Caesarean_section" title="Caesarean section">caesarean section</a> postnatal stay is 3–4 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> During this time, the mother is monitored for <a href="/wiki/Bleeding" title="Bleeding">bleeding</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bowel" title="Bowel">bowel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urinary_bladder" title="Urinary bladder">bladder</a> function, and baby care. The <a href="/wiki/Infant" title="Infant">infant</a>'s health is also monitored.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical">Physical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postpartum_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Sex_after_pregnancy" title="Sex after pregnancy">Sex after pregnancy</a></div>
<p>The mother is <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/assessed" title="wikt:assessed">assessed</a> for <a href="/wiki/Tearing" title="Tearing">tears</a>, and is <a href="/wiki/Surgical_suture" title="Surgical suture">sutured</a> if necessary. Also, she may suffer from <a href="/wiki/Constipation" title="Constipation">constipation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hemorrhoid" title="Hemorrhoid">hemorrhoids</a>, both of which would be managed. The bladder is also assessed for <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urinary_retention" title="Urinary retention">retention</a>, and any problems in the <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscles</a>.</p>
<p>The major focus of postpartum care is ensuring that the mother is healthy and capable of taking care of her newborn, equipped with all the information she needs about <a href="/wiki/Breastfeeding" title="Breastfeeding">breastfeeding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_health" title="Reproductive health">reproductive health</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contraception" title="Contraception">contraception</a>, and the imminent life adjustment.</p>
<p>Some medical conditions may occur in the postpartum period, such as <a href="/wiki/Sheehan%27s_syndrome" title="Sheehan's syndrome">Sheehan's syndrome</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Peripartum_cardiomyopathy" title="Peripartum cardiomyopathy">peripartum cardiomyopathy</a>.</p>
<p>In some cases, this adjustment is not made easily, and women may suffer from <a href="/wiki/Postpartum_depression" title="Postpartum depression">postpartum depression</a>, <a href="/wiki/Posttraumatic_stress_disorder" title="Posttraumatic stress disorder">posttraumatic stress disorder</a> or even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puerperal_psychosis" title="Puerperal psychosis">puerperal psychosis</a>.</p>
<p>Postpartum <a href="/wiki/Urinary_incontinence" title="Urinary incontinence">urinary incontinence</a> is experienced by about 33% of all women; women who deliver vaginally are about twice as likely to have urinary incontinence as women who give birth via a cesarean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the postpartum period, a woman may urinate out up to nine pounds of water. The extra fluid that her body has taken on is no longer needed, so the mother may note that her fluid output is disproportionate to her fluid input.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychological">Psychological</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postpartum_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Psychological">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Postpartum mental illness can affect both mothers and fathers, and is not uncommon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Early detection and adequate treatment is required. Approximately 25% - 85% of postpartum women will experience the "blues" for a few days. Between 7% and 17% may experience clinical depression, with a higher risk among those women with a history of clinical depression. Rarely, in 1 in 1,000 cases, women experience a psychotic episode, again with a higher risk among those women with pre-existing mental illness. Despite the widespread myth of hormonal involvement, repeated studies have not linked hormonal changes with postpartum psychological symptoms. Rather, these are symptoms of a pre-existing mental illness, exacerbated by fatigue, changes in schedule and other common parenting stressors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Postpartum psychosis (also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puerperal_psychosis" title="Puerperal psychosis">puerperal psychosis</a>) is a more severe form of mental illness than postpartum depression, with an incidence of approximately 0.2%.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postpartum_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lying-in" title="Lying-in">Lying-in</a></div>
<p>Lying-in is the term for the European and Western custom of a woman resting in bed for a period after giving birth. Though the term is now usually defined as "the condition of a woman in the process of giving birth," it previously referred to a period of <a href="/wiki/Bed_rest" title="Bed rest">bed rest</a> required even if there were no medical complications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 1932 publication refers to lying-in as ranging from 2 weeks to 2 months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nusche_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nusche-9">[9]</a></sup> It also does not suggest "Getting Up" (getting out of bed post-birth) for at least nine days and ideally for 20 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nusche_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nusche-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> When lying-in was a more common term, it was used in the names of several hospitals, for example the <a href="/wiki/General_Lying-In_Hospital" title="General Lying-In Hospital">General Lying-In Hospital</a> in London.</p>
<p>Women received congratulatory visits from friends and family during the period and, among the many traditional customs around the world, the <i><a href="/wiki/Desco_da_parto" title="Desco da parto">desco da parto</a></i> was a special form of painted tray presented to the mother in <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>. The many scenes after childbirth painted on these show female visitors bringing presents, received by the mother in bed, while other women tend to the baby. No fixed term of lying-in is recommended in Renaissance manuals on family life (unlike in some other cultures), but it appears from documentary records that the mother was rarely present at the <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptism</a>, in Italian cities usually held within a week of the birth at the local <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parish church</a>, normally a few minutes walk from any house.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are many local variations of customs, and the period of lying-in expected. Traditionally, Eastern Orthodox mothers would spend 40 days confined at home with their infant after giving birth. At the end of the 40 days (the <i>sarántisma</i>, or "fortying"), the child was symbolically taken to church for the first time, where the mother asked for a special blessing on the conclusion of her puerperium. There are many modern theories seeking to justify this traditional practice, including <a href="/wiki/Neonatal_sepsis" title="Neonatal sepsis">weakness of infant immune systems</a>, unimpeded establishment of <a href="/wiki/Breastfeeding" title="Breastfeeding">breastfeeding</a>, and the need for bonding time between parent and child.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Asia">East Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postpartum_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: East Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Postpartum_confinement" title="Postpartum confinement">Postpartum confinement</a></div>
<p>In some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Asian" title="East Asian">East Asian</a> cultures, such as <a href="/wiki/Chinese_culture" title="Chinese culture">Chinese</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Korean_culture" title="South Korean culture">South Korean</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vietnamese_culture" title="Vietnamese culture">Vietnamese</a>, there is a traditional custom of <a href="/wiki/Postpartum_confinement" title="Postpartum confinement">postpartum confinement</a> known in English as <b>doing the month</b> or <b>sitting the month</b> (<a href="/wiki/Standard_Chinese" title="Standard Chinese">Mandarin</a> zuò yuèzi 坐月子). Confinement traditionally lasts 30 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This tradition combines prescribed foods with a number of restrictions on activities considered to be harmful. The new mother is also given special postnatal foods, such as <a href="/wiki/Miyeokguk" title="Miyeokguk">seaweed soup</a> in Korea and "<a href="/wiki/Pork_Knuckles_and_Ginger_Stew" title="Pork Knuckles and Ginger Stew">Pork Knuckles and Ginger Stew</a>" in China. It is widely believed in many East Asian societies that this custom helps heal injuries to the <a href="/wiki/Perineum" title="Perineum">perineum</a>, promote the contraction of the <a href="/wiki/Uterus" title="Uterus">uterus</a>, and promote <a href="/wiki/Lactation" title="Lactation">lactation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Thailand "<i>yu-fai</i> (lie down by a fire) treatment is traditional postpartum healing. Performed in an open area, it involves using smokeless tamarind wood, local herbs and massage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postpartum_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most traditional Indians follow the 40-day confinement and recuperation period also known as the 'Jaappa' (in Hindi). A special diet to facilitate milk production and increase hemoglobin levels is followed. Sex is not allowed during this time. In Hindu culture, the puerperium was traditionally considered a period of relative impurity (<i>asaucham</i>) due to the processes of childbirth, and a period of confinement of 10–40 days (known as <i>purudu</i>) was recommended for the mother. During this period, she was exempted from usual household chores and religious rites. The father was purified by a ritual bath before visiting the mother in confinement. In the event of a stillbirth, the period of impurity for both parents was 24 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Many Indian sub cultures have own traditions after birth, birth period is called as Virdi(Marathi) starting from birth till 10 days where complete abstinence from puja or temple visit.</p>
