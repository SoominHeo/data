<p>In <a href="/wiki/Group_theory" title="Group theory">group theory</a>, a discipline within mathematics, a <b>sporadic group</b> is one of the 26 exceptional <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">groups</a> found in the <a href="/wiki/Classification_of_finite_simple_groups" title="Classification of finite simple groups">classification of finite simple groups</a>.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Simple_group" title="Simple group">simple group</a> is a group <i>G</i> that does not have any <a href="/wiki/Normal_subgroup" title="Normal subgroup">normal subgroups</a> except for the trivial group and <i>G</i> itself. The classification theorem states that the <a href="/wiki/List_of_finite_simple_groups" title="List of finite simple groups">list of finite simple groups</a> consists of 18 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Countably" title="Countably">countably</a> infinite families, plus 26 exceptions that do not follow such a systematic pattern. These are the sporadic groups. They are also known as the sporadic simple groups, or the sporadic finite groups. Because it is not strictly a <a href="/wiki/Group_of_Lie_type" title="Group of Lie type">group of Lie type</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tits_group" title="Tits group">Tits group</a> is sometimes regarded as a sporadic group,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> in which case the sporadic groups number 27.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Monster_group" title="Monster group">monster group</a> is the largest of the sporadic groups and contains all but six of the other sporadic groups as <a href="/wiki/Subgroup" title="Subgroup">subgroups</a> or <a href="/wiki/Subquotient" title="Subquotient">subquotients</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_of_the_sporadic_groups">Names of the sporadic groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sporadic_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Names of the sporadic groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Five of the sporadic groups were discovered by <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_L%C3%A9onard_Mathieu" title="Émile Léonard Mathieu">Mathieu</a> in the 1860s and the other 21 were found between 1965 and 1975. Several of these groups were predicted to exist before they were constructed. Most of the groups are named after the mathematician(s) who first predicted their existence. The full list is:</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tits_group" title="Tits group">Tits group</a> <i>T</i> is sometimes also regarded as a sporadic group (it is almost but not strictly a group of Lie type), which is why in some sources the number of sporadic groups is given as 27 instead of 26.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In some other sources, the Tits group is regarded as neither sporadic nor of Lie type.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Matrix <a href="/wiki/Group_representation" title="Group representation">representations</a> over finite fields for all the sporadic groups have been constructed.</p>
<p>The earliest use of the term "sporadic group" may be <a href="#CITEREFBurnside1911">Burnside (1911</a>, p. 504, note N) where he comments about the Mathieu groups: "These apparently sporadic simple groups would probably repay a closer examination than they have yet received".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sporadic_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Of the 26 sporadic groups, 20 can be seen inside the <a href="/wiki/Monster_group" title="Monster group">Monster group</a> as <a href="/wiki/Subgroup" title="Subgroup">subgroups</a> or <a href="/wiki/Quotient_group" title="Quotient group">quotients</a> of subgroups (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Section_(group_theory)" title="Section (group theory)">sections</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="I._Pariah">I. Pariah</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sporadic_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: I. Pariah">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pariah_group" title="Pariah group">Pariah group</a></div>
<p>The six exceptions are <i>J</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>J</i><sub>3</sub>, <i>J</i><sub>4</sub>, <i>O'N</i>, <i>Ru</i> and <i>Ly</i>. These six are sometimes known as the <a href="/wiki/Pariah_group" title="Pariah group">pariahs</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="II._Happy_Family">II. Happy Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sporadic_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: II. Happy Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The remaining twenty have been called the <i>Happy Family</i> by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Griess" title="Robert Griess">Robert Griess</a>, and can be organized into three generations.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="First_generation_.285_groups.29:_the_Mathieu_groups">First generation (5 groups): the Mathieu groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sporadic_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: First generation (5 groups): the Mathieu groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathieu_groups" title="Mathieu groups">Mathieu groups</a></div>
<p>M<sub><i>n</i></sub> for <i>n</i> = 11, 12, 22, 23 and 24 are multiply transitive <a href="/wiki/Permutation_group" title="Permutation group">permutation groups</a> on <i>n</i> points. They are all subgroups of M<sub>24</sub>, which is a permutation group on <a href="/wiki/24_(number)" title="24 (number)">24</a> points.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_generation_.287_groups.29:_the_Leech_lattice">Second generation (7 groups): the Leech lattice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sporadic_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Second generation (7 groups): the Leech lattice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Leech_lattice" title="Leech lattice">Leech lattice</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conway_groups" title="Conway groups">Conway groups</a></div>
<p>All the <a href="/wiki/Subquotient" title="Subquotient">subquotients</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automorphism_group" title="Automorphism group">automorphism group</a> of a lattice in <a href="/wiki/24_(number)" title="24 (number)">24</a> dimensions called the <a href="/wiki/Leech_lattice" title="Leech lattice">Leech lattice</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Co</i><sub>1</sub> is the quotient of the automorphism group by its center {±1}</li>
<li><i>Co</i><sub>2</sub> is the stabilizer of a type 2 (i.e., length 2) vector</li>
<li><i>Co</i><sub>3</sub> is the stabilizer of a type 3 (i.e., length √6) vector</li>
<li><i>Suz</i> is the group of automorphisms preserving a complex structure (modulo its center)</li>
<li><i>McL</i> is the stabilizer of a type 2-2-3 triangle</li>
<li><i>HS</i> is the stabilizer of a type 2-3-3 triangle</li>
<li><i>J</i><sub>2</sub> is the group of automorphisms preserving a quaternionic structure (modulo its center).</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Third_generation_.288_groups.29:_other_subgroups_of_the_Monster">Third generation (8 groups): other subgroups of the Monster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sporadic_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Third generation (8 groups): other subgroups of the Monster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Consists of subgroups which are closely related to the Monster group <i>M</i>:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>B</i> or <i>F</i><sub>2</sub> has a double cover which is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centralizer" title="Centralizer">centralizer</a> of an element of order 2 in <i>M</i></li>
<li><i>Fi</i><sub>24</sub>′ has a triple cover which is the centralizer of an element of order 3 in <i>M</i> (in <a href="/wiki/Conjugacy_class" title="Conjugacy class">conjugacy class</a> "3A")</li>
<li><i>Fi</i><sub>23</sub> is a subgroup of <i>Fi</i><sub>24</sub>′</li>
<li><i>Fi</i><sub>22</sub> has a double cover which is a subgroup of <i>Fi</i><sub>23</sub></li>
<li>The product of <i>Th</i> = <i>F</i><sub>3</sub> and a group of order 3 is the centralizer of an element of order 3 in <i>M</i> (in conjugacy class "3C")</li>
<li>The product of <i>HN</i> = <i>F</i><sub>5</sub> and a group of order 5 is the centralizer of an element of order 5 in <i>M</i></li>
<li>The product of <i>He</i> = <i>F</i><sub>7</sub> and a group of order 7 is the centralizer of an element of order 7 in <i>M</i>.</li>
<li>Finally, the Monster group itself is considered to be in this generation.</li>
</ul>
<p>(This series continues further: the product of <i>M</i><sub>12</sub> and a group of order 11 is the centralizer of an element of order 11 in <i>M</i>.)</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tits_group" title="Tits group">Tits group</a> also belongs in this generation: there is a subgroup S<sub>4</sub> ×<sup>2</sup>F<sub>4</sub>(2)′ normalising a 2C<sup>2</sup> subgroup of <i>B</i>, giving rise to a subgroup 2·S<sub>4</sub> ×<sup>2</sup>F<sub>4</sub>(2)′ normalising a certain Q<sub>8</sub> subgroup of the Monster. <sup>2</sup>F<sub>4</sub>(2)′ is also a subgroup of the Fischer groups <i>Fi</i><sub>22</sub>, <i>Fi</i><sub>23</sub> and <i>Fi</i><sub>24</sub>′, and of the Baby Monster <i>B</i>. <sup>2</sup>F<sub>4</sub>(2)′ is also a subgroup of the (pariah) Rudvalis group <i>Ru</i>, and has no involvements in sporadic simple groups except the containments we have already mentioned.</p>
