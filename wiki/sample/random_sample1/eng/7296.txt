<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Sanjo (music)</h1>

<p><i><b>Sanjo</b></i>, literally meaning 'scattered melodies,' is a style of traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_music" title="Korean music">Korean music</a>, involving an instrumental solo accompanied by drumming on the <i><a href="/wiki/Janggu" title="Janggu">janggu</a></i>, an <a href="/wiki/Hourglass_drum" title="Hourglass drum">hourglass-shaped drum</a>. The art of <i>sanjo</i> is a real crystalliization of traditional Korean melody and rhythm which may have been handed down by rote generation after generation. The drummer who beats the <i>janggu</i> also makes <i><a href="/wiki/Chuimsae" title="Chuimsae">chuimsae</a></i> (exclamations) in order to please the audience. The audience can also express their excited feeling with <i>chuimsae</i> while listening to <i>sanjo</i>. A big <i>chuimsae</i> indicates a good performance, so the musician can make a better performance. Like <i><a href="/wiki/Pansori" title="Pansori">pansori</a></i>, <i>chuimsae</i> plays an important role in <i>sanjo</i>. Without <i>chuimsae</i>, the music is meaningless. <i>Chuimsae</i> connects musician and audience during a <i>sanjo</i> performance. Almost every Korean traditional musical instrument is used in <i>sanjo</i>: <a href="/wiki/Gayageum" title="Gayageum">gayageum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geomungo" title="Geomungo">geomungo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daegeum" title="Daegeum">daegeum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haegeum" title="Haegeum">haegeum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piri" title="Piri">piri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taepyeongso" title="Taepyeongso">taepyeongso</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ajaeng" title="Ajaeng">ajaeng</a>, <a href="/wiki/Danso" title="Danso">danso</a>.</p>
<p><i>Sanjo</i> was said to be developed around 1890 by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kim_Chang-jo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kim Chang-jo (page does not exist)">Kim Chang-jo</a> (1865–1920) for the <i><a href="/wiki/Gayageum" title="Gayageum">gayageum</a></i>. Thereafter, it was expanded to other traditional Korean instruments, including the <i>geomungo</i> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_flute" title="Korean flute">Korean flutes</a>. Its early development was informed by other genres of traditional music, including <i><a href="/wiki/Pansori" title="Pansori">pansori</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sinawi" title="Sinawi">sinawi</a></i>, and the performances of <a href="/wiki/Korean_shamanism" title="Korean shamanism">Korean shamanism</a>.</p>
<p><i>Daegeum sanjo</i>, played on the <i><a href="/wiki/Daegeum" title="Daegeum">daegeum</a></i> (a traditional Korean <a href="/wiki/Transverse_flute" title="Transverse flute">transverse flute</a>) was developed in the 1920s. It has since become one of the most popular forms of <i>sanjo</i>. Its leading practitioner today is <a href="/wiki/Yi_Saenggang" title="Yi Saenggang">Yi Saenggang</a>.</p>
<p>Sanjo is traditionally identified as a form of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minsogak" title="Minsogak">minsogak</a></i>, or folk music.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanjo_(music)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The composition of <i>sanjo</i> varies depending on the people, instruments and time. However, usually <i>sanjo</i> starts with a slow <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jinyangjo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jinyangjo (page does not exist)">jinyangjo</a></i> rhythm (hangul: 진양조장단; very slow rhythm used in <i>pansori</i> or <i>sanjo</i>) and becomes faster, ending with a very fast rhythm like a <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Danmori&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Danmori (page does not exist)">danmori</a></i> rhythm and creating enthusiasm in the audience. Starting from a slow rhythm, the audience can gradually sink into the melody of the song. <i>Sanjo</i> expresses various aspects of the player. <i>Sanjo</i>s are not fixed music. The musician can make new music with original variations. <i>Sanjo</i> has endless melodies in which musicians make new compositions that change with the times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
