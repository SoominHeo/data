<p><b>Santa Maria Novella</b> is a church in <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, Italy, situated just across from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_Novella_Station" title="Santa Maria Novella Station">main railway station</a> named after it. Chronologically, it is the first great basilica in Florence, and is the city's principal <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominican</a> church.</p>
<p>The church, the adjoining cloister, and <a href="/wiki/Chapter_house" title="Chapter house">chapter house</a> contain a multiplicity of art treasures and funerary monuments. Especially famous are frescoes by masters of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gothic_Art" title="Gothic Art">Gothic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Renaissance_painting" title="Early Renaissance painting">early Renaissance</a>. They were financed by the most important Florentine families, who ensured themselves funerary chapels on consecrated ground.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_Novella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Via_degli_Avelli_3.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2112" data-file-width="2816" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Via_degli_Avelli_3.JPG/220px-Via_degli_Avelli_3.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Via_degli_Avelli_3.JPG/330px-Via_degli_Avelli_3.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Via_degli_Avelli_3.JPG/440px-Via_degli_Avelli_3.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Via_degli_Avelli_3.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Via degli Avelli side</div>
</div>

<p>This church was called <i>Novella</i> (New) because it was built on the site of the 9th-century <a href="/wiki/Oratory_(worship)" title="Oratory (worship)">oratory</a> of Santa Maria delle Vigne. When the site was assigned to <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominican Order</a> in 1221, they decided to build a new church and adjoining cloister. The church was designed by two Dominican friars, Fra Sisto Fiorentino and Fra Ristoro da Campi. Building began in the mid-13th century (about 1246), and was finished about 1360 under the supervision of Friar Iacopo Talenti with the completion of the <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque</a>-<a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Campanile" title="Campanile">bell tower</a> and sacristy. At that time, only the lower part of the Tuscan <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gothic_(architecture)" title="Gothic (architecture)">gothic</a> façade was finished. The three portals are spanned by round arches, while the rest of the lower part of the facade is spanned by blind arches, separated by pilasters, with below Gothic pointed arches, striped in green and white, capping tombs of the nobility. This same design continues in the adjoining wall around the old churchyard. The church was consecrated in 1420.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Santa_Maria_Novella_1.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Side view from Piazza Unità d'Italia</div>

<p>On a commission from <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_di_Paolo_Rucellai" title="Giovanni di Paolo Rucellai">Giovanni di Paolo Rucellai</a>, a local textile merchant, <a href="/wiki/Leon_Battista_Alberti" title="Leon Battista Alberti">Leon Battista Alberti</a> designed the upper part of the inlaid green marble of Prato, also called "serpentino" and white marble façade of the church (1456–1470). He was already famous as the architect of the <a href="/wiki/Tempio_Malatestiano" title="Tempio Malatestiano">Tempio Malatestiano</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rimini" title="Rimini">Rimini</a>, but even more for his seminal treatise on architecture <i>De Re Aedificatoria</i>, based on the book <i>De Architectura</i> of the classical Roman writer <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvius</a>. Alberti had also designed the façade for the <a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Rucellai" title="Palazzo Rucellai">Rucellai Palace</a> in Florence.</p>
<p>Alberti attempted to bring the ideals of humanist architecture, proportion and classically inspired detailing to bear on the design, while also creating harmony with the already existing medieval part of the façade. His contribution consists of a broad <a href="/wiki/Frieze" title="Frieze">frieze</a> decorated with squares, and the full upper part, including the four white-green pilasters and a round window, crowned by a <a href="/wiki/Pediment" title="Pediment">pediment</a> with the Dominican solar emblem, and flanked on both sides by enormous S-curved <a href="/wiki/Volute" title="Volute">volutes</a>. The four columns with Corinthian capitals on the lower part of the façade were also added. The pediment and the frieze are clearly inspired by antiquity, but the S-curved scrolls in the upper part are new and without precedent in antiquity. The scrolls (or variations of them), found in churches all over Italy, all draw their origins from the design of this church.</p>
<p>The frieze below the pediment carries the name of the patron : <i>IOHAN(N)ES ORICELLARIUS PAU(LI) F(ILIUS) AN(NO) SAL(UTIS) MCCCCLXX (Giovanni Rucellai son of Paolo in the year of salvation 1470).</i></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interior">Interior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_Novella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Interior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tornabuoni_Chapel" title="Tornabuoni Chapel">Tornabuoni Chapel</a></div>
<p>The vast interior is based on a basilica plan, designed as a Latin cross, and is divided into a nave, two aisles with stained-glass windows and a short transept. The large nave is 100 metres long and gives an impression of austerity. There is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trompe_l%27oeil" title="Trompe l'oeil">trompe l'oeil</a> effect by which towards the <a href="/wiki/Apse" title="Apse">apse</a> the nave seems longer than its actual length. The slender compound piers between the nave and the aisles are progressively closer the deeper the observer moves into the nave. The ceiling in the vault consists of pointed arches with the four diagonal buttresses in black and white.</p>
<p>The interior also contains Corinthian columns that were inspired by Greek and Roman classical models.</p>
<p>The stained-glass windows date from the 14th and 15th century, such as 15th century <i>Madonna and Child</i> and St. John and St. Philip (designed by <a href="/wiki/Filippino_Lippi" title="Filippino Lippi">Filippino Lippi</a>), both in the <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Strozzi_the_Elder" title="Filippo Strozzi the Elder">Filippo Strozzi</a> Chapel. Some stained glass windows have been damaged in the course of centuries and have been replaced. The one on the façade, a depiction of the <i>Coronation of Mary</i>, dates from the 14th century, and is based on a design of <a href="/wiki/Andrea_di_Bonaiuto_da_Firenze" title="Andrea di Bonaiuto da Firenze">Andrea di Bonaiuto da Firenze</a>.</p>
<p>The pulpit, commissioned by the Rucellai family in 1443, was designed by <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Filippo Brunelleschi</a> and executed by his adopted son <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Andrea_Calvalcanti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Andrea Calvalcanti (page does not exist)">Andrea Calvalcanti</a>. This pulpit has a particular historical significance, since it was from this pulpit that the first verbal attack was made on <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a>, leading eventually to his indictment.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Masaccio_trinity.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1073" data-file-width="531" height="344" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Masaccio_trinity.jpg/170px-Masaccio_trinity.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Masaccio_trinity.jpg/255px-Masaccio_trinity.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Masaccio_trinity.jpg/340px-Masaccio_trinity.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Masaccio_trinity.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Holy_Trinity_(Masaccio)" title="The Holy Trinity (Masaccio)">The Holy Trinity</a></i> by Masaccio.</div>
</div>

<p><i>The <a href="/wiki/Holy_Trinity_(Masaccio)" title="Holy Trinity (Masaccio)">Holy Trinity</a></i>, situated almost halfway along the left aisle, is a pioneering early Renaissance work of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tommaso_Masaccio" title="Tommaso Masaccio">Masaccio</a>, showing his new ideas about perspective and mathematical proportions. Its meaning for the art of painting can easily be compared to the importance of <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Brunelleschi</a> for architecture and <a href="/wiki/Donatello" title="Donatello">Donatello</a> for sculpture. The patrons were the judge and his wife, members of the Lenzi family, here depicted kneeling. The <a href="/wiki/Cadaver_tomb" title="Cadaver tomb">cadaver tomb</a> below carries in Latin the <a href="/wiki/Epigram" title="Epigram">epigram</a>: "I was once what you are, and what I am you will become".</p>
<p>Of particular note in the right aisle is the <i>Tomba della Beata Villana</i>, a monument by <a href="/wiki/Bernardo_Rossellino" title="Bernardo Rossellino">Bernardo Rossellino</a> executed in 1451. In the same aisle, are located tombs of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Fiesole" title="Bishop of Fiesole">bishops of Fiesole</a>, one by <a href="/wiki/Tino_di_Camaino" title="Tino di Camaino">Tino di Camaino</a> and another by <a href="/wiki/Nino_Pisano" title="Nino Pisano">Nino Pisano</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filippo_Strozzi_Chapel">Filippo Strozzi Chapel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_Novella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Filippo Strozzi Chapel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Strozzi_the_Elder" title="Filippo Strozzi the Elder">Filippo Strozzi</a> Chapel is situated on the right side of the main altar. The series of <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">frescoes</a> by Filippino Lippi depict the lives of <a href="/wiki/Philip_the_Apostle" title="Philip the Apostle">Apostle Philip</a> and the Apostle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_James_the_Great" title="Saint James the Great">Saint James the Great</a> and were completed in 1502. On the right wall is the fresco <i>St Philip Driving the Dragon from the Temple of Hieropolis</i> and in the lunette above it, the <i>Crucifixion of St Philip</i>. On the left wall is the fresco <i>St John the Evangelist Resuscitating Druisana</i> and in the lunette above it <i>The Torture of St John the Evangelist</i>. Adam, Noah, Abraham and Jacob are represented on the <a href="/wiki/Rib_vault" title="Rib vault">ribbed vault</a>. Behind the altar is the tomb of <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Strozzi_the_Elder" title="Filippo Strozzi the Elder">Filippo Strozzi</a> with a sculpture by <a href="/wiki/Benedetto_da_Maiano" title="Benedetto da Maiano">Benedetto da Maiano</a> (1491).</p>
<p>The bronze crucifix on the main altar is by <a href="/wiki/Giambologna" title="Giambologna">Giambologna</a> (16th century). The choir (or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cappella_Tornabuoni" title="Cappella Tornabuoni">Cappella Tornabuoni</a>) contains another series of famous <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">frescoes</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Domenico_Ghirlandaio" title="Domenico Ghirlandaio">Domenico Ghirlandaio</a> and his apprentice the young <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a> (1485–1490). They represent themes from the life of the Virgin and John the Baptist, situated in the Florence of the late 15th century and containing portrayals of several members of important Florentine families. The vaults are covered with paintings of the Evangelists. On the rear wall are the paintings <i>Saint Dominic burns the Heretical Books</i> and <i>Saint Peter's Martyrdom</i>, the <i>Annunciation</i>, and <i>Saint John goes into the Desert</i>.</p>
<p>The stained-glass windows were made in 1492 by the Florentine artist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Agolanti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alessandro Agolanti (page does not exist)">Alessandro Agolanti</a>, known also as Il Bidello, and were based on cartoons by <a href="/wiki/Ghirlandaio" title="Ghirlandaio">Ghirlandaio</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gondi_Chapel">Gondi Chapel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_Novella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gondi Chapel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This chapel, designed by <a href="/wiki/Giuliano_da_Sangallo" title="Giuliano da Sangallo">Giuliano da Sangallo</a>, is situated on the left side of the main altar and dates from the end of the 13th century. Here, on the rear wall, is the famous wooden <i>Crucifix</i> by <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Brunelleschi</a>, one of his very few sculptures. The legend goes that he was so disgusted by the "primitive" Crucifix of <a href="/wiki/Donatello" title="Donatello">Donatello</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Santa_Croce,_Florence" title="Basilica of Santa Croce, Florence">Florence's church of Santa Croce</a> that he made this one. The vault contains fragments of frescoes by 13th-century Greek painters. The polychrome marble decoration was applied by <a href="/wiki/Giuliano_da_Sangallo" title="Giuliano da Sangallo">Giuliano da Sangallo</a> (c. 1503). The stained-glass window and dates from the 20th century.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cappella_Strozzi_di_Mantova">Cappella Strozzi di Mantova</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_Novella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cappella Strozzi di Mantova">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Cappella Strozzi di Mantova is situated at the end of the left transept. The frescoes were commissioned from <a href="/wiki/Nardo_di_Cione" title="Nardo di Cione">Nardo di Cione</a> (1350–1357) by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tommaso_Strozzi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tommaso Strozzi (page does not exist)">Tommaso Strozzi</a>, an ancestor of <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Strozzi_the_Elder" title="Filippo Strozzi the Elder">Filippo Strozzi</a>. The [fresco]es are inspired by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dante" title="Dante">Dante</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Divine_Comedy" title="Divine Comedy">Divine Comedy</a></i>: Last Judgment (on the back wall; including a portrait of Dante), Hell (on the right wall) and paradise (on the left wall). The main altarpiece of <i>The Redeemer with the Madonna and Saints</i> was done by <a href="/wiki/Nardo_di_Cione" title="Nardo di Cione">Nardo di Cione</a>'s brother, Andrea di Cione, better known as <a href="/wiki/Orcagna" title="Orcagna">Orcagna</a>. The large stained-glass window on the back was made from a cartoon by the two brothers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Della_Pura_Chapel">Della Pura Chapel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_Novella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Della Pura Chapel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Della Pura Chapel is situated north of the old cemetery. It dates from 1474 and was constructed with Renaissance columns. It was restored in 1841 by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gaetano_Baccani&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gaetano Baccani (page does not exist)">Gaetano Baccani</a>. On the left side there is a <a href="/wiki/Lunette" title="Lunette">lunette</a> with a 14th-century fresco <i>Madonna and Child with St Catherine</i>. On the front altar there is a wooden crucifix by <a href="/wiki/Baccio_da_Montelupo" title="Baccio da Montelupo">Baccio da Montelupo</a> (1501).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rucellai_Chapel">Rucellai Chapel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_Novella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rucellai Chapel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rucellai&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rucellai (page does not exist)">Rucellai</a> Chapel, at the end of the right aisle, dates from the 14th century. Besides the tomb of Paolo Rucellai (15th century) and the marble statue of the <i>Madonna and the Child</i> by Nino Pisano, it houses several art treasures such as remains of frescoes by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Maestro_di_Santa_Cecilia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Maestro di Santa Cecilia (page does not exist)">Maestro di Santa Cecilia</a> (end 13th – beginning 14th century). The panel on the left wall, <i>the Martyrdom of St Catherine</i>, was painted by <a href="/wiki/Giuliano_Bugiardini" title="Giuliano Bugiardini">Giuliano Bugiardini</a> (possibly with assistance from Michelangelo). The bronze tomb, in the centre of the floor, was made by <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_Ghiberti" title="Lorenzo Ghiberti">Lorenzo Ghiberti</a> in 1425.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bardi_Chapel">Bardi Chapel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_Novella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Bardi Chapel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Bardi Chapel, the second chapel on the right of the apse, was founded by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Riccardo_Bardi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Riccardo Bardi (page does not exist)">Riccardo Bardi</a> and dates from early 14th century. The high-relief on a pillar on the right depicts <i>St Gregory blessing Riccardo Bardi</i>. The walls show us some early 14th-century frescoes attributed to <a href="/wiki/Spinello_Aretino" title="Spinello Aretino">Spinello Aretino</a>. The <i>Madonna del Rosario</i> on the altar is by <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Vasari" title="Giorgio Vasari">Giorgio Vasari</a> (1568)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sacristy">Sacristy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_Novella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sacristy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The sacristy, at the end of the left aisle, was built as the Chapel of the Annunciation by the Cavalcanti family in 1380. It houses, after a recent period of fourteen years of cleaning and renovation, the enormous painted <i>Crucifix with the Madonna and John the Evangelist</i>, an early work by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giotto_di_Bondone" title="Giotto di Bondone">Giotto</a>. He had rediscovered the ideal proportions for the human body, as established by the Roman architect Vitruvius (1st century AD), the model later called by some <i><a href="/wiki/Vitruvian_Man" title="Vitruvian Man">Vitruvian Man</a></i>. The sacristy is also embellished by a <a href="/wiki/Glazed_architectural_terra-cotta" title="Glazed architectural terra-cotta">glazed terra cotta</a> and a marble font, masterpieces by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_della_Robbia" title="Giovanni della Robbia">Giovanni della Robbia</a> (1498). The cupboards were designed by <a href="/wiki/Bernardo_Buontalenti" title="Bernardo Buontalenti">Bernardo Buontalenti</a> in 1593. The paintings on the wall are ascribed to <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Vasari" title="Giorgio Vasari">Giorgio Vasari</a> and to other contemporary Florentine painters. The large Gothic window with three <a href="/wiki/Mullion" title="Mullion">mullions</a> at the back wall dates from 1386 and was based on cartoons by <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_di_Pietro_Gerini" title="Niccolò di Pietro Gerini">Niccolò di Pietro Gerini</a></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish_Chapel">Spanish Chapel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_Novella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Spanish Chapel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Museo_di_santa_maria_novella,_cappellone_degli_spagnoli,_affreschi_di_andrea_di_bonaiuto_8.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Museo_di_santa_maria_novella%2C_cappellone_degli_spagnoli%2C_affreschi_di_andrea_di_bonaiuto_8.JPG/290px-Museo_di_santa_maria_novella%2C_cappellone_degli_spagnoli%2C_affreschi_di_andrea_di_bonaiuto_8.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Museo_di_santa_maria_novella%2C_cappellone_degli_spagnoli%2C_affreschi_di_andrea_di_bonaiuto_8.JPG/435px-Museo_di_santa_maria_novella%2C_cappellone_degli_spagnoli%2C_affreschi_di_andrea_di_bonaiuto_8.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Museo_di_santa_maria_novella%2C_cappellone_degli_spagnoli%2C_affreschi_di_andrea_di_bonaiuto_8.JPG/580px-Museo_di_santa_maria_novella%2C_cappellone_degli_spagnoli%2C_affreschi_di_andrea_di_bonaiuto_8.JPG 2x" width="290"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Museo_di_santa_maria_novella,_cappellone_degli_spagnoli,_affreschi_di_andrea_di_bonaiuto_8.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Fresco by <a href="/wiki/Andrea_di_Bonaiuto_da_Firenze" title="Andrea di Bonaiuto da Firenze">Andrea di Bonaiuto da Firenze</a> in the Spanish Chapel: <i>Allegory of the Active and Triumphant Church and of the Dominican order</i> (c. 1365)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Spanish Chapel (or <i>Cappellone degli Spagnoli</i>) is the former <a href="/wiki/Chapter_house" title="Chapter house">chapter house</a> of the monastery. It is situated at the north side of the green Cloister (<i>Chiostro Verde</i>). It was commissioned by Buonamico (Mico) Guidalotti as his funerary chapel. Construction started c. 1343 and was finished in 1355. The Guidalotti chapel was later called "Spanish Chapel", because <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosimo_I" title="Cosimo I">Cosimo I</a> assigned it to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eleonora_of_Toledo" title="Eleonora of Toledo">Eleonora of Toledo</a> and her Spanish retinue. Within the Spanish Chapel there is a smaller Chapel of the Most Holy Sacrament. The Spanish Chapel was decorated from 1365 to 1367 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andrea_di_Bonaiuto" title="Andrea di Bonaiuto">Andrea di Bonaiuto</a>, also known as Andrea da Firenze. The large fresco on the right wall depicts an <i>Allegory of the Active and Triumphant Church and of the Dominican order</i>. It is especially interesting because in the background it shows a large pink building that some think may provide some insight into the original designs for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duomo_of_Florence" title="Duomo of Florence">Florence Cathedral</a> by <a href="/wiki/Arnolfo_di_Cambio" title="Arnolfo di Cambio">Arnolfo di Cambio</a> (before Brunelleschi's dome was built). However, such an interpretation is fantasy since the Duomo was never intended to be pink, nor to have the belltower at the rear. This fresco also contains portraits of pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedict_IX" title="Benedict IX">Benedict IX</a>, cardinal Friar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Albertini" title="Niccolò Albertini">Niccolò Albertini</a>, count Guido di Poppi, <a href="/wiki/Arnolfo_di_Cambio" title="Arnolfo di Cambio">Arnolfo di Cambio</a> and the poet <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">Petrarch</a>. The frescoes on the other walls represent scenes from the lives of Christ and St Peter on the entry wall (mostly ruined due to the later installation of a choir), <i>The Triumph of St Thomas Aquinas</i> and the <i>Allegory of Christian Learning</i> on the left wall, and the large "Crucifixion with the Way to Calvalry and the Descent into Limbo" on the archway of the altar wall. The four-part vault contains scenes of Christ's resurrection, the navicella, the Ascension, and Pentecost. The five-panelled Gothic <a href="/wiki/Polyptych" title="Polyptych">polyptych</a> that was probably originally made for the chapel's altar, depicting the <i>Madonna Enthroned with Child and Four Saints</i> by <a href="/wiki/Bernardo_Daddi" title="Bernardo Daddi">Bernardo Daddi</a> dates from 1344 and is currently on display in a small museum area reached ed through glass doors from the far end of the cloister. Together, the complex iconography of the ceiling vault, walls, and altar combine to communicate the message of Dominicans as guides to salvation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_Novella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Giorgio Vasari was the architect, commissioned in 1567 by Grand Duke <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosimo_I_de%27_Medici" title="Cosimo I de' Medici">Cosimo I</a>, for the first remodeling of the church, which included removing its original <a href="/wiki/Rood_screen" title="Rood screen">rood screen</a> and loft, and adding six chapels between the columns. An <a href="/wiki/Armillary_sphere" title="Armillary sphere">armillary sphere</a> (on the left) and a <a href="/wiki/Gnomon" title="Gnomon">gnomon</a> (on the right) were added to the end blind arches of the lower façade by <a href="/wiki/Ignazio_Danti" title="Ignazio Danti">Ignazio Danti</a>, astronomer of Cosimo I, in 1572. The second remodeling was designed by Enrico Romoli, and was carried out between 1858 and 1860.</p>

<p>The square in front the church was used by Cosimo I for the yearly chariot race (<i>Palio dei Cocchi</i>). This custom existed between 1563 and late in the 19th century. The two <a href="/wiki/Obelisks_of_the_Corsa_dei_Cocchi" title="Obelisks of the Corsa dei Cocchi">Obelisks of the Corsa dei Cocchi</a> marked the start and the finish of the race. They were set up to imitate an antique Roman <a href="/wiki/Circus_Maximus" title="Circus Maximus">circus</a>. The obelisks rest on bronze tortoises, made in 1608 by the sculptor <a href="/wiki/Giambologna" title="Giambologna">Giambologna</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_artworks">List of artworks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_Novella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: List of artworks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<li><a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Filippo Brunelleschi</a> – <i>The Crucifix</i> (between 1410 and 1425)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tino_da_Camaino" title="Tino da Camaino">Tino da Camaino</a> – <i>Bust of St. Antoninus</i> (in terra cotta); the <i>Tomb of the Bishop of Fiesole</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nardo_di_Cione" title="Nardo di Cione">Nardo di Cione</a> – frescoes of the Divine Judgment</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Duccio" title="Duccio">Duccio</a> – <i>Rucellai Madonna</i></li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Domenico_Ghirlandaio" title="Domenico Ghirlandaio">Domenico Ghirlandaio</a> – frescoes (late 15th century) in the <a href="/wiki/Tornabuoni_Chapel" title="Tornabuoni Chapel">Tornabuoni Chapel</a>, design of the stained-glass window</li>

<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Giacomo_Marchetti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Giacomo Marchetti (page does not exist)">Giacomo Marchetti</a> : <i>Martyrdom of Saint Laurence</i>.</li>
