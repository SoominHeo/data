<p><b>Santa Maria della Vittoria</b> (English: Our Lady of Victory, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>S. Mariae de Victoria</i></span>) is a Roman Catholic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titulus_(Roman_Catholic)" title="Titulus (Roman Catholic)">titular</a> <a href="/wiki/Churches_of_Rome" title="Churches of Rome">church</a> dedicated to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virgin_Mary" title="Virgin Mary">Virgin Mary</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. The church is known for the masterpiece of <a href="/wiki/Gian_Lorenzo_Bernini" title="Gian Lorenzo Bernini">Gian Lorenzo Bernini</a> in the Cornaro Chapel, the <i><a href="/wiki/Ecstasy_of_Saint_Teresa" title="Ecstasy of Saint Teresa">Ecstasy of Saint Teresa</a></i>. The church is in the Rione <a href="/wiki/Sallustiano" title="Sallustiano">Sallustiano</a>, on number 98 via XX Settembre, where this street intersects with Largo Santa Susanna. It stands to the side of the <a href="/wiki/Fontana_dell%27Acqua_Felice" title="Fontana dell'Acqua Felice">Fontana dell'Acqua Felice</a>. The church mirrors the Church of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Susanna" title="Santa Susanna">Santa Susanna</a> across the Largo. It is about two blocks northwest of <a href="/wiki/Repubblica_%E2%80%93_Teatro_dell%27Opera_(Rome_Metro)" title="Repubblica – Teatro dell'Opera (Rome Metro)">Piazza della Repubblica and Teatro dell'Opera</a> metro stop.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_della_Vittoria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The church was begun in 1605 as a chapel dedicated to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Paul" title="Saint Paul">Saint Paul</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Discalced_Carmelites" title="Discalced Carmelites">Discalced Carmelites</a>. After the Catholic victory at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_White_Mountain" title="Battle of White Mountain">battle of White Mountain</a> in 1620, which reversed the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>, the church was rededicated to the Virgin Mary. Turkish <a href="/wiki/War_flag" title="War flag">standards</a> captured at the 1683 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Vienna" title="Battle of Vienna">siege of Vienna</a> hang in the church, as part of this theme of victory.</p>
<p>The order itself funded the building work until the discovery of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Borghese_Hermaphroditus" title="Borghese Hermaphroditus">Borghese Hermaphroditus</a> in the excavations. <a href="/wiki/Scipione_Borghese" title="Scipione Borghese">Scipione Borghese</a>, nephew of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_V" title="Pope Paul V">Pope Paul V</a>, appropriated this sculpture but in return funded the rest of work on the façade and granted the order his architect <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Soria" title="Giovanni Battista Soria">Giovanni Battista Soria</a>. These grants only came into effect in 1624, and work was completed two years later.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exterior">Exterior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_della_Vittoria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Exterior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The church is the only structure designed and completed by the early <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> architect <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Maderno" title="Carlo Maderno">Carlo Maderno</a>, though the interior suffered a fire in 1833 and required restoration. Its façade, however, was erected by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Soria" title="Giovanni Battista Soria">Giovanni Battista Soria</a> during Maderno's lifetime, 1624–1626, showing the unmistakable influence of Maderno's <a href="/wiki/Santa_Susanna" title="Santa Susanna">Santa Susanna</a> nearby.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interior">Interior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_della_Vittoria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Interior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lazio_Roma_SMVittoria3_tango7174.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Lazio_Roma_SMVittoria3_tango7174.jpg/220px-Lazio_Roma_SMVittoria3_tango7174.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Lazio_Roma_SMVittoria3_tango7174.jpg/330px-Lazio_Roma_SMVittoria3_tango7174.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Lazio_Roma_SMVittoria3_tango7174.jpg/440px-Lazio_Roma_SMVittoria3_tango7174.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lazio_Roma_SMVittoria3_tango7174.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cantoria of the Santa Maria della Vittoria church, decorated by Mattia de Rossi</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Its interior has a single wide nave under a low segmental vault, with three interconnecting side chapels behind arches separated by colossal <a href="/wiki/Corinthian_order" title="Corinthian order">corinthian pilasters</a> with gilded capitals that support an enriched <a href="/wiki/Entablature" title="Entablature">entablature</a>. Contrasting marble revetments are enriched with white and gilded stucco angels and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Putti" title="Putti">putti</a> in full relief. The interior was sequentially enriched after Maderno's death; its vault was frescoed in 1675 with triumphant themes within shaped compartments with feigned frames: <i>The Virgin Mary Triumphing over Heresy</i> and <i>Fall of the Rebel Angels</i> executed by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Domenico_Cerrini" title="Giovanni Domenico Cerrini">Giovanni Domenico Cerrini</a> in 1675.</p>
<p>Other sculptural detail abounds: <i>The Dream of Joseph</i> (left transept, <a href="/wiki/Domenico_Guidi" title="Domenico Guidi">Domenico Guidi</a>, flanked by relief panels by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pierre_Etienne_Monnot" title="Pierre Etienne Monnot">Pierre Etienne Monnot</a>) and the funeral monument to Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Berlinghiero_Gessi" title="Berlinghiero Gessi">Berlinghiero Gessi</a>. There are paintings by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giovanni_Francesco_Barbieri" title="Giovanni Francesco Barbieri">Guercino</a>, Nicolas Lorrain, and <a href="/wiki/Domenichino" title="Domenichino">Domenichino</a>. The church is also the final resting place of <a href="/wiki/Victoria,_Anatolia,_and_Audax" title="Victoria, Anatolia, and Audax">Saint Victoria</a>, whose preserved remains are on display inside.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornaro_Chapel">Cornaro Chapel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_della_Vittoria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cornaro Chapel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The masterpiece in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecstasy_of_St_Theresa" title="Ecstasy of St Theresa">Cornaro Chapel</a>, to the left of the altar, is <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecstasy_of_St_Theresa" title="Ecstasy of St Theresa">Ecstasy of St. Teresa</a></i> by Scipione's favored sculptor, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernini" title="Bernini">Bernini</a>. The statues depict a moment described by Saint <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teresa_of_Avila" title="Teresa of Avila">Teresa of Avila</a> in her autobiography, where she had the vivid vision of a <a href="/wiki/Seraph" title="Seraph">seraph</a> piercing her heart with a golden shaft, causing her both immense joy and pain. The flowing robes and contorted posture abandon classical restraint and repose to depict a more passionate, almost voluptuous trance.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References_in_popular_culture">References in popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_della_Vittoria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: References in popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The church has seen a surge in tourism thanks to the widespread popularity of author <a href="/wiki/Dan_Brown" title="Dan Brown">Dan Brown</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Angels_%26_Demons" title="Angels &amp; Demons">Angels &amp; Demons</a></i>, which features the building (but for purposes of his novel, the writer moved its location down to the <a href="/wiki/Piazza_Barberini" title="Piazza Barberini">Piazza Barberini</a>). The statue is a feature in the 2009 novel, <a href="/wiki/Cutting_for_Stone" title="Cutting for Stone">Cutting for Stone</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Verghese" title="Abraham Verghese">Abraham Verghese</a>. Main characters live with a fading picture of it in their Ethiopian home as a remnant of their deceased mother and friend. It remains a part of their lives and surfaces late in the story as they are witness to the real marble creation in the chapel.</p>
