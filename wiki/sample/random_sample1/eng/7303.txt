<p><b>Santa Maria sopra Minerva</b> (English: <i>Saint Mary above Minerva</i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la"><i>Sancta Maria supra Minervam</i></span>) is one of the major <a href="/wiki/Church_(building)" title="Church (building)">churches</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Order_of_Preachers" title="Order of Preachers">Order of Preachers</a> (better known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dominican_order" title="Dominican order">Dominicans</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, Italy. The church's name derives from the fact that the first Christian church structure on the site was built directly over (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: </i><span lang="la">supra</span>) the ruins or foundations of a temple dedicated to the Egyptian goddess <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a>, which had been erroneously ascribed to the Greco-Roman goddess <a href="/wiki/Minerva" title="Minerva">Minerva</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GF98_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-GF98-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The church is located in <a href="/wiki/Piazza_della_Minerva" title="Piazza della Minerva">Piazza della Minerva</a> one block behind the <a href="/wiki/Pantheon,_Rome" title="Pantheon, Rome">Pantheon</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Pigna_(rione_of_Rome)" title="Pigna (rione of Rome)">Pigna</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Rioni_of_Rome" title="Rioni of Rome">rione</a></i> of Rome within the ancient district known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Campus_Martius" title="Campus Martius">Campus Martius</a></i>. The present church and disposition of surrounding structures is visible in a detail from the <a href="/wiki/Giambattista_Nolli" title="Giambattista Nolli">Nolli Map</a> of 1748.</p>
<p>While many other medieval churches in Rome have been given <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> makeovers that cover Gothic structures, the Minerva is the only extant example of original <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> church building in Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Behind a restrained Renaissance style façade<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ministry_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ministry-3">[3]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> interior features arched vaulting that was painted blue with gilded stars and trimmed with brilliant red ribbing in a 19th-century Neo-Gothic restoration.</p>
<p>The church and adjoining convent served at various times throughout its history as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Order_of_Preachers" title="Order of Preachers">Dominican Order's</a> headquarters. Today the headquarters have been re-established in their original location at the Roman convent of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Sabina" title="Santa Sabina">Santa Sabina</a>. The current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_Priest" title="Cardinal Priest">Cardinal Priest</a> of the <i>Titulus Sanctae Mariae supra Minervam</i> has been <a href="/wiki/Cormac_Murphy-O%27Connor" title="Cormac Murphy-O'Connor">Cormac Murphy-O'Connor</a> since 2001, when he was <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Westminster" title="Archbishop of Westminster">Archbishop of Westminster</a>, the senior position in the English Catholic church, from which he has since retired.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_sopra_Minerva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</div>
<p>In Roman times there were three temples in what is now the area surrounding the basilica and former convent buildings: the <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Minerva_Chalcidica" title="Temple of Minerva Chalcidica">Minervium</a>, built by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gnaeus_Pompey" title="Gnaeus Pompey">Gnaeus Pompey</a> in honour of the goddess <a href="/wiki/Minerva" title="Minerva">Minerva</a> about 50 BC, referred to as <i><span lang="la">Delubrum Minervae</span></i>; the <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Isis_and_Serapis" title="Temple of Isis and Serapis">Iseum</a> dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Isis_and_Serapis" title="Temple of Isis and Serapis">Serapeum</a> dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Serapis" title="Serapis">Serapis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Details of the temple to Minerva are not known but recent investigations indicate that a small round Minervium once stood a little further to east on the Piazza of the Collegio Romano.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GF98_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-GF98-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1665 an <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egyptian</a> <a href="/wiki/Obelisk" title="Obelisk">obelisk</a> was found, buried in the garden of the Dominican cloister adjacent to the church. Several other small obelisks were found at different times near the church, known as the <i><span lang="la">Obelisci Isei Campensis</span></i>, which were probably brought to Rome during the 1st century and grouped in pairs, with others, at the entrances of the temple of Isis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> There are other Roman survivals in the <a href="/wiki/Crypt" title="Crypt">crypt</a>.</p>
<p>The ruined temple is likely to have lasted until the reign of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Zachary" title="Pope Zachary">Pope Zachary</a> (741-752), who finally <a href="/wiki/Christianization" title="Christianization">Christianized the site</a>, offering it to Basilian nuns from Constantinople who maintained an <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikt" title="Wikt">oratorium</a></i> there dedicated to the "Virgin of Minervum.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The structure he commissioned has disappeared.</p>
<p>In 1255 <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_IV" title="Pope Alexander IV">Pope Alexander IV</a> established a community of converted women on the site. A decade later this community was transferred to the Roman Church of <a href="/wiki/San_Pancrazio" title="San Pancrazio">San Pancrazio</a> thereby allowing the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominicans</a> to establish a convent of friars and a <i>studium conventuale</i> there. The Friars were on site beginning in 1266 but took official possession of the Church in 1275. Aldobrandino Cavalcanti (+1279), “vicarius Urbis” or vicar for <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_X" title="Pope Gregory X">Pope Gregory X</a>, and an associate of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a> ratified the donation of Santa Maria sopra Minerva to the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominicans</a> of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Sabina" title="Santa Sabina">Santa Sabina</a> by the sisters of S. Maria in Campo Marzio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The ensemble of buildings that formed around the church and convent came to be known as the 'insula sapientae" or "insula dominicana' (island of wisdom or Dominican island).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beniculturali.it_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-beniculturali.it-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Dominicans began building the present <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> church in 1280 modelling it on their church in Florence <a href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_Novella" title="Santa Maria Novella">Santa Maria Novella</a>. Architectural plans were probably drawn up during the pontificate of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Nicholas_III" title="Pope Nicholas III">Nicholas III</a> by two Dominican friars, Fra Sisto Fiorentino and Fra Ristoro da Campi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GF98_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-GF98-1">[1]</a></sup> With the help of funds contributed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boniface_VIII" title="Boniface VIII">Boniface VIII</a> and the faithful the side aisles were completed in the 14th century.</p>
<p>In 1453 church interior construction was finally completed when Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Torquemada_(cardinal)" title="Juan de Torquemada (cardinal)">Juan Torquemada</a> ordered that the main nave be covered by a vault that reduced the overall projected height of the church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ministry_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ministry-3">[3]</a></sup> In the same year of 1453 Count Francesco Orsini sponsored the construction of the façade at his own expense. However work on the façade remained incomplete until 1725 when it was finally finished by order of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XIII" title="Pope Benedict XIII">Pope Benedict XIII</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beniculturali.it_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-beniculturali.it-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1431, the Church and the adjacent Convent of the Dominicans was the site of a <a href="/wiki/Papal_conclave" title="Papal conclave">Papal conclave</a>. The city of Rome was in an uproar upon the death of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Martin_V" title="Pope Martin V">Pope Martin V</a> (Colonna), whose family had dominated Roman political life for fifteen years, and enriched themselves on the wealth of the Church. There was fighting in the streets on a daily basis, and the Plaza in front of the Minerva, because of the configuration of streets, houses, church and monastery, could easily be fortified and defended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The Sacristy of the Church served as the meeting hall for the fourteen cardinals (out of nineteen) who attended the Conclave, which began on 1 March 1431. The dormitory of the monks in the Convent to the immediate north of the Church, served as the living quarters for the cardinals and their refectory and kitchen. On 3 March they elected Cardinal Gabriele Condulmaro, who took the name <a href="/wiki/Pope_Eugene_IV" title="Pope Eugene IV">Eugenius IV</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> A second Conclave was held at the Minerva, on 4-6 March 1447, following the death of Pope Eugenius, once again in the midst of disturbances involving the Orsini supporters of Pope Eugenius and his enemies the Colonna. Eighteen cardinals (out of a total of twenty-six) were present and elected Cardinal Tommaso Parentucelli da Sarzana as <a href="/wiki/Pope_Nicholas_V" title="Pope Nicholas V">Pope Nicholas V</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Minerva has been a <a href="/wiki/Titular_church" title="Titular church">titular church</a> since 1557,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and a <a href="/wiki/Minor_basilica" title="Minor basilica">minor basilica</a> since 1566. The church's first titular cardinal was Michele Ghislieri who became Pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pius_V" title="Pius V">Pius V</a> in 1566. He raised the church to the level of <a href="/wiki/Minor_basilica" title="Minor basilica">minor basilica</a> in that same year.</p>
<p>In the 16th century <a href="/wiki/Giuliano_da_Sangallo" title="Giuliano da Sangallo">Giuliano da Sangallo</a> made changes in the choir area, and in 1600 <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Maderno" title="Carlo Maderno">Carlo Maderno</a> enlarged the apse, added <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> decorations and created the present façade with its pilastered tripartite division in <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ministry_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ministry-3">[3]</a></sup> Marks on this façade dating back to the 16th and 17th centuries indicate various flood levels of the Tiber 65 feet (20 metres).</p>
<p>Between 1848 and 1855 Girolamo Bianchedi directed an important program of restoration when most of the Baroque additions were removed and the blank walls were covered with neo-gothic frescos giving the interior the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival" title="Gothic Revival">Neo-Gothic</a> appearance that it has today.</p>
<p>The basilica's stained glass windows are mostly from the 19th century. In 1909, the great organ was constructed by the firm of Carlo Vegezzi Bossi. The organ was restored in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The inscriptions found in S. Maria sopra Minerva have been collected and published by Vincenzo Forcella.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Convent_and_Studium">Convent and <i>Studium</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_sopra_Minerva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Convent and Studium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1288 the theology component of the provincial curriculum for the education of the friars was relocated from the <i>studium provinciale</i> at the Roman basilica of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Sabina" title="Santa Sabina">Santa Sabina</a> to the <i>studium conventuale</i> at Santa Maria sopra Minerva which was redesignated as a <i>studium particularis theologiae</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> At various times in its history this <i>studium</i> served as a <i><a href="/wiki/Studium_generale" title="Studium generale">studium generale</a></i> for the Roman province of the Order.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="College_of_Saint_Thomas">College of Saint Thomas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_sopra_Minerva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: College of Saint Thomas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The late 16th century saw the <i>studium</i> at Santa Maria sopra Minerva undergo transformation. Thomas Aquinas, who had been canonized in 1323 by Pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_XXII" title="John XXII">John XXII</a>, was proclaimed the fifth Latin <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_the_Church" title="Doctor of the Church">Doctor of the Church</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pius_V" title="Pius V">Pius V</a> in 1567. To honor this great doctor, in 1577 the Spanish Dominican Msgr. <a href="/wiki/Juan_Solano" title="Juan Solano">Juan Solano</a>, O.P., former bishop of Cusco, Peru, generously funded the reorganization of the <i>studium</i> at the convent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_Sopra_Minerva" title="Santa Maria Sopra Minerva">Santa Maria Sopra Minerva</a> on the model of the College of <a href="/wiki/Colegio_de_San_Gregorio" title="Colegio de San Gregorio">St. Gregory at Valladolid</a> in his native Spain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The result of Solano's initiative, which underwent structural change shortly before Solano's death in 1580, was the College of Saint Thomas (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la"><i>Collegium Divi Thomae</i></span>) at Santa Maria sopra Minerva, the future <a href="/wiki/Pontifical_University_of_Saint_Thomas_Aquinas" title="Pontifical University of Saint Thomas Aquinas">Pontifical University of Saint Thomas Aquinas</a> (<i>Angelicum</i>). The college occupied several existing convent structures and new construction was required. At that time the convent underwent considerable reconstruction to accommodate the college and the cloister was redesigned so that side chapels could be added to the church's northern flank. A detail from the <a href="/wiki/Giambattista_Nolli" title="Giambattista Nolli">Nolli Map</a> of 1748 gives some idea of the disposition of buildings when the Minerva convent housed the College of St. Thomas.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Offices_of_the_Inquisition">Offices of the Inquisition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_sopra_Minerva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Offices of the Inquisition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 14 September 1628, by papal decree, the convent of Minerva was designated as the seat of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congregation_of_the_Holy_Office" title="Congregation of the Holy Office">Congregation of the Holy Office</a>. It thus became the place where the tribunal of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Inquisition" title="Roman Inquisition">Roman Inquisition</a> set up by Paul III in 1542 held the Secret Congregation meetings during which the sentences were read out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seminario_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seminario-17">[17]</a></sup> It was in a room of the Minerva Convent on 22 June 1633 that the father of modern astronomy <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galileo_affair" title="Galileo affair">after being tried for heresy, abjured his scientific theses in favour of the Copernican theory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seminario_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Seminario-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the late 18th and early 19th century the suppression of religious orders hampered the mission of the Order and the College of St. Thomas. During the French occupation of Rome from 1797 to 1814 the College declined and even briefly closed its doors from 1810 to 1815.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The Order gained control of the convent once again in 1815 only for it to be expropriated by the Italian government in 1870.</p>
<p>In 1873 the <i>Collegium Divi Thomæ de Urbe</i> was forced to leave the Minerva for good eventually being relocated at the convent of <a href="/wiki/Santi_Domenico_e_Sisto" title="Santi Domenico e Sisto">Saints Dominic and Sixtus</a> in 1932 and being transformed into the <a href="/wiki/Pontifical_University_of_Saint_Thomas_Aquinas" title="Pontifical University of Saint Thomas Aquinas">Pontifical University of Saint Thomas Aquinas, <i>Angelicum</i></a> in 1963.</p>
<p>The Dominicans eventually were allowed to return to the Minerva and part of the convent.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interior">Interior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_sopra_Minerva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Interior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Among several important works of art in the church are <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>'s statue <i><a href="/wiki/Cristo_della_Minerva" title="Cristo della Minerva">Cristo della Minerva</a></i> (1521) and the late 15th-century (1488–93) cycle of frescos in the <a href="/wiki/Carafa_Chapel" title="Carafa Chapel">Carafa Chapel</a> by <a href="/wiki/Filippino_Lippi" title="Filippino Lippi">Filippino Lippi</a>. The basilica also houses many funerary monuments including the tombs of <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_the_Church" title="Doctor of the Church">Doctor of the Church</a> Saint <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Siena" title="Catherine of Siena">Catherine of Siena</a> (1347-1380), who was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Third_Order_of_Saint_Dominic" title="Third Order of Saint Dominic">Third Order of Saint Dominic</a>, and the Dominican friar <a href="/wiki/Beatification" title="Beatification">Blessed</a> John of Fiesole (Fra Giovanni da Fiesole, born Guido di Piero) better known as <a href="/wiki/Fra_Angelico" title="Fra Angelico">Fra Angelico</a> (c. 1395-1455).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carafa_Chapel">Carafa Chapel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_sopra_Minerva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Carafa Chapel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Carafa_Chapel" title="Carafa Chapel">Carafa Chapel</a>, with late 15th-century frescoes (1488–1493) by <a href="/wiki/Filippino_Lippi" title="Filippino Lippi">Filippino Lippi</a>, was commissioned by Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Oliviero_Carafa" title="Oliviero Carafa">Oliviero Carafa</a> in honour of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas_Aquinas" title="Saint Thomas Aquinas">Saint Thomas Aquinas</a>. There are two Marian scenes, the <i><a href="/wiki/Annunciation" title="Annunciation">Annunciation</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Assumption_of_Mary" title="Assumption of Mary">Assumption</a></i>; over the altar is his St Thomas presenting Cardinal Carafa to the Blessed Virgin, and on the right-hand wall his Glory of St Thomas. It was inaugurated in 1493, and is also known as the Chapel of St <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a>. The relics of St Thomas Aquinas were kept in this chapel until 1511, when they were moved to Naples. Designed by <a href="/wiki/Pirro_Ligorio" title="Pirro Ligorio">Pirro Ligorio</a> in 1559, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_papal_tombs" title="List of papal tombs">tomb of Gian Pietro Carafa</a>, who became <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_IV" title="Pope Paul IV">Pope Paul IV</a> in 1555, is also in the chapel.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cappella_Capranica">Cappella Capranica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_sopra_Minerva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cappella Capranica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The chapel is also known as the Chapel of the Rosary. The stucco ceiling was made in 1573 by <a href="/wiki/Marcello_Venusti" title="Marcello Venusti">Marcello Venusti</a>. The chapel contains the tomb of Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Domenico_Capranica" title="Domenico Capranica">Domenico Capranica</a> by <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Bregno" title="Andrea Bregno">Andrea Bregno</a>.</p>

<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Cristo_della_Minerva" title="Cristo della Minerva">Cristo della Minerva</a></i>, also known as <i>Christ the Redeemer</i> or <i>Christ Carrying the Cross</i>, is a marble sculpture by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michelangelo_Buonarroti" title="Michelangelo Buonarroti">Michelangelo Buonarroti</a>, finished in 1521, located to the left of the main altar.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cappella_Aldobrandini">Cappella Aldobrandini</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_sopra_Minerva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cappella Aldobrandini">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aldobrandini" title="Aldobrandini">Aldobrandini</a> chapel was designed by <a href="/wiki/Giacomo_della_Porta" title="Giacomo della Porta">Giacomo della Porta</a> but it is <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Maderno" title="Carlo Maderno">Carlo Maderno</a> that completed della Porta's project (after 1602). It was consecrated in 1611. The canvas depicting the Institution of the Eucharist and dated from 1594 is by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federico_Fiori" title="Federico Fiori">Federico Fiori</a>. The monument to the parents of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_VIII" title="Pope Clement VIII">Pope Clement VIII</a>, Salvestro Aldobrandini and Luisa Dati, is by <a href="/wiki/Giacomo_della_Porta" title="Giacomo della Porta">Giacomo della Porta</a>. The first <a href="/wiki/Confraternity_of_the_Blessed_Sacrament" title="Confraternity of the Blessed Sacrament">Confraternity of the Blessed Sacrament</a> to be approved by the Holy See was established in this chapel, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Ignatius_of_Loyola" title="St. Ignatius of Loyola">St. Ignatius of Loyola</a> as one of its earliest members. This chapel contains the <a href="/wiki/Federico_Barocci" title="Federico Barocci">Federico Barocci</a> altarpiece depicting the <i><a href="/wiki/Communion_of_the_Apostles_(Barocci)" title="Communion of the Apostles (Barocci)">Communion of the Apostles</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cappella_Raymond_of_Penyafort">Cappella Raymond of Penyafort</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_sopra_Minerva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cappella Raymond of Penyafort">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The chapel dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Raymond_of_Penyafort" title="Raymond of Penyafort">Raymond of Penyafort</a> houses the tomb of Cardinal <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Juan_D%C3%ADaz_de_Coca&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Juan Díaz de Coca (page does not exist)">Juan Díaz de Coca</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Bregno" title="Andrea Bregno">Andrea Bregno</a>. The ceiling fresco <i>Jesus Christ as a Judge, between two angels</i> is by <a href="/wiki/Melozzo_da_Forl%C3%AC" title="Melozzo da Forlì">Melozzo da Forlì</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Burials">Burials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_sopra_Minerva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Burials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sarcophagus_of_Saint_Catherine_of_Siena.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1358" data-file-width="2100" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Sarcophagus_of_Saint_Catherine_of_Siena.jpg/220px-Sarcophagus_of_Saint_Catherine_of_Siena.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Sarcophagus_of_Saint_Catherine_of_Siena.jpg/330px-Sarcophagus_of_Saint_Catherine_of_Siena.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Sarcophagus_of_Saint_Catherine_of_Siena.jpg/440px-Sarcophagus_of_Saint_Catherine_of_Siena.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sarcophagus_of_Saint_Catherine_of_Siena.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sarcophagus of Saint Catherine of Siena beneath the High Altar</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Saint <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Siena" title="Catherine of Siena">Catherine of Siena</a> is buried here (except her head, which is in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basilica_of_San_Domenico_(Siena)" title="Basilica of San Domenico (Siena)">Basilica of San Domenico</a> in <a href="/wiki/Siena" title="Siena">Siena</a>). Beyond the sacristy, the room where she died in 1380 was reconstructed here by <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Barberini" title="Antonio Barberini">Antonio Barberini</a> in 1637. This room is the first transplanted interior, and the progenitor of familiar 19th and 20th century museum "period rooms." The <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">frescoes</a> by <a href="/wiki/Antoniazzo_Romano" title="Antoniazzo Romano">Antoniazzo Romano</a> that decorated the original walls, however, are now lost.</p>
<p>The famous early <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> painter <a href="/wiki/Fra_Angelico" title="Fra Angelico">Fra Angelico</a> died in the adjoining convent and was buried in the church. (He had painted a fresco cycle in the cloister on the initiative of Cardinal Juan de Torquemada, but those paintings have not survived.) Popes <a href="/wiki/Pope_Urban_VII" title="Pope Urban VII">Urban VII</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_IV" title="Pope Paul IV">Paul IV</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medici" title="Medici">Medici</a> popes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leo_X" title="Leo X">Leo X</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clement_VII" title="Clement VII">Clement VII</a> were also buried in the church.</p>
<p>Before the construction of <a href="/wiki/San_Giovanni_dei_Fiorentini" title="San Giovanni dei Fiorentini">San Giovanni dei Fiorentini</a>, the Minerva was the church in Rome of the <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florentines</a>, and therefore it contains numerous tombs of prelates, nobles and citizens coming from that <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscan</a> city. Curiously, <a href="/wiki/Diotisalvi_Neroni" title="Diotisalvi Neroni">Diotisalvi Neroni</a>, a refugee who had taken part in the plot against <a href="/wiki/Piero_di_Cosimo_de%27_Medici" title="Piero di Cosimo de' Medici">Piero de' Medici</a>, was buried here in 1482, and was later joined by other members of the family.</p>
<p>Also buried here are the <a href="/wiki/Cardinal-nephew" title="Cardinal-nephew">Cardinal-nephew</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Nicholas_III" title="Pope Nicholas III">Pope Nicholas III</a> <a href="/wiki/Latino_Malabranca_Orsini" title="Latino Malabranca Orsini">Latino Malabranca Orsini</a>, Michel Mazarin (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Aix" title="Archbishop of Aix">Archbishop of Aix</a>) who was the brother of Cardinal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jules_Mazarin" title="Jules Mazarin">Jules Mazarin</a>, the Byzantine philosopher <a href="/wiki/George_of_Trebizond" title="George of Trebizond">George of Trebizond</a>, and two Renaissance theorists and practitioners, <a href="/wiki/Filarete" title="Filarete">Filarete</a> in architecture and <a href="/wiki/Mariano_Santo" title="Mariano Santo">Mariano Santo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgery</a>.</p>
<p>Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Astorgio_Agnensi" title="Astorgio Agnensi">Astorgio Agnensi</a> has his tomb monument in the cloister.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_churches_with_this_name">Other churches with this name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_sopra_Minerva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Other churches with this name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Assisi" title="Assisi">Assisi</a>, another church of <b>Santa Maria sopra Minerva</b> was built in the 16th century within the surviving <a href="/wiki/Cella" title="Cella">cella</a> of a late Republican temple of Minerva. Its <a href="/wiki/Corinthian_order" title="Corinthian order">Corinthian</a> portico still stands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Minerva.27s_Pulcino">Minerva's Pulcino</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_sopra_Minerva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Minerva's Pulcino">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Elephant_and_Obelisk" title="Elephant and Obelisk">Elephant and Obelisk</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Elephant_and_Obelisk_-_Bernini.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4470" data-file-width="3292" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Elephant_and_Obelisk_-_Bernini.jpg/170px-Elephant_and_Obelisk_-_Bernini.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Elephant_and_Obelisk_-_Bernini.jpg/255px-Elephant_and_Obelisk_-_Bernini.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Elephant_and_Obelisk_-_Bernini.jpg/340px-Elephant_and_Obelisk_-_Bernini.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Elephant_and_Obelisk_-_Bernini.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <i>Pulcino della Minerva</i>, the famous elephant sculpture by <a href="/wiki/Gian_Lorenzo_Bernini" title="Gian Lorenzo Bernini">Bernini</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ercole_Ferrata" title="Ercole Ferrata">Ercole Ferrata</a>, making the base of one of Rome's eleven Egyptian obelisks.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In front of the church there is one of the most curious monuments of Rome, the so-called <i>Pulcino della Minerva</i>. It is a statue designed by the Baroque era sculptor <a href="/wiki/Gian_Lorenzo_Bernini" title="Gian Lorenzo Bernini">Gian Lorenzo Bernini</a> (and executed by his pupil <a href="/wiki/Ercole_Ferrata" title="Ercole Ferrata">Ercole Ferrata</a> in 1667) of an elephant as the supporting base for the Egyptian obelisk found in the Dominicans' garden. It is the shortest of the eleven Egyptian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obelisks_in_Rome" title="Obelisks in Rome">obelisks in Rome</a> and is said to have been one of two obelisks moved from Sais, where they were built during the 589 BC-570 BC reign of the pharaoh <a href="/wiki/Apries" title="Apries">Apries</a>, from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twenty-sixth_dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Twenty-sixth dynasty of Egypt">Twenty-sixth dynasty of Egypt</a>. The two obelisks were brought to Rome by <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a>, during his reign as emperor from 284 to 305, for placement at the Temple of Isis, which stood nearby. The Latin inscription on the base, chosen by the pope who commissioned the sculpture to support the obelisk found on the site, Alexander VII, is said to represent that "...a strong mind is needed to support a solid knowledge".</p>
<p>The inspiration for the unusual composition came from <i><a href="/wiki/Hypnerotomachia_Poliphili" title="Hypnerotomachia Poliphili">Hypnerotomachia Poliphili</a></i> ("Poliphilo's Dream of the Strife of Love"), an unusual 15th century novel probably by <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Colonna" title="Francesco Colonna">Francesco Colonna</a>. The novel's main character meets an elephant made of stone carrying an obelisk, and the accompanying woodcut illustration<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> in the book is quite similar to Bernini's design for the base for the obelisk. The curious placement of the obelisk through the body of the elephant is identical.</p>
<p>The sturdy appearance of the structure earned it the popular nickname of "Porcino" ("Piggy") for a while. The name for the structure eventually changed to <i>Pulcino</i>, the Italian for a small or little "chick". This may have been a reference to the comparatively short height of the obelisk or, an obscure reference to the major charity of the Dominicans to assist young women needing dowries, who made a procession in the courtyard every year. The latter were once depicted in a local painting as three tiny figures with the Virgin Mary presenting purses to them.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_references">Cultural references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_sopra_Minerva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Cultural references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The elephant and obelisk monument and the church of Santa Maria sopra Minerva feature in the novel 'The Tomb of Alexander' by Sean Hemingway. In the novel it is claimed that a secret passageway beneath the church leads to a chamber beneath the elephant monument which contains the body of Alexander the Great, placed there in the 17th century by Pope Alexander VII. This is entirely a work of fiction and the theory is unproven.</p>
