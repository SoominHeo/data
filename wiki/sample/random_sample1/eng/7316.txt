<p><b>Santiago Ramón y Cajal</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (<small>Spanish: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Spanish" title="Help:IPA for Spanish">[sanˈtjaɣo raˈmon i kaˈxal]</a></span>; 1 May 1852 – 17 October 1934)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nobel_lectures_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nobel_lectures-2">[2]</a></sup> was a Spanish <a href="/wiki/Pathology" title="Pathology">pathologist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Histology" title="Histology">histologist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neuroscientist" title="Neuroscientist">neuroscientist</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_laureate" title="Nobel laureate">Nobel laureate</a>. His original pioneering investigations of the microscopic structure of the brain have led to his being designated by many as the father of modern <a href="/wiki/Neuroscience" title="Neuroscience">neuroscience</a>. His medical artistry was legendary, and hundreds of his drawings illustrating the delicate arborizations of brain cells are still in use for educational and training purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_often_misstated">Names often misstated</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santiago_Ram%C3%B3n_y_Cajal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Names often misstated">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Santiago Ramón y Cajal is often called <i>Cajal</i> despite the fact that <i>Ramón</i> was his paternal surname and his maternal surname was Cajal. Thus, for example, the greatest living neuroscientific expert on Ramón y Cajal, the Spaniard Javier DeFelipe, titled his review of Cajal's scientific work in Nature Reviews Neuroscience "Brain plasticity and mental processes: Cajal again." (Vol 7, issue 10, pp 811-817). Even in Spain (where this naming convention is the norm and it would be expected that he be referred to as "Ramón"), when Ramón y Cajal's work is referred to, there is a preference for his mother's surname, Cajal, perhaps because "Ramón" is also a forename, or simply because Cajal is a less frequent surname, what is common practice for personalities.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Thus, many texts say for example, "escuela cajaliana" (Cajal's school) or "Cajal's disciples." It should, however, be noted that some of Cajal's own articles were published under the name "S. R. Cajal", even in his own journal (e.g., Algunas consideraciones sobre la mesoglia de Robertson y Rio-Hortega. <i>Trabajos del Laboratorio de Investigaciones Biológicas de la Universidad de Madrid</i> 18 (1920): 109-127). Further, his European contemporaries generally referred to him as 'Cajal' (see, for example, reviews in <i>Zeitschrift für die gesamte Neurologie und Psychiatrie</i>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santiago_Ram%C3%B3n_y_Cajal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a child Ramón y Cajal was transferred many times from one school to another because of behavior that was declared poor, rebellious, and showing an anti-<a href="/wiki/Authoritarianism" title="Authoritarianism">authoritarian</a> attitude. An extreme example of his precociousness and rebelliousness is his imprisonment at the age of eleven for destroying his neighbor's yard gate with a homemade cannon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was an avid painter, artist, and gymnast, but his father neither appreciated nor encouraged these abilities, even though these artistic talents would contribute to his success later in life. In order to tame the unruly character of his son, his father apprenticed him to a shoemaker and barber. "To try and give his son much-needed discipline and stability, Don Justo apprenticed him out to a barber".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He was well known for his pugnacious attitude as he worked.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Over the summer of 1868, Ramón y Cajal's father, hoping to interest his son in a medical career, took him to graveyards to find human remains for anatomical study. Sketching bones was a turning point for him and subsequently, he did pursue studies in medicine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Finger2000_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Finger2000-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:207</sup></p>
<p>Ramón y Cajal attended the medical school of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Zaragoza" title="University of Zaragoza">University of Zaragoza</a>, where his father was an anatomy teacher. He graduated in 1873. After a competitive examination, he served as a medical <a href="/wiki/Officer_(armed_forces)" title="Officer (armed forces)">officer</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Army" title="Spanish Army">Spanish Army</a>. He took part in an <a href="/wiki/Ten_Years%27_War" title="Ten Years' War">expedition to Cuba</a> in 1874–75, where he contracted <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nobel_lectures_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nobel_lectures-2">[2]</a></sup> In order to cure these conditions, he visited the <a href="/wiki/Panticosa" title="Panticosa">Panticosa</a> spa-town in the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>After returning to Spain he married Silveria Fañanás García in 1879, with whom he had four daughters and three sons. In 1877, he received his doctorate in medicine in <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a> and was awarded the position of anatomy professor of the University of <a href="/wiki/Valencia" title="Valencia">Valencia</a> in 1883. He later held professorships in both <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a> (1887) and <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a> (1892).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nobel_lectures_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nobel_lectures-2">[2]</a></sup> He was also the director of the <a href="/wiki/Zaragoza" title="Zaragoza">Zaragoza</a> Museum (1879), director of the <i>Instituto Nacional de Higiene –</i> translated as <i>National Institute of Hygiene</i> - (1899), and founder of the <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><i>Laboratorio de Investigaciones Biológicas</i></span> – translated as the <i>Laboratory of Biological Investigations</i> - (1922), later renamed to the <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><i>Instituto Cajal</i></span>, or <a href="/wiki/Cajal_Institute" title="Cajal Institute">Cajal Institute</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nobel_lectures_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nobel_lectures-2">[2]</a></sup> He died in Madrid in 1934, at the age of 82, continuing to work even on his deathbed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nobel_lectures_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nobel_lectures-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>On his political and religious views, it was first said that he "was a liberal in politics, an evolutionist in philosophy, an agnostic in religion".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Nonetheless, he later regretted having left religion,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and ultimately, he became convinced of a belief in God as a creator, as stated during his first lecture before the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Royal_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Spanish Royal Academy of Sciences">Spanish Royal Academy of Sciences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> He joined a <a href="/wiki/Masonic_lodge" title="Masonic lodge">Masonic lodge</a> in 1877.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works_and_theories">Works and theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santiago_Ram%C3%B3n_y_Cajal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Works and theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cajal-mi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="330" data-file-width="234" height="310" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Cajal-mi.jpg/220px-Cajal-mi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c6/Cajal-mi.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cajal-mi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ramón y Cajal in his laboratory</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Ramón y Cajal's early work was accomplished at the University of Zaragoza and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Valencia" title="University of Valencia">University of Valencia</a>, where he focused on the pathology of <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammation</a>, the microbiology of <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a>, and the structure of <a href="/wiki/Epithelium" title="Epithelium">epithelial</a> cells and tissues. It was not until he moved to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Barcelona" title="University of Barcelona">University of Barcelona</a> in 1887 that he learned about <a href="/wiki/Golgi%27s_method" title="Golgi's method">Golgi's method</a>, which uses potassium dichromate and silver nitrate to (randomly) stain a few neurons a dark black color, while leaving the surrounding cells transparent. This staining method, which Ramón y Cajal improved, was central to his work, allowing him to turn his attention to the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a> (brain and spinal cord), in which neurons are so densely intertwined that standard microscopic inspection would be nearly impossible. During this period he made extensive studies of neural material, covering many species and most major regions of the brain, leaving behind a legacy of detailed drawings.</p>
<p>Ramón y Cajal made several major contributions to neuroanatomy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Finger2000_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Finger2000-6">[6]</a></sup> He discovered the axonal <a href="/wiki/Growth_cone" title="Growth cone">growth cone</a>, and demonstrated experimentally that the relationship between nerve cells was not <i>continuous</i>, but <i><a href="/wiki/Contiguity" title="Contiguity">contiguous</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Finger2000_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Finger2000-6">[6]</a></sup> This provided definitive evidence for what would later be known as "<a href="/wiki/Neuron_doctrine" title="Neuron doctrine">neuron doctrine</a>", now widely considered the foundation of modern neuroscience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Finger2000_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Finger2000-6">[6]</a></sup> In debating neural network theories (e.g. neuron theory, <a href="/wiki/Reticular_theory" title="Reticular theory">reticular theory</a>), Ramón y Cajal was a fierce defender of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neuron_theory" title="Neuron theory">neuron theory</a>.</p>
<p>He provided detailed descriptions of cell types associated with neural structures, and produced excellent depictions of structures and their connectivity.</p>
<p>He was an advocate of the existence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dendritic_spines" title="Dendritic spines">dendritic spines</a>, although he did not recognize them as the site of contact from presynaptic cells. He was a proponent of polarization of nerve cell function and his student, <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Lorente_de_N%C3%B3" title="Rafael Lorente de Nó">Rafael Lorente de Nó</a>, would continue this study of input-output systems into <a href="/wiki/Cable_theory" title="Cable theory">cable theory</a> and some of the earliest circuit analysis of neural structures.</p>
<p>He discovered a new type of cell, which was subsequently named after him: the <a href="/wiki/Interstitial_cell_of_Cajal" title="Interstitial cell of Cajal">interstitial cell of Cajal</a> (ICC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> This cell is found interleaved among neurons embedded within the <a href="/wiki/Smooth_muscle_tissue" title="Smooth muscle tissue">smooth muscles</a> lining the <a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract" title="Gastrointestinal tract">gut</a>, serving as the generator and pacemaker of the <a href="/wiki/Catastalsis" title="Catastalsis">slow waves of contraction</a> that move <a href="/wiki/Chyme" title="Chyme">material</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract" title="Gastrointestinal tract">gastrointestinal tract</a>, mediating neurotransmission from motor neurons to smooth muscle cells.</p>
<p>In his 1894 <a href="/wiki/Croonian_Lecture" title="Croonian Lecture">Croonian Lecture</a>, Ramón y Cajal suggested (in an extended metaphor) that cortical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyramidal_cells" title="Pyramidal cells">pyramidal cells</a> may become more elaborate with time, as a tree grows and extends its branches. He also devoted a considerable amount of time studying hypnosis (which he used to help his wife with labor) and parapsychological phenomena. A book he had written on these topics was lost during the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a>.</p>
<p>Ramón y Cajal's efforts to improve the state of scientific research and education in Spain were part of a broader preoccupation with <a href="/wiki/Regenerationism" title="Regenerationism">Regenerationism</a> among Spanish intellectuals.</p>

<p>Ramón y Cajal received many prizes, distinctions, and societal memberships during his scientific career, including <a href="/wiki/Honorary_degree" title="Honorary degree">honorary doctorates in medicine</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambridge_University" title="Cambridge University">Cambridge University</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg_University" title="Würzburg University">Würzburg University</a> and an honorary <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">doctorate in philosophy</a> from <a href="/wiki/Clark_University" title="Clark University">Clark University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nobel_lectures_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Nobel_lectures-2">[2]</a></sup> The most famous distinction he was awarded was the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a> in 1906, together with the Italian scientist <a href="/wiki/Camillo_Golgi" title="Camillo Golgi">Camillo Golgi</a> "in recognition of their work on the structure of the nervous system".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nobel_lectures_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Nobel_lectures-2">[2]</a></sup> This caused some controversy because Golgi, a staunch supporter of <a href="/wiki/Reticular_theory" title="Reticular theory">reticular theory</a>, disagreed with Ramón y Cajal in his view of the neuron doctrine.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/117413_Ramonycajal" title="117413 Ramonycajal">117413 Ramonycajal</a> is named in his honor. Spanish public television filmed a biopic series to commemorate his life.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publications">Publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santiago_Ram%C3%B3n_y_Cajal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He published more than 100 scientific works and articles in <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>. Among his most notable works were:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nobel_lectures_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Nobel_lectures-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>Rules and advices on scientific investigation</i></li>
<li><i>Histology</i></li>
<li><i>Degeneration and regeneration of the nervous system</i></li>
<li><i>Manual of normal histology and micrographic technique</i></li>
<li><i>Elements of histology</i></li>
<li><i>Manual of general <a href="/wiki/Pathology" title="Pathology">Anatomic Pathology</a></i></li>
<li><i>New ideas on the fine anatomy of the nerve centres</i></li>
<li><i>Textbook on the nervous system of Man and the <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a></i></li>
<li><i>The <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">retina</a> of vertebrates</i></li>
</ul>
<p>In 1905, under the pen name "Dr. Bacteria", he published five science-fiction stories called "Vacation Stories".</p>
