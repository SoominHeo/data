<p>The <b>Sanhedrin</b> (Hebrew: <span class="script-hebrew" dir="rtl" style="font-size: 115%; font-family: Alef, 'SBL BibLit', 'SBL Hebrew', 'David CLM', 'Frenk Ruehl CLM', 'Hadasim CLM', Cardo, Shofar, David, 'Ezra SIL', 'Ezra SIL SR', 'Noto Sans Hebrew', FreeSerif, 'Times New Roman', FreeSans, Arial;">סַנְהֶדְרִין</span>‎ <i>sanhedrîn</i>, <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: Συνέδριον,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Synedrion" title="Synedrion">synedrion</a></i>, "sitting together," hence "<a href="/wiki/Deliberative_assembly" title="Deliberative assembly">assembly</a>" or "council") was an assembly of twenty-three to seventy-one men appointed in every city in the <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Israel" title="Land of Israel">Land of Israel</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a> and the Israelites were commanded by God to establish courts of judges who were given full authority over the people of Israel, who were commanded by God to obey every word the judges instructed and every law they established. Judges in ancient Israel were the religious leaders and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rabbis" title="Rabbis">Teachers</a> of the nation of Israel. The <a href="/wiki/Mishnah" title="Mishnah">Mishnah</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> arrives at the number twenty-three based on an <a href="/wiki/Exegesis" title="Exegesis">exegetical</a> derivation: it must be possible for a "<a href="/wiki/Minyan" title="Minyan">community</a>" to vote for both conviction and exoneration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The minimum size of a "community" is 10 men<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> (10 vs 10). One more is required to achieve a majority (11 vs 10), but a simple majority cannot convict,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and so an additional judge is required (12 vs 10). Finally, a court should not have an even number of judges to prevent deadlocks; thus 23 (12 vs 10 and 1). This court dealt with only religious matters.</p>
<p>The <b>Great Sanhedrin</b> was made up of a <i><a href="/wiki/Nasi_(Hebrew_title)" title="Nasi (Hebrew title)">Nasi (President)</a></i>, who functioned as head or representing president, but was not a member of the court, an <i><a href="/wiki/Av_Beit_Din" title="Av Beit Din">Av Beit Din</a></i>, the chief of the court, and sixty-nine general members (<i>Mufla</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In the Second Temple period, the Great Sanhedrin met in the <a href="/wiki/Hall_of_Hewn_Stones" title="Hall of Hewn Stones">Hall of Hewn Stones</a> in the Temple in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>. The court convened every day except <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_Holidays" title="Jewish Holidays">festivals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shabbat" title="Shabbat">Shabbat</a>. The <i>Sanhedrin</i> is mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Gospel" title="Gospel">Gospels</a> in relation to the <a href="/wiki/Sanhedrin_trial_of_Jesus" title="Sanhedrin trial of Jesus">Sanhedrin trial of Jesus</a> and several times in the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">Acts of the Apostles</a>, including a Great Sanhedrin in chapter 5 where <a href="/wiki/Gamaliel" title="Gamaliel">Gamaliel</a> appeared, and in the stoning death of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Stephen" title="Saint Stephen">Stephen</a> the deacon in chapter 7.</p>
<p>After the destruction of the Second Temple and the failure of the Bar Kokhba Revolt, the Great Sanhedrin moved to Galilee, which became part of the Roman <a href="/wiki/Syria_Palaestina" title="Syria Palaestina">Syria Palaestina</a> province and was sometimes referred as <i>Galilean Patriarchate</i> or <i>Patriarchate of Palaestina</i>, being the governing legalistic body of Galilean Jewry until about 425 CE. In the late 3rd century, to avoid persecution, its authoritative decisions were issued under the name of <i>Beit HaMidrash</i>. Historically, the last binding decision of the Great Sanhedrin appeared in 358 CE, when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew_Calendar" title="Hebrew Calendar">Hebrew Calendar</a> was adopted. The <i>Great Sanhedrin</i> was dissolved after continued persecution by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire" title="Eastern Roman Empire">Eastern Roman Empire</a> and aspiring <a href="/wiki/Christendom" title="Christendom">Christendom</a>. Over the centuries, there have been attempts to revive the institution, such as the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Sanhedrin" title="Grand Sanhedrin">Grand Sanhedrin</a> convened by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">Napoleon Bonaparte</a> and modern attempts in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanhedrin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Sanhedrin">Early Sanhedrin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanhedrin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early Sanhedrin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Hasmonean court in the <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Israel" title="Land of Israel">Land of Israel</a>, presided over by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Jannaeus" title="Alexander Jannaeus">Alexander Jannaeus</a>, king of Judea until 76 BCE, followed by his wife, was called <i>Synhedrion</i> or <i>Sanhedrin.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wanderings_p._191_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wanderings_p._191-8">[8]</a></sup> The exact nature of this early Sanhedrin is not clear. It may have been a body of sages or priests, or a political, legislative and judicial institution. The first historical record of the body was during the administration of <a href="/wiki/Aulus_Gabinius" title="Aulus Gabinius">Aulus Gabinius</a>, who, according to Josephus, organized five <i>synedra</i> in 57 BCE as Roman administration was not concerned with religious affairs unless sedition was suspected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Only after the destruction of the Second Temple was the Sanhedrin made up only of sages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wanderings_p._191_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wanderings_p._191-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Herodian_and_early_Roman_rule">Herodian and early Roman rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanhedrin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Herodian and early Roman rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Second Temple period, the Great Sanhedrin met in the <a href="/wiki/Hall_of_Hewn_Stones" title="Hall of Hewn Stones">Hall of Hewn Stones</a> in the Temple in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>. The court convened every day except <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_Holidays" title="Jewish Holidays">festivals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shabbat" title="Shabbat">Shabbat</a>. The <i>Sanhedrin</i> is mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Gospel" title="Gospel">Gospels</a> in relation to the <a href="/wiki/Sanhedrin_trial_of_Jesus" title="Sanhedrin trial of Jesus">Sanhedrin trial of Jesus</a> and several times in the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">Acts of the Apostles</a>, including a Great Sanhedrin in chapter 5 where <a href="/wiki/Gamaliel" title="Gamaliel">Gamaliel</a> appeared, and in the stoning death of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Stephen" title="Saint Stephen">Stephen</a> the deacon in chapter 7.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="During_Jewish-Roman_Wars">During Jewish-Roman Wars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanhedrin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: During Jewish-Roman Wars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the destruction of the Second Temple in 70, the Sanhedrin was re-established in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yavneh" title="Yavneh">Yavneh</a> with reduced authority. The seat of the Patriarchate moved to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Usha_(ancient)" title="Usha (ancient)">Usha</a> under the presidency of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shimon_ben_Gamliel" title="Shimon ben Gamliel">Gamaliel II</a> in 80 CE. In 116 it moved back to Yavneh, and then again back to Usha.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_Bar_Kokhba_Revolt">After Bar Kokhba Revolt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanhedrin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: After Bar Kokhba Revolt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ancient_Galilee.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="516" data-file-width="393" height="289" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Ancient_Galilee.jpg/220px-Ancient_Galilee.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Ancient_Galilee.jpg/330px-Ancient_Galilee.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/66/Ancient_Galilee.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ancient_Galilee.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Galilee" title="Galilee">Galilee</a> in <a href="/wiki/Late_Antiquity" title="Late Antiquity">late antiquity</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Rabbinic texts indicate that following the <a href="/wiki/Bar_Kokhba_revolt" title="Bar Kokhba revolt">Bar Kokhba revolt</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Galilee" title="Galilee">Galilee</a> became the seat of rabbinic learning in the <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Israel" title="Land of Israel">Land of Israel</a>. This region was the location of the court of the Patriarch which was situated first at <a href="/wiki/Usha_(city)" title="Usha (city)">Usha</a>, then at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bet_Shearim" title="Bet Shearim">Bet Shearim</a>, later at <a href="/wiki/Sepphoris" title="Sepphoris">Sepphoris</a> and finally at <a href="/wiki/Tiberias" title="Tiberias">Tiberias</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LightstoneReligion2002_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-LightstoneReligion2002-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Great Sanhedrin moved in 140 to <a href="/wiki/Shefa-%27Amr" title="Shefa-'Amr">Shefaram</a> under the presidency of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shimon_ben_Gamliel_II" title="Shimon ben Gamliel II">Shimon ben Gamliel II</a>, and to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beit_Shearim" title="Beit Shearim">Beit Shearim</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tzippori" title="Tzippori">Sepphoris</a> in 163, under the presidency of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judah_I" title="Judah I">Judah I</a>. Finally, it moved to <a href="/wiki/Tiberias" title="Tiberias">Tiberias</a> in 193, under the presidency of <a href="/wiki/Gamaliel_III" title="Gamaliel III">Gamaliel III</a> (193–230) ben <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judah_haNasi" title="Judah haNasi">Judah haNasi</a>, where it became more of a consistory, but still retained, under the presidency of <a href="/wiki/Judah_II" title="Judah II">Judah II</a> (230–270), the power of excommunication.</p>
<p>During the presidency of <a href="/wiki/Gamaliel_IV" title="Gamaliel IV">Gamaliel IV</a> (270–290), due to Roman persecution, it dropped the name Sanhedrin; and its authoritative decisions were subsequently issued under the name of <i><a href="/wiki/Yeshiva" title="Yeshiva">Beth HaMidrash</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Sanhedrin demise begun in 313 with the <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Milan" title="Edict of Milan">Edict of Milan</a> regarding religious tolerance and marking the end of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persecution_of_Christians_in_the_Roman_Empire" title="Persecution of Christians in the Roman Empire">the persecutions against Christians</a>, thus seen as the first step towards Christianity becoming the official state religion of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chisholm1911_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chisholm1911-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 363, emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julian_the_Apostate" title="Julian the Apostate">Julian</a> ordered the Temple rebuilt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> A personal friend of his, <a href="/wiki/Ammianus_Marcellinus" title="Ammianus Marcellinus">Ammianus Marcellinus</a>, wrote this about the effort:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Julian thought to rebuild at an extravagant expense the proud Temple once at Jerusalem, and committed this task to <a href="/wiki/Alypius_of_Antioch" title="Alypius of Antioch">Alypius of Antioch</a>. Alypius set vigorously to work, and was seconded by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_governor" title="Roman governor">governor</a> of the province, when fearful balls of fire, breaking out near the foundations, continued their attacks, till the workmen, after repeated scorchings, could approach no more: and he gave up the attempt.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <small>Ammianus Marcellinus</small></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The failure to rebuild the Temple has been ascribed to the <a href="/wiki/Galilee_earthquake_of_363" title="Galilee earthquake of 363">Galilee earthquake of 363</a>, and to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jews</a>' ambivalence about the project. Sabotage is a possibility, as is an accidental fire. Divine intervention was the common view among Christian historians of the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solomon_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Solomon-13">[13]</a></sup> Julian's support of <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Jews</a>, coming after the hostility of many earlier Emperors, meant that Jews called him <i>Julian the <a href="/wiki/Names_of_the_Greeks#Hellene_comes_to_mean_.22pagan.22" title="Names of the Greeks">Hellene</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a reaction to Julian's pro-Jewish stance, <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">Theodosius I</a> forbade the Sanhedrin to assemble and declared <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semicha" title="Semicha">ordination</a> illegal. Capital punishment was prescribed for any Rabbi who received ordination and complete destruction of the town where the ordination occurred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_History_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-A_History-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, since the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">Hebrew calendar</a> was based on witnesses' testimony, that had become far too dangerous to collect, <a href="/wiki/Hillel_II" title="Hillel II">Hillel II</a> recommended change to a mathematically based calendar that was adopted at a clandestine, and maybe final, meeting in 358 CE. This marked the last universal decision made by that body.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamaliel_VI" title="Gamaliel VI">Gamaliel VI</a> (400–425) was the Sanhedrin's last president. With his death in 425, Theodosius II outlawed the title of <a href="/wiki/Nasi_(Hebrew_title)" title="Nasi (Hebrew title)">Nasi</a>, the last remains of the ancient Sanhedrin. An imperial decree of 426 diverted the patriarchs' tax (<i>post excessum patriarchorum</i>) into the imperial treasury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_History_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-A_History-15">[15]</a></sup> The exact reason for the abrogation of the patriarchate is not clear,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LangeGerber1997_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-LangeGerber1997-16">[16]</a></sup> though <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamaliel_VI" title="Gamaliel VI">Gamaliel VI</a>, the last holder of the office who had been for a time elevated by the emperor to the rank of <a href="/wiki/Prefect" title="Prefect">prefect</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chisholm1911_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chisholm1911-11">[11]</a></sup> may have fallen out with the imperial authorities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LangeGerber1997_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-LangeGerber1997-16">[16]</a></sup> Thereafter, Jews were gradually excluded from holding public office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edersheim1856_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Edersheim1856-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_and_Lesser_Sanhedrin">Great and Lesser Sanhedrin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanhedrin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Great and Lesser Sanhedrin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Talmud (tractate <a href="/wiki/Sanhedrin_(tractate)" title="Sanhedrin (tractate)">Sanhedrin</a>) identifies two classes of rabbinical courts called Sanhedrin, a Great Sanhedrin (בית דין הגדול) and a Lesser Sanhedrin (בית דין הקטן). Each city could have its own lesser Sanhedrin of 23 judges, but there could be only one Great Sanhedrin of 71, which among other roles acted as the Supreme Court, taking appeals from cases decided by lesser courts. The numbers of judges were predicated on eliminating the possibility of a tie and the last to cast their vote was the head of the court.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Function_and_procedures">Function and procedures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanhedrin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Function and procedures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Sanhedrin as a body claimed powers that lesser Jewish courts did not have. As such, they were the only ones who could try the king, extend the boundaries of the Temple and Jerusalem, and were the ones to whom all questions of law were finally put. Before 191 BCE the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kohen_Gadol" title="Kohen Gadol">High Priest</a> acted as the <i>ex officio</i> head of the Sanhedrin,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> but in 191 BCE, when the Sanhedrin lost confidence in the High Priest, the office of <a href="/wiki/Nasi_(Hebrew_title)" title="Nasi (Hebrew title)">Nasi</a> was created. After the time of <a href="/wiki/Hillel_the_Elder" title="Hillel the Elder">Hillel the Elder</a> (late 1st century BCE and early 1st century CE), the Nasi was almost invariably a descendant of Hillel. The second highest-ranking member of the Sanhedrin was called the <a href="/wiki/Av_Beit_Din" title="Av Beit Din">Av Beit Din</a>, or "Head of the Court" (literally, Av Beit Din = "father of the house of judgment"), who presided over the Sanhedrin when it sat as a criminal court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>During Second Temple era, the Sanhedrin met in a building known as the <a href="/wiki/Hall_of_Hewn_Stones" title="Hall of Hewn Stones">Hall of Hewn Stones</a> (<i>Lishkat ha-Gazit</i>), which has been placed by the Talmud and many scholars as built into the north wall of the <a href="/wiki/Temple_Mount" title="Temple Mount">Temple Mount</a>, half inside the sanctuary and half outside, with doors providing access both to the Temple and to the outside. The name presumably arises to distinguish it from the buildings in the Temple complex used for ritual purposes, which had to be constructed of stones unhewn by any <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> implements.</p>
<p>In some cases, it was only necessary for a 23-member panel (functioning as a Lesser Sanhedrin) to convene. In general, the full panel of 71 judges was only convened on matters of national significance (e.g., a declaration of war) or in the event that the 23-member panel could not reach a conclusive verdict.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the end of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Temple" title="Second Temple">Second Temple</a> period, the Sanhedrin reached its pinnacle of importance, legislating all aspects of Jewish religious and political life within the parameters laid down by Biblical and Rabbinic tradition.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary_of_Patriarchal_powers">Summary of Patriarchal powers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanhedrin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Summary of Patriarchal powers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following is a summary of the powers and responsibilities of the Patriarchate from the onset of the third century, based on rabbinic sources as portrayed by L.I. Levine:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LightstoneReligion2002p.189_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-LightstoneReligion2002p.189-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Representative to Imperial authorities;</li>
<li>Focus of leadership in the Jewish community:
<ol>
<li>Receiving daily visits from prominent families;</li>
<li>Declaration of public fast days;</li>
<li>Initiating or abrogating the ban (<i><a href="/wiki/Herem_(war_or_property)" title="Herem (war or property)">herem</a></i>);</li>
</ol>
</li>
<li>Appointment of judges to Jewish courts in the Land of Israel;</li>
<li>Regulation of the calendar;</li>
<li>Issuing enactments and decrees with respect to the applicability or release from legal requirements, e.g.:
<ol>
<li>Use of <a href="/wiki/Shmita" title="Shmita">sabbatical year</a> produce and applicability of sabbatical year injunctions;</li>
<li>Repurchase or redemption of formerly Jewish land from gentile owners;</li>
<li>Status of Hellenistic cities of the Land of Israel re: purity, tithing, sabbatical year;</li>
<li>Exemptions from tithing;</li>
<li>Conditions in divorce documents;</li>
<li>Use of oil produced by gentiles;</li>
</ol>
</li>
<li>Dispatching emissaries to diaspora communities;</li>
<li>Taxation: both the power to tax and the authority to rule/intervene on the disposition of taxes raised for local purposes by local councils.</li>
</ol>
<p>Up to the middle of the fourth century, the Patriarchate retained the prerogative of determining the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">Hebrew calendar</a> and guarded the intricacies of the calculation process in an effort to subdue interference from the Babylonian community. Due to Christian persecution, <a href="/wiki/Hillel_II" title="Hillel II">Hillel II</a> was obliged to fix the calendar in permanent form in 359 CE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inc2003_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-inc2003-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TausGarber2008_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-TausGarber2008-22">[22]</a></sup> This institution symbolized the passing of authority from the Patriarchate to the <a href="/wiki/Talmudic_Academies_in_Babylonia" title="Talmudic Academies in Babylonia">Babylonian Academies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Landman1941_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Landman1941-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeological_findings">Archaeological findings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanhedrin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Archaeological findings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2004, excavations in Tiberias conducted by the <a href="/wiki/Israel_Antiquities_Authority" title="Israel Antiquities Authority">Israel Antiquities Authority</a> uncovered a structure dating to the 3rd century CE that may have been the seat of the Sanhedrin when it convened in that city. At the time it was called Beit Hava'ad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Presidents">Presidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanhedrin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Presidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nasi_(Hebrew_title)" title="Nasi (Hebrew title)">Nasi (Hebrew title)</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Tannaim" title="Tannaim">Tannaim</a></div>
<p>Before 191 BCE the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kohen_Gadol" title="Kohen Gadol">High Priest</a> acted as the <i>ex officio</i> head of the Sanhedrin,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> but in 191 BCE, when the Sanhedrin lost confidence in the High Priest, the office of <a href="/wiki/Nasi_(Hebrew_title)" title="Nasi (Hebrew title)">Nasi</a> was created. The Sanhedrin was headed by the chief scholars of the great <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Talmudic_Academies_in_the_Land_of_Israel" title="Talmudic Academies in the Land of Israel">Talmudic Academies in the Land of Israel</a>, and with the decline of the Sanhedrin, their spiritual and legal authority was generally accepted, the institution itself being supported by voluntary contributions by Jews throughout the ancient world. Being a member of the house of <a href="/wiki/Hillel_the_Elder" title="Hillel the Elder">Hillel</a> and thus a descendant of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_David" title="King David">King David</a>, the Patriarch, known in Hebrew as the <i><a href="/wiki/Nasi_(Hebrew_title)" title="Nasi (Hebrew title)">Nasi</a></i> (prince), enjoyed almost royal authority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chisholm1911_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chisholm1911-11">[11]</a></sup> Their functions were political rather than religious, though their influence was not limited to the secular realm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chisholm1911_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Chisholm1911-11">[11]</a></sup> The Patriarchate attained its zenith under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judah_ha-Nasi" title="Judah ha-Nasi">Judah ha-Nasi</a> who compiled the <a href="/wiki/Mishnah" title="Mishnah">Mishnah</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chisholm1911_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Chisholm1911-11">[11]</a></sup> a compendium of views from Judean thought leaders of Judaism other than the Torah.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Revival_attempts">Revival attempts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanhedrin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Revival attempts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semicha#Attempts_to_revive_classical_semicha" title="Semicha">Semicha § Attempts to revive classical semicha</a></div>
<p>The Sanhedrin is traditionally viewed as the last institution that commanded universal Jewish authority among the Jewish people in the long chain of tradition from <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a> until the present day. Since its dissolution in 358 CE by imperial decree, there have been several attempts to re-establish this body either as a self-governing body, or as a puppet of a sovereign government.</p>
<p>There are records of what may have been of attempts to reform the Sanhedrin in Arabia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conquest_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-conquest-25">[25]</a></sup> in Jerusalem under the Caliph 'Umar,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conquest_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-conquest-25">[25]</a></sup> and in Babylon (Iraq),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> but none of these attempts were given any attention by Rabbinic authorities and little information is available about them.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Napoleon_Bonaparte.27s_.22Grand_Sanhedrin.22">Napoleon Bonaparte's "Grand Sanhedrin"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanhedrin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Napoleon Bonaparte's &quot;Grand Sanhedrin&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte%27s_%22Grand_Sanhedrin%22" title="Napoleon Bonaparte's &quot;Grand Sanhedrin&quot;">Napoleon Bonaparte's "Grand Sanhedrin"</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Frenchsanhedrin2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="166" data-file-width="329" height="111" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Frenchsanhedrin2.jpg/220px-Frenchsanhedrin2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fd/Frenchsanhedrin2.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Frenchsanhedrin2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Medallion struck in honor of the "Grand Sanhedrin" convened by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">Emperor Napoleon I of France</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The "Grand Sanhedrin" was a Jewish high court convened by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_I" title="Napoleon I">Napoleon I</a> to give legal sanction to the principles expressed by the <a href="/wiki/Assembly_of_Notables" title="Assembly of Notables">Assembly of Notables</a> in answer to the twelve questions submitted to it by the government (see <i>Jew. Encyc. v. 468, s.v. France</i>).</p>
<p>On October 6, 1806, the Assembly of Notables issued a proclamation to all the Jewish communities of Europe, inviting them to send delegates to the Sanhedrin, to convene on October 20. This proclamation, written in Hebrew, French, German, and Italian, speaks in extravagant terms of the importance of this revived institution and of the greatness of its imperial protector. While the action of Napoleon aroused in many Jews of Germany the hope that, influenced by it, their governments also would grant them the rights of citizenship, others looked upon it as a political contrivance. When in the war against Prussia (1806–07) the emperor invaded Poland and the Jews rendered great services to his army, he remarked, laughing, "The sanhedrin is at least useful to me."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> David Friedländer and his friends in Berlin described it as a spectacle that Napoleon offered to the <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Parisians</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_attempts_in_Israel">Modern attempts in Israel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanhedrin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Modern attempts in Israel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Modern_attempts_to_revive_the_Sanhedrin" title="Modern attempts to revive the Sanhedrin">Modern attempts to revive the Sanhedrin</a></div>
<p>Since the dissolution of the Sanhedrin in 358 CE,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> there has been no universally recognized authority within <a href="/wiki/Halakha" title="Halakha">Halakha</a>. <a href="/wiki/Maimonides" title="Maimonides">Maimonides</a> (1135–1204) was one of the greatest scholars of the Middle Ages, and is arguably one of the most widely accepted scholars among the Jewish people since the closing of the Talmud in 500. Influenced by the rationalist school of thought and generally showing a preference for a natural (as opposed to miraculous) redemption for the Jewish people, <a href="/wiki/Maimonides" title="Maimonides">Maimonides</a> proposed a rationalist solution for achieving the goal of re-establishing the highest court in Jewish tradition and reinvesting it with the same authority it had in former years. There have been several attempts to implement <a href="/wiki/Maimonides" title="Maimonides">Maimonides</a>' recommendations, the latest being in modern times.</p>
<p>There have been rabbinical attempts to renew <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semicha" title="Semicha">Semicha</a> and re-establish a Sanhedrin by Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Berab" title="Jacob Berab">Jacob Berab</a> in 1538, Rabbi <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yisroel_Shklover" title="Yisroel Shklover">Yisroel Shklover</a> in 1830, Rabbi Aharon Mendel haCohen in 1901, Rabbi Zvi Kovsker in 1940 and Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Yehuda_Leib_Maimon" title="Yehuda Leib Maimon">Yehuda Leib Maimon</a> in 1949.</p>
<p>In October 2004 (Tishrei 5765), a group of <a href="/wiki/Rabbi" title="Rabbi">rabbis</a> representing varied Orthodox communities in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> undertook a ceremony in <a href="/wiki/Tiberias" title="Tiberias">Tiberias</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> where the original Sanhedrin was disbanded, in which it claimed to re-establish the body according to the proposal of <a href="/wiki/Maimonides" title="Maimonides">Maimonides</a> and the Jewish legal rulings of Rabbi <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yosef_Karo" title="Yosef Karo">Yosef Karo</a>. The controversial attempt has been subject to debate within different Jewish communities.</p>
