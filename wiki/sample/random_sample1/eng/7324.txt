<p><b>Iodine oxides</b> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_compounds" title="Chemical compounds">chemical compounds</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a>. The chemistry of these compound is complicated with only a few having been well characterized. Many have been detected in the atmosphere and are believed to be particularly important at in the marine boundary layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>Diiodine monoxide has largely been the subject of theoretical study,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> but there is some evidence that it may be prepared in a similar manner to <a href="/wiki/Dichlorine_monoxide" title="Dichlorine monoxide">dichlorine monoxide</a>, via a reaction between <a href="/wiki/Mercury(II)_oxide" title="Mercury(II) oxide">HgO</a> and I<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The compound appears to be highly unstable but can react with alkenes to give halogenated products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_radical" title="Free radical">Radical</a> iodine oxide (IO), iodine dioxide (IO<sub>2</sub>) and diiodine tetroxide ((IO<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub>) all possess significant and interconnected atmospheric chemistry. They are formed, in very small quantities, at the marine boundary layer by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photooxidation" title="Photooxidation">photooxidation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Diiodomethane" title="Diiodomethane">diiodomethane</a>, which is produced by macroalga such as <a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweed</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Despite the small quantities produced (typically below <a href="/wiki/Parts-per_notation" title="Parts-per notation">ppt</a>) they are thought to be powerful <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depletion</a> agents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diiodine_pentoxide" title="Diiodine pentoxide">Diiodine pentoxide</a> (I<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>) is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acid_anhydride" title="Acid anhydride">anhydride</a> of <a href="/wiki/Iodic_acid" title="Iodic acid">iodic acid</a> and the only stable anhydride of iodine.</p>
<p>Tetraiodine nonoxide (I<sub>4</sub>O<sub>9</sub>) has been prepared by the gas-phase reaction of I<sub>2</sub> with O<sub>3</sub> but has not been extensively studied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
