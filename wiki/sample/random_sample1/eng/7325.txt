<p><b>Silver(I) oxide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> Ag<sub>2</sub>O. It is a fine black or dark brown powder that is used to prepare other <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> compounds.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silver_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Silver(I)_oxide.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="344" data-file-width="499" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Silver%28I%29_oxide.JPG/160px-Silver%28I%29_oxide.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Silver%28I%29_oxide.JPG/240px-Silver%28I%29_oxide.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Silver%28I%29_oxide.JPG/320px-Silver%28I%29_oxide.JPG 2x" width="160"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Silver(I)_oxide.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Silver(I) oxide produced by reacting <a href="/wiki/Lithium_hydroxide" title="Lithium hydroxide">lithium hydroxide</a> with a very dilute silver nitrate solution</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Silver oxide can be prepared by combining aqueous solutions of <a href="/wiki/Silver_nitrate" title="Silver nitrate">silver nitrate</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Alkali_hydroxide" title="Alkali hydroxide">alkali hydroxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This reaction does not afford appreciable amounts of silver hydroxide due to the favorable energetics for the following reaction:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>2 AgOH â Ag<sub>2</sub>O + H<sub>2</sub>O (<a href="/wiki/Equilibrium_constant" title="Equilibrium constant">p<i>K</i></a> = 2.875<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>)</dd>
</dl>
<p>US patent 20050050990 describes the preparation of Ag<sub>2</sub>O with properties suitable for use as a fine grained conductive paste filler.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_properties">Structure and properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silver_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure and properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ag<sub>2</sub>O features linear, two-coordinate Ag centers linked by tetrahedral oxides. It is isostructural with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copper_(I)_oxide" title="Copper (I) oxide">Cu<sub>2</sub>O</a>. It "dissolves" in solvents that degrade it. It is slightly soluble in water due to the formation of the ion Ag(OH)<sub>2</sub><sup>â</sup> and possibly related hydrolysis products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c_w_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-c_w-11">[11]</a></sup> It dissolves in <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> solution to give soluble derivatives.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A slurry of Ag<sub>2</sub>O is readily attacked by <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a>:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Ag<sub>2</sub>O + 2 HX â 2 AgX + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
</dl>
<p>where HX = <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">HF</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">HCl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrobromic_acid" title="Hydrobromic acid">HBr</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Hydroiodic_acid" title="Hydroiodic acid">HI</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trifluoroacetic_acid" title="Trifluoroacetic acid">HO<sub>2</sub>CCF<sub>3</sub></a>. It will also react with solutions of alkali chlorides to precipitate <a href="/wiki/Silver_chloride" title="Silver chloride">silver chloride</a>, leaving a solution of the corresponding alkali hydroxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c_w_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-c_w-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pauling_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-pauling-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Like many silver compounds, silver oxide is photosensitive. It also decomposes at temperatures above 280Â Â°C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merck_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-merck-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silver_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This oxide is used in some silver-oxide batteries, as is the silver(I,III)oxide, Ag<sub>4</sub>O<sub>4</sub>. In organic chemistry, silver oxide is used as a mild oxidizing agent. For example, it oxidizes aldehydes to carboxylic acids. Such reactions often work best when the silver oxide is prepared <i>in situ</i> from silver nitrate and alkali hydroxide.</p>
