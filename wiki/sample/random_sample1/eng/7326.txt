<p><b>Potassium oxide</b> (<a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">K<sub>2</sub></a><a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">O</a>) is an ionic <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compound</a> of <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>. This pale yellow solid, the simplest oxide of potassium, is a rarely encountered, highly reactive compound. Some materials of commerce, such as fertilizers and cements, are assayed assuming the percent composition that would be equivalent to the chemical compoud mixture K<sub>2</sub>O.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Potassium oxide is produced from the reaction of oxygen and potassium; this reaction affords potassium oxide, K<sub>2</sub>O. Treatment of the peroxide with potassium produces the oxide:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>K<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> + 2 K → 2 K<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
</dl>
<p>Alternatively and more conveniently, K<sub>2</sub>O is synthesized by heating <a href="/wiki/Potassium_nitrate" title="Potassium nitrate">potassium nitrate</a> with metallic potassium:</p>
<dl>
<dd>2 KNO<sub>3</sub> + 10 K → 6 K<sub>2</sub>O + N<sub>2</sub></dd>
</dl>
<p>Other possibility is to heat potassium peroxide at 500 °C which decomposes at that temperature giving pure potassium oxide and oxygen.</p>
<dl>
<dd>2 K<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<mo>=</mo>
</mstyle>
</mrow>

</math></span><img alt="=" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/505a4ceef454c69dffd23792c84b90f488543743" style="vertical-align: 0.286ex; margin-bottom: -0.457ex; width:1.819ex; height:1.343ex;"/></span> 2 K<sub>2</sub>O + O<sub>2</sub></dd>
</dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">Potassium hydroxide</a> cannot be further dehydrated to the oxide but it can react with molten potassium to produce it, releasing <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> as a byproduct.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_and_reactions">Properties and reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Properties and reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>K<sub>2</sub>O crystallises in the antifluorite structure. In this motif the positions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anions</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cations</a> are reversed relative to their positions in <a href="/wiki/Calcium_fluoride" title="Calcium fluoride">CaF<sub>2</sub></a>, with potassium ions coordinated to 4 oxide ions and oxide ions coordinated to 8 potassium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> K<sub>2</sub>O is a basic oxide and reacts with water violently to produce the caustic <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a>. It is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deliquescent" title="Deliquescent">deliquescent</a> and will absorb water from the atmosphere, initiating this vigorous reaction.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Term_use_in_industry">Term use in industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Term use in industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The chemical formula K<sub>2</sub>O (or simply 'K') is used in several industrial contexts: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N-P-K" title="N-P-K">N-P-K</a> numbers for <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Cement_chemist_notation" title="Cement chemist notation">cement formulas</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Glass_batch_calculation" title="Glass batch calculation">glassmaking formulas</a>. Potassium oxide is often not used directly in these products, but the amount of potassium is reported in terms of the K<sub>2</sub>O equivalent for whichever type of <a href="/wiki/Potash" title="Potash">potash</a> was used, such as <a href="/wiki/Potassium_carbonate" title="Potassium carbonate">potassium carbonate</a>. For example, potassium oxide is about 83% <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> by weight, while <a href="/wiki/Potassium_chloride" title="Potassium chloride">potassium chloride</a> is only 52%. <a href="/wiki/Potassium_chloride" title="Potassium chloride">Potassium chloride</a> provides less <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> than an equal amount of potassium oxide. Thus, if a fertilizer is 30% <a href="/wiki/Potassium_chloride" title="Potassium chloride">potassium chloride</a> by weight, its standard <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> rating, based on potassium oxide, would be only 18.8%.</p>
