<p>The <b>Salesians of Don Bosco</b> (or the <b>Salesian Society</b>, officially named the <b>Society of St.¬†Francis de Sales</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Roman Catholic</a> <a href="/wiki/Religious_institute" title="Religious institute">religious institute</a> founded in the late nineteenth century by Italian priest Saint <a href="/wiki/John_Bosco" title="John Bosco">John Bosco</a> to help poor children during the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>.</p>
<p>The Salesians' charter describes the society's mission as "the Christian perfection of its associates obtained by the exercise of spiritual and corporal works of charity towards the young, especially the poor, and the education of boys to the priesthood".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The institute is named after <a href="/wiki/Francis_de_Sales" title="Francis de Sales">Francis de Sales</a>, an early-modern bishop from <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salesians_of_Don_Bosco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1845 Don <a href="/wiki/John_Bosco" title="John Bosco">John Bosco</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Don_(honorific)#Italy" title="Don (honorific)">Don</a>" being a traditional Italian honorific for a priest) opened a night school for boys in <a href="/wiki/Valdocco_(Turin)" title="Valdocco (Turin)">Valdocco</a>, now part of the municipality of <a href="/wiki/Turin" title="Turin">Turin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. In the following years, he opened several more schools, and in 1857 drew up a set of rules for his helpers, which became the Rule of the Society of St. <a href="/wiki/Francis_de_Sales" title="Francis de Sales">Francis de Sales</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_IX" title="Pope Pius IX">Pope Pius IX</a> approved definitively in 1873. The Society grew rapidly, with houses established in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> within a year of the Society's formal recognition. Its official print organ, the <i>Salesian Bulletin,</i> was first published in 1877.</p>
<p>Over the next decade the Salesians expanded into <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, and several countries in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. The death of Don Bosco in 1888 did not slow the Society's growth. By 1911 the Salesians were established throughout the world, including <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.</p>
<p>The Society continues to operate worldwide; in 2000, it counted more than 17,000 members in 2,711 houses. It is the third-largest missionary organization in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Salesian_Coat_of_Arms">Salesian Coat of Arms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salesians_of_Don_Bosco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Salesian Coat of Arms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Salesian Coat of Arms was designed by Professor Boidi. It was published for the first time in a circular letter of Don Bosco on 8 December 1885. It consist of a shining star, the large anchor, and the heart on fire to symbolize the <a href="/wiki/Theological_virtues" title="Theological virtues">theological virtues</a> of Faith, Hope and Charity. The figure of St. Francis de Sales recalls the Patron of the Society. The small wood in the lower part refers to the Founder of the society; the high mountains signify the heights of perfection towards which members strive; the interwoven palm and laurel that enfold the shield on either side are emblematic of the prize reserved for a virtuous and sacrificial life. The motto <i>Da mihi animas, caetera tolle</i> is featured at the bottom.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Logo_of_the_society">Logo of the society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salesians_of_Don_Bosco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Logo of the society">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The logo of the Salesians of Don Bosco is made up of two superimposed images: in the background a stylised ‚ÄúS‚Äù (Salesians) in white is formed within a sphere like a globe, marked to the right and left by two cuttings between the hills/dunes. The second image is in the centre of the globe bridging the ‚ÄúS‚Äù road. This is an arrow pointing upwards, resting on three perpendicular legs on top of which are three closed circles, making a stylised image of three people: the first of these in the middle and taller than the others is the point of the arrow, and the other two beside it appear as it were to be embraced by the central figure. The three stylised figures with the arrow pointing upwards can also be viewed as a simple dwelling with a sloping roof and with pillars holding it up (the bodies of the three people).</p>
<p>The logo contains elements from German and Brazilian provinces. It is designed with the central theme "Don Bosco and the Salesians walking with the young through the world."</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Process_of_logo_selection">Process of logo selection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salesians_of_Don_Bosco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Process of logo selection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The new logo is the result of combining two logos already established for years in some parts of the Congregation: the German logo and the Brazilian logo.</p>
<p>The idea of combining the two came out of suggestions from an enquiry about the new logo conducted throughout the Congregation and from contributions by the General Council.</p>
<p>The combination, besides profiting from the mutual enrichment of the elements, is intended to be an expression of communion and of intercultural dialogue.</p>
<p>The artistic work of combining the two was carried out by the designer Fabrizio Emigli, from the Litos Company, in Rome.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salesians_of_Don_Bosco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%C3%81ngel_Fern%C3%A1ndez_Artime.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1838" data-file-width="1332" height="304" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/%C3%81ngel_Fern%C3%A1ndez_Artime.jpg/220px-%C3%81ngel_Fern%C3%A1ndez_Artime.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/%C3%81ngel_Fern%C3%A1ndez_Artime.jpg/330px-%C3%81ngel_Fern%C3%A1ndez_Artime.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/%C3%81ngel_Fern%C3%A1ndez_Artime.jpg/440px-%C3%81ngel_Fern%C3%A1ndez_Artime.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The Salesians of Don Bosco are headed by the <i>Rector Major</i> and the society's general council; each of the ninety-four geographical provinces is headed by a <i>Provincial</i>. These officers serve six-year terms; the Rector Major and the members of the general council are elected by the Chapter General, which meets every six years or upon the death of the Rector Major. Each local Salesian community is headed by a superior, called a <i>Rector</i> (or more commonly, "Director"), who is appointed to a three-year term and can be renewed for a second three-year term.</p>
<p>The current Rector Major of the Salesians of Don Bosco is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Very_Reverend" title="Very Reverend">Very Reverend</a> Father <a href="/wiki/%C3%81ngel_Fern%C3%A1ndez_Artime" title="√Ångel Fern√°ndez Artime">√Ångel Fern√°ndez Artime</a>. Father Artime, a Spaniard who most recently served as provincial in southern Argentina, was elected on 25 March 2014. Fr. Fernandez was born in Gozon-Luanco in Spain in 1960, and made his first profession in 1978. He took perpetual profession in 1984, and was ordained a priest in 1987. He holds a doctorate in <a href="/wiki/Pastoral_theology" title="Pastoral theology">pastoral theology</a>, and a licentiate in <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pedagogy" title="Pedagogy">pedagogy</a>.</p>
<p>He has served the congregation as youth ministry delegate, director of the school at <a href="/wiki/Ourense" title="Ourense">Ourense</a>. He was a member of the provincial council of Leon, later serving as vice provincial, and then provincial from 2000 to 2006. In 2009, he was appointed Salesian provincial for southern <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, a post he held until his election as rector major. It was in this capacity that he came to know, and worked with, Cardinal Jorge Bergoglio (now <a href="/wiki/Pope_Francis" title="Pope Francis">Pope Francis</a>), the archbishop of <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>. Prior to his election as Rector Major, Artime was appointed in December 2013 as head of the new <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a> province. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salesians_of_Don_Bosco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Salesian communities primarily operate shelters for homeless or at-risk youths; schools; technical, vocational, and language instruction centers for youths and adults; and boys' clubs and community centers. In some areas they run parish churches. Salesians are also active in publishing and other public communication activities, as well as <a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">mission</a> work, especially in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> - in the <a href="/wiki/Yakutsk" title="Yakutsk">Yakutsk</a> area), <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> (<a href="/wiki/Yanomami" title="Yanomami">Yanomami</a>). The <i>Salesian Bulletin</i> is now published in fifty-two editions, in thirty languages.</p>
<p>In the 1990s Salesians launched new works in the area of tertiary education, and today have a network of over 58 colleges and universities. The official university of the Salesian Society is the <a href="/wiki/Salesian_Pontifical_University" title="Salesian Pontifical University">Salesian Pontifical University</a> in Rome.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Women.27s_institute">Women's institute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salesians_of_Don_Bosco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Women's institute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The women's institute is known as the <a href="/wiki/Salesian_Sisters_of_Don_Bosco" title="Salesian Sisters of Don Bosco">Salesian Sisters of Don Bosco</a> or, more officially, the Daughters of Mary Help of Christians (FMA).</p>
<p>Visitationist sisters, members of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Visitation_of_Holy_Mary" title="Order of the Visitation of Holy Mary">Order of the Visitation of Holy Mary</a>, are also sometimes called Salesian Sisters, in honor of one of their founders, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Francis_de_Sales" title="Saint Francis de Sales">Saint Francis de Sales</a>. However, the two societies are not the same and their membership does not overlap.</p>
