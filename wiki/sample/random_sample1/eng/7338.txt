<p>The <b>Saluki</b>, also known as <b>Persian Greyhound</b>, is a dog originally bred in the <a href="/wiki/Fertile_Crescent" title="Fertile Crescent">Fertile Crescent</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freedman2013_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-freedman2013-1">[1]</a></sup> The Saluki is classed as a <a href="/wiki/Sighthound" title="Sighthound">sighthound</a> and is typically deep-chested and long-legged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-behavior_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-behavior-2">[2]</a></sup> Salukis are "sight" hounds—hunting by sight—and run their quarry down to kill or retrieve it. Historically, Salukis were used for hunting by nomadic tribes. Typical quarry included the gazelle, hare, fox and <a href="/wiki/Jackal" title="Jackal">jackal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-behavior_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-behavior-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saluki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The two ancient <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_language" title="Sumerian language">Sumerian</a> words "Salu-ki" translate into "plunge-earth."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, there is no evidence the breed was referred to by the Sumerians with this name nor what "plunge to earth" might have meant in reference to the Saluki. The name of the breed first appeared in writing in <a href="/wiki/Pre-Islamic_Arabia" title="Pre-Islamic Arabia">pre-Islamic</a> <a href="/wiki/Arabic_poetry" title="Arabic poetry">Arabic poetry</a> and may have derived from "Saluqiyyah," the Arabic form of <a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">Seleucia</a>. However, this is disputed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royalhunt_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-royalhunt-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:56</sup> British diplomat <a href="/wiki/Terence_Clark" title="Terence Clark">Sir Terence Clark</a> wrote that the Arabic word "Saluqi" describes a person or thing from a place named Saluq.</p>
<p>Arab tradition states that Saluq was an ancient town in <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a> not far from modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ta%27izz" title="Ta'izz">Ta'izz</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Arabs" title="Arabs">Arabs</a> associate this town with the origin of the breed. However, the word "saluqi" might have been derived from reference to several other places: Saluq in <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a>, and three towns called Saluqiyah. One has become modern <a href="/wiki/Silifke" title="Silifke">Silifke</a> (<a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>), another is near <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a> (modern Antakya, Turkey), and third is located near <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a> (<a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>). Baghdad eclipsed Ctesiphon, the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Empire" title="Persian Empire">Persian Empire</a>, which was located some 30 km (19 mi) to the southeast. Ctesiphon itself had replaced and absorbed Seleucia, the first capital of the <a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">Seleucid Empire</a> (312 BC - 65 AD) and the adjective saluqi may have been derived by the Arabs of the Arabian Peninsula from the similar-sounding word for Seleucid used in the <a href="/wiki/Aramaic_language" title="Aramaic language">Aramaic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a> languages spoken there by the various peoples of that part of <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, but there is no irrefutable evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clark.27s_website_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clark.27s_website-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saluki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Salukis are "sight" hounds—hunting by sight—and run their quarry down to kill or retrieve it. The normal size range for the breed is 23–28 inches (58–71 cm) high at the <a href="/wiki/Withers" title="Withers">withers</a> and 40–60 pounds (18–27 kg) in weight. Females Salukis are slightly smaller than males.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palika342_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-palika342-6">[6]</a></sup> The head is long and narrow with large eyes and drop ears.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palika342_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-palika342-6">[6]</a></sup> The tail of the breed is long and curved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palika342_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-palika342-6">[6]</a></sup> It has the typical deep-chested, long-legged body of sighthounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-behavior_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-behavior-2">[2]</a></sup> The coat comes in a variety of colors including white, cream, fawn, red, grizzle/tan, black/tan, and tri-color (white, black and tan).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>The overall appearance of the Saluki is one of grace and symmetry. Two coat types—smooth and feathered—are evident in the breed's gene pool. The latter variety has light feathering on the back of the legs and thighs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-behavior_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-behavior-2">[2]</a></sup> The fur on both types is silky<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toptotail_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-toptotail-8">[8]</a></sup> and is low-shedding when compared to other breeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meetsalukis_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-meetsalukis-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Swiftness_and_physical_capacity">Swiftness and physical capacity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saluki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Swiftness and physical capacity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the <a href="/wiki/Greyhound" title="Greyhound">Greyhound</a> is credited as being the fastest dog breed up to distances of around 800 metres (2,600 ft), both the Saluki and <a href="/wiki/Whippet" title="Whippet">Whippet</a> breeds are thought to be faster over longer distances. In 1996, The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinness_Book_of_Records" title="Guinness Book of Records">Guinness Book of Records</a> listed a Saluki as being the fastest dog, capable of reaching a speed of 68.8 kilometres (42.8 mi) per hour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Due to its heavily padded feet being able to absorb the impact on its body, the Saluki has remarkable stamina when running.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toptotail_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-toptotail-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historically, Salukis were used for hunting by nomadic tribes. Typical quarry included the gazelle, hare, fox and <a href="/wiki/Jackal" title="Jackal">jackal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-behavior_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-behavior-2">[2]</a></sup> While hunting hares, Bedouin hunters would sometimes ride close to their quarry on a <a href="/wiki/Camel" title="Camel">camel</a> holding a Saluki, which would be thrown towards the prey while at speed to give the dog a running start.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Gazelle hunters also used hawks to gouge out the eyes of the prey so that a Saluki could then bring down the blinded animal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperament">Temperament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saluki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Temperament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The modern Saluki has retained qualities of hunting hounds and may seem reserved to strangers. The often independent and aloof breed may be difficult to train. Training methods should always be gentle and patient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whatdog_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-whatdog-13">[13]</a></sup> Salukis may bore easily and are not an ideal breed to leave unattended for long periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The Saluki does not typically enjoy rough games or activities such as retrieving balls. Early socialization will help prevent timidity and shyness in later life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whatdog_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-whatdog-13">[13]</a></sup> Given its hunting instincts, the dog is prone to chasing moving objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meetsalukis_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-meetsalukis-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saluki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chart_perski_0002.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1691" data-file-width="1900" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Chart_perski_0002.jpg/170px-Chart_perski_0002.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Chart_perski_0002.jpg/255px-Chart_perski_0002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Chart_perski_0002.jpg/340px-Chart_perski_0002.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chart_perski_0002.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A close-up Saluki</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hip_dysplasia_(canine)" title="Hip dysplasia (canine)">Hip dysplasia</a> is uncommon in Salukis, with the breed ranking joint lowest in a survey by the <a href="/wiki/British_Veterinary_Association" title="British Veterinary Association">British Veterinary Association</a> in 2003. The breed scored an average of 5 points, with a score of 0 being low, while 106 is high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In a 2006 breed specific survey conducted by The Kennel Club and the British Small Animal Veterinary Association Scientific Committee, responses highlighted several health issues. The primary cause of death identified was that of cancer, being responsible for 35.6% of deaths, with the most common forms being that of <a href="/wiki/Liver_cancer_in_cats_and_dogs" title="Liver cancer in cats and dogs">liver cancer</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lymphoma_in_animals" title="Lymphoma in animals">lymphoma</a>. The secondary cause of death was cardiac related, with forms such as heart failure, or unspecified heart defects. Old age is listed as the third most frequent cause of death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kennelsurvey_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-kennelsurvey-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cardiomyopathy" title="Cardiomyopathy">Cardiomyopathy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heart_murmur" title="Heart murmur">heart murmur</a> and other cardiac issues were present in 17.2% of responses while <a href="/wiki/Dermatology" title="Dermatology">dermatolic</a> conditions such as <a href="/wiki/Dermatitis" title="Dermatitis">dermatitis</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alopecia" title="Alopecia">alopecia</a> were reported by 10.8% of responses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kennelsurvey_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-kennelsurvey-16">[16]</a></sup> Salukis have an average lifespan of 12 to 14 years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whatdog_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-whatdog-13">[13]</a></sup> which is similar to other breeds of their size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cassidy_Weight_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cassidy_Weight-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saluki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Xuande-salukis-092x0507_01lg.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1048" data-file-width="1400" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Xuande-salukis-092x0507_01lg.jpg/220px-Xuande-salukis-092x0507_01lg.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Xuande-salukis-092x0507_01lg.jpg/330px-Xuande-salukis-092x0507_01lg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Xuande-salukis-092x0507_01lg.jpg/440px-Xuande-salukis-092x0507_01lg.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Xuande-salukis-092x0507_01lg.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Two Salukis</i>, painted by the Xuande Emperor of China (1398–1435).</div>
</div>

<p>The Saluki was historically bred in the Fertile Crescent where agriculture originated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freedman2013_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-freedman2013-1">[1]</a></sup> Images of running dogs with long, narrow bodies adorn pottery found in <a href="/wiki/Susa" title="Susa">Susa</a> that dates back to 6,000 years ago in ancient <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, with one writer suggesting that these might depict the ancestor of the saluki,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Susa_Bushnell_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Susa_Bushnell-18">[18]</a></sup> despite the depictions bearing erect, pointed ears.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Susa_Beaker_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Susa_Beaker-19">[19]</a></sup> Salukis and greyhounds were increasingly depicted on Egyptian tombs from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="Middle Kingdom of Egypt">The Middle Kingdom</a> (2134 BC–1785 BC) onward,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royalhunt_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-royalhunt-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:55</sup> however it was during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eighteenth_dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Eighteenth dynasty of Egypt">Eighteenth dynasty of Egypt</a> that Salukis rose to prominence,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldwasser106_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-goldwasser106-20">[20]</a></sup> replacing the <a href="/wiki/Tesem" title="Tesem">Tesem</a> (thought to be similar to modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pariah_dogs" title="Pariah dogs">Pariah dogs</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldwasser93_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-goldwasser93-21">[21]</a></sup> or a generic term for a dog) in ancient Egyptian art.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldwasser109_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-goldwasser109-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The breed spread southward into the <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lucas_Cranach_d._%C3%84._042.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1763" data-file-width="776" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Lucas_Cranach_d._%C3%84._042.jpg/100px-Lucas_Cranach_d._%C3%84._042.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Lucas_Cranach_d._%C3%84._042.jpg/150px-Lucas_Cranach_d._%C3%84._042.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Lucas_Cranach_d._%C3%84._042.jpg/200px-Lucas_Cranach_d._%C3%84._042.jpg 2x" width="100"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lucas_Cranach_d._%C3%84._042.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The painting of <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV,_Duke_of_Saxony" title="Henry IV, Duke of Saxony">Henry IV, Duke of Saxony</a> by <a href="/wiki/Lucas_Cranach_the_Elder" title="Lucas Cranach the Elder">Lucas Cranach</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>From <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, the breed is mentioned in the poetry of Khaghani (1121-1190), depicted in miniature paintings of hunting scenes along with horseback archers by Master <a href="/wiki/Kam%C4%81l_ud-D%C4%ABn_Behz%C4%81d" title="Kamāl ud-Dīn Behzād">Kamāl ud-Dīn Behzād</a> (1450–1535),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> depicted in book illustrations by 'Abd al-Wahhab ibn 'Abd al-Fattah ibn 'Ali (1516),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and in metal-smithing from the reign of the <a href="/wiki/Injuids" title="Injuids">Injuid</a> prince, Jamal al Dine Abu Is'haq, created between 1342–1353.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metalsmithing_in_Injuids_dynasty_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Metalsmithing_in_Injuids_dynasty-27">[27]</a></sup> One of the more outstanding pieces of sculpture in Iran is the <a href="/wiki/Tangeh_Savashi" title="Tangeh Savashi">Savashi</a> Canyon Relief, carved around 1800, that was commissioned by Shar <a href="/wiki/Fath-Ali_Shah_Qajar" title="Fath-Ali Shah Qajar">Fath-Ali Shah Qajar</a> to commemorate his hunting exploits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Savashi_relief_Close-up_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Savashi_relief_Close-up-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road#Mongol_age" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a> was a trading route that stretched from ancient Iran to China. From <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, examples of the breed were painted by the fifth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_emperors_of_the_Ming_Dynasty" title="List of emperors of the Ming Dynasty">Ming Emperor</a> Zhū Zhānjī, known more commonly as the <a href="/wiki/Xuande_Emperor" title="Xuande Emperor">Xuande Emperor</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a> (1368–1644). The inscription on the painting reads "playfully painted [by the] imperial brush" in 1427. Additional red seals were added in later years by owners of the painting, which also reveals that the painting was in the Imperial Chinese collection in the 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>From Europe, the legend maintains that the returning crusaders brought the Saluki back from the Middle East.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leighton478_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-leighton478-30">[30]</a></sup> The painting of <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV,_Duke_of_Saxony" title="Henry IV, Duke of Saxony">Henry IV, Duke of Saxony</a> with his hunting dog, painted by <a href="/wiki/Lucas_Cranach_the_Elder" title="Lucas Cranach the Elder">Lucas Cranach the Elder</a> in 1514, is thought by some to show a Saluki. The dog wears a collar decorated with a scallop shell, which is the badge of a pilgrim who has traveled the <a href="/wiki/Camino_de_Santiago#Scallop_symbol" title="Camino de Santiago">Way of Saint James</a> in Spain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Salukis appear in <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Veronese" title="Paolo Veronese">Paolo Veronese</a>'s 1573 work <i>The Adoration of the Magi</i> (also known as the <i>Adoration of the Kings</i>), currently located at the <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery" title="National Gallery">National Gallery</a>, London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Veronese painted the breed in another two of his religious paintings: <i>The Marriage at Cana</i> and <i>The Finding of Moses</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leighton478_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-leighton478-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sheikh" title="Sheikh">Sheik</a> <a href="/wiki/Hamad_ibn_Isa_Al_Khalifa_(1872%E2%80%931942)" title="Hamad ibn Isa Al Khalifa (1872–1942)">Hamad ibn Isa Al Khalifa</a>, <a href="/wiki/King_of_Bahrain" title="King of Bahrain">King of Bahrain</a> during the 1930s, was known for a pack of Salukis that accompanied him throughout the Arab world on hunting trips. Following his death, his son <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salman_ibn_Hamad_Al_Khalifa_(1894%E2%80%931961)" title="Salman ibn Hamad Al Khalifa (1894–1961)">Salman ibn Hamad Al Khalifa</a> attempted to keep the lines pure-bred but they became interbred with other breeds. However, the pure-bred lines of the royal kennel were saved by the efforts of Dana Al Khalifa who was given two pure-bred puppies by the King, and about a decade later had pure-bred Salukis registered with the Kennel Club of Bahrain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whelanbahrein_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-whelanbahrein-34">[34]</a></sup> Today, the breed is still held in high regard throughout the Middle East and were hunting dogs for nobles and rulers around the region. They are considered clean by the <a href="/wiki/Bedouin" title="Bedouin">Bedouins</a>, and are allowed to be in women's quarters, while other dogs must be kept outside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whelanbahrein_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-whelanbahrein-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, a DNA study compared dogs and wolves for <a href="/wiki/AMY2B" title="AMY2B">AMY2B</a> (alpha amylase 2B), which is a gene gan enzyme that assists with the first step in the digestion of dietary starch and glycogen. An expansion of this gene in dogs would enable early dogs to exploit a starch-rich diet as they fed on refuse from agriculture. Data indicated that the wolves and dingo had just two copies of the gene and the Siberian husky that is associated with hunter-gatherers had just 3–4 copies, "whereas the Saluki, which was historically bred in the Fertile Crescent where agriculture originated, has 29 copies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freedman2013_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-freedman2013-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>It was not until 1840, that the Salukis were first brought to England. Referred to as Slughis, they and the modern <a href="/wiki/Sloughi" title="Sloughi">Sloughi</a> were treated as the same breed;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leighton478_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-leighton478-30">[30]</a></sup> however, recent genetic tests have shown that the two breeds are genetically separate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The first successful modern breeding line of Salukis began in 1895, with Florence Amherst (daughter of the <a href="/wiki/William_Tyssen-Amherst,_1st_Baron_Amherst_of_Hackney" title="William Tyssen-Amherst, 1st Baron Amherst of Hackney">1st Baron Amherst of Hackney</a>). Having seen Salukis on a <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> tour in that year, she imported a breeding pair from the Al Salihah area of <a href="/wiki/Lower_Egypt" title="Lower Egypt">Lower Egypt</a>. A champion of breed purity, she struggled alone for nearly three decades, and real Saluki popularity did not take hold until the early 1920s, when officers returning from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Eastern_theatre_of_World_War_I" title="Middle Eastern theatre of World War I">war in the Middle East</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arab_Revolt" title="Arab Revolt">Arab Revolt</a> brought their pet Salukis home with them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-duggan36_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-duggan36-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of these was Brigadier General Frederick Lance of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/19th_Lancers_(Fane%27s_Horse)" title="19th Lancers (Fane's Horse)">19th Lancers</a>, and his wife, Gladys, returned to Britain with two Salukis from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarona,_Palestine" title="Sarona, Palestine">Sarona</a>, where he was stationed during the post-war occupation. The Lances were both keen hunters, and rode with their pack of dogs, including both Salukis and terriers, to course <a href="/wiki/Jackal" title="Jackal">jackal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dorcas_Gazelle" title="Dorcas Gazelle">Dorcas Gazelle</a> whilst stationed in the desert. They imported a male, called Sarona Kelb, who became an influence on the breed in the West.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-duggan127_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-duggan127-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Together, the Lances and Florence Amherst mounted a campaign for recognition of the Middle Eastern breed, that coincided with the phenomenon of "Tutmania" caused by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Howard_Carter_(archaeologist)" title="Howard Carter (archaeologist)">Howard Carter's</a> discovery of <a href="/wiki/Tutankhamun" title="Tutankhamun">Tutankhamun's tomb</a> in late 1922. In 1923, the Saluki or Gazelle Hound Club was formed, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kennel_Club_(UK)" title="Kennel Club (UK)">Kennel Club</a> granted official recognition to the breed. The first registered Salukis in the western studbook were Cyrus and Slongha Peri imported from Iran and registered with the DWZRV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DWZRV_-_Deutscher_Windhundzucht-_und_Rennverband_e.V._39-0"><a href="#cite_note-DWZRV_-_Deutscher_Windhundzucht-_und_Rennverband_e.V.-39">[39]</a></sup> DWZRV also records the first litter in 1922.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DWZRV_First_registered_litter_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-DWZRV_First_registered_litter-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Imports to England during the inter-war years were chiefly from areas of British military influence and commerce: <a href="/wiki/Bahrain" title="Bahrain">Bahrain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Transjordan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>. Both Florence Amherst and the Lances imported breeding stock from the latter two countries. Despite substantial populations of Salukis in Germany, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, none of these were imported to England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-duggan154_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-duggan154-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>English Salukis (chiefly descendants of Sarona Kelb) were exported to many countries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-duggan181_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-duggan181-42">[42]</a></sup> but by the mid-1930s, interest slackened, and with the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, breeding and show activities almost entirely stopped. The number of litters was minimal – just enough to keep the breed alive. <a href="/wiki/Home_front_during_World_War_II#Rationing" title="Home front during World War II">Food rationing</a> reserved all edible meat for humans, and to prevent the Salukis from dying from starvation or being killed by <a href="/wiki/The_Blitz" title="The Blitz">bombs</a>, some owners euthanized entire kennels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-duggan242_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-duggan242-43">[43]</a></sup> A small number of Saluki kennels survived the war, and along with fresh imports belonging to a second wave of soldiers returning from the Middle East, the slow process of re-establishing the breed began again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-duggan242_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-duggan242-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Popularity of Salukis dramatically increased, and the Saluki Club of America was founded in 1927, Salukis were recognized by <a href="/wiki/The_Kennel_Club" title="The Kennel Club">The Kennel Club</a> in 1923, and by the <a href="/wiki/American_Kennel_Club" title="American Kennel Club">American Kennel Club</a> in 1929.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meetsalukis_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-meetsalukis-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-duggan150_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-duggan150-44">[44]</a></sup> The breed is also the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Illinois_Salukis" title="Southern Illinois Salukis">mascot</a> of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Illinois_University_Carbondale" title="Southern Illinois University Carbondale">Southern Illinois University Carbondale</a>.</p>
<p>The popularity of the Saluki in the United States, according to the American Kennel Club, has remained relatively stable over the past decade, with the breed ranked 107th in 1999, had decreased to 118th in 2008, but by 2008 had increased once again to 112th.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-akcdogreg_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-akcdogreg-45">[45]</a></sup> Between 2000 and 2009, 1215 Salukis were registered with <a href="/wiki/The_Kennel_Club" title="The Kennel Club">The Kennel Club</a> in the UK,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-houndreg_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-houndreg-46">[46]</a></sup> while this does not approach the numbers of the more popular breeds,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gundogreg_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-gundogreg-47">[47]</a></sup> it is in line with similar breeds in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hound_Group" title="Hound Group">Hound Group</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Borzoi" title="Borzoi">Borzoi</a>, which had 1399 puppies registered in the same period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-houndreg_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-houndreg-46">[46]</a></sup> In September 2007, The Kennel Club Art Gallery's 12th exhibition celebrated the Saluki, <i>The Saluki in Art</i> showed a range of exhibits including <a href="/wiki/Terracotta" title="Terracotta">terracotta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> works, along with contemporary artists and a range of trophies from Saluki breed clubs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
