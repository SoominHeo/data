<p><b>Salty liquorice</b>, also known as <b>salmiak</b> or <b>salmiakki</b> (in <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>), is a variety of <a href="/wiki/Liquorice_(confectionery)" title="Liquorice (confectionery)">liquorice</a> flavoured with <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a>, common in the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">Nordic countries</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Germany" title="Northern Germany">northern Germany</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thecrimson_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-thecrimson-1">[1]</a></sup> Ammonium chloride gives salty liquorice an astringent, salty taste<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> (hence the name), which has been described as "tongue-numbing"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and "almost-stinging".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thecrimson_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-thecrimson-1">[1]</a></sup> Salty liquorice is an <a href="/wiki/Acquired_taste" title="Acquired taste">acquired taste</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thecrimson_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-thecrimson-1">[1]</a></sup> and people not familiar with ammonium chloride might find the taste physically overwhelming and unlikeable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thecrimson_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-thecrimson-1">[1]</a></sup> Salty liquorice candies are almost always black or very dark brown and can range from very soft to very hard and may be brittle. The other colours used are white and variants of grey. Salty liquorice is also used as a flavouring in other products, such as ice creams and alcoholic beverages.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salty_liquorice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ammonium_chloride.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="776" data-file-width="758" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Ammonium_chloride.jpg/200px-Ammonium_chloride.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Ammonium_chloride.jpg/300px-Ammonium_chloride.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Ammonium_chloride.jpg/400px-Ammonium_chloride.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>

A sample of <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a> which gives salty liquorice its distinctive flavour.</div>

<p>The words <i>salmiak</i> and <i>salmiakki</i> are derived from an archaic Latin name for ammonium chloride, <i><a href="/wiki/Sal_ammoniac" title="Sal ammoniac">sal ammoniacus</a></i>, meaning "salt of Ammon". "Ammon" in turn refers to the temple of <a href="/wiki/Amun" title="Amun">Ammon</a> at <a href="/wiki/Siwa_Oasis" title="Siwa Oasis">Siwa Oasis</a>, where ancient Greeks found ammonium chloride. The word <i><a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a></i> has the same origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Ammonium chloride has a history of being used as a <a href="/wiki/Cough_medicine" title="Cough medicine">cough medicine</a>, as it works as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expectorant" title="Expectorant">expectorant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Finnish author <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jukka_Annala&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jukka Annala (page does not exist)">Jukka Annala</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">Â (<a class="extiw" href="https://fi.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jukka_Annala" title="fi:Jukka Annala">fi</a>)</span> speculates that salty liquorice has its origins at drug stores that manufactured their own cough medicine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kysy1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-kysy1-7">[7]</a></sup> Where and when ammonium chloride and liquorice were first combined to produce salty liquorice is unclear,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> but by the 1930s it was produced in Sweden, Finland, Norway, Denmark, and the Netherlands as a candy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kysy1_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-kysy1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salty_liquorice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Different languages often refer to salty liquorice as either "salmiac liquorice" (such as <i>salmiaklakrits</i> in <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>, or <i>salmiaklakrids</i> in <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a>), or simply "salty liquorice". The Dutch refer to it as "<a class="extiw" href="https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drop" title="nl:Drop">Zoute Drop</a>" or even a variety called "Dubbel Zoute Drop" (double salted liquorice). In addition to ammonium chloride, salty liquorice candies are sometimes flavoured with other strong flavours like table salt. A common shape for salty liquorice candies is a black diamond-shaped <a href="/wiki/Lozenge" title="Lozenge">lozenge</a>.</p>
<p>The strength of the confectionery depends on the amount of ammonium chloride used, which varies by country and what's considered a safe amount. In Sweden for example, the most popular types of salty liquorice contain an average of 7% of ammonium chloride. In 2012 there was a European Union proposal to limit the amount to 0.3%, which was met with wide opposition. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Although the European Union now regulate the use of ammonium chloride to 0.3% in most foodstuffs, there is no specific restriction for it in liquorice or ice cream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salty_liquorice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to being used in candy, salmiak is also used to flavour <a href="/wiki/Vodka" title="Vodka">vodka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chocolate" title="Chocolate">chocolate</a>, distilled <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a> <a href="/wiki/Brandy" title="Brandy">brandy</a>, ice cream, cola drinks, <a href="/wiki/Snus" title="Snus">snus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a>.</p>
