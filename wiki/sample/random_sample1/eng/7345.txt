<p><b>Salsa</b> is the Italian and Spanish term for <a href="/wiki/Sauce" title="Sauce">sauce</a>, and in English-speaking countries usually refers to the sauces typical of Mexican cuisine known as <b>salsa picante</b>, particularly those used as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dip_(food)" title="Dip (food)">dips</a>.</p>
<p>Salsa is often a tomato-based sauce or dip which is heterogeneous and includes additional components such as onions, chilies, beans, corn, and various spices. They are typically piquant, ranging from mild to extremely hot.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pronunciation_and_etymology">Pronunciation and etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salsa_(sauce)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pronunciation and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Mexican salsas were traditionally produced using the <a href="/wiki/Mortar_and_pestle" title="Mortar and pestle">mortar and pestle</a>-like <i><a href="/wiki/Molcajete" title="Molcajete">molcajete</a></i>, although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blender_(device)" title="Blender (device)">blenders</a> are now more commonly used. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maya_people" title="Maya people">Maya</a> made salsa also, using a mortar and pestle. Well-known <i>salsas</i> include:</p>
<ul>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salsa_roja_picante_(Mexico)" title="Salsa roja picante (Mexico)">Salsa roja</a></i>, "red sauce", is used as a <a href="/wiki/Condiment" title="Condiment">condiment</a> in Mexican and <a href="/wiki/Cuisine_of_the_Southwestern_United_States" title="Cuisine of the Southwestern United States">Southwestern</a> (U.S.) cuisines; usually includes cooked <a href="/wiki/Tomato" title="Tomato">tomatoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chili_pepper" title="Chili pepper">chili peppers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Onion" title="Onion">onion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Garlic" title="Garlic">garlic</a>, and fresh <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cilantro" title="Cilantro">cilantro</a> (coriander).</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Pico_de_gallo" title="Pico de gallo">Pico de gallo</a></i> ("rooster's beak"), also known as <i>salsa fresca</i> ("fresh sauce"), <i>salsa picada</i> ("chopped sauce"), or <i>salsa mexicana</i> ("Mexican sauce"), is made with raw tomatoes, lime juice, chilies, onions, cilantro leaves, and other coarsely chopped raw ingredients.</li>
<li><i>Salsa cruda</i>, "raw sauce", is an uncooked mixture of chopped tomatoes, onions, jalapeÃ±o chilies, and cilantro, or coriander leaf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Salsa_verde_(Mexico)" title="Salsa verde (Mexico)">Salsa verde</a></i>, "green sauce", in Mexican versions, is made with <a href="/wiki/Tomatillo" title="Tomatillo">tomatillos</a>, usually cooked. The Italian version is made with herbs.</li>
<li><i>Salsa negra</i>, "black sauce" is a Mexican sauce made from dried chilies, oil, and garlic.</li>
<li><i>Salsa taquera</i>, "<a href="/wiki/Taco" title="Taco">taco</a> sauce": Made with tomatillos and <a href="/wiki/Chipotle" title="Chipotle">morita</a> chili</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Salsa_criolla" title="Salsa criolla">Salsa criolla</a></i> is a South American salsa with a sliced-onion base.</li>
<li><i>Salsa ranchera</i>, "ranch-style sauce": Made with roasted tomatoes, various chilies, and spices, it typically is served warm, and possesses a thick, <a href="/wiki/Soup" title="Soup">soupy</a> quality. Though it contains none, it imparts a characteristic flavor reminiscent of <a href="/wiki/Black_pepper" title="Black pepper">black pepper</a>.</li>
<li><i>Salsa brava</i>, "wild sauce", is a mildly spicy sauce made with tomato, garlic, onion, and vinegar, often flavored with <a href="/wiki/Paprika" title="Paprika">paprika</a>. On top of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potato_wedge" title="Potato wedge">potato wedges</a>, it makes the dish <i><a href="/wiki/Patatas_bravas" title="Patatas bravas">patatas bravas</a></i>, typical of <i><a href="/wiki/Tapas" title="Tapas">tapas</a></i> bars in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Guacamole" title="Guacamole">Guacamole</a></i> is thicker than a sauce and generally used as a dip; it refers to any sauce where the main ingredient is <a href="/wiki/Avocado" title="Avocado">avocado</a>.</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mole_(sauce)" title="Mole (sauce)">Mole</a></i> (<small>Spanish pronunciation:Â </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Spanish" title="Help:IPA for Spanish">[Ëmole]</a></span>) is a Mexican sauce made from chilies mixed with spices, unsweetened <a href="/wiki/Chocolate" title="Chocolate">chocolate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Almond" title="Almond">almonds</a>, and other ingredients.</li>
<li>Creamy avocado salsa is a sauce made from avocado, lime, cilantro, jalapeÃ±o or serrano peppers, garlic, olive oil, cumin, and salt.</li>
<li>Mango salsa is a spicy-sweet sauce made from mangoes, used as a topping for nachos. It is often also used as a garnish on grilled chicken or grilled fish due to the sauce's gamut of complementary flavors.</li>
<li>Pineapple salsa is a spicy and sweet sauce made from pineapples, used as an alternative to the mango salsa.</li>
<li>Chipotle salsa is a smoky, spicy sauce made from smoked jalapeÃ±o chilies, tomatoes, garlic and spices.</li>
<li>Habanero salsa is an extremely spicy salsa, where the piquancy comes from <a href="/wiki/Habanero" title="Habanero">habanero chilies</a>.</li>
<li>Corn salsa is a chunky salsa made with sweetcorn and other ingredients, such as onions, and chiles (either poblano, bell chilies, and/or jalapeÃ±os), made popular by the burrito chains for burritos, tacos, and quesadillas.</li>
<li>Carrot salsa is made with carrots as the base.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<p>There are many other salsas, both traditional and <i>nouveau</i>, some are made with <a href="/wiki/Mentha" title="Mentha">mint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pineapple" title="Pineapple">pineapple</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Mango" title="Mango">mango</a>.</p>
<p>Outside of Mexico and Central America, the following salsas are common to each of the following regions; in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Cone" title="Southern Cone">Southern Cone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chimichurri" title="Chimichurri">chimichurri</a> sauce is common. Chimichurri is "a spicy vinegar-parsley sauce that is the salsa (and leading condiment) in Argentina and <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>, served with grilled meat. It is made of chopped fresh parsley and onion, seasoned with garlic, oregano, salt, cayenne chilies and black pepper and bound with oil and vinegar."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In Costa Rica, dishes are prepared with <a href="/wiki/Salsa_Lizano" title="Salsa Lizano">salsa Lizano</a>, a thin, smooth, light brown sauce. In <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, a typical salsa is <i><a href="/wiki/Mojo_(sauce)" title="Mojo (sauce)">mojo</a></i>. Unlike the tomato-based salsas, <i>mojo</i> typically consists of <a href="/wiki/Olive_oil" title="Olive oil">olive oil</a>, garlic, and citrus juice, and is used both to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marinade" title="Marinade">marinate</a> meats and as a dipping sauce. In <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, a traditional salsa is <i>peri peri</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Piri_piri" title="Piri piri">piri piri</a></i> sauce: "The national condiment of Peru, <i>peri-peri</i> sauce is made in medium to hot levels of spicinessâthe more chili, or the hotter variety of chile used, the hotter the sauce. Original <i>peri-peri</i> uses the African birdâs eye chili (the African word for the chili is <i>peri-peri</i>). Milder sauces may use only cayenne and serrano chilies. To a base of vinegar and oil, garlic and lemon juice are added, plus other seasonings, which often include paprika or tomato paste for flavor and color, onions and herbâeach company has its own recipe. It is also used as a cooking sauce."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>Most jarred, canned, and bottled salsa and picante sauces sold in the United States in <a href="/wiki/Supermarket" title="Supermarket">grocery stores</a> are forms of <i>salsa cruda</i> or <i>pico de gallo</i>, and typically have a semi-liquid texture. To increase their shelf lives, these salsas have been cooked to a temperature of 175Â Â°F (79Â Â°C). Some have added <a href="/wiki/Vinegar" title="Vinegar">vinegar</a>, and some use <a href="/wiki/Pickled_pepper" title="Pickled pepper">pickled peppers</a> instead of fresh ones. Tomatoes are strongly acidic by nature, which, along with the heat processing, is enough to stabilize the product for grocery distribution.</p>
<p><i>Picante</i> sauce of the American type is often thinner in consistency than what is labelled as "salsa". <i>Picante</i> is a Spanish adjective meaning "piquant", which derives from <i>picar</i> ("to sting"), referring to the feeling caused by salsas on one's tongue.</p>
<p>Many grocery stores in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and Canada also sell "fresh" refrigerated salsa, usually in plastic containers. Fresh salsa is usually more expensive and has a shorter shelf life than canned or jarred salsa. It may or may not contain vinegar.</p>
<p>Taco sauce is a condiment sold in American grocery stores and fast food <a href="/wiki/Tex-Mex" title="Tex-Mex">Tex-Mex</a> outlets. Taco sauce is similar to its Mexican counterpart in that it is smoothly blended, having the consistency of thin ketchup. It is made from tomato paste instead of whole tomatoes and lacks the seeds and chunks of vegetables found in picante sauce.</p>
<p>While some salsa fans do not consider jarred products to be real <i>salsa cruda</i>, their widespread availability and long shelf life have been credited with much of salsa's enormous popularity in states outside of the southwest, especially in areas where salsa is not a traditional part of the cuisine. In 1992, the dollar total of salsa sales in the United States exceeded those of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tomato_ketchup" title="Tomato ketchup">tomato ketchup</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance_of_proper_storage">Importance of proper storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salsa_(sauce)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Importance of proper storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> says care should be taken in the preparation and storage of salsas and any other types of sauces, since many raw-served varieties can act as growth media for potentially dangerous <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, especially when unrefrigerated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2002 a study by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_Health_Science_Center_at_Houston" title="University of Texas Health Science Center at Houston">University of TexasâHouston</a>, found sauces contaminated with <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">E. coli</a></i> in:</p>
<ul>
<li>66% of the sauces from restaurants tested in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guadalajara,_Jalisco" title="Guadalajara, Jalisco">Guadalajara, Jalisco</a></li>
<li>40% of those from restaurants tested in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" title="Houston, Texas">Houston, Texas</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>In 2010 the <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention" title="Centers for Disease Control and Prevention">CDC</a> reported that 1 in 25 <a href="/wiki/Foodborne_illness" title="Foodborne illness">foodborne illnesses</a> between 1998 and 2008 was traced back to restaurant sauces (carelessly prepared or stored).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention">Prevention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salsa_(sauce)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Prevention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A 2010 paper on salsa food hygiene described refrigeration as "the key to safe" sauces. This study also found that fresh <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lime_juice" title="Lime juice">lime juice</a> and fresh garlic (but not powdered garlic) would prevent the growth of bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
