<p><i><b>Cannabis sativa</b></i> is an annual herbaceous plant in the <i><a href="/wiki/Cannabis" title="Cannabis">Cannabis</a></i> genus, a species of the <a href="/wiki/Cannabaceae" title="Cannabaceae">Cannabaceae</a> family. People have cultivated <i>Cannabis sativa</i> throughout <a href="/wiki/Recorded_history" title="Recorded history">recorded history</a> as a source of <a href="/wiki/Hemp#Fibre" title="Hemp">industrial fibre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemp_oil" title="Hemp oil">seed oil</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hempnut" title="Hempnut">food</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">recreation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Entheogenic_use_of_cannabis" title="Entheogenic use of cannabis">religious and spiritual moods</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Medical_cannabis" title="Medical cannabis">medicine</a>. Each part of the plant is harvested differently, depending on the purpose of its use. The species was first classified by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1753.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_uses">Common uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cannabis_sativa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Common uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Cannabis#Industrial_and_personal_uses" title="Cannabis">Industrial and personal uses of <i>Cannabis</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">Cannabis (drug)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">Hemp</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hemp-sack,asabukuro,japan.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1577" data-file-width="1183" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Hemp-sack%2Casabukuro%2Cjapan.JPG/220px-Hemp-sack%2Casabukuro%2Cjapan.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Hemp-sack%2Casabukuro%2Cjapan.JPG/330px-Hemp-sack%2Casabukuro%2Cjapan.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Hemp-sack%2Casabukuro%2Cjapan.JPG/440px-Hemp-sack%2Casabukuro%2Cjapan.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hemp-sack,asabukuro,japan.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A sack made from hemp fiber</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Its seeds are chiefly used to make hempseed oil which can be used for cooking, lamps, lacquers, or paints. They can also be used as caged-bird feed, as they provide a moderate source of nutrients for most birds. The flowers (and to a lesser extent the leaves, stems, and seeds) contain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychoactive" title="Psychoactive">psychoactive</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a> known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cannabinoids" title="Cannabinoids">cannabinoids</a> that are consumed for recreational, medicinal, and spiritual purposes. When so used, preparations of flowers (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marijuana" title="Marijuana">marijuana</a>) and leaves and preparations derived from resinous extract (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Hashish" title="Hashish">hashish</a>) are consumed by smoking, vaporizing and oral ingestion. Historically, <a href="/wiki/Tincture" title="Tincture">tinctures</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herbal_tea" title="Herbal tea">teas</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ointment" title="Ointment">ointments</a> have also been common preparations. In <a href="/wiki/Traditional_medicine" title="Traditional medicine">traditional medicine</a> of India in particular <i>C. sativa</i> has been used as hallucinogenic, hypnotic, <a href="/wiki/Sedative" title="Sedative">sedative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Analgesic" title="Analgesic">analgesic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anti-inflammatory" title="Anti-inflammatory">anti-inflammatory</a> agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plant_physiology">Plant physiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cannabis_sativa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plant physiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Cannabis" title="Cannabis">Cannabis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_cultivation" title="Cannabis cultivation">Cannabis cultivation</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a> of the female plant are arranged in <a href="/wiki/Raceme" title="Raceme">racemes</a> and can produce hundreds of seeds. Male plants shed their pollen and die several weeks prior to seed ripening on the female plants. Although genetic factors dispose a plant to become male or female, environmental factors including the <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_cycle" title="Diurnal cycle">diurnal light cycle</a> can alter sexual expression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Naturally occurring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoecious" title="Monoecious">monoecious</a> plants, with both male and female parts, are either sterile or fertile but artificially induced "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermaphrodites" title="Hermaphrodites">hermaphrodites</a>" (a commonly used misnomer) can have fully functional reproductive organs. "Feminized" seed sold by many commercial seed suppliers are derived from artificially "hermaphrodytic" females that lack the male gene, or by treating the plants with hormones or silver <a href="/wiki/Thiosulfate" title="Thiosulfate">thiosulfate</a>. In the case of production related to recreational use of Cannabis, the process of "cloning" provides the grower with DNA identical female plants eliminating the need of seeds for the growing process.</p>
<p>A <i>Cannabis</i> plant in the vegetative growth phase of its life requires more than 12–13 hours of light per day to stay vegetative. Flowering usually occurs when darkness equals at least 12 hours per day<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (June 2015)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Cannabis_sativa#Dubious" title="Talk:Cannabis sativa">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup>. The flowering cycle can last anywhere between nine and fifteen weeks, depending on the strain and environmental conditions.</p>
<p>In soil, the optimum <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> for the plant is 6.3 to 6.8. In <a href="/wiki/Hydroponics" title="Hydroponics">hydroponic</a> growing, the nutrient solution is best at 5.2 to 5.8, making <i>Cannabis</i> well-suited to hydroponics because this pH range is hostile to most bacteria and fungi.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivars">Cultivars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cannabis_sativa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cultivars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Broadly, there are three main <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivar groups</a> of cannabis that are cultivated today:</p>
<ul>
<li>Cultivars primarily cultivated for their fibre, characterised by long stems and little branching.</li>
<li>Cultivars grown for seed which can be eaten entirely raw or from which <a href="/wiki/Hemp_oil" title="Hemp oil">hemp oil</a> is extracted.</li>
<li>Cultivars grown for medicinal or <a href="/wiki/Recreational_drug_use" title="Recreational drug use">recreational</a> purposes. A nominal if not legal distinction is often made between industrial hemp, with concentrations of psychoactive compounds far too low to be useful for that purpose, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marijuana" title="Marijuana">marijuana</a>.</li>
</ul>

<p>Although the main psychoactive constituent of <i>Cannabis</i> is <a href="/wiki/Tetrahydrocannabinol" title="Tetrahydrocannabinol">tetrahydrocannabinol</a> (THC), the plant is known to contain more than 500 compounds, among them at least 113 <a href="/wiki/Cannabinoid" title="Cannabinoid">cannabinoids</a>; however, most of these "minor" cannabinoids are only produced in trace amounts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Besides THC, another cannabinoid produced in high concentrations by some plants is <a href="/wiki/Cannabidiol" title="Cannabidiol">cannabidiol</a> (CBD), which is not psychoactive but has recently been shown to block the effect of THC in the nervous system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Differences in the chemical composition of <i>Cannabis</i> varieties may produce different effects in humans. Synthetic THC, called dronabinol, does not contain <a href="/wiki/Cannabidiol" title="Cannabidiol">CBD</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cannabinol" title="Cannabinol">CBN</a>, or other cannabinoids, which is one reason why its <a href="/wiki/Pharmacology" title="Pharmacology">pharmacological</a> effects may differ significantly from those of natural <i>Cannabis</i> preparations.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_constituents">Chemical constituents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cannabis_sativa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Chemical constituents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Beside cannabinoids, Cannabis</i> chemical constituents include about 120 compounds responsible for its <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_flower_essential_oil" title="Cannabis flower essential oil">characteristic aroma</a>. These are mainly volatile <a href="/wiki/Terpene" title="Terpene">terpenes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sesquiterpene" title="Sesquiterpene">sesquiterpenes</a>.</p>

<li>α-<a href="/wiki/Pinene" title="Pinene">Pinene</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-essential_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-essential-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Myrcene" title="Myrcene">Myrcene</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-essential_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-essential-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Linalool" title="Linalool">Linalool</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-essential_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-essential-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Limonene" title="Limonene">Limonene</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-essential_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-essential-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>Trans-β-<a href="/wiki/Ocimene" title="Ocimene">ocimene</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-essential_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-essential-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>α-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terpinolene" title="Terpinolene">Terpinolene</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-essential_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-essential-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>Trans-<a href="/wiki/Caryophyllene" title="Caryophyllene">caryophyllene</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-essential_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-essential-6">[6]</a></sup></li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Caryophyllene" title="Caryophyllene">Caryophyllene</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-essential_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-essential-6">[6]</a></sup> with which some hashish detection dogs are trained<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Difference_between_C._sativa_and_C._indica">Difference between <i>C. sativa</i> and <i>C. indica</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cannabis_sativa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Difference between C. sativa and C. indica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some studies indicate that, on average, <i><a href="/wiki/Cannabis_indica" title="Cannabis indica">Cannabis indica</a></i> has higher levels of THC to CBD, whereas <i>Cannabis sativa</i> has higher levels of <a href="/wiki/Cannabidiol" title="Cannabidiol">CBD</a> compared to <a href="/wiki/Tetrahydrocannabinol" title="Tetrahydrocannabinol">THC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hillig_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hillig-8">[8]</a></sup> However, huge variability exists within either species, and there is an increasing discussion whether the existing paradigm of the difference between species adequately represents the variability found within the <i>Cannabis</i> genus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> There are five chemotaxonomic types of <i>Cannabis</i>: one with high levels of THC, one which is more fibrous and has higher levels of CBD, one that is an intermediate between the two, another one with high levels of <a href="/wiki/Cannabigerol" title="Cannabigerol">cannabigerol</a> (CBG), and the last one almost without cannabinoids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Cannabis</i> strains with relatively high CBD:THC ratios are less likely to induce <a href="/wiki/Anxiety" title="Anxiety">anxiety</a> than vice versa. This may be due to CBD's <a href="/wiki/Receptor_antagonist" title="Receptor antagonist">antagonistic</a> effects at the <a href="/wiki/Cannabinoid_receptor" title="Cannabinoid receptor">cannabinoid receptors</a>, compared to THC's <a href="/wiki/Partial_agonist" title="Partial agonist">partial agonist</a> effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> CBD is also a <a href="/wiki/5-HT1A_receptor" title="5-HT1A receptor">5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptor</a> (<a href="/wiki/Serotonin" title="Serotonin">serotonin</a>) agonist, which may also contribute to an anxiolytic-content effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joy-Watson-Benson_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Joy-Watson-Benson-14">[14]</a></sup> The effects of <i>sativa</i> are well known for its cerebral high, while <i>indica</i> is well known for its sedative effects which some prefer for night time use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joy-Watson-Benson_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Joy-Watson-Benson-14">[14]</a></sup> Both types are used as <a href="/wiki/Medical_cannabis" title="Medical cannabis">medical cannabis</a>. <i>Indica</i> plants are normally shorter and stockier than <i>sativas</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fischedick_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fischedick-15">[15]</a></sup> They have broader, deeply serrated leaves and a compact and dense flower cluster. The effects of <i>indicas</i> are predominantly physical and sedative.</p>
