<p>The <b>three-state solution</b>, also called the <b>Egyptian-Jordanian solution</b> or the <b>Jordan-Egypt option</b>, is an approach to peace in the <a href="/wiki/Israeli%E2%80%93Palestinian_conflict" title="Israeli–Palestinian conflict">Israeli–Palestinian conflict</a> by returning control of the <a href="/wiki/West_Bank" title="West Bank">West Bank</a> to <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a> and control of the <a href="/wiki/Gaza_Strip" title="Gaza Strip">Gaza Strip</a> to <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>The three-state solution essentially replicates the situation that existed between the <a href="/wiki/1949_Armistice_Agreements" title="1949 Armistice Agreements">1949 Armistice Agreements</a> and the 1967 <a href="/wiki/Six-Day_War" title="Six-Day War">Six-Day War</a>. Beginning in 1949, <a href="/wiki/Occupation_of_the_Gaza_Strip_by_Egypt" title="Occupation of the Gaza Strip by Egypt">Egypt occupied the Gaza Strip</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jordanian_occupation_of_the_West_Bank" title="Jordanian occupation of the West Bank">Jordan occupied the West Bank</a>, and no Palestinian Arab state existed. In 1950, Jordan officially annexed the <a href="/wiki/West_Bank" title="West Bank">West Bank</a> and granted the Arab residents Jordanian citizenship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feasibility">Feasibility</span></h2>
<p>While the <a href="/wiki/Two-state_solution" title="Two-state solution">two-state solution</a> is still the prevailing option, the three-state solution is being raised with increasing frequency as the viability of the two-state solution has been repeatedly called into question.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> reported in January 2009 that Egypt and Jordan are increasingly concerned about the possibility of having to retake responsibility for Gaza and the West Bank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a September 2008 publication<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sunset_for_the_Two-State_Solution.3F_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sunset_for_the_Two-State_Solution.3F-3">[3]</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Institute_for_Near_East_Policy" title="The Washington Institute for Near East Policy">The Washington Institute for Near East Policy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giora_Eiland" title="Giora Eiland">Giora Eiland</a> wrote in support of the proposal.</p>
<p>Proposals that the Palestinians be given Jordanian citizenship are strongly opposed by the Jordanian government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpost1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-jpost1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jordanian_parliamentary_election,_2010" title="Jordanian parliamentary election, 2010">parliamentary election</a>, Jordanian politicians expressed fears that if the 2010 Israeli-Palestinian direct talks broke down and the Palestinian Authority collapsed, Jordan would be forced to reabsorb the West Bank and grant citizenship to its residents. Concern was also expressed that Israel may prefer this solution over the traditional two-state solution.</p>
<p>Jordan, which back then was more than 50 percent Palestinian, would be further cemented as a <i>de facto</i> Palestinian state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> However, some Jordanian officials have supported Jordanian control over the West Bank. In May 2010, the President of the Jordanian Senate <a href="/wiki/Taher_al-Masri" title="Taher al-Masri">Taher al-Masri</a> made reference in a speech to "the two united banks [of the Jordan River], with the Hashemite Kingdom of Jordan emerging on both banks of the holy river".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proponents">Proponents</span></h2>
<p>The three-state solution is advocated by an editorial in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Sun" title="The New York Sun">The New York Sun</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Ian_Bremmer" title="Ian Bremmer">Ian Bremmer</a>, neither of whom believe that the <a href="/wiki/Two-state_solution" title="Two-state solution">two-state solution</a> or the <a href="/wiki/One-state_solution" title="One-state solution">one-state solution</a> is viable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Former American Ambassador to the United Nations <a href="/wiki/John_R._Bolton" title="John R. Bolton">John R. Bolton</a> has suggested a "'three-state' approach, where Gaza is returned to Egyptian control and the West Bank in some configuration reverts to Jordanian sovereignty".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Daniel_Pipes" title="Daniel Pipes">Daniel Pipes</a> describes the “Jordan-Egypt option" as "a uniquely sober way” to bring peace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Israeli MK <a href="/wiki/Aryeh_Eldad" title="Aryeh Eldad">Aryeh Eldad</a> has proposed that Palestinian Arabs be given Jordanian citizenship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpost1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-jpost1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gerald_Levin" title="Gerald Levin">Gerald Levin</a> partook in discussions on building a canal from the Dead Sea at the <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> <a href="/wiki/Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Office" title="Foreign and Commonwealth Office">Foreign and Commonwealth Office</a> in August 1997. The <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a> is 430 m below sea level, and so a canal for fresh sea water could be built along the <a href="/wiki/Jordan_River" title="Jordan River">Jordan River</a>. With desalination, agricultural jobs for 1 million people within Jordan, Egypt and Israel would be sustainable. It was reported that Jordan agreed to administer between 17% and 21% of the West Bank, to facilitate canal construction with international assistance, which would increase Jordan's area to about 70% of the 1918 Palestine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_use_of_the_phrase">Alternative use of the phrase</span></h2>
<p>The phrase <i>three-state solution</i> is also used not as a peace proposal, but as a description of the status quo that has existed since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatah-Hamas_conflict" title="Fatah-Hamas conflict">Hamas took control of Gaza</a> away from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palestinian_Authority" title="Palestinian Authority">Palestinian Authority</a> in 2007, effectively leaving three states, the Palestinian Authority-controlled West Bank, Israel, and Hamas-controlled Gaza in the territory west of the Jordan river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Others, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaveh_L_Afrasiabi" title="Kaveh L Afrasiabi">Kaveh L Afrasiabi</a>, argue that the Hamas coup rendered the two-state solution impossible, and advocate the regularization of the status quo into three permanent sovereign states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In July 2012, it was reported Hamas was considering a declaration of independence with support of Egypt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-english.alarabiya.net_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-english.alarabiya.net-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
