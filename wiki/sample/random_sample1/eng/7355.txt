<p><i><b>Samguk Yusa</b></i> (<small>Korean pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Korean" title="Help:IPA for Korean">[sʰamɡuŋ njusʰa]</a></span>) or <i><b>Memorabilia of the Three Kingdoms</b></i> is a collection of legends, folktales and historical accounts relating to the <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms_of_Korea" title="Three Kingdoms of Korea">Three Kingdoms of Korea</a> (<a href="/wiki/Goguryeo" title="Goguryeo">Goguryeo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baekje" title="Baekje">Baekje</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a>), as well as to other periods and states before, during and after the Three Kingdoms period. It is the earliest extant record of the <a href="/wiki/Dangun" title="Dangun">Dangun</a> legend, which records the founding of <a href="/wiki/Gojoseon" title="Gojoseon">Gojoseon</a> as the first Korean nation.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Authorship_and_date">Authorship and date</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samgungnyusa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Authorship and date">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The text was written in <a href="/wiki/Classical_Chinese" title="Classical Chinese">Classical Chinese</a>, which was used by literate Koreans at the time of its composition. The earliest version of the text is believed to have been compiled in the 1280s, and the earliest extant publication of the text is from 1512 CE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>20th-century Korean scholars such as <a href="/wiki/Choe_Nam-seon" title="Choe Nam-seon">Choe Nam-seon</a> established the <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhist</a> monk <a href="/wiki/Il-yeon" title="Il-yeon">Iryeon</a> (1206–1289) as the main compiler of the text, on the basis that his name (and full official title) was indicated in the fifth <a href="/wiki/Serial_(literature)" title="Serial (literature)">fascicle</a>. This view is widely accepted among modern scholars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarah2012_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sarah2012-2">[2]</a></sup> The compilation is believed to have been expanded by Iryeon's disciple Muguk (1250-1322) and several others prior to the definitive 1512 recension.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-John2006_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-John2006-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ha Chongnyong and Yi Kunjik produced a <a href="/wiki/Textual_criticism" title="Textual criticism">critical edition</a> of <i>Samgungnyusa</i> in 1997. According to Ha Chongnyong, Iryeon wrote only the fifth fascicle, since his name is mentioned only in that section of the text.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-John2006_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-John2006-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 1512 edition of the text mentions a dynastic chronology at the beginning, which has several discrepancies with the information that appears later in the text. According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Buswell,_Jr." title="Robert Buswell, Jr.">Robert Buswell, Jr.</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Donald_S._Lopez,_Jr." title="Donald S. Lopez, Jr.">Donald S. Lopez, Jr.</a>, this chronology may have been a fourteenth-century addition to Iryeon's compilation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert2013_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Robert2013-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_reliability">Historical reliability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samgungnyusa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical reliability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many of the founding legends of the various kingdoms in Korean history are recorded in <i>Samgungnyusa</i>. The text covers legends from many Korean kingdoms, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dangun_Joseon" title="Dangun Joseon">Dangun Joseon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gija_Joseon" title="Gija Joseon">Gija Joseon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wiman_Joseon" title="Wiman Joseon">Wiman Joseon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buyeo_(state)" title="Buyeo (state)">Buyeo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goguryeo" title="Goguryeo">Goguryeo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baekje" title="Baekje">Baekje</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gaya_confederacy" title="Gaya confederacy">Gaya</a>.</p>
<p>Unlike the more factually-oriented <i><a href="/wiki/Samguk_Sagi" title="Samguk Sagi">Samguk Sagi</a></i>, the <i>Samgungnyusa</i> focuses on various folktales, legends and biographies from early Korean history. Given its mythical narratives, <i>Samgungnyusa</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s reliability is questionable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-John2006_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-John2006-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
