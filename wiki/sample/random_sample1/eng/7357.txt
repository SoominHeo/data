<p>The <b>trifunctional hypothesis</b> of prehistoric <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_society" title="Proto-Indo-European society">Proto-Indo-European society</a> postulates a tripartite ideology ("<i>idéologie tripartite</i>") reflected in the existence of three classes or castes—<a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">priests</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warrior" title="Warrior">warriors</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commoners" title="Commoners">commoners</a> (farmers or tradesmen)—corresponding to the three functions of the <a href="/wiki/Sacred" title="Sacred">sacral</a>, the <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">martial</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic" title="Economic">economic</a>, respectively. The trifunctional thesis is primarily associated with the <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mythography" title="Mythography">mythographer</a> <a href="/wiki/Georges_Dum%C3%A9zil" title="Georges Dumézil">Georges Dumézil</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> who proposed it in 1929 in the book <i>Flamen-Brahman</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flamen-Brahman_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flamen-Brahman-2">[2]</a></sup> and later in <i>Mitra-Varuna</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitra-Varuna_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mitra-Varuna-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Three-way_division">Three-way division</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trifunctional_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Three-way division">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Georges_Dum%C3%A9zil" title="Georges Dumézil">Dumézil</a> (1898-1986), <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_society" title="Proto-Indo-European society">Proto-Indo-European society</a> comprised three main groups corresponding to three distinct functions:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flamen-Brahman_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Flamen-Brahman-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitra-Varuna_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mitra-Varuna-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">Sovereignty</a>, which fell into two distinct and complementary sub-parts:</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>
<ul>
<li>one formal, juridical and priestly but worldly;</li>
<li>the other powerful, unpredictable, and also priestly but rooted in the supernatural world.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">Military</a>, connected with force, the <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> and <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">war</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">Productivity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pastoralism" title="Pastoralism">herding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">farming</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crafts" title="Crafts">crafts</a>; ruled by the other two.</li>
</ul>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_mythology" title="Proto-Indo-European mythology">Proto-Indo-European mythology</a> each <a href="/wiki/Social_group" title="Social group">social group</a> had its own <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">god</a> or family of gods to represent it and the function of the god or gods matched the function of the group. Many such divisions occur in the history of Indo-European societies:</p>
<ul>
<li>Southern Russia: <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Sergent" title="Bernard Sergent">Bernard Sergent</a> associates the <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European language family</a> with certain archaeological cultures in Southern <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and reconstructs an Indo-European religion based upon the tripartite functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Early Germanic society: The supposed division between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_king" title="Germanic king">king</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">nobility</a> and regular freemen in early <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic</a> society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>Norse mythology: <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a> (sovereignty), <a href="/wiki/T%C3%BDr" title="Týr">Týr</a> (law and justice), the <a href="/wiki/Vanir" title="Vanir">Vanir</a> (fertility).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETurville-Petre1964103_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETurville-Petre1964103-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPolom.C3.A9197058.E2.80.9459_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPolom.C3.A9197058.E2.80.9459-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[note 1]</a></sup> Odin has been interpreted as a death-god<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVries1970b93_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVries1970b93-10">[9]</a></sup> and connected to cremations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavidson1990147_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavidson1990147-11">[10]</a></sup> and has also been associated with ecstatic practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVries1970b94.E2.80.9497_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVries1970b94.E2.80.9497-12">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavidson1990147_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavidson1990147-11">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>Classic Greece: The three divisions of the ideal society as described by <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">Socrates</a> in <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Republic_(Plato)" title="The Republic (Plato)">The Republic</a></i>. Bernard Sergent examined the trifunctional hypothesis in Greek <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lyric_poetry" title="Lyric poetry">lyric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Verse_drama_and_dramatic_verse" title="Verse drama and dramatic verse">dramatic</a> poetry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>India: The three Hindu castes, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahmans" title="Brahmans">Brahmans</a> or priests; the <a href="/wiki/Kshatriya" title="Kshatriya">Kshatriya</a>, the warriors and military; and the <a href="/wiki/Vaishya" title="Vaishya">Vaishya</a>, the agriculturalists, cattle rearers and traders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Shudra" title="Shudra">Shudra</a>, a fourth Indian caste, is an "outer" or serf caste serving the other three. A 2001 study found that the genetic affinity of Indians to Europeans is proportionate to caste rank, the upper castes being most similar to Europeans whereas lower castes are more like Asians. The researchers believe that the Indo-European speakers entered India from the Northwest, mixing with or displacing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Dravidian" title="Proto-Dravidian">proto-Dravidian</a> speakers, and may have established a caste system with themselves primarily in higher castes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trifunctional_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg"><img alt="[icon]" data-file-height="31" data-file-width="44" height="14" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" width="20"/></a></td>
<td class="mbox-text"><span class="mbox-text-span"><b>This section needs expansion</b>. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Trifunctional_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small> <small><i>(April 2012)</i></small></span></td>

<p>Supporters of the hypothesis include scholars such as <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Benveniste" title="Émile Benveniste">Émile Benveniste</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Sergent" title="Bernard Sergent">Bernard Sergent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iaroslav_Lebedynsky" title="Iaroslav Lebedynsky">Iaroslav Lebedynsky</a>, who concludes that "the basic idea seems proven in a convincing way".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hypothesis was embraced outside the field of <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_studies" title="Indo-European studies">Indo-European studies</a> by some mythographers, anthropologists, and historians such as <a href="/wiki/Mircea_Eliade" title="Mircea Eliade">Mircea Eliade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Claude_L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="Claude Lévi-Strauss">Claude Lévi-Strauss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Sahlins" title="Marshall Sahlins">Marshall Sahlins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Needham" title="Rodney Needham">Rodney Needham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Vernant" title="Jean-Pierre Vernant">Jean-Pierre Vernant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Georges_Duby" title="Georges Duby">Georges Duby</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Theorizing_myth_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Theorizing_myth-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the other hand, Allen<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> concludes that the tripartite division may be an artefact, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Selection_effect" title="Selection effect">selection effect</a> rather than an organizing principle used in the societies themselves. Benjamin W. Fortson reports<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> a sense that Dumézil blurred the lines between the three functions and the examples that he gave often had contradictory characteristics, causing detractors<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> to reject his categories as non-existent. John Brough surmises that societal divisions are common outside of Indo-European societies as well, and consequently the hypothesis has only limited utility in illuminating prehistoric Indo-European society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> Cristiano Grottanelli states that while Dumézilian trifunctionalism may be seen in modern and medieval contexts, its projection onto earlier cultures is mistaken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> Belier is strongly critical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hypothesis has been criticised by historians <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Ginzburg" title="Carlo Ginzburg">Carlo Ginzburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arnaldo_Momigliano" title="Arnaldo Momigliano">Arnaldo Momigliano</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Lincoln" title="Bruce Lincoln">Bruce Lincoln</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aryan_idols_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-aryan_idols-25">[24]</a></sup> as being based on Dumézil's sympathies with the political right. Guy G. Stroumsa sees these criticisms as unfounded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
