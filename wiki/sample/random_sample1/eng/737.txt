<p><b>AC St. Louis</b> was an American professional <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">soccer</a> team based in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis, Missouri</a>, United States. Founded in December 2009, the team played its first and only season the next year in the NASL Conference of the temporary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSF_D2_Pro_League" title="USSF D2 Pro League">USSF D2 Pro League</a>, the second tier of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Soccer_Pyramid" title="American Soccer Pyramid">American Soccer Pyramid</a>. With plans to join the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American_Soccer_League_(2010)" title="North American Soccer League (2010)">North American Soccer League</a> the following season, the club folded in January 2011 under unmanageable financial strain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-insidemnsoccer1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-insidemnsoccer1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>St. Louis played its home games at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anheuser-Busch_Center" title="Anheuser-Busch Center">Anheuser-Busch Soccer Park</a> in nearby <a href="/wiki/Fenton,_Missouri" title="Fenton, Missouri">Fenton, Missouri</a>. The team's colors were green, white, and yellow. St. Louis was first coached by <a href="/wiki/Claude_Anelka" title="Claude Anelka">Claude Anelka</a>, the older brother of French international striker <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Anelka" title="Nicolas Anelka">Nicolas Anelka</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> however, he was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Dale_Schilly" title="Dale Schilly">Dale Schilly</a> midway through the club's only season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The men's team was one of three parts of the sports club owned by St. Louis-native Jeff Cooper. <a href="/wiki/Saint_Louis_Athletica" title="Saint Louis Athletica">Saint Louis Athletica</a> of <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_Professional_Soccer" title="Women's Professional Soccer">Women's Professional Soccer</a>, folding in 2010, and a large youth soccer league throughout the metropolitan area were the other components of AC St. Louis.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AC_St._Louis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origins of A.C. St. Louis lie in Jeff Cooper's bid to land a <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Soccer" title="Major League Soccer">Major League Soccer</a> <a href="/wiki/Expansion_team" title="Expansion team">expansion team</a>. With his investment group St. Louis Soccer United (SLSU), Cooper twice attempted to bring MLS to the <a href="/wiki/Greater_St._Louis" title="Greater St. Louis">St. Louis metropolitan area</a> only to have both bids turned down in favor of other cities in 2008 and 2009. Despite approved stadium plans to build the <a href="/wiki/Collinsville_Soccer_Complex" title="Collinsville Soccer Complex">Collinsville Soccer Complex</a> in suburban <a href="/wiki/Collinsville,_Illinois" title="Collinsville, Illinois">Collinsville, Illinois</a>, MLS was not impressed with the bid's financial backing and suggested Cooper expand his group of investors. As the price of the expansion fee rose, Cooper refocused his efforts to building a second division club.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="USSF_D2_Pro_League">USSF D2 Pro League</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AC_St._Louis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: USSF D2 Pro League">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ac03louie.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="566" data-file-width="543" height="208" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Ac03louie.jpg/200px-Ac03louie.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Ac03louie.jpg/300px-Ac03louie.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Ac03louie.jpg/400px-Ac03louie.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>

<p>This involved Cooper selling a majority interest to investors in the fall of 2009. By November SLSU changed its name to the Athletic Club of St. Louis with their new men's team AC St. Louis. Also announced was their intent to become co-founders of the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American_Soccer_League_(2010)" title="North American Soccer League (2010)">North American Soccer League</a> formed from teams breaking away from the established <a href="/wiki/United_Soccer_Leagues" title="United Soccer Leagues">United Soccer Leagues</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> After filed lawsuits and heated press statements between the two conflicting leagues, the USSF declared neither league would be sanctioned for the coming year, and ordered both to work together on a plan to temporarily allow their teams to play a 2010 season. On January 7, 2010, the interim solution was announced to be the new USSF D2 Professional League comprising clubs from both USL and NASL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2010, AC St. Louis announced that <a href="/wiki/Steve_Ralston" title="Steve Ralston">Steve Ralston</a> â MLS career leader in games played, minutes played, and assists â would become the first player ever to officially sign with the club.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The rest of the roster was primarily filled out with lower division signees and former college players.</p>
<p>After a successful launch in April, the club fell into financial trouble by May 2010 when the investors pulled out of funding both AC St. Louis and St. Louis Athletica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Despite Cooper resuming financial control of the teams, Athletica was unable to be saved. Other cost-cutting measures included selling Ralston's contract back to the <a href="/wiki/New_England_Revolution" title="New England Revolution">New England Revolution</a> and negotiating contract buyouts of players who did not reside in the St. Louis area. AC St. Louis finished the season second to last in both their conference and league.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>During the following off season, Cooper sought to sell the club to keep it financially afloat. However, despite his best efforts to secure the long-term future of the club,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> AC St. Louis folded in January 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-insidemnsoccer1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-insidemnsoccer1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Colors_and_badge">Colors and badge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AC_St._Louis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Colors and badge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The inverted shield features a <a href="/wiki/Fleur-de-lis" title="Fleur-de-lis">fleur-de-lis</a> with a golden flame beneath a <a href="/wiki/Catenary_arch" title="Catenary arch">catenary arch</a>. The fleur-de-lis, a long time symbol of the city featured heavily in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iconography_of_St._Louis,_Missouri" title="Iconography of St. Louis, Missouri">region's iconography</a>, was a symbol of French monarchs including <a href="/wiki/Louis_IX_of_France" title="Louis IX of France">Louis IX of France</a>, for whom the city is named. The flame alludes to the <a href="/wiki/1904_Summer_Olympics" title="1904 Summer Olympics">1904 Summer Olympics</a> which were held in St. Louis as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Louis_World%27s_Fair" title="St. Louis World's Fair">St. Louis World's Fair</a>, and is seen by many St. Louisans to be one of the formative events in the city's history. The arch represents the <a href="/wiki/Gateway_Arch" title="Gateway Arch">Gateway Arch</a>, which was built in 1965 as a memorial to the city's placement in American history as the "Gateway to the West." A.C. is an homage to the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_Athletic_Club" title="Missouri Athletic Club">Missouri Athletic Club</a>, which has promoted soccer in the region for more than 100 years.</p>
<p>On February 11, 2010 the team announced it had entered into an agreement with <a href="/wiki/Nike,_Inc." title="Nike, Inc.">Nike</a> upon a multi-year apparel and footwear sponsorship. The team's color scheme was green, gold, and white.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supporters">Supporters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AC_St._Louis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Supporters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The St. Louligans grew out of the multiple supporters groups already established in the St. Louis and surrounding areas. The fans united under this one banner while also keeping their individual identities as groups alive. They comprised the "Seven Nation Army" of the St. Louligans supporters movement, with those being Chickenhead, Day Pints Club, Eads Brigade, Henry Shaw Collective, Laclede's Army, River City Saints and United Knights.</p>
