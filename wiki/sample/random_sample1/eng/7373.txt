<p>The <b>Leeum, Samsung Museum of Art</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Museum" title="Museum">museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hannam-dong" title="Hannam-dong">Hannam-dong</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yongsan-gu" title="Yongsan-gu">Yongsan-gu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, run by the <a href="/wiki/Samsung" title="Samsung">Samsung</a> Foundation of Culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It comprises two parts that house traditional Korean art and contemporary art. Museum 1 is designed by Swiss architect <a href="/wiki/Mario_Botta" title="Mario Botta">Mario Botta</a> and Museum 2 is by French architect <a href="/wiki/Jean_Nouvel" title="Jean Nouvel">Jean Nouvel</a> and Dutch architect <a href="/wiki/Rem_Koolhaas" title="Rem Koolhaas">Rem Koolhaas</a> designed the Samsung Child Education &amp; Culture Center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collection">Collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leeum,_Samsung_Museum_of_Art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Museum 1</b>, designed by Mario Botta, houses a collection of traditional Korean art, of which 36 pieces are designated national treasures. Included in the collection are landscapes and folk paintings, traditional ceramics and porcelain, such as <a href="/wiki/Celadon" title="Celadon">Celadon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buncheong" title="Buncheong">Buncheong</a>, a bluish-green traditional Korean stoneware. As well as 14th century daggers, crowns, earrings and ornaments; and Buddhist art, sculptures, paintings and manuscripts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two large volumes, a reverse cone and a simple hexahedral shape, form Museum 1. Mario Botta utilized terra cotta bricks on the building’s façade which creates not only a strong presence on the grounds but also creates a chameleon like façade which changes colors, and plays with shadows, with the changing intensity and angle of sunlight. The shapes and materials create a modern interpretation of Seoul’s history as a walled city and reference the traditional ceramic art inside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-samsungfoundation1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-samsungfoundation1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The interior of Museum 1 includes a lobby, rotunda, and exhibition spaces. The core of the Leeum Museum is this basement lobby created by the reverse cone penetrating the ground. Museum 2 and the Samsung Child Education &amp; Culture Center are all connected here. Museum goers begin and end their tours in this area. Above the lobby is the white walled rotunda. Spiraling down like a helix, the rotunda delivers natural light into the basement lobby and exhibition spaces. The exhibition spaces in Museum 1 are hexahedral. Visitors begin at the top and walk down visiting each of the four floors which house ceramics, swords, jewelry and other traditional Korean artifacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-samsungfoundation1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-samsungfoundation1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Museum 2</b>, designed by Jean Nouvel, features modern and contemporary art from both Korean and foreign artists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Many famous artists such as <a href="/wiki/Damien_Hirst" title="Damien Hirst">Damien Hirst</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warhol" title="Warhol">Warhol</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rothko" title="Rothko">Rothko</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yves_Klein" title="Yves Klein">Yves Klein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Donald_Judd" title="Donald Judd">Donald Judd</a> have permanent exhibition spaces. The basement levels of the museum face the sunken garden and gabion walls. The gabion cages are composed of iron and filled with rocks unearthed during the construction process. The sunken garden includes birch trees and ferns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-samsungfoundation2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-samsungfoundation2-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Seemingly floating in place, Nouvel’s creation personifies modern art’s constant growth, evolution, and challenge/inquisition of society. Nouvel used extra white glass and rusted stainless-steel, the first to do so, to create the exhibition boxes. Many tests were performed before the current black-filmed stainless-steel could be created. The freely arranged exhibition boxes penetrate the glass skin and allow for a more controlled exhibition space. Light and other factors can be readily controlled for each box. The main exhibition hall in Museum 2 is a completely open space without any supporting posts thanks to post-tension building techniques.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-samsungfoundation2_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-samsungfoundation2-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rem Koolhaas was charged with designing the <b>Samsung Child Education &amp; Culture Center</b> as well as the museum’s master plan. The main entrance into the lobby of Museum 1 is through Koolhaas’s glass structure. As they enter the museum, visitors follow along on a wooden ramp and pass under a pavilion, demarcating the threshold. He used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilotis" title="Pilotis">pilotis</a> which not only lighten the structure but also provide a view of the surrounding buildings and site. The skin of the three story outer building is composed of steel and glass. Inside, a mixing chamber was created between the glass wall and Black box and the placement of lighting, piloti and ramps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The Black concrete box holds different exhibits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Located over the parking garage, the Sculpture Garden showcases sculptural exhibits. The garden consists of a long rectangular strip of gravel and vegetation and the wood deck which frames it. Recently two of <a href="/wiki/Louise_Bourgeois" title="Louise Bourgeois">Louise Bourgeois</a>’s <i><a href="/wiki/Maman_(sculpture)" title="Maman (sculpture)">Maman</a></i> sculptures were featured in the Sculpture Garden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
