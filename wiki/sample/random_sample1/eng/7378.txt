<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Triple bond</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>A <b>triple bond</b> in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bond</a> between two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atoms" title="Atoms">atoms</a> involving six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonding_electron" title="Bonding electron">bonding electrons</a> instead of the usual two in a <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent single bond</a>. The most common triple bond, that between two <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> atoms, can be found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkynes" title="Alkynes">alkynes</a>. Other <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functional groups</a> containing a triple bond are <a href="/wiki/Cyanide" title="Cyanide">cyanides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isocyanide" title="Isocyanide">isocyanides</a>. Some diatomic molecules, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dinitrogen" title="Dinitrogen">dinitrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> are also triple bonded. In <a href="/wiki/Skeletal_formula" title="Skeletal formula">skeletal formula</a> the triple bond is drawn as three parallel lines (≡) between the two connected atoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Triple bonds are stronger and shorter than the equivalent <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">single bonds</a> or <a href="/wiki/Double_bond" title="Double bond">double bonds</a>, with a <a href="/wiki/Bond_order" title="Bond order">bond order</a> of three.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bonding">Bonding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triple_bond&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Bonding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The types of bonding can be explained through in terms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_hybridization" title="Orbital hybridization">orbital hybridization</a>. In the case of acetylene each carbon atom has two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sp_orbital" title="Sp orbital">sp orbitals</a> and two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-orbital" title="P-orbital">p-orbitals</a>. The two sp orbitals are linear with 180° angles and occupy the x-axis (<a href="/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system" title="Cartesian coordinate system">cartesian coordinate system</a>). The p-orbitals are <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">perpendicular</a> on the y-axis and the z-axis. When the carbon atoms approach each other the sp orbitals overlap to form a sp-sp <a href="/wiki/Sigma_bond" title="Sigma bond">sigma bond</a>. At the same time the p<sub>z</sub>-orbitals approach and together they form a p<sub>z</sub>-p<sub>z</sub> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pi-bond" title="Pi-bond">pi-bond</a>. Likewise, the other pair of p<sub>y</sub>-orbitals form a p<sub>y</sub>-p<sub>y</sub> pi-bond. The result is formation of one sigma bond and two pi bonds.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Bent_bond" title="Bent bond">bent bond model</a> the triple bond can also formed by the overlapping of three sp<sup>3</sup> lobes without the need to invoke a pi-bond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
