<p>A <b>triple junction</b> is the point where the boundaries of three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">tectonic plates</a> meet. At the triple junction each of the three boundaries will be one of 3 types - a <a href="/wiki/Mid-ocean_ridge" title="Mid-ocean ridge">ridge</a> (R), <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_trench" title="Oceanic trench">trench</a> (T) or <a href="/wiki/Transform_fault" title="Transform fault">transform fault</a> (F) - and triple junctions can be described according to the types of plate margin that meet at them (e.g. Transform-Transform-Trench, Ridge-Ridge-Ridge, or abbreviated F-F-T, R-R-R). Of the many possible types of triple junction only a few are <a href="/wiki/Triple_junction_stability" title="Triple junction stability">stable</a> through time ('stable' in this context means that the geometrical configuration of the triple junction will not change through geologic time).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triple_junction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first scientific paper detailing the triple junction concept was published in 1969 by <a href="/wiki/W._Jason_Morgan" title="W. Jason Morgan">W. Jason Morgan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dan_McKenzie_(professor)" title="Dan McKenzie (professor)">Dan McKenzie</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tanya_Atwater" title="Tanya Atwater">Tanya Atwater</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The term had traditionally been used for the intersection of three divergent boundaries or spreading ridges. These three divergent boundaries ideally meet at near 120° angles.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a> theory during the breakup of a continent, three divergent boundaries form, radiating out from a central point (the triple junction). One of these divergent plate boundaries fails (see <a href="/wiki/Aulacogen" title="Aulacogen">aulacogen</a>) and the other two continue spreading to form an ocean. The <a href="/wiki/Seafloor_spreading" title="Seafloor spreading">opening</a> of the south <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> started at the south of the <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South American</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African</a> continents, reaching a triple junction in the present <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Guinea" title="Gulf of Guinea">Gulf of Guinea</a>, from where it continued to the west. The NE-trending <a href="/wiki/Benue_Trough" title="Benue Trough">Benue Trough</a> is the failed arm of this junction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Petters1978_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Petters1978-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the years since, the term triple-junction has come to refer to any point where three tectonic plates meet.</p>

<ul>
<li>The junction of the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Aden" title="Gulf of Aden">Gulf of Aden</a> and the <a href="/wiki/East_African_Rift" title="East African Rift">East African Rift</a> centered in the <a href="/wiki/Afar_Triangle" title="Afar Triangle">Afar Triangle</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Afar_Triple_Junction" title="Afar Triple Junction">Afar Triple Junction</a>) is the only Ridge-Ridge-Ridge (R-R-R) triple junction above sea level.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rodrigues_Triple_Point" title="Rodrigues Triple Point">Rodrigues Triple Point</a> is a R-R-R triple junction in the southern Indian Ocean, where the <a href="/wiki/African_Plate" title="African Plate">African</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Australian_Plate" title="Indo-Australian Plate">Indo-Australian</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Plate" title="Antarctic Plate">Antarctic</a> Plates meet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Galapagos_Triple_Junction" title="Galapagos Triple Junction">Galapagos Triple Junction</a> is an R-R-R triple junction where the <a href="/wiki/Nazca_Plate" title="Nazca Plate">Nazca</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cocos_Plate" title="Cocos Plate">Cocos</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Plate" title="Pacific Plate">Pacific Plates</a> meet. The <a href="/wiki/East_Pacific_Rise" title="East Pacific Rise">East Pacific Rise</a> extends north and south from this junction and the <a href="/wiki/Galapagos_Rise" title="Galapagos Rise">Galapagos Rise</a> goes to the east. This example is made more complex by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galapagos_Microplate" title="Galapagos Microplate">Galapagos Microplate</a> which is a small separate plate on the rise just to the southeast of the triple junction.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a> coast off Tapachula where <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a> join and small earthquakes occur weekly. This is pushed eastward by the Cocos plate.</li>
<li>On the west coast of North America is another unstable triple junction offshore of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Mendocino" title="Cape Mendocino">Cape Mendocino</a>. To the south, the <a href="/wiki/San_Andreas_Fault" title="San Andreas Fault">San Andreas Fault</a>, a strike-slip fault and transform plate boundary, separates the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Plate" title="Pacific Plate">Pacific Plate</a> and the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Plate" title="North American Plate">North American Plate</a>. To the north lies the <a href="/wiki/Cascadia_subduction_zone" title="Cascadia subduction zone">Cascadia subduction zone</a>, where a section of the <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Fuca_Plate" title="Juan de Fuca Plate">Juan de Fuca Plate</a> called the <a href="/wiki/Gorda_Plate" title="Gorda Plate">Gorda Plate</a> is being subducted under the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Plate" title="North American Plate">North American Plate</a>, forming a trench (T). Another transform fault, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mendocino_Fault" title="Mendocino Fault">Mendocino Fault</a> (F), runs along the boundary between the Pacific Plate and the Gorda Plate. Where the three intersect is the seismically active, F-F-T <a href="/wiki/Mendocino_Triple_Junction" title="Mendocino Triple Junction">Mendocino Triple Junction</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Amurian_Plate" title="Amurian Plate">Amurian Plate</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Okhotsk_Plate" title="Okhotsk Plate">Okhotsk Plate</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Sea_Plate" title="Philippine Sea Plate">Philippine Sea Plate</a> meet in Japan near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mt._Fuji" title="Mt. Fuji">Mt. Fuji</a>. (see <a href="/wiki/Mount_Fuji#Geology" title="Mount Fuji">Mount Fuji's Geology</a>)</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Azores_Triple_Junction" title="Azores Triple Junction">Azores Triple Junction</a> is a geologic triple junction where the boundaries of three tectonic plates intersect: the North American Plate, the Eurasian Plate and the African Plate, R-R-R.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Boso_Triple_Junction" title="Boso Triple Junction">Boso Triple Junction</a> offshore Japan is a T-T-T triple junction between the <a href="/wiki/Okhotsk_Plate" title="Okhotsk Plate">Okhotsk Plate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Plate" title="Pacific Plate">Pacific Plate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Sea_Plate" title="Philippine Sea Plate">Philippine Sea Plate</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> is located at the extinct triple junction of three former continental plates of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palaeozoic" title="Palaeozoic">Palaeozoic</a> era: <a href="/wiki/Avalonia" title="Avalonia">Avalonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laurentia" title="Laurentia">Laurentia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baltica" title="Baltica">Baltica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretation">Interpretation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triple_junction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Interpretation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The properties of triple junctions are most easily understood from the purely kinematic point of view where the plates are rigid and moving over the surface of the Earth. No knowledge of the Earth's interior or the geological details of the crust are then needed. Another useful simplification is that the kinematics of triple junctions on a flat Earth are essentially the same as those on the surface of a sphere. On a sphere, plate motions are described as relative rotations about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euler_pole" title="Euler pole">Euler Poles</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Plate_reconstruction" title="Plate reconstruction">Plate reconstruction</a>), and the relative motion at every point along a plate boundary can be calculated from this rotation. But the area around a triple junction is small enough (relative to the size of the sphere) and (usually) far enough from the pole of rotation, that the relative motion across a boundary can be assumed to be constant along that boundary. Thus, analysis of triple junctions can usually be done on a flat surface with motions defined by vectors.</p>
