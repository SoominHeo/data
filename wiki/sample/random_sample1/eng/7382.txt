<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Three-body problem</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">For the novel, see <a href="/wiki/The_Three-Body_Problem" title="The Three-Body Problem">The Three-Body Problem</a>.</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">classical mechanics</a>, the <b>three-body problem</b> is the problem of taking an initial set of data that specifies the positions, masses and velocities of three bodies for some particular point in time and then determining the motions of the three bodies, in accordance with <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion" title="Newton's laws of motion">Newton's laws of motion</a> and of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_gravitation" title="Universal gravitation">universal gravitation</a> which are the laws of classical mechanics. The three-body problem is a special case of the <a href="/wiki/N-body_problem" title="N-body problem"><i>n</i>-body problem</a>.</p>
<p>Historically, the first specific three-body problem to receive extended study was the one involving the Moon, the Earth and the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-first-1">[1]</a></sup> In an extended modern sense, a three-body problem is a class of problems in <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">classical</a> or <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> that model the motion of three particles.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-body_problem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The gravitational problem of three bodies in its traditional sense dates in substance from 1687, when <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a> published his "Principia" (<i><a href="/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica" title="Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica">Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica</a></i>). In Proposition 66 of Book 1 of the "Principia", and its 22 Corollaries, Newton took the first steps in the definition and study of the problem of the movements of three massive bodies subject to their mutually perturbing gravitational attractions. In Propositions 25 to 35 of Book 3, Newton also took the first steps in applying his results of Proposition 66 to the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_theory#Newton" title="Lunar theory">lunar theory</a>, the motion of the Moon under the gravitational influence of the Earth and the Sun.</p>
<p>The physical problem was addressed by <a href="/wiki/Amerigo_Vespucci" title="Amerigo Vespucci">Amerigo Vespucci</a> and subsequently by <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a>; in 1499 Vespucci used knowledge of the position of the moon to determine his position in Brazil. It became of technical importance in the 1720s, as an accurate solution would be applicable to navigation, specifically for the <a href="/wiki/History_of_longitude#Problem_of_longitude" title="History of longitude">determination of longitude at sea</a>, solved in practice by <a href="/wiki/John_Harrison" title="John Harrison">John Harrison</a>'s invention of the <a href="/wiki/Marine_chronometer" title="Marine chronometer">Marine chronometer</a>. However the accuracy of the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_theory" title="Lunar theory">lunar theory</a> was low, due to the perturbing effect of the Sun and planets on the motion of the Moon around the Earth.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d%27Alembert" title="Jean le Rond d'Alembert">Jean le Rond d'Alembert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexis_Clairaut" title="Alexis Clairaut">Alexis Clairaut</a>, who developed a longstanding rivalry, both attempted to analyze the problem in some degree of generality, and by the use of <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">differential equations</a> to be solved by successive approximations. They submitted their competing first analyses to the Académie Royale des Sciences in 1747.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was in connection with these researches, in Paris, in the 1740s, that the name "three-body problem" (Problème des Trois Corps) began to be commonly used. An account published in 1761 by Jean le Rond d'Alembert indicates that the name was first used in 1747.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1887, mathematicians <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Bruns" title="Heinrich Bruns">Heinrich Bruns</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9#The_three-body_problem" title="Henri Poincaré">Henri Poincaré</a> showed that there is no general analytical solution for the three-body problem given by algebraic expressions and integrals. The motion of three bodies is generally non-repeating, except in special cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13solutions_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-13solutions-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-body_problem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

The circular restricted three-body problem is a valid approximation of elliptical orbits found in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, and this can be visualized as a combination of the potentials due to the gravity of the two primary bodies along with the centrifugal effect from their rotation (Coriolis effects are dynamic and not shown). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lagrange_points" title="Lagrange points">Lagrange points</a> can then be seen as the five places where the gradient on the resultant surface is zero (shown as blue lines) indicating that the forces are in balance there.</div>
</div>
</div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gravitational_systems">Gravitational systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-body_problem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Gravitational systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A prominent example of the classical three-body problem is the movement of a planet with a satellite around a star. In most cases such a system can be <a href="/wiki/Factorization" title="Factorization">factorized</a>, considering the movement of the complex system (planet and satellite) around a star as a single particle; then, considering the movement of the satellite around the planet, neglecting the movement around the star. In this case, the problem is simplified to two instances of the <a href="/wiki/Two-body_problem" title="Two-body problem">two-body problem</a>. However, the effect of the star on the movement of the satellite around the planet can be considered as a <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_theory" title="Perturbation theory">perturbation</a>.</p>
<p>A three-body problem also arises from the situation of a <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> and two relevant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_bodies" title="Celestial bodies">celestial bodies</a>, e.g. the Earth and the Moon, such as when considering a <a href="/wiki/Free_return_trajectory" title="Free return trajectory">free return trajectory</a> around the Moon, or other <a href="/wiki/Trans-lunar_injection" title="Trans-lunar injection">trans-lunar injection</a>. While a spaceflight involving a <a href="/wiki/Gravity_assist" title="Gravity assist">gravity assist</a> tends to be at least a four-body problem (spacecraft, Earth, Sun, Moon), once far away from the Earth when Earth's gravity becomes negligible, it is approximately a three-body problem.</p>

<p>A complete solution for a particular three-body problem provides the positions for all three particles for all time given three initial positions and initial velocities.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Circular_restricted_three-body_problem">Circular restricted three-body problem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-body_problem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Circular restricted three-body problem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the circular restricted three-body problem, two massive bodies move in circular <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbits</a> around their common <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">center of mass</a>, and the third mass is negligible with respect to the other two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> With respect to a <a href="/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame" title="Rotating reference frame">rotating reference frame</a>, the two co-orbiting bodies are stationary, and the third can be stationary as well at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lagrangian_points" title="Lagrangian points">Lagrangian points</a>, or orbit around them, for instance on a <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe_orbit" title="Horseshoe orbit">horseshoe orbit</a>. It can be useful to consider the <a href="/wiki/Effective_potential" title="Effective potential">effective potential</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Periodic_solutions">Periodic solutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-body_problem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Periodic solutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1767 <a href="/wiki/Leonhard_Euler" title="Leonhard Euler">Leonhard Euler</a> found three families of periodic solutions in which the three masses are collinear at each instant. In 1772 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Louis_Lagrange" title="Joseph Louis Lagrange">Lagrange</a> found a family of solutions in which the three masses form an equilateral triangle at each instant. Together, these solutions form the <i>central configurations</i> for the three-body problem. These solutions are valid for any mass ratios, and the masses move on Keplerian ellipses. These five families are the only known solutions for which there are explicit analytic formulae. In the special case of the circular restricted three-body problem, these solutions, viewed in a frame rotating with the primaries, become points which are referred to as L1, L2, L3, L4, and L5 and called <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">Lagrangian points</a> .</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Additional_solutions">Additional solutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-body_problem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Additional solutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>In work summarized in 1892-1899, <a href="/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9" title="Henri Poincaré">Henri Poincaré</a> established the existence of an infinite number of periodic solutions to the restricted three-body problem, and established techniques for continuing these solutions into the honest three-body problem.</p>
<p>In 1893 Meissel stated what is nowadays called the Pythagorean three-body problem: three masses in the ratio 3:4:5 are placed at rest at the vertices of a 3:4:5 triangle. Burrau <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burrau_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burrau-9">[9]</a></sup> further investigated this problem in 1913. In 1967 <a href="/wiki/Victor_Szebehely" title="Victor Szebehely">Victor Szebehely</a> and coworkers established eventual escape for this problem using numerical integration, while at the same time finding a nearby periodic solution.</p>
<p>In 1911, United States scientist <a href="/wiki/William_Duncan_MacMillan" title="William Duncan MacMillan">William Duncan MacMillan</a> found one special solution. In 1961, Russian mathematician Sitnikov improved this solution. See <a href="/wiki/Sitnikov_problem" title="Sitnikov problem">Sitnikov problem</a>.</p>
<p>In the 1970s <a href="/wiki/Michel_H%C3%A9non" title="Michel Hénon">Michel Hénon</a> and R. Broucke each found a set of solutions which form part of the same family of solutions: the Broucke-Henon-Hadjidemetriou family. In this family the three objects all have the same mass and can exhibit both retrograde and direct forms. In some of Broucke solutions two of the bodies follow the same path.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TBG_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-TBG-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1993 a solution with three equal masses moving around a figure-eight shape was discovered by physicist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cris_Moore" title="Cris Moore">Cris Moore</a> at the Santa Fe Institute. This solution has zero total angular momentum.</p>
<p>In 2013, physicists Milovan Šuvakov and Veljko Dmitrašinović at the Institute of Physics in Belgrade discovered 13 new families of solutions for the equal mass zero angular momentum three body problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13solutions_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-13solutions-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TBG_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-TBG-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_versus_quantum_mechanics">Classical versus quantum mechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-body_problem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Classical versus quantum mechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Physicist <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Krivchenkov" title="Vladimir Krivchenkov">Vladimir Krivchenkov</a> used the three-body problem as an example, showing the simplicity of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> in comparison to <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">classical mechanics</a>. The quantum three-body problem is studied in university courses of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gold_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-gold-11">[11]</a></sup> For a special case of the quantum three-body problem known as the <a href="/wiki/Dihydrogen_cation" title="Dihydrogen cation">hydrogen molecular ion</a>, the energies are solvable analytically (see discussion in quantum mechanical version of <a href="/wiki/Euler%27s_three-body_problem#Quantum_mechanical_version" title="Euler's three-body problem">Euler's three-body problem</a>) in terms of a <i>generalization</i> of the <a href="/wiki/Lambert_W_function" title="Lambert W function">Lambert W function</a>.</p>
<p>However this is possible only by taking certain assumptions which basically reduce the problem to a single-body problem within an energy potential. Generally even a <a href="/wiki/Two-body_problem" title="Two-body problem">two-body problem</a> is not solvable analytically in quantum mechanics, since there is usually no analytical solution to the multi-particle <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation" title="Schrödinger equation">Schrödinger partial differential equation</a>. Some mathematical research in quantum mechanics is still dedicated either to finding a good numerical solution <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> or finding ways to reduce the problem into a more simple system that can be solved analytically such as the <a href="/wiki/Hartree%E2%80%93Fock_method" title="Hartree–Fock method">Hartree–Fock method</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Franck%E2%80%93Condon_principle" title="Franck–Condon principle">Franck–Condon principle</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sundman.27s_theorem">Sundman's theorem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-body_problem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sundman's theorem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1912, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish-speaking_Finns" title="Swedish-speaking Finns">Finnish</a> mathematician <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karl_Fritiof_Sundman" title="Karl Fritiof Sundman">Karl Fritiof Sundman</a> proved there exists a series solution in powers of <i>t</i><sup>1/3</sup> for the 3-body problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This series is convergent for all real <i>t</i>, except initial data that correspond to zero angular momentum. However, these initial data are not generic since they have <a href="/wiki/Lebesgue_measure" title="Lebesgue measure">Lebesgue measure</a> zero.</p>
<p>An important issue in proving this result is the fact that the radius of convergence for this series is determined by the distance to the nearest singularity. Therefore, it is necessary to study the possible singularities of the 3-body problems. As it will be briefly discussed below, the only singularities in the 3-body problem are binary collisions (collisions between two particles at an instant), and triple collisions (collisions between three particles at an instant).</p>
<p>Now collisions, whether binary or triple (in fact any number), are somewhat improbable—since it has been shown they correspond to a set of initial data of measure zero. However, there is no criterion known to be put on the initial state in order to avoid collisions for the corresponding solution. So, Sundman's strategy consisted of the following steps:</p>

<li>Using an appropriate change of variables, to continue analyzing the solution beyond the binary collision, in a process known as <a href="/wiki/Regularization_(physics)" title="Regularization (physics)">regularization</a>.</li>
<li>Prove that triple collisions only occur when the angular momentum <b>L</b> vanishes. By restricting the initial data to <b>L</b> ≠ <b>0</b> he removed all <i>real</i> singularities from the transformed equations for the 3-body problem.</li>
<li>Showing that if <b>L</b> ≠ <b>0</b>, then not only can there be no triple collision, but the system is strictly bounded away from a triple collision. This implies, by using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cauchy" title="Cauchy">Cauchy</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Existence_theorem" title="Existence theorem">existence theorem</a> for differential equations, there are no complex singularities in a strip (depending on the value of <b>L</b>) in the complex plane centered around the real axis (shades of Kovalevskaya).</li>
<li>Find a conformal transformation that maps this strip into the unit disc. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="n-body_problem"><i>n</i>-body problem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-body_problem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: n-body problem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The three-body problem is a special case of the <a href="/wiki/N-body_problem" title="N-body problem"><i>n</i>-body problem</a>, which describes how <i>n</i> objects will move under one of the physical forces, such as gravity. These problems have a global analytical solution in the form of a convergent power series, as was proven by <a href="/wiki/Karl_F._Sundman" title="Karl F. Sundman">Sundman</a> for <i>n</i> = 3 and by <a href="/wiki/Qiudong_Wang" title="Qiudong Wang">Wang</a> for <i>n</i> &gt; 3 (see <a href="/wiki/N-body_problem" title="N-body problem"><i>n</i>-body problem</a> for details). However, the Sundman and Wang series converge so slowly that they are useless for practical purposes;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> therefore, it is currently necessary to approximate solutions by <a href="/wiki/Numerical_analysis" title="Numerical analysis">numerical analysis</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Numerical_integration" title="Numerical integration">numerical integration</a> or, for some cases, classical <a href="/wiki/Trigonometric_series" title="Trigonometric series">trigonometric series</a> approximations (see <a href="/wiki/N-body_simulation" title="N-body simulation"><i>n</i>-body simulation</a>). Atomic systems, e.g. atoms, ions, and molecules, can be treated in terms of the quantum <i>n</i>-body problem. Among classical physical systems, the <i>n</i>-body problem usually refers to a <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxy</a> or to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cluster_of_galaxies" title="Cluster of galaxies">cluster of galaxies</a>; planetary systems, such as star(s), planets, and their satellites, can also be treated as <i>n</i>-body systems. Some applications are conveniently treated by <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">perturbation</a> theory, in which the system is considered as a two-body problem plus additional forces causing deviations from a hypothetical unperturbed two-body trajectory.</p>
