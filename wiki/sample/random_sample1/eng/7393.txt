<p><i><b>Respondeat superior</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>: "let the master answer"; plural: <i>respondeant superiores</i>) is a doctrine that a party is responsible for (has <a href="/wiki/Vicarious_liability" title="Vicarious liability">vicarious liability</a> for) acts of their agents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CL_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-CL-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:794</sup> For example, in the United States, there are circumstances when an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Employer" title="Employer">employer</a> is liable for acts of employees performed within the course of their employment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DFS_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-DFS-2">[2]</a></sup> This rule is also called the <b>master-servant rule</b>, recognized in both <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil law</a> jurisdictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Owen_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Owen-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a broader scope, <i>respondeat superior</i> is based upon the concept of <a href="/wiki/Vicarious_liability" title="Vicarious liability">vicarious liability</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_common_law">In common law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Respondeat_superior&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In common law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When applied to physical <a href="/wiki/Tort" title="Tort">torts</a>, an employer-employee relationship must be established (meaning that no vicarious liability is established for work performed as an <a href="/wiki/Independent_contractor" title="Independent contractor">independent contractor</a>) and the act must be committed within the scope of employment (i.e., substantially within time and geographical limits, job description and at least with partial intent to further employer's business).</p>
<p>Historically, this doctrine was applied in master-servant and employer-employee relationships. When an employee or a servant commits a <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(common_law)" title="Civil law (common law)">civil wrong</a> against a third party, the employer or master could be liable for the acts of the servant or employee when those acts are committed within the scope of the relationship. The third party could proceed against the servant and master, that is, the employee and employer. The action against the employee would be based on his conduct. The action against the employer is based on the theory of <a href="/wiki/Vicarious_liability" title="Vicarious liability">vicarious liability</a>, wherein a party can be held liable for the acts of a different party.</p>
<p>The employer-employee relationship is the most common area respondeat superior is applied, but the doctrine is also used in the agency relationship. In this relationship, the principal becomes liable for the actions of the agent even if the principal did not commit the act. There are three considerations generally:</p>
<ol>
<li>Was the act committed within the time and space limits of the agency?</li>
<li>Was the offense incidental to, or of the same general nature as, the responsibilities the agent is authorized to perform?</li>
<li>Was the agent motivated to any degree to benefit the principal by committing the act?</li>
</ol>
<p>The degree to which these are answered in the affirmative dictates the degree to which the doctrine can be applied.</p>
<p>Common law distinguishes between civil and criminal forms of respondeat superior.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_international_law">In international law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Respondeat_superior&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In international law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At issue in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Trials" title="Nuremberg Trials">Nuremberg war crimes tribunal</a> following the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied</a> occupation of <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> was a question concerning principles closely related to respondeat superior, which came to be known by the term <a href="/wiki/Command_responsibility" title="Command responsibility">command responsibility</a>. The Nuremberg trials established that persons cannot use the defense that they were only following the orders of their superiors, if that order violates international norms but especially that superiors who ordered or "should have known" of such violations yet failed to intervene are also criminally liable.</p>
