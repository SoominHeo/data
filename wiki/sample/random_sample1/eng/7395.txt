<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electromechanics">Electromechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativistic_electromagnetism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Electromechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After Maxwell proposed the differential equation model of the electromagnetic field in 1873, the mechanism of action of fields came into question, for instance in the <a href="/wiki/Master_class#Kelvin_in_Baltimore" title="Master class">Kelvin’s master class</a> held at <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Johns Hopkins University">Johns Hopkins University</a> in 1884 and commemorated a century later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The requirement that the equations remain consistent when viewed from various moving observers led to <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a>, a geometric theory of 4-space where intermediation is by light and radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">spacetime</a> geometry provided a context for technical description of electric technology, especially generators, motors, and lighting at first. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coulomb_force" title="Coulomb force">Coulomb force</a> was generalized to the <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_force" title="Lorentz force">Lorentz force</a>. For example, with this model <a href="/wiki/Transmission_line" title="Transmission line">transmission lines</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_grid" title="Power grid">power grids</a> were developed and <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">radio frequency</a> communication explored.</p>
<p>An effort to mount a full-fledged electromechanics on a relativistic basis is seen in the work of <a href="/wiki/Leigh_Page" title="Leigh Page">Leigh Page</a>, from the project outline in 1912<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> to his <a href="/wiki/Textbook" title="Textbook">textbook</a> <i>Electrodynamics</i> (1940)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The interplay (according to the differential equations) of electric and magnetic field as viewed over moving observers is examined. What is <a href="/wiki/Charge_density" title="Charge density">charge density</a> in <a href="/wiki/Electrostatics" title="Electrostatics">electrostatics</a> becomes <b>proper charge density</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and generates a magnetic field for a moving observer.</p>
<p>A revival of interest in this method for <a href="/wiki/Education_and_training_of_electrical_and_electronics_engineers" title="Education and training of electrical and electronics engineers">education and training of electrical and electronics engineers</a> broke out in the 1960s after <a href="/wiki/Richard_Feynman" title="Richard Feynman">Richard Feynman</a>’s textbook.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Rosser’s book <i>Classical Electromagnetism via Relativity</i> was popular,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> as was <a href="/wiki/Anthony_French" title="Anthony French">Anthony French</a>’s treatment in his textbook<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> which illustrated diagrammatically the proper charge density. One author proclaimed, "Maxwell — Out of Newton, Coulomb and Einstein".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The use of <a href="/wiki/Retarded_potential" title="Retarded potential">retarded potentials</a> to describe electromagnetic fields from source-charges is an expression of relativistic electromagnetism.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principle">Principle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relativistic_electromagnetism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Principle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The question of how an electric field in one <a href="/wiki/Inertial_frame_of_reference" title="Inertial frame of reference">inertial frame of reference</a> looks in different reference frame moving with respect to the first is crucial to understanding fields created by moving sources. In the special case, the sources that create the field are at rest with respect to one of the reference frames. Given the <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric field</a> in the frame where the sources are at rest, one can ask: what is the electric field in some other frame?<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Knowing the electric field at some point (in space and time) in the rest frame of the sources, and knowing the relative <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a> of the two frames provided all the information needed to calculate the electric field at the same point in the other frame. In other words, the electric field in the other frame does not depend on the particular distribution of the source <a href="/wiki/Charge_(physics)" title="Charge (physics)">charges</a>, only on the local value of the electric field in the first frame at that point. Thus, the electric field is a <b>complete</b> representation of the influence of the far-away charges.</p>
<p>Alternatively, introductory treatments of <a href="/wiki/Magnetism" title="Magnetism">magnetism</a> introduce the <a href="/wiki/Biot%E2%80%93Savart_law" title="Biot–Savart law">Biot–Savart law</a>, which describes the magnetic field associated with an <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a>. An observer at rest with respect to a system of static, free charges will see no magnetic field. However, a moving observer looking at the same set of charges does perceive a current, and thus a magnetic field. That is, the magnetic field is simply the electric field, as seen in a moving coordinate system.</p>
