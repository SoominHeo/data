<p>A <b>kinglet</b>, or <b>crest</b>, is a small <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in a group that is sometimes included in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">Old World warblers</a>, but is frequently placed in its own <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a>, <b>Regulidae</b>, because of resemblance to <a href="/wiki/Baeolophus" title="Baeolophus">titmice</a>. "Regulidae" is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word <i>regulus</i> for "petty king" or prince, and refers to the coloured crowns of adult birds. This family has representatives in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>. There are seven species in this family; one, the <a href="/wiki/Madeira_firecrest" title="Madeira firecrest">Madeira firecrest</a>, <i>Regulus madeirensis</i>, was only recently split from <a href="/wiki/Common_firecrest" title="Common firecrest">common firecrest</a> as a separate species. One species, the <a href="/wiki/Ruby-crowned_kinglet" title="Ruby-crowned kinglet">ruby-crowned kinglet</a>, differs sufficiently in its voice and plumage to occasionally be afforded its own genus, <i>Corthylio</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinglet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kinglets are among the smallest of all <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerines</a>, ranging in size from 8–11 cm (3–4.5 in) and weighing 6–8 g (0.21–0.28 oz); the sexes are the same size. They have medium-length wings and tails, and small needle-like <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bills</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> is overall grey-green, offset by pale wingbars, and the tail tip is <a href="/wiki/Incised#Biology" title="Incised">incised</a>. Five species have a single stiff feather covering the nostrils, but in the ruby-crowned kinglet, this is replaced by several short, stiff bristles. Most kinglets have distinctive head markings, and the males possess a colourful crown patch. In the females, the crown is duller and yellower. The long feathers forming the central crown stripe can be erected; they are inconspicuous most of the time, but are used in courtship and territorial displays when the raised crest is very striking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hbw_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are two species in North America with largely overlapping distributions, and two in Eurasia which also have a considerable shared range. In each continent, one species (goldcrest in Eurasia and golden-crowned kinglet in North America) is a conifer specialist; these have deeply grooved pads on their feet for perching on conifer twigs, and a long hind toe and claw for clinging vertically. The two generalists, ruby-crowned kinglet and common firecrest hunt more in flight, and have smoother soles, shorter hind claws and a longer tail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hbw_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinglet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The kinglets are a small group of birds sometimes included in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">Old World warblers</a>, but frequently given family status,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BB852_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BB852-2">[2]</a></sup> especially as recent research showed that, despite superficial similarities, the crests are taxonomically remote from the warblers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barker_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barker-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spicer_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spicer-4">[4]</a></sup> The names of the family, Regulidae, and its only genus, <i>Regulus</i>, are derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>regulus</i>, a diminutive of <i>rex</i>, "a king",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chambers_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers-5">[5]</a></sup> and refer to the characteristic orange or yellow crests of adult kinglets. The kinglets were allocated to the warbler genus <i><a href="/wiki/Typical_warbler" title="Typical warbler">Sylvia</a></i> by English <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">naturalist</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Latham_(ornithologist)" title="John Latham (ornithologist)">John Latham</a> in 1790,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latham_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-latham-6">[6]</a></sup> but moved to their current genus by French <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Georges_Cuvier" title="Georges Cuvier">Georges Cuvier</a> in 1800.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cuvier_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-cuvier-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several forms have only recently had their status clarified. The <a href="/wiki/Madeira_firecrest" title="Madeira firecrest">Madeira firecrest</a> was formerly considered to be a subspecies, <i>R. i. madeirensis</i>, of the <a href="/wiki/Common_firecrest" title="Common firecrest">common firecrest</a> <i>R. ignicapillus</i>. A <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">phylogenetic</a> analysis based on the <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome b</a> <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">gene</a> showed that the Madeiran form is distinct at the species level from the firecrest <a href="/wiki/Subspecies#Nomenclature" title="Subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> <i>R. i. ignicapillus</i>. Cytochrome <i>b</i> gene divergence between the Madeira firecrest and the European bird is 8.5%, comparable with the divergence level between other recognised <i>Regulus</i> species, such as the 9% between the <a href="/wiki/Goldcrest" title="Goldcrest">goldcrest</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Golden-crowned_kinglet" title="Golden-crowned kinglet">golden-crowned kinglet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hbw_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-1">[1]</a></sup> The split was accepted by the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_European_Rarities_Committees" title="Association of European Rarities Committees">Association of European Rarities Committees</a> (AERC) in 2003,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aerc_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-aerc-8">[8]</a></sup> but some authorities, like Clements, have not yet recognised the new species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clements_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clements-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Golden-crowned_kinglet" title="Golden-crowned kinglet">golden-crowned kinglet</a> is similar in appearance to the common firecrest and has been considered to be its New World equivalent, but it is actually closer to the <a href="/wiki/Goldcrest" title="Goldcrest">goldcrest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hbw_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Goldcrests from the Canary Islands are particularly distinctive having a black forehead, pink-buff underparts and a darker closed wing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mullarney_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-10">[10]</a></sup> and have been sometimes treated either as a subspecies of the common firecrest or as a different <i>Regulus</i> species altogether.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BB89_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-BB89-11">[11]</a></sup> They were sometimes called the <a href="/wiki/Tenerife_goldcrest" title="Tenerife goldcrest">Tenerife goldcrest</a>, no matter which of the islands they lived on; however, a 2006 study of the vocalisations of these birds indicate that they actually comprise two subspecies of the Goldcrest that are separable on voice; <i>R. r. teneriffae</i> occurring on Tenerife and the newly described subspecies, <i>R. r. ellenthalerae</i>, occurring on the smaller islands of La Palma and El Hierro.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pack2006_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-pack2006-12">[12]</a></sup> The three goldcrest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxa" title="Taxa">taxa</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_goldcrest" title="Santa Maria goldcrest">Santa Maria goldcrest</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sao_Miguel_goldcrest" title="Sao Miguel goldcrest">Sao Miguel goldcrest</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Azores_goldcrest" title="Western Azores goldcrest">Western Azores goldcrest</a>, represent recent colonisations from Europe, and are best treated as subspecies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The relationships of the <a href="/wiki/Flamecrest" title="Flamecrest">flamecrest</a> or Taiwan firecrest (<i>Regulus goodfellowi</i>) of Taiwan have also been a source of much debate. It is sometimes viewed as a race of firecrest, but its territorial song resembles those of the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayan</a> races of goldcrest, and genetic data show that it is the closest relative of that species, and, despite its alternative name, only distantly related to the firecrest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flamecrest_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-flamecrest-14">[14]</a></sup> The flamecrest diverged from the Goldcrest 3.0–3.1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mya_(unit)" title="Mya (unit)">mya</a> (million years ago).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erfurt_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-erfurt-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most members of the <i>Regulus</i> genus are similar in size and colour pattern. The exception is the <a href="/wiki/Ruby-crowned_kinglet" title="Ruby-crowned kinglet">ruby-crowned kinglet</a>, the largest species, which has a strongly red crest and no black crown stripes. It has distinctive vocalisations, and is different enough from the Old World kinglets and the other American species, the golden-crowned kinglet, to be sometimes assigned to a separate genus, <i>Corthylio</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hbw_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossils">Fossils</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinglet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fossils">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are a few Pleistocene (2.6 million to 12,000 years <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">BP</a>) records from Europe of extant <i>Regulus</i> species, mostly goldcrests or unidentifiable to species. The only fossil of an extinct <i>Regulus</i> is a left <a href="/wiki/Ulna" title="Ulna">ulna</a> from 2.6–1.95 mya in Bulgaria, which was identified as belonging to an extinct species, <i><a href="/wiki/Regulus_bulgaricus" title="Regulus bulgaricus">Regulus bulgaricus</a></i>. The goldcrest lineage diverged from this apparent ancestor of the common firecrest in the Middle Pleistocene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boev_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boev-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinglet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kinglets are birds of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nearctic_ecozone" title="Nearctic ecozone">Nearctic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palearctic_ecozone" title="Palearctic ecozone">Palearctic ecozones</a>, with representatives in temperate North America, Europe and Asia, northernmost Africa, <a href="/wiki/Macaronesia" title="Macaronesia">Macaronesia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>. They are adapted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer">conifer</a> forests, although there is a certain amount of adaptability and most species will use other habitats, particularly during migration. In Macaronesia, they are adapted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laurisilva" title="Laurisilva">laurisilva</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erica_arborea" title="Erica arborea">tree heaths</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hbw_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinglet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet_and_feeding">Diet and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinglet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Diet and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The tiny size and rapid metabolism of kinglets means that they must constantly forage in order to provide their energy needs. They will continue feeding even when <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a> building. Kinglets prevented from feeding may lose a third of their body weight in twenty minutes and may starve to death in an hour. Kinglets are insectivores, preferentially feeding on insects such as <a href="/wiki/Aphid" title="Aphid">aphids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Springtail" title="Springtail">springtails</a> that have soft cuticles. Prey is generally <a href="/wiki/Gleaning_(birds)" title="Gleaning (birds)">gleaned</a> from the branches and leaves of trees, although in some circumstances prey may be taken on the wing or from the leaf litter on the ground.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle">Life cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinglet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Life cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The nest are small, very neat cups, almost spherical in shape, made of moss and lichen held together with spiderwebs and hung from twigs near the end of a high branch of a conifer. They are lined with hair and feathers, and a few feathers are placed over the opening. These characteristics provide good insulation against the cold environment. The female lays 7 to 12 eggs, which are white or pale buff, some having fine dark brown spots. Because the nest is small, they are stacked in layers. The female incubates; she pushes her legs (which are well supplied with blood vessels, hence warm) down among the eggs. A unique feature of kinglets is the "size hierarchy" among eggs, with early-laid eggs being smaller than later ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eggs hatch asynchronously after 15 to 17 days. The young stay in the nest for 19 to 24 days. After being fed, nestlings make their way down to the bottom of the nest, pushing their still-hungry siblings up to be fed in their turn (but also to be cold).</p>
<p>Kinglets are the most fecund and shortest-living of all altricial birds,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and probably the shortest-lived apart from a few smaller <a href="/wiki/Galliformes" title="Galliformes">galliform</a> species. Adult mortality for the goldcrest is estimated at over 80 percent per year<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and the maximum lifespan is only six years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinglet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The four continental <i>Regulus</i> species all have very large ranges and populations. The two single-island endemics are common within their habitat, and are not thought to be at risk. All kinglets are therefore classed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BirdLife_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLife-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
