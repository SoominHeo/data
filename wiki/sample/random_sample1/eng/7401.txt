<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Pervasive developmental disorder not otherwise specified</h1>

<p>A <b>pervasive developmental disorder not otherwise specified</b> (<b>PDD-NOS</b>) is one of the three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autism_spectrum_disorders" title="Autism spectrum disorders">autism spectrum disorders</a> (ASD)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-1">[1]</a></sup> and also one of the five disorders classified as a <a href="/wiki/Pervasive_developmental_disorder" title="Pervasive developmental disorder">pervasive developmental disorder</a> (PDD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-factsheet_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-factsheet-2">[2]</a></sup> According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DSM-IV" title="DSM-IV">DSM-IV</a>, PDD-NOS is a diagnosis that is used for "severe and pervasive impairment in the development of reciprocal social interaction or verbal and nonverbal communication skills, or when stereotyped behavior, interests, and activities are present, but the criteria are not met for a specific PDD" or for several other disorders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-firstsigns_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-firstsigns-3">[3]</a></sup> PDD-NOS is often called <b>atypical autism</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-4">[4]</a></sup> because the criteria for autistic disorder are not met, for instance because of late age of onset, atypical symptomatology, or subthreshold symptomatology, or all of these.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-firstsigns_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-firstsigns-3">[3]</a></sup> Even though PDD-NOS is considered milder than typical <a href="/wiki/Autism" title="Autism">autism</a>, this is not always true. While some characteristics may be milder, others may be more severe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">Signs and symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pervasive_developmental_disorder_not_otherwise_specified&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Signs and symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is common for individuals with PDD-NOS to have more intact social skills and a lower level of intellectual deficit than individuals with other PDDs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-factsheet_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-factsheet-2">[2]</a></sup> Characteristics of many individuals with PDD-NOS are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Communication difficulties (e.g., using and understanding language)</li>
<li>Difficulty with social behavior</li>
<li>Difficulty with changes in routines or environments</li>
<li>Uneven skill development (strengths in some areas and delays in others)</li>
<li>Unusual play with toys and other objects</li>
<li>Repetitive body movements or behavior patterns</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">Diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pervasive_developmental_disorder_not_otherwise_specified&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>PDD-NOS is an old diagnostic category. It is no longer included as an option for an Autism Spectrum Disorder and is not part of the <a href="/wiki/DSM-5" title="DSM-5">DSM-5</a>, but is included in the <a href="/wiki/ICD-10" title="ICD-10">ICD-10</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SmithReichow2015_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-SmithReichow2015-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The diagnosis of a pervasive developmental disorder not otherwise specified is given to individuals with difficulties in the areas of social interaction, communication, and/or stereotyped behavior patterns or interests, but who do not meet the full DSM-IV criteria for autism or another PDD. This does not necessarily mean that PDD-NOS is a milder disability than the other PDDs. It only means that individuals who receive this diagnosis do not meet the diagnostic criteria of the other PDDs, but that there is still a pervasive developmental disorder that affects the individual in the areas of communication, socialization and behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-factsheet_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-factsheet-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>As for the other pervasive developmental disorders, diagnosis of PDD-NOS requires the involvement of a team of specialists. The individual needs to undergo a full diagnostic evaluation, including a thorough medical, social, adaptive, motor skills and communication history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-factsheet_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-factsheet-2">[2]</a></sup> Other parts of an assessment can be behavioral rating scales, direct behavioral observations, psychological assessment, educational assessment, communication assessment, and occupational assessment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Description of PDD-NOS merely as a "subthreshold" category without a more specific case definition poses methodological problems for research regarding the relatively heterogeneous group of people who receive this diagnosis. However, it appears that children with PDD-NOS show fewer intellectual deficits than autistic children, and that they may come to professional attention at a later age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subgroups">Subgroups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pervasive_developmental_disorder_not_otherwise_specified&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Subgroups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Studies suggest that persons with PDD-NOS belong to one of three very different subgroups:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walker-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>A high-functioning group (around 25 percent) whose symptoms largely overlap with that of <a href="/wiki/Asperger_syndrome" title="Asperger syndrome">Asperger syndrome</a>, but who differ in terms of having a lag in language development and/or mild cognitive impairment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Walker-9">[9]</a></sup> (The criteria for Asperger syndrome excludes a speech delay or a cognitive impairment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BehaveNet_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-BehaveNet-10">[10]</a></sup>)</li>
<li>A group (around 25 percent) whose symptoms more closely resemble those of autistic disorder, but do not fully meet all its diagnostic signs and symptoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Walker-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>The biggest group (around 50 percent) consists of those who meet all the diagnostic criteria for autistic disorder, but whose stereotypical and repetitive behaviors are noticeably mild.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Walker-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pervasive_developmental_disorder_not_otherwise_specified&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is no known "cure" for PDD-NOS, but there are interventions that can have a positive influence. Early and intensive implementation of evidence-based practices and interventions are generally believed to improve outcomes. Most of these are individualized special education strategies rather than medical or pharmaceutical treatment; the best outcomes are achieved when a team approach among supporting individuals is utilized.</p>
<p>Some of the more common therapies and services include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-factsheet_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-factsheet-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Visual and environmental supports, visual schedules</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Applied_behavior_analysis" title="Applied behavior analysis">Applied behavior analysis</a></li>
<li>Discrete trial instruction (part of applied behavior analysis)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_stories" title="Social stories">Social stories</a> and comic strip conversations</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Physical_therapy" title="Physical therapy">Physical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Occupational_therapy" title="Occupational therapy">occupational therapy</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-factsheet_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-factsheet-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
