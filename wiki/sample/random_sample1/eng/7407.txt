<p><b>Lettuce</b> (<i>Lactuca sativa</i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Annual_plant" title="Annual plant">annual plant</a> of the daisy family, <a href="/wiki/Asteraceae" title="Asteraceae">Asteraceae</a>. It is most often grown as a <a href="/wiki/Leaf_vegetable" title="Leaf vegetable">leaf vegetable</a>, but sometimes for its stem and seeds. Lettuce is most often used for <a href="/wiki/Salad" title="Salad">salads</a>, although it is also seen in other kinds of food, such as soups, sandwiches and wraps; it can also be grilled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> One variety, the <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Chinese transliteration">woju</span></i> (<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">莴苣</span>), or <a href="/wiki/Celtuce" title="Celtuce">asparagus lettuce (celtuce)</a>, is grown for its stems, which are eaten either raw or cooked. In addition to its main use as a leafy green, it has also gathered religious and medicinal significance over centuries of human consumption. Europe and North America originally dominated the market for lettuce, but by the late 20th century the consumption of lettuce had spread throughout the world. World production of lettuce and <a href="/wiki/Chicory" title="Chicory">chicory</a> for <a href="/wiki/Calendar_year" title="Calendar year">calendar year</a> 2013 was 24.9 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tonnes" title="Tonnes">tonnes</a>, over half of which came from China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faostat_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-faostat-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lettuce was first cultivated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptians" title="Ancient Egyptians">ancient Egyptians</a> who turned it from a <a href="/wiki/Weed" title="Weed">weed</a> whose seeds were used to produce oil, into a food plant grown for its succulent leaves and oil-rich seeds. Lettuce spread to the Greeks and Romans, the latter of whom gave it the name <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><a href="/wiki/Lactuca" title="Lactuca">lactuca</a></span></i>, from which the English <i>lettuce</i> is ultimately derived. By 50 AD, multiple types were described, and lettuce appeared often in medieval writings, including several <a href="/wiki/Herbal" title="Herbal">herbals</a>. The 16th through 18th centuries saw the development of many <a href="/wiki/Variety_(botany)" title="Variety (botany)">varieties</a> in Europe, and by the mid-18th century cultivars were described that can still be found in gardens.</p>
<p>Generally grown as a <a href="/wiki/Hardiness_(plants)" title="Hardiness (plants)">hardy</a> annual, lettuce is easily cultivated, although it requires relatively low temperatures to prevent it from flowering quickly. It can be plagued by numerous <a href="/wiki/Micronutrient_deficiency" title="Micronutrient deficiency">nutrient deficiencies</a>, as well as insect and mammal pests, and fungal and bacterial diseases. <i>L. sativa</i> <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">crosses</a> easily within the species and with some other species within the <i>Lactuca</i> genus. Although this trait can be a problem to home gardeners who attempt to save seeds, biologists have used it to broaden the <a href="/wiki/Gene_pool" title="Gene pool">gene pool</a> of cultivated lettuce varieties.</p>
<p>Lettuce is a rich source of <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_K" title="Vitamin K">vitamin K</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_A" title="Vitamin A">vitamin A</a>, and a moderate source of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folate" title="Folate">folate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>. Contaminated lettuce is often a source of bacterial, viral and parasitic outbreaks in humans, including <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">E. coli</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Salmonella" title="Salmonella">Salmonella</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_etymology">Taxonomy and etymology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lactuca_sativa_seeds.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Lactuca_sativa_seeds.jpg/220px-Lactuca_sativa_seeds.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Lactuca_sativa_seeds.jpg/330px-Lactuca_sativa_seeds.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Lactuca_sativa_seeds.jpg/440px-Lactuca_sativa_seeds.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lactuca_sativa_seeds.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>L. sativa</i> seeds</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Lactuca sativa</i> is a member of the <i><a href="/wiki/Lactuca" title="Lactuca">Lactuca</a></i> (lettuce) genus and the Asteraceae (sunflower or aster) family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The species was first described in 1753 by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in the second volume of his <i><a href="/wiki/Species_Plantarum" title="Species Plantarum">Species Plantarum</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Synonyms for <i>L. sativa</i> include <i>Lactuca scariola</i> var. <i>sativa</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kew_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kew-1">[1]</a></sup> <i>L. scariola</i> var. <i>integrata</i> and <i>L. scariola</i> var. <i>integrifolia</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <i>L. scariola</i> is itself a <a href="/wiki/Synonym" title="Synonym">synonym</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/Lactuca_serriola" title="Lactuca serriola">L. serriola</a></i>, the common wild or prickly lettuce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USDA_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-USDA-2">[2]</a></sup> <i>L. sativa</i> also has many identified taxonomic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Species_group" title="Species group">groups</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Variety_(botany)" title="Variety (botany)">varieties</a>, which delineate the various <a href="/wiki/Cultivar_group" title="Cultivar group">cultivar groups</a> of domesticated lettuce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Lettuce is closely related to several <i>Lactuca</i> species from southwest Asia; the closest relationship is to <i>L. serriola</i>, an aggressive <a href="/wiki/Weed" title="Weed">weed</a> common in temperate and subtropical zones in much of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Romans referred to lettuce as <i>lactuca</i> (<i>lac</i> meaning milk in Latin), an allusion to the white substance, now called latex, exuded by cut stems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weaver170_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weaver170-10">[10]</a></sup> This word has become the genus name, while <i>sativa</i> (meaning "sown" or "cultivated") was added to create the species name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Katz376_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Katz376-11">[11]</a></sup> The current word <i>lettuce</i>, originally from <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a>, came from the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i>letues</i> or <i>laitues</i>, which derived from the Roman name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The name <i>romaine</i> came from that type's use in the Roman papal gardens, while <i>cos</i>, another term for <a href="/wiki/Romaine_lettuce" title="Romaine lettuce">romaine lettuce</a>, came from the earliest European seeds of the type from the Greek island of <a href="/wiki/Kos" title="Kos">Cos</a>, a center of lettuce farming in the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine period</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weaver172_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weaver172-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kropsla_vruchten_(Lactuca_sativa_fruits).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="829" data-file-width="769" height="237" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Kropsla_vruchten_%28Lactuca_sativa_fruits%29.jpg/220px-Kropsla_vruchten_%28Lactuca_sativa_fruits%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Kropsla_vruchten_%28Lactuca_sativa_fruits%29.jpg/330px-Kropsla_vruchten_%28Lactuca_sativa_fruits%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Kropsla_vruchten_%28Lactuca_sativa_fruits%29.jpg/440px-Kropsla_vruchten_%28Lactuca_sativa_fruits%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kropsla_vruchten_(Lactuca_sativa_fruits).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Mature lettuce <a href="/wiki/Inflorescence" title="Inflorescence">inflorescence</a> in fruit</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Lettuce's native range spreads from the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a> to <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, although it has been transported to almost all areas of the world. Plants generally have a height and spread of 6 to 12 inches (15 to 30 cm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The leaves are colorful, mainly in the green and red color spectrums, with some <a href="/wiki/Variegation" title="Variegation">variegated</a> varieties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FineCooking_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FineCooking-15">[15]</a></sup> There are also a few varieties with yellow, gold or blue-teal leaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryder_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ryder-16">[16]</a></sup> Lettuces have a wide range of shapes and textures, from the dense heads of the iceberg type to the notched, scalloped, frilly or ruffly leaves of leaf varieties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FineCooking_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-FineCooking-15">[15]</a></sup> Lettuce plants have a root system that includes a main <a href="/wiki/Taproot" title="Taproot">taproot</a> and smaller secondary roots. Some varieties, especially those found in the United States and Western Europe, have long, narrow taproots and a small set of secondary roots. Longer taproots and more extensive secondary systems are found in varieties from Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryder_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ryder-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Depending on the variety and time of year, lettuce generally lives 65–130 days from planting to harvesting. Because lettuce that flowers (through the process known as "<a href="/wiki/Bolting_(horticulture)" title="Bolting (horticulture)">bolting</a>") becomes bitter and unsaleable, plants grown for consumption are rarely allowed to grow to maturity. Lettuce flowers more quickly in hot temperatures, while freezing temperatures cause slower growth and sometimes damage to outer leaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Once plants move past the edible stage, they develop flower stalks up to 3 feet (0.9 m) high with small yellow blossoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weaver175_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weaver175-18">[18]</a></sup> Like other members of the tribe <a href="/wiki/Cichorieae" title="Cichorieae">Cichorieae</a>, lettuce <a href="/wiki/Inflorescence" title="Inflorescence">inflorescences</a> (also known as <a href="/wiki/Pseudanthium" title="Pseudanthium">flower heads</a> or capitula) are composed of multiple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floret" title="Floret">florets</a>, each with a modified <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calyx_(botany)" title="Calyx (botany)">calyx</a> called a <a href="/wiki/Pappus_(flower_structure)" title="Pappus (flower structure)">pappus</a> (which becomes the feathery "parachute" of the fruit), a corolla of five petals fused into a <a href="/wiki/Ligule" title="Ligule">ligule</a> or strap, and the reproductive parts. These include fused <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anther" title="Anther">anthers</a> that form a tube which surrounds a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Style_(botany)" title="Style (botany)">style</a> and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bipartite#Adjective" title="wikt:bipartite">bipartite</a> <a href="/wiki/Stigma_(botany)" title="Stigma (botany)">stigma</a>. As the anthers shed <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a>, the style elongates to allow the stigmas, now coated with pollen, to emerge from the tube.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryder_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ryder-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The ovaries form compressed, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obovate" title="Obovate">obovate</a> (teardrop-shaped) dry fruits that do not open at maturity, measuring 3 to 4 mm long. The fruits have 5–7 ribs on each side and are tipped by two rows of small white hairs. The pappus remains at the top of each fruit as a <a href="/wiki/Biological_dispersal" title="Biological dispersal">dispersal</a> structure. Each fruit contains one seed, which can be white, yellow, gray or brown depending on the variety of lettuce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kew_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kew-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The domestication of lettuce over the centuries has resulted in several changes through <a href="/wiki/Selective_breeding" title="Selective breeding">selective breeding</a>: delayed bolting, larger seeds, larger leaves and heads, better taste and texture, a lower <a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a> content, and different leaf shapes and colors. Work in these areas continues through the present day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davey222_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davey222-20">[20]</a></sup> Scientific research into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genetic_modification" title="Genetic modification">genetic modification</a> of lettuce is ongoing, with over 85 field trials taking place between 1992 and 2005 in the European Union and United States to test modifications allowing greater <a href="/wiki/Herbicide" title="Herbicide">herbicide</a> tolerance, greater resistance to insects and fungi and slower bolting patterns. However, genetically modified lettuce is not currently used in commercial agriculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Romaine.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2520" data-file-width="2136" height="201" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Romaine.jpg/170px-Romaine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Romaine.jpg/255px-Romaine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Romaine.jpg/340px-Romaine.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Romaine.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Romaine_lettuce" title="Romaine lettuce">Romaine lettuce</a>, a descendant of some of the earliest cultivated lettuce</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Lettuce was first cultivated in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a> for the production of oil from its seeds. This plant was probably selectively bred by the Egyptians into a plant grown for its edible leaves,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Katz375_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Katz375-22">[22]</a></sup> with evidence of its cultivation appearing as early as 2680 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weaver170_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weaver170-10">[10]</a></sup> Lettuce was considered a sacred plant of the reproduction god <a href="/wiki/Min_(god)" title="Min (god)">Min</a>, and it was carried during his festivals and placed near his images. The plant was thought to help the god "perform the sexual act untiringly."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Its use in religious ceremonies resulted in the creation of many images in tombs and wall paintings. The cultivated variety appears to have been about 30 inches (76 cm) tall and resembled a large version of the modern <a href="/wiki/Romaine_lettuce" title="Romaine lettuce">romaine lettuce</a>. These upright lettuces were developed by the Egyptians and passed to the Greeks, who in turn shared them with the Romans. Circa 50 AD, Roman agriculturalist <a href="/wiki/Columella" title="Columella">Columella</a> described several lettuce varieties – some of which may have been ancestors of today's lettuces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weaver170_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Weaver170-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lettuce appears in many medieval writings, especially as a medicinal herb. <a href="/wiki/Hildegard_of_Bingen" title="Hildegard of Bingen">Hildegard of Bingen</a> mentioned it in her writings on medicinal herbs between 1098 and 1179, and many early <a href="/wiki/Herbal" title="Herbal">herbals</a> also describe its uses. In 1586, <a href="/wiki/Joachim_Camerarius" title="Joachim Camerarius">Joachim Camerarius</a> provided descriptions of the three basic modern lettuces – head lettuce, loose-leaf lettuce, and romaine (or cos) lettuce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weaver172_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weaver172-13">[13]</a></sup> Lettuce was first brought to the Americas from Europe by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> in the late 15th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDC-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Between the late 16th century and the early 18th century, many varieties were developed in Europe, particularly Holland. Books published in the mid-18th and early 19th centuries describe several varieties found in gardens today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weaver173_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weaver173-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to its short lifespan after harvest, lettuce was originally sold relatively close to where it was grown. The early 20th century saw the development of new packing, storage and shipping technologies that improved the lifespan and transportability of lettuce and resulted in a significant increase in availability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CommProfile_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-CommProfile-27">[27]</a></sup> During the 1950s, lettuce production was revolutionized with the development of <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_cooling" title="Vacuum cooling">vacuum cooling</a>, which allowed field cooling and packing of lettuce, replacing the previously used method of ice-cooling in packing houses outside the fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lettuce is very easy to grow, and as such has been a significant source of sales for many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seed_companies" title="Seed companies">seed companies</a>. Tracing the history of many varieties is complicated by the practice of many companies, particularly in the US, of changing a variety's name from year to year. This was done for several reasons, the most prominent being to boost sales by promoting a "new" variety or to prevent customers from knowing that the variety had been developed by a competing seed company. Documentation from the late 19th century shows between 65 and 140 distinct varieties of lettuce, depending on the amount of variation allowed between types – a distinct difference from the 1,100 named lettuce varieties on the market at the time. Names also often changed significantly from country to country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Although most lettuce grown today is used as a vegetable, a minor amount is used in the production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tobacco-free_cigarettes" title="Tobacco-free cigarettes">tobacco-free cigarettes</a>; however, domestic lettuce's wild relatives produce a leaf that visually more closely resembles tobacco.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Katz377_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Katz377-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Park_Lane_Nurseries_-_geograph.org.uk_-_583201.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="424" data-file-width="640" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Park_Lane_Nurseries_-_geograph.org.uk_-_583201.jpg/200px-Park_Lane_Nurseries_-_geograph.org.uk_-_583201.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Park_Lane_Nurseries_-_geograph.org.uk_-_583201.jpg/300px-Park_Lane_Nurseries_-_geograph.org.uk_-_583201.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Park_Lane_Nurseries_-_geograph.org.uk_-_583201.jpg/400px-Park_Lane_Nurseries_-_geograph.org.uk_-_583201.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Park_Lane_Nurseries_-_geograph.org.uk_-_583201.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A lettuce-growing facility in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Hardiness_(plants)" title="Hardiness (plants)">hardy</a> annual, some varieties of lettuce can be <a href="/wiki/Overwintering" title="Overwintering">overwintered</a> even in relatively cold climates under a layer of straw, and older, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heirloom_variety" title="Heirloom variety">heirloom varieties</a> are often grown in <a href="/wiki/Cold_frame" title="Cold frame">cold frames</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weaver173_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weaver173-26">[26]</a></sup> Lettuces meant for the cutting of individual leaves are generally planted straight into the garden in thick rows. Heading varieties of lettuces are commonly started in flats, then <a href="/wiki/Transplanting" title="Transplanting">transplanted</a> to individual spots, usually 8 to 14 inches (20 to 36 cm) apart, in the garden after developing several leaves. Lettuce spaced further apart receives more sunlight, which improves color and nutrient quantities in the leaves. Pale to white lettuce, such as the centers in some iceberg lettuce, contain few nutrients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weaver175_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weaver175-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Planche_Lactuca_sativa.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Planche_Lactuca_sativa.jpg/220px-Planche_Lactuca_sativa.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Planche_Lactuca_sativa.jpg/330px-Planche_Lactuca_sativa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Planche_Lactuca_sativa.jpg/440px-Planche_Lactuca_sativa.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Planche_Lactuca_sativa.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A transplanted bed of lettuce in a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouse</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Lettuce grows best in full sun in loose, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>-rich soils with a <a href="/wiki/Soil_pH" title="Soil pH">pH</a> of between 6.0 and 6.8. Heat generally prompts lettuce to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolt_(horticulture)" title="Bolt (horticulture)">bolt</a>, with most varieties growing poorly above 75 °F (24 °C); cool temperatures prompt better performance, with 60 to 65 °F (16 to 18 °C) being preferred and as low as 45 °F (7 °C) being tolerated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradley129_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bradley129-31">[31]</a></sup> Plants in hot areas that are provided partial shade during the hottest part of the day will bolt more slowly. Temperatures above 80 °F (27 °C) will generally result in poor or non-existent germination of lettuce seeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradley129_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bradley129-31">[31]</a></sup> After harvest, lettuce lasts the longest when kept at 32 °F (0 °C) and 96 percent humidity. Lettuce quickly degrades when stored with fruit such as apples, pears and bananas that release the ripening agent <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene gas</a>. The high water content of lettuce (94.9 percent) creates problems when attempting to preserve the plant – it cannot be successfully frozen, canned or dried and must be eaten fresh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UI_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-UI-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lettuce varieties will <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">cross</a> with each other, making spacing of 5 to 20 feet (1.5 to 6.1 m) between varieties necessary to prevent contamination when saving seeds. Lettuce will also cross with <i><a href="/wiki/Lactuca_serriola" title="Lactuca serriola">Lactuca serriola</a></i> (wild lettuce), with the resulting seeds often producing a plant with tough, bitter leaves. <a href="/wiki/Celtuce" title="Celtuce">Celtuce</a>, a lettuce variety grown primarily in Asia for its stems, crosses easily with lettuces grown for their leaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weaver175_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Weaver175-18">[18]</a></sup> This propensity for crossing, however, has led to breeding programs using closely related species in <i>Lactuca</i>, such as <i>L. serriola</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Lactuca_saligna" title="Lactuca saligna">L. saligna</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Lactuca_virosa" title="Lactuca virosa">L. virosa</a></i>, to broaden the available <a href="/wiki/Gene_pool" title="Gene pool">gene pool</a>. Starting in the 1990s, such programs began to include more distantly related species such as <i><a href="/wiki/Lactuca_tatarica" title="Lactuca tatarica">L. tatarica</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Seeds keep best when stored in cool conditions, and, unless stored <a href="/wiki/Cryogenics" title="Cryogenics">cryogenically</a>, remain viable the longest when stored at −4 °F (−20 °C); they are relatively short lived in storage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kew_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kew-1">[1]</a></sup> At room temperature, lettuce seeds remain viable for only a few months. However, when newly harvested lettuce seed is stored cryogenically, this life increases to a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 500 years for vaporized nitrogen and 3,400 years for liquid nitrogen; this advantage is lost if seeds are not frozen promptly after harvesting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivars">Cultivars</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lettuce_mix.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="940" data-file-width="1250" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Lettuce_mix.jpg/220px-Lettuce_mix.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Lettuce_mix.jpg/330px-Lettuce_mix.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Lettuce_mix.jpg/440px-Lettuce_mix.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lettuce_mix.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A selection of lettuce cultivars</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There are several types of lettuce, but three (leaf, head and cos or romaine) are the most common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradley129_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bradley129-31">[31]</a></sup> There are seven main cultivar groups of lettuce, each including many varieties:</p>
<ul>
<li>Leaf – Also known as looseleaf, cutting or bunching lettuce,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSU_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-WSU-35">[35]</a></sup> this type has loosely bunched leaves and is the most widely planted. It is used mainly for salads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UI_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-UI-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Romaine_lettuce" title="Romaine lettuce">Romaine</a>/Cos – Used mainly for salads and sandwiches, this type forms long, upright heads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UI_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-UI-32">[32]</a></sup> This is the most often used lettuce in <a href="/wiki/Caesar_salad" title="Caesar salad">Caesar salads</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-CDC-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li>Iceberg/Crisphead - the most popular type in the US, it is very heat-sensitive and was originally adapted for growth in the northern US. It ships well, but is low in flavor and nutritional content, being composed of even more water than other lettuce types.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UI_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-UI-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li>Butterhead – Also known as Boston or Bibb lettuce,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSU_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-WSU-35">[35]</a></sup> and traditional in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK" title="UK">UK</a> as "round lettuce",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> this type is a head lettuce with a loose arrangement of leaves, known for its sweet flavor and tender texture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UI_32-4"><a href="#cite_note-UI-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yangzhou_-_supermarket_-_woju_-_P1070028.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Yangzhou_-_supermarket_-_woju_-_P1070028.JPG/220px-Yangzhou_-_supermarket_-_woju_-_P1070028.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Yangzhou_-_supermarket_-_woju_-_P1070028.JPG/330px-Yangzhou_-_supermarket_-_woju_-_P1070028.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Yangzhou_-_supermarket_-_woju_-_P1070028.JPG/440px-Yangzhou_-_supermarket_-_woju_-_P1070028.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yangzhou_-_supermarket_-_woju_-_P1070028.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The lettuce variety <a href="/wiki/Celtuce" title="Celtuce">celtuce</a> is grown for its stem, used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_cooking" title="Chinese cooking">Chinese cooking</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>Summercrisp – Also called Batavian or French crisp, this lettuce is midway between the crisphead and leaf types. These lettuces tend to be larger, bolt-resistant and well-flavored.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSU_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-WSU-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Celtuce" title="Celtuce">Stem</a> – This type is grown for its seedstalk, rather than its leaves, and is used in Asian cooking, primarily Chinese, as well as stewed and creamed dishes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UI_32-5"><a href="#cite_note-UI-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li>Oilseed – This type is grown for its seeds, which are pressed to extract an oil mainly used for cooking. It has few leaves, bolts quickly and produces seeds around 50 percent larger than other types of lettuce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The butterhead and crisphead types are sometimes known together as "cabbage" lettuce, because their heads are shorter, flatter, and more cabbage-like than romaine lettuces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<p>Soil nutrient deficiencies can cause a variety of plant problems that range from malformed plants to a lack of head growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradley129_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bradley129-31">[31]</a></sup> Many insects are attracted to lettuce, including <a href="/wiki/Cutworm" title="Cutworm">cutworms</a>, which cut seedlings off at the soil line; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wireworm" title="Wireworm">wireworms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nematode" title="Nematode">nematodes</a>, which cause yellow, stunted plants; <a href="/wiki/Tarnished_plant_bug" title="Tarnished plant bug">tarnished plant bugs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aphid" title="Aphid">aphids</a>, which cause yellow, distorted leaves; <a href="/wiki/Leafhopper" title="Leafhopper">leafhoppers</a>, which cause stunted growth and pale leaves; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thrip" title="Thrip">thrips</a>, which turn leaves gray-green or silver; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leafminer" title="Leafminer">leafminers</a>, which create tunnels within the leaves; <a href="/wiki/Flea_beetle" title="Flea beetle">flea beetles</a>, which cut small holes in leaves and <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slug" title="Slug">slugs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a>, which cut large holes in leaves. Mammals, including rabbits and groundhogs, also eat the plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradley132_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bradley132-39">[39]</a></sup> Lettuce contains several defensive compounds, including <a href="/wiki/Sesquiterpene_lactone" title="Sesquiterpene lactone">sesquiterpene lactones</a>, and other natural phenolics such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flavonol" title="Flavonol">flavonol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flavonols#Flavonols_glycosides" title="Flavonols">glycosides</a>, which help to protect it against pests. Certain varieties contain more than others, and some selective breeding and genetic modification studies have focused on using this trait to identify and produce commercial varieties with increased pest resistance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lettuce also suffers from several <a href="/wiki/Plant_virus" title="Plant virus">viral diseases</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Lettuce_big-vein_disease" title="Lettuce big-vein disease">big vein</a>, which causes yellow, distorted leaves, and <a href="/wiki/Mosaic_virus" title="Mosaic virus">mosaic virus</a>, which is spread by aphids and causes stunted plant growth and deformed leaves. <a href="/wiki/Aster_yellows" title="Aster yellows">Aster yellows</a> are a disease-causing bacteria carried by leafhoppers, which causes deformed leaves. Fungal diseases include <a href="/wiki/Powdery_mildew" title="Powdery mildew">powdery mildew</a> and <a href="/wiki/Downy_mildew" title="Downy mildew">downy mildew</a>, which cause leaves to mold and die and <a href="/wiki/Fungicide_use_in_the_United_States#Bottom_rot" title="Fungicide use in the United States">bottom rot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fungicide_use_in_the_United_States#Lettuce_drop" title="Fungicide use in the United States">lettuce drop</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gray_mold" title="Gray mold">gray mold</a>, which cause entire plants to rot and collapse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradley132_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bradley132-39">[39]</a></sup> Crowding lettuce tends to attract pests and diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weaver175_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-Weaver175-18">[18]</a></sup> Weeds can also be an issue, as cultivated lettuce is generally not competitive with them, especially when directly seeded into the ground. Transplanted lettuce (started in flats and later moved to growing beds) is generally more competitive initially, but can still be crowded later in the season, causing misshapen lettuce and lower yields. Weeds also act as homes for insects and disease and can make harvesting more difficult.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Herbicides are often used to control weeds in commercial production. However, this has led to the development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbicide-resistant" title="Herbicide-resistant">herbicide-resistant</a> weeds and prompted environmental and health concerns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davey222_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Davey222-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2013, world production of lettuce (report combined with <a href="/wiki/Chicory" title="Chicory">chicory</a>) was 24.9 million tonnes, with China alone producing 13.5 million tonnes (table).</p>
<p>Lettuce is the only member of the <i>Lactuca</i> genus to be grown commercially.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Although China is the top world producer of lettuce, the majority of the crop is consumed domestically. Spain is the world's largest exporter of lettuce, with the US ranking second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CommProfile_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-CommProfile-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Western Europe and North America were the original major markets for large-scale lettuce production. By the late 1900s, Asia, South America, Australia and Africa became more substantial markets. Different locations tended to prefer different types of lettuce, with butterhead prevailing in northern Europe and Great Britain, romaine in the Mediterranean and stem lettuce in China and Egypt. By the late 20th century, the preferred types began to change, with crisphead, especially iceberg, lettuce becoming the dominant type in northern Europe and Great Britain and more popular in western Europe. In the US, no one type predominated until the early 20th century, when crisphead lettuces began gaining popularity. After the 1940s, with the development of iceberg lettuce, 95 percent of the lettuce grown and consumed in the US was crisphead lettuce. By the end of the century, other types began to regain popularity and eventually made up over 30 percent of production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Katz378_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Katz378-43">[43]</a></sup> Stem lettuce was first developed in China, and remains primarily cultivated in that country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simoons_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Simoons-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early 21st century, bagged salad products increased in the lettuce market, especially in the US where innovative packaging and shipping methods prolonged freshness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agmrc_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-agmrc-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-npr-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States in 2013, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> (71%) and <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> (29%) produced nearly all of the country's fresh head and leaf lettuce, with head lettuce yielding $9400 of value per acre and leaf lettuce $8000 per acre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agmrc_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-agmrc-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culinary_use">Culinary use</span></h2>
<p>As described around 50 AD, lettuce leaves were often cooked and served by the Romans with an oil-and-vinegar dressing; however, smaller leaves were sometimes eaten raw. During the 81–96 AD reign of <a href="/wiki/Domitian" title="Domitian">Domitian</a>, the tradition of serving a lettuce <a href="/wiki/Salad" title="Salad">salad</a> before a meal began. Post-Roman Europe continued the tradition of <a href="/wiki/Poaching_(cooking)" title="Poaching (cooking)">poaching</a> lettuce, mainly with large romaine types, as well as the method of pouring a hot oil and vinegar mixture over the leaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weaver170_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Weaver170-10">[10]</a></sup> Today, the majority of lettuce is grown for its leaves, although one type is grown for its stem and one for its seeds, which are made into an oil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Katz375_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Katz375-22">[22]</a></sup> Most lettuce is used in salads, either alone or with other greens, vegetables, meats and cheeses. Romaine lettuce is often used for <a href="/wiki/Caesar_salad" title="Caesar salad">Caesar salads</a>, with a dressing that includes <a href="/wiki/Anchovy" title="Anchovy">anchovies</a> and eggs. Lettuce leaves can also be found in soups, sandwiches and wraps, while the stems are eaten both raw and cooked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Katz376_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Katz376-11">[11]</a></sup> The consumption of lettuce in China developed differently from in Western countries, due to health risks and cultural aversion to eating raw leaves. In that country, "salads" were created from cooked vegetables and served hot or cold. Lettuce was also used in a larger variety of dishes than in Western countries, contributing to a range of dishes including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bean_curd" title="Bean curd">bean curd</a> and meat dishes, soups and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stir-fry" title="Stir-fry">stir-frys</a> plain or with other vegetables. Stem lettuce, widely consumed in China, is eaten either raw or cooked, the latter primarily in soups and stir-frys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simoons_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Simoons-44">[44]</a></sup> Lettuce is also used as a primary ingredient in the preparation of <a href="/wiki/Lettuce_soup" title="Lettuce soup">lettuce soup</a>.</p>

<p>Depending on the variety, lettuce is an excellent source (20% of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daily_Value" title="Daily Value">Daily Value</a>, DV, or higher) of <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_K" title="Vitamin K">vitamin K</a> (97% DV) and <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_A" title="Vitamin A">vitamin A</a> (21% DV) (table), with higher concentrations of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Provitamin_A" title="Provitamin A">provitamin A</a> compound, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta-carotene" title="Beta-carotene">beta-carotene</a>, found in darker green lettuces, such as Romaine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UI_32-6"><a href="#cite_note-UI-32">[32]</a></sup> With the exception of the iceberg variety, lettuce is also a good source (10-19% DV) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folate" title="Folate">folate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> (table).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UI_32-7"><a href="#cite_note-UI-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Food-borne_illness">Food-borne illness</span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food-borne_illness" title="Food-borne illness">Food-borne</a> <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a> that can survive on lettuce include <i><a href="/wiki/Listeria_monocytogenes" title="Listeria monocytogenes">Listeria monocytogenes</a></i>, the causative agent of <a href="/wiki/Listeriosis" title="Listeriosis">listeriosis</a>, which multiplies in storage. However, despite high levels of bacteria being found on ready-to-eat lettuce products, a 2008 study found no incidences of food-borne illness related to listeriosis, possibly due to the product's short <a href="/wiki/Shelf_life" title="Shelf life">shelf life</a>, indigenous microflora competing with the <i>Listeria</i> bacteria or inhibition of bacteria to cause listeriosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other bacteria found on lettuce include <i><a href="/wiki/Aeromonas" title="Aeromonas">Aeromonas</a></i> species, which have not been linked to any outbreaks; <i><a href="/wiki/Campylobacter" title="Campylobacter">Campylobacter</a></i> species, which cause <a href="/wiki/Campylobacteriosis" title="Campylobacteriosis">campylobacteriosis</a>; and <i><a href="/wiki/Yersinia_intermedia" title="Yersinia intermedia">Yersinia intermedia</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Yersinia_kristensenii" title="Yersinia kristensenii">Yersinia kristensenii</a></i> (species of <i><a href="/wiki/Yersinia" title="Yersinia">Yersinia</a></i>), which have been found mainly in lettuce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FDA_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-FDA-49">[49]</a></sup> Lettuce has been linked to numerous outbreaks of the bacteria <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E._coli_O157:H7" title="E. coli O157:H7">E. coli O157:H7</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Shigella" title="Shigella">Shigella</a></i>; the plants were most likely contaminated through contact with animal feces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> A 2007 study determined that the vacuum cooling method, especially prevalent in the California lettuce industry, increased the uptake and survival rates of <i>E. coli O157:H7</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Salmonella" title="Salmonella">Salmonella</a></i> bacteria, including the uncommon <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Salmonella_braenderup&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Salmonella braenderup (page does not exist)">Salmonella braenderup</a></i> type, have also caused outbreaks traced to contaminated lettuce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Viruses, including <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_A" title="Hepatitis A">hepatitis A</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calicivirus" title="Calicivirus">calicivirus</a> and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norwalk_virus" title="Norwalk virus">Norwalk-like</a> strain, have been found in lettuce. The vegetable has also been linked to outbreaks of parasitic infestations, including <i><a href="/wiki/Giardia_lamblia" title="Giardia lamblia">Giardia lamblia</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FDA_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-FDA-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_and_medicinal_lore">Religious and medicinal lore</span></h2>
<p>In addition to its usual purpose as an edible leafy vegetable, lettuce has had a number of uses in ancient (and even some more modern) times as a medicinal herb and religious symbol. For example, ancient Egyptians thought lettuce to be a symbol of sexual prowess<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Katz378_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Katz378-43">[43]</a></sup> and a promoter of love and childbearing in women. The Romans likewise claimed that it increased sexual potency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watts226_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Watts226-53">[53]</a></sup> In contrast, the ancient Greeks connected the plant with male impotency,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weaver170_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Weaver170-10">[10]</a></sup> and served it during funerals (probably due to its role in the myth of <a href="/wiki/Adonis" title="Adonis">Adonis</a>' death), and British women in the 19th century believed it would cause infertility and sterility. Lettuce has mild <a href="/wiki/Narcotic" title="Narcotic">narcotic</a> properties; it was called "sleepwort" by the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxons</a> because of this attribute, although the cultivated <i>L. sativa</i> has lower levels of the narcotic than its wild cousins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watts226_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-Watts226-53">[53]</a></sup> This narcotic effect is a property of two <a href="/wiki/Sesquiterpene_lactone" title="Sesquiterpene lactone">sesquiterpene lactones</a> which are found in the white liquid (<a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a>) in the stems of lettuce,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Katz377_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Katz377-30">[30]</a></sup> called <a href="/wiki/Lactucarium" title="Lactucarium">lactucarium</a> or "lettuce opium".</p>
<p>Lettuce is also eaten as part of the Jewish <a href="/wiki/Passover_Seder" title="Passover Seder">Passover Seder</a>, where it is considered the optimal choice for use as the bitter herb, which is eaten together with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matzah" title="Matzah">matzah</a>.</p>
<p>Some American settlers claimed that <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a> could be prevented through the ingestion of lettuce,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watts226_53-2"><a href="#cite_note-Watts226-53">[53]</a></sup> and an Iranian belief suggested consumption of the seeds when afflicted with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoid" title="Typhoid">typhoid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Traditional_medicine" title="Traditional medicine">Folk medicine</a> has also claimed it as a treatment for pain, <a href="/wiki/Rheumatism" title="Rheumatism">rheumatism</a>, tension and nervousness, coughs and insanity; scientific evidence of these benefits in humans has not been found. The religious ties of lettuce continue into the present day among the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yazidi" title="Yazidi">Yazidi</a> people of northern Iraq, who have a religious prohibition against eating the plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
