<p><b>Saemangeum</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuarine</a> tidal flat on the coast of the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Sea" title="Yellow Sea">Yellow Sea</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>. It was dammed by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_South_Korea" title="Government of South Korea">government of South Korea</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Saemangeum_Seawall" title="Saemangeum Seawall">Saemangeum Seawall Project</a>, completed in April 2006, after a long fight between the government and environmental activists, and is scheduled to be converted into either agricultural or industrial land. Prior to 2006, it had played an important role as a habitat for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Migratory_bird" title="Migratory bird">migratory birds</a>. The completion of this seawall is likely to be a major contributor to the decline of many species. Around 400,000 shorebirds depended on the Saemangeum estuarine as an important feeding ground on the 24,000 km migration between Asia and Alaska and Russia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> including the two endangered waders <a href="/wiki/Nordmann%27s_greenshank" title="Nordmann's greenshank">Nordmann's greenshank</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spoon-billed_sandpiper" title="Spoon-billed sandpiper">spoon-billed sandpiper</a> (each species with fewer than a thousand surviving birds).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A conservation organisation has accused authorities of having failed to monitor the project's impact on local wildlife in a transparent way, and carried out an independent monitoring program in 2006.</p>
<p><br/>
The Saemangeum lay at the mouths of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dongjin_River&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dongjin River (page does not exist)">Dongjin</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mangyeong_River&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mangyeong River (page does not exist)">Mangyeong</a> Rivers, on the coast of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeollabuk-do" title="Jeollabuk-do">Jeollabuk-do</a>. It is just south of the estuary of the <a href="/wiki/Geum_River" title="Geum River">Geum River</a>. Neighboring districts include <a href="/wiki/Gunsan" title="Gunsan">Gunsan</a> City, <a href="/wiki/Buan_County" title="Buan County">Buan County</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gimje_City" title="Gimje City">Gimje City</a>.</p>
<p>The project of filling in the estuary began in 1991, but was slowed by a series of court actions by environmentalists. The completed <a href="/wiki/Seawall" title="Seawall">seawall</a> is some 33 kilometers long, and replaces a coastline that was once more than 100 kilometers long. After the estuary has been completely filled, an area of about 400 km<sup>2</sup> (roughly two-thirds the size of <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>) will have been added to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_peninsula" title="Korean peninsula">Korean peninsula</a>, making it one of the biggest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_reclamation_project" title="Land reclamation project">land reclamation projects</a> in history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The estuary was originally called "Mangeum" (萬金). This name was probably formed from combining the first <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">character</a> of "Mangyeong" and that of "Gimje." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Saemanguem was completed on April 27, 2010, officially becoming the longest <a href="/wiki/Seawall" title="Seawall">seawall</a> ever built with the length of 33.9 km, breaking the record of <a href="/wiki/Zuiderzee_Works" title="Zuiderzee Works">Zuiderzee Works</a> from 1932.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 2, 2010, Saemanguem was certified by <a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness World Records</a> as the longest man-made sea barrier in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
