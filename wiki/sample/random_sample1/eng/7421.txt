<p>The <b>Samuel Oschin telescope</b> (also named <b>Oschin Schmidt</b>) is a 48-inch-aperture (1.22 m) <a href="/wiki/Schmidt_camera" title="Schmidt camera">Schmidt camera</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Palomar_Observatory" title="Palomar Observatory">Palomar Observatory</a> in northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Diego_County" title="San Diego County">San Diego County</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. It consists of a 49.75-inch <a href="/wiki/Schmidt_corrector_plate" title="Schmidt corrector plate">Schmidt corrector plate</a> and a 72-inch (f/2.5) mirror. The instrument is strictly a camera; there is no provision for an eyepiece to look through it. It originally used 10- and 14-inch glass <a href="/wiki/Photographic_plate" title="Photographic plate">photographic plates</a>. Since the focal plane is curved, these plates had to be preformed in a special jig before being loaded into the camera.</p>
<p>Construction on the Schmidt telescope began in 1939 and it was completed in 1948. It was named the <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Oschin" title="Samuel Oschin">Samuel Oschin</a> telescope in 1986. Before that it was just called the 48-inch Schmidt.<sup><a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.astro.caltech.edu/palomar/SOTdisc.htm" rel="nofollow">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The camera has been converted to use a <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">CCD</a> imager. This is a mosaic of 12 CCDs covering the whole (4 degree by 4 degree) <a href="/wiki/Field_of_view" title="Field of view">field of view</a> of the camera, the largest CCD mosaic used in an astronomical camera at this time. The corrector plate was recently replaced using glass that is transparent to a wider range of wavelengths. The camera was originally hand-guided through one of two 10-inch-aperture (0.25 m) <a href="/wiki/Refracting_telescope" title="Refracting telescope">refracting telescopes</a> mounted on either side of the camera. The camera is now fully automated and remote-controlled. The data collected is transmitted over the <a href="/wiki/High_Performance_Wireless_Research_and_Education_Network" title="High Performance Wireless Research and Education Network">High Performance Wireless Research and Education Network</a> (HPWREN). It is programmed and operated primarily from <a href="/wiki/Pasadena,_California" title="Pasadena, California">Pasadena, California</a>, with no operator on site (except to open and close the dome).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_plate_archive">The plate archive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_Oschin_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The plate archive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>About half of the large photographic glass plate negatives exposed on the telescope, some 19,000 in all, had been accumulating in the sub-basement of the Robinson building at the <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">California Institute of Technology</a> since 1949. In 2002, astronomer <a href="/wiki/Jean_Mueller" title="Jean Mueller">Jean Mueller</a> approached <a href="/wiki/Richard_Ellis_(astronomer)" title="Richard Ellis (astronomer)">Richard Ellis</a>, the director of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Caltech_Optical_Observatories&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Caltech Optical Observatories (page does not exist)">Caltech Optical Observatories</a>, to volunteer to the task of organizing the Oschin Telescope plate archive. Given the go-ahead, she recruited eleven volunteers from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Wilson_Observatory_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mount Wilson Observatory Association (page does not exist)">Mount Wilson Observatory Association</a> (MWOA) and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Los_Angeles_Astronomical_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Los Angeles Astronomical Society (page does not exist)">Los Angeles Astronomical Society</a> (LAAS), and the team then spent 13 weekends (more than one thousand hours) poring over the stacks, placing plates in protective sleeves, and packing them in more than 500 boxes that were transported to Palomar.</p>
<p>All of the volunteers were presented with the gift of having <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroids</a> named after them, compliments of <a href="/wiki/Carolyn_S._Shoemaker" title="Carolyn S. Shoemaker">Carolyn S. Shoemaker</a>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/10028_Bonus" title="10028 Bonus">10028 Bonus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/12680_Bogdanovich" title="12680 Bogdanovich">12680 Bogdanovich</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/13914_Galegant" title="13914 Galegant">13914 Galegant</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/16452_Goldfinger" title="16452 Goldfinger">16452 Goldfinger</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/19173_Virginiater%C3%A9se" title="19173 Virginiaterése">19173 Virginiaterése</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/20007_Marybrown" title="20007 Marybrown">20007 Marybrown</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/21148_Billramsey" title="21148 Billramsey">21148 Billramsey</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/22294_Simmons" title="22294 Simmons">22294 Simmons</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/27706_Strogen" title="27706 Strogen">27706 Strogen</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/29133_Vargas" title="29133 Vargas">29133 Vargas</a>. Mueller was also rewarded by a visit to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keck_Observatory" title="Keck Observatory">Keck Observatory</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discoveries">Discoveries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_Oschin_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discoveries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Oschin Telescope was responsible for the discovery of <a href="/wiki/90377_Sedna" title="90377 Sedna">90377 Sedna</a> on 2003-11-14 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/136199_Eris" title="136199 Eris">Eris</a>, the "10th Planet" on 2005-01-05 from images taken 2003-10-21. The peculiar <a href="/wiki/Type_Ia_supernova" title="Type Ia supernova">Type Ia supernova</a> <a href="/wiki/SN_2002cx" title="SN 2002cx">SN 2002cx</a> was discovered with the Oschin telescope on 2002 May 12.21 UT.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wood-Vasey02_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wood-Vasey02-1">[1]</a></sup> Other discoveries include <a href="/wiki/90482_Orcus" title="90482 Orcus">90482 Orcus</a> (in 2004) and <a href="/wiki/50000_Quaoar" title="50000 Quaoar">50000 Quaoar</a> (in 2002), both large trans-Neptune objects.</p>
<p>In June 2011 it was reported the telescope discovered 6 <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a> located 8 billion light years from Earth whose composition lacks hydrogen. This is different from normal supernovae, and will contribute to the research of star formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
