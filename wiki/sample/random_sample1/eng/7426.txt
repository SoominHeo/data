<p><i><b>Dawn of the Dead</b></i> is a 2004 American <a href="/wiki/Horror_film" title="Horror film">horror film</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/Zack_Snyder" title="Zack Snyder">Zack Snyder</a> in his <a href="/wiki/List_of_directorial_debuts" title="List of directorial debuts">feature film directorial debut</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Remake" title="Remake">remake</a> of <a href="/wiki/George_A._Romero" title="George A. Romero">George A. Romero</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Dawn_of_the_Dead_(1978_film)" title="Dawn of the Dead (1978 film)">1978 film of the same name</a>, it is written by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Gunn_(filmmaker)" title="James Gunn (filmmaker)">James Gunn</a> and stars <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Polley" title="Sarah Polley">Sarah Polley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ving_Rhames" title="Ving Rhames">Ving Rhames</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jake_Weber" title="Jake Weber">Jake Weber</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mekhi_Phifer" title="Mekhi Phifer">Mekhi Phifer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PM_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-PM-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The special effects were done by <a href="/wiki/Heather_Langenkamp" title="Heather Langenkamp">Heather Langenkamp</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_LeRoy_Anderson" title="David LeRoy Anderson">David LeRoy Anderson</a> who co-own AFX Studio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film depicts a handful of human survivors living in a shopping mall located in the fictional town of Everett, Wisconsin surrounded by swarms of <a href="/wiki/Zombie" title="Zombie">zombies</a>. The movie was produced by <a href="/wiki/Strike_Entertainment" title="Strike Entertainment">Strike Entertainment</a> in association with New Amsterdam Entertainment, released by <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">Universal Pictures</a> and includes <a href="/wiki/Cameo_appearance" title="Cameo appearance">cameos</a> by original cast members <a href="/wiki/Ken_Foree" title="Ken Foree">Ken Foree</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scott_Reiniger" title="Scott Reiniger">Scott Reiniger</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tom_Savini" title="Tom Savini">Tom Savini</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dawn_of_the_Dead_(2004_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After finishing a long shift as a nurse, Ana returns to her suburban neighborhood and her husband, Luis. Caught up in a scheduled date night, they miss an emergency news bulletin. The next morning, a neighborhood girl named Vivian enters their bedroom, looking mutilated. Luis approaches her to see what's wrong and she suddenly bites him on the neck. Ana locks Vivian out of the room and tries to tend to Luis who goes into shock, then dies. She attempts to call the hospital only to find that there is no connection; as she keeps ringing, Luis rises up, now infected with the same disease and immediately goes after Ana. She flees in her car and drives away, seeing chaos all around her neighbourhood, but eventually crashes and passes out. The intro scene rolls in, depicting riots and war in the major cities and the downfall of human civilisation. Upon waking, Ana is confronted by Police Sergeant Kenneth Hall, electronics salesman Michael, petty criminal Andre and his pregnant wife, Luda and joins them, after they prove she is still alive. They break into a nearby mall and are attacked by a zombified security guard, who scratches Luda. Kenneth is also badly injured while wrestling with the zombie but Ana manages to subdue with his shotgun. They are then confronted by three living guards — C.J., Bart, and Terry — who make them surrender their weapons in exchange for refuge. They split into groups to secure the mall and then go to the roof where they see another survivor, Andy, who is stranded alone in his gun store across the zombie-infested parking lot.</p>
<p>The next day, a delivery truck carrying more survivors enters the lot, with zombies in close pursuit. C.J. and Bart wish to turn them away but are overruled and disarmed. The newcomers include Norma, Steve Marcus, Tucker, Monica, Glen, Frank and his daughter, Nicole. Another woman is too ill to walk; she is wheeled inside via wheelbarrow only to die. As Ana drapes her with a cloth, however, the woman reanimates and attacks her, getting stabbed with a poker. Ana theorizes with the group that the disease is passed by bites from the infected individuals, as she had already seen it happen with Luis. Andre leaves to see Luda, who has kept her scratch hidden from the group. They realize that Frank has been bitten and is a potential threat. After some debate, Frank elects to be isolated. When he dies and turns, Kenneth shoots him.</p>
<p>Over time, Kenneth and Andy start a friendship by way of messages written on a whiteboard; romance also buds between Ana and Michael, and Nicole and Terry. One day, the power goes out. CJ, Bart, Michael and Kenneth go to the parking garage to activate the emergency generator and find a friendly dog inside. Zombies attack and kill Bart, forcing the others to douse the zombies in gas and set them ablaze. Meanwhile, Luda—tied up by Andre—gives birth and dies. She reanimates as Norma checks on the couple, killing the zombified Luda. Andre snaps; they exchange gunfire and both are killed. The others arrive to find a zombie baby, which they kill immediately.</p>
<p>The group decides to fight their way to the local marina, and travel on Steve's yacht to an island on <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Lake Michigan</a>. They begin reinforcing two shuttle buses from the parking garage for their escape. However, they also need to pick up Andy, who is starving. They strap food and a walkie-talkie onto the dog, Chips, and lower him into the parking lot; the zombies have no interest in him. Chips gets into Andy's store safely but a zombie follows through the dog door. Nicole, fond of Chips, crashes the delivery truck into the gun store, where she is trapped by a zombified Andy. Kenneth, Michael, Tucker, Terry, and C.J. reach the gun store via the sewers and kill Andy, rescuing Nicole. They grab ammunition and go back to the mall. Along the way, Tucker breaks his legs and C.J shoots him out of mercy. Once inside, they are unable to lock the door, forcing an evacuation.</p>
<p>Everyone boards the buses and navigate through the zombie infested city. Glen loses control of a chainsaw, accidentally killing himself and Monica; blood splatters on the windshield causing the bus to crash. C.J. exits the first van to look for survivors with Kenneth and Terry. They encounter the undead Steve but Ana kills him. She retrieves his boat keys and they take the remaining bus to the marina where C.J sacrifices himself so the rest of the group can escape. Michael reveals a bite wound and kills himself as Ana, Kenneth, Nicole, Terry, and Chips flee on the yacht.</p>
<p>Footage from a camcorder found on the boat begins with Steve's escapades before the outbreak and concludes with the group running out of supplies before finally arriving at an island, where they are attacked by another swarm of zombies. The camcorder drops, recording dozens of zombies chasing them.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dawn_of_the_Dead_(2004_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Gunn_(filmmaker)" title="James Gunn (filmmaker)">James Gunn</a> is partially responsible for the screenplay although he received a solo writing credit. After he left the project to concentrate on <i><a href="/wiki/Scooby-Doo_2:_Monsters_Unleashed" title="Scooby-Doo 2: Monsters Unleashed">Scooby-Doo 2: Monsters Unleashed</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Tolkin" title="Michael Tolkin">Michael Tolkin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scott_Frank" title="Scott Frank">Scott Frank</a> were brought in for rewriting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In a commentary track on the Ultimate Edition DVD for the original <i>George A. Romero's Dawn of the Dead</i>, Richard P. Rubinstein, producer of the original and the remake, explained that Tolkin further developed the characters, while Frank provided some of the bigger and upbeat action sequences.</p>
<p>The mall scenes and rooftop scenes were shot in the former Thornhill Square Shopping Centre in <a href="/wiki/Thornhill,_Ontario" title="Thornhill, Ontario">Thornhill, Ontario</a> and the other scenes were shot in the Aileen-Willowbrook neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Thornhill,_Ontario" title="Thornhill, Ontario">Thornhill, Ontario</a>. The set for Ana and Luis's bedroom was constructed in a backroom of the mall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Commentary_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Commentary-7">[7]</a></sup> The mall was defunct, which is the reason the production used it; the movie crew completely renovated the structure, and stocked it with fictitious stores after <a href="/wiki/Starbucks" title="Starbucks">Starbucks</a> and numerous other corporations refused to let their names be used<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Commentary_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Commentary-7">[7]</a></sup> (two exceptions to this are <a href="/wiki/Roots_Canada" title="Roots Canada">Roots</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panasonic_Corporation" title="Panasonic Corporation">Panasonic</a>). Most of the mall was demolished shortly after the film was shot. The fictitious stores include a coffee shop called Hallowed Grounds (a lyric from <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Cash" title="Johnny Cash">Johnny Cash</a>'s song "<a href="/wiki/The_Man_Comes_Around_(song)" title="The Man Comes Around (song)">The Man Comes Around</a>", which was used over the opening credits), and an upscale department store called <a href="/wiki/Gaylen_Ross" title="Gaylen Ross">Gaylen Ross</a> (an in-joke reference to one of the stars of the original 1978 film).</p>
<p>The first half of the film was shot almost entirely in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronological_order" title="Chronological order">chronological order</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Commentary_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Commentary-7">[7]</a></sup> while the final sequences on the boat and island were shot much later and at a different location (<a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios_Hollywood" title="Universal Studios Hollywood">Universal Studios Hollywood</a>) than the rest of the film, after preview audiences objected to the sudden ending of the original print.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Commentary_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Commentary-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Dawn of the Dead</i> is the second film that co-starred actresses <a href="/wiki/Lindy_Booth" title="Lindy Booth">Lindy Booth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kim_Poirier" title="Kim Poirier">Kim Poirier</a>. They first worked together on <i><a href="/wiki/American_Psycho_2" title="American Psycho 2">American Psycho 2</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deleted_scenes">Deleted scenes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dawn_of_the_Dead_(2004_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Deleted scenes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Deleted_scene" title="Deleted scene">Deleted scenes</a> were added back for the "Unrated Director's Cut" DVD edition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVD.2C_DirCut_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-DVD.2C_DirCut-8">[8]</a></sup> Along with gore effects removed to obtain an MPAA R rating,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> they include a clearer depiction of how the survivors originally break into the mall, and a short scene where the character of Glen regales the imprisoned C.J. and Bart with his reminiscing about his homosexual coming-of-age.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Additional_material">Additional material</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dawn_of_the_Dead_(2004_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Additional material">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The DVD release includes, as a bonus feature, the short film <i>We Interrupt This Program</i>, an expanded version of the fictional live broadcasts shown in the mall's televisions, which chronicles the worldwide effects of the zombie plague and the impact it has on a newscaster. Aside from featuring additional footage of cameos by actors from the original film, the film features <a href="/wiki/Richard_Biggs" title="Richard Biggs">Richard Biggs</a> as the newscaster (in his final performance before his death in May 2004), and a voiceover cameo by <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Boxleitner" title="Bruce Boxleitner">Bruce Boxleitner</a> as the President of the United States.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dawn_of_the_Dead_(2004_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the United Kingdom, <i>Dawn of the Dead</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Shaun_of_the_Dead" title="Shaun of the Dead">Shaun of the Dead</a></i> were originally scheduled to be released the same week, but due to the similarity in the names of the two films and plot outline, <a href="/wiki/United_International_Pictures" title="United International Pictures">UIP</a> opted to push back <i>Shaun</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> release by two weeks. It was screened out of competition at the <a href="/wiki/2004_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2004 Cannes Film Festival">2004 Cannes Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-festival-canuses.com_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-festival-canuses.com-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film grossed $59 million at the US box office and $102 million worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mojo_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-mojo-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dawn_of_the_Dead_(2004_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="Critics"></span><span id="Critical_response"></span> <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Review_aggregator" title="Review aggregator">review aggregator</a>, reports that 75% of 182 surveyed critics gave the film a positive review; the average rating is 6.7/10. The site's consensus reads: "A kinetic, violent and surprisingly worthy remake of George Romero's horror classic that pays homage to the original while working on its own terms."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> rated it 59/100 based on 37 reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> said the film "works and it delivers just about what you expect when you buy your ticket" but felt that it "lacks the mordant humor of the Romero version" and the "plot flatlines compared to the 1979 version, which was trickier, wittier and smarter."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Scott Foundas of <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> described it as an unnecessary remake that will appeal mostly to young adult audiences who have not seen the original film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Michael Rechtshaffen of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i> wrote that the film "has no patience for such subtleties" as Romero's thematic concerns or suspense-building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Mitchell" title="Elvis Mitchell">Elvis Mitchell</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> wrote, "Mr. Snyder's blood feast is strictly by the numbers: this second-rater could be the world's most expensive Troma film."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Manohla_Dargis" title="Manohla Dargis">Manohla Dargis</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> wrote, "Good zombie fun, the remake of George A. Romero's "Dawn of the Dead" is the best proof in ages that cannibalizing old material sometimes works fiendishly well."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mick_LaSalle" title="Mick LaSalle">Mick LaSalle</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i> wrote, "Instead, the new "Dawn of the Dead" satirizes itself and satirizes its genre, and, on its own unambitious terms, the movie succeeds. It's silly, witty and good-natured, not scary so much as icky, and not horrifying or horrible but consistently amusing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lisa_Schwarzbaum" title="Lisa Schwarzbaum">Lisa Schwarzbaum</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> rated it "A" and wrote, "Commercial director Zack Snyder, making a killer feature debut, trades homemade cheesiness for knowing style, revels in the sophistication of modern special effects, and stomps off with the best remake – er, ”re-envisioning” – of a horror classic in memory."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Scott Tobias of <i><a href="/wiki/The_A.V._Club" title="The A.V. Club">The A.V. Club</a></i> wrote that the remake streamlines the original film "by discarding everything special about it in favor of pure visceral effect".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bloody_Disgusting" title="Bloody Disgusting">Bloody Disgusting</a> ranked the film eighth in their list of the "Top 20 Horror Films of the Decade", with the article saying "Truly, you can analogize the two films [original and remake] based on their zombies alone – where Romero's lumbered and took their time (in a good way), Snyder's came at us, fast, with teeth bared like rabid dogs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> ranked it #3 in their "Top 10 Best Zombie Movies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> It was third in <a href="/wiki/Dread_Central" title="Dread Central">Dread Central</a>'s "Best Horror Films of the Decade".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/George_A._Romero" title="George A. Romero">George A. Romero</a> said, "It was better than I expected. ... The first 15, 20 minutes were terrific, but it sort of lost its reason for being. It was more of a video game. I'm not terrified of things running at me; it's like <i><a href="/wiki/Space_Invaders" title="Space Invaders">Space Invaders</a></i>. There was nothing going on underneath."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/South_Park" title="South Park">South Park</a></i> parodied the film in the episode, "<a href="/wiki/Night_of_the_Living_Homeless" title="Night of the Living Homeless">Night of the Living Homeless</a>". The show's creators, <a href="/wiki/Trey_Parker" title="Trey Parker">Trey Parker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matt_Stone" title="Matt Stone">Matt Stone</a>, called the film "amazing" in the episode's DVD commentary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparisons_to_the_original">Comparisons to the original</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dawn_of_the_Dead_(2004_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Comparisons to the original">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Original_research" role="presentation">

<p>In the original film, the <a href="/wiki/Zombie" title="Zombie">zombies</a> moved very slowly and were most menacing when they collected in large groups. In the remake, the zombies are fast and agile. Many admirers of the original, as well as Romero himself, protested this change, feeling that it limited the impact of the undead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> This is somewhat borne out by the fact that the remake has almost no close-up shots of zombies that last more than a second or two. Snyder mentions this in the commentary track of the remake's DVD, pointing out that they seem too human when the camera lingers upon them for longer. However, it was for this change that Wizard Magazine ranked the zombies #5 on their "100 Greatest Villains Ever" list.</p>
<p>The original had a smaller cast than the remake, allowing more screen time for each character. Many fans and critics criticized the resulting loss of character development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the original version, the story unfolds over several months, indicated by the advancing stages of Fran's pregnancy. In the remake, the events transpire within approximately one month, as evidenced by the supplemental feature <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_Tape:_Andy%27s_Terrifying_Last_Days_Revealed" title="The Lost Tape: Andy's Terrifying Last Days Revealed">The Lost Tape: Andy's Terrifying Last Days Revealed</a></i>, located on the DVD in the special features section. Another big change from the original is that unlike Romero, Snyder treats zombification more like a disease, pointing to the bites as the source, instead of anyone who is dead turning into a zombie.</p>
<p>Three actors from the original film have cameos in the remake, appearing on the televisions the survivors watch: <a href="/wiki/Ken_Foree" title="Ken Foree">Ken Foree</a>, who played Peter from the original, plays an evangelist who asserts that <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> is punishing mankind; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scott_H._Reiniger" title="Scott H. Reiniger">Scott H. Reiniger</a>, who played Roger in the original, plays an army general telling everyone to stay at home for safety; and <a href="/wiki/Tom_Savini" title="Tom Savini">Tom Savini</a>, who did the special effects for many of Romero's films and played the motorcycle gang member Blades in the original <i>Dawn of the Dead</i>, plays the Monroeville Sheriff explaining the only way to kill the zombies is to "shoot 'em in the head". Monroeville is also the location of the mall used in the 1978 film. In addition, a store shown in the mall is called "Gaylen Ross", an obvious tribute to actress <a href="/wiki/Gaylen_Ross" title="Gaylen Ross">Gaylen Ross</a>, who played Francine in the original film. In the beginning of the film, a helicopter that is very similar to the one in the original, flys across the screen.</p>
<p>Writing in <i>The Zombie Encyclopedia, Volume 2</i>, academic <a href="/wiki/Peter_Dendle" title="Peter Dendle">Peter Dendle</a> said that the original film "served as a bridge between the talky, slow-paced 1970s horror and the fast-paced splatter to come in the 1980s", whereas the 2004 remake "generally forsakes slow-mounting suspense in favor of frenetic action".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cancelled_and_planned_sequel">Cancelled and planned sequel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dawn_of_the_Dead_(2004_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cancelled and planned sequel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Sequel" title="Sequel">sequel</a> was planned but was later cancelled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Zack_Snyder" title="Zack Snyder">Zack Snyder</a> stated that he would only be producing the sequel instead of reprising his role as the director due to working on <i><a href="/wiki/Watchmen_(film)" title="Watchmen (film)">Watchmen</a></i> when he announced the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The script of <i>Army of the Dead</i> was written by Zack Snyder and <a href="/wiki/Joby_Harold" title="Joby Harold">Joby Harold</a>. Filming for <i>Army of the Dead</i> was to start once they got a director as the producing studios had approved the script. Also according to Deborah Snyder, the film was set in Las Vegas, and the town had to be contained to stop the outbreak of zombies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The film's producing studios were <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">Universal Studios</a> (who released the first) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Entertainment" title="Warner Bros. Entertainment">Warner Bros. Entertainment</a> (who released most of Snyder's films since <i><a href="/wiki/300_(film)" title="300 (film)">300</a></i>) and the film was set to be directed by <a href="/wiki/Matthijs_van_Heijningen_Jr." title="Matthijs van Heijningen Jr.">Matthijs van Heijningen Jr.</a>, director of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Thing_(2011_film)" title="The Thing (2011 film)">The Thing</a></i>, the 2011 prequel to <a href="/wiki/John_Carpenter" title="John Carpenter">John Carpenter</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Thing_(1982_film)" title="The Thing (1982 film)">1982 cult classic of the same name</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
