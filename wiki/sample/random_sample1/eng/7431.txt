<p><b>Colour revolution</b> (sometimes called the <b>coloured revolution</b>) or <b>color revolution</b> is a term that was widely used by worldwide media<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> to describe various related movements that developed in several societies in the <a href="/wiki/Post-Soviet_states" title="Post-Soviet states">former Soviet Union</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a> during the early 2000s. The term has also been applied to a number of revolutions elsewhere, including in the Middle East. Some observers (such as <a href="/wiki/Justin_Raimondo" title="Justin Raimondo">Justin Raimondo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Lind" title="Michael Lind">Michael Lind</a>) have called the events a <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary_wave" title="Revolutionary wave">revolutionary wave</a>, the origins of which can be traced back to the 1986 <a href="/wiki/People_Power_Revolution" title="People Power Revolution">People Power Revolution</a> (also known as the "Yellow Revolution") in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>.</p>
<p>Participants in the colour revolutions have mostly used <a href="/wiki/Nonviolent_resistance" title="Nonviolent resistance">nonviolent resistance</a>, also called <a href="/wiki/Civil_resistance" title="Civil resistance">civil resistance</a>. Such methods as demonstrations, <a href="/wiki/General_strike" title="General strike">strikes</a> and interventions have been intended protest against <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">governments</a> seen as corrupt and/or authoritarian, and to advocate <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a>; and they have also created strong pressure for change. These movements generally adopted a specific colour or flower as their symbol. The colour revolutions are notable for the important role of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organisations" title="Non-governmental organisations">non-governmental organisations</a> (NGOs) and particularly <a href="/wiki/Student_activism" title="Student activism">student activists</a> in organising creative non-violent resistance.</p>
<p>Such movements have had a measure of success, as for example in the former <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Overthrow_of_Slobodan_Milo%C5%A1evi%C4%87" title="Overthrow of Slobodan Milošević">Bulldozer Revolution</a> (2000), in <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Rose_Revolution" title="Rose Revolution">Rose Revolution</a> (2003), and in <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Orange_Revolution" title="Orange Revolution">Orange Revolution</a> (2004). In most but not all cases, massive street protests followed disputed elections, or requests for fair elections, and led to the resignation or overthrow of leaders considered by their opponents to be <a href="/wiki/Authoritarianism" title="Authoritarianism">authoritarian</a>. Some events have been called "color revolutions" but are different from the above cases in certain basic characteristics. Examples include Lebanon's <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Revolution" title="Cedar Revolution">Cedar Revolution</a> (2005); and <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a>'s Blue Revolution (2005).</p>
<p>Government figures in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, such as Defense Minister <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sergei_Shoigu" title="Sergei Shoigu">Sergei Shoigu</a> and Foreign Minister <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sergei_Lavrov" title="Sergei Lavrov">Sergei Lavrov</a>, have stated that colour revolutions are a new form of warfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> President Putin said that Russia must prevent colour revolutions, "We see what tragic consequences the wave of so-called color revolutions led to. For us this is a lesson and a warning. We should do everything necessary so that nothing similar ever happens in Russia."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<td>The 1986 <a href="/wiki/People_Power_Revolution" title="People Power Revolution">People Power Revolution</a> (also called the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epifanio_de_los_Santos_Avenue" title="Epifanio de los Santos Avenue">EDSA</a>" or the "Yellow" Revolution) in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> was the first successful non-violent uprising in the contemporary period. It was the culmination of peaceful demonstrations against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_Philippines_(1965-1986)" title="History of the Philippines (1965-1986)">rule</a> of then-<a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Philippines" title="President of the Philippines">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Marcos" title="Ferdinand Marcos">Ferdinand Marcos</a> – all of which increased after the 1983 <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_Benigno_Aquino_Jr." title="Assassination of Benigno Aquino Jr.">assassination</a> of opposition <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_the_Philippines" title="Senate of the Philippines">Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/Benigno_Aquino_Jr." title="Benigno Aquino Jr.">Benigno S. Aquino, Jr.</a> A contested <a href="/wiki/Snap_election" title="Snap election">snap election</a> on 7 February 1986 and a call by the powerful <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church_in_the_Philippines" title="Catholic Church in the Philippines">Filipino Catholic Church</a> sparked mass protests across <a href="/wiki/Metro_Manila" title="Metro Manila">Metro Manila</a> from 22–25 February. The Revolution's iconic L-shaped <a href="/wiki/Laban_sign" title="Laban sign">Laban sign</a> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Filipino_language" title="Filipino language">Filipino</a> term for People Power, "<i>Lakás ng Bayan</i>", whose acronym is "<i>LABAN</i>" ("fight"). The yellow-clad protesters, later joined by the <a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_of_the_Philippines" title="Armed Forces of the Philippines">Armed Forces</a>, ousted Marcos and installed Aquino's widow <a href="/wiki/Corazon_Aquino" title="Corazon Aquino">Corazón</a> as the country's eleventh President, ushering in the present <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Philippines_(1986%E2%80%93present)" title="History of the Philippines (1986–present)">Fifth Republic</a>.</td>

<td>The 'Bulldozer Revolution' in 2000, which led to the <a href="/wiki/Overthrow_of_Slobodan_Milo%C5%A1evi%C4%87" title="Overthrow of Slobodan Milošević">overthrow of Slobodan Milošević</a>. These demonstrations are usually considered to be the first example of the peaceful revolutions which followed. However, the Serbians adopted an approach that had already been used in parliamentary elections in <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_parliamentary_election,_1997" title="Bulgarian parliamentary election, 1997">Bulgaria (1997)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovak_parliamentary_election,_1998" title="Slovak parliamentary election, 1998">Slovakia (1998)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Croatian_parliamentary_election,_2000" title="Croatian parliamentary election, 2000">Croatia (2000)</a>, characterised by civic mobilisation through get-out-the-vote campaigns and unification of the political opposition. The nationwide protesters did not adopt a colour or a specific symbol; however, the slogan <i>"<a href="/wiki/Gotov_je" title="Gotov je">Gotov je</a>"</i> (Serbian Cyrillic: <i>Готов је</i>, English: <i>He is finished</i>) did become an aftermath symbol celebrating the completion of the task. Despite the commonalities, many others refer to Georgia as the most definite beginning of the series of "colour revolutions". The demonstrations were supported by the youth movement <a href="/wiki/Otpor!" title="Otpor!">Otpor!</a>, some of whose members were involved in the later revolutions in other countries.</td>

<td><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Purple_Revolution" title="Purple Revolution">Purple Revolution</a> was a name first used by some hopeful commentators and later picked up by United States President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> to describe the coming of democracy to Iraq following the <a href="/wiki/Iraqi_parliamentary_election,_January_2005" title="Iraqi parliamentary election, January 2005">2005 Iraqi legislative election</a> and was intentionally used to draw the parallel with the Orange and Rose revolutions. However, the name "purple revolution" has not achieved widespread use in Iraq, the United States or elsewhere. The name comes from the colour that voters' index fingers were stained to prevent fraudulent multiple voting. The term first appeared shortly after the January 2005 election in various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weblog" title="Weblog">weblogs</a> and editorials of individuals supportive of the U.S. invasion of Iraq.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The term received its widest usage during a visit by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._President" title="U.S. President">U.S. President</a> <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush stated: "In recent times, we have witnessed landmark events in the history of liberty: A Rose Revolution in Georgia, an Orange Revolution in Ukraine, and now, a Purple Revolution in Iraq."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></td>

<td>The <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Revolution" title="Cedar Revolution">Cedar Revolution</a> in Lebanon between February and April 2005 followed not a disputed election, but rather the assassination of opposition leader <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rafik_Hariri" title="Rafik Hariri">Rafik Hariri</a> in 2005. Also, instead of the annulment of an election, the people demanded an end to the <a href="/wiki/Syrian_occupation_of_Lebanon" title="Syrian occupation of Lebanon">Syrian occupation of Lebanon</a>. Nonetheless, some of its elements and some of the methods used in the protests have been similar enough that it is often considered and treated by the press and commentators as one of the series of "colour revolutions". The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lebanon_cedar" title="Lebanon cedar">Cedar of Lebanon</a> is the symbol of the country, and the revolution was named after it. The peaceful demonstrators used the colours white and red, which are found in the Lebanese flag. The protests led to the pullout of <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Syria" title="Politics of Syria">Syrian</a> troops in April 2005, ending their nearly 30-year presence there, although Syria retains some influence in Lebanon.</td>

<td><a href="/wiki/Jeans_Revolution" title="Jeans Revolution">Jeans Revolution</a></td>
<td><span class="flagicon"><img alt="" class="thumbborder" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="900" height="12" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Flag_of_Belarus_%281995-2012%29.svg/23px-Flag_of_Belarus_%281995-2012%29.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Flag_of_Belarus_%281995-2012%29.svg/35px-Flag_of_Belarus_%281995-2012%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Flag_of_Belarus_%281995-2012%29.svg/46px-Flag_of_Belarus_%281995-2012%29.svg.png 2x" width="23"/></span> <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a></td>
<td>19 March 2006</td>

<td>In <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>, there have been a number of protests against President <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Lukashenko" title="Alexander Lukashenko">Alexander Lukashenko</a>, with participation from student group <a href="/wiki/Zubr_(political_organization)" title="Zubr (political organization)">Zubr</a>. One round of protests culminated on 25 March 2005; it was a self-declared attempt to emulate the Kyrgyzstan revolution, and involved over a thousand citizens. However, police severely suppressed it, arresting over 30 people and imprisoning opposition leader <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mikhail_Marinich" title="Mikhail Marinich">Mikhail Marinich</a>.
<p>A second, much larger, round of protests began almost a year later, on 19 March 2006, soon after the <a href="/wiki/Belarusian_presidential_election,_2006" title="Belarusian presidential election, 2006">presidential election</a>. Official results had Lukashenko winning with 83% of the vote; protesters claimed the results were achieved through fraud and voter intimidation, a charge echoed by many foreign governments.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Protesters camped out in October Square in <a href="/wiki/Minsk" title="Minsk">Minsk</a> over the next week, calling variously for the resignation of Lukashenko, the installation of rival candidate <a href="/wiki/Alaksandar_Milinkievi%C4%8D" title="Alaksandar Milinkievič">Alaksandar Milinkievič</a>, and new, fair elections.</p>
<p>The opposition originally used as a symbol the white-red-white former <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Belarus" title="Flag of Belarus">flag of Belarus</a>; the movement has had significant connections with that in neighbouring Ukraine, and during the Orange Revolution some white-red-white flags were seen being waved in Kiev. During the 2006 protests some called it the "<a href="/wiki/Jeans_Revolution" title="Jeans Revolution">Jeans Revolution</a>" or "Denim Revolution",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BELAFRAUD_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-BELAFRAUD-9">[9]</a></sup> blue jeans being considered a symbol for freedom. Some protesters cut up jeans into ribbons and hung them in public places.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It is claimed that Zubr was responsible for coining the phrase.</p>
<p>Lukashenko has said in the past: "In our country, there will be no pink or orange, or even banana revolution." More recently he's said "They [the West] think that Belarus is ready for some 'orange' or, what is a rather frightening option, 'blue' or '<a href="/wiki/Cornflower_blue" title="Cornflower blue">cornflower blue</a>' revolution. Such 'blue' revolutions are the last thing we need".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ucpb.org_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ucpb.org-10">[10]</a></sup> On 19 April 2005, he further commented: "All these coloured revolutions are pure and simple banditry."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eubusiness.com_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-eubusiness.com-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influencing_factors">Influencing factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Influencing factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-Communist_revolutions">Anti-Communist revolutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Anti-Communist revolutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many have cited the influence of <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1989" title="Revolutions of 1989">the series of revolutions</a> which occurred in Central and Eastern Europe in the late 1980s and early 1990s, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Velvet_Revolution" title="Velvet Revolution">Velvet Revolution</a> in <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a> in 1989. A peaceful demonstration by students (mostly from <a href="/wiki/Charles_University" title="Charles University">Charles University</a>) was attacked by the police – and in time contributed to the collapse of the communist regime in Czechoslovakia. Yet the roots of the pacifist floral imagery may go even further back to the non-violent <a href="/wiki/Carnation_Revolution" title="Carnation Revolution">Carnation Revolution</a> of Portugal in April 1974, which is associated with the colour carnation because <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carnation" title="Carnation">carnations</a> were worn, and the 1986 <a href="/wiki/People_Power_Revolution" title="People Power Revolution">Yellow Revolution</a> in the Philippines where demonstrators offered peace flowers to military personnel manning armoured tanks.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_movements">Student movements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Student movements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first of these was <a href="/wiki/Otpor!" title="Otpor!">Otpor!</a> ("Resistance!") in Serbia, which was founded at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belgrade_University" title="Belgrade University">Belgrade University</a> in October 1998 and began protesting against Miloševic' during the <a href="/wiki/Kosovo_War" title="Kosovo War">Kosovo War</a>. Most of them were already veterans of anti-Milošević demonstrations such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1996%E2%80%931997_protests_in_Serbia" title="1996–1997 protests in Serbia">1996-97 protests</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/March_9,_1991_protest" title="March 9, 1991 protest">9 March 1991 protest</a>. Many of its members were arrested or beaten by the police. Despite this, during the presidential campaign in September 2000, Otpor launched its "<a href="/wiki/Gotov_je" title="Gotov je">Gotov je</a>" (He's finished) campaign that galvanised Serbian discontent with Miloševic' and resulted in his defeat.</p>
<p>Members of Otpor have inspired and trained members of related student movements including <a href="/wiki/Kmara" title="Kmara">Kmara</a> in Georgia, Pora in Ukraine, <a href="/wiki/Zubr_(political_organization)" title="Zubr (political organization)">Zubr</a> in Belarus and <a href="/wiki/MJAFT!" title="MJAFT!">MJAFT!</a> in Albania. These groups have been explicit and scrupulous in their practice of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-violent_resistance" title="Non-violent resistance">non-violent resistance</a> as advocated and explained in <a href="/wiki/Gene_Sharp" title="Gene Sharp">Gene Sharp</a>'s writings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The massive protests that they have organised, which were essential to the successes in Serbia, Georgia and Ukraine, have been notable for their colourfulness and use of ridiculing humor in opposing authoritarian leaders.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_analysis">Critical analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Critical analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russian_assessment">Russian assessment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Russian assessment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Cordesman" title="Anthony Cordesman">Anthony Cordesman</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Strategic_and_International_Studies" title="Center for Strategic and International Studies">Center for Strategic and International Studies</a>, Russian military leaders view the color revolutions as a "new US and European approach to warfare that focuses on creating destabilizing revolutions in other states as a means of serving their security interests at low cost and with minimal casualties."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_view">Chinese view</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Chinese view">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Articles published by the <a href="/wiki/Global_Times" title="Global Times">Global Times</a>, a state-run nationalist newspaper, indicate that Chinese leaders also anticipate the Western powers, such as the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, using 'color revolutions' as a mean to undermine the one-party state. An article published 2016-5-8 claims: A variation of containment seeks to press China on human rights and democracy with the hope of creating a "color revolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions_and_connected_movements_in_other_countries">Reactions and connected movements in other countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reactions and connected movements in other countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Armenia">Armenia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Armenia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aram_Karapetyan" title="Aram Karapetyan">Aram Karapetyan</a>, leader of the New Times political party in <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a>, has declared his intention to start a "revolution from below" in April 2005, saying that the situation was different now that people had seen the developments in the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Independent_States" title="Commonwealth of Independent States">CIS</a>. He added that the Armenian revolution will be peaceful but not have a colour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ARMENIA_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-ARMENIA-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Azerbaijan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A number of movements were created in <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a> in mid-2005, inspired by the examples of both Georgia and Ukraine. A youth group, calling itself <a href="/wiki/Yox!" title="Yox!">Yox!</a> (which means No!), declared its opposition to governmental corruption. The leader of Yox! said that unlike Pora or <a href="/wiki/Kmara" title="Kmara">Kmara</a>, he wants to change not just the leadership, but the entire system of governance in Azerbaijan. The Yox movement chose green as its colour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AZERBAIJAN_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-AZERBAIJAN-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The spearhead of Azerbaijan's attempted colour revolution was Yeni Fekir ("New Idea"), a youth group closely aligned with the Azadlig (Freedom) Bloc of opposition political parties. Along with groups such as Magam ("It's Time") and Dalga ("Wave"), Yeni Fekir deliberately adopted many of the tactics of the Georgian and Ukrainian colour revolution groups, even borrowing the colour orange from the Ukrainian revolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-POISED_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-POISED-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FLASHMOB_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FLASHMOB-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2005 protesters took to the streets, waving orange flags and banners, to protest what they considered government fraud in recent parliamentary elections.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Azerbaijani colour revolution finally fizzled out with the police riot on 26 November, during which dozens of protesters were injured and perhaps hundreds teargassed and sprayed with water cannons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRUSH_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRUSH-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Bangladesh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2013_Shahbag_protests" title="2013 Shahbag protests">2013 Shahbag protests</a></div>
<p>In February 5, 2013, protests began in <a href="/wiki/Shahbag" title="Shahbag">Shahbag</a> and later spread to other parts of <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> following demands for capital punishment for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abdul_Quader_Mollah" title="Abdul Quader Mollah">Abdul Quader Mollah</a>, who had been sentenced to life imprisonment, and for others convicted of war crimes by the International Crimes Tribunal of <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-act-1973_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-act-1973-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters-1_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-reuters-1-30">[30]</a></sup> On that day, the <a href="/wiki/International_Crimes_Tribunal_(Bangladesh)" title="International Crimes Tribunal (Bangladesh)">International Crimes Tribunal</a> had sentenced Mollah to life in prison after he was convicted on five of six counts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_crimes" title="War crimes">war crimes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brief_background_history_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brief_background_history-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rabbi_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rabbi-32">[32]</a></sup> Later demands included banning the <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh_Jamaat-e-Islami" title="Bangladesh Jamaat-e-Islami">Bangladesh Jamaat-e-Islami</a> party from politics including election and a boycott of institutions supporting (or affiliated with) the party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tribune-ban_Jamaat_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-tribune-ban_Jamaat-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Protesters considered Mollah's sentence too lenient, given his crimes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OUTRAGED_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-OUTRAGED-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Verdict_surprises_some_top_jurists_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Verdict_surprises_some_top_jurists-35">[35]</a></sup> Bloggers and online activists called for additional protests at Shahbag.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ajstream-1_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-ajstream-1-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Priyo_compilation_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Priyo_compilation-37">[37]</a></sup> Tens of thousands of people joined the demonstration, which gave rise to protests across the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>The movement demanding trial of war criminals is a protest movement in Bangladesh, from 1972 to present.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Belarus">Belarus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Belarus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Belarusian_democracy_movement" title="Belarusian democracy movement">Belarusian democracy movement</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jeans_Revolution" title="Jeans Revolution">Jeans Revolution</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>, there have been a number of protests against President <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Lukashenko" title="Alexander Lukashenko">Alexander Lukashenko</a>, with participation from student group <a href="/wiki/Zubr_(political_organization)" title="Zubr (political organization)">Zubr</a>. One round of protests culminated on 25 March 2005; it was a self-declared attempt to emulate the Kyrgyzstan revolution, and involved over a thousand citizens. However, police severely suppressed it, arresting over 30 people and imprisoning opposition leader <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mikhail_Marinich" title="Mikhail Marinich">Mikhail Marinich</a>.</p>
<p>A second, much larger, round of protests began almost a year later, on 19 March 2006, soon after the <a href="/wiki/Belarusian_presidential_election,_2006" title="Belarusian presidential election, 2006">presidential election</a>. Official results had Lukashenko winning with 83% of the vote; protesters claimed the results were achieved through fraud and voter intimidation, a charge echoed by many foreign governments.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Protesters camped out in October Square in <a href="/wiki/Minsk" title="Minsk">Minsk</a> over the next week, calling variously for the resignation of Lukashenko, the installation of rival candidate <a href="/wiki/Alaksandar_Milinkievi%C4%8D" title="Alaksandar Milinkievič">Alaksandar Milinkievič</a>, and new, fair elections.</p>
<p>The opposition originally used as a symbol the white-red-white former <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Belarus" title="Flag of Belarus">flag of Belarus</a>; the movement has had significant connections with that in neighbouring Ukraine, and during the Orange Revolution some white-red-white flags were seen being waved in Kiev. During the 2006 protests some called it the "<a href="/wiki/Jeans_Revolution" title="Jeans Revolution">Jeans Revolution</a>" or "Denim Revolution",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BELAFRAUD_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-BELAFRAUD-9">[9]</a></sup> blue jeans being considered a symbol for freedom. Some protesters cut up jeans into ribbons and hung them in public places.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It is claimed that Zubr was responsible for coining the phrase.</p>
<p>Lukashenko has said in the past: "In our country, there will be no pink or orange, or even banana revolution." More recently he's said "They [the West] think that Belarus is ready for some 'orange' or, what is a rather frightening option, 'blue' or '<a href="/wiki/Cornflower_blue" title="Cornflower blue">cornflower blue</a>' revolution. Such 'blue' revolutions are the last thing we need".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ucpb.org_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-ucpb.org-10">[10]</a></sup> On 19 April 2005, he further commented: "All these coloured revolutions are pure and simple banditry."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eubusiness.com_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-eubusiness.com-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Burma">Burma</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Burma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Saffron_Revolution" title="Saffron Revolution">Saffron Revolution</a></div>
<p>In Burma (officially called Myanmar), a series of anti-government protests were referred to in the press as the <a href="/wiki/Saffron_Revolution" title="Saffron Revolution">Saffron Revolution</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timesonline.co.uk_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-timesonline.co.uk-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3_Web_.E2.80.93_http:.2F.2Fm3web.bg_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-M3_Web_.E2.80.93_http:.2F.2Fm3web.bg-13">[13]</a></sup> after Buddhist monks (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theravada_Buddhism" title="Theravada Buddhism">Theravada Buddhist</a> monks normally wear the colour saffron) took the vanguard of the protests. A previous, student-led revolution, the <a href="/wiki/8888_Uprising" title="8888 Uprising">8888 Uprising</a> on 8 August 1988, had similarities to the colour revolutions, but was violently repressed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Chinese_democracy_movement" title="Chinese democracy movement">Chinese democracy movement</a> and <a href="/wiki/2011_Chinese_pro-democracy_protests" title="2011 Chinese pro-democracy protests">2011 Chinese pro-democracy protests</a></div>
<p>A call which first appeared on 17 February 2011 on the Chinese language site <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boxun.com" title="Boxun.com">Boxun.com</a> in the United States for a "Jasmine revolution" in the People's Republic of China and repeated on social networking sites in China resulted in blocking of internet searches for "jasmine" and a heavy police presence at designated sites for protest such as the McDonald's in central Beijing, one of the 13 designated protest sites, on 20 February 2011. A crowd did gather there, but their motivations were ambiguous as a crowd tends to draw a crowd in that area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andrew_Jacobs_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Andrew_Jacobs-17">[17]</a></sup> Boxun experienced a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denial_of_service_attack" title="Denial of service attack">denial of service attack</a> during this period and was inaccessible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cara_Anna.2C_Associated_Press_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cara_Anna.2C_Associated_Press-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Moldova">Moldova</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Moldova">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2009_Moldova_civil_unrest" title="2009 Moldova civil unrest">2009 Moldova civil unrest</a></div>

<p>The opposition is reported to have hoped for and urged some kind of Orange revolution, similar to that in Ukraine, in the follow-up of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moldovan_parliamentary_elections,_2005" title="Moldovan parliamentary elections, 2005">Moldovan parliamentary elections, 2005</a>, while the <a href="/wiki/Christian-Democratic_People%27s_Party_(Moldova)" title="Christian-Democratic People's Party (Moldova)">Christian Democratic People's Party</a> adopted orange for its colour in a clear reference to the events of Ukraine.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>A name hypothesised for such an event was "Grape Revolution" because of the abundance of vineyards in the country; however, such a revolution failed to materialise after the governmental victory in the elections. Many reasons have been given for this, including a fractured opposition and the fact that the government had already co-opted many of the political positions that might have united the opposition (such as a perceived pro-European and anti-Russian stance). Also the elections themselves were declared fairer in the OSCE election monitoring reports than had been the case in other countries where similar revolutions occurred, even though the CIS monitoring mission strongly condemned them.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>There was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2009_Moldova_civil_unrest" title="2009 Moldova civil unrest">civil unrest</a> all over Moldova following the <a href="/wiki/Moldovan_parliamentary_election,_April_2009" title="Moldovan parliamentary election, April 2009">2009 Parliamentary election</a> due to the opposition claiming that the communists had fixed the election. Eventually, the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_for_European_Integration" title="Alliance for European Integration">Alliance for European Integration</a> created a governing coalition that pushed the <a href="/wiki/Party_of_Communists_of_the_Republic_of_Moldova" title="Party of Communists of the Republic of Moldova">Communist party</a> into opposition.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mongolia">Mongolia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Mongolia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 25 March 2005, activists wearing yellow scarves held protests in the capital city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulan_Bator" title="Ulan Bator">Ulan Bator</a>, disputing the results of the <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_legislative_election,_2004" title="Mongolian legislative election, 2004">2004 Mongolian parliamentary elections</a> and calling for fresh elections. One of the chants heard in that protest was "Let's congratulate our Kyrgyz brothers for their revolutionary spirit. Let's free Mongolia of corruption."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>An uprising commenced in Ulan Bator on 1 July 2008, with a peaceful meeting in protest of the election of 29 June. The results of these elections were (it was claimed by opposition political parties) corrupted by the <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_People%27s_Party" title="Mongolian People's Party">Mongolian People's Party</a> (MPRP). Approximately 30,000 people took part in the meeting. Afterwards, some of the protesters left the central square and moved to the HQ of the Mongolian People's Revolutionary Party - which they attacked and then burned down. A police station was also attacked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC1_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC1-40">[40]</a></sup> By the night rioters vandalised and then set fire to the Cultural Palace (which contained a theatre, museum and National art gallery). Cars torching,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-41">[41]</a></sup> bank robberies and looting were reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC1_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC1-40">[40]</a></sup> The organisations in the burning buildings were vandalised and looted. Police used tear gas, rubber bullets and water cannon against stone-throwing protesters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC1_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBC1-40">[40]</a></sup> A 4-day state of emergency was installed, the capital has been placed under a 2200 to 0800 curfew, and alcohol sales banned,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC2_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2-42">[42]</a></sup> rioting not resumed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC3_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC3-43">[43]</a></sup> 5 people were shot dead by the police<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, dozens of teenagers were wounded from the police firearms<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and disabled and 800 people, including the leaders of the civil movements J. Batzandan, O. Magnai and B. Jargalsakhan, were arrested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> International observers said 1 July general election was free and fair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC4_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC4-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pakistan">Pakistan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Pakistan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Lawyers%27_Movement" title="Lawyers' Movement">Lawyers' Movement</a> and <a href="/wiki/Movement_to_impeach_Pervez_Musharraf" title="Movement to impeach Pervez Musharraf">Movement to impeach Pervez Musharraf</a></div>
<p>In 2007 the <a href="/wiki/Lawyers%27_Movement" title="Lawyers' Movement">Lawyers' Movement</a> started in <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> with the aim of restoration of deposed judges. However, within a month the movement took a turn and started working towards the goal of removing <a href="/wiki/Pervez_Musharraf" title="Pervez Musharraf">Pervez Musharraf</a> from power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia">Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_opposition" title="Russian opposition">Russian opposition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dissenters%27_March" title="Dissenters' March">Dissenters' March</a>, <a href="/wiki/Strategy-31" title="Strategy-31">Strategy-31</a>, and <a href="/wiki/2011%E2%80%9313_Russian_protests" title="2011–13 Russian protests">2011–13 Russian protests</a></div>
<p>The liberal opposition in Russia is represented by several parties and movements.</p>
<p>An active part of the opposition is the <a href="/wiki/Oborona" title="Oborona">Oborona</a> youth movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Oborona claims that its aim is to provide free and honest elections and to establish in Russia a system with democratic political competition. This movement under leadership of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Oleg_Kozlovsky&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Oleg Kozlovsky (page does not exist)">Oleg Kozlovsky</a> is one of the most active and radical ones and is represented in a number of Russian cities. The movement contributed with its activities recently during the elections of 8 September 2013 to the success of Navalny in Moscow and other opposition candidates in various regions and towns of Russia. The "oboronkis" also took part with other oppositional groups in protests against fraud in the Moscow mayoral elections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 2012 protests it was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aleksei_Navalny" title="Aleksei Navalny">Aleksei Navalny</a>, who mobilized with support of the various and fractured oppositional parties and groups masses of young people against alleged repression and fraud of the Kremlin apparatus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> After a strong campaign for the 8 September elections in Moscow and many regions the opposition had won remarkable successes. So Navalny reached in Moscow a second place with surprising 27% behind Kremlin-backed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sergei_Sobyanin" title="Sergei Sobyanin">Sergei Sobyanin</a> with 51%. In other regions oppositions candidates received remarkable successes. So in the big industrial town Yekaterinburg In 2013 opposition candidate <a href="/wiki/Yevgeny_Roizman" title="Yevgeny Roizman">Yevgeny Roizman</a> received the majority of votes and became the mayor of that town. The slow but gradual sequence of opposition successes reached by mass protests, election campaigns and other peaceful strategies has been recently called by observers and analysts as of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_Free_Europe" title="Radio Free Europe">Radio Free Europe</a> "Tortoise Revolution" in contrast to the radical "rose" or "orange" ones the Kremlin tried to prevent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>The opposition in the <a href="/wiki/Republics_of_Russia" title="Republics of Russia">Republic</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bashkortostan" title="Bashkortostan">Bashkortostan</a> has held protests demanding that the federal authorities intervene to dismiss <a href="/wiki/Murtaza_Rakhimov" title="Murtaza Rakhimov">Murtaza Rakhimov</a> from his position as president of the republic, accusing him of leading an "arbitrary, corrupt, and violent" regime. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Airat_Dilmukhametov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Airat Dilmukhametov (page does not exist)">Airat Dilmukhametov</a>, one of the opposition leaders, and leader of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bashkir_National_Front&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bashkir National Front (page does not exist)">Bashkir National Front</a>, has said that the opposition movement has been inspired from the mass protests of Ukraine and Kyrgyzstan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Another opposition leader, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Marat_Khaiyirulin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Marat Khaiyirulin (page does not exist)">Marat Khaiyirulin</a>, has said that if an Orange Revolution were to happen in Russia, it would begin in Bashkortostan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Uzbekistan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a>, there has been longstanding opposition to President <a href="/wiki/Islam_Karimov" title="Islam Karimov">Islam Karimov</a>, from liberals and Islamists. Following protests in 2005, security forces in Uzbekistan carried out the <a href="/wiki/Andijan_massacre" title="Andijan massacre">Andijan massacre</a> that successfully halted country-wide demonstrations. These protests otherwise could have turned into colour revolution, according to many analysts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>The revolution in neighbouring Kyrgyzstan began in the largely ethnic Uzbek south, and received early support in the city of <a href="/wiki/Osh" title="Osh">Osh</a>. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nigora_Hidoyatova&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nigora Hidoyatova (page does not exist)">Nigora Hidoyatova</a>, leader of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Free_Peasants&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Free Peasants (page does not exist)">Free Peasants</a> opposition party, has referred to the idea of a peasant revolt or 'Cotton Revolution'. She also said that her party is collaborating with the youth organisation <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiddat" title="Shiddat">Shiddat</a>, and that she hopes it can evolve to an organisation similar to Kmara or Pora.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Other nascent youth organisations in and for Uzbekistan include <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bolga_organisation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bolga organisation (page does not exist)">Bolga</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Freeuzbek&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Freeuzbek (page does not exist)">freeuzbek</a> group.</p>
<p>Uzbekistan has also had an active Islamist movement, led by the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Movement_of_Uzbekistan" title="Islamic Movement of Uzbekistan">Islamic Movement of Uzbekistan</a>, most notable for the <a href="/wiki/1999_Tashkent_bombings" title="1999 Tashkent bombings">1999 Tashkent bombings</a>, though the group was largely destroyed following the 2001 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NATO_invasion_of_Afghanistan" title="NATO invasion of Afghanistan">NATO invasion of Afghanistan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Response_in_other_countries">Response in other countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Response in other countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When groups of young people protested the closure of Venezuela's <a href="/wiki/RCTV" title="RCTV">RCTV</a> television station in June 2007, president <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="Hugo Chávez">Hugo Chávez</a> said that he believed the protests were organised by the West in an attempt to promote a "soft coup" like the revolutions in Ukraine and Georgia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Similarly, Chinese authorities claimed repeatedly in the state-run media that the <a href="/wiki/2014_Hong_Kong_protests" title="2014 Hong Kong protests">2014 Hong Kong protests</a>, known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umbrella_Revolution" title="Umbrella Revolution">Umbrella Revolution</a>, was organised and controlled by the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2007, Iranian state television released footage of two Iranian-American prisoners, both of whom work for western NGOs, as part of a documentary called "In the Name of Democracy." The documentary purportedly discusses the colour revolutions in Ukraine and Georgia and accuses the United States of attempting to foment a similar ouster in Iran.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_examples_of_color_revolutions_and_political_movements_around_the_world">Other examples of color revolutions and political movements around the world</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Other examples of color revolutions and political movements around the world">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The imagery of a colour revolution has been adopted by various non-revolutionary electoral campaigns. The 'Purple Revolution' social media campaign of <a href="/wiki/Naheed_Nenshi" title="Naheed Nenshi">Naheed Nenshi</a> catapulted his platform from 8% to become Calgary's 36th Mayor. The platform advocated city sustainability and to inspire the high voter turn out of 56%, particularly among young voters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015 the <a href="/wiki/New_Democratic_Party" title="New Democratic Party">NDP</a> of Alberta earned a majority mandate and ended the 44-year-old dynasty of the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Conservative_Association_of_Alberta" title="Progressive Conservative Association of Alberta">Progressive Conservatives</a>. During the campaign <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Notley" title="Rachel Notley">Rachel Notley</a>'s popularity gained momentum, and the news and NDP supporters referred to this phenomenon as the "Orange Crush" per the party's colour. NDP parodies of Orange flavored <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crush_(beverage)" title="Crush (beverage)">Crush</a> soda logo became a popular meme on social media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
