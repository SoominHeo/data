<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Embolus</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">This article is about the medical use of the term. For its use in spider anatomy, see <a href="/wiki/Palpal_bulb" title="Palpal bulb">Palpal bulb</a>.</div>
<p>An <b>embolus</b> (plural <b>emboli</b>; from the Greek ἔμβολος "wedge", "plug") is an unattached mass that travels through the <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">bloodstream</a> and is capable of clogging arterial capillary beds (create an <a href="/wiki/Vascular_occlusion" title="Vascular occlusion">arterial occlusion</a>) at a site distant from its point of origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robbins.26Cotran_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Robbins.26Cotran-1">[1]</a></sup> There are a number of different types of emboli, including <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> clots, <a href="/wiki/Cholesterol" title="Cholesterol">cholesterol</a> plaque or crystals, fat globules, gas bubbles, and <a href="/wiki/Foreign_body" title="Foreign body">foreign bodies</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Blausen_0089_BloodClot_Motion.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="930" data-file-width="700" height="292" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Blausen_0089_BloodClot_Motion.png/220px-Blausen_0089_BloodClot_Motion.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Blausen_0089_BloodClot_Motion.png/330px-Blausen_0089_BloodClot_Motion.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Blausen_0089_BloodClot_Motion.png/440px-Blausen_0089_BloodClot_Motion.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>By contrast there are non-traveling blockages that develop locally from vascular trauma or epithelial pathology and <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">vascular inflammation</a> — such as <a href="/wiki/Atheroma" title="Atheroma">atheromata</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thrombus" title="Thrombus">thrombi</a>. However, if a thrombus breaks loose from its genesis site it becomes a thrombo-embolus and if not broken down during transit, may cause <a href="/wiki/Embolism" title="Embolism">embolism</a>(s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term was coined in 1848 by <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Virchow" title="Rudolf Virchow">Rudolf Virchow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_by_substance">Classification by substance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Embolus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification by substance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thromboembolism" title="Thromboembolism">Thromboembolism</a> – embolism of thrombus (blood clot).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cholesterol_embolism" title="Cholesterol embolism">Cholesterol embolism</a> – embolism of cholesterol, often from atherosclerotic plaque inside a vessel.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fat_embolism" title="Fat embolism">Fat embolism</a> – embolism of bone fracture or fat droplets.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Air_embolism" title="Air embolism">Air embolism</a> (also known as a gas embolism) – embolism of air bubbles.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Septic_embolism" title="Septic embolism">Septic embolism</a> – embolism of bacteria-containing pus.</li>
<li>Tissue embolism – embolism of small fragments of tissue.</li>
<li>Foreign body embolism – embolism of foreign materials such as talc and other small objects.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Amniotic_fluid_embolism" title="Amniotic fluid embolism">Amniotic fluid embolism</a> – embolism of amniotic fluid, foetal cells, hair, or other debris that enters the mother's bloodstream via the placental bed of the uterus and triggers an allergic reaction.</li>
</ul>
<p>In <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thromboembolism" title="Thromboembolism">thromboembolism</a></b>, the thrombus (blood clot) from a blood vessel is completely or partially detached from the site of thrombosis (clot). The blood flow will then carry the embolus (via blood vessels) to various parts of the body where it can block the lumen (vessel cavity) and cause vessel obstruction or occlusion. The free-moving thrombus is called an embolus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A thrombus is always attached to the vessel wall and is never freely moving in the blood circulation. This is also the key difference for pathologists to determine the cause of a blood clot, either by thrombosis or by post-mortem blood clot. Vessel obstruction will then lead to different pathological issues such as blood <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stasis_(medicine)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stasis (medicine) (page does not exist)">stasis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ischemia" title="Ischemia">ischemia</a>. However, not only thromboembolism will cause the obstruction of blood flow in vessels, but also any kind of embolism is capable of causing the same problem.</p>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Fat_embolism" title="Fat embolism">Fat embolism</a></b> usually occurs when endogenous (from sources within the organism) fat tissue escapes into the blood circulation. The usual cause of fat embolism is therefore the <a href="/wiki/Fracture" title="Fracture">fracture</a> of tubular bones (such as the <a href="/wiki/Femur" title="Femur">femur</a>), which will lead to the leakage of fat tissue within the bone marrow into ruptured vessels. There are also exogenous (from sources of external origin) causes such as intravenous injection of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emulsions" title="Emulsions">emulsions</a>.</p>
<p>An <b><a href="/wiki/Air_embolism" title="Air embolism">air embolism</a></b>, on the other hand, is usually always caused by exogenic factors. This can be the rupture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveoli" title="Alveoli">alveoli</a>, and inhaled air can be leaked into the blood vessels. Other more-common causes include the puncture of the <a href="/wiki/Subclavian_vein" title="Subclavian vein">subclavian vein</a> by accident or during operation where there is negative pressure. Air is then sucked into the veins by the negative pressure caused by thoracic expansion during the inhalation phase of respiration. Air embolism can also happen during intravenous therapy, when air is leaked into the system (however this <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iatrogenic" title="Iatrogenic">iatrogenic</a> error in modern medicine is extremely rare).</p>
<p><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_embolism" title="Gas embolism">Gas embolism</a></b> is a common concern for deep-sea divers because the gases in human blood (usually nitrogen and helium) can be easily dissolved at higher amounts during the descent into deep sea. However, when the diver ascends to the normal atmospheric pressure, the gases become insoluble, causing the formation of small bubbles in the blood. This is also known as <a href="/wiki/Decompression_sickness" title="Decompression sickness">decompression sickness</a> or the bends. This phenomenon is explained by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry%27s_Law" title="Henry's Law">Henry's Law</a> in physical chemistry.</p>
<p>Embolism by <b>other materials</b> are rather rare. Septic embolism happens when a purulent tissue (<a href="/wiki/Pus" title="Pus">pus</a>-containing tissue) is dislodged from its original focus. Tissue embolism is a near-equivalent to <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> <a href="/wiki/Metastasis" title="Metastasis">metastasis</a>, which happens when cancer tissue infiltrates blood vessels, and small fragments of them are released into the blood stream. Foreign-body embolism happens when exogenous—and only exogenous—materials such as <a href="/wiki/Talc" title="Talc">talc</a> enter the blood stream and cause occlusion or obstruction of blood circulation. <a href="/wiki/Bullet" title="Bullet">Bullet</a> embolism occurs in approximately 0.3% cases of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gunshot_wound" title="Gunshot wound">gunshot wounds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amniotic-fluid_embolism" title="Amniotic-fluid embolism">Amniotic-fluid embolism</a> is a rare complication of childbirth.</p>
