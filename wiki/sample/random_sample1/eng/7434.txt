<p>An <b>achromatic lens</b> or <b>achromat</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">lens</a> that is designed to limit the effects of <a href="/wiki/Chromatic_aberration" title="Chromatic aberration">chromatic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spherical_aberration" title="Spherical aberration">spherical aberration</a>. Achromatic lenses are corrected to bring two wavelengths (typically red and blue) into focus in the same plane.</p>
<p>The most common type of achromat is the <b>achromatic <a href="/wiki/Doublet_(lens)" title="Doublet (lens)">doublet</a></b>, which is composed of two individual lenses made from <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glasses</a> with different amounts of <a href="/wiki/Dispersion_(optics)" title="Dispersion (optics)">dispersion</a>. Typically, one element is a negative (<a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)#Types_of_simple_lenses" title="Lens (optics)">concave</a>) element made out of <a href="/wiki/Flint_glass" title="Flint glass">flint glass</a> such as F2, which has relatively high dispersion, and the other is a positive (<a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)#Types_of_simple_lenses" title="Lens (optics)">convex</a>) element made of <a href="/wiki/Crown_glass_(optics)" title="Crown glass (optics)">crown glass</a> such as BK7, which has lower dispersion. The lens elements are mounted next to each other, often cemented together, and shaped so that the chromatic aberration of one is counterbalanced by that of the other.</p>
<p>In the most common type (shown), the positive <a href="/wiki/Optical_power" title="Optical power">power</a> of the crown lens element is not quite equalled by the negative power of the flint lens element. Together they form a weak positive lens that will bring two different <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a> of light to a common <a href="/wiki/Focus_(optics)" title="Focus (optics)">focus</a>. Negative doublets, in which the negative-power element predominates, are also made.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Achromatic_lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Theoretical considerations of the feasibility of correcting chromatic aberration were debated in the 18th century following <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Newton</a>'s statement that such a correction was impossible (see <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_telescope#Achromatic_refracting_telescopes" title="History of the telescope">History of the telescope</a>). Credit for the invention of the first achromatic doublet is often given to an English <a href="/wiki/Barrister" title="Barrister">barrister</a> and amateur optician named <a href="/wiki/Chester_Moore_Hall" title="Chester Moore Hall">Chester Moore Hall</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daumas_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-daumas-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stargazer_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stargazer-2">[2]</a></sup> Hall wished to keep his work on the achromatic lenses a secret and contracted the manufacture of the <a href="/wiki/Crown_glass_(optics)" title="Crown glass (optics)">crown</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flint_glass" title="Flint glass">flint</a> lenses to two different opticians, Edward Scarlett and James Mann.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> They in turn sub-contracted the work to the same person, <a href="/wiki/George_Bass_(optician)" title="George Bass (optician)">George Bass</a>. He realized the two components were for the same client and, after fitting the two parts together, noted the achromatic properties. Hall failed to appreciate the importance of his invention, and it remained known to only a few opticians.</p>
<p>In the late 1750s, Bass mentioned Hall's lenses to <a href="/wiki/John_Dollond" title="John Dollond">John Dollond</a>, who understood their potential and was able to reproduce their design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stargazer_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stargazer-2">[2]</a></sup> Dollond applied for and was granted a patent on the technology in 1758, which led to bitter fights with other opticians over the right to make and sell achromatic doublets.</p>
<p>Dollond's son <a href="/wiki/Peter_Dollond" title="Peter Dollond">Peter</a> invented the <a href="/wiki/Apochromat" title="Apochromat">apochromat</a>, an improvement on the achromat, in 1763.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stargazer_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stargazer-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Achromatic_lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several different types of achromat have been devised. They differ in the shape of the included lens elements as well as in the optical properties of their glass (most notably in their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_dispersion" title="Optical dispersion">optical dispersion</a> or <a href="/wiki/Abbe_number" title="Abbe number">Abbe number</a>).</p>
<p>In the following, 'R' denotes the <a href="/wiki/Radius" title="Radius">radius</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">spheres</a> that define the optically relevant <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refracting</a> lens surfaces. By convention, R<sub>1</sub> denotes the first lens surface counted from the object. A doublet lens has four surfaces with radii R<sub>1</sub> to R<sub>4</sub>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Littrow_doublet">Littrow doublet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Achromatic_lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Littrow doublet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Uses an equiconvex crown glass lens with R<sub>1</sub>=R<sub>2</sub>, and a second flint glass lens with R<sub>3</sub>=-R<sub>2</sub>. The back of the flint glass lens is flat. A Littrow doublet can produce a ghost image between R<sub>2</sub> and R<sub>3</sub> because the lens surfaces of the two lenses have the same radii. When used in a telescope, it may also produce a ghost image between the flat R<sub>4</sub> surface and rear of the tube.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fraunhofer_doublet_.28Fraunhofer_objective.29">Fraunhofer doublet (Fraunhofer objective)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Achromatic_lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fraunhofer doublet (Fraunhofer objective)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first lens has positive refractive power, the second negative. R<sub>1</sub> is set greater than R<sub>2</sub>, and R<sub>2</sub> is set close to, but not equal to, R<sub>3</sub>. R<sub>4</sub> is usually greater than R<sub>3</sub>. In a Fraunhofer doublet, the dissimilar curvatures of R<sub>2</sub> and R<sub>3</sub> are mounted close, but not in contact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This design yields more degrees of freedom (one more free radius, length of the air space) to correct for <a href="/wiki/Optical_aberration" title="Optical aberration">optical aberrations</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clark_doublet">Clark doublet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Achromatic_lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Clark doublet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Uses an equiconvex crown with R<sub>1</sub>=R<sub>2</sub>, and a flint with R<sub>3</sub>≃R<sub>2</sub> and R<sub>4</sub>≫R<sub>3</sub>. R<sub>3</sub> is set slightly shorter than R<sub>2</sub> to create a focus mismatch between R<sub>2</sub> and R<sub>3</sub>, thereby reducing ghosting between the crown and flint.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil-spaced_doublet">Oil-spaced doublet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Achromatic_lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Oil-spaced doublet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The use of oil between the crown and flint eliminates the effect of ghosting, particularly where R<sub>2</sub>=R<sub>3</sub>. It can also increase light transmission slightly and reduce the impact of errors in R<sub>2</sub> and R<sub>3</sub>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steinheil_doublet">Steinheil doublet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Achromatic_lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Steinheil doublet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Steinheil doublet, devised by <a href="/wiki/Carl_August_von_Steinheil" title="Carl August von Steinheil">Carl August von Steinheil</a>, is a flint-first doublet. In contrast to the Fraunhofer doublet, it has a negative lens first followed by a positive lens. It needs stronger curvature than the Fraunhofer doublet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialyte">Dialyte</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Achromatic_lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dialyte">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dialyte_lens" title="Dialyte lens">Dialyte lenses</a> have a wide air space between the two elements. They were originally devised in the 19th century to allow much smaller flint glass elements down stream since flint glass was hard to produce and expensive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> They are also lenses where R<sub>2</sub> and R<sub>3</sub> can not be cemented because they have dissimilar curvatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
