<p>The <b>Basilica of Saint Denis</b> (French: <span lang="fr"><i>known as Basilique royale de Saint-Denis</i></span>, or simply <i>Basilique Saint-Denis</i>) is a large medieval abbey church in the city of <a href="/wiki/Saint-Denis,_Seine-Saint-Denis" title="Saint-Denis, Seine-Saint-Denis">Saint-Denis</a>, now a northern suburb of <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>. The building is of unique importance historically and architecturally as its <a href="/wiki/Choir_(architecture)" title="Choir (architecture)">choir</a>, completed in 1144, shows the first use of all of the elements of <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> architecture.</p>
<p>The site originated as a Gallo-Roman cemetery in late Roman times. The archeological remains still lie beneath the cathedral; the people buried there seem to have had a faith that was a mix of Christian and pre-Christian beliefs and practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Around 475 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Genevieve" title="St. Genevieve">St. Genevieve</a> purchased some land and built <a href="/wiki/Saint-Denys_de_la_Chapelle" title="Saint-Denys de la Chapelle">Saint-Denys de la Chapelle</a>. In 636 on the orders of <a href="/wiki/Dagobert_I" title="Dagobert I">Dagobert I</a> the relics of <a href="/wiki/Denis" title="Denis">Saint Denis</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saint</a> of France, were reinterred in the basilica. The relics of St-Denis, which had been transferred to the parish church of the town in 1795, were brought back again to the abbey in 1819.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newadvent.org_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-newadvent.org-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The basilica became a place of <a href="/wiki/Pilgrimage" title="Pilgrimage">pilgrimage</a> and the burial place of the French kings with nearly every king from the 10th to the 18th centuries being buried there, as well as many from previous centuries. (It was not used for the coronations of kings, that function being reserved for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Notre-Dame_de_Reims" title="Notre-Dame de Reims">Cathedral of Reims</a>; however, queens were commonly crowned there.) "Saint-Denis" soon became the abbey church of a growing monastic complex.</p>
<p>In the 12th century the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbot_Suger" title="Abbot Suger">Abbot Suger</a> rebuilt portions of the abbey church using innovative structural and decorative features. In doing so, he is said to have created the first truly <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The basilica's 13th-century nave is the prototype for the <a href="/wiki/Rayonnant" title="Rayonnant">Rayonnant</a> Gothic style, and provided an architectural model for many medieval cathedrals and abbeys of northern France, Germany, England and other countries.</p>
<p>The abbey church became a cathedral in 1966 and is the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Saint-Denis" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Saint-Denis">Bishop of Saint-Denis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pascal_Michel_Ghislain_Delannoy" title="Pascal Michel Ghislain Delannoy">Pascal Michel Ghislain Delannoy</a>. Although known as the "Basilica of St Denis", the cathedral has not been granted the title of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minor_Basilica" title="Minor Basilica">Minor Basilica</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Vatican</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Denis" title="Denis">Saint Denis</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saint</a> of France, became the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Paris" title="Bishop of Paris">bishop of Paris</a>. He was decapitated on the hill of <a href="/wiki/Montmartre" title="Montmartre">Montmartre</a> in the mid-third century with two of his followers, and is said to have subsequently carried his head to the site of the current church, indicating where he wanted to be buried. A <a href="/wiki/Martyrium_(architecture)" title="Martyrium (architecture)">martyrium</a> was erected on the site of his grave, which became a famous place of pilgrimage during the fifth and sixth centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newadvent.org_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-newadvent.org-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>Dagobert, the king of the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a> (reigned 628 to 637), refounded the church as the <a href="/wiki/Abbey" title="Abbey">Abbey</a> of Saint Denis, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedictine" title="Benedictine">Benedictine</a> <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastery</a>. Dagobert also commissioned a new shrine to house the saint's remains, which was created by his chief councillor, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Eligius" title="Saint Eligius">Eligius</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Goldsmith" title="Goldsmith">goldsmith</a> by training. An early <i><a href="/wiki/Hagiography" title="Hagiography">vita</a></i> of Saint Eligius describes the shrine:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Above all, Eligius fabricated a mausoleum for the holy martyr Denis in the city of Paris with a wonderful marble <a href="/wiki/Ciborium_(architecture)" title="Ciborium (architecture)">ciborium</a> over it marvelously decorated with gold and gems. He composed a crest [at the top of a tomb] and a magnificent frontal and surrounded the throne of the altar with golden axes in a circle. He placed golden apples there, round and jeweled. He made a pulpit and a gate of silver and a roof for the throne of the altar on silver axes. He made a covering in the place before the tomb and fabricated an outside altar at the feet of the holy martyr. So much industry did he lavish there, at the king's request, and poured out so much that scarcely a single ornament was left in <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a> and it is the greatest wonder of all to this very day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>None of this work survives.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Basilica_of_St_Denis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Basilica of St Denis ranks as an architectural landmark—as the first major structure of which a substantial part was designed and built in the Gothic style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HF_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-HF-7">[7]</a></sup> Both stylistically and structurally, it heralded the change from <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque architecture</a> to <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic architecture</a>. Before the term "Gothic" came into common use, it was known as the "French Style" (<i>Opus Francigenum</i>).</p>
<p>As it now stands, the church is a large cruciform building of "basilica" form; that is, it has a central nave with lower aisles and <a href="/wiki/Clerestory" title="Clerestory">clerestory</a> windows. It has an additional aisle on the northern side formed of a row of chapels. The west front has three portals, a rose window and one tower, on the southern side. The eastern end, which is built over a crypt, is <a href="/wiki/Apse" title="Apse">apsidal</a>, surrounded by an <a href="/wiki/Ambulatory" title="Ambulatory">ambulatory</a> and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chevet" title="Chevet">chevet</a> of nine radiating chapels. The basilica retains stained glass of many periods (although most of the panels from Suger's time have been removed for long-term conservation and replaced with photographic transparencies), including exceptional modern glass, and a set of twelve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Misericords" title="Misericords">misericords</a>. The basilica measures 108 meters long, and its width is 39 meters.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Carolingian_church">The Carolingian church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Basilica_of_St_Denis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Carolingian church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Little is known about the earliest buildings on the site. The first church mentioned in the chronicles was begun in 754 under <a href="/wiki/Pepin_the_Short" title="Pepin the Short">Pepin the Short</a> and completed under <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a>, who was present at its consecration in 775. By 832 the Abbey had been granted a remunerative whaling concession on the <a href="/wiki/Cotentin_Peninsula" title="Cotentin Peninsula">Cotentin Peninsula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Most of what is now known about the Carolingian church at St Denis resulted from a lengthy series of excavations begun under the American art historian <a href="/wiki/Sumner_McKnight_Crosby" title="Sumner McKnight Crosby">Sumner McKnight Crosby</a> in 1937.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The building was about 60m long, with a monumental <a href="/wiki/Westwork" title="Westwork">westwork</a>, single transepts, a crossing tower and a lengthy eastern apse over a large crypt (parts of which survive). According to one of the Abbey's many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myth_of_origins" title="Myth of origins">foundation myths</a> a leper, who was sleeping in the nearly completed church the night before its planned consecration, witnessed a blaze of light from which Christ, accompanied by St Denis and a host of angels, emerged to conduct the consecration ceremony himself. Before leaving, Christ healed the leper, tearing off his diseased skin to reveal a perfect complexion underneath. A misshapen patch on a marble column was said to be the leper's former skin, which stuck there when Christ discarded it. Fantastical though they may seem now, the popularity of such myths in medieval accounts go some way to explaining why any redevelopment of the original church was a sensitive matter and why Suger found it necessary to go to such lengths to justify his changes. Having been consecrated by Christ, the fabric of the building was itself regarded as sacred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Early_Gothic_rebuilding">The Early Gothic rebuilding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Basilica_of_St_Denis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Early Gothic rebuilding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbot_Suger" title="Abbot Suger">Abbot Suger</a> (c. 1081 – 1151), friend and confidant of French kings and Abbot of St Denis from 1122, began work around 1135 on the rebuild and enlargement of the abbey to which he had been given as an <a href="/wiki/Oblate" title="Oblate">oblate</a> at the age of 10. In his famous account of the work undertaken during his administration, Suger was careful to explain and justify his decision to rebuild the church, complaining at length about the parlous state of the old structure and its inability to cope with the crowds of pilgrims visiting the shrine of St Denis, particularly</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FelixBenoistStDenis.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2550" data-file-width="2915" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/FelixBenoistStDenis.jpg/250px-FelixBenoistStDenis.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/FelixBenoistStDenis.jpg/375px-FelixBenoistStDenis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/FelixBenoistStDenis.jpg/500px-FelixBenoistStDenis.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FelixBenoistStDenis.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Apse and northern facade, lithography by Felix Benoist.</div>
</div>
</div>
<blockquote>
<p>...on special days such as the feast of the blessed Denis [...] when the narrowness of the place forced women to run to the altar on the heads of men as on a pavement with great anguish and confusion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Suger's fascination with light was not merely aesthetic. Like many French clerics in the 12th century CE, he was a follower of <a href="/wiki/Pseudo-Dionysius_the_Areopagite" title="Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite">Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite</a>, a 6th-century mystic who equated the slightest reflection or glint with divine light. When renovated, the Saint-Denis basilica included Suger's own words carved in the nave: "For bright is that which is brightly coupled with the bright/and bright is the noble edifice which is pervaded by the new light."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is important to emphasise that Suger was the patron of the rebuilding of St Denis but not the architect, as was often assumed in the 19th and early 20th centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In fact it appears that two distinct architects, or master masons, were involved in the 12th-century changes. Both remain anonymous but their work can be distinguished on stylistic grounds. The first, who was responsible for the initial work at the western end, favoured conventional Romanesque capitals and moulding profiles with rich and individualised detailing. His successor, who completed the western facade and upper stories of the <a href="/wiki/Narthex" title="Narthex">narthex</a>, before going on to build the new choir, displayed a more restrained approach to decorative effects, relying on a simple repertoire of motifs, which may have proved more suitable for the lighter Gothic style that he helped to create.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="First_phase:_the_west_front.2C_c._1135.E2.80.9340">First phase: the west front, <i>c</i>. 1135–40</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Basilica_of_St_Denis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: First phase: the west front, c. 1135–40">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Suger began his rebuilding project at the western end of St Denis, demolishing the old Carolingian <a href="/wiki/Westwork" title="Westwork">westwork</a>, with its single, centrally located door. He extended the old nave westwards by an additional four bays and added a massive western narthex, incorporating a new façade and three chapels on the first floor level. This new façade, 34 metres (112 ft) wide and 20 metres (66 ft) deep, has three portals, the central one larger than those either side, reflecting the relative width of the central nave and lateral aisles. This tripartite arrangement was clearly influenced by the late 11th-century façades of the abbey churches of <a href="/wiki/Abbey_of_Saint-%C3%89tienne,_Caen" title="Abbey of Saint-Étienne, Caen">St Etienne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Abbey_of_Sainte-Trinit%C3%A9,_Caen" title="Abbey of Sainte-Trinité, Caen">La Trinité, Caen</a>, with which it also shared a three-story elevation and <a href="/wiki/Flanking_tower" title="Flanking tower">flanking towers</a>. Only the south tower survives, its partner having been dismantled following clumsy repairs in the 1840s.</p>
<p>The major innovation in the façade at St Denis is the way the unknown architects have chosen to emphasise the divisions between the different parts with massive vertical buttresses separating the three doorways and horizontal string-courses and window arcades clearly marking out the divisions. This clear delineation of parts was to influence subsequent west façade designs as a common theme in the development of Gothic architecture and a marked departure from the Romanesque. The <a href="/wiki/Rose_window" title="Rose window">rose window</a> at the centre of the upper story of the west portal was also innovative and influential. Although small circular windows (oculi) within triangular tympana were common on the west facades of Italian Romanesque churches, this was probably the first example of a rose window within a square frame, which was to become a dominant feature of the Gothic facades of northern France (soon to be imitated at <a href="/wiki/Chartres_Cathedral" title="Chartres Cathedral">Chartres Cathedral</a> and many others).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The overall design of the façade has an obvious resemblance to a Roman city gatehouse (an impression strengthened by the buttresses and by the <a href="/wiki/Battlement" title="Battlement">crenellations</a> around the top), which helps to emphasise the traditional notion of great churches as earthly embodiments of the <a href="/wiki/New_Jerusalem" title="New Jerusalem">Heavenly City</a>, as described in the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Ezekiel" title="Book of Ezekiel">Book of Ezekiel</a>.</p>

<p>The many influential features of the new façade include the tall, thin statues of Old Testament prophets and kings attached to columns (<i>jamb figures</i>) flanking the portals (destroyed in 1771 but recorded in <a href="/wiki/Bernard_de_Montfaucon" title="Bernard de Montfaucon">Montfaucon</a>'s drawings). These were also adopted at the cathedrals of Paris and Chartres, constructed a few years later, and became a feature of almost every Gothic portal thereafter. Above the doorways, the central <a href="/wiki/Tympanum_(architecture)" title="Tympanum (architecture)">tympanum</a> was carved with <a href="/wiki/Christ_in_Majesty" title="Christ in Majesty">Christ in Majesty</a> displaying his wounds with the dead emerging from their tombs below. Scenes from the martyrdom of St Denis were carved above the south (right hand) portal, while above the north portal was a mosaic (lost), even though this was, as Suger put it 'contrary to the modern custom'. Of the original sculpture, very little remains, most of what is now visible being the result of rather clumsy restoration work in 1839.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Some fragments of the original sculptures survive in the collection of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_de_Cluny" title="Musée de Cluny">Musée de Cluny</a>. The portals themselves were sealed by gilded bronze doors, ornamented with scenes from Christ's Passion and clearly recording Suger's patronage with the following inscription;</p>
<blockquote>
<p>For the glory of the church which nurtured and raised him, Suger strove for the glory of the church, Sharing with you what is yours, oh martyr Denis. He prays that by your prayers he should become a sharer in Paradise. The year when it was consecrated was the one thousand, one hundred and fortieth year of the Word.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>On the lintel below the great tympanum showing the Last Judgement, beneath a carved figure of the kneeling Abbot, was inscribed the more modest plea;</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Receive, stern Judge, the prayers of your Suger, Let me be mercifully numbered among your sheep.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Suger's western extension was completed in 1140 and the three new chapels in the narthex were consecrated on 9 June of that year.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_phase:_the_new_choir.2C_1140.E2.80.9344">Second phase: the new choir, 1140–44</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Basilica_of_St_Denis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Second phase: the new choir, 1140–44">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On completion of the west front, Abbot Suger moved on to the reconstruction of the eastern end, leaving the Carolingian nave in use. He wanted a <i>choir</i> (chancel) that would be suffused with light. To achieve his aims, Suger's masons drew on the several new elements which evolved or had been introduced to Romanesque architecture: the pointed arch, the ribbed <a href="/wiki/Vault_(architecture)" title="Vault (architecture)">vault</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ambulatory" title="Ambulatory">ambulatory</a> with radiating chapels, the clustered columns supporting ribs springing in different directions and the <a href="/wiki/Flying_buttress" title="Flying buttress">flying buttresses</a> which enabled the insertion of large <a href="/wiki/Clerestory" title="Clerestory">clerestory</a> windows.</p>
<p>It was the first time that these features had all been drawn together, and the style evolved radically from the previous Romanesque architecture by the lightness of the structure and the unusually large size of the stained glass windows. <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Panofsky" title="Erwin Panofsky">Erwin Panofsky</a> argued that Suger was inspired to create a physical representation of the Heavenly <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>; however, the extent to which Suger had any aims higher than aesthetic pleasure has been called into doubt by more recent art historians on the basis of Suger's own writings.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:St_denis_nave.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Nave" title="Nave">nave</a></div>
</div>

<p>The new structure was finished and dedicated on June 11, 1144, in the presence of the King.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The Abbey of St Denis thus became the prototype for further building in the royal domain of northern France. Through the rule of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Plantagenet" title="House of Plantagenet">Angevin dynasty</a>, the style was introduced to England and spread throughout France, the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a>, Germany, Spain, northern Italy and <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gothicplantagenet_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-gothicplantagenet-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JH1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-JH1-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:left;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reconstruction_of_the_nave">Reconstruction of the nave</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Basilica_of_St_Denis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reconstruction of the nave">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In 1231, Abbot Odo Clement began work on the rebuilding of the Carolingian nave, which remained sandwiched incongruously between Suger's Gothic works to the east and west. Both the nave and the upper parts of Suger's choir were replaced in the <a href="/wiki/Rayonnant" title="Rayonnant">Rayonnant</a> Gothic style. From the start it appears that Abbot Odo, with the approval of the Regent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blanche_of_Castille" title="Blanche of Castille">Blanche of Castille</a> and her son, the young <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Louis_IX" title="King Louis IX">King Louis IX</a>, planned for the new nave and its large crossing to have a much clearer focus as the French 'royal necropolis'. That plan was fulfilled in 1264 when the bones of 16 former kings and queens were relocated to new tombs arranged around the crossing, 8 Carolingian monarchs to the south and 8 Capetians to the north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> These tombs, featuring lifelike carved recumbent <a href="/wiki/Effigy" title="Effigy">effigies</a> or <i>gisants</i> lying on raised bases, were badly damaged during the French revolution though all but two were subsequently restored by Viollet le Duc in 1860.</p>
<p>The dark Romanesque nave, with its thick walls and small window-openings, was rebuilt using the very latest techniques, in what is now known as <a href="/wiki/French_Gothic_architecture" title="French Gothic architecture">Rayonnant Gothic</a>. This new style, which differed from Suger's earlier works as much as they had differed from their Romanesque precursors, reduced the wall area to an absolute minimum. Solid masonry was replaced with vast window openings filled with brilliant stained glass (all destroyed in the Revolution) and interrupted only by the most slender of bar <a href="/wiki/Tracery" title="Tracery">tracery</a>—not only in the clerestory but also, perhaps for the first time, in the normally dark <a href="/wiki/Triforium" title="Triforium">triforium</a> level. The upper facades of the two much-enlarged transepts were filled with two spectacular 12m-wide <a href="/wiki/Rose_window" title="Rose window">rose windows</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> As with Suger's earlier rebuilding work, the identity of the architect or master mason remains unknown. Although often attributed to <a href="/wiki/Pierre_de_Montreuil" title="Pierre de Montreuil">Pierre de Montreuil</a>, the only evidence for his involvement is an unrelated document of 1247 which refers to him as 'a mason from Saint-Denis'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:left;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Valois_Mortuary_Chapel">The Valois Mortuary Chapel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Basilica_of_St_Denis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Valois Mortuary Chapel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A plan of circa 1700 by Félibien shows the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valois_Chapel" title="Valois Chapel">Valois Chapel</a>, a large mortuary chapel in the form of a domed colonnaded "<a href="/wiki/Rotunda_(architecture)" title="Rotunda (architecture)">rotunda</a>", adjoining the north transept of the basilica and containing the tomb of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Valois" title="House of Valois">Valois</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="St._Denis_and_the_French_Revolution">St. Denis and the French Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Basilica_of_St_Denis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: St. Denis and the French Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The French Revolution of 1789 ended the might of the Saint-Denis abbey. The abbey, a symbol of the royals, was completely demolished in 1792; only the church was left standing. The revolutionaries also severely damaged the church's sculptures, interior and graves. Fortunately the many sepulchral monuments had been safely stowed away. The church was restored in the mid-nineteenth century by Viollet-le-Duc, the same architect responsible for the restoration of the Cathedral of Notre-Dame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Burial_site">Burial site</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Basilica_of_St_Denis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Burial site">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The abbey is where the kings of France and their families were buried for centuries and is therefore often referred to as the "royal <a href="/wiki/Necropolis" title="Necropolis">necropolis</a> of France".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> All but three of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_French_monarchs" title="List of French monarchs">monarchs of France</a> from the 10th century until 1789 have their remains here. Some monarchs, like <a href="/wiki/Clovis_I" title="Clovis I">Clovis I</a> (465–511), were not originally buried at this site. The remains of Clovis I were exhumed from the despoiled <a href="/wiki/Abbey_of_St_Genevieve" title="Abbey of St Genevieve">Abbey of St Genevieve</a> which he founded.</p>
<p>The abbey church contains some fine examples of <a href="/wiki/Cadaver_tomb" title="Cadaver tomb">cadaver tombs</a>. The effigies of many of the kings and queens are on their tombs, but their bodies were removed during the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>. The ancient monarchs were removed in August 1793 to celebrate the revolutionary Festival of Reunion, then the Bourbon and Valois monarchs were removed to celebrate the execution of <a href="/wiki/Marie_Antoinette" title="Marie Antoinette">Marie Antoinette</a> in October 1793. The bodies were dumped into three trenches and covered with lime to destroy them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lindsay_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-lindsay-27">[27]</a></sup> Archaeologist <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Lenoir" title="Alexandre Lenoir">Alexandre Lenoir</a> saved many of the monuments by claiming them as artworks for his <a href="/wiki/Monument_historique" title="Monument historique">Museum of French Monuments</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Burial_places_of_British_royalty" title="Burial places of British royalty">bodies of several Plantagenet monarchs</a> of England were likewise removed from <a href="/wiki/Fontevraud_Abbey" title="Fontevraud Abbey">Fontevraud Abbey</a> during the French Revolution.</p>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">Napoleon Bonaparte</a> reopened the church in 1806, but left the royal remains in their mass graves. In 1817 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bourbon_Dynasty,_Restored" title="Bourbon Dynasty, Restored">restored Bourbons</a> ordered the mass graves to be opened, but only portions of three bodies remained intact. The remaining bones from 158 bodies were collected into an <a href="/wiki/Ossuary" title="Ossuary">ossuary</a> in the crypt of the church, behind marble plates bearing their names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lindsay_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-lindsay-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bourbon_crypt">Bourbon crypt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Basilica_of_St_Denis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Bourbon crypt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Louis_XVI_et_Marie-Antoinette.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Memorial to King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_XVI" title="Louis XVI">Louis XVI</a> and Queen <a href="/wiki/Marie_Antoinette" title="Marie Antoinette">Marie Antoinette</a>, sculptures (1830) by <a href="/wiki/Edme_Gaulle" title="Edme Gaulle">Edme Gaulle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Petitot" title="Pierre Petitot">Pierre Petitot</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The bodies of the <a href="/wiki/Guillotine" title="Guillotine">beheaded</a> King <a href="/wiki/Louis_XVI_of_France" title="Louis XVI of France">Louis XVI</a>, his wife <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marie_Antoinette_of_Austria" title="Marie Antoinette of Austria">Marie Antoinette of Austria</a>, and his sister <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madame_%C3%89lisabeth" title="Madame Élisabeth">Madame Élisabeth</a> were not initially buried in Saint-Denis, but rather in the churchyard of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Madeleine" title="Church of the Madeleine">Madeleine</a>, where they were covered with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quicklime" title="Quicklime">quicklime</a>. The body of the <a href="/wiki/Louis_XVII_of_France" title="Louis XVII of France">Dauphin</a>, who died of an illness, was buried in an unmarked grave in a Parisian churchyard near the <a href="/wiki/Temple_(Paris)" title="Temple (Paris)">Temple</a>. During Napoleon's exile in <a href="/wiki/Elba" title="Elba">Elba</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bourbon_Dynasty,_Restored" title="Bourbon Dynasty, Restored">restored Bourbons</a> ordered a search for the corpses of Louis XVI and Marie Antoinette. The few remains, a few bones that were presumably the king's and a clump of greyish matter containing a lady's garter, were found on January 21, 1815, brought to Saint-Denis and buried in the new Bourbon <a href="/wiki/Crypt" title="Crypt">crypt</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Louis_XVIII_of_France" title="Louis XVIII of France">King Louis XVIII</a>, upon his death in 1824, was buried in the center of the crypt, near the graves of Louis XVI and Marie Antoinette. The coffins of royal family members who died between 1815 and 1830 were also placed in the vaults. Under the direction of architect <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Viollet-le-Duc" title="Eugène Viollet-le-Duc">Viollet-le-Duc</a>, famous for his work on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Notre-Dame_de_Paris" title="Notre-Dame de Paris">Notre-Dame de Paris</a>, church monuments that had been taken to the Museum of French Monuments were returned to the church. The corpse of <a href="/wiki/Louis_VII_of_France" title="Louis VII of France">King Louis VII</a>, who had been buried at <a href="/wiki/Barbeau_Abbey" title="Barbeau Abbey">Barbeau Abbey</a> and whose tomb had not been touched by the revolutionaries, was brought to Saint-Denis and buried in the crypt. In 2004 the mummified heart of the Dauphin, the boy who would have been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_XVII" title="Louis XVII">Louis XVII</a>, was sealed into the wall of the crypt.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kings">Kings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Basilica_of_St_Denis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Kings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>All but three of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kings_of_France" title="Kings of France">Kings of France</a> are buried in the basilica, as well as a few other monarchs. The remains of the earlier monarchs were removed from the destroyed <a href="/wiki/Abbey_of_St_Genevieve" title="Abbey of St Genevieve">Abbey of St Genevieve</a>. Some of the prominent of these are:</p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Clovis_I" title="Clovis I">Clovis I</a> (465–511)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Childebert_I" title="Childebert I">Childebert I</a> (496–558)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ar%C3%A9gonde" title="Arégonde">Arégonde</a> (c.515–c.573)</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Dagobert_I" title="Dagobert I">Dagobert I</a> (603–639)</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Charles_Martel" title="Charles Martel">Charles Martel</a> (686–741)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pippin_the_Younger" title="Pippin the Younger">Pippin the Younger</a> (714–768) and his wife <a href="/wiki/Bertrada_of_Laon" title="Bertrada of Laon">Bertrada of Laon</a> (726–783)</li>
