<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Premature burial</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Premature burial</b>, also known as <b>live burial</b>, <b>burial alive</b>, or <b>vivisepulture</b>, means to be <a href="/wiki/Burial" title="Burial">buried</a> while still alive. Animals or humans may be buried alive accidentally or intentionally. The victim may accidentally be buried by others in the mistaken assumption that they are dead. Intentional burial may occur as a form of torture, murder, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Execution_(legal)" title="Execution (legal)">execution</a>; it may also occur with consent of the victim as a part of a stunt (with the intention to escape). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fear_of_being_buried_alive" title="Fear of being buried alive">Fear of being buried alive</a> is reported to be among the most common <a href="/wiki/Phobia" title="Phobia">phobias</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Premature_burial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Premature burial can lead to death through the following: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asphyxiation" title="Asphyxiation">asphyxiation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dehydration" title="Dehydration">dehydration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Starvation" title="Starvation">starvation</a>, or (in cold climates) <a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">hypothermia</a>. A person trapped with fresh air to breathe can last a considerable time and burial has been used as a very cruel method of execution (as in cases of <a href="/wiki/Vestal_Virgin" title="Vestal Virgin">Vestal Virgins</a> who violated the oath of celibacy), lasting sufficiently long for the victim to comprehend and imagine every stage of what is happening (being trapped in total darkness with very limited or no movement) and to experience great psychological and physical torment including extreme panic. The medical term for the fear of being buried alive is <a href="/wiki/Taphophobia" title="Taphophobia">taphophobia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unintentional_live_burial">Unintentional live burial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Premature_burial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Unintentional live burial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At least one (almost certainly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apocryphal" title="Apocryphal">apocryphal</a>) report of accidental burial dates back to the fourteenth century. Upon the reopening of his tomb, the philosopher <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Duns_Scotus" title="John Duns Scotus">John Duns Scotus</a> (1266-1308) was reportedly found outside his coffin with his hands torn and bloody after attempting to escape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alice_Blunden" title="Alice Blunden">Alice Blunden</a> of <a href="/wiki/Basingstoke" title="Basingstoke">Basingstoke</a> was said in a contemporaneous account to have been buried alive, not once but twice, in 1674.</p>
<p>Revivals of supposed "corpses" have been triggered by dropped coffins, grave robbers, embalming, and attempted dissections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Folklorist Paul Barber has argued that the incidence of unintentional live burial has been overestimated, and that the normal, physical effects of decomposition are sometimes misinterpreted as signs that the person whose remains are being exhumed had revived in his or her coffin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Nevertheless, patients have been documented as late as the 1890s as accidentally being sent to the <a href="/wiki/Morgue" title="Morgue">morgue</a> or trapped in a steel box after erroneously being declared dead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Newspapers have reported cases of exhumed corpses which appear to have been accidentally buried alive. On February 21, 1885, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> gave a disturbing account of such a case. The victim was a man from <a href="/wiki/Buncombe_County,_North_Carolina" title="Buncombe County, North Carolina">Buncombe County</a> whose name was given as "Jenkins." His body was found turned over onto its front inside the coffin, with much of his hair pulled out. Scratch marks were also visible on all sides of the coffin's interior. His family were reportedly "distressed beyond measure at the criminal carelessness" associated with the case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Another similar story was reported in <i>The Times</i> on January 18, 1886, the victim of this case being described simply as a "girl" named "Collins" from <a href="/wiki/Woodstock,_Ontario" title="Woodstock, Ontario">Woodstock, Ontario</a>, Canada. Her body was described as being found with the knees tucked up under the body, and her burial shroud "torn into shreds."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2005, a body bag was delivered to the Matarese Funeral home in Ashland, Massachusetts with a live occupant. Funeral director John Matarese discovered this, called paramedics, and avoided live embalming or premature burial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014 in <a href="/wiki/Peraia,_Thessaloniki" title="Peraia, Thessaloniki">Peraia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thessaloniki" title="Thessaloniki">Thessaloniki</a>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_Macedonia" title="Greek Macedonia">Macedonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, the police discovered that a 45-year-old woman was buried alive and died of asphyxia after being declared clinically dead by a private hospital; she was discovered just shortly after being buried by children playing near the cemetery who heard screams from inside the earth and afterwards her family was reported as considering suing the private hospital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2015 it was reported that in 2014 again in Peraia, Thessaloniki, in Macedonia, Greece, police investigation concluded that a 49-year-old woman was buried alive after being declared dead due to cancer; her family reported that they could hear her scream from inside the earth at the cemetery shortly after burial and the investigation revealed that she died of heart failure inside the coffin and found out that it was the medicines given to her by her doctors for her cancer that caused her to be declared clinically dead and buried alive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attempts_to_prevent_live_burial">Attempts to prevent live burial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Premature_burial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Attempts to prevent live burial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Robinson_(Dissenting_minister)" title="Robert Robinson (Dissenting minister)">Robert Robinson</a> died in Manchester in 1791. A movable glass pane was inserted in his coffin, and the mausoleum had a door for purposes of inspection by a watchman, who was to see if he breathed on the glass. He instructed his relatives to visit his grave periodically to check that he was still dead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Safety_coffin" title="Safety coffin">Safety coffins</a> were devised to prevent premature burial, although there is no evidence that any have ever been successfully used to save an accidentally buried person. On 5 December 1882, J. G. Krichbaum received <span><a class="external text" href="//www.google.com/patents/US268693" rel="nofollow">U.S. Patent 268,693</a></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> for his "Device For Life In Buried Persons." It consisted of a movable periscope-like pipe which provided air and, when rotated or pushed by the person interred, indicated to passersby that someone was buried alive. The patent text refers to "that class of devices for indicating life in buried persons," suggesting that such inventions were common at the time.</p>
<p>In 1890, a family designed and built a burial vault at the Wildwood Cemetery in <a href="/wiki/Williamsport,_Pennsylvania" title="Williamsport, Pennsylvania">Williamsport, Pennsylvania</a>, with an internal hatch to allow the victim of accidental premature burial to escape. The vault had an air supply and was lined in felt to protect a panic-stricken victim from self-inflicted injury before escape. Bodies were to be removed from the casket before interment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Association_for_the_Prevention_of_Premature_Burial" title="London Association for the Prevention of Premature Burial">London Association for the Prevention of Premature Burial</a> was co-founded in 1896 by <a href="/wiki/William_Tebb" title="William Tebb">William Tebb</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Walter_Hadwen" title="Walter Hadwen">Walter Hadwen</a>.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a>, in his work <i><a href="/wiki/Germania" title="Germania">Germania</a></i>, records that German tribes practiced two forms of <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">capital punishment</a>; the first where the victim was hanged from a tree, and another where the victim was tied to a wicker frame, pushed face down into mud and buried. The first was used to make an example of traitors; the second was used for punishment of dishonourable or shameful vices, such as cowardice. According to Tacitus, the Ancient Germans thought that crime should be exposed, whereas infamy should be buried out of sight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In ancient Rome, a <a href="/wiki/Vestal_Virgin" title="Vestal Virgin">Vestal Virgin</a> convicted of violating her vows of <a href="/wiki/Celibacy" title="Celibacy">celibacy</a> was "buried alive" by being sealed in a cave with a small amount of bread and water, ostensibly so that the goddess <a href="/wiki/Vesta_(mythology)" title="Vesta (mythology)">Vesta</a> could save her were she truly innocent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This practice was, strictly speaking, <a href="/wiki/Immurement" title="Immurement">immurement</a> (i.e., being walled up and left to die) rather than premature burial. According to Christian tradition, a number of saints were martyred this way, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Castulus" title="Saint Castulus">Saint Castulus</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Vitalis_of_Milan" title="Saint Vitalis of Milan">Saint Vitalis of Milan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Denmark, in the <a href="/wiki/Ribe" title="Ribe">Ribe</a> city statute, which was promulgated in 1269, a female thief was to be buried alive, and in the law by <a href="/wiki/Margaret_I_of_Denmark" title="Margaret I of Denmark">Queen Margaret I</a>, adulterous women were to be punished with premature burial, men with beheading.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Within the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> a variety of offenses including rape, infanticide and theft could be punished with live burial. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Schwabenspiegel" title="Schwabenspiegel">Schwabenspiegel</a>, a law code from the 13th century, specified that the rape of a virgin should be punished by live burial (whereas the rapist of a non-virgin was to be beheaded).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Female murderers of their own employers also risked being buried alive. In <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a> 1505, for example, a 12-year-old boy and a 13-year-old girl were found guilty of killing their master, in conspiracy with the cook. The boy was beheaded, and the girl and the cook were buried alive beneath the gallows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The jurist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eduard_Henke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eduard Henke (page does not exist)">Eduard Henke</a> observed that in the Middle Ages, live burial of women guilty of infanticide was a "very frequent" punishment in city statutes and <i><a href="/wiki/Landrecht_(medieval)" title="Landrecht (medieval)">Landrechten</a></i>. For example, he notes those in <a href="/wiki/Landgraviate_of_Hesse" title="Landgraviate of Hesse">Hesse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_of_Tyrol" title="County of Tyrol">Tyrol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The "Berlinisches Stadtbuch" records that between 1412 and 1447, 10 women were buried alive there,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and as late as in 1583, the archbishop of Bremen promulgated (alongside the somewhat milder 1532 <a href="/wiki/Constitutio_Criminalis_Carolina" title="Constitutio Criminalis Carolina">Constitutio Criminalis Carolina</a> punishment of <a href="/wiki/Drowning" title="Drowning">drowning</a>) live burial as an alternate execution method for punishing mothers found guilty of infanticide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Observationes_iuris_universi_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Observationes_iuris_universi-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>As noted by Elias Pufendorf,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Observationes_iuris_universi_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Observationes_iuris_universi-26">[26]</a></sup> a woman buried alive would afterwards be impaled through the heart. This combined punishment of live burial and impalement was practiced in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> until 1508 also for women found guilty of theft, but the city council decided in 1515 that the punishment was too cruel, and opted for drowning instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Impalement was, however, not always mentioned together with live burial. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eduard_Osenbr%C3%BCggen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eduard Osenbrüggen (page does not exist)">Eduard Osenbrüggen</a> relates how the live burial of a woman convicted of infanticide could be pronounced in a court verdict. For example, in a 1570 case in <a href="/wiki/Ensisheim" title="Ensisheim">Ensisheim</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The verdict commanded the executioner to place the perpetrator in the grave alive, "and place two layers of thorns, the one beneath, the other above her. Prior to that he should place a bowl over her face, in which he had made a hole, and to give her through that<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> a reed/tube into the mouth, then jump three times upon her, and lastly cover her with earth"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In this particular case, however, some noblewomen made an appeal for mercy, and the convicted woman was drowned instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dieter Furcht speculates that the impalement was not so much to be regarded as an execution method, but as a way to prevent the condemned from becoming an avenging, undead <a href="/wiki/Wiederg%C3%A4nger" title="Wiedergänger">Wiedergänger</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In medieval Italy, unrepentant murderers were buried alive, head down, feet in the air, a practice referred to in passing in Canto XIX of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dante" title="Dante">Dante</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Divine_Comedy#Inferno" title="The Divine Comedy">Inferno</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 16th Century <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Netherlands" title="Habsburg Netherlands">Habsburg Netherlands</a>, where the Catholic authorities made a prolonged effort to stamp out the Protestant churches, live burial was commonly used as the punishment of women found guilty of heresy. The last to be so executed was Anna Utenhoven, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anabaptist" title="Anabaptist">Anabaptist</a> buried alive at <a href="/wiki/Vilvoorde" title="Vilvoorde">Vilvoorde</a> in 1597. Reportedly, when her head was still above the ground she was given a last chance to recant her faith, and upon her refusal she was completely covered up and suffocated. The case aroused a great deal of protest in the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Revolt" title="Dutch Revolt">rebellious north provinces</a>, and foiled the peace feelers which King <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Philip_III" title="Philip III">Philip III</a> was at the time extending to the Dutch. Thereafter the Habsburg authorities avoided further such cases, punishing heresy with fines and deportations rather than death<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>Into the seventeenth century in feudal Russia, live burial as execution method was known as "the pit" and used against women who were condemned for killing their husbands. In 1689, the punishment of live burial was changed to beheading<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Live burial of Jews in such countries as Ukraine is reported; for example some instances occurred during the <a href="/wiki/Khmelnytsky_Uprising" title="Khmelnytsky Uprising">Chmielnicki Massacre</a> (1648–1649) in Ukraine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_examples">Modern examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Premature_burial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Modern examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There have been a number of cases of people pronounced incorrectly dead and thereafter buried alive.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_disasters">Natural disasters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Premature_burial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Natural disasters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Natural disasters have also largely buried people alive, as well as collapsing mines.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wars">Wars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Premature_burial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Wars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It has been used during wars and by mafia organizations.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Serbia_in_the_Balkan_Wars" title="Serbia in the Balkan Wars">Serbian</a> officials are documented to have buried alive Bulgarian civilians from <a href="/wiki/Peh%C4%8Devo" title="Pehčevo">Pehčevo</a> (now in the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Macedonia" title="Republic of Macedonia">Republic of Macedonia</a>) during the <a href="/wiki/Balkan_Wars" title="Balkan Wars">Balkan Wars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> During World War II, Japanese soldiers were documented to have buried Chinese civilians alive, notably during the <a href="/wiki/Nanking_Massacre" title="Nanking Massacre">Nanking Massacre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> This method of execution was also used by German leaders against Jews in Ukraine and Belarus during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> (American War), burials alive were documented at the <a href="/wiki/Massacre_at_Hu%E1%BA%BF" title="Massacre at Huế">Massacre at Huế</a> in 1968.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a>, Iraqi soldiers were knowingly buried alive by American tanks of the <a href="/wiki/1st_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="1st Infantry Division (United States)">First Infantry Division</a> shoveling earth into their trenches. Estimates for the number of soldiers killed this way vary: one source puts it at "between 80 and 250", while Col. Anthony Moreno suggested it may have been thousands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014 <a href="/wiki/Islamic_State_of_Iraq_and_the_Levant" title="Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant">ISIS</a> buried <a href="/wiki/Yazidis" title="Yazidis">Yazidi</a> women and children alive in an attempt to annihilate the Yazidi tribe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Execution">Execution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Premature_burial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Execution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are also accounts of the <a href="/wiki/Khmer_Rouge" title="Khmer Rouge">Khmer Rouge</a> using premature burials as a form of execution in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Killing_Fields" title="Killing Fields">Killing Fields</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao Zedong</a>'s regime, there are some accounts that premature burials were used in executions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Honour_killings">Honour killings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Premature_burial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Honour killings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>So-called "honour killings" take place every year in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, where people are buried alive, usually by their family, regarding <a href="/wiki/Gender_role" title="Gender role">gender roles</a> or only for talking to the opposite sex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a> alone there is one <a href="/wiki/Honor_killing" title="Honor killing">honor killing</a> every week and over 1,000 reported during the previous five years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Voluntary_burial">Voluntary burial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Premature_burial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Voluntary burial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On rare occasions, people have willingly arranged to be buried alive, reportedly as a demonstration of their controversial ability to survive such an event. In one story taking place around 1840, <a href="/wiki/Sadhu_Haridas" title="Sadhu Haridas">Sadhu Haridas</a>, an Indian <a href="/wiki/Fakir" title="Fakir">fakir</a>, is said to have been buried in the presence of a British military officer and under the supervision of the local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maharajah" title="Maharajah">maharajah</a>, by being placed in a sealed bag in a wooden box in a vault. The vault was then interred, earth was flattened over the site and crops were sown over the place for a very long time. The whole location was guarded day and night to prevent fraud and the site was dug up twice in a ten-month period to verify the burial, before the fakir was finally dug out and slowly revived in the presence of another officer. The fakir said that his only fear during his "wonderful sleep" was to be eaten by underground worms. However, according to current medical science, it is not possible for a human to survive for a period of ten months without food, water, and air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> According to other sources the entire burial was 40 days long. The Indian government has since made the act of voluntary premature burial illegal, because of the unintended deaths of individuals attempting to recreate this feat.</p>
<p>The practice of being buried alive is not uncommon in Russia; in 2010 a man died after being buried alive to try to overcome his fear of death but he was crushed to death by the earth on top of him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc2011_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc2011-51">[51]</a></sup> The following year, another Russian died after being buried overnight in a makeshift coffin "for good luck".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc2011_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc2011-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Buried_Alive_(performance)" title="Buried Alive (performance)">Buried Alive</a></i> is a controversial<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> art and lecture performance series by art-tech group <a href="/wiki/Monochrom" title="Monochrom">monochrom</a>. Participants have the opportunity to be buried alive in a coffin for fifteen to twenty minutes. As a framework program monochrom offers lectures about the history of the science of determining death and the medical cultural history of premature burial.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Myths_and_legends">Myths and legends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Premature_burial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Myths and legends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Otteran" title="Saint Otteran">St. Oran</a> was a <a href="/wiki/Druid" title="Druid">druid</a> living on the <a href="/wiki/Iona" title="Iona">Island of Iona</a> in Scotland's <a href="/wiki/Inner_Hebrides" title="Inner Hebrides">Inner Hebrides</a>. He became a follower of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Columba" title="St. Columba">St. Columba</a>, who brought Christianity to Iona from Ireland in 563 AD. When St. Columba had repeated problems building the original <a href="/wiki/Iona_Abbey" title="Iona Abbey">Iona Abbey</a>, citing interference from the <a href="/wiki/Devil" title="Devil">Devil</a>, St. Oran offered himself as a human sacrifice and was buried alive. He was later dug up and found to be still alive, but he uttered such words describing what of the afterlife he had seen and how it involved no heaven or hell, that he was ordered to be covered up again. The building of the abbey went ahead, untroubled, and St. Oran's chapel marks the spot where the saint was buried.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the fourteenth through nineteenth centuries, a popular tale about premature burial in European <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a> was the "<a href="/wiki/Lady_with_the_Ring" title="Lady with the Ring">Lady with the Ring</a>". In the story, a woman who was prematurely buried awakens to frighten a grave robber who is attempting to cut a ring off her finger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>The TV show <i><a href="/wiki/MythBusters" title="MythBusters">MythBusters</a></i> tested the myth to see if someone could survive being buried alive for two hours before being rescued. Host <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Hyneman" title="Jamie Hyneman">Jamie Hyneman</a> attempted the feat, but when his steel coffin began to bend under the weight of the earth used to cover it, the experiment was aborted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
