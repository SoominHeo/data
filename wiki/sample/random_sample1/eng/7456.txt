<p><b>Biotechnology</b> is the use of living systems and organisms to develop or make products, or "any technological application that uses biological systems, living organisms, or derivatives thereof, to make or modify products or processes for specific use" (UN Convention on Biological Diversity, Art. 2).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Depending on the tools and applications, it often overlaps with the (related) fields of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioengineering" title="Bioengineering">bioengineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biomedical_engineering" title="Biomedical engineering">biomedical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biomanufacturing" title="Biomanufacturing">biomanufacturing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molecular_engineering" title="Molecular engineering">molecular engineering</a>, etc.</p>
<p>For thousands of years, humankind has used biotechnology in <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_production" title="Food production">food production</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BioREACH_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BioREACH-2">[2]</a></sup> The term is largely believed to have been coined in 1919 by Hungarian <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineer</a> <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1roly_Ereky" title="Károly Ereky">Károly Ereky</a>. In the late 20th and early 21st centuries, biotechnology has expanded to include new and diverse <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">sciences</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Genomics" title="Genomics">genomics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recombinant_gene" title="Recombinant gene">recombinant gene</a> techniques, applied <a href="/wiki/Immunology" title="Immunology">immunology</a>, and development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical" title="Pharmaceutical">pharmaceutical</a> therapies and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diagnostic_tests" title="Diagnostic tests">diagnostic tests</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BioREACH_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-BioREACH-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span></h2>
<p>The wide concept of "biotech" or "biotechnology" encompasses a wide range of procedures for modifying living organisms according to human purposes, going back to <a href="/wiki/Domestication" title="Domestication">domestication</a> of animals, cultivation of the plants, and "improvements" to these through breeding programs that employ <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_selection" title="Artificial selection">artificial selection</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridization</a>. Modern usage also includes <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetic engineering</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Cell_culture" title="Cell culture">cell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tissue_culture" title="Tissue culture">tissue culture</a> technologies. The <a href="/wiki/American_Chemical_Society" title="American Chemical Society">American Chemical Society</a> defines biotechnology as the application of biological organisms, systems, or processes by various industries to learning about the science of life and the improvement of the value of materials and organisms such as pharmaceuticals, crops, and livestock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> As per <a href="/wiki/European_Federation_of_Biotechnology" title="European Federation of Biotechnology">European Federation of Biotechnology</a>, biotechnology is the integration of natural science and organisms, cells, parts thereof, and molecular analogues for products and services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Biotechnology also writes on the pure biological sciences (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_cell_culture" title="Animal cell culture">animal cell culture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cell_biology" title="Cell biology">cell biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Embryology" title="Embryology">embryology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microbiology" title="Microbiology">microbiology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">molecular biology</a>). In many instances, it is also dependent on knowledge and methods from outside the sphere of biology including:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bioinformatics" title="Bioinformatics">bioinformatics</a>, a new brand of <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bioprocess_engineering" title="Bioprocess engineering">bioprocess engineering</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Biorobotics" title="Biorobotics">biorobotics</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chemical_engineering" title="Chemical engineering">chemical engineering</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Conversely, modern biological sciences (including even concepts such as <a href="/wiki/Molecular_ecology" title="Molecular ecology">molecular ecology</a>) are intimately entwined and heavily dependent on the methods developed through biotechnology and what is commonly thought of as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_sciences" title="Life sciences">life sciences</a> industry. Biotechnology is the <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Laboratory" title="Laboratory">laboratory</a> using <a href="/wiki/Bioinformatics" title="Bioinformatics">bioinformatics</a> for exploration, extraction, exploitation and production from any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Living_organisms" title="Living organisms">living organisms</a> and any source of <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> by means of <a href="/wiki/Biochemical_engineering" title="Biochemical engineering">biochemical engineering</a> where high value-added products could be planned (reproduced by <a href="/wiki/Biosynthesis" title="Biosynthesis">biosynthesis</a>, for example), forecasted, formulated, developed, manufactured, and marketed for the purpose of sustainable operations (for the return from bottomless initial investment on R &amp; D) and gaining durable patents rights (for exclusives rights for sales, and prior to this to receive national and international approval from the results on animal experiment and human experiment, especially on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical" title="Pharmaceutical">pharmaceutical</a> branch of biotechnology to prevent any undetected side-effects or safety concerns by using the products).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>By contrast, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioengineering" title="Bioengineering">bioengineering</a> is generally thought of as a related field that more heavily emphasizes higher systems approaches (not necessarily the altering or using of biological materials <i>directly</i>) for interfacing with and utilizing living things. Bioengineering is the application of the principles of engineering and natural sciences to tissues, cells and molecules. This can be considered as the use of knowledge from working with and manipulating biology to achieve a result that can improve functions in plants and animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Relatedly, <a href="/wiki/Biomedical_engineering" title="Biomedical engineering">biomedical engineering</a> is an overlapping field that often draws upon and applies <i>biotechnology</i> (by various definitions), especially in certain sub-fields of biomedical and/or chemical engineering such as <a href="/wiki/Tissue_engineering" title="Tissue engineering">tissue engineering</a>, bio<a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_engineering" title="Pharmaceutical engineering">pharmaceutical engineering</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetic engineering</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="921" data-file-width="723" height="280" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png/220px-The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png/330px-The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png/440px-The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Brewing" title="Brewing">Brewing</a> was an early application of biotechnology</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_biotechnology" title="History of biotechnology">History of biotechnology</a></div>
<p>Although not normally what first comes to mind, many forms of human-derived <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> clearly fit the broad definition of "'utilizing a biotechnological system to make products". Indeed, the cultivation of plants may be viewed as the earliest biotechnological enterprise.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">Agriculture</a> has been theorized to have become the dominant way of producing food since the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Revolution" title="Neolithic Revolution">Neolithic Revolution</a>. Through early biotechnology, the earliest farmers selected and bred the best suited crops, having the highest yields, to produce enough food to support a growing population. As crops and fields became increasingly large and difficult to maintain, it was discovered that specific organisms and their by-products could effectively <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertilize" title="Fertilize">fertilize</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">restore nitrogen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">control pests</a>. Throughout the history of agriculture, farmers have inadvertently altered the genetics of their crops through introducing them to new environments and <a href="/wiki/Plant_breeding" title="Plant breeding">breeding</a> them with other plants — one of the first forms of biotechnology.</p>
<p>These processes also were included in early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> These processes were introduced in early <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, and still use the same basic biological methods. In <a href="/wiki/Brewing" title="Brewing">brewing</a>, malted grains (containing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enzymes" title="Enzymes">enzymes</a>) convert starch from grains into sugar and then adding specific <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yeasts" title="Yeasts">yeasts</a> to produce beer. In this process, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbohydrates" title="Carbohydrates">carbohydrates</a> in the grains were broken down into alcohols such as ethanol. Later other cultures produced the process of <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid_fermentation" title="Lactic acid fermentation">lactic acid fermentation</a> which allowed the fermentation and preservation of other forms of food, such as <a href="/wiki/Soy_sauce" title="Soy sauce">soy sauce</a>. Fermentation was also used in this time period to produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leavened_bread" title="Leavened bread">leavened bread</a>. Although the process of fermentation was not fully understood until <a href="/wiki/Louis_Pasteur" title="Louis Pasteur">Louis Pasteur</a>'s work in 1857, it is still the first use of biotechnology to convert a food source into another form.</p>
<p>Before the time of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>'s work and life, animal and plant scientists had already used selective breeding. Darwin added to that body of work with his scientific observations about the ability of science to change species. These accounts contributed to Darwin's theory of natural selection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>For thousands of years, humans have used selective breeding to improve production of crops and livestock to use them for food. In selective breeding, organisms with desirable characteristics are mated to produce offspring with the same characteristics. For example, this technique was used with corn to produce the largest and sweetest crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thieman_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thieman-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early twentieth century scientists gained a greater understanding of <a href="/wiki/Microbiology" title="Microbiology">microbiology</a> and explored ways of manufacturing specific products. In 1917, <a href="/wiki/Chaim_Weizmann" title="Chaim Weizmann">Chaim Weizmann</a> first used a pure microbiological culture in an industrial process, that of manufacturing <a href="/wiki/Corn_starch" title="Corn starch">corn starch</a> using <i><a href="/wiki/Clostridium_acetobutylicum" title="Clostridium acetobutylicum">Clostridium acetobutylicum</a>,</i> to produce <a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">acetone</a>, which the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> desperately needed to manufacture <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosive" title="Explosive">explosives</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Springham_biotechnology_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Springham_biotechnology-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Biotechnology has also led to the development of antibiotics. In 1928, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Fleming" title="Alexander Fleming">Alexander Fleming</a> discovered the mold <i><a href="/wiki/Penicillium" title="Penicillium">Penicillium</a></i>. His work led to the purification of the antibiotic compound formed by the mold by Howard Florey, Ernst Boris Chain and Norman Heatley – to form what we today know as <a href="/wiki/Penicillin" title="Penicillin">penicillin</a>. In 1940, penicillin became available for medicinal use to treat bacterial infections in humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thieman_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thieman-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The field of modern biotechnology is generally thought of as having been born in 1971 when Paul Berg's (Stanford) experiments in gene splicing had early success. Herbert W. Boyer (Univ. Calif. at San Francisco) and Stanley N. Cohen (Stanford) significantly advanced the new technology in 1972 by transferring genetic material into a bacterium, such that the imported material would be reproduced. The commercial viability of a biotechnology industry was significantly expanded on June 16, 1980, when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" title="United States Supreme Court">United States Supreme Court</a> ruled that a <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetically modified</a> <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganism</a> could be <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patented</a> in the case of <i><a href="/wiki/Diamond_v._Chakrabarty" title="Diamond v. Chakrabarty">Diamond v. Chakrabarty</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiamondvChakrabarty_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-DiamondvChakrabarty-13">[13]</a></sup> Indian-born Ananda Chakrabarty, working for <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a>, had modified a bacterium (of the <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudomonas" title="Pseudomonas">Pseudomonas</a></i> genus) capable of breaking down crude oil, which he proposed to use in treating oil spills. (Chakrabarty's work did not involve gene manipulation but rather the transfer of entire organelles between strains of the <i>Pseudomonas</i> bacterium.</p>
<p>Revenue in the industry is expected to grow by 12.9% in 2008. Another factor influencing the biotechnology sector's success is improved intellectual property rights legislation—and enforcement—worldwide, as well as strengthened demand for medical and pharmaceutical products to cope with an ageing, and ailing, U.S. population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rising demand for biofuels is expected to be good news for the biotechnology sector, with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">Department of Energy</a> estimating <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> usage could reduce U.S. petroleum-derived fuel consumption by up to 30% by 2030. The biotechnology sector has allowed the U.S. farming industry to rapidly increase its supply of corn and soybeans—the main inputs into biofuels—by developing genetically modified seeds which are resistant to pests and drought. By boosting farm productivity, biotechnology plays a crucial role in ensuring that biofuel production targets are met.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rose_grown_from_tissue_culture.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Biotechnology has applications in four major industrial areas, including health care (medical), crop production and agriculture, non food (industrial) uses of crops and other products (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Biodegradable_plastic" title="Biodegradable plastic">biodegradable plastics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_oil" title="Vegetable oil">vegetable oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a>), and environmental uses.</p>
<p>For example, one application of biotechnology is the directed use of <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> for the manufacture of organic products (examples include <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a> products). Another example is using naturally present <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> by the mining industry in <a href="/wiki/Bioleaching" title="Bioleaching">bioleaching</a>. Biotechnology is also used to recycle, treat waste, clean up sites contaminated by industrial activities (<a href="/wiki/Bioremediation" title="Bioremediation">bioremediation</a>), and also to produce <a href="/wiki/Biological_warfare" title="Biological warfare">biological weapons</a>.</p>
<p>A series of derived terms have been coined to identify several branches of biotechnology; for example:</p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Bioinformatics" title="Bioinformatics">Bioinformatics</a></b> is an interdisciplinary field which addresses biological problems using computational techniques, and makes the rapid organization as well as analysis of biological data possible. The field may also be referred to as <i>computational biology</i>, and can be defined as, "conceptualizing biology in terms of molecules and then applying informatics techniques to understand and organize the information associated with these molecules, on a large scale."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gerstein_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-gerstein-16">[16]</a></sup> Bioinformatics plays a key role in various areas, such as <a href="/wiki/Functional_genomics" title="Functional genomics">functional genomics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Structural_genomics" title="Structural genomics">structural genomics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Proteomics" title="Proteomics">proteomics</a>, and forms a key component in the biotechnology and pharmaceutical sector.</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_biotechnology" title="Blue biotechnology">Blue biotechnology</a></b> is a term that has been used to describe the marine and aquatic applications of biotechnology, but its use is relatively rare.</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_biotechnology" title="Green biotechnology">Green biotechnology</a></b> is biotechnology applied to agricultural processes. An example would be the selection and domestication of plants via <a href="/wiki/Micropropagation" title="Micropropagation">micropropagation</a>. Another example is the designing of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transgenic_plant" title="Transgenic plant">transgenic plants</a> to grow under specific environments in the presence (or absence) of chemicals. One hope is that green biotechnology might produce more environmentally friendly solutions than traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_agriculture" title="Industrial agriculture">industrial agriculture</a>. An example of this is the engineering of a plant to express a <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticide</a>, thereby ending the need of external application of pesticides. An example of this would be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transgenic_maize" title="Transgenic maize">Bt corn</a>. Whether or not green biotechnology products such as this are ultimately more environmentally friendly is a topic of considerable debate.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Biopharmaceutical" title="Biopharmaceutical">Red biotechnology</a></b> is applied to medical processes. Some examples are the designing of organisms to produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotics</a>, and the engineering of genetic cures through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genetic_manipulation" title="Genetic manipulation">genetic manipulation</a>.</li>
<li><b>White biotechnology</b>, also known as industrial biotechnology, is biotechnology applied to <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industrial</a> processes. An example is the designing of an organism to produce a useful chemical. Another example is the using of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enzymes" title="Enzymes">enzymes</a> as industrial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalysts</a> to either produce valuable chemicals or destroy hazardous/polluting chemicals. White biotechnology tends to consume less in resources than traditional processes used to produce industrial goods.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The investment and economic output of all of these types of applied biotechnologies is termed as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioeconomy" title="Bioeconomy">bioeconomy</a>".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medicine">Medicine</span></h3>
<p>In medicine, modern biotechnology finds applications in areas such as <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_drug" title="Pharmaceutical drug">pharmaceutical drug</a> discovery and production, <a href="/wiki/Pharmacogenomics" title="Pharmacogenomics">pharmacogenomics</a>, and genetic testing (or genetic screening).</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Pharmacogenomics" title="Pharmacogenomics">Pharmacogenomics</a> (a combination of <a href="/wiki/Pharmacology" title="Pharmacology">pharmacology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genomics" title="Genomics">genomics</a>) is the technology that analyses how genetic makeup affects an individual's response to drugs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> It deals with the influence of <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> variation on drug response in patients by correlating <a href="/wiki/Gene_expression" title="Gene expression">gene expression</a> or <a href="/wiki/Single-nucleotide_polymorphism" title="Single-nucleotide polymorphism">single-nucleotide polymorphisms</a> with a drug's <a href="/wiki/Efficacy" title="Efficacy">efficacy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxicity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid20836007_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid20836007-18">[18]</a></sup> By doing so, pharmacogenomics aims to develop rational means to optimize drug therapy, with respect to the patients' <a href="/wiki/Genotype" title="Genotype">genotype</a>, to ensure maximum efficacy with minimal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adverse_effect_(medicine)" title="Adverse effect (medicine)">adverse effects</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid19530963_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid19530963-19">[19]</a></sup> Such approaches promise the advent of "<a href="/wiki/Personalized_medicine" title="Personalized medicine">personalized medicine</a>"; in which drugs and drug combinations are optimized for each individual's unique genetic makeup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid20712531_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid20712531-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>Biotechnology has contributed to the discovery and manufacturing of traditional <a href="/wiki/Small_molecule" title="Small molecule">small molecule</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_drugs" title="Pharmaceutical drugs">pharmaceutical drugs</a> as well as drugs that are the product of biotechnology – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biopharmaceutics" title="Biopharmaceutics">biopharmaceutics</a>. Modern biotechnology can be used to manufacture existing medicines relatively easily and cheaply. The first genetically engineered products were medicines designed to treat human diseases. To cite one example, in 1978 <a href="/wiki/Genentech" title="Genentech">Genentech</a> developed synthetic humanized <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">insulin</a> by joining its gene with a <a href="/wiki/Plasmid" title="Plasmid">plasmid</a> vector inserted into the bacterium <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i>. Insulin, widely used for the treatment of diabetes, was previously extracted from the pancreas of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abattoir" title="Abattoir">abattoir</a> animals (cattle and/or pigs). The resulting genetically engineered bacterium enabled the production of vast quantities of synthetic human insulin at relatively low cost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USIS_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-USIS-23">[23]</a></sup> Biotechnology has also enabled emerging therapeutics like <a href="/wiki/Gene_therapy" title="Gene therapy">gene therapy</a>. The application of biotechnology to basic science (for example through the <a href="/wiki/Human_Genome_Project" title="Human Genome Project">Human Genome Project</a>) has also dramatically improved our understanding of <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> and as our scientific knowledge of normal and disease biology has increased, our ability to develop new medicines to treat previously untreatable diseases has increased as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USIS_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-USIS-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Genetic_testing" title="Genetic testing">Genetic testing</a> allows the <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Medical_diagnosis" title="Medical diagnosis">diagnosis</a> of vulnerabilities to inherited <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diseases" title="Diseases">diseases</a>, and can also be used to determine a child's parentage (genetic mother and father) or in general a person's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancestry" title="Ancestry">ancestry</a>. In addition to studying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromosomes" title="Chromosomes">chromosomes</a> to the level of individual genes, genetic testing in a broader sense includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biochemical" title="Biochemical">biochemical</a> tests for the possible presence of genetic diseases, or mutant forms of genes associated with increased risk of developing genetic disorders. Genetic testing identifies changes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromosomes" title="Chromosomes">chromosomes</a>, genes, or proteins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Most of the time, testing is used to find changes that are associated with inherited disorders. The results of a genetic test can confirm or rule out a suspected genetic condition or help determine a person's chance of developing or passing on a <a href="/wiki/Genetic_disorder" title="Genetic disorder">genetic disorder</a>. As of 2011 several hundred genetic tests were in use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Since genetic testing may open up ethical or psychological problems, genetic testing is often accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Genetic_counseling" title="Genetic counseling">genetic counseling</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_crops" title="Genetically modified crops">Genetically modified crops</a> ("GM crops", or "biotech crops") are plants used in <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, the <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> of which has been modified with <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetic engineering</a> techniques. In most cases the aim is to introduce a new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trait_(biology)" title="Trait (biology)">trait</a> to the plant which does not occur naturally in the species.</p>
<p>Examples in food crops include resistance to certain pests,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news.google.co.uk_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-news.google.co.uk-27">[27]</a></sup> diseases,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> stressful environmental conditions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> resistance to chemical treatments (e.g. resistance to a <a href="/wiki/Herbicide" title="Herbicide">herbicide</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>), reduction of spoilage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haroldsen1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haroldsen1-31">[31]</a></sup> or improving the nutrient profile of the crop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Examples in non-food crops include production of <a href="/wiki/Plant_manufactured_pharmaceuticals" title="Plant manufactured pharmaceuticals">pharmaceutical agents</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and other industrially useful goods,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> as well as for <a href="/wiki/Bioremediation" title="Bioremediation">bioremediation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diaz_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Diaz-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Farmers have widely adopted GM technology. Between 1996 and 2011, the total surface area of land cultivated with GM crops had increased by a factor of 94, from 17,000 square kilometers (4,200,000 acres) to 1,600,000 km<sup>2</sup> (395 million acres).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-James2011_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-James2011-38">[38]</a></sup> 10% of the world's crop lands were planted with GM crops in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-James2011_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-James2011-38">[38]</a></sup> As of 2011, 11 different transgenic crops were grown commercially on 395 million acres (160 million hectares) in 29 countries such as the USA, Brazil, Argentina, India, Canada, China, Paraguay, Pakistan, South Africa, Uruguay, Bolivia, Australia, Philippines, Myanmar, Burkina Faso, Mexico and Spain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-James2011_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-James2011-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_food" title="Genetically modified food">Genetically modified foods</a> are foods produced from <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> that have had specific changes introduced into their <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> with the methods of <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetic engineering</a>. These techniques have allowed for the introduction of new crop traits as well as a far greater control over a food's genetic structure than previously afforded by methods such as <a href="/wiki/Selective_breeding" title="Selective breeding">selective breeding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mutation_breeding" title="Mutation breeding">mutation breeding</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Commercial sale of genetically modified foods began in 1994, when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calgene" title="Calgene">Calgene</a> first marketed its <a href="/wiki/Flavr_Savr" title="Flavr Savr">Flavr Savr</a> delayed ripening tomato.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-James_1996_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-James_1996-40">[40]</a></sup> To date most genetic modification of foods have primarily focused on <a href="/wiki/Cash_crop" title="Cash crop">cash crops</a> in high demand by farmers such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transgenic_soybean" title="Transgenic soybean">soybean</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transgenic_maize" title="Transgenic maize">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canola" title="Canola">canola</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cotton_seed_oil" title="Cotton seed oil">cotton seed oil</a>. These have been engineered for resistance to pathogens and herbicides and better nutrient profiles. GM livestock have also been experimentally developed, although as of November 2013 none are currently on the market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is a <a href="/wiki/Scientific_consensus" title="Scientific consensus">scientific consensus</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> that currently available food derived from GM crops poses no greater risk to human health than conventional food,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> but that each GM food needs to be tested on a case-by-case basis before introduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Nonetheless, members of the public are much less likely than scientists to perceive GM foods as safe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> The legal and regulatory status of GM foods varies by country, with some nations banning or restricting them, and others permitting them with widely differing degrees of regulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>GM crops also provide a number of ecological benefits, if not used in excess.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-62">[62]</a></sup> However, opponents have objected to GM crops per se on several grounds, including environmental concerns, whether food produced from GM crops is safe, whether GM crops are needed to address the world's food needs, and economic concerns raised by the fact these organisms are subject to intellectual property law.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial"><span id="Industrial_biotechnology"></span><span id="Industrial">Industrial</span></span></h3>
<p>Industrial biotechnology (known mainly in Europe as white biotechnology) is the application of biotechnology for industrial purposes, including <a href="/wiki/Industrial_fermentation" title="Industrial fermentation">industrial fermentation</a>. It includes the practice of using <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micro-organisms" title="Micro-organisms">micro-organisms</a>, or components of cells like <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a>, to generate <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industrially</a> useful products in sectors such as chemicals, food and feed, detergents, paper and pulp, textiles and <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> In doing so, biotechnology uses renewable raw materials and may contribute to lowering greenhouse gas emissions and moving away from a petrochemical-based economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental">Environmental</span></h3>
<p>The environment can be affected by biotechnologies, both positively and adversely. Vallero and others have argued that the difference between beneficial biotechnology (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Bioremediation" title="Bioremediation">bioremediation</a> to clean up an oil spill or hazard chemical leak) versus the adverse effects stemming from biotechnological enterprises (e.g. flow of genetic material from transgenic organisms into wild strains) can be seen as applications and implications, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Cleaning up environmental wastes is an example of an application of environmental biotechnology; whereas loss of biodiversity or loss of containment of a harmful microbe are examples of environmental implications of biotechnology.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation">Regulation</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Regulation_of_genetic_engineering" title="Regulation of genetic engineering">Regulation of genetic engineering</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regulation_of_the_release_of_genetic_modified_organisms" title="Regulation of the release of genetic modified organisms">Regulation of the release of genetic modified organisms</a></div>
<p>The regulation of genetic engineering concerns approaches taken by governments to assess and manage the risks associated with the use of <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetic engineering</a> technology, and the development and release of genetically modified organisms (GMO), including <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_crops" title="Genetically modified crops">genetically modified crops</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_fish" title="Genetically modified fish">genetically modified fish</a>. There are differences in the regulation of GMOs between countries, with some of the most marked differences occurring between the USA and Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Regulation varies in a given country depending on the intended use of the products of the genetic engineering. For example, a crop not intended for food use is generally not reviewed by authorities responsible for food safety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PotatoPro_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-PotatoPro-67">[67]</a></sup> The European Union differentiates between approval for cultivation within the EU and approval for import and processing. While only a few GMOs have been approved for cultivation in the EU a number of GMOs have been approved for import and processing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wesseler-2011_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wesseler-2011-68">[68]</a></sup> The cultivation of GMOs has triggered a debate about coexistence of GM and non GM crops. Depending on the coexistence regulations incentives for cultivation of GM crops differ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beckman-2011_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beckman-2011-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Learning">Learning</span></h2>
<p>In 1988, after prompting from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_General_Medical_Sciences" title="National Institute of General Medical Sciences">National Institute of General Medical Sciences</a> (<a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a>) (NIGMS) instituted a funding mechanism for biotechnology training. Universities nationwide compete for these funds to establish <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Biotechnology_Training_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Biotechnology Training Program (page does not exist)">Biotechnology Training Programs</a> (BTPs). Each successful application is generally funded for five years then must be competitively renewed. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graduate_students" title="Graduate students">Graduate students</a> in turn compete for acceptance into a BTP; if accepted, then stipend, tuition and health insurance support is provided for two or three years during the course of their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ph.D." title="Ph.D.">Ph.D.</a> thesis work. Nineteen institutions offer NIGMS supported BTPs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Biotechnology training is also offered at the undergraduate level and in community colleges.</p>
