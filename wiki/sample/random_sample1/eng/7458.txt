<p>A <b>bioreactor</b> may refer to any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manufacture" title="Manufacture">manufactured</a> or <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineered</a> device or system that supports a biologically active environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In one case, a bioreactor is a vessel in which a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical process</a> is carried out which involves <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> or <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemically</a> active <a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">substances</a> derived from such organisms. This process can either be <a href="/wiki/Aerobic_organism" title="Aerobic organism">aerobic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_organism" title="Anaerobic organism">anaerobic</a>. These bioreactors are commonly cylindrical, ranging in size from litres to cubic metres, and are often made of <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a>.</p>
<p>A bioreactor may also refer to a device or system meant to grow <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_tissue" title="Biological tissue">tissues</a> in the context of <a href="/wiki/Cell_culture" title="Cell culture">cell culture</a>. These devices are being developed for use in <a href="/wiki/Tissue_engineering" title="Tissue engineering">tissue engineering</a> or <a href="/wiki/Biochemical_engineering" title="Biochemical engineering">biochemical engineering</a>.</p>
<p>On the basis of <b>mode of operation</b>, a bioreactor may be classified as <a href="/wiki/Batch_reactor" title="Batch reactor">batch</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fed-batch" title="Fed-batch">fed batch</a> or <a href="/wiki/Continuous_reactor" title="Continuous reactor">continuous</a> (e.g. a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continuous_stirred-tank_reactor_model" title="Continuous stirred-tank reactor model">continuous stirred-tank reactor model</a>). An example of a continuous bioreactor is the <a href="/wiki/Chemostat" title="Chemostat">chemostat</a>.</p>
<p>Organisms growing in bioreactors may be submerged in liquid medium or may be attached to the surface of a solid medium. Submerged cultures may be suspended or immobilized. Suspension bioreactors can use a wider variety of organisms, since special attachment surfaces are not needed, and can operate at much larger scale than immobilized cultures. However, in a continuously operated process the organisms will be removed from the reactor with the effluent. Immobilization is a general term describing a wide variety of cell or particle attachment or entrapment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It can be applied to basically all types of biocatalysis including enzymes, cellular organelles, animal and plant cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Immobilization is useful for continuously operated processes, since the organisms will not be removed with the reactor effluent, but is limited in scale because the microbes are only present on the surfaces of the vessel.</p>
<p>Large scale immobilized cell bioreactors are:</p>
<ul>
<li>moving media, also known as <a href="/wiki/Moving_bed_biofilm_reactor" title="Moving bed biofilm reactor">moving bed biofilm reactor (MBBR)</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Packed_bed" title="Packed bed">packed bed</a></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fibrous_bed&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fibrous bed (page does not exist)">fibrous bed</a></li>
<li>membrane</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bioreactor_design">Bioreactor design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bioreactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Bioreactor design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pg166_bioreactor.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="480" height="333" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Pg166_bioreactor.jpg/250px-Pg166_bioreactor.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Pg166_bioreactor.jpg/375px-Pg166_bioreactor.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c7/Pg166_bioreactor.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pg166_bioreactor.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A closed bioreactor used in cellulosic ethanol research</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Bioreactor design is a relatively complex engineering task, which is studied in the discipline of <a href="/wiki/Biochemical_engineering" title="Biochemical engineering">biochemical engineering</a>. Under optimum conditions, the microorganisms or cells are able to perform their desired function with limited production of impurities. The environmental conditions inside the bioreactor, such as temperature, nutrient concentrations, pH, and dissolved gases (especially oxygen for aerobic fermentations) affect the growth and productivity of the organisms. The temperature of the fermentation medium is maintained by a cooling jacket, coils, or both. Particularly exothermic fermentations may require the use of external heat exchangers. Nutrients may be continuously added to the fermenter, as in a fed-batch system, or may be charged into the reactor at the beginning of fermentation. The pH of the medium is measured and adjusted with small amounts of acid or base, depending upon the fermentation. For aerobic (and some anaerobic) fermentations, reactant gases (especially oxygen) must be added to the fermentation. Since oxygen is relatively insoluble in water (the basis of nearly all fermentation media), air (or purified oxygen) must be added continuously. The action of the rising bubbles helps mix the fermentation medium and also "<a href="/wiki/Air_stripping" title="Air stripping">strips</a>" out waste gases, such as carbon dioxide. In practice, bioreactors are often pressurized; this increases the <a href="/wiki/Henry%27s_law" title="Henry's law">solubility of oxygen</a> in water. In an aerobic process, optimal oxygen transfer is sometimes the rate limiting step. <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">Oxygen</a> is poorly soluble in water—even less in warm fermentation broths—and is relatively scarce in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> (20.95%). Oxygen transfer is usually helped by agitation, which is also needed to mix nutrients and to keep the fermentation homogeneous. <a href="/wiki/Mixing_(process_engineering)#Liquid-gas_mixing" title="Mixing (process engineering)">Gas dispersing agitators</a> are used to break up air bubbles and circulate them throughout the vessel.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Fouling" title="Fouling">Fouling</a></i> can harm the overall efficiency of the bioreactor, especially the <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">heat exchangers</a>. To avoid it, the bioreactor must be easily cleaned. Interior surfaces are typically made of stainless steel for easy cleaning and sanitation. Typically bioreactors are cleaned between batches, or are designed to reduce fouling as much as possible when operated continuously. Heat transfer is an important part of bioreactor design; small vessels can be cooled with a cooling jacket, but larger vessels may require coils or an external heat exchanger.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Photobioreactor">Photobioreactor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bioreactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Photobioreactor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Photobioreactor" title="Photobioreactor">photobioreactor</a> (PBR) is a bioreactor which incorporates some type of light source (that may be natural sunlight or artificial illumination). Virtually any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Translucent" title="Translucent">translucent</a> container could be called a PBR, however the term is more commonly used to define a closed system, as opposed to an open <a href="/wiki/Tank" title="Tank">tank</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">pond</a>. Photobioreactors are used to grow small <a href="/wiki/Phototroph" title="Phototroph">phototrophic</a> organisms such as <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alga" title="Alga">algae</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">moss</a> plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> These organisms use light through <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> as their <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> source and do not require <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a> as energy source. Consequently, risk of <a href="/wiki/Contamination" title="Contamination">contamination</a> with other organisms like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterium" title="Bacterium">bacteria</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a> is lower in photobioreactors when compared to bioreactors for <a href="/wiki/Heterotroph" title="Heterotroph">heterotroph</a> organisms.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sewage_treatment">Sewage treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bioreactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sewage treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bioreactors are also designed to treat <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a>. In the most efficient of these systems, there is a supply of a free-flowing, chemically inert medium which acts as a receptacle for the bacteria that break down the raw sewage. Examples of these bioreactors often have separate, sequential tanks and a mechanical separator or cyclone to speed the separation of water and biosolids. <a href="/wiki/Water_aeration#Aeration_methods" title="Water aeration">Aerators</a> supply oxygen to the sewage and medium, further accelerating breakdown. <a href="/wiki/Submersible_mixer" title="Submersible mixer">Submersible mixers</a> provide agitation in anoxic bioreactors to keep the solids in suspension and thereby ensure that the bacteria and the organic materials "meet". In the process, the liquid's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biochemical_Oxygen_Demand" title="Biochemical Oxygen Demand">Biochemical Oxygen Demand</a> (BOD) is reduced sufficiently to render the contaminated water fit for reuse. The biosolids can be collected for further processing, or dried and used as fertilizer. An extremely simple version of a sewage bioreactor is a septic tank whereby the sewage is left in situ, with or without additional media to house bacteria. In this instance, the biosludge itself is the primary host (activated sludge) for the bacteria. Septic systems are best suited where there is sufficient landmass, and the system is not subject to flooding or overly saturated ground, and where time and efficiency are not prioritized.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Because they are the engine that drives biological wastewater treatment, it is critical to closely monitor the quantity and quality of microorganisms in bioreactors. One method for this is via 2nd Generation <a href="/wiki/ATP_test" title="ATP test">ATP tests</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Up_and_down_agitation_bioreactor">Up and down agitation bioreactor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bioreactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Up and down agitation bioreactor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

Unique up and down agitation in the bioreactor.</div>

<p>Up and down agitators are useful to avoid shear stress to the cells. These are done by instead of a traditional propeller agitator, which requires an expensive motor and magnetic coupling. Vertical up and down motion is achieved by a motor together with an inexpensive membrane perfectly assure sterility and produce an efficient mixing without formation of a vortex (no baffles needed). At the same time this type of mixing is gentler on cells and produces less foam. Novel biomimicking “fish-tail” stirring discs offer maximum mixing efficiency without cutting edges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>In bioreactors in which the goal is to grow cells or tissues for experimental or therapeutic purposes, the design is significantly different from industrial bioreactors. Many cells and tissues, especially mammalian ones, must have a surface or other structural support in order to grow, and agitated environments are often destructive to these cell types and tissues. Higher organisms, being <a href="/wiki/Auxotrophy" title="Auxotrophy">auxotrophic</a>, also require highly specialized growth media.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> has developed a new type of bioreactor that artificially grows tissue in cell cultures. NASA's tissue bioreactor can grow heart tissue, skeletal tissue, ligaments, cancer tissue for study, and other types of tissue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>For more information on artificial tissue culture, see <a href="/wiki/Tissue_engineering" title="Tissue engineering">tissue engineering</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modelling_of_bioreactor">Modelling of bioreactor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bioreactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Modelling of bioreactor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mathematical models act as an important tool in various bio-reactor applications including wastewater treatment. These models are useful for planning efficient <a href="/wiki/Process_control" title="Process control">process control</a> strategies and predicting the future plant performance. Moreover, these models are beneficial in education and research areas.</p>
<p>Bioreactors are generally used in those industries which are concerned with food, beverages and pharmaceuticals. The emergence of <i><a href="/wiki/Biochemical_engineering" title="Biochemical engineering">Biochemical engineering</a></i> is of recent origin. Processing of biological materials using biological agents such as cells, enzymes or antibodies are the major pillars of biochemical engineering. Applications of biochemical engineering cover major fields of civilization such as agriculture, food and healthcare, <a href="/wiki/Resource_recovery" title="Resource recovery">resource recovery</a> and fine chemicals.</p>
<p>Till now, the industries associated with biotechnology have been lagged behind other industries in implementing control over the process and optimization strategies. A main drawback in biotechnological process control is the problem to measure key physical and biochemical parameters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_stages_in_a_bio-process">Operational stages in a bio-process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bioreactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Operational stages in a bio-process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A bioprocess is composed mainly of three stages — upstream processing, bioreaction, and downstream processing — to convert raw material to finished product.</p>
<p>The raw material can be of biological or non-biological origin. It is first converted to more suitable form for processing. This is done in upstream processing step which involves chemical hydrolysis, preparation of liquid medium, separation of particulate, air purification and many other preparatory operations.</p>
<p>After upstream processing step, the resulting feed is transferred to one or more Bioreaction stages. The Biochemical reactors or bioreactors form the base of the Bioreaction step. This step is mainly consists of three operations namely, production of <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>, metabolize biosynthesis and biotransformation.</p>
<p>Finally, the material produced in the bioreactor must be further processed in the downstream section to convert it into more useful form. The downstream process is mainly consists of physical separation operations which includes, solid liquid separation, <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liquid%E2%80%93liquid_extraction" title="Liquid–liquid extraction">liquid-liquid extraction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drying" title="Drying">drying</a> etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Specifications_of_a_bioreactor">Specifications of a bioreactor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bioreactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Specifications of a bioreactor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A typical bioreactor consists of following parts:</p>
<p><b>Agitator –</b> used for the mixing of the contents of the reactor which keeps the “cells” in the perfect homogenous condition for better transport of nutrients and oxygen to the desired product(s).</p>
<p><b>Baffle –</b> used to break the vortex formation in the vessel, which is usually highly undesirable as it changes the center of gravity of the system and consumes additional power.</p>
<p><b>Sparger –</b> In aerobic cultivation process, the purpose of the sparger is to supply adequate oxygen to the growing cells.</p>
<p><b>Jacket –</b> The jacket provides the annular area for circulation of constant temperature of water which keeps the temperature of the bioreactor at a constant value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>Assumptions –</p>
<ul>
<li>The reactor contents are perfectly mixed together.</li>
<li>The reactor is operating at a constant temperature (i.e., it is isothermal).</li>
<li>The feed should be clean and pure (i.e., no biomass in the feed stream).</li>
<li>The feed stream and reactor contents have equal and constant density (<i>ρ).</i></li>
<li>The feed and product streams have the same flow rate (F).</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Total_continuity_equation">Total continuity equation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bioreactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Total continuity equation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Making overall mass balance, we get the following equation:</p>
<p>d(<i>ρV)/dt = Fρ – Fρ = 0 (1)</i></p>
<p>Equation(1) states that the reactor volume (V) is constant since dV/dt = 0.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Biomass_continuity_equation">Biomass continuity equation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bioreactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Biomass continuity equation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>We know,</p>
<p>Flow rate of biomass into the reactor = Fx<sub>i</sub></p>
<pre>
Flow rate of biomass out of the reactor = Fx
</pre>
<p>Rate of generation of biomass by reaction = Vr<sub>1</sub></p>
<p>Rate of accumulation of biomass within the reactor = d(Vx)/dt</p>
<p>Now, apply general mass balance equation i.e,</p>
<p><i>Rate of Mass In – Rate of Mass Out + Rate of Generation = Accumulation</i></p>
<p>d(Vx)/dt = Fx<sub>i</sub> – Fx + Vr<sub>1</sub> (2)</p>
<p>Where <b>r<sub>1</sub></b> is the rate of cell generation. Dividing both sides of the above equation by V, we obtain</p>
<p>dx/dt = (F/V)x<sub>i</sub> – (F/V)x + r<sub>1</sub> (3)</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction_engineering" title="Chemical reaction engineering">chemical reaction engineering</a>, <b>F/V</b> is called <b>space velocity</b>(s<sup>−1</sup>) and <b>V/F</b> is called the <b>residence time</b> (s). But in <a href="/wiki/Biochemical_engineering" title="Biochemical engineering">biochemical engineering</a>, <b>F/V</b> is known as <b>Dilution rate (D<sub>r</sub>)</b>. Accordingly, equation(3) yields:</p>
<p>dx/dt = D<sub>r</sub>x<sub>i</sub> – D<sub>r</sub>x + r<sub>1</sub> (4)</p>
<p>dx/dt = D<sub>r</sub>(x<sub>i</sub> – x) + r<sub>1</sub> (5)</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Substrate_continuity_equation">Substrate continuity equation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bioreactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Substrate continuity equation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>For substrate balance,</p>
<p>Flow rate of substrate into the bioreactor = FS<sub>i</sub></p>
<p>Flow of the substrate out of the bioreactor = FS</p>
<p>Rate of generation of substrate by reaction = –Vr<sub>2</sub></p>
<p>Rate of accumulation of substrate within the reactor = d(VS)/dt</p>
<p>Now, apply general mass balance equation i.e,</p>
<p><i>Rate of Mass In – Rate of Mass Out + Rate of Generation = Accumulation</i></p>
<p>d(VS)/dt = FS<sub>i</sub> – FS – Vr<sub>2</sub> (6)</p>
<p>rearranging above equation, we get</p>
<p>dS/dt = D<sub>r</sub>(S<sub>i</sub> –S ) – r<sub>2</sub> (7)</p>
<p>where <b>r<sub>2</sub></b> is the rate of substrate consumption.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Biochemical_reaction_kinetics">Biochemical reaction kinetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bioreactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Biochemical reaction kinetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>For the chemical reaction,</p>
<p>A ----&gt; P</p>
<p>We can write</p>
<p>( –r<sub>A</sub>) = k (C<sub>A</sub>)<sup>n</sup> (8)</p>
<p>(r<sub>A</sub>) = – k (C<sub>A</sub>)<sup>n</sup> (9)</p>
<p>Where,</p>
<p>( –r<sub>A</sub>) = rate of disappearance of A</p>
<p>(r<sub>A</sub>) = rate of formation of A</p>
<p>k = reaction rate constant</p>
<p>C<sub>A</sub> = Concentration of reactant A</p>
<p>n = order of reaction with respect to component A</p>
<p>For first order reaction, n = 1 and accordingly,</p>
<p>–r<sub>A</sub> = k C<sub>A</sub></p>
<p>The reaction kinetics involved in biochemical operations is comparatively difficult to obtain than the chemical reaction kinetics. In biochemical operations, the cell kinetics is used for the unstructured models where balanced growth condition is assumed.</p>
<p>The following equation is used to represent the net rate of cell mass growth:</p>
<p>r<sub>1</sub> = <i>μx (10)</i></p>
<p><i>where μ</i> is the specific growth rate or specific growth rate coefficient(s<sup>−1</sup>). Here, <i>μ</i> is analogous to first order rate constant k but however, <i>μ</i> is not a constant.</p>
<p>In biochemical engineering, <b>yield</b> is defined as the ratio of mass or moles of product formed to the mass or moles of the reactants consumed. The yield (Y) of product (P) with respect to reactant A is defined as:</p>
<p>Y = (mass of P formed )/(mass of A consumed) (11)</p>
<p>In case of bioreactor,</p>
<p>Y = (mass of cells formed)/(mass of substrate consumed) (12)</p>
<p>Thus,</p>
<p>Y = r<sub>1</sub>/ r<sub>2</sub></p>
<p>Or,</p>
<p>r<sub>2</sub> = r<sub>1</sub>/Y</p>
<p>Or,</p>
<p>r<sub>2</sub> = <i>μx/</i>Y ( from 10) (13)</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_form_of_equation_of_modelling">Final form of equation of modelling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bioreactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Final form of equation of modelling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>By substituting equations (10) &amp; (13) in equations (5) &amp; (7) respectively, we get,</p>
<p><b>dx/dt = D<sub>r</sub>(x<sub>i</sub> – x) + <i>μx</i></b> (14)</p>
<p><b>dS/dt = S(S<sub>i</sub> – S) – (<i>μx/Y)</i></b> (15)</p>
<p>Since we have assumed that the feed stream does not contain any biomass i.e., <b>x<sub>i</sub> = 0</b>, then, bioreactor modelling equation finally get the following form:</p>
<p><b>dx/dt = (<i>μ</i> – D<sub>r</sub>)x</b> (16)</p>
<p><b>dS/dt = S(S<sub>i</sub> – S) – (<i>μx/Y)</i></b> <i>(from 15)</i></p>
<p>Thus,Equations (15) and (16) are the basic equations which are used for the modelling of any bioreactor.</p>
