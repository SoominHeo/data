<p><b>Biological control</b> is a method of <a href="/wiki/Pest_control" title="Pest control">controlling pests</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mite" title="Mite">mites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weed" title="Weed">weeds</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phytopathology" title="Phytopathology">plant diseases</a> <a href="/wiki/Bioeffector" title="Bioeffector">using other organisms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It relies on <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parasitism" title="Parasitism">parasitism</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbivory" title="Herbivory">herbivory</a>, or other natural mechanisms, but typically also involves an active human management role. It can be an important component of <a href="/wiki/Integrated_pest_management" title="Integrated pest management">integrated pest management</a> (IPM) programs.</p>
<p>There are three basic types of biological pest control strategies: importation (sometimes called <b>classical biological control</b>), in which a natural enemy of a pest is introduced in the hope of achieving control; augmentation, in which locally-occurring natural enemies are bred and released to improve control; and conservation, in which measures are taken to increase natural enemies, such as by planting nectar-producing crop plants in the borders of rice fields.</p>
<p>Natural enemies of insect pests, also known as biological control agents, include predators, <a href="/wiki/Parasitoid" title="Parasitoid">parasitoids</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a>. Biological control agents of plant <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">diseases</a> are most often referred to as antagonists. Biological control agents of weeds include seed predators, <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivores</a> and plant pathogens.</p>
<p>Biological control can have side-effects on biodiversity through predation, parasitism, pathogenicity, competition, or other attacks on non-target species, especially when a species is introduced without thorough understanding of the possible consequences.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biological_pest_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "biological control" was first used by Harry Scott Smith at the 1919 meeting of the Pacific Slope Branch of the American Association of Economic Entomologists, at the Mission Inn in downtown Riverside, California,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and later defined by P. DeBach and K. S. Hagen in 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, the practice has previously been used for centuries. The first report of the use of an insect species to control an insect pest comes from "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nan_Fang_Cao_Mu_Zhuang&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nan Fang Cao Mu Zhuang (page does not exist)">Nan Fang Cao Mu Zhuang</a>" (南方草木狀 <i>Plants of the Southern Regions</i>) (ca. 304 AD), which is attributed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jin_dynasty_(265-420)" title="Jin dynasty (265-420)">Western Jin dynasty</a> botanist <i>Ji Han</i> (嵇含, 263–307), in which it is mentioned that "<i><a href="/wiki/Jiaozhi" title="Jiaozhi">Jiaozhi</a> people sell ants and their nests attached to twigs looking like thin cotton envelopes, the reddish-yellow ant being larger than normal. Without such ants, southern citrus fruits will be severely insect-damaged</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http-server.carleton.ca_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-http-server.carleton.ca-4">[4]</a></sup> The ants used are known as <i>huang gan</i> (<i>huang</i> = yellow, <i>gan</i> = citrus) ants (<i><a href="/wiki/Oecophylla_smaragdina" title="Oecophylla smaragdina">Oecophylla smaragdina</a></i>). The practice was later reported by Ling Biao Lu Yi (late <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang Dynasty</a> or Early <a href="/wiki/Five_Dynasties" title="Five Dynasties">Five Dynasties</a>), in <i>Ji Le Pian</i> by <i>Zhuang Jisu</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" title="Song Dynasty">Southern Song Dynasty</a>), in the <i>Book of Tree Planting</i> by Yu Zhen Mu (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a>), in the book <i>Guangdong Xing Yu</i> (17th century), <i>Lingnan</i> by Wu Zhen Fang (Qing Dynasty), in <i>Nanyue Miscellanies</i> by Li Diao Yuan, and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http-server.carleton.ca_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-http-server.carleton.ca-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Biological control techniques as we know them today started to emerge in the 1870s. During this decade, in the USA, the Missouri State Entomologist C. V. Riley and the Illinois State Entomologist W. LeBaron began within-state redistribution of parasitoids to control crop pests. The first international shipment of an insect as biological control agent was made by Charles V. Riley in 1873, shipping to France the predatory mites <i>Tyroglyphus phylloxera</i> to help fight the grapevine phylloxera (<a href="/wiki/Phylloxera" title="Phylloxera"><i>Daktulosphaira vitifoliae</i></a>) that was destroying grapevines in France. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">United States Department of Agriculture</a> (USDA) initiated research in classical biological control following the establishment of the Division of Entomology in 1881, with C. V. Riley as Chief. The first importation of a parasitoid into the United States was this of <i><a href="/wiki/Cotesia_glomerata" title="Cotesia glomerata">Cotesia glomerata</a></i> in 1883–1884, imported from Europe to control the imported cabbage white butterfly, <i><a href="/wiki/Pieris_rapae" title="Pieris rapae">Pieris rapae</a></i>. In 1888–1889 the vedalia beetle, <i><a href="/wiki/Rodolia_cardinalis" title="Rodolia cardinalis">Rodolia cardinalis</a></i>, which is a ladybug, was introduced from Australia to California to control the cottony cushion scale, <i><a href="/wiki/Icerya_purchasi" title="Icerya purchasi">Icerya purchasi</a></i>. This had become a major problem for the newly developed citrus industry in California, and by the end of 1889 the cottony cushion scale population had already declined. This great success led to further introductions of beneficial insects into the USA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coulson_J._R._2000_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coulson_J._R._2000-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1905 the USDA initiated its first large-scale biological control program, sending entomologists to Europe and Japan to look for natural enemies of the gypsy moth, <i><a href="/wiki/Lymantria_dispar_dispar" title="Lymantria dispar dispar">Lymantria dispar dispar</a></i>, and brown-tail moth, <a href="/wiki/Brown-tail" title="Brown-tail"><i>Euproctis chrysorrhoea</i></a>, invasive pests of trees and shrubs. As a result, nine parasitoids of gypsy moth, seven of brown-tail moth, and two predators for both moths became established in the USA. Although the gypsy moth was not fully controlled by these natural enemies, the frequency, duration, and severity of its outbreaks were reduced and the program was regarded as successful. This program also led to the development of many concepts, principles, and procedures for the implementation of biological control programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coulson_J._R._2000_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Coulson_J._R._2000-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first reported case of a classical biological control attempt in Canada involves the hymenopteran parasitoid <i>Trichogramma minutum</i>. Individuals were caught in New York State and released in Ontario gardens in 1882 by William Saunders, trained chemist and first Director of the Dominion Experimental Farms, for controlling the imported currantworm <i><a href="/wiki/Nematus_ribesii" title="Nematus ribesii">Nematus ribesii</a></i>. Between 1884 and 1908, the first Dominion Entomologist, James Fletcher, continued introductions of other parasitoids and pathogens for the control of pests in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_biological_pest_control">Types of biological pest control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biological_pest_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types of biological pest control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are three basic biological pest control strategies: importation (classical biological control), augmentation and conservation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cornell_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Importation">Importation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biological_pest_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Importation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vedalia_Beetle_(15959056801).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Rodolia_cardinalis" title="Rodolia cardinalis">Rodolia cardinalis</a></i>, the vedalia beetle, was imported to Australia in the 19th century, successfully controlling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cottony_cushion_scale" title="Cottony cushion scale">cottony cushion scale</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Importation or classical biological control involves the introduction of a pest's natural enemies to a new locale where they do not occur naturally. Early instances were often unofficial and not based on research, and some introduced species became serious pests themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Classical_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Classical-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>To be most effective at controlling a pest, a biological control agent requires a colonizing ability which allows it to keep pace with the spatial and temporal disruption of the habitat. Control is greatest if the agent has temporal persistence, so that it can maintain its population even in the temporary absence of the target species, and if it is an opportunistic forager, enabling it to rapidly exploit a pest population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-follett_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-follett-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Needham" title="Joseph Needham">Joseph Needham</a> noted a Chinese text dating from 304 AD, <i>Records of the Plants and Trees of the Southern Regions</i>, by Hsi Han, which describes mandarin oranges protected by large reddish-yellow citrus ants which attack and kill insect pests of the orange trees. The citrus ant (<i><a href="/wiki/Oecophylla_smaragdina" title="Oecophylla smaragdina">Oecophylla smaragdina</a></i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> was rediscovered in the 20th century, and since 1958 has been used in China to protect orange groves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the earliest successes in the west was in controlling <i><a href="/wiki/Icerya_purchasi" title="Icerya purchasi">Icerya purchasi</a></i> (cottony cushion scale) in Australia, using a predatory insect <i><a href="/wiki/Rodolia_cardinalis" title="Rodolia cardinalis">Rodolia cardinalis</a></i> (the vedalia beetle). This success was repeated in California using the beetle and a parasitoid fly, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cryptochaetum_iceryae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cryptochaetum iceryae (page does not exist)">Cryptochaetum iceryae</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<i><a href="/wiki/Cactoblastis_cactorum" title="Cactoblastis cactorum">Cactoblastis cactorum</a></i> larvae feeding on <i><a href="/wiki/Opuntia" title="Opuntia">Opuntia</a></i> cacti</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Opuntia" title="Opuntia">Prickly pear cacti</a> were introduced into Queensland, Australia as ornamental plants. They quickly spread to cover over 25 million hectares of Australia. Two control agents were used to help control the spread of the plant, the cactus moth <i><a href="/wiki/Cactoblastis_cactorum" title="Cactoblastis cactorum">Cactoblastis cactorum</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Dactylopius" title="Dactylopius">Dactylopius</a></i> scale insects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Damage from <i><a href="/wiki/Hypera_postica" title="Hypera postica">Hypera postica</a></i>, the alfalfa weevil, a serious introduced pest of forage, was substantially reduced by the introduction of natural enemies. 20 years after their introduction the population of <a href="/wiki/Weevil" title="Weevil">weevils</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Alfalfa" title="Alfalfa">alfalfa</a> area treated for alfalfa weevil in the <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_United_States" title="Northeastern United States">Northeastern United States</a> remained 75 percent down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alternanthera_philoxeroides_NRCS-1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="783" data-file-width="1340" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Alternanthera_philoxeroides_NRCS-1.jpg/220px-Alternanthera_philoxeroides_NRCS-1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Alternanthera_philoxeroides_NRCS-1.jpg/330px-Alternanthera_philoxeroides_NRCS-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Alternanthera_philoxeroides_NRCS-1.jpg/440px-Alternanthera_philoxeroides_NRCS-1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alternanthera_philoxeroides_NRCS-1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The invasive species <i><a href="/wiki/Alternanthera_philoxeroides" title="Alternanthera philoxeroides">Alternanthera philoxeroides</a></i> (alligator weed) was controlled in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> (U.S.) by introducing <a href="/wiki/Agasicles_hygrophila" title="Agasicles hygrophila">alligator weed flea beetle</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alligator_weed" title="Alligator weed">Alligator weed</a> was introduced to the United States from <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. It takes root in shallow water, interfering with <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">navigation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Flood_control" title="Flood control">flood control</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Agasicles_hygrophila" title="Agasicles hygrophila">alligator weed flea beetle</a> and two other biological controls were released in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, enabling the state to ban the use of herbicides to control alligator weed three years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Another aquatic weed, the giant salvinia (<i><a href="/wiki/Salvinia_molesta" title="Salvinia molesta">Salvinia molesta</a></i>) is a serious pest, covering waterways, reducing water flow and harming native species. Control with the salvinia weevil (<i><a href="/wiki/Cyrtobagous_salviniae" title="Cyrtobagous salviniae">Cyrtobagous salviniae</a></i>) is effective in warm climates,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and in Zimbabwe, a 99% control of the weed was obtained over a two-year period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Small commercially reared parasitoidal <a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">wasps</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cornell_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-7">[7]</a></sup> <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Trichogramma_ostriniae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Trichogramma ostriniae (page does not exist)">Trichogramma ostriniae</a></i>, provide limited and erratic control of the <a href="/wiki/European_corn_borer" title="European corn borer">European corn borer</a> (<i>Ostrinia nubilalis</i>), a serious pest. Careful formulations of the bacterium <i><a href="/wiki/Bacillus_thuringiensis" title="Bacillus thuringiensis">Bacillus thuringiensis</a></i> are more effective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The population of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Levuana_iridescens" title="Levuana iridescens">Levuana iridescens</a></i>, the Levuana moth, a serious coconut pest in <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a>, was brought under control by a classical biological control program in the 1920s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Augmentation">Augmentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biological_pest_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Augmentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lady_bugs_are_a_beneficial_insect_commonly_sold_for_biological_control_of_aphids..jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1224" data-file-width="1632" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Lady_bugs_are_a_beneficial_insect_commonly_sold_for_biological_control_of_aphids..jpg/220px-Lady_bugs_are_a_beneficial_insect_commonly_sold_for_biological_control_of_aphids..jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Lady_bugs_are_a_beneficial_insect_commonly_sold_for_biological_control_of_aphids..jpg/330px-Lady_bugs_are_a_beneficial_insect_commonly_sold_for_biological_control_of_aphids..jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Lady_bugs_are_a_beneficial_insect_commonly_sold_for_biological_control_of_aphids..jpg/440px-Lady_bugs_are_a_beneficial_insect_commonly_sold_for_biological_control_of_aphids..jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lady_bugs_are_a_beneficial_insect_commonly_sold_for_biological_control_of_aphids..jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Hippodamia_convergens" title="Hippodamia convergens">Hippodamia convergens</a></i>, the convergent lady beetle, is commonly sold for biological control of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphids" title="Aphids">aphids</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Augmentation involves the supplemental release of natural enemies, boosting the naturally occurring population. In inoculative release, small numbers of the control agents are released at intervals to allow them to reproduce, in the hope of setting up longer-term control, and thus keeping the pest down to a low level, constituting prevention rather than cure. In inundative release, in contrast, large numbers are released in the hope of rapidly reducing a damaging pest population, correcting a problem that has already arisen. Augmentation can be effective, but is not guaranteed to work, and relies on understanding of the situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Augmentation_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Augmentation-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>An example of inoculative release occurs in <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouse</a> production of several crops. Periodic releases of the parasitoid, <i><a href="/wiki/Encarsia_formosa" title="Encarsia formosa">Encarsia formosa</a></i>, are used to control greenhouse <a href="/wiki/Whitefly" title="Whitefly">whitefly</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoddle1998_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoddle1998-21">[21]</a></sup> while the predatory mite <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phytoseiulus_persimilis" title="Phytoseiulus persimilis">Phytoseiulus persimilis</a></i> is used for control of the two-spotted spider mite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The egg parasite <i><a href="/wiki/Trichogramma" title="Trichogramma">Trichogramma</a></i> is frequently released inundatively to control harmful moths. Similarly, <i>Bacillus thuringiensis</i> and other microbial insecticides are similarly used in large enough quantities for a rapid effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Augmentation_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Augmentation-20">[20]</a></sup> Recommended release rates for <i>Trichogramma</i> in vegetable or field crops range from 5,000 to 200,000 per acre (1 to 50 per square metre) per week according to the level of pest infestation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peter2009_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peter2009-23">[23]</a></sup> Similarly, entomopathogenic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nematodes" title="Nematodes">nematodes</a> are released at rates of millions and even billions per acre for control of certain soil-dwelling insect pests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biological_pest_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The conservation of existing natural enemies in an environment is the third method of biological pest control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conservation_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Conservation-25">[25]</a></sup> Natural enemies are already adapted to the <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> and to the target pest, and their conservation can be simple and cost-effective, as when nectar-producing crop plants are grown in the borders of rice fields. These provide nectar to support parasitoids and predators of planthopper pests and have been demonstrated to be so effective (reducing pest densities by 10- or even 100-fold) that farmers sprayed 70% less insecticides, enjoyed yields boosted by 5%, and this led to an economic advantage of 7.5%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Predators of aphids were similarly found to be present in tussock grasses by field boundary hedges in England, but they spread too slowly to reach the centres of fields. Control was improved by planting a metre-wide strip of tussock grasses in field centres, enabling aphid predators to overwinter there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conservation_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Conservation-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dermaptera_flowerpot.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1988" data-file-width="1660" height="263" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Dermaptera_flowerpot.jpg/220px-Dermaptera_flowerpot.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Dermaptera_flowerpot.jpg/330px-Dermaptera_flowerpot.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Dermaptera_flowerpot.jpg/440px-Dermaptera_flowerpot.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dermaptera_flowerpot.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An inverted flowerpot filled with straw to attract <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dermaptera" title="Dermaptera">earwigs</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Cropping systems can be modified to favor natural enemies, a practice sometimes referred to as habitat manipulation. Providing a suitable habitat, such as a <a href="/wiki/Windbreak" title="Windbreak">shelterbelt</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hedgerow" title="Hedgerow">hedgerow</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Beetle_bank" title="Beetle bank">beetle bank</a> where beneficial insects can live and reproduce, can help ensure the survival of populations of natural enemies. Things as simple as leaving a layer of fallen leaves or mulch in place provides a suitable food source for worms and provides a shelter for insects, in turn being a food source for such beneficial mammals as <a href="/wiki/Hedgehog" title="Hedgehog">hedgehogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shrew" title="Shrew">shrews</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compost_pile" title="Compost pile">Compost piles</a> and stacks of wood can provide shelter for invertebrates and small mammals. Long grass and <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">ponds</a> support amphibians. Not removing dead annuals and non-hardy plants in the autumn allows insects to make use of their hollow stems during winter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-27">[27]</a></sup> In California, prune trees are sometimes planted in grape vineyards to provide an improved overwintering habitat or refuge for a key grape pest parasitoid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The providing of artificial shelters in the form of wooden caskets, <a href="/wiki/Box" title="Box">boxes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Flowerpot" title="Flowerpot">flowerpots</a> is also sometimes undertaken, particularly in gardens, to make a cropped area more attractive to natural enemies. For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dermaptera" title="Dermaptera">earwigs</a> are natural predators which can be encouraged in gardens by hanging upside-down flowerpots filled with <a href="/wiki/Straw" title="Straw">straw</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wood_wool" title="Wood wool">wood wool</a>. Green <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lacewings" title="Lacewings">lacewings</a> can be encouraged by using plastic bottles with an open bottom and a roll of cardboard inside. Birdhouses enable insectivorous birds to nest; the most useful birds can be attracted by choosing an opening just large enough for the desired species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_control_agents">Biological control agents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biological_pest_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Biological control agents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators">Predators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biological_pest_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Predators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chrysopidae_3035.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="328" data-file-width="300" height="241" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Chrysopidae_3035.jpg/220px-Chrysopidae_3035.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/56/Chrysopidae_3035.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chrysopidae_3035.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Chrysopidae" title="Chrysopidae">Lacewings</a> are available from biocontrol dealers.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Predators are mainly free-living species that directly consume a large number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prey" title="Prey">prey</a> during their whole lifetime. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ladybugs" title="Ladybugs">Ladybugs</a>, and in particular their larvae which are active between May and July in the northern hemisphere, are voracious predators of <a href="/wiki/Aphid" title="Aphid">aphids</a>, and also consume <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mites" title="Mites">mites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scale_insect" title="Scale insect">scale insects</a> and small <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Spotted_lady_beetle" title="Spotted lady beetle">spotted lady beetle</a> (<i>Coleomegilla maculata</i>) is also able to feed on the eggs and larvae of the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_potato_beetle" title="Colorado potato beetle">Colorado potato beetle</a> (<i>Leptinotarsa decemlineata</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The larvae of many <a href="/wiki/Hoverfly" title="Hoverfly">hoverfly</a> species principally feed upon greenfly (aphids), one larva devouring up to 400 in its lifetime. Their effectiveness in commercial crops has not been studied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Organic-agriculture_biocontrol-cotton_polistes-wasp3.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="271" data-file-width="300" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Organic-agriculture_biocontrol-cotton_polistes-wasp3.png/220px-Organic-agriculture_biocontrol-cotton_polistes-wasp3.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ae/Organic-agriculture_biocontrol-cotton_polistes-wasp3.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Organic-agriculture_biocontrol-cotton_polistes-wasp3.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator">Predatory</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Paper_wasp" title="Paper wasp">Polistes</a></i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vespid" title="Vespid">wasp</a> looking for bollworms or other <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a> on a cotton plant</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Several species of <a href="/wiki/Entomopathogenic_nematode" title="Entomopathogenic nematode">entomopathogenic nematode</a> are important predators of insect and other invertebrate pests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Phasmarhabditis_hermaphrodita" title="Phasmarhabditis hermaphrodita">Phasmarhabditis hermaphrodita</a></i> is a microscopic <a href="/wiki/Nematode" title="Nematode">nematode</a> that kills slugs. Its complex life cycle include a free-living, infective stage in the soil where it becomes associated with a pathogenic bacteria such as <i><a href="/wiki/Moraxella_osloensis" title="Moraxella osloensis">Moraxella osloensis</a></i>. The nematode enters the slug through the posterior mantle region, thereafter feeding and reproducing inside, but it is the bacteria that kill the slug. The nematode is available commercially in Europe and is applied by watering onto moist soil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Species used to control spider mites include the predatory mites <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phytoseiulus_persimilis" title="Phytoseiulus persimilis">Phytoseiulus persimilis</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Neoseilus_californicus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Neoseilus californicus (page does not exist)">Neoseilus californicus</a>,</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UConnMites_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-UConnMites-34">[34]</a></sup> and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Amblyseius_cucumeris&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Amblyseius cucumeris (page does not exist)">Amblyseius cucumeris</a></i>, the predatory midge <i><a href="/wiki/Feltiella_acarisuga" title="Feltiella acarisuga">Feltiella acarisuga</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UConnMites_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-UConnMites-34">[34]</a></sup> and a ladybird <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stethorus_punctillum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stethorus punctillum (page does not exist)">Stethorus punctillum</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UConnMites_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-UConnMites-34">[34]</a></sup> The bug <i><a href="/wiki/Orius_insidiosus" title="Orius insidiosus">Orius insidiosus</a></i> has been successfully used against the <a href="/wiki/Tetranychus_urticae" title="Tetranychus urticae">two-spotted spider mite</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Western_flower_thrips" title="Western flower thrips">western flower thrips</a> (<i>Frankliniella occidentalis</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parasitoids">Parasitoids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biological_pest_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Parasitoids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Parasitoid" title="Parasitoid">parasitoid</a></div>
<p>Parasitoids lay their eggs on or in the body of an insect host, which is then used as a food for developing larvae. The host is ultimately killed. Most insect <a href="/wiki/Parasitoid" title="Parasitoid">parasitoids</a> are wasps or <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a>, and may have a very narrow host range. The most important groups are the <a href="/wiki/Ichneumonoidea" title="Ichneumonoidea">ichneumonid wasps</a>, which prey mainly on <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a> of <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterflies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moths</a>; <a href="/wiki/Braconidae" title="Braconidae">braconid wasps</a>, which attack caterpillars and a wide range of other insects including greenfly; <a href="/wiki/Chalcid_wasp" title="Chalcid wasp">chalcid wasps</a>, which parasitize eggs and larvae of <a href="/wiki/Aphid" title="Aphid">greenfly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whitefly" title="Whitefly">whitefly</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabbage_caterpillar" title="Cabbage caterpillar">cabbage caterpillars</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scale_insect" title="Scale insect">scale insects</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Tachinidae" title="Tachinidae">tachinid flies</a>, which parasitize a wide range of insects including caterpillars, adult and larval <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hemiptera" title="Hemiptera">true bugs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>

<p><i><a href="/wiki/Encarsia_formosa" title="Encarsia formosa">Encarsia formosa</a></i> is a small predatory <a href="/wiki/Chalcid_wasp" title="Chalcid wasp">chalcid wasp</a> which is a parasitoid of <a href="/wiki/Whitefly" title="Whitefly">whitefly</a>, a sap-feeding insect which can cause wilting and <a href="/wiki/Sooty_mold" title="Sooty mold">black sooty moulds</a> in glasshouse vegetable and ornamental crops. It is most effective when dealing with low level infestations, giving protection over a long period of time. The wasp lays its eggs in young whitefly 'scales', turning them black as the parasite larvae pupates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoddle1998_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hoddle1998-21">[21]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Gonatocerus_ashmeadi" title="Gonatocerus ashmeadi">Gonatocerus ashmeadi</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">Hymenoptera</a>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mymaridae" title="Mymaridae">Mymaridae</a>) has been introduced to control the <a href="/wiki/Glassy-winged_sharpshooter" title="Glassy-winged sharpshooter">glassy-winged sharpshooter</a> <i>Homalodisca vitripennis</i> (Hemipterae: Cicadellidae) in <a href="/wiki/French_Polynesia" title="French Polynesia">French Polynesia</a> and has successfully controlled ~95% of the pest density.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoddle2006_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoddle2006-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Parasitoids are among the most widely used biological control agents. Commercially, there are two types of rearing systems: short-term daily output with high production of parasitoids per day, and long-term low daily output with a range in production of 4-1000million female parasitoids per week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-smith-38">[38]</a></sup> Larger production facilities produce on a yearlong basis, whereas some facilities produce only seasonally. Rearing facilities are usually a significant distance from where the agents are to be used in the field, and transporting the parasitoids from the point of production to the point of use can pose problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Shipping conditions can be too hot, and even vibrations from planes or trucks can adversely affect parasitoids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-smith-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pathogens">Pathogens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biological_pest_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pathogens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Biopesticide" title="Biopesticide">biopesticide</a></div>
<p>Pathogenic micro-organisms include <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viruses" title="Viruses">viruses</a>. They kill or debilitate their host and are relatively host-specific. Various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbial" title="Microbial">microbial</a> insect diseases occur naturally, but may also be used as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_pesticide" title="Biological pesticide">biological pesticides</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> When naturally occurring, these outbreaks are density-dependent in that they generally only occur as insect populations become denser.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bacteria">Bacteria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biological_pest_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Bacteria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Bacteria used for biological control infect insects via their digestive tracts, so they offer only limited options for controlling insects with sucking mouth parts such as aphids and scale insects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Bacillus_thuringiensis" title="Bacillus thuringiensis">Bacillus thuringiensis</a></i> is the most widely applied species of bacteria used for biological control, with at least four sub-species used against <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">Lepidopteran</a> (<a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterfly</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coleoptera" title="Coleoptera">Coleopteran</a> (beetle) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diptera" title="Diptera">Dipteran</a> (true fly) insect pests. The bacterium is available in sachets of dried spores which are mixed with water and sprayed onto vulnerable plants such as <a href="/wiki/Brassica" title="Brassica">brassicas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fruit_tree" title="Fruit tree">fruit trees</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lemaux_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lemaux-43">[43]</a></sup> <i>B. thuringiensis</i> has also been incorporated into crops, making them resistant to these pests and thus reducing the use of pesticides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kumar_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kumar-44">[44]</a></sup> The bacterium <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paenibacillus_popilliae" title="Paenibacillus popilliae">Paenibacillus popilliae</a></i> causes <a href="/wiki/Milky_spore" title="Milky spore">milky spore disease</a> has been found useful in the control of <a href="/wiki/Japanese_beetle" title="Japanese beetle">Japanese beetle</a>, killing the larvae. It is very specific to its host species and is harmless to vertebrates and other invertebrates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fungi">Fungi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biological_pest_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Fungi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entomopathogenic_fungi" title="Entomopathogenic fungi">Entomopathogenic fungi</a>, which cause disease in insects, include at least 14 species that attack <a href="/wiki/Aphid" title="Aphid">aphids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Beauveria_bassiana" title="Beauveria bassiana">Beauveria bassiana</a></i> is mass-produced and used to manage a wide variety of insect pests including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whiteflies" title="Whiteflies">whiteflies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thrips" title="Thrips">thrips</a>, aphids and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weevils" title="Weevils">weevils</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McNeil_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-McNeil-47">[47]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Lecanicillium" title="Lecanicillium">Lecanicillium</a></i> spp. are deployed against white flies, thrips and aphids. <i><a href="/wiki/Metarhizium" title="Metarhizium">Metarhizium</a></i> spp. are used against pests including beetles, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Locusts" title="Locusts">locusts</a> and other grasshoppers, <a href="/wiki/Hemiptera" title="Hemiptera">Hemiptera</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Spider_mite" title="Spider mite">spider mites</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Paecilomyces_fumosoroseus" title="Paecilomyces fumosoroseus">Paecilomyces fumosoroseus</a></i> is effective against white flies, thrips and aphids; <i><a href="/wiki/Purpureocillium" title="Purpureocillium">Purpureocillium</a> lilacinus</i> is used against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Root-knot_nematodes" title="Root-knot nematodes">root-knot nematodes</a>, and 89 <i><a href="/wiki/Trichoderma" title="Trichoderma">Trichoderma</a></i> <a href="/wiki/List_of_Trichoderma_species" title="List of Trichoderma species">species</a> against certain plant pathogens. <i><a href="/wiki/Trichoderma_viride" title="Trichoderma viride">Trichoderma viride</a></i> has been used against <a href="/wiki/Dutch_elm_disease" title="Dutch elm disease">Dutch elm disease</a>, and has shown some effect in suppressing <a href="/wiki/Chondrostereum_purpureum" title="Chondrostereum purpureum">silver leaf</a>, a disease of stone fruits caused by the pathogenic fungus <i><a href="/wiki/Chondrostereum_purpureum" title="Chondrostereum purpureum">Chondrostereum purpureum</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fry_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fry-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>The fungi <i><a href="/wiki/Cordyceps" title="Cordyceps">Cordyceps</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Metacordyceps" title="Metacordyceps">Metacordyceps</a></i> are deployed against a wide spectrum of arthropods.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Entomophaga_(fungus)" title="Entomophaga (fungus)">Entomophaga</a></i> is effective against pests such as the <a href="/wiki/Myzus_persicae" title="Myzus persicae">green peach aphid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Capinera_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Capinera-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several members of <a href="/wiki/Chytridiomycota" title="Chytridiomycota">Chytridiomycota</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blastocladiomycota" title="Blastocladiomycota">Blastocladiomycota</a> have been explored as agents of biological control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Li_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Li-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frog_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frog-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sparrow1960_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sparrow1960-52">[52]</a></sup> From <a href="/wiki/Chytridiomycota" title="Chytridiomycota">Chytridiomycota</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Synchytrium" title="Synchytrium">Synchytrium solstitiale</a></i> is being considered as a control agent of the <a href="/wiki/Centaurea_solstitialis" title="Centaurea solstitialis">yellow star thistle</a> (<i>Centaurea solstitialis</i>) in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gleason_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gleason-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Viruses">Viruses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biological_pest_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Viruses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Baculoviridae" title="Baculoviridae">Baculoviruses</a> are specific to individual insect host species and have been shown to be useful in biological pest control. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Lymantria_dispar_multicapsid_nuclear_polyhedrosis_virus" title="Lymantria dispar multicapsid nuclear polyhedrosis virus">Lymantria dispar multicapsid nuclear polyhedrosis virus</a> has been used to spray large areas of forest in North America where larvae of the <a href="/wiki/Lymantria_dispar_dispar" title="Lymantria dispar dispar">gypsy moth</a> are causing serious defoliation. The moth larvae are killed by the virus they have eaten and die, the disintegrating cadavers leaving virus particles on the foliage to infect other larvae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>A mammalian virus, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rabbit_haemorrhagic_disease_virus" title="Rabbit haemorrhagic disease virus">rabbit haemorrhagic disease virus</a> has been introduced to Australia and to New Zealand to attempt to control the <a href="/wiki/European_rabbit" title="European rabbit">European rabbit</a> populations there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Algae">Algae</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biological_pest_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Algae">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lagenidium_giganteum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lagenidium giganteum (page does not exist)">Lagenidium giganteum</a></i> is a water-borne mould that parasitizes the larval stage of mosquitoes. When applied to water, the motile spores avoid unsuitable host species and search out suitable mosquito larval hosts. This alga has the advantages of a dormant phase, resistant to desiccation, with slow-release characteristics over several years. Unfortunately, it is susceptible to many chemicals used in mosquito abatement programmes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plants">Plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biological_pest_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">legume</a> vine <i><a href="/wiki/Mucuna_pruriens" title="Mucuna pruriens">Mucuna pruriens</a></i> is used in the countries of <a href="/wiki/Benin" title="Benin">Benin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> as a biological control for problematic <i><a href="/wiki/Imperata_cylindrica" title="Imperata cylindrica">Imperata cylindrica</a></i> grass. <i>Mucuna pruriens</i> is said not to be invasive outside its cultivated area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tropical_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-tropical-57">[57]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Desmodium" title="Desmodium">Desmodium</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Desmodium_uncinatum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Desmodium uncinatum (page does not exist)">uncinatum</a></i> can be used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Push-pull_technology" title="Push-pull technology">push-pull farming</a> to stop the <a href="/wiki/Parasitic_plant" title="Parasitic plant">parasitic plant</a>, witchweed (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Striga_(plant)" title="Striga (plant)">Striga</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_methods">Other methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biological_pest_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Other methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combined_use_of_parasitoids_and_pathogens">Combined use of parasitoids and pathogens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biological_pest_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Combined use of parasitoids and pathogens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In cases of massive and severe infection of invasive pests, techniques of pest control are often used in combination. An example is the <a href="/wiki/Emerald_ash_borer" title="Emerald ash borer">emerald ash borer</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agrilus_planipennis" title="Agrilus planipennis">Agrilus planipennis</a></i>, an invasive <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetle</a> from <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, which has destroyed tens of millions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ash_trees" title="Ash trees">ash trees</a> in its introduced range in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. As part of the campaign against it, from 2003 American scientists and the Chinese Academy of Forestry searched for its natural enemies in the wild, leading to the discovery of several parasitoid wasps, namely <i>Tetrastichus planipennisi</i>, a gregarious larval endoparasitoid,<i><a href="/wiki/Oobius_agrili" title="Oobius agrili">Oobius agrili</a></i>, a solitary, parthenogenic egg parasitoid, and <i><a href="/wiki/Spathius_agrili" title="Spathius agrili">Spathius agrili</a></i>, a gregarious larval ectoparasitoid. These have been introduced and released into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" title="United States of America">United States of America</a> as a possible biological control of the emerald ash borer. Initial results have shown promise with <i>Tetrastichus planipennisi</i> and it is now being released along with <i><a href="/wiki/Beauveria_bassiana" title="Beauveria bassiana">Beauveria bassiana</a></i>, a fungal <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogen</a> with known insecticidal properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APHIS_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-APHIS-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bauer_et_al_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bauer_et_al-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceDaily_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceDaily-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indirect_control">Indirect control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biological_pest_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Indirect control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pests may be controlled by biological control agents that do not prey directly upon them. For example, the Australian bush fly, <i>Musca vetustissima</i>, is a major nuisance pest in Australia, but native decomposers found in Australia are not adapted to feeding on cow dung, which is where bush flies breed. Therefore, the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Dung_Beetle_Project" title="Australian Dung Beetle Project">Australian Dung Beetle Project</a> (1965–1985), led by <a href="/wiki/George_Bornemissza" title="George Bornemissza">Dr. George Bornemissza</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Scientific_and_Industrial_Research_Organisation" title="Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation">Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation</a>, released forty-nine species of <a href="/wiki/Dung_beetle" title="Dung beetle">dung beetle</a>, with the aim of reducing the amount of dung and therefore also the potential breeding sites of the fly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adbp_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-adbp-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Side-effects">Side-effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biological_pest_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Side-effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Biological control can affect <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-follett_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-follett-9">[9]</a></sup> through predation, parasitism, pathogenicity, competition, or other attacks on non-target species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-council_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-council-63">[63]</a></sup> An introduced control does not always target only the intended pest species; it can also target native species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> In Hawaii during the 1940s parasitic wasps were introduced to control a lepidopteran pest and the wasps are still found there today. This may have a negative impact on the native ecosystem, however, host range and impacts need to be studied before declaring their impact on the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wright_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-wright-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>Vertebrate animals tend to be generalist feeders, and seldom make good biological control agents; many of the classic cases of "biocontrol gone awry" involve vertebrates. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Cane_toad" title="Cane toad">cane toad</a> (<i>Bufo marinus</i>) was intentionally introduced to <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> to control the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cane_beetle" title="Cane beetle">greyback cane beetle</a> (<i>Dermolepida albohirtum</i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> and other pests of sugar cane. 102 toads were obtained from <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> and bred in captivity to increase their numbers until they were released into the sugar cane fields of the tropic north in 1935. It was later discovered that the toads could not jump very high and so were unable to eat the cane beetles which stayed up on the upper stalks of the cane plants. However the toad thrived by feeding on other insects and it soon spread very rapidly; it took over native <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibian</a> <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> and brought foreign disease to native <a href="/wiki/Toad" title="Toad">toads</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a>, dramatically reducing their populations. Also when it is threatened or handled, the cane toad releases <a href="/wiki/Poison" title="Poison">poison</a> from <a href="/wiki/Parotoid_gland" title="Parotoid gland">parotoid glands</a> on its shoulders; native Australian species such as <a href="/wiki/Goanna" title="Goanna">goannas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tiger_snake" title="Tiger snake">tiger snakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dingo" title="Dingo">dingos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_quoll" title="Northern quoll">northern quolls</a> that attempted to eat the toad were harmed or killed. However, there has been some recent evidence that native predators are adapting, both physiologically and through changing their behaviour, so in the long run, their populations may recover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Rhinocyllus_conicus" title="Rhinocyllus conicus">Rhinocyllus conicus</a></i>, a seed-feeding weevil, was introduced to North America to control exotic <a href="/wiki/Carduus_nutans" title="Carduus nutans">musk thistle</a> (<i>Carduus nutans</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Cirsium_arvense" title="Cirsium arvense">Canadian thistle</a> (<i>Cirsium arvense</i>). However the weevil also attacks native thistles, harming such species as the <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> <a href="/wiki/Cirsium_neomexicanum" title="Cirsium neomexicanum">Platte thistle</a> (<i>Cirsium neomexicanum</i>) by selecting larger plants (which reduced the gene pool), reducing seed production and ultimately threatening the species' survival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Small_Asian_mongoose" title="Small Asian mongoose">small Asian mongoose</a> (<i>Herpestus javanicus</i>) was introduced to <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> in order to control the <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rat</a> population. However it was diurnal and the rats emerged at night, and it preyed on the <a href="/wiki/Endemic_birds_of_Hawaii" title="Endemic birds of Hawaii">endemic birds of Hawaii</a>, especially their <a href="/wiki/Egg" title="Egg">eggs</a>, more often than it ate the rats, and now both rats and mongooses threaten the birds. This introduction was undertaken without understanding the consequences of such an action. No regulations existed at the time, and more careful evaluation should prevent such releases now.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sturdy and prolific <a href="/wiki/Eastern_mosquitofish" title="Eastern mosquitofish">eastern mosquitofish</a> (<i>Gambusia holbrooki</i>) is a native of the southeastern United States and was introduced around the world in the 1930s and 40s to feed on mosquito larvae and thus combat <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>. However, it has thrived at the expense of local species, causing a decline of endemic fish and frogs through competition for food resources, as well as through eating their eggs and larvae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> In Australia, the mosquitofish is the subject of discussion as to how best to control it; in 1989 it was said that "biological population control is well beyond present capabilities", and this remains the position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grower_education">Grower education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biological_pest_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Grower education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A potential obstacle to the adoption of biological pest control measures is growers sticking to the familiar use of pesticides. It has been claimed that many of the pests that are controlled today using pesticides, actually became pests because pesticide use reduced or eliminated natural predators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> A method of increasing grower adoption of biocontrol involves is letting growers learn by doing, for example showing them simple field experiments, having observations of live predation of pests, or collections of parasitised pests. In the Philippines, early season sprays against leaf folder caterpillars were common practice, but growers were asked to follow a 'rule of thumb' of not spraying against leaf folders for the first 30 days after transplanting; participation in this resulted in a reduction of insecticide use by 1/3 and a change in grower perception of insecticide use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
