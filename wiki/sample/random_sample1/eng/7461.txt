<p><b>Biostratigraphy</b> is the branch of <a href="/wiki/Stratigraphy" title="Stratigraphy">stratigraphy</a> which focuses on correlating and assigning relative ages of rock <a href="/wiki/Stratum" title="Stratum">strata</a> by using the <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> assemblages contained within them. Usually the aim is correlation, demonstrating that a particular <a href="/wiki/Horizon_(geology)" title="Horizon (geology)">horizon</a> in one geological section represents the same period of time as another horizon at some other section. The fossils are useful because <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediments</a> of the same age can look completely different because of local variations in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedimentary_depositional_environment" title="Sedimentary depositional environment">sedimentary environment</a>. For example, one section might have been made up of <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clays</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marl" title="Marl">marls</a> while another has more <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalky</a> <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestones</a>, but if the fossil species recorded are similar, the two sediments are likely to have been laid down at the same time.</p>
<p>Biostratigraphy originated in the early 19th century, where geologists recognised that the correlation of fossil assemblages between rocks of similar type but different age decreased as the difference in age increased. The method was well-established before <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> explained the mechanism behind it - <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ammonite" title="Ammonite">Ammonites</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graptolite" title="Graptolite">graptolites</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archeocyathid" title="Archeocyathid">archeocyathids</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Trilobite" title="Trilobite">trilobites</a> are <a href="/wiki/Index_fossil" title="Index fossil">index fossils</a> that are widely used in biostratigraphy. <a href="/wiki/Micropaleontology" title="Micropaleontology">Microfossils</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acritarchs" title="Acritarchs">acritarchs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chitinozoa" title="Chitinozoa">chitinozoans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conodont" title="Conodont">conodonts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dinoflagellate" title="Dinoflagellate">dinoflagellate</a> cysts, <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spores" title="Spores">spores</a> and <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">foraminiferans</a> are also frequently used. Different fossils work well for sediments of different ages; <a href="/wiki/Trilobite" title="Trilobite">trilobites</a>, for example, are particularly useful for sediments of <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian</a> age. To work well, the fossils used must be widespread geographically, so that they can occur in many different places. They must also be short lived as a species, so that the period of time during which they could be incorporated in the sediment is relatively narrow. The longer lived the species, the poorer the stratigraphic precision, so fossils that evolve rapidly, such as ammonites, are favoured over forms that evolve much more slowly, like <a href="/wiki/Nautiloid" title="Nautiloid">nautiloids</a>. Often biostratigraphic correlations are based on a fauna, not an individual species, as this allows greater precision. Further, if only one species is present in a sample, it can mean that (1) the strata were formed in the known fossil range of that organism; (2) that the fossil range of the organism was incompletely known, and the strata extend the known fossil range. For instance, the presence of the fossil <i><a href="/wiki/Treptichnus_pedum" title="Treptichnus pedum">Treptichnus pedum</a></i> was used to define the base of the Cambrian period, but it has since been found in older strata.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fossil assemblages were traditionally used to designate the duration of periods. Since a large change in fauna was required to make early stratigraphers create a new period, most of the periods we recognise today are terminated by a major extinction event or faunal turnover.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossils_as_a_basis_for_stratigraphic_subdivision">Fossils as a basis for stratigraphic subdivision</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biostratigraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Fossils as a basis for stratigraphic subdivision">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Concept_of_stage">Concept of stage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biostratigraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Concept of stage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faunal_stage" title="Faunal stage">Faunal stage</a></div>
<p>A stage is a major subdivision of strata, each systematically following the other each bearing a unique assemblage of fossils. Therefore, stages can be defined as a group of strata containing the same major fossil assemblages. French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palaeontologist" title="Palaeontologist">palaeontologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Alcide_d%27Orbigny" title="Alcide d'Orbigny">Alcide d'Orbigny</a> is credited for the invention of this concept. He named stages after geographic localities with particularly good sections of rock strata that bear the characteristic fossils on which the stages are based.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Concept_of_zone">Concept of zone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biostratigraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Concept of zone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Chronozone" title="Chronozone">Chronozone</a></div>
<p>In 1856 German palaeontologist <a href="/wiki/Albert_Oppel" title="Albert Oppel">Albert Oppel</a> introduced the concept of zone (also known as biozones or Oppel zone). A zone includes strata characterised by the overlapping range of fossils. They represent the time between the appearance of species chosen at the base of the zone and the appearance of other species chosen at the base of the next succeeding zone. Oppel's zones are named after a particular distinctive fossil species, called an index fossil. Index fossils are one of the species from the assemblage of species that characterise the zone.</p>
<p>The zone is the fundamental biostratigraphic unit. Its thickness range from a few to hundreds of metres, and its extant range from local to worldwide. Biostratigraphic units are divided into six principal kinds of biozones:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Taxon range biozone</i> represent the known stratigraphic and geographic range of occurrence of a single taxon.</li>
<li><i>Concurrent range biozone</i> include the concurrent, coincident, or overlapping part of the range of two specified taxa.</li>
<li><i>Interval biozone</i> include the strata between two specific biostratigraphic surfaces. It can be based on lowest or highest occurrences.</li>
<li><i>Lineage biozone</i> are strata containing species representing a specific segment of an evolutionary lineage.</li>
<li><i>Assemblage biozones</i> are strata that contain a unique association of three or more taxa.</li>
<li><i>Abundance biozone</i> are strata in which the abundance of a particular taxon or group of taxa is significantly greater than in the adjacent part of the section.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Index_fossils">Index fossils</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biostratigraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Index fossils">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DiplograptusCaneySprings.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1515" data-file-width="1989" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/DiplograptusCaneySprings.jpg/220px-DiplograptusCaneySprings.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/DiplograptusCaneySprings.jpg/330px-DiplograptusCaneySprings.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/DiplograptusCaneySprings.jpg/440px-DiplograptusCaneySprings.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DiplograptusCaneySprings.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Amplexograptus</i>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graptolite" title="Graptolite">graptolite</a> index fossil, from the <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> near Caney Springs, <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>To be useful in stratigraphic correlation index fossils should be:</p>
<ul>
<li>Independent of their environment</li>
<li>Geographically widespread (provincialism/isolation of species should be avoided as much as possible)</li>
<li>Rapidly evolving</li>
<li>Abundant (easy to find in the rock record)</li>
<li>Easy to preserve (Easier in low-energy, non-oxidized environment)</li>
<li>Easy to identify</li>
</ul>
