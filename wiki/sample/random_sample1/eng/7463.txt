<p>The <b>reproductive system</b> or <b>genital system</b> is a system of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sex_organs" title="Sex organs">sex organs</a> within an <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organism</a> which work together for the purpose of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_reproduction" title="Sexual reproduction">sexual reproduction</a>. Many non-living substances such as fluids, <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormones</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pheromone" title="Pheromone">pheromones</a> are also important accessories to the reproductive system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Unlike most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organ_system" title="Organ system">organ systems</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sex" title="Sex">sexes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_differentiation" title="Sexual differentiation">differentiated</a> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> often have significant differences. These differences allow for a combination of genetic material between two individuals, which allows for the possibility of greater <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genes" title="Genes">genetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Fitness_(biology)" title="Fitness (biology)">fitness</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Offspring" title="Offspring">offspring</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adam_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Adam-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Animals">Animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Sexual_reproduction#Animals" title="Sexual reproduction">Sexual reproduction § Animals</a></div>
<p>In mammals, the major organs of the reproductive system include the external <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genitalia" title="Genitalia">genitalia</a> (penis and vulva) as well as a number of internal organs including the <a href="/wiki/Gamete" title="Gamete">gamete</a> producing <a href="/wiki/Gonad" title="Gonad">gonads</a> (testicles and ovaries). Diseases of the human reproductive system are very common and widespread, particularly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_disease" title="Infectious disease">communicable</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexually_transmitted_disease" title="Sexually transmitted disease">sexually transmitted diseases</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most other <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrate</a> animals have generally similar reproductive systems consisting of gonads, ducts, and openings. However, there is a great diversity of physical <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adaptations</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">reproductive strategies</a> in every group of vertebrates.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vertebrates">Vertebrates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vertebrates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">Vertebrate</a> <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> all share key elements of their reproductive systems. They all have gamete-producing organs or <a href="/wiki/Gonad" title="Gonad">gonads</a>. In females, these gonads are then connected by <a href="/wiki/Oviduct" title="Oviduct">oviducts</a> to an opening to the outside of the body, typically the cloaca, but sometimes to a unique pore such as a <a href="/wiki/Vagina" title="Vagina">vagina</a> or <a href="/wiki/Intromittent_organ" title="Intromittent organ">intromittent organ</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Humans">Humans<span id="Human_reproductive_system"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Human_reproductive_system" title="Human reproductive system">Human reproductive system</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Human_reproductive_system" title="Human reproductive system">human reproductive system</a> usually involves <a href="/wiki/Internal_fertilization" title="Internal fertilization">internal fertilization</a> by <a href="/wiki/Sexual_intercourse" title="Sexual intercourse">sexual intercourse</a>. During this process, the male inserts his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erect_penis" title="Erect penis">erect penis</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Female" title="Female">female's</a> <a href="/wiki/Vagina" title="Vagina">vagina</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ejaculate" title="Ejaculate">ejaculates</a> <a href="/wiki/Semen" title="Semen">semen</a>, which contains <a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a>. The sperm then travels through the vagina and cervix into the <a href="/wiki/Uterus" title="Uterus">uterus</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fallopian_tube" title="Fallopian tube">fallopian tubes</a> for fertilization of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ovum" title="Ovum">ovum</a>. Upon successful <a href="/wiki/Human_fertilization" title="Human fertilization">fertilization</a> and implantation, <a href="/wiki/Gestation" title="Gestation">gestation</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetus</a> then occurs within the female's uterus for approximately nine months, this process is known as <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a> in humans. Gestation ends with <a href="/wiki/Birth" title="Birth">birth</a>, the process of birth is known as labor. Labor consists of the <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscles</a> of the uterus contracting, the <a href="/wiki/Cervical_dilation" title="Cervical dilation">cervix dilating</a>, and the baby passing out the vagina (the female genital organ). Human's babies and children are nearly helpless and require high levels of <a href="/wiki/Parental_care" title="Parental care">parental care</a> for many years. One important type of parental care is the use of the <a href="/wiki/Mammary_gland" title="Mammary gland">mammary glands</a> in the female <a href="/wiki/Breast" title="Breast">breasts</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lactation" title="Lactation">nurse</a> the baby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The female reproductive system has two functions: The first is to produce egg cells, and the second is to protect and nourish the offspring until birth. The male reproductive system has one function, and it is to produce and deposit sperm. Humans have a high level of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_differentiation" title="Sexual differentiation">sexual differentiation</a>. In addition to differences in nearly every reproductive organ, numerous differences typically occur in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secondary_sexual_characteristic" title="Secondary sexual characteristic">secondary sexual characteristics</a>.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Male">Male</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Male">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Male_reproductive_system" title="Male reproductive system">Male reproductive system</a></div>
<p>The male reproductive system is a series of organs located outside of the body and around the pelvis region of a <a href="/wiki/Male" title="Male">male</a> that contribute towards the reproduction process. The primary direct function of the male reproductive system is to provide the male sperm for fertilization of the ovum.</p>
<p>The major reproductive organs of the male can be grouped into three categories. The first category is sperm production and storage. Production takes place in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Testes" title="Testes">testes</a> which are housed in the temperature regulating <a href="/wiki/Scrotum" title="Scrotum">scrotum</a>, immature sperm then travel to the <a href="/wiki/Epididymis" title="Epididymis">epididymis</a> for development and storage. The second category are the ejaculatory fluid producing glands which include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seminal_vesicles" title="Seminal vesicles">seminal vesicles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prostate" title="Prostate">prostate</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Vas_deferens" title="Vas deferens">vas deferens</a>. The final category are those used for copulation, and deposition of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spermatozoa" title="Spermatozoa">spermatozoa</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a>) within the male, these include the <a href="/wiki/Penis" title="Penis">penis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urethra" title="Urethra">urethra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vas_deferens" title="Vas deferens">vas deferens</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cowper%27s_gland" title="Cowper's gland">Cowper's gland</a>.</p>
<p>Major secondary sexual characteristics includes: larger, more muscular stature, deepened voice, <a href="/wiki/Facial_hair" title="Facial hair">facial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Body_hair" title="Body hair">body hair</a>, broad shoulders, and development of an <a href="/wiki/Adam%27s_apple" title="Adam's apple">adam's apple</a>. An important sexual hormone of males is <a href="/wiki/Androgen" title="Androgen">androgen</a>, and particularly <a href="/wiki/Testosterone" title="Testosterone">testosterone</a>.</p>
<p>The testes release a hormone that controls the development of sperm. This hormone is also responsible for the development of physical characteristics in men such as facial hair and a deep voice.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Female">Female</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Female">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Female_reproductive_system" title="Female reproductive system">Female reproductive system</a></div>
<p>The human female reproductive system is a series of organs primarily located inside of the body and around the <a href="/wiki/Pelvis" title="Pelvis">pelvic</a> region of a <a href="/wiki/Female" title="Female">female</a> that contribute towards the <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">reproductive</a> process. The human female reproductive system contains three main parts: the <a href="/wiki/Vagina" title="Vagina">vagina</a>, which leads from the vulva, the vaginal opening, to the uterus; the <a href="/wiki/Uterus" title="Uterus">uterus</a>, which holds the developing fetus; and the <a href="/wiki/Ovary" title="Ovary">ovaries</a>, which produce the female's ova. The <a href="/wiki/Breast" title="Breast">breasts</a> are involved during the parenting stage of reproduction, but in most classifications they are not considered to be part of the female reproductive system.</p>
<p>The vagina meets the outside at the <a href="/wiki/Vulva" title="Vulva">vulva</a>, which also includes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labia_(genitalia)" title="Labia (genitalia)">labia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clitoris" title="Clitoris">clitoris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urethra" title="Urethra">urethra</a>; during intercourse this area is lubricated by mucus secreted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bartholin%27s_glands" title="Bartholin's glands">Bartholin's glands</a>. The vagina is attached to the uterus through the <a href="/wiki/Cervix" title="Cervix">cervix</a>, while the uterus is attached to the ovaries via the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fallopian_tubes" title="Fallopian tubes">fallopian tubes</a>. Each ovary contains hundreds of egg cells or ova (singular <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ovum" title="Ovum">ovum</a></i>).</p>
<p>Approximately <a href="/wiki/Menstrual_cycle" title="Menstrual cycle">every 28 days</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pituitary_gland" title="Pituitary gland">pituitary gland</a> releases a <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormone</a> that stimulates some of the ova to develop and grow. One ovum is released and it passes through the fallopian tube into the uterus. Hormones produced by the ovaries prepare the uterus to receive the ovum. The lining of the uterus, called the <a href="/wiki/Endometrium" title="Endometrium">endometrium</a>, and unfertilized ova are shed each cycle through the process of <a href="/wiki/Menstruation" title="Menstruation">menstruation</a>. If the ovum is fertilized by sperm, it attaches to the endometrium and the fetus develops.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_mammals">Other mammals<span id="Mammals"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other mammals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammalian_reproductive_system" title="Mammalian reproductive system">Mammalian reproductive system</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Joey_in_pouch.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="645" data-file-width="765" height="185" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Joey_in_pouch.jpg/220px-Joey_in_pouch.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Joey_in_pouch.jpg/330px-Joey_in_pouch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Joey_in_pouch.jpg/440px-Joey_in_pouch.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

A newborn <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joey_(marsupial)" title="Joey (marsupial)">joey</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suckles" title="Suckles">suckles</a> from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teat" title="Teat">teat</a> found within its mother's <a href="/wiki/Pouch_(marsupial)" title="Pouch (marsupial)">pouch</a></div>

<p>Most <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammal</a> reproductive systems are similar, however, there are some notable differences between the non-human mammals and humans. For instance, most male mammals have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammal_penis" title="Mammal penis">a penis</a> which is <a href="/wiki/Penile_sheath" title="Penile sheath">stored internally until erect</a>, and most have a penis bone or <a href="/wiki/Baculum" title="Baculum">baculum</a>. Additionally, males of most species do not remain continually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexually_fertile" title="Sexually fertile">sexually fertile</a> as humans do. Like humans, most groups of mammals have descended testicles found within a scrotum, however, others have descended testicles that rest on the ventral body wall, and a few groups of mammals, such as elephants, have undescended testicles found deep within their body cavities near their <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidneys</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Marsupial#Reproductive_system" title="Marsupial">reproductive system of marsupials</a> is unique in that the female has two vaginae, both of which open externally through one orifice but lead to different compartments within the uterus; males usually have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marsupial_penis" title="Marsupial penis">two-pronged penis</a> which corresponds to the females' two vaginae. Marsupials typically develop their offspring in an external <a href="/wiki/Pouch_(marsupial)" title="Pouch (marsupial)">pouch</a> containing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teat" title="Teat">teats</a> to which their newborn young (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joey_(marsupial)" title="Joey (marsupial)">joeys</a>) attach themselves for post uterine development. Also, marsupials have a unique prepenial scrotum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The 15mm (5/8 in) long newborn joey instinctively crawls and wriggles the several inches (15 cm), while clinging to fur, on the way to its mother's pouch.</p>
<p>The uterus and vagina are unique to mammals with no homologue in birds, reptiles, amphibians, or fish.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In place of the uterus the other <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrate</a> groups have an unmodified oviduct leading directly to a <a href="/wiki/Cloaca" title="Cloaca">cloaca</a>, which is a shared exit-hole for gametes, <a href="/wiki/Urine" title="Urine">urine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a>. Monotremes (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Platypus" title="Platypus">platypus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Echidna" title="Echidna">echidnas</a>), a group of egg-laying mammals, also lack a uterus and vagina, and in that respect have a reproductive system resembling that of a reptile.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Dogs">Dogs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dogs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Canine_reproduction" title="Canine reproduction">Canine reproduction</a></div>
<p>In domestic canines, sexual maturity (puberty) occurs between the ages of 6 to 12 months for both males and females, although this can be delayed until up to two years of age for some large breeds.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Horses">Horses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Horses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Equine_anatomy#Reproductive_system" title="Equine anatomy">Equine anatomy § Reproductive system</a></div>
<p>The mare's reproductive system is responsible for controlling gestation, birth, and lactation, as well as her estrous cycle and mating behavior. The stallion's reproductive system is responsible for his sexual behavior and secondary sex characteristics (such as a large crest).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Birds">Birds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Birds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bird_anatomy#Reproductive_system" title="Bird anatomy">Bird anatomy § Reproductive system</a></div>
<p>Male and female birds have a <a href="/wiki/Cloaca" title="Cloaca">cloaca</a>, an opening through which eggs, sperm, and wastes pass. Intercourse is performed by pressing the lips of the cloacae together, which is sometimes known as <a href="/wiki/Intromittent_organ" title="Intromittent organ">intromittent organ</a> which is known as a phallus that is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analogous" title="Analogous">analogous</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammal_penis" title="Mammal penis">mammals' penis</a>. The female lays <a href="/wiki/Amniote" title="Amniote">amniotic</a> eggs in which the young fetus continues to develop after it leaves the female's body. Unlike most vertebrates female birds typically have only one functional ovary and <a href="/wiki/Oviduct" title="Oviduct">oviduct</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> As a group, birds, like mammals, are noted for their high level of parental care.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reptiles">Reptiles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reptiles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reptiles#Reproduction" title="Reptiles">Reptiles § Reproduction</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reptiles" title="Reptiles">Reptiles</a> are almost all <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexually dimorphic</a>, and exhibit internal fertilization through the cloaca. Some reptiles lay <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> while others are viviparous (animals that deliver live young). Reproductive organs are found within the cloaca of reptiles. Most male reptiles have copulatory organs, which are usually retracted or inverted and stored inside the body. In turtles and crocodilians, the male has a single median penis-like organ, while male snakes and lizards each possess a pair of penis-like organs.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Amphibians">Amphibians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Amphibians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Amphibian#Reproduction" title="Amphibian">Amphibian § Reproduction</a></div>
<p>Most <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a> exhibit external fertilization of eggs, typically within the water, though some amphibians such as <a href="/wiki/Caecilian" title="Caecilian">caecilians</a> have internal fertilization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> All have paired, internal gonads, connected by ducts to the cloaca.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fish">Fish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Fish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fish_reproduction" title="Fish reproduction">Fish reproduction</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">Fish</a> exhibit a wide range of different reproductive strategies. Most fish however are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oviparous" title="Oviparous">oviparous</a> and exhibit external fertilization. In this process, females use their cloaca to release large quantities of their gametes, called <a href="/wiki/Spawn_(biology)" title="Spawn (biology)">spawn</a> into the water and one or more males release "milt", a white fluid containing many sperm over the unfertilized eggs. Other species of fish are oviparous and have internal fertilization aided by pelvic or anal <a href="/wiki/Fin" title="Fin">fins</a> that are modified into an <a href="/wiki/Intromittent_organ" title="Intromittent organ">intromittent organ</a> analogous to the human penis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> A small portion of fish species are either viviparous or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ovoviviparous" title="Ovoviviparous">ovoviviparous</a>, and are collectively known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Livebearer" title="Livebearer">livebearers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fish gonads are typically pairs of either ovaries or testes. Most fish are sexually dimorphic but some species are <a href="/wiki/Hermaphrodite" title="Hermaphrodite">hermaphroditic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unisexual" title="Unisexual">unisexual</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Invertebrates">Invertebrates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Invertebrates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_system_of_gastropods" title="Reproductive system of gastropods">Reproductive system of gastropods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_system_of_planarians" title="Reproductive system of planarians">Reproductive system of planarians</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">Invertebrates</a> have an extremely diverse array of reproductive systems, the only commonality may be that they all lay eggs. Also, aside from <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod" title="Cephalopod">cephalopods</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a>, nearly all other invertebrates are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermaphroditic" title="Hermaphroditic">hermaphroditic</a> and exhibit <a href="/wiki/External_fertilization" title="External fertilization">external fertilization</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cephalopods">Cephalopods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cephalopods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod#Reproduction_and_life_cycle" title="Cephalopod">Cephalopod § Reproduction and life cycle</a></div>
<p>All <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod" title="Cephalopod">cephalopods</a> are <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexually dimorphic</a> and reproduce by laying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>. Most cephalopods have semi-internal fertilization, in which the male places his gametes inside the female's mantle cavity or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pallial_cavity" title="Pallial cavity">pallial cavity</a> to fertilize the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ovum" title="Ovum">ova</a> found in the female's single <a href="/wiki/Ovary" title="Ovary">ovary</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Likewise, male cephalopods have only a single <a href="/wiki/Testicle" title="Testicle">testicle</a>. In the female of most cephalopods the <a href="/wiki/Nidamental_gland" title="Nidamental gland">nidamental glands</a> aid in development of the egg.</p>
<p>The "penis" in most unshelled male cephalopods (<a href="/wiki/Coleoidea" title="Coleoidea">Coleoidea</a>) is a long and muscular end of the gonoduct used to transfer spermatophores to a modified arm called a <a href="/wiki/Hectocotylus" title="Hectocotylus">hectocotylus</a>. That in turn is used to transfer the spermatophores to the female. In species where the hectocotylus is missing, the "penis" is long and able to extend beyond the mantle cavity and transfer the spermatophores directly to the female.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Insects">Insects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Insects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Insect_reproductive_system" title="Insect reproductive system">Insect reproductive system</a></div>
<p>Most <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> reproduce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oviparous" title="Oviparous">oviparously</a>, i.e. by laying <a href="/wiki/Egg" title="Egg">eggs</a>. The eggs are produced by the female in a pair of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ovaries" title="Ovaries">ovaries</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">Sperm</a>, produced by the male in one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Testis" title="Testis">testis</a> or more commonly two, is transmitted to the female during <a href="/wiki/Mating" title="Mating">mating</a> by means of external <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genitalia" title="Genitalia">genitalia</a>. The sperm is stored within the female in one or more <a href="/wiki/Spermatheca" title="Spermatheca">spermathecae</a>. At the time of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertilization" title="Fertilization">fertilization</a>, the eggs travel along <a href="/wiki/Oviduct" title="Oviduct">oviducts</a> to be fertilized by the sperm and are then expelled from the body ("laid"), in most cases via an <a href="/wiki/Ovipositor" title="Ovipositor">ovipositor</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Arachnids">Arachnids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Arachnids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arachnids#Reproduction" title="Arachnids">Arachnids § Reproduction</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Opiliones_penis" title="Opiliones penis">Opiliones penis</a></div>
<p>Arachnids may have one or two <a href="/wiki/Gonad" title="Gonad">gonads</a>, which are located in the abdomen. The genital opening is usually located on the underside of the second abdominal segment. In most species, the male transfers sperm to the female in a package, or <a href="/wiki/Spermatophore" title="Spermatophore">spermatophore</a>. Complex courtship rituals have evolved in many arachnids to ensure the safe delivery of the sperm to the female.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IZ_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-IZ-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Arachnids usually lay yolky <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>, which hatch into immatures that resemble adults. Scorpions, however, are either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ovoviviparous" title="Ovoviviparous">ovoviviparous</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viviparous" title="Viviparous">viviparous</a>, depending on species, and bear live young.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plants">Plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Plant_reproductive_morphology" title="Plant reproductive morphology">Plant reproductive morphology</a></div>
<p>Among all living organisms, <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a>, which are the reproductive structures of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flowering_plants" title="Flowering plants">angiosperms</a>, are the most varied physically and show a correspondingly great diversity in methods of reproduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barr02_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barr02-14">[14]</a></sup> Plants that are not flowering plants (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_alga" title="Green alga">green algae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">mosses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marchantiophyta" title="Marchantiophyta">liverworts</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hornworts" title="Hornworts">hornworts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">ferns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gymnosperm" title="Gymnosperm">gymnosperms</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer">conifers</a>) also have complex interplays between morphological adaptation and environmental factors in their sexual reproduction. The breeding system, or how the sperm from one plant fertilizes the ovum of another, depends on the reproductive morphology, and is the single most important determinant of the genetic structure of nonclonal plant populations. <a href="/wiki/Christian_Konrad_Sprengel" title="Christian Konrad Sprengel">Christian Konrad Sprengel</a> (1793) studied the reproduction of flowering plants and for the first time it was understood that the <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a> process involved both <a href="/wiki/Biotic_component" title="Biotic component">biotic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abiotic" title="Abiotic">abiotic</a> interactions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fungi">Fungi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Fungi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi#Reproduction" title="Fungi">Fungi § Reproduction</a></div>
<p>Fungal reproduction is complex, reflecting the differences in lifestyles and genetic makeup within this diverse kingdom of organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It is estimated that a third of all fungi reproduce using more than one method of propagation; for example, reproduction may occur in two well-differentiated stages within the <a href="/wiki/Biological_life_cycle" title="Biological life cycle">life cycle</a> of a species, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teleomorph" title="Teleomorph">teleomorph</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anamorph" title="Anamorph">anamorph</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Environmental conditions trigger genetically determined developmental states that lead to the creation of specialized structures for sexual or asexual reproduction. These structures aid reproduction by efficiently dispersing spores or spore-containing <a href="/wiki/Propagule" title="Propagule">propagules</a>.</p>
