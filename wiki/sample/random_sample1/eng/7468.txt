<p>A <b>mouse</b> (plural: <b>mice</b>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodent</a> characteristically having a pointed snout, small rounded ears, a body-length scaly tail and a high breeding rate. The best known mouse species is the common <a href="/wiki/House_mouse" title="House mouse">house mouse</a> (<i>Mus musculus</i>). It is also a popular <a href="/wiki/Pet" title="Pet">pet</a>. In some places, certain kinds of <a href="/wiki/Apodemus" title="Apodemus">field mice</a> are locally common. They are known to invade homes for food and shelter.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fancy_mouse" title="Fancy mouse">Domestic mice</a> sold as pets often differ substantially in size from the common house mouse. This is attributable both to breeding and to different conditions in the wild. The most well known strain, the white <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_mouse" title="Laboratory mouse">lab mouse</a>, has more uniform traits that are appropriate to its use in research.</p>
<p>The American <a href="/wiki/White-footed_mouse" title="White-footed mouse">white-footed mouse</a> (<i>Peromyscus leucopus</i>) and the <i>deer mouse</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Peromyscus_maniculatus" title="Peromyscus maniculatus">Peromyscus maniculatus</a></i>), as well as other common species of mouse-like rodents around the world, also sometimes live in houses. These, however, are in other <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genera</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">Cats</a>, wild <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">foxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">birds of prey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakes</a> and even certain kinds of <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> have been known to prey heavily upon mice. Nevertheless, because of its remarkable adaptability to almost any <a href="/wiki/Ecological_niche" title="Ecological niche">environment</a>, the mouse is one of the most successful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammalian" title="Mammalian">mammalian</a> genera living on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> today.</p>
<p>Mice, in certain contexts, can be considered <a href="/wiki/Vermin" title="Vermin">vermin</a> which are a major source of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crops" title="Crops">crop</a> damage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> causing structural damage and spreading <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">diseases</a> through their <a href="/wiki/Parasitism" title="Parasitism">parasites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In North America, breathing dust that has come in contact with mouse excrement has been linked to <a href="/wiki/Hantavirus" title="Hantavirus">hantavirus</a>, which may lead to <a href="/wiki/Hantavirus_pulmonary_syndrome" title="Hantavirus pulmonary syndrome">hantavirus pulmonary syndrome</a> (HPS).</p>
<p>Primarily <a href="/wiki/Nocturnality" title="Nocturnality">nocturnal</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noc1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-noc1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noc2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-noc2-4">[4]</a></sup> animals, mice compensate for their poor eyesight with a keen sense of hearing, and rely especially on their sense of smell to locate food and avoid predators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mice build intricate burrows in the wild. These burrows typically have long entrances and are equipped with escape tunnels or routes. In at least one species, the architectural design of a burrow is a genetic trait.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Day-old_mice.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="769" data-file-width="813" height="208" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Day-old_mice.JPG/220px-Day-old_mice.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Day-old_mice.JPG/330px-Day-old_mice.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Day-old_mice.JPG/440px-Day-old_mice.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Day-old_mice.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
one-day-old pups</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Breeding onset is at about 50 days of age in both females and males, although females may have their first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estrus" title="Estrus">estrus</a> at 25–40 days. Mice are polyestrous and breed year round; ovulation is spontaneous. The duration of the <a href="/wiki/Estrous_cycle" title="Estrous cycle">estrous cycle</a> is 4–5 days and estrus itself lasts about 12 hours, occurring in the evening. Vaginal smears are useful in timed matings to determine the stage of the estrous cycle. Mating is usually nocturnal and may be confirmed by the presence of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copulatory_plug" title="Copulatory plug">copulatory plug</a> in the vagina up to 24 hours post-copulation. The presence of sperm on a vaginal smear is also a reliable indicator of mating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lvma_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-lvma-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Female mice housed together tend to go into anestrus and do not cycle. If exposed to a male mouse or the pheromones of a male mouse, most of the females will go into estrus in about 72 hours. This synchronization of the estrous cycle is known as the <a href="/wiki/Whitten_effect" title="Whitten effect">Whitten effect</a>. The exposure of a recently bred mouse to the pheromones of a strange male mouse may prevent implantation (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudopregnancy" title="Pseudopregnancy">pseudopregnancy</a>), a phenomenon known as the <a href="/wiki/Bruce_effect" title="Bruce effect">Bruce effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lvma_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-lvma-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The average gestation period is 20 days. A fertile <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postpartum_estrus" title="Postpartum estrus">postpartum estrus</a> occurs 14–24 hours following <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parturition" title="Parturition">parturition</a>, and simultaneous lactation and gestation prolongs gestation 3–10 days owing to delayed implantation. The average <a href="/wiki/Litter_(animal)" title="Litter (animal)">litter</a> size is 10–12 during optimum production, but is highly strain-dependent. As a general rule, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inbred" title="Inbred">inbred</a> mice tend to have longer gestation periods and smaller litters than outbred and hybrid mice. The young are called pups and weigh 0.5–1.5 g (0.018–0.053 oz) at birth, are hairless, and have closed eyelids and ears. Cannibalism is uncommon, but females should not be disturbed during parturition and for at least 2 days postpartum. Pups are weaned at 3 weeks of age; weaning weight is 10–12 g (0.35–0.42 oz). If the postpartum estrus is not utilized, the female resumes cycling 2–5 days post-weaning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lvma_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-lvma-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Newborn male mice are distinguished from newborn females by noting the greater <a href="/wiki/Anogenital_distance" title="Anogenital distance">anogenital distance</a> and larger <a href="/wiki/Genital_papilla" title="Genital papilla">genital papilla</a> in the male. This is best accomplished by lifting the tails of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Littermate" title="Littermate">littermates</a> and comparing <a href="/wiki/Perineum" title="Perineum">perineums</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lvma_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-lvma-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Laboratory_mice">Laboratory mice</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_mouse" title="Laboratory mouse">Laboratory mouse</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PCWmice1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="97" data-file-width="200" height="97" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/04/PCWmice1.jpg" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PCWmice1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Knockout_mouse" title="Knockout mouse">Knockout mice</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Mice are common <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experimental</a> animals in laboratory research of <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a> fields primarily because they are mammals, and also because they share a high degree of <a href="/wiki/Homology_(biology)" title="Homology (biology)">homology</a> with humans. They are the most commonly used <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammalian</a> <a href="/wiki/Model_organism" title="Model organism">model organism</a>, more common than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rats" title="Rats">rats</a>. The mouse <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a> has been sequenced, and virtually all mouse <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a> have human homologs. The mouse has approximately 2.7 billion base pairs and 20 chromosomes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> They can also be manipulated in ways that are illegal with humans, although <a href="/wiki/Animal_rights" title="Animal rights">animal rights</a> activists often object. A <b><a href="/wiki/Knockout_mouse" title="Knockout mouse">knockout mouse</a></b> is a <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetically engineered</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mus_musculus" title="Mus musculus">mouse</a> that has had one or more of its genes made inoperable through a <a href="/wiki/Gene_knockout" title="Gene knockout">gene knockout</a>.</p>
<p>Reasons for common selection of mice are small size, inexpensive, widely varied diet, easily maintained, and can reproduce quickly. Several <a href="/wiki/Generation" title="Generation">generations</a> of mice can be observed in a relatively short time. Mice are generally very docile if raised from birth and given sufficient human contact. However, certain strains have been known to be quite temperamental. Mice and rats have the same organs in the same places, with the difference of size.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subgenera">Subgenera</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mouse_vermin02.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Mouse_vermin02.jpg/250px-Mouse_vermin02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Mouse_vermin02.jpg/375px-Mouse_vermin02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Mouse_vermin02.jpg/500px-Mouse_vermin02.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mouse_vermin02.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>All members of the <i>Mus</i> genus are referred to as mice. However, the term <i>mouse</i> can also be applied to species outside of this genus. <i>Mouse</i> often refers to any small muroid rodent, while <i><a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rat</a></i> refers to larger muroid rodents. Therefore, these terms are not <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">taxonomically</a> specific. For simplicity, only the rodent <a href="/wiki/Subgenus" title="Subgenus">subgenera</a> belonging to the <i>Mus</i> genus are listed here.</p>
<p>Genus <i><b>Mus</b></i> – Typical mice</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_pets">As pets</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fancy_mice.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1396" data-file-width="2516" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Fancy_mice.jpg/250px-Fancy_mice.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Fancy_mice.jpg/375px-Fancy_mice.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Fancy_mice.jpg/500px-Fancy_mice.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fancy_mice.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Pet mice</div>
</div>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fancy_mouse" title="Fancy mouse">Fancy mouse</a></div>
<p>Many people buy mice as companion pets. They can be playful, loving and can grow used to being handled. Like pet rats, pet mice should not be left unsupervised outside as they have many natural predators, including (but not limited to) birds, snakes, lizards, cats, and dogs. Male mice tend to have a stronger odor than the females. However, mice are careful groomers and as pets they never need bathing. Well looked-after mice can make ideal pets. Some common mouse care products are:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fancy_mouse#Caging" title="Fancy mouse">Cage</a> – Usually a <a href="/wiki/Hamster" title="Hamster">hamster</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gerbil" title="Gerbil">gerbil</a> <a href="/wiki/Cage_(enclosure)" title="Cage (enclosure)">cage</a>, but a variety of special mouse cages are now available. Most should have a secure door.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vanderlip2001_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vanderlip2001-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mouse_food" title="Mouse food">Food</a> – Special pelleted and seed-based food is available. Mice can generally eat most rodent food (for rats, mice, hamsters, gerbils, etc.)</li>
<li>Bedding – Usually made of hardwood pulp, such as <a href="/wiki/Aspen" title="Aspen">aspen</a>, sometimes from shredded, un<a href="/wiki/Ink" title="Ink">inked</a> <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> or recycled virgin <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wood_pulp" title="Wood pulp">wood pulp</a>. Using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corn_husk" title="Corn husk">corn husk</a> bedding is avoided because it promotes <i><a href="/wiki/Aspergillus" title="Aspergillus">Aspergillus</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungus</a>, and can grow <a href="/wiki/Mold" title="Mold">mold</a> once it gets wet, which is rough on their feet.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span></h2>
<p>In nature, mice are largely <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivores</a>, consuming any kind of fruit or grain from plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ecodome_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ecodome-10">[10]</a></sup> However, mice adapt well to urban areas and are known for eating almost all types of food scraps. In captivity, mice are commonly fed commercial pelleted mouse diet. These diets are nutritionally complete, but they still need a large variety of vegetables. Food intake is approximately 15 g (0.53 oz) per 100 g (3.5 oz) of body weight per day; water intake is approximately 15 ml (0.53 imp fl oz; 0.51 US fl oz) per 100 g of body weight per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lvma_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-lvma-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_food">As food</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Feeder_mice.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="600" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Feeder_mice.jpg/250px-Feeder_mice.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Feeder_mice.jpg/375px-Feeder_mice.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Feeder_mice.jpg/500px-Feeder_mice.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Feeder_mice.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
"Pinkie" mice for sale as <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptile</a> food</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Mice are a staple in the diet of many small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carnivores" title="Carnivores">carnivores</a>. Humans have eaten mice since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prehistoric" title="Prehistoric">prehistoric</a> times and still eat them as a delicacy throughout eastern <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Malawi" title="Malawi">Malawi</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> where they are a seasonal source of <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>. Mice are no longer routinely consumed by humans elsewhere.</p>
<p>Prescribed cures in Ancient Egypt included mice as medicine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In Ancient Egypt, when infants were ill, mice were eaten as treatment by their mothers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hart2001_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hart2001-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PressPress2002_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-PressPress2002-14">[14]</a></sup> It was believed that mouse eating by the mother would help heal the baby who was ill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shuter2003_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shuter2003-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FontanelD.27Harcourt1997_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FontanelD.27Harcourt1997-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Col.C3.B3nCol.C3.B3n1999_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Col.C3.B3nCol.C3.B3n1999-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BlumBlum1970_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-BlumBlum1970-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In various countries mice are used as food<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> for pets such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snakes" title="Snakes">snakes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lizards" title="Lizards">lizards</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frogs" title="Frogs">frogs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tarantulas" title="Tarantulas">tarantulas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">birds of prey</a>, and many pet stores carry mice for this purpose.</p>
<p>Common terms used to refer to different ages/sizes of mice when sold for pet food are "pinkies", "fuzzies", "crawlers", "hoppers", and "adults".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Pinkies are newborn mice that have not yet grown fur; fuzzies have some fur but are not very mobile; hoppers have a full coat of hair and are fully mobile but are smaller than adult mice. Mice without fur are easier for the animal to consume; however, mice with fur may be more convincing as animal feed. These terms are also used to refer to the various growth stages of <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Fancy_rat" title="Fancy rat">Fancy rat</a>).</p>
