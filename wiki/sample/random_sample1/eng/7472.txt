<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Living wage</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Basic_income" title="Basic income">Basic income</a>.</div>
<p>In public policy, a <b>living wage</b> is the minimum income necessary for a worker to meet their basic needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20141027-LA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20141027-LA-1">[1]</a></sup> This is not necessarily the same as subsistence, which refers to a biological minimum, though the two terms are commonly confused. These needs include shelter (housing) and other incidentals such as clothing and <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutrition</a>. In some nations such as the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, this standard generally means that a person working forty hours a week, with no additional income, should be able to afford the basics for quality of life, such as, <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>, <a href="/wiki/Housing" title="Housing">shelter</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utilities" title="Utilities">utilities</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">health care</a>, minimal <a href="/wiki/Recreation" title="Recreation">recreation</a>, one course a year to upgrade their education, and childcare. However, in many cases <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>, saving for <a href="/wiki/Retirement" title="Retirement">retirement</a>, and less commonly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal_fees" title="Legal fees">legal fees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a>, or taking care of a sick or elderly family member are not included. It also does not allow for debt repayment of any kind. In addition to this definition, living wage activists further define a living wage as the wage equivalent to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a> for a family of four. This is two adults working full-time with one child age nine and another of age four.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The living wage differs from the <a href="/wiki/Minimum_wage" title="Minimum wage">minimum wage</a> in that the latter is set by law and can fail to meet the requirements to have a basic quality of life and leaves the family to rely on government programs for additional income.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20141027-LA_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20141027-LA-1">[1]</a></sup> It differs somewhat from <a href="/wiki/Basic_needs" title="Basic needs">basic needs</a> in that the basic needs model usually measures a minimum level of consumption, without regard for the source of the income.</p>
<p>A living wage is defined as the wage that can meet the basic needs to maintain a safe, decent standard of living within the community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The particular amount that must be earned per hour to meet these needs varies depending on location. In the 1990s the first living wage campaigns were launched by community initiatives in the US addressing increasing poverty faced by workers and their families. They argued that employee, employer, and the community win with a living wage. Employees would be more willing to work, helping the employer reduce <a href="/wiki/Turnover_(employment)" title="Turnover (employment)">worker turnover</a>, and it would help the community when the citizens have enough to have a decent life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Poverty_threshold" title="Poverty threshold">poverty threshold</a> is the income necessary for a household to be able to consume a low cost, nutritious diet and purchase non-food necessities in a given country. Poverty lines and living wages are measured differently. Poverty lines are measured by <a href="/wiki/Household" title="Household">household units</a> and living wage is based on individual workers.</p>
<p>A related concept is that of a <a href="/wiki/Family_wage" title="Family wage">family wage</a> – one sufficient to not only support oneself, but also to raise a family.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_wage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Activists argue that a wage is more than just compensation for labor. It is a means of securing a living and it leads to public policies that address both the level of the wage and its decency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clary.2C_B._2009_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clary.2C_B._2009-4">[4]</a></sup> In his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wealth_of_Nations" title="Wealth of Nations">Wealth of Nations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a> recognized that rising real wages lead to the "improvement in the circumstances of the lower ranks of people" and are therefore an advantage to society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup> Growth and a system of liberty were the means by which the laboring poor were able to secure high wages and an acceptable standard of living. Rising real wages are secured by growth through increasing productivity against stable price levels, i.e. prices not affected by inflation. A system of liberty, secured through political institutions whereupon even the "lower ranks of people" could secure the opportunity for higher wages and an acceptable standard of living.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Servants, labourers and workmen of different kinds, make up the far greater part of every great political society. But what improves the circumstances of the greater part can never be regarded as an inconvenience to the whole. No society can surely be ﬂourishing and happy, of which the far greater part of the members are poor and miserable. It is but equity, besides, that they who feed, clothe, and lodge the whole body of the people, should have such a share of the produce of their own labour as to be themselves tolerably well fed, clothed and lodged.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Adam Smith, <i>Wealth of Nations</i>, I .viii.36<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>According to living wage advocates Smith advocated that labor should receive an equitable share of what labor produces; According to Smith, this equitable share amounts to more than subsistence. Smith equated the interests of labor and the interests of land with overarching societal interests. He reasoned that as wages and rents rise, as a result of higher productivity, societal growth will occur thus increasing the quality of life for the greater part of its members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clary.2C_B._2009_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Clary.2C_B._2009-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Activists argue that the greater good for society is achieved through justice. They argue that government should in turn attempt to align the interests of those pursuing profits with the interests of the labor in order to produce societal advantages for the majority of society. Smith argued that higher productivity and overall growth led to higher wages that in turn led to greater benefits for society. Based on his writings, one can infer that Smith would support a living wage commensurate with the overall growth of the economy. This, in turn, would lead to more happiness and joy for people, while helping to keep families and people out of poverty. Political institutions can create a system of liberty for individuals to ensure opportunity for higher wages through higher production and thus stable growth for society.</p>

<p>In 1891, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_XIII" title="Pope Leo XIII">Pope Leo XIII</a> issued a papal bull entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Rerum_novarum" title="Rerum novarum">Rerum novarum</a></i>, which is considered the Catholic Church's first expression of a view supportive of a living wage. The Church recognized that wages should be sufficient to <a href="/wiki/Family_wage" title="Family wage">support a family</a>. This position has been widely supported by the church since that time, and has been reaffirmed by the papacy on multiple occasions, such as by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XI" title="Pope Pius XI">Pope Pius XI</a> in 1931 <i><a href="/wiki/Quadragesimo_anno" title="Quadragesimo anno">Quadragesimo anno</a></i> and again in 1961, by <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XXIII" title="Pope John XXIII">Pope John XXIII</a> writing in the encyclical <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mater_et_magistra" title="Mater et magistra">Mater et magistra</a>.</i> More recently, <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a> wrote, "Hence in every case a just wage is the concrete means of verifying the whole socioeconomic system and, in any case, of checking that it is functioning justly."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Today, one of major supporting groups for the Living Wage is the Universal Living Wage group. The group currently has over 1,500 followers and continues to grow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013 the University of Manchester published a report suggesting that the competition amongst buying organizations has implications for poor wages in countries such as Bangladesh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementations">Implementations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_wage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Implementations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_wage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, the 1907 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvester_Judgment" title="Harvester Judgment">Harvester Judgment</a> ruled that an employer was obliged to pay his employees a wage that guaranteed them a standard of living which was reasonable for "a human being in a civilised community" to live in "frugal comfort estimated by current... standards,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> regardless of the employer's capacity to pay. Justice Higgins established a wage of 7/- (<a href="/wiki/Coins_of_the_Australian_pound#Australian_.C2.A3sd" title="Coins of the Australian pound">7 shillings</a>) per day or 42/- per week as a 'fair and reasonable' minimum wage for unskilled workers. The judgment was later overturned but remains influential. From the Harvester Judgement arose the Australian industrial concept of the "basic wage". For most skilled workers, in addition to the basic wage they received a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Margin_(Australian_industrial_relations)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Margin (Australian industrial relations) (page does not exist)">margin</a> on top of the basic wage, in proportion to a court or commission's judgement of a group of worker's skill levels. In 1913, to compensate for the rising cost of living, the basic wage was increased to 8/- per day, the first increase since the minimum was set. The first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retail_Price_Index" title="Retail Price Index">Retail Price Index</a> in Australia was published late in 1912, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=A_Series_Index&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="A Series Index (page does not exist)">A Series Index</a>. From 1934, the basic wage was <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Indexation_(Australian_industrial_relations)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Indexation (Australian industrial relations) (page does not exist)">indexed</a> against the <a href="/wiki/C_Series_Index" title="C Series Index">C Series Index</a> of household prices. The concept of a basic wage was repeatedly challenged by employer groups through the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Basic_wage_cases&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Basic wage cases (page does not exist)">Basic wage cases</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Metal_Trades_Award_cases&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Metal Trades Award cases (page does not exist)">Metal Trades Award cases</a> where the employers argued that the basic wage and margin ought to be replaced by a "total wage". The basic wage system remained in place in Australia until 1967. It was also adopted by some state tribunals and was in use in some states during the 1980s.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_wage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bangladesh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Bangladesh salaries are among the lowest in the world. During 2012 wages hovered around US$38 per month depending on the exchange rate. Studies by Professor Doug Miller during 2010 to 2012, has highlighted the evolving global trade practices in <i>Towards Sustainable Labour Costing in UK Fashion Retail</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This white paper published in 2013 by University of Manchester, appear to suggest that the competition among buying organizations have implications to poor wages in countries such as Bangladesh. It has laid down a road map to achieve sustainable wages.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_wage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Municipal regulation of wage levels began in some towns in the United Kingdom in 1524. National <a href="/wiki/Minimum_wage_law" title="Minimum wage law">minimum wage law</a> began with the <a href="/wiki/Trade_Boards_Act_1909" title="Trade Boards Act 1909">Trade Boards Act 1909</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Wages_Councils_Act_1945" title="Wages Councils Act 1945">Wages Councils Act 1945</a> set minimum wage standards in many sectors of the economy. Wages Councils were abolished in 1993 and subsequently replaced with a single statutory national <a href="/wiki/Minimum_wage" title="Minimum wage">minimum wage</a> by the <a href="/wiki/National_Minimum_Wage_Act_1998" title="National Minimum Wage Act 1998">National Minimum Wage Act 1998</a>, which is still in force. The rates are reviewed each year by the country's <a href="/wiki/Low_Pay_Commission" title="Low Pay Commission">Low Pay Commission</a>. From 1 April 2016 the minimum wage has been paid as a mandatory <a href="/wiki/National_Living_Wage" title="National Living Wage">National Living Wage</a> for workers over 25. It is being phased in between 2016 and 2020 and is set at a significantly higher level than previous minimum wage rates. By 2020 it is expected to have risen to at least £9 per hour and represent a full-time annual pay equivalent to 60% of the median UK earnings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian-Live_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-Live-12">[12]</a></sup> The National Living Wage is nevertheless lower than the value of the Living Wage calculated by the <a href="/wiki/Living_Wage_Foundation" title="Living Wage Foundation">Living Wage Foundation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Some organisations voluntarily pay a living wage to their staff, at a level somewhat higher than the statutory level.</p>
<p>From September 2014 all <a href="/wiki/NHS_Wales" title="NHS Wales">NHS Wales</a> staff have been paid a minimum of the "living wage" recommended by the Living Wage Commission. About 2,400 employees received an initial salary increase of up to £470 above the UK-wide <a href="/wiki/Agenda_for_Change" title="Agenda for Change">Agenda for Change</a> rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mark_Smith_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mark_Smith-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_wage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the state of <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> and several municipalities and local governments have enacted ordinances which set a minimum wage higher than the federal minimum that requires all jobs to meet the living wage for that region. This usually works out to be $3 to $7 above the federal minimum wage. However, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Francisco,_California" title="San Francisco, California">San Francisco, California</a> and <a href="/wiki/Santa_Fe,_New_Mexico" title="Santa Fe, New Mexico">Santa Fe, New Mexico</a> have notably passed very wide-reaching living wage ordinances.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> U.S. cities with living wage laws include <a href="/wiki/Santa_Fe,_New_Mexico" title="Santa Fe, New Mexico">Santa Fe</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albuquerque" title="Albuquerque">Albuquerque</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>; <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>; and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_D.C." title="Washington D.C.">Washington D.C.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicago,_Illinois" title="Chicago, Illinois">Chicago, Illinois</a> also passed a <a href="/wiki/2006_Chicago_Big_Box_Ordinance" title="2006 Chicago Big Box Ordinance">living wage ordinance</a> in 2006, but it was vetoed by Mayor <a href="/wiki/Richard_M._Daley" title="Richard M. Daley">Richard M. Daley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Living wage laws typically cover only businesses that receive state assistance or have contracts with the government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>This effort began in 1994 when an alliance between a labor union and religious leaders in <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a> launched a successful campaign requiring city service contractors to pay a living wage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Subsequent to this effort, community advocates have won similar ordinances in cities such as <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, San Francisco, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis</a>. In 2007, there were at least 140 living wage ordinances in cities throughout the United States and more than 100 living wage campaigns underway in cities, counties, states, and college campuses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In 2014, Wisconsin Service Employees International Union teamed up with public officials against legislation to eliminate local living wages. According to U.S. Department of Labor data, Wisconsin Jobs Now - a non-profit organization fighting inequality through higher wages - has received at least $2.5 million from SEIU organizations from 2011 to 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although these ordinances are recent, a number of studies have attempted to measure the impact of these policies on wages and employment. Researchers have had difficulty measuring the impact of these policies because it is difficult to isolate a control group for comparison. A notable study defined the control group as the subset of cities that attempted to pass a living wage law but were unsuccessful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> This comparison indicates that living wages raise the average wage level in cities, however, it reduces the likelihood of employment for individuals in the bottom percentile of wage distribution.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_wage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Research shows that minimum wage laws and living wage legislation impact poverty differently: evidence demonstrates that living wage legislation reduces poverty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JoLR_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-JoLR-22">[22]</a></sup> The parties impacted by minimum wage laws and living wage laws differ as living wage legislation generally applies to a more limited sector of the population. It is estimated that workers who qualify for the living wage legislation are currently between 1-2% of the bottom quartile of wage distribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JoLR_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-JoLR-22">[22]</a></sup> One must consider that the impact of living wage laws depends heavily on the degree to which these ordinances are enforced.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Neumark and Adams, in their paper, "Do living wage ordinances reduce urban poverty?", state, "There is evidence that living wage ordinances modestly reduce the poverty rates in locations in which these ordinances are enacted. However, there is no evidence that state minimum wage laws do so."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Living_wage_estimates">Living wage estimates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_wage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Living wage estimates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 2003, there are 122 living wage ordinances in American cities and an additional 75 under discussion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anker.2C_R._2006_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anker.2C_R._2006-24">[24]</a></sup> Article 23 of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="United Nations Universal Declaration of Human Rights">United Nations Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a> states that "Everyone who works has the right to just and favourable remuneration ensuring for himself and for his family an existence worthy of human dignity."</p>
<p>In addition to legislative acts, many corporations have adopted voluntary codes of conduct. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sullivan_Principles" title="Sullivan Principles">Sullivan Principles</a> in South Africa are an example of a voluntary code of conduct which state that firms should compensate workers to at least cover their basic needs.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Living_wage_movements">Living wage movements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_wage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Living wage movements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Living_Wage_Foundation">Living Wage Foundation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_wage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Living Wage Foundation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Living_Wage_Foundation" title="Living Wage Foundation">Living Wage Foundation</a></div>
<p>The Living Wage Campaign in the United Kingdom originated in London, where it was launched in 2001 by members of the community organisation London Citizens (now <a href="/wiki/Citizens_UK" title="Citizens UK">Citizens UK</a>). It engaged in a series of Living Wage campaigns and in 2005 the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Authority" title="Greater London Authority">Greater London Authority</a> established the Living Wage Unit to calculate the London Living Wage, although the authority had no power to enforce it. The London Living Wage was developed in 2008 when Trust for London awarded a grant of over £1 million for campaigning, research and an employer accreditation scheme. The Living Wage campaign subsequently grew into a national movement with local campaigns across the UK. The <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Rowntree_Foundation" title="Joseph Rowntree Foundation">Joseph Rowntree Foundation</a> funded the <a href="/wiki/Centre_for_Research_in_Social_Policy" title="Centre for Research in Social Policy">Centre for Research in Social Policy</a> (CRSP) at <a href="/wiki/Loughborough_University" title="Loughborough University">Loughborough University</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> to calculate a UK-wide Minimum Income Standard (MIS) figure, an average across the whole of the UK independent of the higher living costs in London.</p>
<p>In 2011 the CRSP used the MIS as the basis for developing a standard model for setting the UK Living Wage outside of London. <a href="/wiki/Citizens_UK" title="Citizens UK">Citizens UK</a>, a nationwide community organising institution developed out of London Citizens, launched the <a href="/wiki/Living_Wage_Foundation" title="Living Wage Foundation">Living Wage Foundation</a> and Living Wage Employer mark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Since 2011, the Living Wage Foundation has accredited over 1,800 employers that pay its proposed living wage. The living wage in London is calculated by GLA Economics and the CRSP calculates the out-of-London Living Wage. Their recommended rates for 2015 are £9.40 for London and £8.25 for the rest of the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lwf-calc_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-lwf-calc-27">[27]</a></sup> These rates are updated annually in November. In January 2016 the Living Wage Foundation set up a new Living Wage Commission to oversee the calculation of the Living Wage rates in the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, research into the costs and benefits of a living wage in London was funded by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_Parochial_Foundation" title="City Parochial Foundation">Trust for London</a> and carried out by <a href="/wiki/Queen_Mary_University_of_London" title="Queen Mary University of London">Queen Mary University of London</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Further research was published in 2014 in a number of reports on the potential impact of raising the UK's statutory national minimum wage to the same level as the Living Wage Foundation's living wage recommendation. This included two reports funded by the Trust for London<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> and carried out by the <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_Public_Policy_Research" title="Institute for Public Policy Research">Institute for Public Policy Research</a> (IPPR) and <a href="/wiki/Resolution_Foundation" title="Resolution Foundation">Resolution Foundation</a>: "Beyond the Bottom Line"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and "What Price a Living Wage?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Additionally, Landman Economics published "The Economic Impact of Extending the Living Wage to all Employees in the UK".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2014 report by the Living Wage Commission, chaired by Doctor <a href="/wiki/John_Sentamu" title="John Sentamu">John Sentamu</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_York" title="Archbishop of York">Archbishop of York</a>, recommended that the UK government should pay its own workers a "living wage", but that it should be voluntary for the private sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Data published in late 2014 by <a href="/wiki/New_Policy_Institute" title="New Policy Institute">New Policy Institute</a> and Trust for London found 20% of employees in London were paid below the Living Wage Foundation's recommended living wage between 2011 and 2013. The proportion of residents paid less than this rate was highest in <a href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Newham" title="London Borough of Newham">Newham</a> (37%) and <a href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Brent" title="London Borough of Brent">Brent</a> (32%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Research by the <a href="/wiki/Office_for_National_Statistics" title="Office for National Statistics">Office for National Statistics</a> in 2014 indicated that at that time the proportion of jobs outside London paying less than the living wage was 23%. The equivalent figure within London was 19%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ed_Miliband" title="Ed Miliband">Ed Miliband</a>, the leader of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labour_Party_UK" title="Labour Party UK">Labour Party</a> in opposition from 2010 until 2015, supported a living wage<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Living_Wage_Campaign_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Living_Wage_Campaign-37">[37]</a></sup> and proposed tax breaks for employers who adopted it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_News_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_News-38">[38]</a></sup> The Labour Party has implemented a living wage in some <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Local government in the United Kingdom">local councils</a> which it controls, such as in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-39">[39]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-40">[40]</a></sup> councils. The <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales" title="Green Party of England and Wales">Green Party</a> also supports the introduction of a living wage, believing that the national minimum wage should be 60% of net national average earnings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenparty.org.uk_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-greenparty.org.uk-41">[41]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn Féin">Sinn Féin</a> also supports the introduction of a living wage for <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. Other supporters include <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> newspaper columnist <a href="/wiki/Polly_Toynbee" title="Polly Toynbee">Polly Toynbee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Church_Action_on_Poverty" title="Church Action on Poverty">Church Action on Poverty</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Living_wage_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Living_wage-42">[42]</a></sup> the Scottish Low Pay Unit, and Bloomsbury Fightback!.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-test-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Living_Wage_Movement_Aotearoa_New_Zealand">Living Wage Movement Aotearoa New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_wage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Living Wage Movement Aotearoa New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> a new social movement, Living Wage Movement Aotearoa New Zealand, was formed in April 2013. It emerged from a loose network that launched a Living Wage Campaign in May 2012. In 2015 there were over 50 faith, union and community member organisations.</p>
<p>In February 2013, independent research by the Family Centre Social Policy Research Unit identified the New Zealand Living Wage as $18.40 per hour. This was increased in 2014 to $18.80 per hour and in 2015 to $19.25 per hour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> On July 1, 2016, the Living Wage rose to be 19.80 per hour,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> while minimum wage rose to 15.25 in April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 1, 2014 the first accredited NZ Living Wage Employers were announced. The twenty businesses for 2014-15 included food manufacturing, social service agencies, community organisations and a café. This number has now increased to 30 businesses.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_York_City_Living_Wage">New York City Living Wage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_wage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: New York City Living Wage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The proposed law will inform tax-payers of where their investment dollars go and will hold developers to more stringent employment standards. The proposed act will require developers who receive substantial tax-payer funded subsidies to pay employees a minimum living wage. The law is designed to raise quality of life and stimulate local economy. Specifically the proposed act will guarantee that workers in large developmental projects will receive a wage of at least $10.00 an hour. The living wage will get indexed so that it keeps up with cost of living increases. Furthermore, the act will require that employees who do not receive health insurance from their employer will receive an additional $1.50 an hour to subsidize their healthcare expenses. Workers employed at a subsidized development will also be entitled to the living wage guarantee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Folbre_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Folbre-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many city officials have opposed living wage requirements because they believe that they restrict business climate thus making cities less appealing to potential industries. Logistically cities must hire employees to administer the ordinance. Conversely advocates for the legislation have acknowledged that when wages aren't sufficient, low-wage workers are often forced to rely on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_assistance" title="Public assistance">public assistance</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Supplemental_Nutrition_Assistance_Program" title="Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program">food stamps</a> or <a href="/wiki/Medicaid" title="Medicaid">Medicaid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Folbre_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-Folbre-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>James Parrott of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fiscal_Policy_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fiscal Policy Institute (page does not exist)">Fiscal Policy Institute</a> testified during a May 2011 New York City Council meeting that real wages for low-wage workers in the city have declined substantially over the last 20 years, despite dramatic increases in average education levels. A <a class="external text" href="http://www.fiscalpolicy.org/TopTenReasonsALivingWageMakesSenseForNYC_20110505.pdf" rel="nofollow">report</a> by the Fiscal Policy Institute indicated that business tax subsidies have grown two and a half times faster than overall New York City tax collections and asks why these public resources are invested in poverty-level jobs. Mr. Parrott testified that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Income_inequality" title="Income inequality">income inequality</a> in New York City exceeds that of other large cities, with the highest-earning 1 percent receiving 44 percent of all income.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Harvard_Living_Wage_Campaign">Harvard Living Wage Campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_wage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Harvard Living Wage Campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> students began organizing a campaign to combat the issue of low living wages for Harvard workers beginning in 1998. After failed attempts to get a meeting with Harvard president <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neil_Rudenstine" title="Neil Rudenstine">Neil Rudenstine</a>, The Living Wage Campaign began to take action. As the movement gained momentum, The Living Wage Campaign held rallies with the support of students, alumni, faculty, staff, community members and organizations. Most importantly, the rallies gained the support of the Harvard workers, strengthening the campaign's demands for a higher wage. After various measures trying to provoke change among the administration, the movement took its most drastic measure. Approximately fifty students occupied the office of the president and university administrators in 2001 for a three-week sit-in. While students were in the office of the president, supporters would sleep outside the building to show solidarity. At the end of the sit-in, dining hall workers were able to agree on a contract to raise the pay of workers. After the sit-in, The Living Wage Campaign sparked unions, contract and service workers to begin negotiating for fair wages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Miami_University_Living_Wage_Campaign">Miami University Living Wage Campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_wage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Miami University Living Wage Campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Miami_University" title="Miami University">Miami University</a> Living Wage Campaign began after it became known that Miami University wage was 18-19% below the market value. In 2003 the members of the Miami University Fair Labor Coalition began marching for university staff wages. After negotiations failed between the university and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=American_Federation_of_State_and_County_Municipal_Employees&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="American Federation of State and County Municipal Employees (page does not exist)">American Federation of State and County Municipal Employees</a> (AFSCME), workers went on strike. For two weeks workers protested and students created a tent city as a way of showing support for the strikers. Eventually more students, faculty and community members came out to show support. Even the union president at the time also went on a hunger strike as another means of protesting wages. In late 2003 the union was able to make an agreement with the university for gradual raises totaling about 10.25%. There was still an ongoing push for Miami University to adopt a living wage policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Johns_Hopkins_University">Johns Hopkins University</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_wage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Johns Hopkins University">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Student Labor Action Committee (SLAC) of <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Johns Hopkins University">Johns Hopkins University</a> took action by conducting a sit-in until the administration listen to their demands. In 1999, after a petition with thousands of signatures, Johns Hopkins University president, William R. Brody raised the hourly wage (to only $7.75) but did not include healthcare benefits nor would the wage adjust for inflation. The sit-in began in early 2000 to meet the demands of students for the university to adopt a living wage. A few weeks later, a settlement was made with the administration. SLAC now just ensures that the living wage policy is implemented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Swarthmore_Living_Wage_and_Democracy_Campaign">Swarthmore Living Wage and Democracy Campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_wage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Swarthmore Living Wage and Democracy Campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Starting in 2000, the Living Wage and Democracy Campaign of <a href="/wiki/Swarthmore_College" title="Swarthmore College">Swarthmore College</a> began as small meetings between students and staff to voice concerns about their wages. Over the next two years the Living Wage and Democracy Campaign voiced concerns to the university administration. As a response in 2002, the wage was increased from $6.66 to $9 an hour. While the campaigners were pleased with this first result, they believed the college still had a long way to go. The college president, Al Bloom created the Ad Hoc Committee to help learn what the living wage was and released a committee report. In the report suggested an hourly wage, childcare benefit, health coverage for employees and families.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia_Floor_Wage">Asia Floor Wage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_wage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Asia Floor Wage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Launched in 2009, Asia Floor Wage is a loose coalition of labour and other groups seeking to implement a Living Wage throughout Asia, with a particular focus on <a href="/wiki/Textile_manufacturing" title="Textile manufacturing">textile manufacturing</a>. There are member associations in Bangladesh, Cambodia, Hong Kong S.A.R., India, Indonesia, Malaysia, Pakistan, the Philippines, Sri Lanka, Thailand and Turkey as well as supporters in Europe and North America. The campaign targets multinational employers who do not pay their developing world workers a living wage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="University_of_Virginia_Living_Wage_Campaign">University of Virginia Living Wage Campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_wage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: University of Virginia Living Wage Campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Living Wage Campaign at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Virginia" title="University of Virginia">University of Virginia</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charlottesville" title="Charlottesville">Charlottesville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, composed of University students, faculty, staff, and community members, began in 1995 during the administration of University President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Casteen" title="John Casteen">John Casteen</a> and continues under the administration of President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teresa_Sullivan" title="Teresa Sullivan">Teresa Sullivan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LWCUVA_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-LWCUVA-53">[53]</a></sup> The campaign has demanded that the university raise wages to meet basic standards of cost-of-living in the Charlottesville area, as calculated by the nonpartisan <a href="/wiki/Economic_Policy_Institute" title="Economic Policy Institute">Economic Policy Institute</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LWCUVA_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-LWCUVA-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2000, the campaign succeeded in persuading university administrators to raise the wage floor from $6.10 to $8.19; however, this wage only applied to direct employees, not contracted workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LWCUVA_53-2"><a href="#cite_note-LWCUVA-53">[53]</a></sup> In the spring of 2006, the campaign garnered national media attention when 17 students staged a sit-in in the university president's office in Madison Hall. A professor was arrested on the first day of the protest. The 17 students were arrested after 4 days of protest and later acquitted at trial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beginning in 2010, the campaign has staged a series of rallies and other events to draw attention to the necessity of the living wage for UVA employees. They have also met with members of the administration numerous times, including with the president.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LWCUVA_53-3"><a href="#cite_note-LWCUVA-53">[53]</a></sup> In making the argument for a living wage, the campaign has claimed that continuing to pay low wages is inconsistent with the University's values of the "Community of Trust."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LWCUVA_53-4"><a href="#cite_note-LWCUVA-53">[53]</a></sup> They have also noted that University President Sullivan's 2011 co-written textbook, <i>The Social Organization of Work</i>, states that, "Being paid a living wage for one's work is a necessary condition for self-actualization."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> After rallies and meetings in the spring of 2011, President Sullivan posted a "Commitment to Lowest-Paid Employees" on the University President's website including a letter addressed to the Campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 8, 2012, the Campaign released a series of demands to University administrators calling for a living wage policy at the University. These demands included a requirement that the University "explicitly address" the issue by Feb. 17. Although University President Teresa Sullivan did respond to the demands in a mass email sent to the University community shortly before the end of the day on February 17, the Campaign criticized her response as "intentionally misleading" and vowed to take action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 18, the campaign announced that 12 students would begin a hunger strike to publicize the plight of low-paid workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_wage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Setting the minimum wage at a living wage has been criticized for possibly destroying jobs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another issue that has emerged is that living wages may be a less effective anti-poverty tool than other measures. Since living wage ordinances attempt to address the issue of a living wage, defined by its proponents as a <i>family</i> wage, rather than as an <i>individual</i> wage, many of the beneficiaries may already be in families that make substantially more than that necessary to provide an adequate standard of living. According to a survey of labor economists by the Employment Policies Institute in 2000, only 31% viewed living wages as a very or somewhat effective anti-poverty tool, while 98% viewed policies like the US earned income tax credit and general welfare grants in a similar vein.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> On the other hand, according to Zagros Madjd-Sadjadi, an economist with the State of California's Division of Labor Statistics and Research, the living wage may be seen by the public as preferable to other methods because it reinforces the "work ethic and ensures that there is something of value produced, unlike welfare, that is often believed to be a pure cash "gift" from the public coffers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
