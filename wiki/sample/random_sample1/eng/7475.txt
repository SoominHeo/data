<p><b>Schadenfreude</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ʃ/ 'sh' in 'shy'">ʃ</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ən/ 'on' in 'button'">ən</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɔɪ/ 'oy' in 'boy'">ɔɪ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ᵊ/ optional 'ə' in 'jewelry'">ᵊ</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <small>German:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[ˈʃaːdn̩ˌfʁɔʏ̯də]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata"> (<a href="/wiki/File:Schadenfreude-native-german.flac" title="File:Schadenfreude-native-german.flac"><img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a> <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e6/Schadenfreude-native-german.flac" title="Schadenfreude-native-german.flac">listen</a>)</small>; <a href="/wiki/Literal_translation" title="Literal translation"><abbr title="literal translation">lit.</abbr></a> 'harm-joy') is pleasure derived from the misfortune of others. Borrowed from German into English and several other languages, it is a feeling of <a href="/wiki/Joy" title="Joy">joy</a> that comes from seeing or hearing about another person's troubles or failures. It is an expression of pleasure or self-satisfaction at another's failure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_analysis">Linguistic analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schadenfreude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Linguistic analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spelling_and_etymology">Spelling and etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schadenfreude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Spelling and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Though normally not capitalized in English, the term <i>schadenfreude</i> is sometimes capitalized to mimic German-language convention, as German nouns are always capitalized.</p>
<p>The corresponding German adjective is <i>schadenfroh</i>. The word derives from <i>Schaden</i> (damage, harm) and <i>Freude</i> (joy). <i>Schaden</i> derives from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_High_German" title="Middle High German">Middle High German</a> <i>schade</i>, from the <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> <i>scado</i>, and is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cognate_(etymology)" title="Cognate (etymology)">cognate</a> with English <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/scathe" title="wikt:scathe">scathe</a></i>. <i>Freude</i> comes from the Middle High German <i>freude</i>, from the Old High German <i>frewida</i>, and is a cognate with the (usually <a href="/wiki/Archaism" title="Archaism">archaic</a>) English word <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/frith" title="wikt:frith">frith</a></i>. Schadenfreude can be enjoyed in private or it can be celebrated openly.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English_equivalents">English equivalents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schadenfreude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: English equivalents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Little-used <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> words synonymous with <i>schadenfreude</i> derive from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> word, <i>epichairekakia</i> (ἐπιχαιρεκακία, first attested in <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nathan_Bailey" title="Nathan Bailey">Nathan Bailey</a>'s 18th-century <i>Universal Etymological English Dictionary</i>, for example, contains an entry for <i>epicharikaky</i> that gives its <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a> as a compound of ἐπί <i>epi</i> (upon), χαρά <i>chara</i> (joy), and κακόν <i>kakon</i> (evil).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A popular modern collection of rare words, however, gives its spelling as <i>epicaricacy</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>An English expression with a similar meaning is <i>Roman holiday</i>, a metaphor from the poem <i><a href="/wiki/Childe_Harold%27s_Pilgrimage" title="Childe Harold's Pilgrimage">Childe Harold's Pilgrimage</a></i> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Gordon,_Lord_Byron" title="George Gordon, Lord Byron">George Gordon, Lord Byron</a>, where a <a href="/wiki/Gladiator" title="Gladiator">gladiator</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Rome</a> expects to be "butchered to make a Roman holiday" while the audience would take pleasure from watching his suffering. The term suggests debauchery and disorder in addition to sadistic enjoyment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another phrase with a meaning similar to <i>Schadenfreude</i> is "morose delectation" (<i>delectatio morosa</i> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>), meaning, "The habit of dwelling with enjoyment on evil thoughts".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The medieval church taught that morose delectation was a sin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> writer <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Klossowski" title="Pierre Klossowski">Pierre Klossowski</a> maintained that the appeal of <a href="/wiki/Sadomasochism" title="Sadomasochism">sadism</a> is morose delectation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>An English word of similar meaning is "gloating", where "gloat" means "to observe or think about something with triumphant and often malicious satisfaction, gratification, or delight" (e.g. to gloat over an enemy's misfortune).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Gloating is differentiated from <i>Schadenfreude</i> in that it does not necessarily require malice (one may gloat to a friend about having defeated him in a game without ill intent), and that it describes an action rather than a state of mind (one typically gloats to the subject of the misfortune or to a third party). On the other hand, unlike Schadenfreude, where the focus is on someone's misfortune, gloating often brings to mind inappropriately celebrating or bragging about one's own good fortune without any particular focus on the misfortune of others.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_words">Related words</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schadenfreude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Related words">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhist</a> concept of <i><a href="/wiki/Mudita" title="Mudita">mudita</a></i>, "sympathetic joy" or "happiness in another's good fortune", is cited as an example of the opposite of schadenfreude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Alternatively, <a href="/wiki/Envy" title="Envy">envy</a>, which is <i>un</i>happiness in another's good fortune, could be considered the counterpart of schadenfreude. Completing the quartet is "unhappiness at another's misfortune"—which can be called <a href="/wiki/Sympathy" title="Sympathy">sympathy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pity" title="Pity">pity</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Compassion" title="Compassion">compassion</a>.</p>
<p>The transposed variant "freudenschade" has been invented in English to mean sorrow at another person's success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compersion" title="Compersion">compersion</a>", taking joy in the joy of loved ones, is generally considered an antonym of schadenfreude.</p>
<p>In Chinese, the <a href="/wiki/Set_phrase" title="Set phrase">set phrase</a> "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%B9%B8" title="wikt:幸">幸</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%81%BE" title="wikt:灾">灾</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E4%B9%90" title="wikt:乐">乐</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%A5%B8" title="wikt:祸">祸</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">xìng zāi lè huò</span></i>) literally translates to take "joy in calamity and delight in disaster", and serves the same meaning as schadenfreude.</p>
<p>The Finnish language contains a word with a meaning similar to schadenfreude, <i>vahingonilo</i>, which literally means "joy of misfortune". Likewise, Swedish also has a term equivalent to schadenfreude: <i>skadeglädje</i>, which translates literally as "injury joy" (the joy of watching someone's injury, be it figurative or literal). Similar terms exist in Danish (<i>skadefro</i>), Norwegian (<i>skadefryd</i>), and Estonian (<i>kahjurõõm</i>) as well.</p>
<p>The Dutch word <i>leedvermaak</i> (literally translatable as "suffer entertainment") is said to be a <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">calque</a> of the German "Schadenfreude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Afrikaans inherited <i>leedvermaak</i> without any changes in spelling or meaning from Dutch, though the pronunciation is slightly different.</p>
<p>The Arabic language contains the term <i>shamaatah</i>, which, according to the Arabic thesaurus means "to enjoy the calamity upon the enemy".</p>
<p>French uses <i>joie mauvaise</i> (bad or evil joy) and a few similar terms in a sense close to <i>Schadenfreude</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Bulgarian has the noun <i>злорадство</i> <small>Bulgarian pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Bulgarian_and_Macedonian" title="Help:IPA for Bulgarian and Macedonian">[zɫorˈadstvo]</a></span>, from <i>зло</i> (evil) and <i>радост</i> (joy), with a corresponding adjective <i>злорад</i>.</p>
<p>Portuguese possesses no single word that can exactly translate the term, but it has a common adage for the same emotion covered by schadenfreude in other languages, <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><i>pimenta nos olhos dos outros é refresco</i></span>, which means "to have someone else's eyes peppered is a refreshment" or, more literally, "pepper in someone else's eyes is [to the unaffected] a refreshing drink". According to popular folklore, this might often be the consequence of one putting an <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><i><a href="/wiki/Evil_eye" title="Evil eye">olho gordo</a></i></span> over another person and the consequential failure that might ensue to their fate. To consciously place a bad omen on someone and then relish with their affliction is called <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><i>agourar</i></span>. The meaning of the verb <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><i>agourar</i></span> is ambiguous, though, as it might imply just common ill omen (for example, the literal, folkloric bird of ill omen<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neologisms_and_variants">Neologisms and variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schadenfreude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Neologisms and variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Neologisms and <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> words were coined from the word as early as 1993, when Lincoln Caplan, in his book <i>Skadden: Power, Money, and the Rise of a Legal Empire</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> used the word <i>Skaddenfreude</i> to describe the delight that competitors of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skadden_Arps" title="Skadden Arps">Skadden Arps</a> took in its troubles of the early 1990s. Others include <i>spitzenfreude</i>, coined by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> to refer to the fall of <a href="/wiki/Eliot_Spitzer_prostitution_scandal" title="Eliot Spitzer prostitution scandal">Eliot Spitzer</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and <i>Schadenford</i>, coined by <i><a href="/wiki/Toronto_Life" title="Toronto Life">Toronto Life</a></i> in regards to Canadian politician <a href="/wiki/Rob_Ford" title="Rob Ford">Rob Ford</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_usage_and_philosophical_analysis">Literary usage and philosophical analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schadenfreude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Literary usage and philosophical analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Proverbs" title="Book of Proverbs">Book of Proverbs</a> mentions an emotion similar to schadenfreude: "Rejoice not when thine enemy falleth, and let not thine heart be glad when he stumbleth: Lest the LORD see it, and it displease him, and he turn away his wrath from him." (Proverbs 24:17–18, <a href="/wiki/King_James_Version" title="King James Version">King James Version</a>).</p>
<p>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Nicomachean_Ethics" title="Nicomachean Ethics">Nicomachean Ethics</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> used <i>epikhairekakia</i> (ἐπιχαιρεκακία in Greek) as part of a triad of terms, in which <i>epikhairekakia</i> stands as the opposite of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phthonos" title="Phthonos">phthonos</a></i> (φθόνος), and <i><a href="/wiki/Archenemy" title="Archenemy">nemesis</a></i> (νέμεσις) occupies the mean. <i>Nemesis</i> is "a painful response to another's undeserved good fortune", while <i>phthonos</i> is a painful response to any good fortune, deserved or not. The <i>epikhairekakos</i> (ἐπιχαιρέκακος) person takes pleasure in another's ill fortune.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lucretius" title="Lucretius">Lucretius</a> characterises the emotion in an extended <a href="/wiki/Simile" title="Simile">simile</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/De_rerum_natura" title="De rerum natura">De rerum natura</a></i>: <i>Suave, mari magno turbantibus aequora ventis, e terra magnum alterius spectare laborem</i>, "It is pleasant to watch from the land the great struggle of someone else in a sea rendered great by turbulent winds." The abbreviated <a href="/wiki/List_of_Latin_phrases" title="List of Latin phrases">Latin tag</a> <i>suave mare magno</i> recalled the passage to generations familiar with the Latin classics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Caesarius_of_Heisterbach" title="Caesarius of Heisterbach">Caesarius of Heisterbach</a> regards "delight in the adversity of a neighbour" as one of the "daughters of envy ... which follows anger" in his <i>Dialogue on Miracles</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/17th_century_philosophy" title="17th century philosophy">17th century</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Burton_(scholar)" title="Robert Burton (scholar)">Robert Burton</a> wrote in his work <i><a href="/wiki/The_Anatomy_of_Melancholy" title="The Anatomy of Melancholy">The Anatomy of Melancholy</a></i>, "Out of these two [the concupiscible and irascible powers] arise those mixed affections and passions of <a href="/wiki/Anger" title="Anger">anger</a>, which is a desire of revenge; hatred, which is inveterate anger; zeal, which is offended with him who hurts that he loves; and ἐπιχαιρεκακία, a compound affection of joy and hate, when we rejoice at other men's mischief, and are grieved at their prosperity; <a href="/wiki/Pride" title="Pride">pride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Self-love" title="Self-love">self-love</a>, emulation, envy, <a href="/wiki/Shame" title="Shame">shame</a>, &amp;c., of which elsewhere."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The philosopher <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Schopenhauer" title="Arthur Schopenhauer">Arthur Schopenhauer</a> mentioned schadenfreude as the most evil sin of human feeling, famously saying "To feel envy is human, to savor schadenfreude is diabolic."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rabbi <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harold_S._Kushner" title="Harold S. Kushner">Harold S. Kushner</a> in his book <i><a href="/wiki/When_Bad_Things_Happen_to_Good_People" title="When Bad Things Happen to Good People">When Bad Things Happen to Good People</a></i> describes schadenfreude as a universal, even wholesome reaction that cannot be helped. "There is a German psychological term, <i>Schadenfreude</i>, which refers to the embarrassing reaction of relief we feel when something bad happens to someone else instead of to us." He gives examples and writes, "[People] don't wish their friends ill, but they can’t help feeling an embarrassing spasm of gratitude that [the bad thing] happened to someone else and not to them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Susan_Sontag" title="Susan Sontag">Susan Sontag</a>'s book <i><a href="/wiki/Regarding_the_Pain_of_Others" title="Regarding the Pain of Others">Regarding the Pain of Others</a></i>, published in 2003, is a study of the issue of how the pain and misfortune of some affects others, namely whether <a href="/wiki/War_photography" title="War photography">war photography</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_art" title="War art">war paintings</a> may be helpful as anti-war tools or, whether they only serve some sense of schadenfreude in some viewers.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">Philosopher</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sociologist" title="Sociologist">sociologist</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theodor_Adorno" title="Theodor Adorno">Theodor Adorno</a> defined schadenfreude as "... largely unanticipated delight in the suffering of another, which is cognized as trivial and/or appropriate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_studies">Scientific studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schadenfreude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Scientific studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> article in 2002 cited a number of scientific studies of schadenfreude, which it defined as, "delighting in others' misfortune". Many such studies are based on <a href="/wiki/Social_comparison_theory" title="Social comparison theory">social comparison theory</a>, the idea that when people around us have bad luck, we look better to ourselves. Other researchers have found that people with low <a href="/wiki/Self-esteem" title="Self-esteem">self-esteem</a> are more likely to feel schadenfreude than are people who have high self-esteem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2003 study examined intergroup schadenfreude within the context of sports, specifically an international football (soccer) competition. The study focused on the German and Dutch football teams and their fans. The results of this study indicated that the emotion of schadenfreude is very sensitive to circumstances that make it more or less legitimate to feel such malicious pleasure towards a sports rival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2011 study by Cikara and colleagues using fMRI examined Schadenfreude among <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> fans found that fans' showed increased activation in brain areas correlated with self-reported pleasure (<a href="/wiki/Ventral_striatum" title="Ventral striatum">ventral striatum</a>) when observing the rival team experience a negative outcome (e.g., a strike out).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> By contrast, fans exhibited increased activation in the <a href="/wiki/Anterior_cingulate_cortex" title="Anterior cingulate cortex">anterior cingulate</a> and insula when viewing their own team experience a negative outcome.</p>
<p>A 2006 experiment about justice served suggests that men, but not women, enjoy seeing "bad people" suffer. The study was designed to measure empathy, by watching which brain centers are stimulated when subjects inside an <a href="/wiki/Functional_magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Functional magnetic resonance imaging">fMRI</a> observe someone experiencing physical <a href="/wiki/Pain" title="Pain">pain</a>. Researchers expected that the brain's empathy center of subjects would show more stimulation when those seen as "good" got an electric shock, than would occur if the shock was given to someone the subject had reason to consider "bad". This was indeed the case, but for male subjects, the brain's pleasure centers also lit up when someone got a shock that the male thought was "well-deserved".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brain-scanning studies show that schadenfreude is correlated with envy in subjects. Strong feelings of envy activated physical pain nodes in the brain's dorsal <a href="/wiki/Anterior_cingulate_cortex" title="Anterior cingulate cortex">anterior cingulate cortex</a>; the brain's reward centers, such as the <a href="/wiki/Ventral_striatum" title="Ventral striatum">ventral striatum</a>, were activated by news that other people envied had suffered misfortune. The magnitude of the brain's schadenfreude response could even be predicted from the strength of the previous envy response.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2009 study indicates that the hormone <a href="/wiki/Oxytocin" title="Oxytocin">oxytocin</a> may be involved in the feeling of schadenfreude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In that study, it was reported that when participants in a game of chance were pitted against a player they considered arrogant, inhaling oxytocin through the nose enhanced their feelings of schadenfreude when their opponent lost, as well as their feelings of envy when their opponent won.</p>
<p>A study conducted in 2009 provides evidence for people's capacity to feel schadenfreude in response to negative events in politics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The study was designed to determine whether or not there was a possibility that events containing objective misfortunes might produce schadenfreude. It was reported in the study that the likelihood of experiencing feelings of schadenfreude depends upon whether an individual's own party or the opposing party is suffering harm. This study suggests that the domain of politics is prime territory for feelings of schadenfreude, especially for those who identify strongly with their political party.</p>

<p>In "<a href="/wiki/When_Flanders_Failed" title="When Flanders Failed">When Flanders Failed</a>", a 1991 episode of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">The Simpsons</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Lisa_Simpson" title="Lisa Simpson">Lisa Simpson</a> defined "schadenfreude" to <a href="/wiki/Homer_Simpson" title="Homer Simpson">Homer</a> as "shameful joy", repopularizing the term, which had faded from English publications since spiking after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 2003 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tony_Awards" title="Tony Awards">Tony Award</a>-winning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musical_comedy" title="Musical comedy">musical</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Avenue_Q" title="Avenue Q">Avenue Q</a></i>, the song "Schadenfreude" parodies the language instruction songs of <i><a href="/wiki/Sesame_Street" title="Sesame Street">Sesame Street</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The song, sung by characters Gary Coleman and Nicky, describes schadenfreude as "German for 'happiness at the misfortune of others'". In the song, schadenfreude is also described as "making me feel glad that I'm not you" and "people taking pleasure in your pain". The characters use examples like "D'ja ever clap when a waitress falls and drops a tray of glasses?" and "Don'tcha feel all warm and cozy, watching people out in the rain?" as being schadenfreude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2005 episode of the television drama <i><a href="/wiki/Boston_Legal" title="Boston Legal">Boston Legal</a></i> carries the term as its title. In the episode, attorney <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alan_Shore" title="Alan Shore">Alan Shore</a> describes this condition to a jury in order to describe the only way they could possibly attain a guilty verdict against his client.</p>
<p>A 2005 episode of the television police drama <i><a href="/wiki/Cold_Case" title="Cold Case">Cold Case</a></i> also carries the term as its title. The episode describes this condition as the media's motive for the quickened prosecution of a doctor on his wife's murder and the re-opening of the case years later after new evidence comes to light.</p>
<p>New Orleans-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackened_death_metal" title="Blackened death metal">blackened</a> <a href="/wiki/Thrash_metal" title="Thrash metal">thrash metal</a> band <a href="/wiki/Goatwhore" title="Goatwhore">Goatwhore</a> has a track titled "Schadenfreude" in their 2014 album <i><a href="/wiki/Constricting_Rage_of_the_Merciless" title="Constricting Rage of the Merciless">Constricting Rage of the Merciless</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 2013, electronic music group <a href="/wiki/The_M_Machine" title="The M Machine">The M Machine</a> released a track titled "Schadenfreude" on their album <i>Metropolis, Pt. II</i>.</p>
<p>In 2008, American <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">electronic</a>-<a href="/wiki/Industrial_music" title="Industrial music">industrial</a> group <a href="/wiki/Aesthetic_Perfection" title="Aesthetic Perfection">Aesthetic Perfection</a> released a track titled "Schadenfreude" on their second album <a href="/wiki/A_Violent_Emotion" title="A Violent Emotion">A Violent Emotion</a>.</p>
<p>The video game <a href="/wiki/Team_Fortress_2" title="Team Fortress 2">Team Fortress 2</a> has a taunt in the game called "The Schadenfreude" where classes will point and laugh.</p>
<p>A frequent guest on the BS Report and other <a href="/wiki/Bill_Simmons" title="Bill Simmons">Bill Simmons</a> related podcasts named John O'Connell (aka JackO, aka The Gregarious Raconteur) refers to his own sense of schadenfreude frequently as it relates to watching athletic teams he dislikes either losing games or making poor decisions with personnel.</p>
