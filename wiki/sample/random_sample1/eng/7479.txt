<p>The <b>Chartreux</b> is a rare <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cat_breed" title="Cat breed">breed</a> of domestic <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cat</a> from <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and is recognised by a number of registries around the world. It is not currently recognised by the GCCF in the UK, ostensibly for being too similar to the <a href="/wiki/British_Shorthair" title="British Shorthair">British Shorthair</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russian_Blue" title="Russian Blue">Russian Blue</a> one of whose colours is a similar blue-grey. The Chartreux is large and muscular (called <i>cobby</i>) with relatively short, fine-boned limbs, and very fast reflexes. They are known for their <a href="/wiki/Blue#Animals" title="Blue">blue</a> (grey) water-resistant short hair double coats which are often slightly nappy in texture (often showing "breaks" like a sheepskin) and orange- or copper-colored eyes. Chartreux cats are also known for their "smile": due to the structure of their heads and their tapered muzzles, they often appear to be smiling. Chartreux are exceptional hunters and are highly prized by farmers.</p>
<p>As for every French cat with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedigree_(animal)" title="Pedigree (animal)">pedigree</a>, the first letter of the official name of a Chartreux cat encodes the year of its birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loof_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-loof-1">[1]</a></sup> All Chartreux born in the same year have official names beginning with the same letter. The code letters rotate through the alphabet each year, omitting the letters K, Q, W, X, Y, and Z. For example, a Chartreux born in 2011 would have an official name starting with the letter G.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chartreux&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jean-Baptiste_Perronneau_-_Magdaleine_Pinceloup_de_la_Grange,_n%C3%A9e_de_Parseval.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="11010" data-file-width="8839" height="274" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Jean-Baptiste_Perronneau_-_Magdaleine_Pinceloup_de_la_Grange%2C_n%C3%A9e_de_Parseval.jpg/220px-Jean-Baptiste_Perronneau_-_Magdaleine_Pinceloup_de_la_Grange%2C_n%C3%A9e_de_Parseval.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Jean-Baptiste_Perronneau_-_Magdaleine_Pinceloup_de_la_Grange%2C_n%C3%A9e_de_Parseval.jpg/330px-Jean-Baptiste_Perronneau_-_Magdaleine_Pinceloup_de_la_Grange%2C_n%C3%A9e_de_Parseval.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Jean-Baptiste_Perronneau_-_Magdaleine_Pinceloup_de_la_Grange%2C_n%C3%A9e_de_Parseval.jpg/440px-Jean-Baptiste_Perronneau_-_Magdaleine_Pinceloup_de_la_Grange%2C_n%C3%A9e_de_Parseval.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The Chartreux is mentioned for the first time in 1558 by <a href="/wiki/Joachim_du_Bellay" title="Joachim du Bellay">Joachim du Bellay</a> in a poem entitled <i>Vers Français sur la mort d'un petit chat</i> (<i>French verse on a small kitten's death</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Larousse_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Larousse-2">[2]</a></sup> There is another representation of a Chartreux in 1747 in the <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Perronneau" title="Jean-Baptiste Perronneau">Jean-Baptiste Perronneau</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painting</a> <i>Magdaleine Pinceloup de la Grange</i> into which the cat is painted as a <a href="/wiki/Pet" title="Pet">pet</a> which is quite rare at this time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> There is a legend that the Chartreux are descended from cats brought to France by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carthusian" title="Carthusian">Carthusian</a> monks to live in the order's head monastery, the <a href="/wiki/Grande_Chartreuse" title="Grande Chartreuse">Grande Chartreuse</a>, located in the <a href="/wiki/Chartreuse_Mountains" title="Chartreuse Mountains">Chartreuse Mountains</a> north of the city of <a href="/wiki/Grenoble" title="Grenoble">Grenoble</a> (<a href="#Siegal_1997">Siegal 1997</a>:27). But in 1972, the <a href="/wiki/Prior" title="Prior">Prior</a> of the Grande Chartreuse denied that the monastery's archives held any records of the monks' use of any breed of cat resembling the Chartreux (<a href="#Simonnet_1990">Simonnet 1990</a>:36–37). Legend also has it that the Chartreux's ancestors were feral mountain cats from what is now <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, brought back to France by returning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crusade" title="Crusade">Crusaders</a> in the 13th century, many of whom entered the Carthusian monastic order.</p>
<p>The first documented mention of the breed was by the French naturalist <a href="/wiki/Georges-Louis_Leclerc,_Comte_de_Buffon" title="Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon">Buffon</a> in the 18th century. The breed was greatly diminished during the first World War and wild populations (<a href="#Helgren_2013">Helgren 2013</a>:100-103) were not seen after World War II. A concerted effort by European breeders kept the breed from extinction. The first Chartreux were brought to the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> in 1971 by Helen and John Gamon of La Jolla, California. In 1987, the <a href="/wiki/Cat_Fanciers%27_Association" title="Cat Fanciers' Association">Cat Fanciers' Association</a> (CFA) advanced the Chartreux breed to championship status (<a href="#Siegal_1997">Siegal 1997</a>:27). There are fewer than two dozen active Chartreux breeders in North America as of 2007.</p>
<p>Historically famous Chartreux owners include the French novelist <a href="/wiki/Colette" title="Colette">Colette</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Baudelaire" title="Charles Baudelaire">Charles Baudelaire</a> and French president <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle" title="Charles de Gaulle">Charles de Gaulle</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperament">Temperament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chartreux&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Temperament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pukki.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1936" data-file-width="1296" height="329" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Pukki.jpg/220px-Pukki.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Pukki.jpg/330px-Pukki.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Pukki.jpg/440px-Pukki.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pukki.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A Chartreux with his blue short hair.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Chartreux cats tend to be quiet, rarely making noises such as mewing or crying, and some are mute. They are quite observant and intelligent, with some Chartreux learning to operate radio on/off buttons and to open screen door latches. They take about two years to reach adulthood. Chartreux cats are playful cats well into their adult years; some can be taught to fetch small objects in the same manner as a dog. Chartreux are good with children and other animals. They are non-aggressive, affectionate, good travelers and generally very healthy. Chartreux tend to bond with one person in their household, preferring to be in their general vicinity (often following their favoured person from room to room), though they are still loving and affectionate to the other members of the household.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture">Popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chartreux&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The mascot of the world's largest <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> festival, the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_International_Jazz_Festival" title="Montreal International Jazz Festival">Montreal International Jazz Festival</a>, is a blue Chartreux affectionately named 'Ste Cat <a class="external autonumber" href="http://blog.montrealjazzfest.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/chat1.jpg" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>', after the festival's hub, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sainte_Catherine_Street" title="Sainte Catherine Street">Sainte Catherine Street</a> in Montreal.</p>
<p>Gris-Gris, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_De_Gaulle" title="Charles De Gaulle">Charles De Gaulle</a>'s cat, who followed him from room to room.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A more detailed citation is required. (January 2013)">full citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>French writer <a href="/wiki/Colette" title="Colette">Colette</a> made one of her Chartreux the heroine of her books "<a href="/wiki/La_Chatte" title="La Chatte">La Chatte</a>" and "Les Vrilles de la vigne."</p>
