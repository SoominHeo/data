<p><b>Charles III, Duke of Bourbon</b> (17 February 1490 – 6 May 1527) was a French military leader, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Count_of_Montpensier" title="Count of Montpensier">Count of Montpensier</a>, Clermont and Auvergne, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rulers_of_Auvergne" title="Rulers of Auvergne">Dauphin of Auvergne</a> from 1501 to 1523, then Duke of Bourbon and Auvergne, Count of Clermont-en-Beauvaisis, Forez and La Marche, and Lord of Beaujeu from 1505 to 1521. He was also the <a href="/wiki/Constable_of_France" title="Constable of France">Constable of France</a> from 1515 to 1521. Also known as the Constable of Bourbon, he was the last of the great feudal lords to oppose the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_France" title="King of France">King of France</a> himself. He commanded the Imperial troops of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor_Charles_V" title="Holy Roman Emperor Charles V">Holy Roman Emperor Charles V</a> in what became known as the <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Rome_(1527)" title="Sack of Rome (1527)">Sack of Rome</a> in 1527, where he was killed.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_III,_Duke_of_Bourbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Charles was born at <a href="/wiki/Montpensier,_Puy-de-D%C3%B4me" title="Montpensier, Puy-de-Dôme">Montpensier</a>, the second son of <a href="/wiki/Gilbert,_Count_of_Montpensier" title="Gilbert, Count of Montpensier">Gilbert, Count of Montpensier</a> by his wife <a href="/wiki/Clara_Gonzaga" title="Clara Gonzaga">Clara Gonzaga</a> (1 July 1464- 2 June 1503). Clara was a daughter of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federico_I_Gonzaga" title="Federico I Gonzaga">Federico I Gonzaga</a>, Marquess of <a href="/wiki/Mantua" title="Mantua">Mantua</a>, by his wife <a href="/wiki/Margaret_of_Bavaria,_Marchioness_of_Mantua" title="Margaret of Bavaria, Marchioness of Mantua">Margaret of Bavaria</a>. Gilbert died in 1496, and his elder son <a href="/wiki/Louis_II,_Count_of_Montpensier" title="Louis II, Count of Montpensier">Louis II, Count of Montpensier</a> died unwed in 1501, leaving Charles the heir to the family's titles and extensive lands in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auvergne_(province)" title="Auvergne (province)">Auvergne</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage">Marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_III,_Duke_of_Bourbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 10 May 1505, Charles married his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agnatic" title="Agnatic">agnatic</a> second cousin, <a href="/wiki/Suzanne,_Duchess_of_Bourbon" title="Suzanne, Duchess of Bourbon">Suzanne, Duchess of Bourbon</a>. It was a dynastic match, intended to settle the question of succession to the Bourbon estates, which had arisen because Suzanne's father, the last of the senior Bourbon line, had died without sons. Charles was the scion of the next-senior Bourbon line, and thus the "heir male" of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon" title="House of Bourbon">House of Bourbon</a>, while Suzanne was the "heir general." With the marriage, Charles's position as <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Bourbon" title="Duke of Bourbon">Duke of Bourbon</a> became undisputed. This cementing of position was important for another reason: with the death of <a href="/wiki/Charles_IV,_Duke_of_Alen%C3%A7on" title="Charles IV, Duke of Alençon">Charles IV, Duke of Alençon</a> in 1525, Charles became the agnatic heir to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Valois" title="House of Valois">French royal line</a> in case of a failure of male heirs (which indeed happened in 1589).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_III,_Duke_of_Bourbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Already distinguished as a soldier in the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a>, Charles was appointed <a href="/wiki/Constable_of_France" title="Constable of France">Constable of France</a> by <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">Francis I of France</a> in 1515, and was rewarded for his services at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Marignano" title="Battle of Marignano">Battle of Marignano</a> (where he commanded the vanguard) with the Governorship of <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, Francis was uneasy with the proud and wealthy duke, and soon recalled him from Milan and refused to honor his debts. Charles was further angered by the appointment of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_IV_of_Alen%C3%A7on" title="Charles IV of Alençon">Charles IV of Alençon</a>, the King's brother-in-law, as commander of the vanguard during the campaigns in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, an office which should have been his.</p>
<p>The death of his wife in 1521 provoked the final breach between Charles and Francis I. Suzanne had left all her estates to Charles, but the King's mother, <a href="/wiki/Louise_of_Savoy" title="Louise of Savoy">Louise of Savoy</a>, claimed them as the heir in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proximity_in_blood" title="Proximity in blood">proximity in blood</a>, due to their previous entailments. She proposed to settle the question by marrying Charles; he refused the proposal because Louise was over forty-five years of age and fourteen years older than him. On behalf of his mother, Francis confiscated a portion of the Bourbon estates before the lawsuit had even been opened. Seeing no hope of prevailing, Charles made a secret agreement to betray his King and offer his services to the <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor Charles V</a>. The Emperor, the Constable, and King <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII of England</a> devised a grand plan to partition France. This however came to nothing because the plot was discovered; Charles was stripped of his offices and proclaimed a traitor. He fled into Italy in 1523.</p>
<p>In 1524, he drove the French under <a href="/wiki/Guillaume_Gouffier,_seigneur_de_Bonnivet" title="Guillaume Gouffier, seigneur de Bonnivet">Bonnivet</a> from <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">Lombardy</a>, and fought at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Pavia" title="Battle of Pavia">Battle of Pavia</a>. The Emperor gave Duke Charles command of a mixed Spanish-German army (which included a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutherans" title="Lutherans">Lutherans</a>) sent to chastise <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_VII" title="Pope Clement VII">Pope Clement VII</a>. He neglected to supply this army with money or food, and Charles was only able to keep it together by promises of loot. Though Clement arranged a truce with the Emperor, the army continued its advance, reaching <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> in May 1527. The death of Duke Charles — the artist and goldsmith <a href="/wiki/Benvenuto_Cellini" title="Benvenuto Cellini">Benvenuto Cellini</a> claimed that he fired the shot that killed him — outside the walls removed the last restraints from the army, which resulted in the <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Rome_(1527)" title="Sack of Rome (1527)">sack of Rome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Progeny_and_succession">Progeny and succession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_III,_Duke_of_Bourbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Progeny and succession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By Suzanne, Charles was the father of twins and of Francis of Bourbon, Count of Clermont. Officially, since neither survived a year of age, the senior line of the Dukes of Bourbon was extinct in male line with his death in battle, and the junior line (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dukes_of_Vend%C3%B4me" title="Dukes of Vendôme">Dukes of Vendôme</a>) were not allowed to inherit, because Charles had forfeited his fiefs by committing treason. However, the county of Montpensier and dauphinate of Auvergne were later returned to his sister Louise. The "<a href="/wiki/Bourbons_of_India" title="Bourbons of India">Bourbons of India</a>" claim descent from <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jean_Philippe_de_Bourbon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jean Philippe de Bourbon (page does not exist)">Jean Philippe de Bourbon</a>, an otherwise illegitimate son of Charles de Bourbon and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alaigne_al-Timur&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alaigne al-Timur (page does not exist)">Alaigne al-Timur</a>, a Mongol princess related to Emperor <a href="/wiki/Akbar" title="Akbar">Akbar</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Mughal_Empire" title="Mughal Empire">Mughal Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
