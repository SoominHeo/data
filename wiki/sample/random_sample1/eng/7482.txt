<p><b>Charles VI</b> (3 December 1368 – 21 October 1422), called <b>the Beloved</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>le Bien-Aimé</i></span>) and <b>the Mad</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>le Fol</i></span> or <i>le Fou</i>), was <a href="/wiki/List_of_French_monarchs" title="List of French monarchs">King of France</a> from 1380 to his death. He was a member of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Valois" title="House of Valois">House of Valois</a>.</p>
<p>Charles VI was only 11 when he inherited the throne in the midst of the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War" title="Hundred Years' War">Hundred Years' War</a>. The government was entrusted to his four uncles: <a href="/wiki/Philip_the_Bold" title="Philip the Bold">Philip the Bold</a>, Duke of Burgundy; <a href="/wiki/John,_Duke_of_Berry" title="John, Duke of Berry">John, Duke of Berry</a>; <a href="/wiki/Louis_I,_Duke_of_Anjou" title="Louis I, Duke of Anjou">Louis I, Duke of Anjou</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Louis_II,_Duke_of_Bourbon" title="Louis II, Duke of Bourbon">Louis II, Duke of Bourbon</a>. Although the royal <a href="/wiki/Age_of_majority" title="Age of majority">age of majority</a> was fixed at 14, the dukes maintained their grip on Charles until he took power at the age of 21.</p>
<p>During the rule of his uncles, the financial resources of the kingdom, painstakingly built up by his father, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_V_the_Wise" title="Charles V the Wise">Charles V</a>, were squandered for the personal profit of the dukes, whose interests were frequently divergent or even opposed. As royal funds drained, new taxes had to be raised, which caused several revolts.</p>
<p>In 1388 Charles VI dismissed his uncles and brought back to power his father's former advisers, known as the <a href="/wiki/Marmousets" title="Marmousets">Marmousets</a>. Political and economic conditions in the kingdom improved significantly, and Charles earned the epithet "the Beloved". But in August 1392 en route to Brittany with his army in the forest of <a href="/wiki/Le_Mans" title="Le Mans">Le Mans</a>, Charles suddenly went mad and slew four knights and almost killed his brother, <a href="/wiki/Louis_I,_Duke_of_Orl%C3%A9ans" title="Louis I, Duke of Orléans">Louis of Orléans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-madmonarchs.guusbeltman.nl_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-madmonarchs.guusbeltman.nl-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>From then on, Charles' bouts of <a href="/wiki/Insanity" title="Insanity">insanity</a> became more frequent and of longer duration. During these attacks, he had delusions, believing he was <a href="/wiki/Glass_delusion" title="Glass delusion">made of glass</a> or denying he had a wife and children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-madmonarchs.guusbeltman.nl_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-madmonarchs.guusbeltman.nl-1">[1]</a></sup> He could also attack servants or run until exhaustion, wailing that he was threatened by his enemies. Between crises, there were intervals of months during which Charles was relatively sane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-madmonarchs.guusbeltman.nl_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-madmonarchs.guusbeltman.nl-1">[1]</a></sup> However, unable to concentrate or make decisions, political power was taken away from him by the <a href="/wiki/Prince_du_sang" title="Prince du sang">princes of the blood</a>, which would cause much chaos and conflict in France.</p>
<p>A fierce struggle for power developed between Louis of Orléans, the king's brother, and <a href="/wiki/John_the_Fearless" title="John the Fearless">John the Fearless</a>, Duke of Burgundy, the son of Philip the Bold. When John instigated the murder of Louis in November 1407, the conflict degenerated into a <a href="/wiki/Armagnac%E2%80%93Burgundian_Civil_War" title="Armagnac–Burgundian Civil War">civil war</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Armagnac_(party)" title="Armagnac (party)">Armagnacs</a> (supporters of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Valois" title="House of Valois">House of Valois</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Burgundian_(party)" title="Burgundian (party)">Burgundians</a>. John offered large parts of France to <a href="/wiki/Henry_V_of_England" title="Henry V of England">King Henry V of England</a>, who was still at war with the Valois monarchy, in exchange for his support. After the assassination of John the Fearless, his son <a href="/wiki/Philip_the_Good" title="Philip the Good">Philip the Good</a> led Charles the Mad to sign the infamous <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Troyes" title="Treaty of Troyes">Treaty of Troyes</a> (1420), which disinherited his offspring and recognized Henry V as his legitimate successor on the throne of France.</p>
<p>When Charles VI died, he was succeeded by his son <a href="/wiki/Charles_VII_of_France" title="Charles VII of France">Charles VII</a>, who found the Valois cause in a desperate situation.</p>

<p>Charles was born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris,_France" title="Paris, France">Paris</a>, in the royal residence of the <i><a href="/wiki/H%C3%B4tel_Saint-Pol" title="Hôtel Saint-Pol">Hôtel Saint-Pol</a></i>, on 3 December 1368, the son of the king of France <a href="/wiki/Charles_V_of_France" title="Charles V of France">Charles V</a>, of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Valois" title="House of Valois">House of Valois</a>, and of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joan_of_Bourbon" title="Joan of Bourbon">Joan of Bourbon</a>. As heir to the French throne, his older brothers having died before he was born, Charles had the title <a href="/wiki/Dauphin_of_France" title="Dauphin of France">Dauphin of France</a>. At his father's death on 16 September 1380, he inherited the throne of France. His coronation took place on 4 November 1380, at <a href="/wiki/Reims_Cathedral" title="Reims Cathedral">Reims Cathedral</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Although the royal <a href="/wiki/Age_of_majority" title="Age of majority">age of majority</a> was 14 (the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Age_of_accountability" title="Age of accountability">age of accountability</a>" under Roman Catholic <a href="/wiki/Canon_law" title="Canon law">canon law</a>), Charles did not terminate the regency and take personal rule until 1388.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>He married <a href="/wiki/Isabeau_of_Bavaria" title="Isabeau of Bavaria">Isabeau of Bavaria</a> on 17 July 1385,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> when he was 17 and she was 14 (and considered an adult at the time). Isabeau had 12 children, most of whom died young. Isabeau's first child, named Charles, was born in 1386, and was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dauphin_of_Viennois" title="Dauphin of Viennois">Dauphin of Viennois</a> (heir apparent), but survived only 3 months. Her second child, Joan, was born on 14 June 1388, but died in 1390. Her third child, <a href="/wiki/Isabella_of_Valois" title="Isabella of Valois">Isabella</a>, was born in 1389. She was married to <a href="/wiki/Richard_II_of_England" title="Richard II of England">Richard II, King of England</a> in 1396, at the age of 6, and became Queen of England. Richard died in 1400 and they had no children. Richard's successor, Henry IV, wanted Isabella to then marry his son, 14-year-old future king Henry V, but she refused. She married again in 1406, this time to her cousin, <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Duke_of_Orl%C3%A9ans" title="Charles, Duke of Orléans">Charles, Duke of Orléans</a>, at the age of 17. She died in childbirth at the age of 19.</p>
<p>Isabeau's fourth child, <a href="/wiki/Joan_of_France,_Duchess_of_Brittany" title="Joan of France, Duchess of Brittany">Joan</a>, was born in 1391, and was married to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_VI,_Duke_of_Brittany" title="John VI, Duke of Brittany">John VI, Duke of Brittany</a> in 1396, at an age of 5; they had children. Isabeau's fifth child born in 1392 was also named Charles, and was Dauphin. Charles VI then became insane. The young Charles was betrothed to <a href="/wiki/Margaret_of_Burgundy,_Dauphine_of_France" title="Margaret of Burgundy, Dauphine of France">Margaret of Burgundy</a> in 1396, but died at the age of 9. Isabeau's sixth child, <a href="/wiki/Marie_of_Valois,_Prioress_of_Poissy" title="Marie of Valois, Prioress of Poissy">Mary</a>, was born in 1393. She was never married, and had no children. Isabeau's seventh child, <a href="/wiki/Michelle_of_Valois" title="Michelle of Valois">Michelle</a>, was born in 1395. She was engaged to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philip_the_Good,_Duke_of_Burgundy" title="Philip the Good, Duke of Burgundy">Philip</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/John_the_Fearless" title="John the Fearless">John the Fearless</a>, Duke of Burgundy, in 1404 (both were then aged 8) and they were married in 1409, aged 14. She had one child who died in infancy, before she died in 1422, aged 27.</p>
<p>Isabeau's eighth child, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis,_Dauphin_of_France_and_Duke_of_Guyenne" title="Louis, Dauphin of France and Duke of Guyenne">Louis</a>, was born in 1397, and was also Dauphin. He was married to the Margaret of Burgundy who had been betrothed to brother Charles, but they did not have any children before he died in 1415, aged 18.</p>
<p>Isabeau's ninth child, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John,_Dauphin_of_France_and_Duke_of_Touraine" title="John, Dauphin of France and Duke of Touraine">John</a>, was born in 1398, and was also Dauphin from 1415, after the death of his brother Louis. He was married to <a href="/wiki/Jacqueline,_Countess_of_Hainaut" title="Jacqueline, Countess of Hainaut">Jacqueline, Countess of Hainaut</a> in 1415, when aged 17, but they did not have any children before he died in 1417, aged 19. Isabeau's tenth child, <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Valois" title="Catherine of Valois">Catherine</a>, was born in 1401. She was married firstly to <a href="/wiki/Henry_V_of_England" title="Henry V of England">Henry V, King of England</a> in 1420, and they had one child, who became <a href="/wiki/Henry_VI_of_England" title="Henry VI of England">Henry VI of England</a>. Henry V died suddenly in 1422. Catherine may then have secretly married <a href="/wiki/Owen_Tudor" title="Owen Tudor">Owen Tudor</a> in 1429, and she also had children with him. She died in 1438, aged 37.</p>
<p>Isabeau's eleventh child, also named <a href="/wiki/Charles_VII_of_France" title="Charles VII of France">Charles</a>, was born in 1403. In 1413, Queen Isabeau and <a href="/wiki/Yolande_of_Aragon" title="Yolande of Aragon">Yolande of Aragon</a> finalized a marriage contract between Charles and Yolande's daughter <a href="/wiki/Marie_of_Anjou" title="Marie of Anjou">Marie of Anjou</a>, Charles' second cousin. Dauphin Louis and then Dauphin John died while in the care of <a href="/wiki/John_the_Fearless" title="John the Fearless">John the Fearless</a>, the Duke of Burgundy and regent for the insane King Charles. Yolande became the protectress of Charles, who became the new Dauphin in 1417. She refused Queen Isabeau's orders to return Charles to the French Court, reportedly replying, "We have not nurtured and cherished this one for you to make him die like his brothers or to go mad like his father, or to become English like you. I keep him for my own. Come and take him away, if you dare." After the death of Charles VI in 1422, the English regents claimed the crown of France for Henry VI, then aged 1, according to the terms of the Treaty of Troyes. However, Charles, aged 19, repudiated the treaty and claimed and became King of France, as Charles VII, sparking fresh fighting with the English. He married Marie of Anjou in 1422, and they had many children, most of which died at a very early age. He died in 1461, the longest living descendant of Isabeau.</p>
<p>Isabeau's twelfth and the last child, Philip, was born in 1407, but died shortly after.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regency">Regency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_VI_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Regency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Charles VI was only 11 years old when he was crowned King of France. Although Charles was entitled to rule personally from the age of 14, the dukes maintained their grip on power until Charles terminated the regency at the age of 21.</p>
<p>During his minority, France was ruled by Charles' uncles, as regents. The regents were Philip the Bold, Duke of Burgundy, <a href="/wiki/Louis_I,_Duke_of_Anjou" title="Louis I, Duke of Anjou">Louis I, Duke of Anjou</a>, <a href="/wiki/John,_Duke_of_Berry" title="John, Duke of Berry">John, Duke of Berry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Louis_II,_Duke_of_Bourbon" title="Louis II, Duke of Bourbon">Louis II, Duke of Bourbon</a>, Charles VI's maternal uncle. Philip took the dominant role during the regency. Louis of Anjou was fighting for his claim to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples" title="Kingdom of Naples">Kingdom of Naples</a> after 1382, dying in 1384, John of Berry was interested mainly in the <a href="/wiki/Languedoc" title="Languedoc">Languedoc</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and not particularly interested in politics; whilst Louis of Bourbon was a largely unimportant figure, due to his personality (he showed signs of mental instability) and his status (since he was not the son of a king).</p>
<p>During the rule of his uncles, the financial resources of the kingdom, painstakingly built up by his father <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_V_the_Wise" title="Charles V the Wise">Charles V</a>, were squandered for the personal profit of the dukes, whose interests were frequently divergent or even opposing. During that time, the power of the royal administration was strengthened and taxes re-established. The latter policy represented a reversal of the deathbed decision of the king's father Charles V to repeal taxes, and led to tax revolts, known as the <a href="/wiki/Harelle" title="Harelle">Harelle</a>. Increased tax revenues were needed to support the self-serving policies of the king's uncles, whose interests were frequently in conflict with those of the crown and with each other. The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Roosebeke" title="Battle of Roosebeke">Battle of Roosebeke</a> (1382), for example, brilliantly won by the royal troops, was prosecuted solely for the benefit of Philip of Burgundy. The treasury surplus carefully accumulated by Charles V was quickly squandered.</p>
<p>Charles VI brought the regency to an end in 1388, taking up personal rule. He restored to power the highly-competent advisors of Charles V, known as the <a href="/wiki/Marmousets" title="Marmousets">Marmousets</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> who ushered in a new period of high esteem for the crown. Charles VI was widely referred to as <i>Charles the Beloved</i> by his subjects.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reign">Reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_VI_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mental_illness">Mental illness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_VI_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mental illness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Charles_VI_double_d%27or_La_Rochelle_1420.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1661" data-file-width="1598" height="229" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Charles_VI_double_d%27or_La_Rochelle_1420.jpg/220px-Charles_VI_double_d%27or_La_Rochelle_1420.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Charles_VI_double_d%27or_La_Rochelle_1420.jpg/330px-Charles_VI_double_d%27or_La_Rochelle_1420.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Charles_VI_double_d%27or_La_Rochelle_1420.jpg/440px-Charles_VI_double_d%27or_La_Rochelle_1420.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Charles_VI_double_d%27or_La_Rochelle_1420.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

</div>
</div>
<p>The early successes of the sole rule of Charles VI quickly dissipated as a result of the bouts of <a href="/wiki/Psychosis" title="Psychosis">psychosis</a> he experienced beginning in his mid-twenties. Mental illness had been passed on for several generations through his mother, <a href="/wiki/Joanna_of_Bourbon" title="Joanna of Bourbon">Joanna of Bourbon</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Although still called by his subjects <i>Charles the Beloved</i>, he became known also as <i>Charles the Mad</i> from then on.</p>
<p>Charles's first known episode occurred in 1392 when his friend and advisor, <a href="/wiki/Olivier_de_Clisson" title="Olivier de Clisson">Olivier de Clisson</a>, was the victim of an attempted murder. Although Clisson survived, Charles was determined to punish the would-be assassin, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_de_Craon" title="Pierre de Craon">Pierre de Craon</a>, who had taken refuge in <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>. <a href="/wiki/John_V,_Duke_of_Brittany" title="John V, Duke of Brittany">John V, Duke of Brittany</a> was unwilling to hand him over, so Charles prepared a military expedition.</p>
<p>Contemporaries said Charles appeared to be in a "fever" to begin the campaign and disconnected in his speech. Charles set off with an army on 1 July 1392. The progress of the army was slow, which nearly drove Charles into a frenzy of impatience.</p>
<p>As the king and his escort were traveling through the forest near <a href="/wiki/Le_Mans" title="Le Mans">Le Mans</a> on a hot August morning, a barefoot leper dressed in rags rushed up to the King's horse and grabbed his bridle. "Ride no further, noble King!" he yelled: "Turn back! You are betrayed!" The king's escorts beat the man back, but did not arrest him, and he followed the procession for half an hour, repeating his cries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The company emerged from the forest at noon. A page who was drowsy from the sun dropped the king's lance, which clanged loudly against a steel helmet carried by another page. Charles shuddered, drew his sword and yelled "Forward against the traitors! They wish to deliver me to the enemy!" The king spurred his horse and began swinging his sword at his companions, fighting until one of his chamberlains and a group of soldiers were able to grab him from his mount and lay him on the ground. He lay still and did not react, but fell into a coma. The king had killed a knight known as "The Bastard of Polignac" and several other men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The king continued to suffer from periods of mental illness throughout his life. During one attack in 1393, he could not remember his name and did not know he was king. When his wife came to visit, he asked his servants who she was and ordered them to take care of what she required so that she would leave him alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> During an episode in 1395–96 he claimed he was <a href="/wiki/Saint_George" title="Saint George">Saint George</a> and that his coat of arms was a lion with a sword thrust through it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> At this time, he recognized all the officers of his household, but did not know his wife nor his children. Sometimes he ran wildly through the corridors of his Parisian residence, the <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B4tel_Saint-Pol" title="Hôtel Saint-Pol">Hôtel Saint-Pol</a>, and to keep him inside, the entrances were walled up. In 1405, he refused to bathe or change his clothes for five months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> His later psychotic episodes were not described in detail, perhaps because of the similarity of his behavior and delusions. <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_II" title="Pope Pius II">Pope Pius II</a>, who was born during the reign of Charles VI, wrote in his <i>Commentaries</i> that there were times when Charles thought that he was made of glass, and this caused him to protect himself in various ways so that he would not break. He reportedly had iron rods sewn in his clothes, so that he would not shatter if he came into contact with another person.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This condition has come to be known as <a href="/wiki/Glass_delusion" title="Glass delusion">glass delusion</a>.</p>
<p>Charles VI's secretary, Pierre Salmon, spent much time in discussions with the king while he was suffering from his intermittent psychosis. In an effort to find a cure for the king's illness, stabilize the turbulent political situation, and secure his own future, Salmon supervised the production of two distinct versions of the beautifully illuminated guidebooks to good kingship known as Pierre Salmon's <i>Dialogues</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bal_des_Ardents"><i>Bal des Ardents</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_VI_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bal des Ardents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bal_des_Ardents" title="Bal des Ardents">Bal des Ardents</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Le_Bal_des_Ardents.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1288" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Le_Bal_des_Ardents.jpg/220px-Le_Bal_des_Ardents.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Le_Bal_des_Ardents.jpg/330px-Le_Bal_des_Ardents.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Le_Bal_des_Ardents.jpg/440px-Le_Bal_des_Ardents.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Le_Bal_des_Ardents.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Bal_des_Ardents" title="Bal des Ardents">Bal des Ardents</a>, miniature of 1450–80.</div>
</div>

<p>On 29 January 1393, a masked ball, which became known as the <i>Bal des Ardents</i> ("Ball of the Burning Men") because of the tragedy that ensued, had been organized by Isabeau of Bavaria to celebrate the wedding of one of her ladies-in-waiting at the <i>Hôtel Saint-Pol</i>. At the suggestion of Huguet de Guisay, the king and four other lords<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Froissart_Chronicles_.p.550_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Froissart_Chronicles_.p.550-13">[13]</a></sup> dressed up as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woodwose" title="Woodwose">wild men</a> and danced about. They were dressed "in costumes of linen cloth sewn onto their bodies and soaked in resinous wax or pitch to hold a covering of frazzled hemp, so that they appeared shaggy &amp; hairy from head to foot".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> At the suggestion of one Yvain de Foix, the king commanded that the torch-bearers were to stand at the side of the room. Nonetheless, the king's brother <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_of_Valois,_Duke_of_Orl%C3%A9ans" title="Louis of Valois, Duke of Orléans">Louis of Valois, Duke of Orléans</a>, who had arrived late, approached with a lighted torch in order to discover the identity of the masqueraders, and he set one of them on fire. There was panic as the fire spread. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joanna_II_of_Auvergne" title="Joanna II of Auvergne">Duchess of Berry</a> threw the train of her gown over the king.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Several knights who tried to put out the flames were severely burned. Four of the wild men perished: Charles de Poitiers, son of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Count_of_Valentinois" title="Count of Valentinois">Count of Valentinois</a>; Huguet de Guisay; Yvain de <a href="/wiki/County_of_Foix" title="County of Foix">Foix</a>; and the Count of <a href="/wiki/Joigny" title="Joigny">Joigny</a>. Another – Jean, son of the Lord of <a href="/wiki/Nantouillet" title="Nantouillet">Nantouillet</a> – saved himself by jumping into a dishwater tub.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expulsion_of_the_Jews.2C_1394">Expulsion of the Jews, 1394</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_VI_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Expulsion of the Jews, 1394">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 17 September 1394, Charles suddenly published an ordinance in which he declared, in substance, that for a long time he had been taking note of the many complaints provoked by the excesses and misdemeanors that the Jews had committed against Christians, and that the prosecutors, having made several investigations, had discovered many violations by the Jews of the agreement they had made with him. Therefore, he decreed, as an irrevocable law and statute, that thenceforth no Jew should dwell in his domains ("Ordonnances", vii. 675). According to the <a href="/wiki/Michel_Pintoin" title="Michel Pintoin">Religieux de St. Denis</a>, the king signed this decree at the instance of the queen ("Chron. de Charles VI." ii. 119).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The decree was not immediately enforced, a respite being granted to the Jews in order that they might sell their property and pay their debts. Those indebted to them were enjoined to redeem their obligations within a set time, otherwise their pledges held in pawn were to be sold by the Jews. The provost was to escort the Jews to the frontier of the kingdom. Subsequently, the king released the Christians from their debts.</p>

<p>With Charles VI mentally ill, from 1393 his wife Isabeau presided over a regency counsel, on which sat the grandees of the kingdom. <a href="/wiki/Philip_the_Bold" title="Philip the Bold">Philip the Bold</a>, Duke of Burgundy, who acted as regent during the king's minority (from 1380 to 1388), was a great influence on the queen (he had organized the royal marriage during his regency). Influence progressively shifted to <a href="/wiki/Louis_I,_Duke_of_Orl%C3%A9ans" title="Louis I, Duke of Orléans">Louis I, Duke of Orléans</a>, the king's brother, another contender for power, and it was suspected, the queen's lover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dignat_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dignat-18">[18]</a></sup> Charles VI's other uncles were less influential during the regency: <a href="/wiki/Louis_II_of_Naples" title="Louis II of Naples">Louis II of Naples</a> was still engaged managing the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples" title="Kingdom of Naples">Kingdom of Naples</a>, and <a href="/wiki/John,_Duke_of_Berry" title="John, Duke of Berry">John, Duke of Berry</a>, served as a mediator between the Orléans party (what would become the <i>Armagnacs</i>) and the Burgundy party (<i>Bourguignons</i>). The rivalry would increase bit by bit and in the end result in outright civil war.</p>
<p>The new regents dismissed the various advisers and officials Charles had appointed. On the death of Philip the Bold in April 1404, his son <a href="/wiki/John_the_Fearless" title="John the Fearless">John the Fearless</a> took over the political aims of his father, and the feud with Louis escalated. John, who was less linked to Isabeau, again lost influence at court.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wars_with_Burgundy_and_England">Wars with Burgundy and England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_VI_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Wars with Burgundy and England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1407, Louis of Orléans was murdered in the <i>rue Vieille du Temple</i> in Paris. John did not deny responsibility, claiming that Louis was a tyrant who squandered money. Louis' son <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Duke_of_Orl%C3%A9ans" title="Charles, Duke of Orléans">Charles</a>, the new Duke of Orléans, turned to his father-in-law, <a href="/wiki/Bernard_VII,_Count_of_Armagnac" title="Bernard VII, Count of Armagnac">Bernard VII, Count of Armagnac</a>, for support against John the Fearless. This resulted in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armagnac-Burgundian_Civil_War" title="Armagnac-Burgundian Civil War">Armagnac-Burgundian Civil War</a>, which lasted from 1407 until 1435, beyond Charles' reign, though the war with the English was still in progress.</p>
<p>With the English taking over much of the country, John the Fearless sought to end the feud with the royal family by negotiating with the Dauphin <a href="/wiki/Charles_VII_of_France" title="Charles VII of France">Charles</a>, the king's heir. They met at the bridge at <a href="/wiki/Montereau-Fault-Yonne" title="Montereau-Fault-Yonne">Montereau</a> on 10 September 1419, but during the meeting, John was killed by <a href="/wiki/Tanneguy_du_Chastel" title="Tanneguy du Chastel">Tanneguy du Chastel</a>, a follower of the Dauphin. John's successor, <a href="/wiki/Philip_the_Good" title="Philip the Good">Philip the Good</a>, the new Duke of Burgundy, threw in his lot with the English.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_English_invasion_and_death">The English invasion and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_VI_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The English invasion and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Charles VI's reign was marked by the continuing conflict with the English, known as the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War" title="Hundred Years' War">Hundred Years' War</a>. An early attempt at peace occurred in 1396 when Charles' daughter, the almost seven-year-old <a href="/wiki/Isabella_of_Valois" title="Isabella of Valois">Isabella of Valois</a>, married the 29-year-old <a href="/wiki/Richard_II_of_England" title="Richard II of England">Richard II of England</a>. By 1415, however, the feud between the French royal family and the House of Burgundy led to chaos and anarchy throughout France that <a href="/wiki/Henry_V_of_England" title="Henry V of England">Henry V of England</a> was eager to take advantage of. <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War_(1415%E2%80%9353)" title="Hundred Years' War (1415–53)">Henry led an invasion</a> that culminated in the defeat of the French army at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Agincourt" title="Battle of Agincourt">Battle of Agincourt</a> in October.</p>
<p>In 1420, the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Troyes" title="Treaty of Troyes">Treaty of Troyes</a> was an agreement signed by Henry V of England and Charles VI of France, recognizing Henry as Charles' successor, and stipulating that Henry's heirs would succeed him on the throne of France. It disinherited the Dauphin Charles (with further claim, in 1421, that the young Charles was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illegitimate" title="Illegitimate">illegitimate</a>). It also betrothed Charles VI's daughter, <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Valois" title="Catherine of Valois">Catherine of Valois</a>, to Henry V (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Kings_of_France" title="English Kings of France">English Kings of France</a>). The treaty disinheriting the Dauphin of France in favor of the English crown was a blatant act against the interests of France. The Dauphin sealed his fate, in the eyes of the mad king, when he declared himself regent, seized royal authority, and refused to obey the king's order to return to Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> When the Treaty of Troyes was finalized in May 1420, the Dauphin Charles was only 17 years old.</p>
<p>Charles VI died on 21 October 1422 in Paris, at the <i>Hôtel Saint-Pol</i>. He was interred in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Denis_Basilica" title="Saint Denis Basilica">Saint Denis Basilica</a>, where his wife Isabeau of Bavaria would join him after her death in September 1435.</p>
<p>Upon the death of Charles VI, his infant grandson, who had become <a href="/wiki/Henry_VI_of_England" title="Henry VI of England">King Henry VI of England</a> at the death of his own father in August 1422, was, according to the Treaty of Troyes, also King of France, and his coronation as such took place at the cathedral of <a href="/wiki/Notre_Dame_de_Paris" title="Notre Dame de Paris">Notre Dame de Paris</a> on 26 December 1431. In the meantime, the Dauphin Charles, who had settled in <a href="/wiki/Bourges" title="Bourges">Bourges</a>, Paris being occupied by the English-Bourguignons since 29 May 1418, had to wait the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Joan_of_Arc" title="Joan of Arc">Joan of Arc</a> to be taken to the <a href="/wiki/Reims_Cathedral" title="Reims Cathedral">cathedral of Reims</a> for his coronation as <a href="/wiki/Charles_VII_of_France" title="Charles VII of France">Charles VII, King of France</a> on 17 July 1429. During his reign, Charles VII, the (disinherited) son of Charles VI, became known as "Charles the Victorious".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_references">Cultural references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_VI_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cultural references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christine_de_Pizan" title="Christine de Pizan">Christine de Pizan</a> dedicates a poem to Charles VI <i>Prière pour le roi Charles</i> in which she pleas for the health of her king.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romantic</a> French poet <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_de_Nerval" title="Gérard de Nerval">Gérard de Nerval</a> wrote a poem dedicated to the king: "Rêverie de Charles VI".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>The novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Notebooks_of_Malte_Laurids_Brigge" title="The Notebooks of Malte Laurids Brigge">The Notebooks of Malte Laurids Brigge</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Rainer_Maria_Rilke" title="Rainer Maria Rilke">Rainer Maria Rilke</a> describes the old age of Charles VI at length.</li>
<li>King Charles VI, and his madness, are mentioned at length in the historical novel <i><a href="/wiki/In_a_Dark_Wood_Wandering" title="In a Dark Wood Wandering">In a Dark Wood Wandering</a></i> (1949) by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hella_S._Haasse" title="Hella S. Haasse">Hella S. Haasse</a>.</li>
<li>The historical novel, "Blood Royal" aka "The Queen's Lover" by Vanora Bennett, about Charles VI's daughter, Catherine of Valois, refers to the King, his reign, family and his madness at length.</li>
<li>Charles VI is a character in <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>'s <i>Henry V</i>, as "King of France".</li>
</ul>
