<p><b>Charles, Prince Napoléon</b> (Charles Marie Jérôme Victor Napoléon; born 19 October 1950) is a French politician, and is recognised by some Bonapartists as the head of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bonaparte" title="House of Bonaparte">Imperial House of France</a> and as heir to the rights and legacy established by his great-great-grand-uncle, Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napol%C3%A9on_Bonaparte" title="Napoléon Bonaparte">Napoléon Bonaparte</a>. Other Bonapartists consider his son <a href="/wiki/Jean-Christophe,_Prince_Napol%C3%A9on" title="Jean-Christophe, Prince Napoléon">Jean-Christophe</a> to be the current head of the house and heir.</p>

<p>Charles is the elder son of the late <a href="/wiki/Louis,_Prince_Napol%C3%A9on" title="Louis, Prince Napoléon">Louis, Prince Napoléon</a> (1914–1997), and as such a great-great-grandson in the male line of <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_Bonaparte" title="Jérôme Bonaparte">Jérôme Bonaparte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Westphalia" title="Kingdom of Westphalia">King of Westphalia</a>, Napoléon's youngest brother. As neither Napoléon I nor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napol%C3%A9on_III_of_France" title="Napoléon III of France">Napoléon III of France</a> have surviving legitimate issue in the male line, Jérôme's descendants represent the only Imperial Bonapartes still living (the <a href="/wiki/Jerome_Napoleon_Bonaparte_II" title="Jerome Napoleon Bonaparte II">American Bonapartes</a> were senior in descent from King Jérôme, but the last male of that line died in 1945, nor was this branch ever considered <a href="/wiki/Dynasty#Dynasts" title="Dynasty">dynastic</a> in France).</p>
<p>In 2011, a study of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> extracted from Napoleon I's beard and compared to that of Charles was found to match substantially, establishing that both were members of the male-line of the Imperial <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bonaparte" title="House of Bonaparte">House of Bonaparte</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (In 2013, published scholarship comparing <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Haplotype" title="Haplotype">haplotype</a> evidence taken from Emperor Napoleon I, from Prince Charles and from a descendant of Napoleon's reputed son, Count <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Colonna-Walewski" title="Alexandre Colonna-Walewski">Alexandre Colonna-Walewski</a>, verified Walewski's descent in the genetic male-line from Napoleon I's <a href="/wiki/Patrilineality" title="Patrilineality">patriline</a>, however Walewski's mother was never married to the emperor and he was raised as the legal son of her husband.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Charles' mother is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alix,_Princess_Napoleon" title="Alix, Princess Napoleon">Alix de Foresta</a> (born 4 April 1926), daughter of Albéric, <i><a href="/wiki/Count" title="Count">comte</a></i> de Foresta. Although she was the only consort of the surviving Imperial line not born a princess, her family had been nobles in <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">Lombardy</a> since the 13th century, becoming <a href="/wiki/Count_palatine" title="Count palatine">counts palatine</a> in 1330, <a href="/wiki/Constable" title="Constable">constables</a> of Venice in 1425, then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retainer_(medieval)" title="Retainer (medieval)">retainers</a> of the powerful <a href="/wiki/Doria_(family)" title="Doria (family)">Doria</a> family in Genoa. They settled in <a href="/wiki/Provence" title="Provence">Provence</a>, France early in the 16th century, where they acquired twenty-two <a href="/wiki/Manorialism" title="Manorialism">manors</a> and the title of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marquis" title="Marquis">marquis</a> by 1651.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carnet_Web_de_G.C3.A9n.C3.A9alogie_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carnet_Web_de_G.C3.A9n.C3.A9alogie-3">[3]</a></sup> Ironically, the Forestas distinguished themselves during the <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_Restoration" title="Bourbon Restoration">French Restoration</a> as <a href="/wiki/Courtier" title="Courtier">courtiers</a> loyal to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon" title="House of Bourbon">House of Bourbon</a>, and to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henri,_comte_de_Chambord" title="Henri, comte de Chambord">Henri, comte de Chambord</a> in particular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carnet_Web_de_G.C3.A9n.C3.A9alogie_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Carnet_Web_de_G.C3.A9n.C3.A9alogie-3">[3]</a></sup> Long established as <a href="/wiki/Squire" title="Squire">squires</a> of large estates and rice <a href="/wiki/Paddy_field" title="Paddy field">paddies</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Camargue" title="Camargue">Camargue</a>, the Forestas often welcomed Charles and his siblings there while they were growing up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Charles was born in <a href="/wiki/Boulogne-Billancourt" title="Boulogne-Billancourt">Boulogne-Billancourt</a>, France along with his twin sister, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Catherine_Napol%C3%A9on&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Catherine Napoléon (page does not exist)">Princess Catherine</a>. He was baptised at <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Invalides" title="Les Invalides">Saint-Louis-des-Invalides</a></i> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuncio" title="Nuncio">Apostolic nuncio</a> to France Archbishop Angelo Roncalli (later <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XXIII" title="Pope John XXIII">Pope John XXIII</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Charles spent much of his youth at the family's ancestral retreat-in-exile, the <i>Villa Prangins</i> on <a href="/wiki/Lake_Geneva" title="Lake Geneva">Lake Geneva</a> between Lausanne and Geneva in Switzerland. He has two younger siblings, Princess Laure (born 1952) and <a href="/wiki/Prince_J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_Napol%C3%A9on" title="Prince Jérôme Napoléon">Prince Jérôme</a> (born 1957). His sisters are married but the French imperial line of succession never descended to or through women, while his brother married at the age of 56 and has no issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_profession">Education and profession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles,_Prince_Napol%C3%A9on&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education and profession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Charles attended school at <i>Sainte-croix-des-neiges</i> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communes_of_the_Haute-Savoie_d%C3%A9partement" title="Communes of the Haute-Savoie département">Abondance</a>, Haute-Savoie, taking off 1964–1965 to study German in the <a href="/wiki/Black_Forest" title="Black Forest">Black Forest</a>. He holds a doctorate in Economics from the <a href="/wiki/Sorbonne" title="Sorbonne">Sorbonne</a>. He has written essays and books, including "History of Urban Transportation" (<i>Histoire des Transports Urbains</i>, Dunod-Bordas), "Bonaparte and Paoli" (<i>Bonaparte et Paoli</i>, Plon-Perrin, 2000), "The Bonapartes, Rebels at Heart" (<i>Les Bonaparte, des esprits rebelles</i>, Plon-Perrin, 2006), and "For a New Republic" (<i>Pour une nouvelle République</i>, to be published by Pharos, 2007). He makes frequent public appearances in support of his political beliefs and candidacies.</p>
<p>Charles has worked professionally as a banker, financial planner, and real estate developer and as a visiting professor at the American Institute on Foreign Policy.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_and_children">Marriage and children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles,_Prince_Napol%C3%A9on&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marriage and children">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 19 December 1978, Charles married his distant cousin, <a href="/wiki/Princess_B%C3%A9atrice_of_Bourbon-Two_Sicilies" title="Princess Béatrice of Bourbon-Two Sicilies">Princess Béatrice of Bourbon-Two Sicilies</a>, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Prince_Ferdinand,_Duke_of_Castro" title="Prince Ferdinand, Duke of Castro">Prince Ferdinand, Duke of Castro</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Pretender" title="Pretender">pretender</a> to the throne of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Two_Sicilies" title="Two Sicilies">Two Sicilies</a>. Although both families are Roman Catholic, the couple declined religious nuptials in favour of a <a href="/wiki/Wedding#Types" title="Wedding">civil wedding</a> in Paris.</p>
<p>Charles and Béatrice had two children:</p>
<ul>
<li>HIH <a href="/wiki/Princess_Caroline_Napol%C3%A9on" title="Princess Caroline Napoléon">Princess Caroline Napoléon</a> (born 24 October 1980) married Eric Alain Marie Quérénet-Onfroy de Bréville (born 28 June 1971), son of François Quérénet-Onfroy de Bréville and his wife Christiane Vincent de Vaugelas, on 27 September 2009 in <a href="/wiki/Castellabate" title="Castellabate">Castellabate</a> nel <a href="/wiki/Cilento" title="Cilento">Cilento</a>, Italy:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
<ul>
<li>Elvire Quérénet-Onfroy de Breville (born 8 August 2010),</li>
<li>Augustin Quérénet-Onfroy de Breville (born 12 February 2013)</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>HIH <a href="/wiki/Jean-Christophe,_Prince_Napol%C3%A9on" title="Jean-Christophe, Prince Napoléon">Jean-Christophe, Prince Napoléon</a> or Prince Impérial (born 11 July 1986)</li>
</ul>
<p>Charles and Béatrice were divorced on 2 May 1989.</p>
<p>On 28 September 1996, Charles was married in a civil ceremony to Jeanne-Françoise Valliccioni (born in <a href="/wiki/Ortiporio" title="Ortiporio">Ortiporio</a>, Corsica on 26 March 1958). She had previously married Erik Langrais on 15 July 1978 at <a href="/wiki/Casaglione" title="Casaglione">Casaglione</a>, Corsica, from whom she was divorced on 24 July 1990. When Charles and Jeanne-Françoise wed, they already had a daughter:</p>
<ul>
<li>Sophie Catherine Napoléon (born in Paris, 18 April 1992).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>In 1998, the couple adopted a daughter of <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnamese</a> extraction:</p>
<ul>
<li>Anh Napoléon (born in <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh_City" title="Ho Chi Minh City">Ho Chi Minh City</a> on 22 April 1998).</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynastic_dispute">Dynastic dispute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles,_Prince_Napol%C3%A9on&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dynastic dispute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although officially recognized as <a href="/wiki/Heir_apparent" title="Heir apparent">heir apparent</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonapartist" title="Bonapartist">Bonapartist</a> claim during the lifetime of his father <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Louis_Napol%C3%A9on" title="Prince Louis Napoléon">Prince Louis Napoléon</a>, when the latter's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Will_(law)" title="Will (law)">will</a> was made public on 2 December 1997 (seven months after his death), it declared that Prince Charles was to be bypassed as <a href="/wiki/Dynasty" title="Dynasty">dynastic</a> heir in favour of his only son, Prince <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jean-Christophe_Louis_Ferdinand_Alb%C3%A9ric_Bonaparte" title="Jean-Christophe Louis Ferdinand Albéric Bonaparte">Jean-Christophe Napoléon</a>, then 11 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an interview published by <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Figaro" title="Le Figaro">Le Figaro</a></i> on 2 December 1997, Jean-Marc Varaut, the attorney who witnessed the late Prince Louis Napoléon's will and subsequently represented the dynastic interests of Prince Jean Christophe against his father, stated that Prince Charles had <a href="/wiki/Social_alienation" title="Social alienation">alienated</a> himself from the Bonaparte legacy by publicly espousing "republican and democratic opinions.... He has deprived himself of all rights to dynastic heritage in remarrying without his father's permission... which is against the rules of the imperial family."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-herb_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-herb-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his will, Louis cited three sources for his authority to exclude his son as dynastic heir:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <i>Senatus Consultus</i> of 7 November 1852 (an amendment to the <a href="/wiki/Second_French_Empire" title="Second French Empire">Second Empire</a> constitution): It states, in relevant part, "<i>Article 3</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napol%C3%A9on_III_of_France" title="Napoléon III of France">Louis Napoleon Bonaparte</a>, if he has no male children, may adopt the legitimate children and descendants in the male line of the brothers of Emperor Napoleon I... his adopted sons may only be called upon to succeed him after his legitimate descendants. Adoption is forbidden to Louis Napoleon's successors and to their descendants.... <i>Article 4</i> Louis Napoleon Bonaparte shall determine, by an <a href="/wiki/Organic_law" title="Organic law">organic decree</a> addressed to the Senate and deposited in its archives, the order of succession to the throne within the Bonaparte family, in case there remains no direct heir, legitimate or adopted.... The members of the family of Louis Napoleon Bonaparte eligible for the succession, and their descendants of both sexes, are members of the Imperial family.... They may not marry without the Emperor's authorization. Their marriage without such authorization entails loss of all rights of succession, both for him who contracts the marriage and his descendants. Nevertheless, if there are no children of the marriage and it is dissolved by death, the prince who contracted the marriage shall recover his succession rights."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Imperial Family Statute of 21 June 1853: It substantially reinstated the <a href="/wiki/House_law" title="House law">house law</a> adopted under Napoleon I on 30 March 1806, which provided: that marriages of dynasts required the prior, written consent of the emperor <i>de jure</i>, or were void; that divorce was forbidden for members of the Imperial family; and that the emperor <i><a href="/wiki/De_jure" title="De jure">de jure</a></i> retained the right to supervise and discipline members of the dynasty, including (under Title IV) the right to arrest, shun, or banish a dynast who "engages in misbehavior and forgets his dignity or duties" for up to one year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>Dynastic tradition: Among the Bonapartes this includes legal changes in the succession order during the first and second empires, and post-monarchy attempts to change heirs by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Will_(law)" title="Will (law)">testament</a>. Napoleon I quarreled openly with some of his brothers, particularly over their marriages, and dethroned or de-<a href="/wiki/Dynasty" title="Dynasty">dynasticized</a> them. When Napoleon III's only son, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napol%C3%A9on_Eug%C3%A8ne,_Prince_Imperial" title="Napoléon Eugène, Prince Imperial">Prince Imperial Louis Napoléon</a>, died in 1879, his will purported to exclude his cousin and <a href="/wiki/Genealogy" title="Genealogy">genealogical</a> heir, Prince <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napol%C3%A9on_Joseph_Charles_Paul_Bonaparte" title="Napoléon Joseph Charles Paul Bonaparte">Napoléon Joseph</a>, from headship of the deposed dynasty due to political differences, in favor of his elder son, Prince <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napol%C3%A9on_Victor_J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Bonaparte" title="Napoléon Victor Jérôme Frédéric Bonaparte">Victor Napoléon</a>. Although almost all Bonapartists therefore recognized his son, Napoléon Joseph insisted that his dynastic claim remained intact. Yet as Imperial pretender he likewise disavowed his heir, writing in his will, "I leave nothing to Victor, my elder son. He is a traitor and a rebel.... I recommend to my son Louis to remain faithful to my political and religious opinions. They are in the true tradition of my uncle Napoleon I. I hope that Louis will be the representative of the cause of the Napoleons. His aim must be to organise French democracy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Charles has expressed scepticism that his father's genuine intention was to disinherit him dynastically, "Without doubt, he had a mood swing, exploited by conservative elements in his entourage with whom I have long clashed.... I am fighting so that my family ceases to be the victim of attempted manipulation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-herb_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-herb-10">[10]</a></sup> Moreover, Prince Charles denies that his father had the authority, by law or tradition, to exclude him from the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_succession" title="Order of succession">order of succession</a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Even if I accept their premise, referring me to the Senatus Consultus of Napoleon III, divorce did not exist under the Second Empire, so it cannot be taken into account for the succession.... Moreover, the hypocrisy of this argument is exposed by the dates: My father's will was written in May 1996. I only re-married in October. All that matters is the Bonaparte tradition, which makes the eldest son the natural heir of his father."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-F._Billaut_18.E2.80.9319_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-F._Billaut_18.E2.80.9319-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Aside from his second marriage, Varaut alleged that Louis was offended that his son unilaterally had French civil authorities change their surname from "Napoléon-Bonaparte" to "Napoléon" during his divorce in 1989. But Charles maintains that the family's legal surname had, in fact, been <i>Napoléon</i> until altered through clerical error on his <a href="/wiki/Birth_certificate" title="Birth certificate">birth certificate</a>. When Charles requested that his surname be corrected, the civil authorities proceeded to apply the same change to the father's surname, "but not at my initiative."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-F._Billaut_18.E2.80.9319_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-F._Billaut_18.E2.80.9319-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Varaut further drew attention to the fact that Charles was warned in advance by his father that he would be purged from the succession, and that he had responded to his father with a letter dated 16 June 1996 in which he asserted that his "republican" beliefs had already alienated him from the principles of the Imperial position, even before his father's decision to exclude him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-F._Billaut_18.E2.80.9319_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-F._Billaut_18.E2.80.9319-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>When queried by the French weekly, <i>Point de Vue</i>, as to why he claimed headship of the Imperial dynasty in view of his republican pretensions, Charles replied, "I assume the 'moral heritage' of my name. To renounce today to my ability to become the head of our House would imply that I accept a certain number of grievances of which I have been accused...I cannot accept this sentence from another era. As for my republican sentiments, those who reproach me misunderstand the history of our family. Bonaparte – General, then First Consul – defended the <a href="/wiki/French_First_Republic" title="French First Republic">Republic</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-F._Billaut_18.E2.80.9319_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-F._Billaut_18.E2.80.9319-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Charles and his son have not engaged in the public feuding for which some of the past Bonaparte pretenders and their heirs have been notorious, and the father has stated of his son that "there will never be conflict between us".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-F._Billaut_18.E2.80.9319_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-F._Billaut_18.E2.80.9319-15">[15]</a></sup> A pre-screening of <i>Un nom en héritage</i>, a documentary television series on France's former dynasties, was the subject of a two-page spread in a December 2006 issue of <i>Point de Vue</i> that pictured Charles side-by-side with Jean-Christophe, both shown as participating in a cordial meeting between Napoleonic and <a href="/wiki/Orl%C3%A9anist" title="Orléanist">Orléanist</a> pretenders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nonetheless, in November 2004 an issue of <i>Point de Vue</i> had announced that henceforth the magazine would accord the Bonapartist <i>title of pretence</i>, "the Prince Napoleon", to Jean-Christophe, whereas since 1997 that title had been attributed to Charles. This decision followed receipt by the magazine of a protest from Jean-Marc Varaut, prompted by publication in an earlier issue of a reference to Charles as "head of the Imperial house".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <i>Point de Vue</i>, which sometimes <a href="/wiki/Gazette" title="Gazette">gazettes</a> monarchist announcements, published Varaut's re-assertion of the dynastic exclusion of Charles along with the prince's response: "...the title of 'head of family' among the Bonapartes devolves, at the death of the father, upon his eldest son. That rule is not susceptible to modification by the titleholder, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_fortiori" title="A fortiori">a fortiori</a></i> when the motives involved are petty and contrary to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_Code" title="Civil Code">Civil Code</a>. I have the honour of bearing this charge since the death of my father, and my son will assume it in turn upon my death."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_career">Political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles,_Prince_Napol%C3%A9on&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2000, Charles stood for election as mayor of <a href="/wiki/Ajaccio" title="Ajaccio">Ajaccio</a>, the historical seat of the Bonapartes in <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a>. Subsequently, he served as a member of the Ajaccio <a href="/wiki/City_council" title="City council">City council</a>, and in 2004 he held the post of deputy mayor in that city.</p>
<p>In early 2008, Charles announced plans to stand for election in March 2008 as mayor of <a href="/wiki/Nemours" title="Nemours">Nemours</a>, where he leads a union list called "Ensemble Pour Les Nemouriens" with local personalities, such as Ginette Tardy. In the election, he was defeated by Valérie Lacroute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
