<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Charles-Valentin Alkan</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p><b>Charles-Valentin Alkan</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[n 1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[n 2]</a></sup> (<small>French: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[ʃaʁl valɑ̃tɛ̃ alkɑ̃]</a></span>; 30 November 1813 – 29 March 1888) was a French-Jewish composer and virtuoso pianist. At the height of his fame in the 1830s and 1840s he was, alongside his friends and colleagues <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Chopin" title="Frédéric Chopin">Frédéric Chopin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Liszt" title="Franz Liszt">Franz Liszt</a>, among the leading pianists in Paris, a city in which he spent virtually his entire life.</p>
<p>Alkan earned many awards at the <a href="/wiki/Conservatoire_de_Paris" title="Conservatoire de Paris">Conservatoire de Paris</a>, which he entered before he was six. His career in the salons and concert halls of Paris was marked by his occasional long withdrawals from public performance, for personal reasons. Although he had a wide circle of friends and acquaintances in the Parisian artistic world, including <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix" title="Eugène Delacroix">Eugène Delacroix</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Sand" title="George Sand">George Sand</a>, from 1848 he began to adopt a reclusive life style, while continuing with his compositions – virtually all of which are for the keyboard. During this period he published, among other works, his collections of large-scale <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tude" title="Étude">studies</a> in all the major keys (Op. 35) and all the minor keys (Op. 39). The latter includes his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symphony_for_Solo_Piano_(Charles-Valentin_Alkan)" title="Symphony for Solo Piano (Charles-Valentin Alkan)">Symphony for Solo Piano</a> (Op. 39, nos. 4–7) and <a href="/wiki/Concerto_for_Solo_Piano_(Alkan)" title="Concerto for Solo Piano (Alkan)">Concerto for Solo Piano</a> (Op. 39, nos. 8–10), which are often considered among his masterpieces and are of great musical and technical complexity. Alkan emerged from self-imposed retirement in the 1870s to give a series of recitals that were attended by a new generation of French musicians.</p>
<p>Alkan's attachment to his Jewish origins is displayed both in his life and his work. He was the first composer to incorporate Jewish melodies in <a href="/wiki/Art_music" title="Art music">art music</a>. Fluent in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew_(language)" title="Hebrew (language)">Hebrew</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>, he devoted much time to a complete new translation of the Bible into French. This work, like many of his musical compositions, is now lost. Alkan never married, but his presumed son <a href="/wiki/%C3%89lie-Miriam_Delaborde" title="Élie-Miriam Delaborde">Élie-Miriam Delaborde</a> was, like Alkan, a virtuoso performer on both the piano and the <a href="/wiki/Pedal_piano" title="Pedal piano">pedal piano</a>, and edited a number of the elder composer's works.</p>
<p>Following his death (which according to persistent but unfounded legend was caused by a falling bookcase) Alkan's music became neglected, supported by only a few musicians including <a href="/wiki/Ferruccio_Busoni" title="Ferruccio Busoni">Ferruccio Busoni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egon_Petri" title="Egon Petri">Egon Petri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kaikhosru_Shapurji_Sorabji" title="Kaikhosru Shapurji Sorabji">Kaikhosru Sorabji</a>. From the late 1960s onwards, led by <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Lewenthal" title="Raymond Lewenthal">Raymond Lewenthal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Smith_(musician)" title="Ronald Smith (musician)">Ronald Smith</a>, many pianists have recorded his music and brought it back into the repertoire.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles-Valentin_Alkan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles-Valentin_Alkan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:112px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alkanpere.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="601" data-file-width="484" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Alkanpere.JPG/110px-Alkanpere.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Alkanpere.JPG/165px-Alkanpere.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Alkanpere.JPG/220px-Alkanpere.JPG 2x" width="110"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alkanpere.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Alkan's father, Alkan Morhange</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Alkan was born <b>Charles-Valentin Morhange</b> on 30 November 1813 at 1, Rue de Braque in Paris to Alkan Morhange (1780–1855) and Julie Morhange, née Abraham.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_2000_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_2000-11">[9]</a></sup> Alkan Morhange was descended from a long-established Jewish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ashkenazi" title="Ashkenazi">Ashkenazic</a> community in the region of <a href="/wiki/Metz" title="Metz">Metz</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup> the village of <a href="/wiki/Morhange" title="Morhange">Morhange</a> is located about 30 miles (48 km) from the city of Metz. Charles-Valentin was the second of six children – one elder sister and four younger brothers; his birth certificate indicates that he was named after a neighbour who witnessed the birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alkan Morhange supported the family as a musician and later as the proprietor of a private music school in <a href="/wiki/Le_Marais" title="Le Marais">le Marais</a>, the Jewish quarter of Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[12]</a></sup> At an early age, Charles-Valentin and his siblings adopted their father's first name as their last (and were known by this during their studies at the <a href="/wiki/Conservatoire_de_Paris" title="Conservatoire de Paris">Conservatoire de Paris</a> and subsequent careers).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[n 3]</a></sup> His brother Napoléon (1826–1906) became professor of <a href="/wiki/Solf%C3%A8ge" title="Solfège">solfège</a> at the Conservatoire, his brother Maxim (1818–1897) had a career writing light music for Parisian theatres, and his sister, Céleste (1812–1897), was also a pianist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[14]</a></sup> His brother Ernest (1816–1876) was a professional <a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">flautist</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[15]</a></sup> while the youngest brother Gustave (1827–1882) was to publish various dances for the piano.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prodigy_.281819.E2.80.931831.29">Prodigy (1819–1831)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles-Valentin_Alkan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prodigy (1819–1831)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Alkan was a <a href="/wiki/Child_prodigy" title="Child prodigy">child prodigy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[17]</a></sup> He entered the Conservatoire de Paris at an unusually early age, and studied both piano and organ. The records of his auditions survive in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archives_nationales_(France)" title="Archives nationales (France)">Archives Nationales</a> in Paris. At his solfège audition on 3 July 1819, when he was just over 5 years 7 months, the examiners noted Alkan (who is referred to even at this early date as "Alkan (Valentin)", and whose age is given incorrectly as six-and-a-half) as "having a pretty little voice". The profession of Alkan Morhange is given as "music-paper ruler". At Charles-Valentin's piano audition on 6 October 1820, when he was nearly seven (and where he is named as "Alkan (Morhange) Valentin"), the examiners comment "This child has amazing abilities."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alkan became a favourite of his teacher at the Conservatoire, <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Joseph-Guillaume_Zimmermann" title="Pierre-Joseph-Guillaume Zimmermann">Joseph Zimmermann</a>, who also taught <a href="/wiki/Georges_Bizet" title="Georges Bizet">Georges Bizet</a>, <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Franck" title="César Franck">César Franck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Gounod" title="Charles Gounod">Charles Gounod</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ambroise_Thomas" title="Ambroise Thomas">Ambroise Thomas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[19]</a></sup> At the age of seven, Alkan won a first prize for solfège and in later years prizes in piano (1824), <a href="/wiki/Harmony" title="Harmony">harmony</a> (1827), and <a href="/wiki/Organ_(music)" title="Organ (music)">organ</a> (1834).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[20]</a></sup> At the age of seven-and-a-half he gave his first public performance, appearing as a violinist and playing an air and variations by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Rode" title="Pierre Rode">Pierre Rode</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[21]</a></sup> Alkan's <a href="/wiki/Opus_number" title="Opus number">Opus</a> 1, a set of <a href="/wiki/Variation_(music)" title="Variation (music)">variations</a> for piano based on a theme by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Steibelt" title="Daniel Steibelt">Daniel Steibelt</a>, dates from 1828, when he was 14 years old. At about this time he also undertook teaching duties at his father's school. <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Fran%C3%A7ois_Marmontel" title="Antoine François Marmontel">Antoine Marmontel</a>, one of Charles-Valentin's pupils there, who was later to become his <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/b%C3%AAte_noire" title="wiktionary:bête noire">bête noire</a></i>, wrote of the school:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Young children, mostly Jewish, were given elementary musical instruction and also learnt the first rudiments of French grammar ... [There] I received a few lessons from the young Alkan, four years my senior ... I see once more ... that really parochial environment where the talent of Valentin Alkan was formed and where his hard-working youth blossomed ... It was like a preparatory school, a juvenile annexe of the Conservatoire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[22]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>From about 1826 Alkan began to appear as a piano soloist in leading Parisian <i><a href="/wiki/Salon_(gathering)" title="Salon (gathering)">salons</a></i>, including those of the Princesse de la Moskova (widow of <a href="/wiki/Michel_Ney" title="Michel Ney">Marshal Ney</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Louise_Antoinette_Lannes,_Duchess_of_Montebello" title="Louise Antoinette Lannes, Duchess of Montebello">Duchesse de Montebello</a>. He was probably introduced to these venues by his teacher Zimmermann.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[23]</a></sup> At the same time, Alkan Morhange arranged concerts featuring Charles-Valentin at public venues in Paris, in association with leading musicians including the <a href="/wiki/Soprano" title="Soprano">sopranos</a> <a href="/wiki/Giuditta_Pasta" title="Giuditta Pasta">Giuditta Pasta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henriette_Sontag" title="Henriette Sontag">Henriette Sontag</a>, the cellist <a href="/wiki/Auguste_Franchomme" title="Auguste Franchomme">Auguste Franchomme</a> and the violinist <a href="/wiki/Lambert_Massart" title="Lambert Massart">Lambert Massart</a>, with whom Alkan gave concerts in a rare visit out of France to <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a> in 1827.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[24]</a></sup> In 1829, at the age of 15, Alkan was appointed joint professor of solfège – among his pupils in this class a few years later was his brother Napoléon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conway_2012.2C_223_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Conway_2012.2C_223-1">[1]</a></sup> In this manner Alkan's musical career was launched well before the <a href="/wiki/July_Revolution" title="July Revolution">July Revolution</a> of 1830, which initiated a period in which "keyboard virtuosity ... completely dominated professional music making" in the capital,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[25]</a></sup> attracting from all over Europe pianists who, as <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Heine" title="Heinrich Heine">Heinrich Heine</a> wrote, invaded "like a plague of locusts swarming to pick Paris clean".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[26]</a></sup> Alkan nonetheless continued his studies and in 1831 enrolled in the organ classes of <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Benoist" title="François Benoist">François Benoist</a>, from whom he may have learnt to appreciate the music of <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Johann Sebastian Bach</a>, of whom Benoist was then one of the few French advocates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_fame_.281831.E2.80.931837.29">Early fame (1831–1837)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles-Valentin_Alkan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early fame (1831–1837)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Throughout the early years of the <a href="/wiki/July_Monarchy" title="July Monarchy">July Monarchy</a>, Alkan continued to teach and play at public concerts and in eminent social circles. He became a friend of many who were active in the world of the arts in Paris, including <a href="/wiki/Franz_Liszt" title="Franz Liszt">Franz Liszt</a> (who had been based there since 1827), <a href="/wiki/George_Sand" title="George Sand">George Sand</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Victor_Hugo" title="Victor Hugo">Victor Hugo</a>. It is not clear exactly when he first met <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Chopin" title="Frédéric Chopin">Frédéric Chopin</a>, who arrived in Paris in September 1831.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[28]</a></sup> In 1832 Alkan took the solo role in his first <i>Concerto da camera</i> for piano and strings at the Conservatoire. In the same year, aged 19, he was elected to the influential <i>Société Académique des Enfants d'Apollon (Society of the Children of <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a>)</i>, whose members included <a href="/wiki/Luigi_Cherubini" title="Luigi Cherubini">Luigi Cherubini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fromental_Hal%C3%A9vy" title="Fromental Halévy">Fromental Halévy</a>, the conductor <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Habeneck" title="François Habeneck">François Habeneck</a>, and Liszt, who had been elected in 1824 at the age of twelve. Between 1833 and 1836 Alkan participated at many of the Society's concerts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[30]</a></sup> Alkan twice competed unsuccessfully for the <a href="/wiki/Prix_de_Rome" title="Prix de Rome">Prix de Rome</a>, in 1832 and again in 1834; the <a href="/wiki/Cantata" title="Cantata">cantatas</a> which he wrote for the competition, <i>Hermann et Ketty</i> and <i>L'Entrée en loge</i>, have remained unpublished and unperformed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1834 Alkan began his friendship with the Spanish musician <a href="/wiki/Santiago_Masarnau_Fern%C3%A1ndez" title="Santiago Masarnau Fernández">Santiago Masarnau</a>, which was to result in an extended and often intimate correspondence which only came to light in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[32]</a></sup> Like virtually all of Alkan's correspondence, this exchange is now one-sided; all of his papers (including his manuscripts and his extensive library) were either destroyed by Alkan himself, as is clear from his will,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[33]</a></sup> or became lost after his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[34]</a></sup> Later in 1834 Alkan made a visit to England, where he gave recitals and where the second <i>Concerto da camera</i> was performed in <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a> by its dedicatee <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ibbot_Field" title="Henry Ibbot Field">Henry Ibbot Field</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_2000_I.2C_22_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_2000_I.2C_22-38">[35]</a></sup> it was published in London together with some solo piano pieces. A letter to Masarnau<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[36]</a></sup> and a notice in a French journal that Alkan played in London with <a href="/wiki/Ignaz_Moscheles" title="Ignaz Moscheles">Moscheles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johann_Baptist_Cramer" title="Johann Baptist Cramer">Cramer</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_2000_I.2C_22_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_2000_I.2C_22-38">[35]</a></sup> indicate that he returned to England in 1835. Later that year, Alkan, having found a place of retreat at <a href="/wiki/Piscop" title="Piscop">Piscop</a> outside Paris, completed his first truly original works for solo piano, the <i>Twelve Caprices</i>, published in 1837 as Opp. 12, 13, 15 and 16.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[37]</a></sup> Op. 16, the <i>Trois <a href="/wiki/Scherzo" title="Scherzo">scherzi</a> de bravoure</i>, is dedicated to Masarnau. In January 1836, Liszt recommended Alkan for the post of Professor at the <a href="/wiki/Conservatoire_de_Musique_de_Gen%C3%A8ve" title="Conservatoire de Musique de Genève">Geneva Conservatoire</a>, which Alkan declined,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[38]</a></sup> and in 1837 he wrote an enthusiastic review of Alkan's Op. 15 Caprices in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Revue_et_gazette_musicale" title="Revue et gazette musicale">Revue et gazette musicale</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="At_the_Square_d.27Orl.C3.A9ans_.281837.E2.80.931848.29">At the Square d'Orléans (1837–1848)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles-Valentin_Alkan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: At the Square d'Orléans (1837–1848)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1837, Alkan lived in the <a href="/wiki/Square_d%27Orl%C3%A9ans" title="Square d'Orléans">Square d'Orléans</a> in Paris, which was inhabited by numerous celebrities of the time including <a href="/wiki/Marie_Taglioni" title="Marie Taglioni">Marie Taglioni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Dumas" title="Alexandre Dumas">Alexandre Dumas</a>, George Sand, and Chopin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[40]</a></sup> Chopin and Alkan were personal friends and often discussed musical topics, including a work on musical theory that Chopin proposed to write.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conway_2012_229_30_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Conway_2012_229_30-44">[41]</a></sup> By 1838, at 25 years old, Alkan had reached a peak of his career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[42]</a></sup> He frequently gave recitals, his more mature works had begun to be published, and he often appeared in concerts with Liszt and Chopin. On 23 April 1837 Alkan took part in Liszt's farewell concert in Paris, together with the 14-year-old César Franck and the virtuoso <a href="/wiki/Johann_Peter_Pixis" title="Johann Peter Pixis">Johann Peter Pixis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[43]</a></sup> On 3 March 1838, at a concert at the piano-maker Pape, Alkan played with Chopin, Zimmerman, and Chopin's pupil <a href="/wiki/Adolphe_Gutmann" title="Adolphe Gutmann">Adolphe Gutmann</a> in a performance of Alkan's transcription, now lost, of two movements of <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Beethoven</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._7_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 7 (Beethoven)">Seventh Symphony</a> for two pianos, eight hands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>At this point, for a period which coincided with the birth and childhood of his <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/natural_son" title="wikt:natural son">natural son</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%89lie-Miriam_Delaborde" title="Élie-Miriam Delaborde">Élie-Miriam Delaborde</a> (1839–1913), Alkan withdrew into private study and composition for six years, returning to the concert platform only in 1844.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[45]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[n 4]</a></sup> Alkan neither asserted or denied his paternity of Delaborde, which, however, his contemporaries seemed to assume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[47]</a></sup> Marmontel wrote cryptically in a biography of Delaborde that "[his] birth is a page from a novel in the life of a great artist".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[48]</a></sup> Alkan gave early piano lessons to Delaborde, who was to follow his natural father as a keyboard virtuoso.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alkan's return to the concert platform in 1844 was greeted with enthusiasm by critics, who noted the "admirable perfection" of his technique, and lauded him as "a model of science and inspiration", a "sensation" and an "explosion".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[50]</a></sup> They also commented on the attending celebrities including Liszt, Chopin, Sand and Dumas. In the same year he published his piano étude <i><a href="/wiki/Le_chemin_de_fer_(Alkan)" title="Le chemin de fer (Alkan)">Le chemin de fer</a></i>, which critics, following Ronald Smith, believe to be the first representation in music of a steam engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[51]</a></sup> Between 1844 and 1848 Alkan produced a series of virtuoso pieces, the 25 <i>Préludes</i> Op. 31 for piano or organ, and the <a href="/wiki/Grande_sonate_%27Les_quatre_%C3%A2ges%27" title="Grande sonate 'Les quatre âges'">sonata Op. 33 <i>Les quatre âges</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[52]</a></sup> Following an Alkan recital in 1848, the composer <a href="/wiki/Giacomo_Meyerbeer" title="Giacomo Meyerbeer">Giacomo Meyerbeer</a> was so impressed that he invited the pianist, whom he considered "a most remarkable artist", to prepare the piano arrangement of the overture to his forthcoming opera, <i><a href="/wiki/Le_proph%C3%A8te" title="Le prophète">Le prophète</a></i>. Meyerbeer heard and approved Alkan's arrangement of the overture for four hands (which Alkan played with his brother Napoléon) in 1849; published in 1850, it is the only record of the overture, which was scrapped during rehearsals at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris_Op%C3%A9ra" title="Paris Opéra">Opéra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retreat_.281848.E2.80.931872.29">Retreat (1848–1872)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles-Valentin_Alkan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Retreat (1848–1872)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1848 Alkan was bitterly disappointed when the head of the Conservatoire, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Auber" title="Daniel Auber">Daniel Auber</a>, replaced the retiring Zimmermann with the mediocre Marmontel as head of the Conservatoire piano department, a position which Alkan had eagerly anticipated, and for which he had strongly lobbied with the support of Sand, Dumas, and many other leading figures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[54]</a></sup> A disgusted Alkan described the appointment in a letter to Sand as "the most incredible, the most shameful nomination";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[55]</a></sup> and Delacroix noted in his journal "By his confrontation with Auber, [Alkan] has been very put out and will doubtless continue to be so."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[56]</a></sup> The upset arising from this incident may account for Alkan's reluctance to perform in public in the ensuing period. His withdrawal was also influenced by the death of Chopin; in 1850 he wrote to Masarnau "I have lost the strength to be of any economic or political use", and lamented "the death of poor Chopin, another blow which I felt deeply."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[57]</a></sup> Chopin, on his deathbed in 1849, had indicated his respect for Alkan by bequeathing him his unfinished work on a piano method, intending him to complete it,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conway_2012_229_30_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Conway_2012_229_30-44">[41]</a></sup> and after Chopin's death a number of his students transferred to Alkan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[58]</a></sup> After giving two concerts in 1853, Alkan withdrew, in spite of his fame and technical accomplishment, into virtual seclusion for some twenty years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>Little is known of this period of Alkan's life, other than that apart from composing he was immersed in the study of the Bible and the <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">Talmud</a>. Throughout this period Alkan continued his correspondence with <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Hiller" title="Ferdinand Hiller">Ferdinand Hiller</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[60]</a></sup> whom he had probably met in Paris in the 1830s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[61]</a></sup> and with Masarnau, from which some insights can be gained. It appears that Alkan completed a full translation into French, now lost, of both the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> and the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, from their original languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[62]</a></sup> In 1865, he wrote to Hiller: "Having translated a good deal of the <a href="/wiki/Apocrypha" title="Apocrypha">Apocrypha</a>, I'm now onto the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Mark" title="Gospel of Mark">second Gospel</a> which I am translating from the <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a> ... In starting to translate the New Testament, I was suddenly struck by a singular idea – that you have to be Jewish to be able to do it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite his seclusion from society, this period saw the composition and publication of many of Alkan's major piano works, including the <i>Douze études dans tous les tons mineurs</i>, Op. 39 (1857), the <i>Sonatine</i>, Op. 61 (1861), the 49 <i><a href="/wiki/Esquisses_(Alkan)" title="Esquisses (Alkan)">Esquisses</a></i>, Op. 63 (1861), and the five collections of <i>Chants</i> (1857–1872), as well as the <i>Sonate de concert</i> for cello and piano, Op. 47 (1856). These did not pass unremarked; <a href="/wiki/Hans_von_B%C3%BClow" title="Hans von Bülow">Hans von Bülow</a>, for example, gave a laudatory review of the Op. 35 <i>Études</i> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Neue_Berliner_Musikzeitung" title="Neue Berliner Musikzeitung">Neue Berliner Musikzeitung</a></i> in 1857, the year in which they were published in Berlin, commenting that "Alkan is unquestionably the most eminent representative of the modern piano school at Paris. The virtuoso's disinclination to travel, and his firm reputation as a teacher, explain why, at present, so little attention has been given to his work in Germany."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the early 1850s Alkan began to turn his attention seriously to the <a href="/wiki/Pedal_piano" title="Pedal piano">pedal piano</a> (pédalier). Alkan gave his first public performances on the pédalier to great critical acclaim in 1852.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[65]</a></sup> From 1859 onwards he began to publish pieces designated as "for organ or piano à pédalier".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[66]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alkanspedalier.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Alkan's Érard grand piano pédalier, now in the <a href="/wiki/Cit%C3%A9_de_la_Musique#Mus.C3.A9e_de_la_Musique" title="Cité de la Musique">Musée de la Musique</a>, Paris</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It is not clear why, in 1873, Alkan decided to emerge from his self-imposed obscurity to give a series of six <i>Petits Concerts</i> at the <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A9bastien_%C3%89rard" title="Sébastien Érard">Érard</a> piano showrooms. It may have been associated with the developing career of Delaborde, who, returning to Paris in 1867, soon became a concert fixture, including in his recitals many works by his father, and who was at the end of 1872 given the appointment that had escaped Alkan himself, Professor at the Conservatoire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[67]</a></sup> The success of the <i>Petits Concerts</i> led to them becoming an annual event (with occasional interruptions caused by Alkan's health) until 1880 or possibly beyond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[68]</a></sup> The <i>Petits Concerts</i> featured music not only by Alkan but of his favourite composers from Bach onwards, played on both the piano and the pédalier, and occasionally with the participation of another instrumentalist or singer. He was assisted in these concerts by his siblings, and by other musicians including Delaborde, <a href="/wiki/Camille_Saint-Sa%C3%ABns" title="Camille Saint-Saëns">Camille Saint-Saëns</a>, and Auguste Franchomme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>Those encountering Alkan at this phase included the young <a href="/wiki/Vincent_d%27Indy" title="Vincent d'Indy">Vincent d'Indy</a>, who recalled Alkan's "skinny, hooked fingers" playing Bach on an Érard pedal piano: "I listened, riveted to the spot by the expressive, crystal-clear playing." Alkan later played Beethoven's <a href="/wiki/Piano_Sonata_No._31_(Beethoven)" title="Piano Sonata No. 31 (Beethoven)">Op. 110 sonata</a>, of which d'Indy said: "What happened to the great Beethovenian poem ... I couldn't begin to describe – above all in the Arioso and the Fugue, where the melody, penetrating the mystery of Death itself, climbs up to a blaze of light, affected me with an excess of enthusiasm such as I have never experienced since. This was not Liszt—perhaps less perfect, technically—but it had greater intimacy and was more humanly moving..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>The biographer of Chopin, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Niecks" title="Frederick Niecks">Frederick Niecks</a>, sought Alkan for his recollections in 1880 but was sternly denied access by Alkan's <a href="/wiki/Concierge" title="Concierge">concierge</a> – "To my ... enquiry when he could be found at home, the reply was a ... decisive 'Never'." However, a few days later he found Alkan at Érard's, and Niecks writes of their meeting that "his reception of me was not merely polite but most friendly."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[71]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles-Valentin_Alkan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alkan_tomb.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="494" data-file-width="475" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Alkan_tomb.jpg/170px-Alkan_tomb.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Alkan_tomb.jpg/255px-Alkan_tomb.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Alkan_tomb.jpg/340px-Alkan_tomb.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alkan_tomb.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tomb of Charles-Valentin Alkan, <a href="/wiki/Montmartre_Cemetery" title="Montmartre Cemetery">Montmartre Cemetery</a>, Paris</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>According to his death certificate, Alkan died in Paris on 29 March 1888 at the age of 74.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[72]</a></sup> Alkan was buried on 1 April (<a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter Sunday</a>) in the Jewish section of <a href="/wiki/Montmartre_Cemetery" title="Montmartre Cemetery">Montmartre Cemetery</a>, Paris,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[73]</a></sup> not far from the tomb of his contemporary <a href="/wiki/Fromental_Hal%C3%A9vy" title="Fromental Halévy">Fromental Halévy</a>; his sister Céleste was later buried in the same tomb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conway_2012_237_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-Conway_2012_237-78">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>For many years it was believed that Alkan met his death when a bookcase toppled over and fell on him as he reached for a volume of the Talmud from a high shelf. This tale, which was circulated by the pianist <a href="/wiki/Isidor_Philipp" title="Isidor Philipp">Isidor Philipp</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[75]</a></sup> is dismissed by <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Macdonald" title="Hugh Macdonald">Hugh Macdonald</a>, who reports the discovery of a contemporary letter by one of his pupils explaining that Alkan had been found prostrate in his kitchen, under a <i>porte-parapluie</i> (a heavy coat/umbrella rack), after his concierge heard his moaning. He had possibly fainted, bringing it down on himself while grabbing out for support. He was reportedly carried to his bedroom and died later that evening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[76]</a></sup> The story of the bookcase may have its roots in a legend told of <a href="/wiki/Aryeh_Leib_ben_Asher_Gunzberg" title="Aryeh Leib ben Asher Gunzberg">Aryeh Leib ben Asher</a>, rabbi of Metz, the town from which Alkan's family originated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[77]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personality">Personality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles-Valentin_Alkan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Personality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Charles-Valentin_Alkan_standing.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="763" data-file-width="472" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Charles-Valentin_Alkan_standing.jpg/170px-Charles-Valentin_Alkan_standing.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Charles-Valentin_Alkan_standing.jpg/255px-Charles-Valentin_Alkan_standing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Charles-Valentin_Alkan_standing.jpg/340px-Charles-Valentin_Alkan_standing.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Charles-Valentin_Alkan_standing.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
One of only two known photographs of Alkan</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Alkan was described by Marmontel (who refers to "a regrettable misunderstanding at a moment of our careers in 1848"), as follows:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"We will not give the portrait of Valentin Alkan from the rear, as in some photographs we have seen. His intelligent and original physiognomy deserves to be taken in profile or head-on. The head is strong; the deep forehead is that of a thinker; the mouth large and smiling, the nose regular; the years have whitened the beard and hair ... the gaze fine, a little mocking. His stooped walk, his puritan comportment, give him the look of an <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Anglican</a> minister or a rabbi – for which he has the abilities."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[78]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Alkan was not always remote or aloof. Chopin describes, in a letter to friend, visiting the theatre with Alkan in 1847 to see the comedian <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tienne_Arnal" title="Étienne Arnal">Arnal</a>: "[Arnal] tells the audience how he was desperate to pee in a train, but couldn't get to a toilet before they stopped at Orléans. There wasn't a single vulgar word in what he said, but everyone understood and split their sides laughing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[79]</a></sup> Hugh Macdonald notes that Alkan "particularly enjoyed the patronage of Russian aristocratic ladies, 'des dames très parfumées et froufroutantes [highly perfumed and frilled ladies]', as Isidore Philipp described them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[80]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alkan's aversion to socialising and publicity, especially following 1850, appeared to be self-willed. Liszt is reported to have commented to the Danish pianist <a href="/wiki/Frits_Hartvigson" title="Frits Hartvigson">Frits Hartvigson</a> that "Alkan possessed the finest technique he had ever known, but preferred the life of a recluse."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[81]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stephanie_McCallum" title="Stephanie McCallum">Stephanie McCallum</a> has suggested that Alkan may have suffered from <a href="/wiki/Asperger_syndrome" title="Asperger syndrome">Asperger syndrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">schizophrenia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Obsessive%E2%80%93compulsive_disorder" title="Obsessive–compulsive disorder">obsessive–compulsive disorder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alkan's later correspondence contains many despairing comments. In a letter of about 1861 he wrote to Hiller: "I'm becoming daily more and more misanthropic and misogynous ... nothing worthwhile, good or useful to do ... no one to devote myself to. My situation makes me horridly sad and wretched. Even musical production has lost its attraction for me for I can't see the point or goal."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[83]</a></sup> This spirit of <a href="/wiki/Anomie" title="Anomie">anomie</a> may have led him to reject requests in the 1860s to play in public, or to allow performances of his orchestral compositions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[84]</a></sup> However, it should not be ignored that he was writing similarly frantic self-analyses in his letters of the early 1830s to Masarnau.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jack_Gibbons" title="Jack Gibbons">Jack Gibbons</a> writes of Alkan's personality: "Alkan was an intelligent, lively, humorous and warm person (all characteristics which feature strongly in his music) whose only crime seems to have been having a vivid imagination, and whose occasional eccentricities (mild when compared with the behaviour of other 'highly-strung' artistes!) stemmed mainly from his hypersensitive nature."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gibbons_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbons-90">[86]</a></sup> Macdonald, however, suggests that "Alkan was a man of profoundly conservative ideas, whose lifestyle, manner of dress, and belief in the traditions of historic music, set him apart from other musicians and the world at large."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[87]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Judaism">Judaism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles-Valentin_Alkan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Judaism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Syna_Nazareth.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1536" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Syna_Nazareth.JPG/170px-Syna_Nazareth.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Syna_Nazareth.JPG/255px-Syna_Nazareth.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Syna_Nazareth.JPG/340px-Syna_Nazareth.JPG 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Syna_Nazareth.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Synagogue de Nazareth in Paris, where Alkan briefly held the post of organist</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Alkan grew up in a religiously observant Jewish household. His grandfather Marix Morhange had been a printer of the Talmud in Metz, and was probably a <i><a href="/wiki/Melamed" title="Melamed">melamed</a></i> (Hebrew teacher) in the Jewish congregation at Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[88]</a></sup> Alkan's widespread reputation as a student of the Old Testament and religion, and the high quality of his Hebrew handwriting<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[89]</a></sup> testify to his knowledge of the religion, and many of his habits indicate that he practised at least some of its obligations, such as maintaining the laws of <a href="/wiki/Kashrut" title="Kashrut">kashrut</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[90]</a></sup> Alkan was regarded by the Paris <a href="/wiki/Consistory_(Judaism)" title="Consistory (Judaism)">Consistory</a>, the central Jewish organisation of the city, as an authority on Jewish music. In 1845 he assisted the Consistory in evaluating the musical ability of <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Naumbourg" title="Samuel Naumbourg">Samuel Naumbourg</a>, who was subsequently appointed as <i><a href="/wiki/Hazzan" title="Hazzan">hazzan</a></i> (cantor) of the main Paris synagogue;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[91]</a></sup> and he later contributed choral pieces in each of Naumbourg's collections of synagogue music (1847 and 1856).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[92]</a></sup> Alkan was appointed organist at the <a href="/wiki/Synagogue_de_Nazareth" title="Synagogue de Nazareth">Synagogue de Nazareth</a> in 1851, although he resigned the post almost immediately for "artistic reasons".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[93]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alkan's Op. 31 set of <i>Préludes</i> includes a number of pieces based on Jewish subjects, including some titled <i>Prière (Prayer)</i>, one preceded by a quote from the <i><a href="/wiki/Song_of_Songs" title="Song of Songs">Song of Songs</a></i>, and another titled <i>Ancienne mélodie de la synagogue (Old synagogue melody).</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[94]</a></sup> The collection is believed to be "the first publication of <a href="/wiki/Art_music" title="Art music">art music</a> specifically to deploy Jewish themes and ideas."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[95]</a></sup> Alkan's three settings of synagogue melodies, prepared for his former pupil Zina de Mansouroff, are further examples of his interest in Jewish music; Kessous Dreyfuss provides a detailed analysis of these works and their origins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[96]</a></sup> Other works evidencing this interest include no. 7 of his Op. 66. <i>11 Grands préludes et 1 Transcription</i> (1866), entitled "Alla giudesca" and marked "con divozione", a parody of excessive <i>hazzanic</i> practice;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[97]</a></sup> and the slow movement of the cello sonata Op. 47 (1857), which is prefaced by a quotation from the Old Testament prophet <a href="/wiki/Micah" title="Micah">Micah</a> and uses melodic <a href="/wiki/Trope_(music)" title="Trope (music)">tropes</a> derived from the <a href="/wiki/Cantillation" title="Cantillation">cantillation</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Haftarah" title="Haftarah">haftarah</a></i> in the synagogue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conway_2012_237_78-1"><a href="#cite_note-Conway_2012_237-78">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>The inventory of Alkan's apartment made after his death indicates over 75 volumes in Hebrew or related to Judaism, left to his brother Napoléon (as well as 36 volumes of music manuscript).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[98]</a></sup> These are all lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[99]</a></sup> Bequests in his will to the Conservatoire to found prizes for composition of cantatas on Old Testament themes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[n 5]</a></sup> and for performance on the pedal-piano, and to a Jewish charity for the training of apprentices, were refused by the beneficiaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[101]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles-Valentin_Alkan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_compositions_by_Charles-Valentin_Alkan" title="List of compositions by Charles-Valentin Alkan">List of compositions by Charles-Valentin Alkan</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles-Valentin_Alkan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Berlioz-1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="330" data-file-width="330" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Berlioz-1.jpg/180px-Berlioz-1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Berlioz-1.jpg/270px-Berlioz-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3e/Berlioz-1.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Berlioz-1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Hector Berlioz</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Brigitte François-Sappey points out the frequency with which Alkan has been compared to <a href="/wiki/Hector_Berlioz" title="Hector Berlioz">Berlioz</a>, both by his contemporaries and later. She mentions that Hans von Bülow called him "the Berlioz of the piano", while Schumann, in criticising the Op. 15 <i>Romances</i>, claimed that Alkan merely "imitated Berlioz on the piano." She further notes that Ferruccio Busoni repeated the comparison with Berlioz in a draft (but unpublished) monograph, while Kaikhosru Sorabji commented that Alkan's Op. 61 <i>Sonatine</i> was like "a Beethoven sonata written by Berlioz".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[102]</a></sup> Berlioz was ten years older than Alkan, but did not attend the Conservatoire until 1826. The two were acquainted, and were perhaps both influenced by the unusual ideas and style of <a href="/wiki/Anton_Reicha" title="Anton Reicha">Anton Reicha</a> who taught at the Conservatoire from 1818 to 1836, and by the sonorities of the composers of the period of the French Revolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[103]</a></sup> They both created individual, indeed, idiosyncratic sound-worlds in their music; there are, however, major differences between them. Alkan, unlike Berlioz, remained closely dedicated to the German musical tradition; his style and composition were heavily determined by his pianism, whereas Berlioz could hardly play at the keyboard and wrote nothing for piano solo. Alkan's works therefore also include miniatures and (among his early works) <i>salon</i> music, genres which Berlioz avoided.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[104]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alkan's attachment to the music of his predecessors is demonstrated throughout his career, from his arrangements for keyboard of Beethoven's Seventh Symphony (1838), and of the minuet of <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Mozart</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._40_(Mozart)" title="Symphony No. 40 (Mozart)">40th Symphony</a> (1844), through the sets <i>Souvenirs des concerts du Conservatoire</i> (1847 and 1861) and the set <i>Souvenirs de musique de chambre</i> (1862), which include <a href="/wiki/Transcription_(music)" title="Transcription (music)">transcriptions</a> of music by Mozart, Beethoven, J. S. Bach, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Haydn" title="Joseph Haydn">Haydn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christoph_Willibald_Gluck" title="Christoph Willibald Gluck">Gluck</a>, and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[105]</a></sup> In this context should be mentioned Alkan's extensive <a href="/wiki/Cadenza" title="Cadenza">cadenza</a> for <a href="/wiki/Piano_Concerto_No._3_(Beethoven)" title="Piano Concerto No. 3 (Beethoven)">Beethoven's 3rd Piano Concerto</a> (1860), which includes quotes from the finale of <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._5_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 5 (Beethoven)">Beethoven's 5th Symphony</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[106]</a></sup> Alkan's transcriptions, together with original music of Bach, Beethoven, <a href="/wiki/George_Frideric_Handel" title="George Frideric Handel">Handel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Felix_Mendelssohn" title="Felix Mendelssohn">Mendelssohn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louis_Couperin" title="Louis Couperin">Couperin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jean-Philippe_Rameau" title="Jean-Philippe Rameau">Rameau</a>, were frequently played during the series of <i>Petits Concerts</i> given by Alkan at Erard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[107]</a></sup></p>
<p>As regards the music of his own time, Alkan was unenthusiastic, or at any rate detached. He commented to Hiller that "<a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Wagner</a> is not a musician, he is a disease."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[108]</a></sup> While he admired Berlioz's talent, he did not enjoy his music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[109]</a></sup> At the <i>Petits Concerts</i>, little more recent than Mendelssohn and Chopin (both of whom had died around 25 years before the series of concerts was initiated) was played, except for Alkan's own works and occasionally some by his favourites such as Saint-Saëns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[110]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles-Valentin_Alkan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alkan_Les_quatre_ages_fugue.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="240" data-file-width="347" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Alkan_Les_quatre_ages_fugue.png/220px-Alkan_Les_quatre_ages_fugue.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Alkan_Les_quatre_ages_fugue.png/330px-Alkan_Les_quatre_ages_fugue.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bf/Alkan_Les_quatre_ages_fugue.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alkan_Les_quatre_ages_fugue.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Extract from the second movement of the sonata <i>Les quatre âges</i> Op. 33, displaying one of Alkan's most complex musical textures</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>"Like ... Chopin", writes pianist and academic <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Hamilton" title="Kenneth Hamilton">Kenneth Hamilton</a>, "Alkan's musical output was centred almost exclusively on the piano".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[111]</a></sup> Some of his music requires extreme technical virtuosity, clearly reflecting his own abilities, often calling for great velocity, enormous leaps at speed, long stretches of fast repeated notes, and the maintenance of widely spaced <a href="/wiki/Counterpoint" title="Counterpoint">contrapuntal</a> lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[112]</a></sup> The illustration (right) from the <i>Grande sonate</i> is analysed by Smith as "six <a href="/wiki/Part_(music)" title="Part (music)">parts</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invertible_counterpoint" title="Invertible counterpoint">invertible counterpoint</a>, plus two extra voices and three doublings – eleven parts in all."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[113]</a></sup> Some typical musical devices, such as a sudden explosive final chord following a quiet passage, were established at an early stage in Alkan's compositions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[114]</a></sup> Macdonald suggests that</p>
<blockquote>
<p>unlike Wagner, Alkan did not seek to refashion the world through opera; nor, like Berlioz, to dazzle the crowds by putting orchestral music at the service of literary expression; nor even, as with Chopin or Liszt, to extend the field of harmonic idiom. Armed with his key instrument, the piano, he sought incessantly to transcend its inherent technical limits, remaining apparently insensible to the restrictions which had withheld more restrained composers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[115]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>However, not all of Alkan's music is either lengthy or technically difficult; for example, many of the Op. 31 <i>Préludes</i> and of the set of <i>Esquisses</i>, Op. 63.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[116]</a></sup></p>
<p>Moreover, in terms of structure, Alkan in his compositions sticks to traditional <a href="/wiki/Musical_form" title="Musical form">musical forms</a>, although he often took them to extremes, as he did with piano technique. The study Op. 39, no. 8 (the first movement of the <i>Concerto for solo piano</i>) takes almost half an hour in performance. Describing this "gigantic" piece, Ronald Smith comments that it convinces for the same reasons as does the music of the classical masters; "the underlying unity of its principal themes, and a <a href="/wiki/Key_(music)" title="Key (music)">key</a> structure that is basically simple and sound."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[117]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some of Alkan's music gives hints of the obsessiveness which some have detected in his personality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[118]</a></sup> The <i>Chant</i> Op. 38, no. 2, entitled <i><a href="/wiki/F_(musical_note)" title="F (musical note)">Fa</a></i>, repeats the note of its title incessantly (in total 414 times) against shifting harmonies which make it "cut ... into the texture with the ruthless precision of a <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> beam."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[119]</a></sup> In modelling his five sets of <i>Chants</i> on the first book of Mendelssohn's <i><a href="/wiki/Songs_Without_Words" title="Songs Without Words">Songs Without Words</a></i>, Alkan ensured that the pieces in each of his sets followed precisely the same key signatures, and even the moods, of the original.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[120]</a></sup> Alkan was rigorous in his <a href="/wiki/Enharmonic" title="Enharmonic">enharmonic</a> spelling, occasionally <a href="/wiki/Modulation_(music)" title="Modulation (music)">modulating</a> to keys containing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double-sharp" title="Double-sharp">double-sharps</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double-flat" title="Double-flat">double-flats</a>, so pianists are occasionally required to come to terms with unusual keys such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-sharp_major" title="E-sharp major">E-sharp major</a>, enharmonic equivalent to <a href="/wiki/F_major" title="F major">F major</a>, and the occasional triple-sharp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[n 6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles-Valentin_Alkan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_works">Early works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles-Valentin_Alkan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Early works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Alkan's earliest works indicate, according to Smith, that in his early teens he "was a formidable musician but as yet ... industrious rather than ... creative".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128">[122]</a></sup> Only with his 12 <i>Caprices</i> (Opp.12–13 and 15–16, 1837) did his compositions begin to attract serious critical attention. The op. 15 set, <i>Souvenirs: Trois morceaux dans le genre pathétique</i>, dedicated to Liszt, contains <i>Le vent (The Wind)</i>, which was at one time the only piece by the composer to figure regularly in recitals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[n 7]</a></sup> These works, however, did not meet with the approval of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Schumann" title="Robert Schumann">Robert Schumann</a>, who wrote: "One is startled by such false, such unnatural art ... the last [piece, titled <i>Morte (Death)</i>, is] a crabbed waste, overgrown with brush and weeds ... nothing is to be found but black on black".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132">[125]</a></sup> Ronald Smith, however, finds in this latter work, which cites the <i><a href="/wiki/Dies_Irae" title="Dies Irae">Dies Irae</a></i> theme also used by Berlioz, Liszt and others, foreshadowings of <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Ravel" title="Maurice Ravel">Maurice Ravel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Modest_Mussorgsky" title="Modest Mussorgsky">Modest Mussorgsky</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Ives" title="Charles Ives">Charles Ives</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-133"><a href="#cite_note-133">[126]</a></sup> Schumann did, however, respond positively to the pieces of <i>Les mois</i> (originally part published as Op. 8 in 1838, later published as a complete set in 1840 as Op. 74):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[n 8]</a></sup> "[Here] we find such an excellent jest on operatic music in no. 6 [<i>L'Opéra</i>] that a better one could scarcely be imagined ... The composer ... well understands the rarer effects of his instrument."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[127]</a></sup> Alkan's technical mastery of the keyboard was asserted by the publication in 1838 of the <i>Trois grandes études</i> (originally without opus number, later republished as Op. 76), the first for the left hand alone, the second for the right hand alone, the third for both hands; and all of great difficulty, described by Smith as "a peak of pianistic transcendentalism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-136"><a href="#cite_note-136">[128]</a></sup> This is perhaps the earliest example of writing for a single hand as "an entity in its own right, capable of covering all registers of the piano, of rendering itself as accompanied soloist or <a href="/wiki/Polyphony" title="Polyphony">polyphonist</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[129]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_maturity">Early maturity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles-Valentin_Alkan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Early maturity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Quasi-Faust_-_Alkan.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="248" data-file-width="988" height="75" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Quasi-Faust_-_Alkan.png/300px-Quasi-Faust_-_Alkan.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Quasi-Faust_-_Alkan.png/450px-Quasi-Faust_-_Alkan.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Quasi-Faust_-_Alkan.png/600px-Quasi-Faust_-_Alkan.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Quasi-Faust_-_Alkan.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Opening of the second movement ("Quasi-Faust") of the Sonate Op. 33, marked "Sataniquement" (<a href="/wiki/Satan" title="Satan">satanically</a>)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Alkan's large scale <i>Duo</i> (in effect a sonata) Op. 21 for violin and piano (dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Chr%C3%A9tien_Urhan" title="Chrétien Urhan">Chrétien Urhan</a>) and his Piano Trio Op. 30 appeared in 1841. Apart from these, Alkan published only a few minor works between 1840 and 1844, after which a series of virtuoso works was issued, many of which he had played at his successful recitals at Érard and elsewhere;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[130]</a></sup> these included the <i>Marche funèbre</i> (Op. 26), the <i>Marche triomphale</i> (Op. 27) and <i>Le chemin de fer</i> (also published, separately, as Op. 27). In 1847 appeared the Op. 31 <i>Préludes</i> and his first large-scale unified piano work, the Grande sonate <i>Les quatre âges</i> (Op. 33). The sonata is structurally innovative in two ways; each movement is slower than its predecessor, and the work anticipates the practice of <a href="/wiki/Progressive_tonality" title="Progressive tonality">progressive tonality</a>, beginning in <a href="/wiki/D_major" title="D major">D major</a> and ending in <a href="/wiki/G-sharp_minor" title="G-sharp minor">G-sharp minor</a>. Dedicated to Alkan Morhange, the sonata depicts in its successive movements its 'hero' at the ages of 20 (optimistic), 30 (<i>"Quasi-<a href="/wiki/Faust" title="Faust">Faust</a>"</i>, impassioned and fatalistic), 40 (domesticated) and 50 (suffering: the movement is prefaced by a quotation from <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">Aeschylus</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Prometheus_Unbound_(Aeschylus)" title="Prometheus Unbound (Aeschylus)">Prometheus Unbound</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-139"><a href="#cite_note-139">[131]</a></sup> In 1848 followed Alkan's set of <i>12 études dans tous les tons majeurs</i> Op. 35, whose substantial pieces range in mood from the hectic <i>Allegro barbaro</i> (no. 5) and the intense <i>Chant d'amour-Chant de mort (Song of Love – Song of Death)</i> (no. 10) to the descriptive and picturesque <i>L'incendie au village voisin (The Fire in the Next Village)</i> (no. 7).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[132]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of Alkan's compositions from this period were never performed and have been lost. Among the missing works are some string <a href="/wiki/Sextet" title="Sextet">sextets</a> and a full-scale orchestral <a href="/wiki/Symphony" title="Symphony">symphony</a> in <a href="/wiki/B_minor" title="B minor">B minor</a>, which was described in an article in 1846 by the critic Léon Kreutzer, to whom Alkan had shown the score.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-141"><a href="#cite_note-141">[133]</a></sup> Kreutzer noted that the introductory <i><a href="/wiki/Tempo#Basic_tempo_markings" title="Tempo">adagio</a></i> of the symphony was headed "by Hebrew characters in red ink ... This is no less than the verse from <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Genesis</a>: And God said, Let there be light: and there was light." Kreutzer opined that, set beside Alkan's conception, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Haydn" title="Joseph Haydn">Joseph Haydn</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Creation_(Haydn)" title="The Creation (Haydn)">Creation</a></i> was a "mere candle (<i>lampion</i>)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-142"><a href="#cite_note-142">[134]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_exile">Internal exile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles-Valentin_Alkan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Internal exile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Le_festin_d%27%C3%89sope_theme_complete.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="532" data-file-width="1065" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Le_festin_d%27%C3%89sope_theme_complete.png/300px-Le_festin_d%27%C3%89sope_theme_complete.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Le_festin_d%27%C3%89sope_theme_complete.png/450px-Le_festin_d%27%C3%89sope_theme_complete.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Le_festin_d%27%C3%89sope_theme_complete.png/600px-Le_festin_d%27%C3%89sope_theme_complete.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Le_festin_d%27%C3%89sope_theme_complete.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Theme of the variations op. 39 no. 12, <i>Le festin d'Ésope</i></div>

<p>During his twenty-year absence from the public between 1853 and 1873 Alkan produced many of his most notable compositions, although there is a ten-year gap between publication of the Op. 35 studies and that of his next group of piano works in 1856 and 1857. Of these, undoubtedly the most significant was the enormous Opus 39 collection of twelve studies in all the minor keys, which contains the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symphony_for_Solo_Piano_(Charles-Valentin_Alkan)" title="Symphony for Solo Piano (Charles-Valentin Alkan)">Symphony for Solo Piano</a></i> (numbers four, five, six and seven), and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concerto_for_solo_piano_(Alkan)" title="Concerto for solo piano (Alkan)">Concerto for Solo Piano</a></i> (numbers eight, nine and ten).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-143"><a href="#cite_note-143">[n 9]</a></sup> The <i>Concerto</i> takes nearly an hour in performance. Number twelve of Op. 39 is a set of variations, <i><a href="/wiki/Le_festin_d%27%C3%89sope" title="Le festin d'Ésope">Le festin d'Ésope</a></i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Aesop" title="Aesop">Aesop</a>'s Feast</i>). The other components of Op. 39 are of a similar stature. Smith describes Op. 39 as a whole as "a towering achievement, gathering ... the most complete manifestation of Alkan's many-sided genius: its dark passion, its vital rhythmic drive, its pungent harmony, its occasionally outrageous humour, and, above all, its uncompromising piano writing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-144"><a href="#cite_note-144">[135]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the same year appeared the <i>Sonate de Concert</i>, Op. 47, for cello and piano, "among the most difficult and ambitious in the romantic repertoire ... anticipating <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Mahler" title="Gustav Mahler">Mahler</a> in its juxtaposition of the sublime and the trivial", and with its four movements showing again an anticipation of progressive tonality, each ascending by a <a href="/wiki/Major_third" title="Major third">major third</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-145"><a href="#cite_note-145">[136]</a></sup> Other anticipations of Mahler (who was born in 1860) can be found in the two "military" Op. 50 piano studies of 1859 <i>Capriccio alla soldatesca</i> and <i>Le tambour bat aux champs (The drum beats the retreat)</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-146"><a href="#cite_note-146">[137]</a></sup> as well as in certain of the miniatures of the 1861 <i>Esquisses</i>, Op. 63.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-147"><a href="#cite_note-147">[138]</a></sup> The bizarre and unclassifiable <i>Marcia funebre, sulla morte d'un Pappagallo</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Funeral_march" title="Funeral march">Funeral march</a> on the death of a parrot</i>, 1859), for three <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bassoon" title="Bassoon">bassoon</a> and voices, described by Kenneth Hamilton as "<a href="/wiki/Dead_Parrot_sketch" title="Dead Parrot sketch">Monty-Pythonesque</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-148"><a href="#cite_note-148">[139]</a></sup> is also of this period.</p>

<p>The <i>Esquisses</i> of 1861 are a set of highly varied miniatures, ranging from the tiny 18-bar no. 4, <i>Les cloches (The Bells)</i>, to the strident <a href="/wiki/Tone_cluster" title="Tone cluster">tone clusters</a> of no. 45, <i>Les diablotins (The Imps)</i>, and closing with a further evocation of church bells in no. 49, <i>Laus Deo (Praise God).</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-149"><a href="#cite_note-149">[140]</a></sup> They were preceded in publication by Alkan's deceptively titled <i>Sonatine</i>, Op. 61, in 'classical' format, but a work of "ruthless economy [which] although it plays for less than twenty minutes ... is in every way a major work."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-150"><a href="#cite_note-150">[141]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two of Alkan's substantial works from this period are musical paraphrases of literary works. <i>Salut, cendre du pauvre</i>, Op. 45 (1856), follows a section of the poem <i>La Mélancolie</i> by <a href="/wiki/Gabriel-Marie_Legouv%C3%A9" title="Gabriel-Marie Legouvé">Gabriel-Marie Legouvé</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-151"><a href="#cite_note-151">[142]</a></sup> while <i>Super flumina Babylonis</i>, Op. 52 (1859), is a blow-by-blow recreation in music of the emotions and prophecies of <a href="/wiki/Psalm_137" title="Psalm 137">Psalm 137</a> ("By the waters of Babylon ..."). This piece is prefaced by a French version of the psalm which is believed to be the sole remnant of Alkan's Bible translation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-152"><a href="#cite_note-152">[143]</a></sup> Alkan's lyrical side was displayed in this period by the five sets of <i>Chants</i> inspired by Mendelssohn, which appeared between 1857 and 1872, as well as by a number of minor pieces.</p>
<p>Alkan's publications for organ or pédalier commenced with his <i>Benedictus</i>, Op. 54 (1859). In the same year he published a set of very spare and simple preludes in the eight <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_mode" title="Gregorian mode">Gregorian modes</a> (1859, without opus number), which, in Smith's opinion, "seem to stand outside the barriers of time and space", and which he believes reveal "Alkan's essential spiritual modesty."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-153"><a href="#cite_note-153">[144]</a></sup> These were followed by pieces such as the <i>13 Prières (Prayers)</i>, Op. 64 (1865), and the <i>Impromptu sur le Choral de <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Luther</a> "<a href="/wiki/A_Mighty_Fortress_Is_Our_God" title="A Mighty Fortress Is Our God">Un fort rempart est notre Dieu</a>"</i> , op. 69 (1866).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-154"><a href="#cite_note-154">[145]</a></sup> Alkan also issued a book of 12 studies for the <a href="/wiki/Pedal_keyboard" title="Pedal keyboard">pedalboard</a> alone (no opus number, 1866) and the <i>Bombardo-carillon</i> for pedalboard duet (four feet) of 1872.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-155"><a href="#cite_note-155">[146]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alkan's return to the concert platform at his <i>Petits Concerts</i>, however, marked the end of his publications; his final work to be issued was the <i>Toccatina</i>, Op. 75, in 1872.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-156"><a href="#cite_note-156">[147]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-157"><a href="#cite_note-157">[n 10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception_and_legacy">Reception and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles-Valentin_Alkan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Reception and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Alkan had few followers;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-159"><a href="#cite_note-159">[n 11]</a></sup> however, he had important admirers, including Liszt, <a href="/wiki/Anton_Rubinstein" title="Anton Rubinstein">Anton Rubinstein</a>, Franck, and, in the early twentieth century, Busoni, <a href="/wiki/Egon_Petri" title="Egon Petri">Petri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kaikhosru_Shapurji_Sorabji" title="Kaikhosru Shapurji Sorabji">Sorabji</a>. Rubinstein dedicated his fifth piano concerto to him,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-160"><a href="#cite_note-160">[149]</a></sup> and Franck dedicated to Alkan his <i>Grand pièce symphonique</i> op. 17 for organ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-161"><a href="#cite_note-161">[150]</a></sup> Busoni ranked Alkan with Liszt, Chopin, Schumann and <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Brahms" title="Johannes Brahms">Brahms</a> as one of the five greatest composers for the piano since Beethoven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-162"><a href="#cite_note-162">[151]</a></sup> Isidor Philipp and Delaborde edited new printings of his works in the early 1900s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-163"><a href="#cite_note-163">[152]</a></sup> In the first half of the twentieth century, when Alkan's name was still obscure, Busoni and Petri included his works in their performances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-164"><a href="#cite_note-164">[153]</a></sup> Sorabji published an article on Alkan in his 1932 book <i>Around Music</i>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-165"><a href="#cite_note-165">[154]</a></sup> he promoted Alkan's music in his reviews and criticism, and his Sixth Symphony for Piano (<i>Symphonia claviensis</i>) (1975–76), includes a section entitled <i>Quasi Alkan</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-166"><a href="#cite_note-166">[155]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-167"><a href="#cite_note-167">[156]</a></sup> The English composer and writer <a href="/wiki/Bernard_van_Dieren" title="Bernard van Dieren">Bernard van Dieren</a> praised Alkan in an essay in his 1935 book, <i>Down Among the Dead Men</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-168"><a href="#cite_note-168">[157]</a></sup> and the composer <a href="/wiki/Humphrey_Searle" title="Humphrey Searle">Humphrey Searle</a> also called for a revival of his music in a 1937 essay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-169"><a href="#cite_note-169">[158]</a></sup> The pianist and writer <a href="/wiki/Charles_Rosen" title="Charles Rosen">Charles Rosen</a> however considers Alkan "a minor figure", whose only music of interest comes after 1850 as an extension of Liszt's techniques and of "the operatic techniques of Meyerbeer."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-170"><a href="#cite_note-170">[159]</a></sup></p>
<p>For much of the 20th century, Alkan's work remained in obscurity, but from the 1960s onwards it was steadily revived. <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Lewenthal" title="Raymond Lewenthal">Raymond Lewenthal</a> gave a pioneering extended broadcast on Alkan on <a href="/wiki/WBAI" title="WBAI">WBAI</a> radio in New York in 1963,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-171"><a href="#cite_note-171">[160]</a></sup> and later included Alkan's music in recitals and recordings. The English pianist <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Smith_(musician)" title="Ronald Smith (musician)">Ronald Smith</a> championed Alkan's music through performances, recordings, a biography and the Alkan Society of which he was president for many years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-172"><a href="#cite_note-172">[161]</a></sup> Works by Alkan have also been recorded by Jack Gibbons, <a href="/wiki/Marc-Andr%C3%A9_Hamelin" title="Marc-André Hamelin">Marc-André Hamelin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Latimer" title="Mark Latimer">Mark Latimer</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Ogdon" title="John Ogdon">John Ogdon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/H%C3%BCseyin_Sermet" title="Hüseyin Sermet">Hüseyin Sermet</a>, among many others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-173"><a href="#cite_note-173">[162]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Stevenson" title="Ronald Stevenson">Ronald Stevenson</a> has composed a piano piece <i>Festin d'Alkan</i> (referring to Alkan's Op. 39, no. 12)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-174"><a href="#cite_note-174">[163]</a></sup> and the composer <a href="/wiki/Michael_Finnissy" title="Michael Finnissy">Michael Finnissy</a> has also written piano pieces referring to Alkan, e.g. <i>Alkan-Paganini</i>, no. 5 of <i>The History of Photography in Sound</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-175"><a href="#cite_note-175">[164]</a></sup> Marc-André Hamelin's <i>Étude No. IV</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Perpetual_motion" title="Perpetual motion">moto perpetuo</a> study combining themes from Alkan's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symphony_for_Solo_Piano_(Charles-Valentin_Alkan)" title="Symphony for Solo Piano (Charles-Valentin Alkan)">Symphony</a>, Op. 39, no. 7, and Alkan's own perpetual motion étude, Op. 76, no. 3. It is dedicated to Averil Kovacs and François Luguenot, respectively activists in the English and French Alkan Societies. As Hamelin writes in his preface to this étude, the idea to combine these came from the composer <a href="/wiki/Alistair_Hinton" title="Alistair Hinton">Alistair Hinton</a>, the finale of whose Piano Sonata No. 5 (1994–95) includes a substantial section entitled "Alkanique".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-176"><a href="#cite_note-176">[165]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alkan's compositions for organ have been among the last of his works to be brought back to the repertoire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-177"><a href="#cite_note-177">[166]</a></sup> As to Alkan's pedal-piano works, due to a recent revival of the instrument, they are once again being performed as originally intended (rather than on an organ), such as by Italian pedal-pianist <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Prosseda" title="Roberto Prosseda">Roberto Prosseda</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-178"><a href="#cite_note-178">[167]</a></sup> and recordings of Alkan on the pedal piano have been made by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Dub%C3%A9_(musician)" title="Jean Dubé (musician)">Jean Dubé</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-179"><a href="#cite_note-179">[168]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Olivier_Latry" title="Olivier Latry">Olivier Latry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-180"><a href="#cite_note-180">[169]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles-Valentin_Alkan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Notes</b></p>
<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">
<ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-8"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-8">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Alkan sometimes added to his signature "aîné" (the elder), to differentiate himself from his four brothers, all of whom were active as professional musicians,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conway_2012.2C_223_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Conway_2012.2C_223-1">[1]</a></sup> and this suffix was occasionally used by his publishers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In his personal correspondence he frequently signed himself "Valentin Alkan", and he was known as Valentin to his acquaintances. As regards the hyphenation of "Charles-Valentin"; his name is found both with and without the hyphen, even during his lifetime. It is reported as being in his <i>acte de naissance</i> (birth certificate) both with hyphen<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and without.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Alkan himself sometimes used the hyphen in his published works – for example in his foreword to the Op. 31 <i>Preludes</i> – and sometimes not – his op. 1 appeared as by "C. V. Alkan".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Other forms also occur: the <i>Sonate de concert</i> Op. 47 has on its title-page "Ch:V<sup>in</sup> Alkan"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> (cf. the signature in the header illustration to this article); Alkan's signature in his letters is frequently in the form "C:V:Alkan".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Authorities on Alkan seem to use both hyphenated and unhyphenated forms, indiscriminately, even changing their opinion between books – compare, for example the titles of François-Sappey (1991) and François-Sappey and Luguenot (2013).</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-10"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-10">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Alkan's forenames are sometimes erroneously given as Charles-Henri Valentin (or Victorin) Morhange.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> It is possible that the use of 'Henri' may have arisen from a misunderstanding of the abbreviation "Ch." "Victorin" arose from a misprint in Sorabji's essay on Alkan in his 1932 book, "Around Music". (See <a class="external text" href="http://www.alkansociety.org/bulletin%2087/Bulletin%2087.pdf" rel="nofollow">Alkan Society Bulletin 87</a>, 5.)</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-16"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-16">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The apparent flexibility of surname may be related to the then relatively recent French law of 1807, which had required Jews to take up new surnames; this may have been a factor in the eventual decision of all of Alkan Morhange's children to use the surname Alkan, rather than Morhange. The name Alkan itself seems to derive from a surname common among Jews of the Moselle region, rather than from a Jewish first name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[13]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-50"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-50">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Delaborde's birth was registered under the name of his mother Lina Eraïm Miriam, aged 38, of Nantes (who remains unidentified), and an unnamed father. Some have sought significance in the fact that the child's surname, Delaborde, which it is presumed was taken from his foster-mother, was the maiden name of the mother of George Sand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[46]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-105"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-105">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Alkan specifies in his will the texts to be used, which include extracts from the books of <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Malachi" title="Book of Malachi">Malachi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Job" title="Book of Job">Job</a> and <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Lamentations" title="Book of Lamentations">Lamentations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[100]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-127"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-127">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">For example, in Alkan's Op. 39, no. 10, where, following a note of E-sharp, F-triple-sharp is used logically as the <a href="/wiki/Nonchord_tone" title="Nonchord tone">approach</a> to G-double-sharp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[121]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-131"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-131">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">It frequently featured in the programmes of <a href="/wiki/Harold_Bauer" title="Harold Bauer">Harold Bauer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adela_Verne" title="Adela Verne">Adela Verne</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-129"><a href="#cite_note-129">[123]</a></sup> Bauer also recorded the piece on a <a href="/wiki/Piano_roll" title="Piano roll">piano roll</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[124]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-134"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-134">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The opus numberings of Alkan's works are erratic, often in non-date order, duplicated or exhibiting gaps in their sequence.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-143"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-143">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Each of the op. 39 studies is in a key which is a <a href="/wiki/Perfect_fourth" title="Perfect fourth">perfect fourth</a> higher than the previous one; the first movement of the <i>Symphony</i>, study no. 4, is in <a href="/wiki/C_minor" title="C minor">C minor</a>, the second in <a href="/wiki/F_minor" title="F minor">F minor</a>, and so on. Neither the <i>Symphony</i> nor the <i>Concerto</i> therefore have a home <a href="/wiki/Tonality" title="Tonality">tonality</a>.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-157"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-157">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">As mentioned above, the Op. 76 is a republication of <i>Trois grandes études</i> from 1839, originally published without opus number.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-159"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-159">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The claim that <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Fanelli" title="Ernest Fanelli">Ernest Fanelli</a> was Alkan's pupil at the Conservatoire<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-158"><a href="#cite_note-158">[148]</a></sup> is mistaken, as Fanelli came to the Conservatoire in 1876, long after Alkan had left it.</span></li>
</ol>
