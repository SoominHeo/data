<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Shine-Dalgarno sequence</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>The <b>Shine-Dalgarno (SD) sequence</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribosomal_binding_site" title="Ribosomal binding site">ribosomal binding site</a> in bacterial and archaeal <a href="/wiki/Messenger_RNA" title="Messenger RNA">messenger RNA</a>, generally located around 8 bases <a href="/wiki/Upstream_and_downstream_(DNA)" title="Upstream and downstream (DNA)">upstream</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Start_codon" title="Start codon">start codon</a> AUG.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> The RNA sequence helps recruit the <a href="/wiki/Ribosome" title="Ribosome">ribosome</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Messenger_RNA" title="Messenger RNA">messenger RNA</a> (mRNA) to initiate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protein_synthesis" title="Protein synthesis">protein synthesis</a> by aligning the ribosome with the start codon.</p>
<p>The Shine-Dalgarno sequence exists both in <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a>. It is also present in some <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplast</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondrial" title="Mitochondrial">mitochondrial</a> transcripts. The six-base <a href="/wiki/Consensus_sequence" title="Consensus sequence">consensus sequence</a> is <b>AGGAGG</b>; in <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i>, for example, the sequence is AGGAGGU, while subsequence GAGG dominates in <i>E. coli</i> virus T4 early genes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (April 2016)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Shine-Dalgarno sequence was proposed by <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> scientists <a href="/wiki/John_Shine" title="John Shine">John Shine</a> (b. 1946) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lynn_Dalgarno&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lynn Dalgarno (page does not exist)">Lynn Dalgarno</a> (b. 1935).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recognition">Recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shine-Dalgarno_sequence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Translation_start_sites">Translation start sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shine-Dalgarno_sequence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Translation start sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Using a method developed by Hunt,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Shine and Dalgarno showed that the nucleotide tract at the 3' terminus of <i>E. coli</i> 16S ribosomal RNA (rRNA) is pyrimidine-rich and has the sequence -PyACCUCCUUA 3' OH.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (April 2016)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> They proposed that these ribosomal nucleotides recognize the complementary purine-rich sequence AGGAGGU, which is found upstream of the start codon AUG in a number mRNAs found in viruses that affect <i>E. coli</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> Many studies have confirmed that base pairing between the Shine-Dalgarno sequence in mRNA and the 3' end of 16S rRNA is of prime importance for initiation of translation by bacterial ribosomes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Given the complementary relationship between rRNA and the Shine-Dalgarno sequence in mRNA, it was proposed that the sequence at the 3'-end of the rRNA determines the capacity of the prokaryotic ribosome to translate a particular gene in an mRNA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Base pairing between the 3'-end of the rRNA and the Shine-Dalgarno sequence in mRNA is a mechanism by which the cell can distinguish between initiator AUGs and internal and/or out-of-frame AUG sequences. The degree of base pairing also plays a role in determining the rate of initiation at different AUG initiator codons.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Translation_termination">Translation termination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shine-Dalgarno_sequence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Translation termination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1973 Dalgarno and Shine proposed that in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eukaryotes" title="Eukaryotes">eukaryotes</a>, the 3'-end of the small 18S rRNA may play a role in the termination of protein synthesis by complementary base pairing with termination codons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This came from their observation that the 3' terminal sequences of 18S rRNA from <i>Drosophila melanogaster</i> , <i>Saccharomyces cerevisiae</i>, and rabbit cells are identical: GAUCAUUA -3'OH.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The conservation of this sequence between such distantly related eukaryotes implied that this nucleotide tract played an important role in the cell. Since this conserved sequence contained the complement of each of the three eukaryotic termination codons (UAA, UAG and UGA) it was proposed to have a role in the termination of protein synthesis in eukaryotes. A similar role for the 3' end of 16S rRNA in recognising termination triplets in <i>E.coli</i> was proposed in 1974 by Shine and Dalgarno on the basis of complementarity relationships between the 3'-terminal UUA-OH in 16S rRNA and <i>E.coli</i> termination codons.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In <a href="/wiki/F1_phage" title="F1 phage">F1 phage</a>, a class of viruses that infect bacteria, the sequence coding for the first few amino acids often contains termination triplets in the two unused reading frames.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (April 2016)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In a commentary on this paper, it was noted that complementary base pairing with the 3'-terminus of 16S rRNA might serve to abort peptide bond formation after out-of-phase initiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequence_and_protein_expression">Sequence and protein expression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shine-Dalgarno_sequence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sequence and protein expression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">Mutations</a> in the Shine-Dalgarno sequence can reduce or increase<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Translation_(biology)" title="Translation (biology)">translation</a> in prokaryotes. This change is due to a reduced or increased mRNA-ribosome pairing efficiency, as evidenced by the fact that compensatory mutations in the 3'-terminal 16S rRNA sequence can restore translation.</p>
