<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Schaffhausen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Schaffhausen was a city-state in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>; it is documented that it struck its own coins starting in 1045. It was then documented as <i>Villa Scafhusun</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_city_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB_city-3">[3]</a></sup> Around 1049 Count Eberhard von Nellenburg founded a Benedictine monastery which led to the development of a community. This community achieved independence in 1190. In 1330 the town lost not only all its lands but also its independence to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habsburgs" title="Habsburgs">Habsburgs</a>. In 1415 the Habsburg Duke <a href="/wiki/Frederick_IV,_Duke_of_Austria" title="Frederick IV, Duke of Austria">Frederick IV of Austria</a> sided with the <a href="/wiki/Antipope_John_XXIII" title="Antipope John XXIII">Antipope John XXIII</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Constance" title="Council of Constance">Council of Constance</a>, and was banned by the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Sigismund,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Sigismund, Holy Roman Emperor">Sigismund</a>. As a result of the ban and Frederick's need of money, Schaffhausen was able to buy its independence from the Habsburgs in 1418. The city allied with six of the Swiss confederates in 1454 and allied with a further two (<a href="/wiki/Canton_of_Uri" title="Canton of Uri">Uri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Unterwalden" title="Unterwalden">Unterwalden</a>) in 1479. Schaffhausen became a full member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Swiss_Confederation" title="Old Swiss Confederation">Old Swiss Confederation</a> in 1501.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_city_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB_city-3">[3]</a></sup> The first railroad came to Schaffhausen in 1857. In 1944 Schaffhausen suffered from a bombing raid by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Forces" title="United States Army Air Forces">United States Army Air Forces</a> planes that accidentally strayed from Germany into neutral Switzerland.</p>
<p>The cantonal constitution was written in 1876 and revised in 1895.</p>
<p>The distinctive coat of arms bears the <i>Schaffhauser Bock</i> (billy goat of Schaffhausen).</p>

<p>Schaffhausen is the northernmost canton of Switzerland and lies almost entirely on the right bank of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>, with only part of <a href="/wiki/Stein_am_Rhein" title="Stein am Rhein">Stein am Rhein</a> on the left bank. It lies west of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Constance" title="Lake Constance">Lake Constance</a> and has an area of 298Â km<sup>2</sup> (115Â sqÂ mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Much of the canton is productive agricultural land, with 134.4Â km<sup>2</sup> (51.9Â sqÂ mi) (about 45%) of the canton used for agriculture while an additional 128.7Â km<sup>2</sup> (49.7Â sqÂ mi) (about 43%) is wooded. Most of the rest of the canton, 31.8Â km<sup>2</sup> (12.3Â sqÂ mi) (about 10%), is developed, while only 3.8Â km<sup>2</sup> (1.5Â sqÂ mi) (1.3%) of the canton is unproductive (rivers, lakes or mountains).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The canton's territory is divided into three non-contiguous segments where German territory reaches the Rhine. The large central part, which includes the capital <a href="/wiki/Schaffhausen" title="Schaffhausen">Schaffhausen</a>, in turn separates the German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exclave" title="Exclave">exclave</a> of <a href="/wiki/B%C3%BCsingen_am_Hochrhein" title="BÃ¼singen am Hochrhein">BÃ¼singen am Hochrhein</a> from the rest of Germany. The small exclave of <a href="/wiki/R%C3%BCdlingen" title="RÃ¼dlingen">RÃ¼dlingen</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buchberg_SH" title="Buchberg SH">Buchberg</a> lies to the southwest, and the third part contains <a href="/wiki/Ramsen" title="Ramsen">Ramsen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stein_am_Rhein" title="Stein am Rhein">Stein am Rhein</a> to the east. With the exception of Vor der Brugg, part of Stein am Rhein, all three segments are separated from the rest of Switzerland by the Rhine.</p>
<p>The canton of Schaffhausen is bordered by the Swiss cantons of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canton_of_Zurich" title="Canton of Zurich">Zurich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thurgau" title="Thurgau">Thurgau</a>, as well as the German districts of <a href="/wiki/Waldshut_(district)" title="Waldshut (district)">Waldshut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schwarzwald-Baar-Kreis" title="Schwarzwald-Baar-Kreis">Schwarzwald-Baar-Kreis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Konstanz" title="Konstanz">Konstanz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-WÃ¼rttemberg">Baden-WÃ¼rttemberg</a>.</p>
<p>Most of the canton lies on a plateau dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Hoher_Randen" title="Hoher Randen">Hoher Randen</a>. The summit of this mountain is at 912Â m (2,992Â ft). The slopes of the mountain are gentle towards the south where it reaches the Rhine valley. Short and narrow valleys intersect these gentle slopes. The <a href="/wiki/Klettgau" title="Klettgau">Klettgau</a> is one such valley.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rhine_Falls" title="Rhine Falls">Rhine Falls</a> are the largest waterfalls in Europe and lie on the border of the cantons of Schaffhausen and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canton_of_Zurich" title="Canton of Zurich">ZÃ¼rich</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cantonal_government">Cantonal government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Schaffhausen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cantonal government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The legislature is the Cantonal Council (<i>Kantonsrat</i>) of Schaffhausen, which consists of 60 members elected proportionally every four years. Until 2008, it consisted of 80 members.</p>
<p>The executive branch is the Government Council (<i>Regierungsrat</i>), which consists of 5 members elected every four years.</p>

<p>Schaffhausen is a part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich_metropolitan_area" title="ZÃ¼rich metropolitan area">ZÃ¼rcher Wirtschaftsraum (Zurich economic region)</a> and the canton's economy is well integrated with that of the wider region.</p>
<p>Well-regarded white <a href="/wiki/Riesling" title="Riesling">Riesling</a> wine is grown here as well as several other varieties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The main industries, however, are the production of machinery and metal goods. There is also watch making and jewellery. Minor industrial branches are textiles, leather goods, glass, cement, paper and chemicals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> There is a brewery in the canton.</p>
<p>At <a href="/wiki/Rheinau,_Switzerland" title="Rheinau, Switzerland">Rheinau</a> there is a hydro electrical power plant generating electricity for the canton and for export. Major electricity customers are the chemical industry in <a href="/wiki/Rheinfelden" title="Rheinfelden">Rheinfelden</a> and the aluminium plant at <a href="/wiki/Neuhausen_am_Rheinfall" title="Neuhausen am Rheinfall">Neuhausen am Rheinfall</a>. The city of Schaffhausen also uses much of the electricity produced at Rheinau.</p>
<p>Schaffhausen lies on the busy <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zurich" title="Zurich">Zurich</a>-<a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a> rail line which is serviced by trains from both the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Federal_Railways" title="Swiss Federal Railways">Swiss Federal Railways</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Railways" title="German Railways">German Railways</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The largest companies are <a href="/wiki/Tyco_International" title="Tyco International">Tyco International</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tyco_Electronics" title="Tyco Electronics">Tyco Electronics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schweizerische_Industrie_Gesellschaft" title="Schweizerische Industrie Gesellschaft">SIG</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georg_Fischer_AG" title="Georg Fischer AG">Georg Fischer AG</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_Watch_Company" title="International Watch Company">International Watch Company</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cilag_AG&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cilag AG (page does not exist)">Cilag AG</a>.</p>
