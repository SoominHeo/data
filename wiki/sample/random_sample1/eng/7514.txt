<p><b>Charlene Marilynn D'Angelo Oliver</b> (born June 1, 1950, <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a>, California) is an American <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">R&amp;B</a> <a href="/wiki/Singing" title="Singing">singer</a> best known as <b>Charlene.</b> She is known mainly for her 1982 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pop_(music)" title="Pop (music)">popular</a> song "<a href="/wiki/I%27ve_Never_Been_to_Me" title="I've Never Been to Me">I've Never Been to Me</a>."</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_recording_career">Early recording career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charlene_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early recording career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She was born as Charlene Marilynn D'Angelo, which was later shortened to Charlene for her record label. In 1973, Charlene signed with <a href="/wiki/Motown" title="Motown">Motown</a> under the name "Charlene Duncan", and released her first (and unsuccessful) single "All That Love Went to Waste" in January 1974. Three years later, she released a second album, <i>It Ain't Easy Comin' Down,</i> on <a href="/wiki/Motown" title="Motown">Motown</a>'s Prodigal label. The release was credited to Charlene, although an album issued the same month had "Charlene Duncan" printed on the spine (and was, confusingly, titled <i>Charlene</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Another 1977 album, <i>Songs of Love,</i> was a repackaging of her song "<a href="/wiki/I%27ve_Never_Been_to_Me" title="I've Never Been to Me">I've Never Been to Me</a>", which omitted a spoken bridge. She released the single in 1977, but it had little success, reaching #97 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">pop chart</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Success_of_.22I.27ve_Never_Been_to_Me.22">Success of "I've Never Been to Me"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charlene_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Success of &quot;I've Never Been to Me&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Charlene recorded "<a href="/wiki/I%27ve_Never_Been_to_Me" title="I've Never Been to Me">I've Never Been to Me</a>" in 1976, and the single reached #97 on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> singles chart in 1977. When released again in 1982, the single reached #3 in the United States, and #1 by June 1982, in the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The song has been her only <a href="/wiki/Top_40" title="Top 40">Top 40</a> hit.</p>
<p>In 1982, Tampa, Florida, <a href="/wiki/Disc_jockey" title="Disc jockey">disc jockey</a> <a href="/wiki/Scott_Shannon" title="Scott Shannon">Scott Shannon</a>, then at <a href="/wiki/WRBQ-FM" title="WRBQ-FM">WRBQ-FM</a>, started playing the song at the behest of a girlfriend, by which time Charlene had lost her recording contract, moved to England and was working in a sweetshop in <a href="/wiki/Ilford" title="Ilford">Ilford</a> near London. Audience reaction was impressive, resulting in the song's second release with the original spoken bridge. The second release was a hit in Britain as well.</p>
<p>When the song was released in the US in 1976, Charlene's legal name was Charlene Duncan from her marriage to record producer Larry Duncan; by the time the song was released in 1982, her name was Charlene Oliver by her subsequent marriage to Englishman Jeff Oliver. The single reached the top ten of the US <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pop_(music)" title="Pop (music)">pop</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adult_contemporary" title="Adult contemporary">adult contemporary</a> charts that year, and the concurrent release of her album <i>I've Never Been to Me</i>, was also relatively successful. The single was one of the year's biggest hits, and the video for the song was filmed at <a href="/wiki/Blickling_Hall" title="Blickling Hall">Blickling Hall</a>, Norfolk, England, and Charlene wore the wedding dress that she got married in.</p>
<p>"I've Never Been to Me" was featured as the opening song to the 1994 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Priscilla,_Queen_of_the_Desert" title="The Adventures of Priscilla, Queen of the Desert">The Adventures of Priscilla, Queen of the Desert</a>.</i> The song is mimed onstage by two Sydney drag queens at the Imperial Hotel in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erskineville" title="Erskineville">Erskineville</a>, Sydney, Australia.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_recordings_and_releases">Subsequent recordings and releases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charlene_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Subsequent recordings and releases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following year, Charlene released another song, "Used to Be," a duet with <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Wonder" title="Stevie Wonder">Stevie Wonder</a> which the authors-composers of "I've Never Been to Me" also wrote and composed. But this did not reach the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pop_(music)" title="Pop (music)">pop</a> top 40 chart, and it only reached #46 on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pop_(music)" title="Pop (music)">pop</a> charts. Both records were ranked the third and fourth worst records of all time by Jimmy Guterman and Owen O'Donnell in their 1991 book, <i>The Worst Rock n Roll Records of All time.</i> The <i>Used to Be</i> album, which accompanied its titular single, was not as successful as her previous album.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1984, Charlene released another album, <i>Hit and Run Lover.</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motown_Records" title="Motown Records">Motown</a> attempted to revamp her image in order to appeal to new audiences by highlighting her in a segment of the film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Dragon" title="The Last Dragon">The Last Dragon</a></i>, which was under development by Motown's founder <a href="/wiki/Berry_Gordy" title="Berry Gordy">Berry Gordy</a>. She and other Motown artists, including <a href="/wiki/DeBarge" title="DeBarge">DeBarge</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanity_(performer)" title="Vanity (performer)">Vanity</a>, were strategically placed in the film to appeal to the <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a> craze of the time. Charlene's song "Fire" was used along with a music video depicting her as a goddess. Although the film managed to improve some of the other artists' notoriety, Charlene's appearance in the film did not catch on and she was dropped from the Motown label the following year.</p>
<p>Because of her one big hit, Charlene became known as a <a href="/wiki/One-hit_wonder" title="One-hit wonder">one-hit-wonder</a> in music. She was featured on <a href="/wiki/VH1" title="VH1">VH1</a>'s <i>100 Greatest One-hit Wonders</i> show, hosted by <a href="/wiki/William_Shatner" title="William Shatner">William Shatner</a> in 2002. Her ranking on the list was #75. In 2006, "<a href="/wiki/I%27ve_Never_Been_to_Me" title="I've Never Been to Me">I've Never Been to Me</a>" was released as one of the songs on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SingStar_Anthems" title="SingStar Anthems">SingStar Anthems</a></i>, one of the popular <a href="/wiki/SingStar" title="SingStar">SingStars</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Charlene has re-released the song in the form of a dance remix via download music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pandora_Radio" title="Pandora Radio">Pandora Radio</a> features a "Charlene" channel that includes songs such as: "Johnny Doesn't Live Here Anymore," "If I Could See Myself," "Can't We Try," "Hey Mama," and others.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attempted_career_revivals_and_most_recent_updates">Attempted career revivals and most recent updates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charlene_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Attempted career revivals and most recent updates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1985, the release of <i>The Last Dragon</i> through Motown brought together Motown signed talents. Along with artists such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanity_(performer)" title="Vanity (performer)">Vanity</a>, they mashed her in with a catchy pop song called "Fire." While the single was a fan favorite for die-hards, the song failed to make an impact in the musical charts. Charlene would have one last album released through Motown titled <i>The Sky's The Limit.</i> By this time, Motown was turning to artists that were offering up the New Jack Swing that would dominate the late 1980s and early 1990s, leaving Charlene and many Motown artists behind.</p>
<p>As of 2012, Charlene was residing in her native California with her family, and she was still recording. Under the name Charlene Oliver, she launched a website, where her new dance/club/house music could be downloaded,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and which included such new releases as "Broken Women," "California Dreamin' (Dance)," "Emotional Scars," "I Wanna Be A Woman," "I Was You," "I've Never Been To Me (Dance)," "Oh Cecilia," "Sea of Tranquility," "Spirit of Woman," "Symphony For A Broken Piano," "There I'll Be," "(Why Can't) Time Stand Still," and "Used to Be," the last of which featured Stevie Wonder.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 2012, Charlene revamped a new video, attached to her club mix of "I've Never Been To Me," which could be found such on media outlets such as <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a>. Charlene also wrote two books, which she also sold via her website.</p>
<p>In 2013, Charlene was scheduled to release a new album. The first single called "Heard You On The Radio," through Gotham Records became available on ITunes October 22, 2012.fd<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
