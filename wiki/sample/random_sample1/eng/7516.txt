<p><b>Shammurāmat</b> or <b>Sammuramat</b> was an <a href="/wiki/Queen_regnant" title="Queen regnant">empress regnant</a> of <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyria</a> between 811 and 808 BCE <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-1">[1]</a></sup> or from 809 to 792 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Syrian_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Syrian-2">[2]</a></sup> She was one of the first known women to rule an empire.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shammuramat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Shammuramat was a wife of King <a href="/wiki/Shamshi-Adad_V" title="Shamshi-Adad V">Shamshi-Adad V</a> and after he died in 811 BC, she ruled the <a href="/wiki/Neo-Assyrian_Empire" title="Neo-Assyrian Empire">Neo-Assyrian Empire</a> as its regent for five years until her son <a href="/wiki/Adad-nirari_III" title="Adad-nirari III">Adad-nirari III</a> came of age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-1">[1]</a></sup> Other chronologies suggest that her regency lasted from 809 to 792 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Syrian_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Syrian-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>She ruled at a time of political uncertainty, which is one of the possible explanations for why Assyrians may have accepted her rule (as normally a woman as ruler would have been unthinkable). In the city of Ashur, she had an obelisk built and inscribed, which read:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>Stele of Sammuramat, queen of Shamshi-Adad, King of the Universe, King of Assyria, Mother of Adad Nirari, King of the Universe, King of Assyria, Daughter-in-Law of Shalmaneser, King of the Four Regions of the World.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Her son was King <a href="/wiki/Adad-nirari_III" title="Adad-nirari III">Adad-nirari III</a> and her grandson was King <a href="/wiki/Shalmaneser_IV" title="Shalmaneser IV">Shalmaneser IV</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Monuments">Monuments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shammuramat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Monuments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Shammuramat's stela (memorial stone) has been found at <a href="/wiki/Assur" title="Assur">Assur</a>, while an inscription at <a href="/wiki/Nimrud" title="Nimrud">Nimrud</a> indicates that she was dominant there after the death of her husband and before the rule of her son.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Semiramis">Semiramis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shammuramat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Semiramis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The legendary <a href="/wiki/Semiramis" title="Semiramis">Semiramis</a> is usually considered a purely mythical figure; however, there is evidence in Assyrian records suggesting that she may, in fact, be a Greek reflection of Shammuramat. This identification is disputed. Another possibility is that she is given that title after death to reflect similarities with an earlier Sumerian deity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
