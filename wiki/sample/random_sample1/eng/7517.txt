<p><b>Battle of Shamkor</b> (<a href="/wiki/Georgian_language" title="Georgian language">Georgian</a>: <span lang="ka" xml:lang="ka">შამქორის ბრძოლა</span>) was fought on June 1, 1195 near the city of Shamkor, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arran_(Azerbaijan)" title="Arran (Azerbaijan)">Arran</a> (present day <a href="/wiki/%C5%9E%C9%99mkir" title="Şəmkir">Shamkir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a>), the <b>Battle of Shamkor</b> was a major victory won by the <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgian</a> army, commanded by <a href="/wiki/David_Soslan" title="David Soslan">David Soslan</a>, over the army of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eldiguzids_(Atabegs_of_Azerbaijan)" title="Eldiguzids (Atabegs of Azerbaijan)">Azerbaijani Atabeg</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Abu_Bakr_(Atabeg_of_Azerbaijan)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Abu Bakr (Atabeg of Azerbaijan) (page does not exist)">Abu Bakr</a>.</p>
<p>The battle was fought as part of several conflicts between the Atabeg State of Azerbaijan, also known as the <a href="/wiki/Ildeniz" title="Ildeniz">Ildenizid</a> State after its ruling dynasty, and Georgia. The consolidation of Ildenizid power, in the 1130s, coincided with a resurgence of military expansionism by the Georgian kings, whose territories intersected with Muslim <a href="/wiki/Shirvan" title="Shirvan">Shirvan</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arran_(Azerbaijan)" title="Arran (Azerbaijan)">Arran</a>.</p>
<p>The battle was preceded by a dynastic war (1191–1195) in the Ildenizid possessions. Victorious in power struggle, Nusrat al-Din Abu Bakr b. Pahlawan Muhammad (ruled 1195-1210) had his elder brother Kutlugh Inanch assassinated and forced the younger brother, Amir Mihran, to take refuge at the court of the latter’s brother-in-law, <a href="/wiki/Shirvanshah" title="Shirvanshah">Shirvanshah</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akhsitan_I_ibn_Minuchihr_III" title="Akhsitan I ibn Minuchihr III">Akhsitan I b. Manuchehr</a> (r. 1160-1196). The Shirvanshah together with Amir Mihran headed for <a href="/wiki/Tbilisi" title="Tbilisi">Tbilisi</a>, Georgia, and appealed for help to Queen <a href="/wiki/Tamar_of_Georgia" title="Tamar of Georgia">Tamar</a>, an official protector of Shirvan. Received with great honors at the Georgian court, they were given desired support, and the Georgian army led by Consort <a href="/wiki/David_Soslan" title="David Soslan">David Soslan</a> marched to Shirvan.</p>
<p>Abu-Bakr, reinforced by his client <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> <a href="/wiki/Emir" title="Emir">emirs</a>, met the enemy at the well-fortified city of <a href="/wiki/%C5%9E%C9%99mkir" title="Şəmkir">Shamkor</a> on June 1, 1195. David Soslan sent a relatively small force to break through the gates of the city, while he led the main <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgian</a> troops to raid deep in the enemy’s rear. However, poor roads and difficult landscape were setback for the Georgians, and the <a href="/wiki/Atabeg" title="Atabeg">Atabeg</a> defended the city for a while. Nevertheless, David Soslan’s maneuver proved to be decisive and Abu Bakr’s army was severely defeated. Shamkor was eventually captured by the Georgians who then chased the enemy’s soldiers up to the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ganja_(city)" title="Ganja (city)">Ganja</a> which in its turn fell to the victors.</p>
<p>The Georgians seized numerous prisoners and huge amount of booty, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khalif" title="Khalif">Khalif</a>'s standard, which Queen Tamar donated to the <a href="/wiki/Khakhuli_triptych" title="Khakhuli triptych">Icon of Our Lady of Khakhuli</a>. Shamkor and the surroundings were turned over to the Shirvanshah on terms of vassalage.</p>
<p>Following the battle, Abu Bakr retreated to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nakhichivan_Autonomous_Republic" title="Nakhichivan Autonomous Republic">Nakhichevan</a> and Amir Mihran was installed as an atabeg in Ganja, only to be poisoned the same year. As a result, Abu Bakr was able to return to the capital, leading to a new confrontation with Georgia. Several Georgian inroads ensued, leaving several cities and towns in ruins between 1196 and 1209.</p>
