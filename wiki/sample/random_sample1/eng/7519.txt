<p>A <b>chandelier</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/ʃ/ 'sh' in 'shy'">ʃ</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/əl/ 'le' in 'bottle'">əl</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɪər/ 'ear' in 'near'">ɪər</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a decorative ceiling-mounted <a href="/wiki/Light_fixture" title="Light fixture">light fixture</a>, a distinct type of <a href="/wiki/Pendant_light" title="Pendant light">pendant light</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dic_chandelier_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-dic_chandelier-1">[1]</a></sup> Chandeliers are often ornate, and normally use <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb" title="Incandescent light bulb">incandescent light bulbs</a>, though some modern designs also use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lamps" title="Fluorescent lamps">fluorescent lamps</a> or even <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">LEDs</a>. Crystal chandeliers have more or less complex arrays of crystal prisms to illuminate a room with <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refracted</a> light. Chandeliers are often located in hallways, living rooms, dining rooms, and recently in bathrooms and bedrooms.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chandelier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The earliest candle chandeliers were used by the wealthy in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> times, this type of chandelier could be moved to different rooms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnston2011_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johnston2011-3">[3]</a></sup> From the 15th century, more complex forms of chandeliers, based on ring or crown designs, became popular decorative features in palaces and homes of nobility, clergy and merchants. Its high cost made the chandelier a symbol of luxury and status.</p>
<p>By the early 18th century, ornate cast <a href="/wiki/Ormolu" title="Ormolu">ormolu</a> forms with long, curved arms and many candles were in the homes of many in the growing merchant class. <a href="/wiki/Neoclassicism" title="Neoclassicism">Neoclassical</a> motifs became an increasingly common element, mostly in cast metals but also in carved and gilded wood. Chandeliers made in this style also drew heavily on the aesthetic of ancient Greece and Rome, incorporating clean lines, classical proportions and mythological creatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Developments in <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glassmaking</a> later allowed cheaper production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead_crystal" title="Lead crystal">lead crystal</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Light_scattering" title="Light scattering">light scattering</a> properties of which quickly made it a popular addition to the form, leading to the crystal chandelier.</p>
<p>During the 18th century glass chandeliers were produced by Bohemiens and Venetian glassmakers who were both masters in the art of making chandeliers. Bohemian style was largely successful across Europe and its biggest draw was the chance to obtain spectacular light refraction due to facets and bevels of crystal prisms. As a reaction to this new taste Italian glass factories in <a href="/wiki/Murano" title="Murano">Murano</a> created new kinds of artistic light sources. Since <a href="/wiki/Murano_glass" title="Murano glass">Murano glass</a> was not suitable for faceting, typical work realized at the time in other countries where crystal was used, venetian glassmakers relied upon the unique qualities of their glass. Typical features of a Murano chandelier are the intricate arabeques of leaves, flowers and fruits that would be enriched by coloured glass, made possible by the specific type of glass used in Murano. This glass they worked with was so unique, as it was soda glass (famed for its extraordinary lightness) and was a complete contrast to all different types of glass produced in the world at that time. An incredible amount of skill and time was required to precisely twist and shape a chandelier. This new type of chandelier was called "ciocca" literally bouquet of flowers, for the characteristic decorations of glazed polychrome flowers. The most sumptuous of them consisted of a metal frame covered with small elements in blown glass, transparent or colored, with decorations of flowers, fruits and leaves, while simpler model had arms made with a unique piece of glass. Their shape was inspired by an original architectural concept: the space on the inside is left almost empty since decorations are spread all around the central support, distanced from it by the length of the arms. One of the common use of the huge Murano Chandeliers was the interior lighting of theatres and rooms in important palaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the mid-19th century, as <a href="/wiki/Gas_lighting" title="Gas lighting">gas lighting</a> caught on, branched ceiling fixtures called <i>gasoliers</i> (a portmanteau of gas and chandelier) were produced, and many candle chandeliers were converted. By the 1890s, with the appearance of <a href="/wiki/Electric_light" title="Electric light">electric light</a>, some chandeliers used both gas and electricity. As distribution of electricity widened, and supplies became dependable, electric-only chandeliers became standard. Another portmanteau word, <a href="/wiki/Electrolier" title="Electrolier">electrolier</a>, was formed for these, but nowadays they are most commonly called chandeliers. Some are fitted with bulbs shaped to imitate candle flames, for example those shown below in Epsom and Chatsworth, or with bulbs containing a <a href="/wiki/Gas-discharge_lamp#Low_pressure_discharge_lamps" title="Gas-discharge lamp">shimmering gas discharge</a>.</p>
<p>The world's largest English Glass chandelier,(Hancock Rixon &amp; Dunt and probably F. &amp; C. Osler)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> is located in the <a href="/wiki/Dolmabah%C3%A7e_Palace" title="Dolmabahçe Palace">Dolmabahçe Palace</a> in <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>. It has 750 lamps and weighs 4.5 tons. Dolmabahçe has the largest collection of British and <a href="/wiki/Baccarat_(company)" title="Baccarat (company)">Baccarat</a> crystal chandeliers in the world, and one of the great staircases has balusters of Baccarat crystal.</p>
<p>More complex and elaborate chandeliers continued to be developed throughout the 18th and 19th centuries, but the widespread introduction of gas and electricity had devalued the chandelier's appeal as a status symbol.</p>
<p>Toward the end of the 20th century, chandeliers were often used as decorative focal points for rooms, and often did not illuminate.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Glossary_of_terms">Glossary of terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chandelier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Glossary of terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Adam_style" title="Adam style">Adam style</a></dt>
<dd>A neoclassical style, light, airy and elegant chandelier – usually English.</dd>
<dt>Arm</dt>
<dd>The light-bearing part of a chandelier also sometimes known as a branch.</dd>
<dt>Arm plate</dt>
<dd>The metal or wooden block placed on the stem, into which the arms slot.</dd>
<dt>Bag</dt>
<dd>A bag of crystal drops formed by strings hanging from a circular frame and looped back into the center underneath, associated especially with early <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> crystal and regency style crystal chandeliers.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Baluster" title="Baluster">Baluster</a></dt>
<dd>A turned wood or moulded stem forming the axis of a chandelier, with alternating narrow and bulbous parts of varying widths.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Bead</dt>
<dd>A glass drop with a hole drilled right through.</dd>
</dl>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Bob%C3%A8che" title="Bobèche">Bobèche</a></b> A dish fitted just below the candle nozzle, designed to catch drips of wax. Also known as a drip pan.</p>
<p><b>Branch</b> Another name for the light-bearing part of a chandelier, also known as an arm.</p>
<p><b>Candelabra</b> Not to be confused with chandeliers, <a href="/wiki/Candelabra" title="Candelabra">candelabras</a> are candlesticks, usually branched, designed to stand on tables, or if large, the floor.</p>
<p><b>Candlebeam</b> A cross made from two wooden beams with one or more cups and prickets at each end for securing candles.</p>
<p><b>Candle nozzle</b> The small cup into which the end of the candle is slotted</p>
<p><b>Canopy</b> An inverted shallow dish at the top of a chandelier from which festoons of beads are often suspended, lending a flourish to the top of the fitting.</p>
<p><b>Cage</b> An arrangement where the central stem supporting arms and decorations is replaced by a metal structure leaving the centre clear for candles and further embellishments.</p>
<p><b>Corona</b> Another term for crown-style chandelier</p>
<p><b>Crown</b> A circular chandelier reminiscent of a crown, usually of gilded metal or brass, and often with upstanding decorative elements.</p>
<p><b>Crystal</b> Glass with a chemical content that gives it special qualities of clarity, resonance and softness, making it especially suitable for cutting. For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead_crystal" title="Lead crystal">lead crystal</a>.</p>
<p><b>Drip pan</b> The dish fitted just below the candle nozzle, designed to catch drips of wax. Know also as a <a href="/wiki/Bob%C3%A8che" title="Bobèche">bobèche</a>.</p>
<p><b>Drop</b> A small piece of glass usually cut into one of many shapes and drilled at one end so that it can be hung from the chandelier with a brass pin. A chain drop is drilled at both ends so that a series can be hung together to form a necklace or festoon.</p>
<p><b>Dutch</b> Also known as Flemish, a style of brass chandelier with a bulbous baluster and arms curving down around a low hung ball.</p>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Festoon" title="Festoon">Festoon</a></b> An arrangement of glass drops or beads draped and hung across or down a glass chandelier, or sometimes a piece of solid glass shaped into a swag. Also known as a garland.</p>
<p><b>Finial</b> The final flourish at the very bottom of the stem. Some Venetian glass chandeliers have little finials hanging from glass rings on the arms.</p>
<p><b>Hoop</b> A circular metal support for arms, usually on a regency-styles or other chandelier with glass pieces. Also known as a ring</p>
<p><b>Montgolfière chandelier</b> Chandelier with shape of "montgolfière", the early French hot air balloon</p>
<p><b>Moulded</b> The process by which a glass piece is shaped by being blown into a mould</p>
<p><b>Neoclassical Style chandelier</b> Glass chandelier featuring many delicate arms, spires and strings of ovals rhomboids or octagons.</p>
<p><b>Prism</b> A straight, many-sided drop</p>
<p><b>Regency Style chandelier</b> A larger chandelier with a multitude of drops. Above a hoop rise strings of beads that diminish in size and attach at the top to form a canopy. A bag, with concentric rings of pointed glass, forms a waterfall beneath. The stem is usually completely hidden.</p>
<p><b>Soda glass</b> A type of glass used typically in Venetian glass chandeliers. Soda glass remains "plastic" for longer when heated, and can therefore be shaped into elegant curving leaves and flowers. Refracts light poorly and is normally fire polished.</p>
<p><b>Spire</b> A tall spike of glass, round in section or flat sided. To which arms and decorative elements may be attached, made from wood, metal or glass.</p>
<p><b>Tent</b> A tent shaped structure on the upper part of a glass chandelier where necklaces of drops attach at the top to a canopy and at the bottom to a larger ring.</p>
<p><b>Venetian</b> A glass from the island of <a href="/wiki/Murano" title="Murano">Murano</a>, Venice but usually used to describe any chandelier in Venetian style.</p>
<p><b>Waterfall</b> or <b>Wedding Cake</b> Concentric rings of icicle drops suspended beneath the hoop or plate.</p>
