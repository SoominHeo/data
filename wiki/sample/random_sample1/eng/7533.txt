<p><b>Sunoco</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical" title="Petrochemical">petrochemical</a> manufacturer headquartered in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newtown_Square" title="Newtown Square">Newtown Square</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, United States, formerly known as <b>Sun Company Inc.</b> (1886–1920 and 1976–1998) and <b>Sun Oil Co.</b> (1920–1976). Sunoco is one of the largest gasoline distribution companies in the United States, with Sunoco brand gasoline being sold in over 4,700 outlets spanning 26 states,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retail_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-retail-3">[3]</a></sup> just over a third hosting convenience stores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Since 2012, Sunoco has been a wholly owned subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Energy_Transfer_Partners" title="Energy Transfer Partners">Energy Transfer Partners</a>, based in <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>.</p>
<p>Sunoco is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fortune_100" title="Fortune 100">Fortune 100</a> Company. In 2011 it was ranked as the third largest company by revenue in Pennsylvania, after <a href="/wiki/AmerisourceBergen" title="AmerisourceBergen">AmerisourceBergen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Comcast" title="Comcast">Comcast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2011, the company decided to exit the <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">oil refinery</a> business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nixon_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nixon-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, the Sunoco brand was licensed for the <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> retail gas station operations of <a href="/wiki/Suncor_Energy" title="Suncor Energy">Suncor Energy</a> until 2010. Following Suncor's acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Petro-Canada" title="Petro-Canada">Petro-Canada</a>, all Canadian Sunoco outlets were converted to Petro-Canada branding.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunoco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1800s_to_1900s:_founding_and_growth">1800s to 1900s: founding and growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunoco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1800s to 1900s: founding and growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The integrated oil company now known as Sunoco began as The Peoples Natural Gas Company in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Pennsylvania" title="Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania">Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania</a>. In 1886, its partners – <a href="/wiki/The_Pew_Charitable_Trusts" title="The Pew Charitable Trusts">Joseph Newton Pew</a>, Philip Pisano and Edward O. Emerson – decided to expand their gas business with a stake in the new oil discoveries in <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>. Four years later, the growing enterprise became the Sun Oil Company of Ohio. Sun Oil diversified quickly, active in production and distribution of oil as well as processing and marketing refined products. By 1901, the company was incorporated in <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> as Sun Company and turned its interest to the new <a href="/wiki/Spindletop" title="Spindletop">Spindletop</a> field in <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>. Pew's sons, <a href="/wiki/J._Howard_Pew" title="J. Howard Pew">J. Howard Pew</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_N._Pew,_Jr." title="Joseph N. Pew, Jr.">Joseph N. Pew, Jr.</a> would take over the company after their father's death.</p>

<p>With a growing portfolio of oil fields and refineries in hand, Sun opened its first service station in <a href="/wiki/Ardmore,_Pennsylvania" title="Ardmore, Pennsylvania">Ardmore, Pennsylvania</a> in 1920. The name changed back to Sun Oil Company in 1922 and, in 1925, Sun became a publicly traded company on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a>. Sunoco ranked 39th among United States corporations in the value of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> production contracts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Sun expanded internationally following the war. Its first Canadian refinery was built in 1953 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarnia,_Ontario" title="Sarnia, Ontario">Sarnia, Ontario</a>, home to a burgeoning new petrochemical industry. Sun established a facility at Venezuela's <a href="/wiki/Lake_Maracaibo" title="Lake Maracaibo">Lake Maracaibo</a> in 1957, which produced over a billion barrels (160,000,000 m<sup>3</sup>) before the operation was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nationalized" title="Nationalized">nationalized</a> in 1975.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Custom_blending">Custom blending</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunoco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Custom blending">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Sunoco is perhaps best known to consumers for its "custom blending" pumps, an innovation that, beginning in 1956, allowed customers of Sunoco service stations to choose from several <a href="/wiki/Octane_rating" title="Octane rating">octane grades</a> through a single pump.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Sunoco stations offered as many as eight grades of "Custom Blended" gasolines from its "Dial A Grade" pumps ranging from subregular Sunoco 190 to Sunoco 260,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the latter a super-premium grade of 102 octane<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> that was advertised as the "highest octane pump gas"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and very popular with <a href="/wiki/V8_engine" title="V8 engine">V8</a>-powered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscle_Cars" title="Muscle Cars">Muscle Cars</a> of the 1960s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1960s_to_2000s:_acquisitions_and_branding">1960s to 2000s: acquisitions and branding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunoco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1960s to 2000s: acquisitions and branding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1967, Sun established its Great Canadian Oil Sands Limited facility in northern <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, Canada, to help unlock the estimated 300 billion barrels (48 km<sup>3</sup>) of recoverable oil in the <a href="/wiki/Athabasca_oil_sands" title="Athabasca oil sands">Athabasca oil sands</a>.</p>
<p>In 1968, Sun Oil merged with <a href="/wiki/Tulsa,_Oklahoma" title="Tulsa, Oklahoma">Tulsa, Oklahoma</a>-based <b>Sunray DX Oil Company</b>, which refined and marketed gasoline under the <b>DX</b> brand in several midwestern states, and included several refineries including one in Tulsa that was operated by Sun until its sale in June 2009 to Holly Corporation of Dallas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This move expanded Sun's marketing area into the mid-continent region.</p>
<p>Sun Oil continued marketing its petroleum products under both the Sunoco and DX brands through the 1970s and into the 1980s. In the late 1980s, Sun began rebranding DX stations in the Midwest to the Sunoco brand and even introduced the high-octane Sunoco ULTRA 94 gasoline to stations in that region, but by the early 1990s, they pulled out of virtually all areas in the southeastern U.S. and west of the Mississippi, resulting in the closing and rebranding of service stations and jobbers to other brands in those areas, notably <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinclair_Oil" title="Sinclair Oil">Sinclair</a> in Oklahoma.</p>
<p>With increased diversification, Sun Oil Company was renamed Sun Company in 1976. In 1980, Sun acquired the U.S. oil and gas properties of <b>Texas Pacific Oil Company, Inc.</b>, a subsidiary of The <a href="/wiki/Seagram" title="Seagram">Seagram</a> Company, Ltd., for US$2.3 billion—the second largest acquisition in U.S. history to that date.</p>
<p>Through the 1980s, Sun developed oil interests in the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> and offshore <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and expanded its holdings in both oil and coal with additional U.S. business acquisitions. In 1983, consumers saw the arrival of Sunoco ULTRA 94, the market's highest octane unleaded gasoline. Then in 1988, Sun undertook a major restructuring to segregate its domestic oil and gas exploration and production business and the focus the company on its refining and marketing business. This led to the acquisition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_Refining_and_Marketing" title="Atlantic Refining and Marketing">Atlantic Refining and Marketing</a> (and, in effect, that company's <a href="/wiki/Convenience_store" title="Convenience store">convenience store</a> chain, <a href="/wiki/A-Plus" title="A-Plus">A-Plus</a>), including its Philadelphia refinery which was later merged with the former <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Oil" title="Gulf Oil">Gulf Oil</a> refinery next door that Sunoco acquired from <a href="/wiki/Chevron_Corporation" title="Chevron Corporation">Chevron</a>.</p>
<p>By the 1990s, Sun had departed the international exploration business and was fully dedicated to its branded products and services. In 1994 Sunoco acquired the Philadelphia <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chevron_Oil" title="Chevron Oil">Chevron Oil</a> refinery consolidating operations with its own adjacent which it had acquired with Atlantic. Sun sold its remaining interest in Canada's <a href="/wiki/Suncor_Energy" title="Suncor Energy">Suncor Energy</a> in 1995, but markets product from two refineries – one in <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Ohio" title="Toledo, Ohio">Toledo, Ohio</a>, and the other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarnia,_Ontario" title="Sarnia, Ontario">Sarnia, Ontario</a> – in joint ventures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1998, Sun Company, Inc. became Sunoco, Inc. In 2011 the Toledo facility was sold to PBF Energy.</p>
<p>By 1998, Sun had acquired Allied Signal, Inc. chemical plants. Allied Signal, Inc. being on the stock exchange sold half of the company to Honeywell, Inc. and the other half to Sunoco, Inc. The chemical portion of the business was called "Sunoco Chemicals, Inc."</p>
<p>In 2003, the <a href="/wiki/Speedway_LLC" title="Speedway LLC">Speedway SuperAmerica</a> chain of gas stations and convenience stores exited the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">Southeastern United States</a> and sold most of their operations to Sunoco. Shortly prior to this, Sunoco acquired many service stations from <b>Coastal Petroleum</b>, especially its stations in Florida. This has led to the reintroduction of the Sunoco brand to areas it pulled out of ten years earlier. Coincidentally, Speedway would later re-enter the Southeast with its purchase of <a href="/wiki/Hess_Corporation" title="Hess Corporation">Hess Corporation</a>'s downstream operations in 2014, mirroring that of Sunoco's return to the Southeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2004, Sunoco replaced <a href="/wiki/ConocoPhillips" title="ConocoPhillips">ConocoPhillips</a>' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/76_(Phillips_66)" title="76 (Phillips 66)">76</a> brand as the official fuel of <a href="/wiki/NASCAR" title="NASCAR">NASCAR</a>.</p>
<p>After ConocoPhillips abandoned the marketing of the <a href="/wiki/Mobil" title="Mobil">Mobil</a> brand name in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington,_DC" title="Washington, DC">Washington, DC</a>, area, Sunoco purchased these rights, and has since been converting Maryland and Virginia Mobil stations to the Sunoco brand, bringing the A-Plus convenience store with them – prior to this, these stations had convenience stores under the <a href="/wiki/Circle_K" title="Circle K">Circle K</a> or <a href="/wiki/On_the_Run_(convenience_store)" title="On the Run (convenience store)">On the Run</a> brands.</p>
<p>In September 2009, Sunoco announced the sale of its retail <a href="/wiki/Heating_oil" title="Heating oil">heating oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a> distribution business to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Superior_Plus_Corp&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Superior Plus Corp (page does not exist)">Superior Plus Corp</a> for $82.5 million in cash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2009, Sunoco announced it will idle and possibly permanently shut down its Eagle Point/Westville, NJ plant, laying off over 400 employees.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exit_from_refinery_business">Exit from refinery business</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunoco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Exit from refinery business">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In December 2010, Sunoco sold its refinery in <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Ohio" title="Toledo, Ohio">Toledo, Ohio</a> to <a href="/wiki/PBF_Energy" title="PBF Energy">PBF Energy</a> for <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>400,000,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nixon_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nixon-6">[6]</a></sup> Effective 6 September 2011, Sunoco announced that it would exit the crude oil refining business and seek to sell its Philadelphia and Marcus Hook refineries by mid-2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nixon_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nixon-6">[6]</a></sup> The company has said that its cost for exiting the refining business could be as high as <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$2.7 billion</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nixon_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nixon-6">[6]</a></sup> According to one report, the company had lost some <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>800,000,000 on refining operations since 2009;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-osullivansunoco_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-osullivansunoco-1">[1]</a></sup> an earlier report provided a figure of <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>772,000,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nixon_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nixon-6">[6]</a></sup> On December 1, 2011, Sunoco announced it would accelerate closure of the Marcus Hook facility, with a target completion in 90 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-osullivansunoco_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-osullivansunoco-1">[1]</a></sup> The Marcus Hook facility, founded in 1902 and covering 781 acres, was dedicated exclusively to the processing of <a href="/wiki/Sweet_crude_oil" title="Sweet crude oil">light sweet crude oil</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-osullivansunoco_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-osullivansunoco-1">[1]</a></sup> this processing focus combined with volatility in crude oil prices are considered contributing factors to both this refinery's closure and Sunoco's exit from the refinery business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-key2012pbj_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-key2012pbj-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Joint_venture_with_Carlyle_Group">Joint venture with Carlyle Group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunoco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Joint venture with Carlyle Group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After weeks of negotiations, it was announced that Sunoco would be forming a <a href="/wiki/Joint_venture" title="Joint venture">joint venture</a> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carlyle_Group" title="Carlyle Group">Carlyle Group</a>, allowing for the continuation of operations at the Philadelphia refinery, and saving over 800 jobs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The joint venture began in late 2012.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sale_to_Energy_Transfer_Partners_and_merger_with_Susser_Holdings">Sale to Energy Transfer Partners and merger with Susser Holdings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunoco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sale to Energy Transfer Partners and merger with Susser Holdings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2012, <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a>-based energy company <a href="/wiki/Energy_Transfer_Partners" title="Energy Transfer Partners">Energy Transfer Partners</a> purchased Sunoco, making Sunoco a wholly owned subsidiary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> As Energy Transfer Partners was more interested in Sunoco's pipeline and refining business that the company was originally planning on exiting, it led to speculation that Energy Transfer Partners would eventually spin-off Sunoco's retail business or sell it outright to a competitor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stripes_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stripes-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, Energy Transfer Partners announced its purchase of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Susser_Holdings_Corporation" title="Susser Holdings Corporation">Susser Holdings Corporation</a>, the parent company of <a href="/wiki/Stripes_Convenience_Stores" title="Stripes Convenience Stores">Stripes Convenience Stores</a>, and plans on merging it into Sunoco.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stripes_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stripes-15">[15]</a></sup> Upon completion of the merger, Sunoco will be placed into a <a href="/wiki/Limited_partnership" title="Limited partnership">limited partnership</a> called Susser Petroleum Partners L.P., in which Energy Transfer will have an ownership stake. The deal saw the arrival of the Sunoco brand in <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, where it replaced <a href="/wiki/Valero_Energy" title="Valero Energy">Valero</a> at the Stripes locations; Stripes and A-Plus remain separate brands. Also in 2014, Sunoco acquired <a href="/wiki/Aloha_Petroleum" title="Aloha Petroleum">Aloha Petroleum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015, Sunoco entered into an agreement to purchase MD America operations in east Texas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stripes_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stripes-15">[15]</a></sup> That same year, Sunoco became a <a href="/wiki/Subway_(restaurant)" title="Subway (restaurant)">Subway</a> franchisee by opening Subway at the North Midway Service Plaza on the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Turnpike" title="Pennsylvania Turnpike">Pennsylvania Turnpike</a> near <a href="/wiki/Bedford,_Pennsylvania" title="Bedford, Pennsylvania">Bedford, Pennsylvania</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exclusive_deals">Exclusive deals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunoco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Exclusive deals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to their sponsorship deal with <a href="/wiki/NASCAR" title="NASCAR">NASCAR</a>, Sunoco also has exclusive deals as the gasoline supplier at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Travel_plaza" title="Travel plaza">travel plazas</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Turnpike" title="Pennsylvania Turnpike">Pennsylvania Turnpike</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey_Turnpike" title="New Jersey Turnpike">New Jersey Turnpike</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_City_Expressway" title="Atlantic City Expressway">Atlantic City Expressway</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palisades_Parkway" title="Palisades Parkway">Palisades Parkway</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Turnpike" title="Delaware Turnpike">Delaware Turnpike</a>. In 2011, Sunoco acquired the rights to operate the nine plazas on the <a href="/wiki/Garden_State_Parkway" title="Garden State Parkway">Garden State Parkway</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and also signed a new contract to operate the 16 plazas on the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_Turnpike" title="Ohio Turnpike">Ohio Turnpike</a> beginning in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Also, the two newly renovated service areas on <a href="/wiki/Interstate_95_in_Maryland" title="Interstate 95 in Maryland">Interstate 95 in Maryland</a> - Chesapeake House and Maryland House - also offer Sunoco as their gas offerings. Sunoco also operates some of the filling stations on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Thruway" title="New York Thruway">New York Thruway</a>.</p>
<p>In addition, Sunoco also owns &amp; operates a station with an <a href="/wiki/A-Plus" title="A-Plus">A-Plus</a> <a href="/wiki/Convenience_store" title="Convenience store">convenience store</a> at <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_International_Airport" title="Pittsburgh International Airport">Pittsburgh International Airport</a>, as Sunoco has a very large market share in <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a>. Due to its large presence in <a href="/wiki/Western_Pennsylvania" title="Western Pennsylvania">Western Pennsylvania</a>, Sunoco is a longtime sponsor of the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a>, with an advertisement for Sunoco being present in left field at <a href="/wiki/PNC_Park" title="PNC Park">PNC Park</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sunoco became the official current fuel supplier of the IZOD <a href="/wiki/IndyCar_Series" title="IndyCar Series">IndyCar Series</a> (now the Verizon IndyCar Series) in mid-2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_record">Environmental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunoco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Environmental record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sunoco was the only oil company to initially sign the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coalition_for_Environmentally_Responsible_Economies" title="Coalition for Environmentally Responsible Economies">Coalition for Environmentally Responsible Economies</a> (Ceres) principal. However, as of March, 2016, Sunoco is not listed as a Ceres Coalition or Company Network member of Ceres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2000, Sunoco leaked 190,000 gallons of oil into the <a href="/wiki/John_Heinz_National_Wildlife_Refuge_at_Tinicum" title="John Heinz National Wildlife Refuge at Tinicum">John Heinz Wildlife Refuge</a> in Pennsylvania through a cracked pipe. Sunoco's safety systems did not detect the leak, which was reported by a hiker in the Wildlife Refuge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pennsylvania Senator <a href="/wiki/H._John_Heinz_III" title="H. John Heinz III">John Heinz</a>, for whom the John Heinz National Wildlife Refuge is named, was killed on April 4, 1991, when a Sun Company helicopter collided with an airplane carrying the Senator. (Sun Company is the predecessor of Sunoco). That accident also killed two children at the Merion Elementary School playground, into which debris from the two aircraft fell after the collision. The National Transportation Safety Board determined that the probable cause of the accident included poor judgment on the part of the captain of the Sun Company helicopter, and his failure to maintain safe separation from the Senator’s airplane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of March, 2016, records maintained by the United States Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration (PHMSA) show that Sunoco has experienced 257 hazardous liquids incidents since 2006. (PHMSA defines an "incident" as an event that involves a release of gas from a pipeline or of liquefied natural gas or gas from an LNG facility that results in a death, or injury necessitating hospitalization; or property damage of $50,000 or more). These 257 incidents resulted in $46,936,215 of property damage. In terms of number of incidents, Sunoco has the worst safety record of 1,518 active pipeline operators who report operating data to PHMSA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, Sunoco, along with other defendants, was sued by the Commonwealth of Pennsylvania, which alleged that Sunoco and the other defendants polluted the waters of the Commonwealth with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MTBE" title="MTBE">MTBE</a>, a gasoline additive. The Commonwealth also alleged that the defendants, including Sunoco, improperly accepted state funding to clean up spills which occurred prior to 1994, and which were therefore not covered by the fund.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_sponsor">As a sponsor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunoco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: As a sponsor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sunoco was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penske_Racing" title="Penske Racing">Penske Racing</a>'s sponsor of choice for many years, particularly in the SCCA <a href="/wiki/Trans-Am_Series" title="Trans-Am Series">Trans-Am Series</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Roger_Penske" title="Roger Penske">Roger Penske</a>'s Sunoco Camaros won the championship in 1968 and 1969. Sunoco also sponsored Penske Porsches in the SCCA <a href="/wiki/Can-Am" title="Can-Am">Can-Am</a> series, and won the <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis_500" title="Indianapolis 500">Indianapolis 500</a> in 1972 with Penske and driver <a href="/wiki/Mark_Donohue" title="Mark Donohue">Mark Donohue</a>.</p>
<p>In 1989, NASCAR team owner <a href="/wiki/Billy_Hagan" title="Billy Hagan">Billy Hagan</a> acquired sponsorship from Sunoco for four seasons. <a href="/wiki/Sterling_Marlin" title="Sterling Marlin">Sterling Marlin</a> drove the #94 Sunoco Ultra94 <a href="/wiki/Oldsmobile" title="Oldsmobile">Oldsmobile</a> during the 1989-90 seasons and <a href="/wiki/Terry_Labonte" title="Terry Labonte">Terry Labonte</a> for the 91-92 seasons.</p>
<p>In 2015, Sunoco became the Official Fuel of the NHRA.</p>
<p>In 2016, as part of the Daytona update coinciding with the <a href="/wiki/2016_Daytona_500" title="2016 Daytona 500">2016 Daytona 500</a>, a NASCAR version of the Toyota Camry with Sunoco as its main sponsor appears in <i><a href="/wiki/Real_Racing_3" title="Real Racing 3">Real Racing 3</a></i>.</p>
