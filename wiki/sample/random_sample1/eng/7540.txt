<p><b>Samantha Reed Smith</b> (June 29, 1972 – August 25, 1985) was an American schoolgirl, <a href="/wiki/List_of_peace_activists" title="List of peace activists">peace activist</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Child_actress" title="Child actress">child actress</a> from <a href="/wiki/Manchester,_Maine" title="Manchester, Maine">Manchester, Maine</a>, who became famous in the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> era <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. In 1982, Smith wrote a letter to the newly appointed <a href="/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="General Secretary of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union">CPSU General Secretary</a> <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Andropov" title="Yuri Andropov">Yuri Andropov</a> and received a personal reply with a personal invitation to visit the Soviet Union, which she accepted.</p>
<p>Smith attracted extensive media attention in both countries as a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goodwill_Ambassador" title="Goodwill Ambassador">Goodwill Ambassador</a>" and became known as "America's Youngest Ambassador" participating in peacemaking activities in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MoscowEmbassy_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-MoscowEmbassy-1">[1]</a></sup> She wrote a book about her visit to the Soviet Union and co-starred in the television series <i><a href="/wiki/Lime_Street_(TV_series)" title="Lime Street (TV series)">Lime Street</a></i>, before her death at the age of 13 in the <a href="/wiki/Bar_Harbor_Airlines_Flight_1808" title="Bar Harbor Airlines Flight 1808">Bar Harbor Airlines Flight 1808</a> plane crash.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_context">Historical context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samantha_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When Yuri Andropov succeeded <a href="/wiki/Leonid_Brezhnev" title="Leonid Brezhnev">Leonid Brezhnev</a> as leader of the Soviet Union in November 1982, the mainstream <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western</a> newspapers and magazines ran numerous front-page photographs and articles about him. Most coverage was negative and tended to give a perception of a new threat to the stability of the Western world. Andropov had been the Soviet Ambassador to <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1956_Hungarian_Revolution" title="1956 Hungarian Revolution">1956 Hungarian Revolution</a> and the Chairman of the <a href="/wiki/KGB" title="KGB">KGB</a> from 1967 to 1982; during his tenure, he was known in the West for <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact_invasion_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Warsaw Pact invasion of Czechoslovakia">crushing the Prague Spring</a> and the brutal suppression of dissidents, such as <a href="/wiki/Andrei_Sakharov" title="Andrei Sakharov">Andrei Sakharov</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aleksandr_Solzhenitsyn" title="Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn">Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn</a>. He began his tenure as Soviet leader by strengthening the powers of the <a href="/wiki/KGB" title="KGB">KGB</a>, and by suppressing dissidents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Andropov declared, "the struggle for human rights was a part of a wide-ranging imperialist plot to undermine the foundation of the Soviet state."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andrew_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Andrew-3">[3]</a></sup> Much international tension surrounded both Soviet and American efforts to develop weapons capable of being launched from satellites in orbit. Both governments had extensive research and development programs to develop such technology. However, both nations were coming under increasing pressure to disband the project. In America, president <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> came under pressure from a lobby of U.S. scientists and arms experts, while in Russia the government issued a statement that read, "To prevent the militarization of space is one of the most urgent tasks facing mankind".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time836_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-time836-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>During this period, large <a href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear_protests" title="Anti-nuclear protests">anti-nuclear protests</a> were taking place across <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, while the November 20, 1983, screening of <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a>'s post-<a href="/wiki/Nuclear_warfare" title="Nuclear warfare">nuclear war</a> dramatization <i><a href="/wiki/The_Day_After" title="The Day After">The Day After</a></i> became one of the most anticipated media events of the decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The two superpowers had by this point abandoned their strategy of <i><a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9tente" title="Détente">détente</a></i> and in response to the Soviet deployment of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS-20" title="SS-20">SS-20s</a>, Reagan moved to deploy <a href="/wiki/Cruise_missile" title="Cruise missile">cruise</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pershing_II" title="Pershing II">Pershing II</a> missiles to Europe. The Soviet Union's involvement in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_invasion_of_Afghanistan" title="Soviet invasion of Afghanistan">war in Afghanistan</a> was in its third year, a matter which was also contributing to international tension. In this atmosphere, on November 22, 1982, <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine published an issue with Andropov on the cover. When Smith viewed the edition, she asked her mother, "If people are so afraid of him, why doesn't someone write a letter asking whether he wants to have a war or not?". Her mother replied, "Why don't you?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samantha_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Smith was born on June 29, 1972, in the small town of <a href="/wiki/Houlton,_Maine" title="Houlton, Maine">Houlton, Maine</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>–<a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> border, to Jane Reed and Arthur Smith. At the age of five, she wrote a letter to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Elizabeth II of the United Kingdom">Queen Elizabeth II</a> in order to express her admiration to the monarch. When Smith had finished second grade in the spring of 1980, the family settled in <a href="/wiki/Manchester,_Maine" title="Manchester, Maine">Manchester, Maine</a>, where she attended Manchester Elementary School. Her father served as an instructor at <a href="/wiki/Ricker_College" title="Ricker College">Ricker College</a> in Houlton<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> before teaching literature and writing at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maine_at_Augusta" title="University of Maine at Augusta">University of Maine at Augusta</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time836_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-time836-4">[4]</a></sup> while her mother worked as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_worker" title="Social worker">social worker</a> with the Maine Department of Human Services.</p>
<p>In November 1982, when Smith was 10 years old, she wrote to <a href="/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="General Secretary of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Soviet leader</a> <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Andropov" title="Yuri Andropov">Yuri Andropov</a>, seeking to understand why the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union_%E2%80%93_United_States_relations" title="Soviet Union – United States relations">relations</a> between the Soviet Union and the United States were so tense:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Dear Mr. Andropov,</p>
<p>My name is Samantha Smith. I am ten years old. Congratulations on your new job. I have been worrying about Russia and the United States getting into a nuclear war. Are you going to vote to have a war or not? If you aren't please tell me how you are going to help to not have a war. This question you do not have to answer, but I would like to know why you want to conquer the world or at least our country. God made the world for us to live together in peace and not to fight.</p>
<p>Sincerely,</p>
<p>Samantha Smith<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-letter_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-letter-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Her letter was published in the Soviet newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Pravda" title="Pravda">Pravda</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Samantha was happy to discover that her letter had been published; however, she had not received a reply. She then sent a letter to the Soviet Union's Ambassador to the United States asking if Mr. Andropov intended to respond. On April 26, 1983, she received a response from Andropov:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Dear Samantha,</p>
<p>I received your letter, which is like many others that have reached me recently from your country and from other countries around the world.</p>
<p>It seems to me – I can tell by your letter – that you are a courageous and honest girl, resembling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Becky_Thatcher" title="Becky Thatcher">Becky</a>, the friend of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_characters_in_the_Tom_Sawyer_series#Tom_Sawyer" title="List of characters in the Tom Sawyer series">Tom Sawyer</a> in the famous book of your compatriot <a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">Mark Twain</a>. This book is well known and loved in our country by all boys and girls.</p>
<p>You write that you are anxious about whether there will be a nuclear war between our two countries. And you ask are we doing anything so that war will not break out.</p>
<p>Your question is the most important of those that every thinking man can pose. I will reply to you seriously and honestly.</p>
<p>Yes, Samantha, we in the Soviet Union are trying to do everything so that there will not be war on Earth. This is what every Soviet man wants. This is what the great founder of our state, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Vladimir Lenin</a>, taught us.</p>
<p>Soviet people well know what a terrible thing war is. Forty-two years ago, <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>, which strove for supremacy over the whole world, <a href="/wiki/Operation_Barbarossa" title="Operation Barbarossa">attacked our country</a>, burned and destroyed many thousands of our towns and villages, killed millions of Soviet men, women and children.</p>
<p>In that war, which ended with our victory, we were in <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">alliance with the United States</a>: together we fought for the liberation of many people from the Nazi invaders. I hope that you know about this from your history lessons in school. And today we want very much to live in peace, to trade and cooperate with all our neighbors on this earth — with those far away and those near by. And certainly with such a great country as the United States of America.</p>
<p>In America and in our country there are nuclear weapons — terrible weapons that can kill millions of people in an instant. But we do not want them to be ever used. That's precisely why the Soviet Union solemnly declared throughout the entire world that never — never — will it <a href="/wiki/No_first_use" title="No first use">use nuclear weapons first</a> against any country. In general we propose to discontinue further production of them and to proceed to the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_disarmament" title="Nuclear disarmament">abolition of all the stockpiles</a> on Earth.</p>
<p>It seems to me that this is a sufficient answer to your second question: 'Why do you want to wage war against the whole world or at least the United States?' We want nothing of the kind. No one in our country–neither workers, peasants, writers nor doctors, neither grown-ups nor children, nor members of the government–want either a big or 'little' war.</p>
<p>We want peace — there is something that we are occupied with: growing wheat, building and inventing, writing books and flying into space. We want peace for ourselves and for all peoples of the planet. For our children and for you, Samantha.</p>
<p>I invite you, if your parents will let you, to come to our country, the best time being this summer. You will find out about our country, meet with your contemporaries, visit an international children's camp – <a href="/wiki/Artek_(camp)" title="Artek (camp)">Artek</a> – on the sea. And see for yourself: in the Soviet Union, everyone is for peace and friendship among peoples.</p>
<p>Thank you for your letter. I wish you all the best in your young life.</p>
<p>Y. Andropov<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-letter_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-letter-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Media_circus" title="Media circus">media circus</a> ensued, with Smith being interviewed by <a href="/wiki/Ted_Koppel" title="Ted Koppel">Ted Koppel</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Carson" title="Johnny Carson">Johnny Carson</a>, among others, and with nightly reports by the major American networks. On July 7, 1983, she flew to Moscow with her parents, and spent two weeks as Andropov's guest. During the trip she visited <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leningrad" title="Leningrad">Leningrad</a> and spent time in <a href="/wiki/Artek_(camp)" title="Artek (camp)">Artek</a>, the main Soviet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Young_Pioneer_organization_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Young Pioneer organization of the Soviet Union">pioneer</a> camp, in the town of <a href="/wiki/Gurzuf" title="Gurzuf">Gurzuf</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimean Peninsula</a>. Smith wrote in her book that in Leningrad she and her parents were amazed by the friendliness of the people and by the presents many people made for them. Speaking at a Moscow press conference, she declared that the Russians were "just like us".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In Artek, Smith chose to stay with the Soviet children rather than accept the privileged accommodations offered to her. For ease of communication, teachers and children who spoke fluent English were chosen to stay in the building where she was lodged. Smith shared a dormitory with nine other girls, and spent her time there swimming, talking and learning Russian songs and dances. While there, she made many friends, including Natasha Kashirina from Leningrad, a fluent English speaker.</p>

Samantha Smith Alley in <a href="/wiki/Artek_(camp)" title="Artek (camp)">Artek Camp</a> "Morskoy"</div>

<p>Andropov, however, was unable to meet with her during her visit,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> although they did speak by telephone. It was later discovered that Andropov had become seriously ill and had withdrawn from the public eye during this time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Smith also received a phone call from Russian cosmonaut <a href="/wiki/Valentina_Tereshkova" title="Valentina Tereshkova">Valentina Tereshkova</a>, the first woman to orbit the Earth. However, not realizing with whom she was speaking, Samantha mistakenly hung up after only a brief conversation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Media followed her every step—photographs and articles about her were published by the main Soviet newspapers and magazines throughout her trip and after it. Smith became widely known to Soviet citizens and was well regarded by many of them. In the United States, the event drew suspicion and some regarded it as an "American-style public relations stunt".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Smith's return to the U.S. on July 22, 1983, was celebrated by the people of <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> with roses, a red carpet, and a limousine<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTBack_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTBack-17">[17]</a></sup> and her popularity continued to grow in her native country. Some critics at the time remained skeptical, believing Smith was unwittingly serving as an instrument of Soviet propaganda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTBack_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYTBack-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USA20_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-USA20-18">[18]</a></sup> In December 1983, continuing in her role as "America's Youngest Ambassador", she was invited to Japan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> where she met with the Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Yasuhiro_Nakasone" title="Yasuhiro Nakasone">Yasuhiro Nakasone</a> and attended the Children's International Symposium in <a href="/wiki/Kobe" title="Kobe">Kobe</a>. In her speech at the symposium, she suggested that Soviet and American leaders exchange granddaughters for two weeks every year, arguing that a president "wouldn't want to send a bomb to a country his granddaughter would be visiting".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kobe_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kobe-20">[20]</a></sup> Her trip inspired other exchanges of child goodwill ambassadors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> including a visit by the eleven-year-old Soviet child <a href="/wiki/Katya_Lycheva" title="Katya Lycheva">Katya Lycheva</a> to the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Later, Smith wrote a book called <i>Journey to the Soviet Union</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> whose cover shows her at Artek,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Samantha_Smith_Project_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Samantha_Smith_Project-24">[24]</a></sup> her favorite part of the Soviet trip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Smith pursued her role as a media celebrity when in 1984, she hosted a children's special for the <a href="/wiki/Disney_Channel" title="Disney Channel">Disney Channel</a> entitled <i>Samantha Smith Goes To Washington...Campaign '84</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The show covered politics, where Smith interviewed several candidates for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1984" title="United States presidential election, 1984">1984 presidential election</a>, including <a href="/wiki/George_McGovern" title="George McGovern">George McGovern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Jackson" title="Jesse Jackson">Jesse Jackson</a>. That same year she guest starred in <i><a href="/wiki/Charles_in_Charge" title="Charles in Charge">Charles in Charge</a></i> as Kim, alongside another celebrity guest star, <a href="/wiki/Julianne_McNamara" title="Julianne McNamara">Julianne McNamara</a>. Her fame resulted in Smith becoming the subject of <a href="/wiki/Stalking" title="Stalking">stalker</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_John_Bardo" title="Robert John Bardo">Robert John Bardo</a>, the man who would later go on to stalk and ultimately murder <i><a href="/wiki/My_Sister_Sam" title="My Sister Sam">My Sister Sam</a></i> actress <a href="/wiki/Rebecca_Schaeffer" title="Rebecca Schaeffer">Rebecca Schaeffer</a>. Bardo traveled to Maine in an attempt to meet Smith, but was stopped by police and returned home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1985 she co-starred with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wagner" title="Robert Wagner">Robert Wagner</a> in a television series called <i><a href="/wiki/Lime_Street_(TV_series)" title="Lime Street (TV series)">Lime Street</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samantha_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On August 25, 1985, Smith and her father were returning home aboard <a href="/wiki/Bar_Harbor_Airlines_Flight_1808" title="Bar Harbor Airlines Flight 1808">Bar Harbor Airlines Flight 1808</a> after filming a segment for <i>Lime Street</i>. While attempting to land at <a href="/wiki/Auburn/Lewiston_Municipal_Airport" title="Auburn/Lewiston Municipal Airport">Lewiston-Auburn Regional Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Auburn,_Maine" title="Auburn, Maine">Auburn, Maine</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Beechcraft_Model_99" title="Beechcraft Model 99">Beechcraft 99</a> commuter plane struck some trees 4,007 feet (1,221 m) short of the runway and crashed, killing all six passengers and two crew on board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Much speculation regarding the cause of the accident circulated afterwards. Accusations of foul play circulated widely in the Soviet Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> An investigation was undertaken in the United States and the official report—which did not show evidence of foul play—was made public. As stated in the report, the accident occurred at about 22:05 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Daylight_Time" title="Eastern Daylight Time">EDT</a>, the ground impact point located one mile (1.6 km) south-west of the airport, at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Samantha_Smith&amp;params=44_02_22_N_70_17_30_W_type:event"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">44°02′22″N</span> <span class="longitude">70°17′30″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">44.03944°N 70.29167°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">44.03944; -70.29167</span></span></span></a></span>. The report goes on to say, "The relatively steep flight path angle and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yaw,_pitch,_and_roll" title="Yaw, pitch, and roll">attitude</a> (the orientation of the aircraft relative to the horizon, direction of motion etc.) and speed of the airplane at ground impact precluded the occupants from surviving the accident."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The main point of the report was that it was a rainy night, the pilots were inexperienced, and an accidental, but not uncommon and not usually critical, ground <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> failure occurred.</p>
<p>Samantha Smith was mourned by about 1,000 people at her funeral in Augusta, Maine, and was eulogized in Moscow as a champion of peace. Attendees included <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wagner" title="Robert Wagner">Robert Wagner</a> and Vladimir Kulagin of the Soviet Embassy in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington</a>, who read a personal message of condolence from <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a>.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Everyone in the Soviet Union who has known Samantha Smith will forever remember the image of the American girl who, like millions of Soviet young men and women, dreamt about peace, and about friendship between the peoples of the United States and the Soviet Union".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>President Ronald Reagan sent his condolences to Smith's mother, in writing,</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Perhaps you can take some measure of comfort in the knowledge that millions of Americans, indeed millions of people, share the burdens of your grief. They also will cherish and remember Samantha, her smile, her <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">idealism</a> and unaffected sweetness of spirit."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The remains of Smith and her father were cremated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>

<p>Smith's contributions have been honored with a number of tributes by Russians and by the people of her home state of Maine. A monument to her was built in Moscow; "Samantha Smith Alley" in the Artek Young Pioneer camp was named after her in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The monument built to Smith was stolen by <a href="/wiki/Metal_theft" title="Metal theft">metal thieves</a> in 2003 following the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Dissolution of the Soviet Union">dissolution of the Soviet Union</a> in 1991. In 2003, <a href="/wiki/Voronezh" title="Voronezh">Voronezh</a> retiree Valentin Vaulin built a monument to her without any support from the government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The Soviet Union issued a <a href="/wiki/Commemorative_stamp" title="Commemorative stamp">commemorative stamp</a> with her likeness. In 1986, when Soviet astronomer <a href="/wiki/Lyudmila_Chernykh" title="Lyudmila Chernykh">Lyudmila Chernykh</a> discovered <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> 3147, she named it <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3147_Samantha" title="3147 Samantha">3147 Samantha</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The Danish composer <a href="/wiki/Per_N%C3%B8rg%C3%A5rd" title="Per Nørgård">Per Nørgård</a> wrote his 1985 viola concerto "Remembering Child" in memory of Smith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamond</a> found in Siberia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> a mountain in the former Soviet Union,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivar</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tulip" title="Tulip">tulips</a> and of <a href="/wiki/Dahlia" title="Dahlia">dahlias</a>, and an <a href="/wiki/Passenger_ship" title="Passenger ship">ocean vessel</a> have been named in Smith's honor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MoscowEmbassy_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-MoscowEmbassy-1">[1]</a></sup> In Maine, the first Monday in June of each year is officially designated as Samantha Smith Day by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_law" title="State law">state law</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> There is a bronze statue of Smith near the Maine State Museum in Augusta, which portrays Smith releasing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dove" title="Dove">dove</a> with a bear cub resting at her feet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The bear cub represents both Maine and Russia. Elementary schools in <a href="/wiki/Sammamish,_Washington" title="Sammamish, Washington">Sammamish, Washington</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/Jamaica,_Queens" title="Jamaica, Queens">Jamaica, Queens</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> have been named after Samantha. In October 1985, Smith's mother founded The Samantha Smith Foundation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> which fostered student exchanges between the United States and the Soviet Union (and, after December 1991, the ex-Soviet successor states) until it became dormant in the mid-1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USA20_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-USA20-18">[18]</a></sup> The Foundation was formally dissolved in 2014 after two decades of dormancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 1987 episode of the U.S. sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/The_Golden_Girls" title="The Golden Girls">The Golden Girls</a></i> entitled "<a href="/wiki/The_Golden_Girls_(season_3)" title="The Golden Girls (season 3)">Letter to Gorbachev</a>" draws inspiration from Smith's story. In addition, the 1987 film <i><a href="/wiki/Superman_IV:_The_Quest_for_Peace" title="Superman IV: The Quest for Peace">Superman IV: The Quest for Peace</a></i> included a scene where a boy writes Superman a letter to control the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_arms_race" title="Nuclear arms race">nuclear arms race</a>; according to <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Reeve" title="Christopher Reeve">Christopher Reeve</a>, this scene was also inspired by Smith's story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the mid-1980s, after Smith's death, a script was written for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_movie" title="Television movie">television movie</a> titled <i>The Samantha Smith Story</i> with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wagner" title="Robert Wagner">Robert Wagner</a> as producer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures_Television" title="Columbia Pictures Television">Columbia Pictures Television</a> and R.J. Wagner Productions were reported to have agreed to produce the film for <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a>, with Soviet company Sovin Film interested in co-producing it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Ultimately, Columbia Pictures Television decided not to film it due to lack of interest from any network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, Smith posthumously received the Peace Abbey Courage of Conscience Award for "helping to bring about better understanding between the peoples of the [USA and the USSR], and as a result, reduce the tension between the superpowers that were poised to engage in nuclear war".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The Peace Abbey has also proposed The Peace Literature Project in Honor of Samantha Smith "to educate students about peace and promote peace literature for school-age children in 50 selected pilot schools across the United States"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Samantha_Smith_Project_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Samantha_Smith_Project-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Elliott_Holt" title="Elliott Holt">Elliott Holt</a>'s 2013 novel <i><a href="/wiki/You_Are_One_of_Them" title="You Are One of Them">You Are One of Them</a></i>, uses the story of Samantha Smith as inspiration for a fictional character, Jennifer Jones. The book, characterized as literary fiction, was well received by critics for both literary style and page-turning intrigue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the 30th anniversary of the plane crash in 2015, the Maine State Museum opened a new exhibit of materials related to Samantha, including photographs of her time at the Artek camp, traditional Russian clothing she was given, and an issue of <i>Soviet Life</i> magazine with her on the cover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
