<p><b>Somerset County</b> is a <a href="/wiki/County_(United_States)" title="County (United States)">county</a> located in the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>. As of the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 census</a>, the population was 77,742.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QF_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-QF-1">[1]</a></sup> Its <a href="/wiki/County_seat" title="County seat">county seat</a> is <a href="/wiki/Somerset,_Pennsylvania" title="Somerset, Pennsylvania">Somerset</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR6_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR6-2">[2]</a></sup> The county was created on April 17, 1795, from part of <a href="/wiki/Bedford_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Bedford County, Pennsylvania">Bedford County</a> and named after <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.</p>
<p>Somerset County comprises the Somerset, PA <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micropolitan_Statistical_Area" title="Micropolitan Statistical Area">Micropolitan Statistical Area</a>, which is included in the <a href="/wiki/Johnstown,_Pennsylvania" title="Johnstown, Pennsylvania">Johnstown</a>-Somerset, PA <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combined_Statistical_Area" title="Combined Statistical Area">Combined Statistical Area</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Somerset_County,_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_of_Somerset_County">Formation of Somerset County</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Somerset_County,_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation of Somerset County">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Southwestern Pennsylvania began as a huge area called Cumberland County. As population increased, the area was split into smaller counties. Bedford County was formed from part from Cumberland in 1771 and is referred to as "Old Bedford County" and contained what are now 20 smaller counties. In 1773 part of Bedford County was split off to form Westmoreland County. In 1787 Bedford County was split in half with northern part becoming Huntingdon County and southern part remained as a smaller Bedford County. Somerset County was split off from western part Bedford County 17 April 1795. In 1804 the northern half of Somerset County was split off to form Cambria County. No further splits from Somerset County occurred since 1804.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_History">Early History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Somerset_County,_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>George Washington passed through the area of Somerset County in 1753 on a scouting expedition at the beginning of the French-Indian War.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The Forbes Road cuts through Somerset County. This 200-mile stretch from Carlisle to what is now Pittsburgh was created by Brigadier General John Forbes in the British Expedition of 1758 to Fort Duquesne. Forbes Ford was one of two great western land routes cut through the wilderness to create supply lines from the east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It was later the primary route of pioneers travelling to Ohio Country.</p>
<p>Fur trappers and hunters were first to stay in the region. The earliest permanent white settlement in what is now Somerset County is a region known as Turkeyfoot. People of "The Jersey Settlement" emigrated from Essex and Morris Counties, New Jersey about 1770.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Somerset_County_in_the_Modern_Day_News">Somerset County in the Modern Day News</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Somerset_County,_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Somerset County in the Modern Day News">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Somerset County gained worldwide attention in 2001 when a hijacked airliner, <a href="/wiki/United_Airlines_Flight_93" title="United Airlines Flight 93">United Airlines Flight 93</a>, crashed in <a href="/wiki/Stonycreek_Township,_Somerset_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Stonycreek Township, Somerset County, Pennsylvania">Stonycreek Township</a>, near the town of <a href="/wiki/Shanksville,_Pennsylvania" title="Shanksville, Pennsylvania">Shanksville</a> as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/September_11_Terrorist_Attacks" title="September 11 Terrorist Attacks">September 11 Terrorist Attacks</a>. The first confirmed report of the plane's crash came from Somerset County Airport as reported on <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Today_Show" title="The Today Show">The Today Show</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The most likely target of this flight was the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">U.S. Capitol</a> in Washington, D.C. The terrorists' plans for this plane were thwarted by the actions of the passengers and crew. Their bravery is honored and the crash site, which is the final resting place of the passengers and crew, is now protected as part of the <a href="/wiki/Flight_93_National_Memorial" title="Flight 93 National Memorial">Flight 93 National Memorial</a>, under the care of the National Park System. See also <a href="/wiki/USS_Somerset_(LPD-25)" title="USS Somerset (LPD-25)">USS <i>Somerset</i></a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a> warship which was named in commemoration of the Flight 93 tragedy.</p>
<p>In July 2002, Somerset County again made worldwide news when nine <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> miners were rescued from several hundred feet underground at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quecreek_Mine_Rescue" title="Quecreek Mine Rescue">Quecreek mine</a> after an intense multi-day struggle.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Somerset_County,_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Census_Bureau" title="U.S. Census Bureau">U.S. Census Bureau</a>, the county has a total area of 1,081 square miles (2,800 km<sup>2</sup>), of which 1,074 square miles (2,780 km<sup>2</sup>) is land and 6.6 square miles (17 km<sup>2</sup>) (0.6%) is water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR1-8">[8]</a></sup> Somerset County is one of the far southern counties of Pennsylvania, along its straight southern edge. The county borders Garrett and Allegany Counties in Maryland, and the Pennsylvania counties of Fayette, Westmoreland, Cambria, and Bedford.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Somerset_County,_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Somerset County along with <a href="/wiki/Garrett_County,_Maryland" title="Garrett County, Maryland">Garrett County</a> is one of the snowiest inhabited locations in the United States, with the highest elevations of the county averaging 150+ inches of snow each winter.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The county's elevation and general proximity to both the Great Lakes and the Atlantic Ocean causes snow from both Nor'easters and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_effect_snow" title="Lake effect snow">lake effect</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orographic_precipitation" title="Orographic precipitation">upslope</a> snow events to fall from late October through early April. Snow has been recorded in Somerset County in every month except July, although local lore has it that even July saw snow in 1816, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_year_without_a_summer" title="The year without a summer">the year without a summer</a>." <a href="/wiki/Mount_Davis_(Pennsylvania)" title="Mount Davis (Pennsylvania)">Mount Davis</a>, the highest natural point in the state of Pennsylvania at 3,213 feet (979 m), is located in the southern part of the County.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Somerset_County,_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Somerset County is situated along the eastern border of the <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Plateau" title="Allegheny Plateau">Allegheny Plateau</a> <a href="/wiki/United_States_physiographic_region" title="United States physiographic region">physiographic province</a>, which is characterized by gently <a href="/wiki/Fold_(geology)" title="Fold (geology)">folded</a> to flat-lying <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a> of middle to late <a href="/wiki/Paleozoic" title="Paleozoic">Paleozoic</a> age. The eastern border of the county is approximately at the <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Front" title="Allegheny Front">Allegheny Front</a>, a geological boundary between the Allegheny Plateau and the <a href="/wiki/Ridge-and-Valley_Appalachians" title="Ridge-and-Valley Appalachians">Ridge and Valley</a> Province (characterized by <a href="/wiki/Fold_(geology)" title="Fold (geology)">folded</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)" title="Fault (geology)">faulted</a> <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a> of early to middle Paleozoic age).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Stratigraphy" title="Stratigraphy">stratigraphic record</a> of sedimentary rocks within the county spans from the <a href="/wiki/Devonian" title="Devonian">Devonian</a> <a href="/wiki/Scherr_Formation" title="Scherr Formation">Scherr Formation</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvanian_(geology)" title="Pennsylvanian (geology)">Pennsylvanian</a> <a href="/wiki/Virgilian_series" title="Virgilian series">Monongahela Formation</a>. Most of these rocks are clastics (<a href="/wiki/Conglomerate_(geology)" title="Conglomerate (geology)">conglomerate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">shale</a>), and there is little or no <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> exposed at the surface. No <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Igneous_Rock" title="Igneous Rock">igneous</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metamorphic_Rock" title="Metamorphic Rock">metamorphic</a> rocks of any kind exist within the county.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structural_Geology" title="Structural Geology">Structurally</a>, Somerset County has many gentle <a href="/wiki/Fold_(geology)" title="Fold (geology)">folds</a>, the axes of which trend roughly north-northeast. <a href="/wiki/Syncline" title="Syncline">Synclines</a> include the Youghiogheny Syncline, New Lexington/Johnstown Syncline, Somerset Syncline, Berlin Syncline, and Wellersburg Syncline (called the George's Creek Syncline in Maryland). The southern end of Wilmore Syncline is at the town of Windber. <a href="/wiki/Anticline" title="Anticline">Anticlines</a> include the Laurel Hill Anticline, Centerville Dome, Boswell Dome, Negro Mountain Anticline, and an unnamed anticline between the Berlin and Wellersburg Synclines.</p>
<p>The primary mountains within the county are (from west to east) <a href="/wiki/Laurel_Hill_(Pennsylvania)" title="Laurel Hill (Pennsylvania)">Laurel Hill</a> (which forms part of the western border), <a href="/wiki/Negro_Mountain" title="Negro Mountain">Negro Mountain</a>, Meadow Mountain, <a href="/wiki/Savage_Mountain" title="Savage Mountain">Savage Mountain</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Mountain_(Pennsylvania)" title="Allegheny Mountain (Pennsylvania)">Allegheny Mountain</a>. Negro Mountain also includes <a href="/wiki/Mount_Davis_(Pennsylvania)" title="Mount Davis (Pennsylvania)">Mount Davis</a>, the highest peak in Pennsylvania. Each mountain trends northeast.</p>
<p>All of Somerset County lies far to the south of the <a href="/wiki/Glacial_boundary" title="Glacial boundary">glacial boundary</a>, and thus it was never glaciated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> However, during the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> epoch (the Ice Age), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periglacial" title="Periglacial">periglacial</a> processes dominated. Most of the county was most likely a <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> during the Pleistocene. <a href="/wiki/Patterned_ground" title="Patterned ground">Patterned ground</a> typical of tundra is still visible at <a href="/wiki/Mount_Davis_(Pennsylvania)" title="Mount Davis (Pennsylvania)">Mount Davis</a>, although it is somewhat obscured by vegetation.</p>
<p>The main drainages in southwestern Somerset county are the <a href="/wiki/Casselman_River" title="Casselman River">Casselman River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laurel_Hill_Creek" title="Laurel Hill Creek">Laurel Hill Creek</a> which flow into the <a href="/wiki/Youghiogheny_River" title="Youghiogheny River">Youghiogheny River</a> along the southwest border. In the northwest, <a href="/wiki/Stonycreek_River" title="Stonycreek River">Stonycreek River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shade_Creek" title="Shade Creek">Shade Creek</a>, and Quemahoning Creek (which flows into the <a href="/wiki/Quemahoning_Reservoir" title="Quemahoning Reservoir">Quemahoning Reservoir</a>) are tributaries of the <a href="/wiki/Conemaugh_River" title="Conemaugh River">Conemaugh River</a>. All these drainages are part of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> Watershed. In the southeast, <a href="/wiki/Wills_Creek_(North_Branch_Potomac_River)" title="Wills Creek (North Branch Potomac River)">Wills Creek</a> flows east into Bedford County and then into Maryland where it joins the <a href="/wiki/Potomac_River" title="Potomac River">Potomac River</a>. Also, the headwaters of the <a href="/wiki/Raystown_Branch_Juniata_River" title="Raystown Branch Juniata River">Raystown Branch of the Juniata River</a> are to the east of the town of Somerset. Both the Potomac and Juniata rivers are part of the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a> Watershed.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">Coal</a> fields exist within Somerset County. The coal is entirely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bituminous" title="Bituminous">bituminous</a>, and much of it has been mined or is being mined by <a href="/wiki/Surface_mining" title="Surface mining">Strip mining</a>. Most of the coal is within the Main Bituminous Field, which stretched north and west to adjacent counties and southward into Maryland and West Virginia. The rest is within the Georges Creek Field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are many abandoned mines in the county, and <a href="/wiki/Acid_mine_drainage" title="Acid mine drainage">acid mine drainage</a> is an environmental problem in many areas. Fishless streams exist as a result of the discharge from the abandoned mines. These include parts of the <a href="/wiki/Casselman_River" title="Casselman River">Casselman River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shade_Creek" title="Shade Creek">Shade Creek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stonycreek_River" title="Stonycreek River">Stonycreek River</a>, and Quemahoning Creek, as well as many of their tributaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are many small, deep <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> fields in the northwestern part of the county.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR2_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR2-19">[19]</a></sup> of 2000, there were 80,023 people, 31,222 households, and 22,042 families residing in the county. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 74 people per square mile (29/km²). There were 37,163 housing units at an average density of 35 per square mile (13/km²). The racial makeup of the county was 97.39% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">White</a>, 1.59% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Black</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">African American</a>, 0.08% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Native American</a>, 0.21% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Asian</a>, 0.01% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 0.31% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 0.40% from two or more races. 0.66% of the population were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Latino</a> of any race. 41.5% were of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_people" title="German people">German</a>, 10.4% <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a>, 7.4% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_people" title="Italian people">Italian</a>, 6.4% <a href="/wiki/Irish_people" title="Irish people">Irish</a>, 6.4% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish_people" title="Polish people">Polish</a> and 5.8% <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English</a> ancestry according to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Census_2000" title="Census 2000">Census 2000</a>.</p>
<p>There were 31,222 households out of which 29.40% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 58.30% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 8.50% had a female householder with no husband present, and 29.40% were non-families. 26.10% of all households were made up of individuals and 13.60% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.45 and the average family size was 2.95.</p>
<p>In the county, the population was spread out with 22.30% under the age of 18, 7.60% from 18 to 24, 27.80% from 25 to 44, 24.30% from 45 to 64, and 18.00% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 40 years. For every 100 females there were 99.80 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 98.90 males.</p>

The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combined_Statistical_Area" title="Combined Statistical Area">Combined Statistical Area</a> ranked 10th in the State of Pennsylvania and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Combined_Statistical_Areas" title="List of Combined Statistical Areas">130th most populous</a> in the United States with a population of 221,421.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Somerset_County,_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Mountain Playhouse in <a href="/wiki/Jennerstown,_Pennsylvania" title="Jennerstown, Pennsylvania">Jennerstown, Pennsylvania</a>, was one of the nation's first "summer-stock" theaters. The Mountain Playhouse has maintained a full schedule of live theater productions nightly from May through October each year for the last sixty years.</p>
<p>Laurel Arts is one of the few, full-service arts centers in rural Pennsylvania. Centered in Somerset borough, it serves all of Somerset County through two locations: one at the Philip Dressler Center for the Arts, and the second, an Education and Dance Center located in the Georgian Place. Founded in 1976, Laurel Arts is a 501(c)(3) that offers art classes, music lessons and dance classes, as well as hosting exhibits by local and regional artists throughout the year.</p>
<p>The Rockwood Opera House is located in Rockwood, Pennsylvania. The building is a historic landmark restored to reflect original design from 1890. Since reopening, Rockwood Opera House has hosted dinner theater since 2000. Productions vary from Tribute Artists of classic performers to community theater.</p>
