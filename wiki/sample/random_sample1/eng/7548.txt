<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Sebastian de Grazia</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Sebastian de Grazia</b> (1917- 2000) was a Pulitzer Prize–winning author. Born in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, he received his <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">bachelor's degree</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Doctorate" title="Doctorate">doctorate</a> in <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a> from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> he served in the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Strategic_Services" title="Office of Strategic Services">Office of Strategic Services</a>, predecessor to the <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">Central Intelligence Agency</a> as an analyst. In 1962-1988 he taught <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political philosophy</a> at <a href="/wiki/Rutgers_University" title="Rutgers University">Rutgers University</a>. He received the 1990 <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize_for_Biography_or_Autobiography" title="Pulitzer Prize for Biography or Autobiography">Pulitzer Prize for Biography or Autobiography</a> for his 1989 book <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Machiavelli_in_Hell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Machiavelli in Hell (page does not exist)">Machiavelli in Hell</a></i>. He is also the author of <i>The Political Community</i> (1948), <i>Errors of Psychotherapy</i> (1952), and <i>A Country with No Name</i> (1997).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leisure">Leisure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sebastian_de_Grazia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Leisure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>De Grazia has been described as the "father of leisure".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Of_Time,_Work,_and_Leisure" title="Of Time, Work, and Leisure">Of Time, Work, and Leisure</a></i> (1962) puts forward the idea that traditionally <a href="/wiki/Leisure" title="Leisure">leisure</a> was not a matter of recreation as much as of contemplation, of expanding one's awareness and understanding of the world, and that the social context of this understanding of leisure has to a large extent been lost, and with it the notion of leisure being the pursuit of philosophy. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
