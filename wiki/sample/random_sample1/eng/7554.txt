<p>A <b>substorm</b>, sometimes referred to as a <b>magnetospheric substorm</b> or an <b>auroral substorm</b>, is a brief disturbance in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetosphere" title="Earth's magnetosphere">Earth's magnetosphere</a> that causes energy to be released from the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetotail" title="Magnetotail">tail</a>" of the magnetosphere and injected into the high latitude ionosphere. Visually, a substorm is seen as a sudden brightening and increased movement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">auroral arcs</a>. Substorms were first described in qualitative terms by <a href="/wiki/Kristian_Birkeland" title="Kristian Birkeland">Kristian Birkeland</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAPE_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NAPE-1">[1]</a></sup> which he called polar elementary storms. <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Chapman_(mathematician)" title="Sydney Chapman (mathematician)">Sydney Chapman</a> used the term substorm about 1960 which is now the standard term. The morphology of aurora during of a substorm was first described by <a href="/wiki/Syun-Ichi_Akasofu" title="Syun-Ichi Akasofu">Syun-Ichi Akasofu</a> in 1964<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> using data collected during the <a href="/wiki/International_Geophysical_Year" title="International Geophysical Year">International Geophysical Year</a>.</p>
<p>Substorms<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> are distinct from <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">geomagnetic storms</a> in that the latter take place over a period of several days, are observable from anywhere on Earth, inject a large number of <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outer_radiation_belt" title="Outer radiation belt">outer radiation belt</a>, and occur once or twice a month during the maximum of the solar cycle and a few times a year during solar minimum. Substorms, on the other hand, take place over a period of a few hours, are observable primarily at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_regions" title="Polar regions">polar regions</a>, do not inject many particles into the radiation belt, and are relatively frequent — often occurring only a few hours apart from each other. Substorms can be more intense and occur more frequently during a geomagnetic storm when one substorm may start before the previous one has completed. The source of the magnetic disturbances observed at the Earth's surface during geomagnetic storms is the <a href="/wiki/Ring_current" title="Ring current">ring current</a>, whereas the sources of magnetic disturbances observed on the ground during substorms are electric currents in the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a> at high latitudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stern_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stern-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Substorms can cause magnetic field disturbances in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">auroral zones</a> up to a magnitude of 1000 <a href="/wiki/Tesla_(unit)" title="Tesla (unit)">nT</a>, roughly 2% of the total magnetic field strength in that region. The disturbance is much greater in space, as some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geosynchronous" title="Geosynchronous">geosynchronous</a> satellites have registered the magnetic field dropping to half of its normal strength during a substorm. The most visible indication of a substorm is an increase in the intensity and size of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">polar auroras</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stern_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stern-5">[5]</a></sup> Substorms can be divided into three phases: the growth phase, the expansion phase, and the recovery phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-image_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-image-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, the THEMIS satellite mission observed the dynamics of rapidly developing substorms, confirming the existence of giant magnetic ropes and witnessed small explosions in the outskirts of Earth's magnetic field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
