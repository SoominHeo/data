<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Classics</h1>

<p><b>Classics</b> or <b>classical studies</b> is the study of <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a>. This covers the ancient Mediterranean world, particularly <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Rome</a>. It encompasses the study of ancient languages, literature, philosophy, history, and archaeology. Traditionally in the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">West</a>, the study of the Greek and Roman classics was considered one of the cornerstones of the <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">humanities</a> and a necessary part of a rounded education. Since the 20th century, however, Classics is no longer a part of a typical elite education.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>Classics</i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjective</a> <i>classicus</i>, meaning "belonging to the highest class of citizens". The word was originally used to describe the members of the highest class in ancient Rome. By the 2nd century AD the word was used in literary criticism to describe writers of the highest quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ziolkowski07-17_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ziolkowski07-17-1">[1]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Aulus_Gellius" title="Aulus Gellius">Aulus Gellius</a>, in his <i>Attic Nights</i>, contrasts "classicus" and "proletarius" writers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> By the 6th century AD, the word had acquired a second meaning, referring to pupils at a school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ziolkowski07-17_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ziolkowski07-17-1">[1]</a></sup> Thus the two modern meanings of the word, referring both to literature considered to be of the highest quality, and to the standard texts used as part of a curriculum, both derive from Roman use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ziolkowski07-17_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ziolkowski07-17-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Catullus,_Itally.jpg"><img alt='Photograph of a bronze bust of a man.  It rests on a stone plinth, on which the words "Gaius Valerius Catullus 87 AC–54 AC" are written.' class="thumbimage" data-file-height="7360" data-file-width="4912" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Catullus%2C_Itally.jpg/220px-Catullus%2C_Itally.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Catullus%2C_Itally.jpg/330px-Catullus%2C_Itally.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Catullus%2C_Itally.jpg/440px-Catullus%2C_Itally.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Catullus,_Itally.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Roman poet Catullus was virtually unknown during the medieval period, in contrast to his modern popularity.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, classics and education were tightly intertwined; according to Jan Ziolkowski, there is no era in history in which the link was tighter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Medieval education taught students to imitate earlier classical models,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ziolkowski07-21_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ziolkowski07-21-4">[4]</a></sup> and Latin continued to be the language of scholarship and culture, despite the increasing difference between literary Latin and the vernacular languages of Europe during the period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ziolkowski07-21_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ziolkowski07-21-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Latin was hugely influential, however, Greek was barely studied, and Greek literature survived almost solely in Latin translation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ziolkowski07-22_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ziolkowski07-22-5">[5]</a></sup> The works of even major Greek authors such as Hesiod, whose names continued to be known by educated Europeans, were unavailable in the Middle Ages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ziolkowski07-22_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ziolkowski07-22-5">[5]</a></sup> Along with the unavailability of Greek authors, there were other differences between the classical canon known today and the works valued in the Middle Ages. <a href="/wiki/Catullus" title="Catullus">Catullus</a>, for instance, was almost entirely unknown in the medieval period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ziolkowski07-22_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ziolkowski07-22-5">[5]</a></sup> The popularity of different authors also waxed and waned throughout the period: <a href="/wiki/Lucretius" title="Lucretius">Lucretius</a>, popular during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carolingian_renaissance" title="Carolingian renaissance">Carolingian</a> period, was barely read in the twelfth century, while for Quintilian the reverse is true.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ziolkowski07-22_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ziolkowski07-22-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance">Renaissance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Renaissance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> led to the increasing study of both ancient literature and ancient history,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kristeller78-586_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kristeller78-586-6">[6]</a></sup> as well as a revival of classical styles of Latin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kristeller78-587_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kristeller78-587-7">[7]</a></sup> From the 14th century, first in Italy and then increasingly across Europe, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renaissance_Humanism" title="Renaissance Humanism">Renaissance Humanism</a>, an intellectual movement that "advocated the study and imitation of classical antiquity",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kristeller78-586_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kristeller78-586-6">[6]</a></sup> developed. Humanism saw a reform in education in Europe, introducing a wider range of Latin authors as well as bringing back the study of Greek language and literature to Western Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kristeller78-587_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kristeller78-587-7">[7]</a></sup> This reintroduction was initiated by <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">Petrarch</a> (1304–1374) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boccaccio" title="Boccaccio">Boccaccio</a> (1313–1375) who commissioned a Calabrian scholar to translate the Homeric poems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This humanist educational reform spread from Italy, in Catholic countries as it was adopted by the Jesuits, and in countries that became Protestant such as England, Germany, and the Low Countries, in order to ensure that future clerics were able to study the New Testament in the original language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kristeller_1978_590_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kristeller_1978_590-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neoclassicism">Neoclassicism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Neoclassicism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The late 17th and 18th centuries are the period in Western European literary history which is most associated with the classical tradition, as writers consciously adapted classical models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaminski_2007_57_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kaminski_2007_57-10">[10]</a></sup> Classical models were so highly prized that the plays of <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> were rewritten along neoclassical lines, and these "improved" versions were performed throughout the 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the beginning of the 18th century, the study of Greek became increasingly important relative to that of Latin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kristeller_1978_591_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kristeller_1978_591-12">[12]</a></sup> In this period <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johann_Winckelmann" title="Johann Winckelmann">Johann Winckelmann</a>'s claims for the superiority of the Greek visual arts influenced a shift in aesthetic judgements, while in the literary sphere, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G.E._Lessing" title="G.E. Lessing">G.E. Lessing</a> "returned Homer to the centre of artistic achievement".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In the United Kingdom, the study of Greek in schools began in the late-eighteenth century. The poet <a href="/wiki/Walter_Savage_Landor" title="Walter Savage Landor">Walter Savage Landor</a> claimed to have been one of the first English schoolboys to write in Greek during his time at <a href="/wiki/Rugby_School" title="Rugby School">Rugby School</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The nineteenth century saw the influence of the classical world, and the value of a classical education, decline,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> especially in the US, where the subject was often criticised for its elitism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> By the nineteenth century, little new literature was still being written in Latin – a practice which had continued as late as the eighteenth century – and a command of Latin declined in importance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kristeller_1978_590_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kristeller_1978_590-9">[9]</a></sup> Correspondingly, classical education from the nineteenth century onwards began to increasingly de-emphasise the importance of the ability to write and speak Latin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kristeller_1978_591_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kristeller_1978_591-12">[12]</a></sup> In the United Kingdom this process took longer than elsewhere. Composition continued to be the dominant classical skill in England until the 1870s, when new areas within the discipline began to increase in popularity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stray_1996_81_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stray_1996_81-17">[17]</a></sup> In the same decade came the first challenges to the requirement of Greek at the universities of Oxford and Cambridge, though it would not be finally abolished for another fifty years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stray_1996_83_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stray_1996_83-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though the influence of classics as the dominant mode of education in Europe and North America was in decline in the nineteenth century, the discipline was rapidly evolving in the same period. Classical scholarship was becoming more systematic and scientific, especially with the "new philology" created at the end of the eighteenth and beginning of the nineteenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rommel_2001_169_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rommel_2001_169-19">[19]</a></sup> Its scope was also broadening: it was during the nineteenth century that ancient history and classical archaeology began to be seen as part of Classics, rather than separate disciplines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stray_1996_81_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stray_1996_81-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century_to_present">20th century to present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 20th century to present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the 20th century, the study of classics became less common. In England, for instance, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">Oxford</a> and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge</a> universities stopped requiring students to have qualifications in Greek in 1920,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stray_1996_83_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stray_1996_83-18">[18]</a></sup> and in Latin at the end of the 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stray_1996_85_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stray_1996_85-20">[20]</a></sup> When the <a href="/wiki/National_Curriculum_(England,_Wales_and_Northern_Ireland)" title="National Curriculum (England, Wales and Northern Ireland)">National Curriculum</a> was introduced in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland in 1988, it did not mention the classics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stray_1996_85_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stray_1996_85-20">[20]</a></sup> By 2003, only about 10% of state schools in Britain offered any classical subjects to their students at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, the study of classics has not declined as fast elsewhere in Europe. In 2009, a review of <i>Meeting the Challenge</i>, a collection of conference papers about the teaching of Latin in Europe, noted that though there is opposition to the teaching of Latin in Italy, it is nonetheless still compulsory in most secondary schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sub-disciplines">Sub-disciplines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sub-disciplines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the most notable characteristics of the modern study of Classics is the diversity of the field. Although traditionally focused on ancient Greece and Rome, the study now encompasses the entire ancient Mediterranean world<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, thus expanding the studies to Northern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> as well as parts of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philology">Philology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Philology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Philology" title="Philology">Philology</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For the journal, see <a href="/wiki/Classical_Philology_(journal)" title="Classical Philology (journal)">Classical Philology (journal)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Philology is the study of language preserved in written sources; <b>classical philology</b> is thus concerned with understanding any texts from the classical period written in the classical languages of Latin and Greek.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The roots of classical philology lie in the Renaissance, as humanist intellectuals attempted to return to the Latin of the classical period, especially of <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and as scholars attempted to produce more accurate editions of ancient texts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Some of the principles of philology still used today developed during this period. For instance, the observation that if a manuscript could be shown to be a copy of an earlier extant manuscript, then it provides no further evidence of the original text, was made as early as 1489 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angelo_Poliziano" title="Angelo Poliziano">Angelo Poliziano</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Other philological tools took longer to be developed: the first statement, for instance, of the principle that a more difficult reading should be preferred over a simpler one, was in 1697 by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Leclerc_(theologian)" title="Jean Leclerc (theologian)">Jean Le Clerc</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The modern discipline of classical philology began in Germany at the turn of the nineteenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rommel_2001_169_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rommel_2001_169-19">[19]</a></sup> It was during this period that scientific principles of philology began to be put together into a coherent whole,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> in order to provide a set of rules by which scholars could determine which manuscripts were most accurate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> This "new philology", as it was known, centred around the construction of a genealogy of manuscripts, with which a hypothetical common ancestor, closer to the original text than any existing manuscript, could be reconstructed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeology">Archaeology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Archaeology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Classical_archaeology" title="Classical archaeology">Classical archaeology</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lion_Gate_Mykene_with_Wilhelm_D%C3%B6rpfeld_and_Heinrich_Schliemann.jpg"><img alt="Black and white photograph of the Lion Gate at Mycenae." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1142" data-file-width="884" height="284" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Lion_Gate_Mykene_with_Wilhelm_D%C3%B6rpfeld_and_Heinrich_Schliemann.jpg/220px-Lion_Gate_Mykene_with_Wilhelm_D%C3%B6rpfeld_and_Heinrich_Schliemann.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Lion_Gate_Mykene_with_Wilhelm_D%C3%B6rpfeld_and_Heinrich_Schliemann.jpg/330px-Lion_Gate_Mykene_with_Wilhelm_D%C3%B6rpfeld_and_Heinrich_Schliemann.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Lion_Gate_Mykene_with_Wilhelm_D%C3%B6rpfeld_and_Heinrich_Schliemann.jpg/440px-Lion_Gate_Mykene_with_Wilhelm_D%C3%B6rpfeld_and_Heinrich_Schliemann.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Classical archaeology is the oldest branch of archaeology,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> with its roots going back to <a href="/wiki/Johann_Joachim_Winckelmann" title="Johann Joachim Winckelmann">J.J. Winckelmann's</a> work on <a href="/wiki/Herculaneum" title="Herculaneum">Herculaneum</a> in the 1760s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Renfrew80-288_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Renfrew80-288-32">[32]</a></sup> It was not until the last decades of the 19th century, however, that classical archaeology became part of the tradition of Western classical scholarship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Renfrew80-288_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Renfrew80-288-32">[32]</a></sup> It was included as part of Cambridge University's <a href="/wiki/Classical_Tripos" title="Classical Tripos">Classical Tripos</a> for the first time after the reforms of the 1880s, though it did not become part of Oxford's <a href="/wiki/Literae_Humaniores" title="Literae Humaniores">Greats</a> until much later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stray_1996_83_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stray_1996_83-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The second half of the 19th century saw <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Schliemann" title="Heinrich Schliemann">Schliemann's</a> excavations of <a href="/wiki/Troy" title="Troy">Troy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mycenae" title="Mycenae">Mycenae</a>; the first excavations at <a href="/wiki/Olympia,_Greece" title="Olympia, Greece">Olympia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Delos" title="Delos">Delos</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Evans" title="Arthur Evans">Arthur Evans</a>' work in Crete, particularly on <a href="/wiki/Knossos" title="Knossos">Knossos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> This period also saw the foundation of important archaeological associations (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_Institute_of_America" title="Archaeological Institute of America">Archaeological Institute of America</a> in 1879),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> including many foreign archaeological institutes in Athens and Rome (the <a href="/wiki/American_School_of_Classical_Studies_at_Athens" title="American School of Classical Studies at Athens">American School of Classical Studies at Athens</a> in 1881, <a href="/wiki/British_School_at_Athens" title="British School at Athens">British School at Athens</a> in 1886, <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_in_Rome" title="American Academy in Rome">American Academy in Rome</a> in 1895, and <a href="/wiki/British_School_at_Rome" title="British School at Rome">British School at Rome</a> in 1900).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>More recently, classical archaeology has taken little part in the theoretical changes<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> in the rest of the discipline,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> largely ignoring the popularity of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Archaeology" title="New Archaeology">New Archaeology</a>" in the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> New Archaeology is still criticized by traditional minded scholars of classical archaeology despite a wide acceptance of its basic techniques.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_history">Art history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Art history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Art_history" title="Art history">Art history</a></div>
<p>Some art historians focus their study on the development of art in the classical world. Indeed, the art and architecture of Ancient Rome and Greece is very well regarded and remains at the heart of much of our art today. For example, Ancient Greek architecture gave us the Classical Orders: <a href="/wiki/Doric_order" title="Doric order">Doric</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ionic_order" title="Ionic order">Ionic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Corinthian_order" title="Corinthian order">Corinthian</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Parthenon" title="Parthenon">Parthenon</a> is still the architectural symbol of the classical world.</p>
<p>Greek sculpture is well known and we know the names of several Ancient Greek artists: for example, <a href="/wiki/Phidias" title="Phidias">Phidias</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_history">Ancient history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ancient history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With philology, archaeology, and art history, scholars seek understanding of the history and culture of a civilisation, through critical study of the extant literary and physical artefacts, in order to compose and establish a continual historic narrative of the Ancient World and its peoples. The task is difficult due to a dearth of physical evidence: for example, <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">Sparta</a> was a leading Greek <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">city-state</a>, yet little evidence of it survives to study, and what is available comes from <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>, Sparta's principal rival; likewise, the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> destroyed most evidence (cultural artefacts) of earlier, conquered civilizations, such as that of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Etruscans" title="Etruscans">Etruscans</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ancient_philosophy" title="Ancient philosophy">Ancient philosophy</a></div>
<p>The English word "philosophy" comes from the Greek word φιλοσοφία, meaning "love of wisdom", probably coined by Pythagoras. Along with the word itself, the discipline of philosophy as we know it today has its roots in ancient Greek thought, and according to Martin West "philosophy as we understand it is a Greek creation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Ancient philosophy was traditionally divided into three branches: logic, physics, and ethics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mann_1996_178_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mann_1996_178-40">[40]</a></sup> However, not all of the works of ancient philosophers fit neatly into one of these three branches. For instance, Aristotle's <i>Rhetoric</i> and <i>Poetics</i> have been traditionally classified in the West as "ethics", but in the Arabic world were grouped with logic; in reality, they do not fit neatly into either category.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mann_1996_178_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mann_1996_178-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the last decade of the eighteenth century, scholars of ancient philosophy began to study the discipline historically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Previously, works on ancient philosophy had been unconcerned with chronological sequence and with reconstructing the reasoning of ancient thinkers; with what Wolfgang-Ranier Mann calls "New Philosophy", this changed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception_studies">Reception studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Reception studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Classical_reception_studies" title="Classical reception studies">Classical reception studies</a></div>
<p>A relatively recent new discipline within the classics is "reception studies",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> which developed in the 1960s at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Konstanz" title="University of Konstanz">University of Konstanz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kallendorf07-02_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kallendorf07-02-44">[44]</a></sup> Reception studies is concerned with how students of classical texts have understood and interpreted them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kallendorf07-02_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kallendorf07-02-44">[44]</a></sup> As such, reception studies is interested in a two-way interaction between reader and text,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martindale07-298_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Martindale07-298-45">[45]</a></sup> taking place within a historical context.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though the idea of an "aesthetics of reception" was first put forward by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Robert_Jauss" title="Hans Robert Jauss">Hans Robert Jauss</a> in 1967, the principles of reception theory go back much earlier than this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martindale07-298_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Martindale07-298-45">[45]</a></sup> As early as 1920, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T.S._Eliot" title="T.S. Eliot">T.S. Eliot</a> wrote that "the past [is] altered by the present as much as the present is directed by the past";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Charles Martindale describes this as a "cardinal principle" for many versions of modern reception theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martindale07-298_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-Martindale07-298-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_Greece">Classical Greece</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Classical Greece">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Outline_of_ancient_Greece" title="Outline of ancient Greece">Outline of ancient Greece</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_ancient_Greece" title="Timeline of ancient Greece">Timeline of ancient Greece</a></div>
<p>Ancient Greece was the civilization belonging to the period of Greek history lasting from the <a href="/wiki/Archaic_Greece" title="Archaic Greece">Archaic period</a>, beginning in the eighth century BC, to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_conquest_of_Greece" title="Roman conquest of Greece">Roman conquest of Greece</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Corinth_(146_BC)" title="Battle of Corinth (146 BC)">Battle of Corinth</a> in 146 BC. The <a href="/wiki/Classical_Greece" title="Classical Greece">Classical period</a>, during the fifth and fourth centuries BC, has traditionally been considered the height of Greek civilisation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The Classical period of Greek history is generally considered to have begun with the <a href="/wiki/First_Persian_invasion_of_Greece" title="First Persian invasion of Greece">first</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Persian_invasion_of_Greece" title="Second Persian invasion of Greece">second Persian invasions of Greece</a> at the start of the Greco-Persian wars,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> and to have ended with the death of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>.</p>
<p>Classical Greek culture had a powerful influence on the Roman Empire, which carried a version of it to many parts of the Mediterranean region and Europe; thus Classical Greece is generally considered to be the seminal culture which provided the foundation of Western civilization.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mycenaean_Greek_language" title="Mycenaean Greek language">Mycenaean Greek language</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Koine_Greek" title="Koine Greek">Koine Greek</a></div>
<p>Ancient Greek is the historical stage in the development of the Greek language spanning the Archaic (c. 8th to 6th centuries BC), Classical (c. 5th to 4th centuries BC), and Hellenistic (c. 3rd century BC to 6th century AD) periods of ancient Greece and the ancient world. It is predated in the 2nd millennium BC by Mycenaean Greek. Its Hellenistic phase is known as Koine ("common") or Biblical Greek, and its late period mutates imperceptibly into Medieval Greek. Koine is regarded as a separate historical stage of its own, although in its earlier form it closely resembles Classical Greek. Prior to the Koine period, Greek of the classical and earlier periods included several regional dialects.</p>
<p>Ancient Greek was the language of Homer and of classical Athenian historians, playwrights, and philosophers. It has contributed many words to the vocabulary of English and many other European languages, and has been a standard subject of study in Western educational institutions since the Renaissance. Latinized forms of Ancient Greek roots are used in many of the scientific names of species and in other scientific terminology.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_literature" title="Ancient Greek literature">Ancient Greek literature</a></div>
<p>The earliest surviving works of Greek literature are <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epic poetry</a>. <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Odyssey" title="Odyssey">Odyssey</a></i> are the earliest to survive to us today, probably composed in the eighth century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> These early epics were oral compositions, created without the use of writing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Around the same time that the Homeric epics were composed, the <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">Greek alphabet</a> was introduced; the earliest surviving inscriptions date from around 750 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kirk-45_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kirk-45-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>European <a href="/wiki/Drama" title="Drama">drama</a> was invented in ancient Greece. Traditionally this was attributed to <a href="/wiki/Thespis" title="Thespis">Thespis</a>, around the middle of the sixth century BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> though the earliest surviving work of Greek drama is <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">Aeschylus</a>' tragedy <i><a href="/wiki/The_Persians" title="The Persians">The Persians</a></i>, which dates to 472 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIGEK-258_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-WIGEK-258-54">[54]</a></sup> Early Greek tragedy was performed by a chorus and two actors, but by the end of Aeschylus' life, a third actor had been introduced, either by him or by <a href="/wiki/Sophocles" title="Sophocles">Sophocles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIGEK-258_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-WIGEK-258-54">[54]</a></sup> The last surviving Greek tragedies are the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacchae" title="Bacchae">Bacchae</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Euripides" title="Euripides">Euripides</a> and Sophocles' <a href="/wiki/Oedipus_at_Colonus" title="Oedipus at Colonus">Oedipus at Colonus</a>, both from the end of the fifth century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Surviving Greek comedy begins later than tragedy; the earliest surviving work, <a href="/wiki/Aristophanes" title="Aristophanes">Aristophanes</a>' <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acharnians" title="Acharnians">Acharnians</a></i>, comes from 425 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Handley-355_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Handley-355-56">[56]</a></sup> However, comedy dates back as early as 486 BC, when the <a href="/wiki/Dionysia" title="Dionysia">Dionysia</a> added a competition for comedy to the much earlier competition for tragedy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Handley-355_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-Handley-355-56">[56]</a></sup> The comedy of the fifth century is known as <a href="/wiki/Old_Comedy" title="Old Comedy">Old Comedy</a>, and it comes down to us solely in the eleven surviving plays of Aristophanes, along with a few fragments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Handley-355_56-2"><a href="#cite_note-Handley-355-56">[56]</a></sup> Sixty years after the end of Aristophanes' career, the next author of comedies to have any substantial body of work survive is <a href="/wiki/Menander" title="Menander">Menander</a>, whose style is known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Comedy" title="New Comedy">New Comedy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two historians flourished during Greece's classical age: Herodotus and Thucydides. Herodotus is commonly called the father of history, and his "History" contains the first truly literary use of prose in Western literature. Of the two, Thucydides was the more careful historian. His critical use of sources, inclusion of documents, and laborious research made his History of the Peloponnesian War a significant influence on later generations of historians. The greatest achievement of the 4th century was in philosophy. There were many Greek philosophers, but three names tower above the rest: Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle. These have had a profound influence on Western society.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mythology_and_religion">Mythology and religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Mythology and religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religion_in_Ancient_Greece" title="Religion in Ancient Greece">Religion in Ancient Greece</a></div>
<p>Greek mythology is the body of myths and legends belonging to the ancient Greeks concerning their gods and heroes, the nature of the world, and the origins and significance of their own cult and ritual practices. They were a part of religion in ancient Greece. Modern scholars refer to the myths and study them in an attempt to throw light on the religious and political institutions of Ancient Greece and its civilization, and to gain understanding of the nature of myth-making itself.</p>
<p>Greek religion encompassed the collection of beliefs and rituals practiced in ancient Greece in the form of both popular public religion and cult practices. These different groups varied enough for it to be possible to speak of Greek religions or "cults" in the plural, though most of them shared similarities. Also, the Greek religion extended out of Greece and out to neighbouring islands.</p>
<p>Many Greek people recognized the major gods and goddesses: Zeus, Poseidon, Hades, Apollo, Artemis, Aphrodite, Ares, Dionysus, Hephaestus, Athena, Hermes, Demeter, Hestia and Hera; though philosophies such as Stoicism and some forms of Platonism used language that seems to posit a transcendent single deity. Different cities often worshipped the same deities, sometimes with epithets that distinguished them and specified their local nature.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy_2">Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_philosophy" title="Ancient Greek philosophy">Ancient Greek philosophy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Socrates_Louvre.jpg"><img alt="A bust of Socrates" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="600" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Socrates_Louvre.jpg/220px-Socrates_Louvre.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Socrates_Louvre.jpg/330px-Socrates_Louvre.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Socrates_Louvre.jpg/440px-Socrates_Louvre.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Socrates_Louvre.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

</div>
<p>The earliest surviving philosophy from ancient Greece dates back to the 6th century BC, when according to Aristotle <a href="/wiki/Thales_of_Miletus" title="Thales of Miletus">Thales of Miletus</a> was considered to have been the first Greek philosopher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Other influential pre-Socratic philosophers include <a href="/wiki/Pythagoras" title="Pythagoras">Pythagoras</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heraclitus" title="Heraclitus">Heraclitus</a>. The most famous and significant figures in classical Athenian philosophy, from the 5th to the 3rd centuries BC, are <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">Socrates</a>, his student <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, who studied at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plato%27s_Academy" title="Plato's Academy">Plato's Academy</a> before founding his own school, known as the <a href="/wiki/Lyceum_(Classical)" title="Lyceum (Classical)">Lyceum</a>. Later Greek schools of philosophy, including the <a href="/wiki/Cynicism_(philosophy)" title="Cynicism (philosophy)">Cynics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stoics" title="Stoics">Stoics</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epicureans" title="Epicureans">Epicureans</a>, continued to be influential after the Roman annexation of Greece, and into the post-Classical world.</p>
<p>Greek philosophy dealt with a wide variety of subjects, including political philosophy, <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontology" title="Ontology">ontology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a>, as well as disciplines which are not today thought of as part of philosophy, such as biology and rhetoric.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_Rome">Classical Rome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Classical Rome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Ancient Rome</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Culture_of_Ancient_Rome" title="Culture of Ancient Rome">Culture of Ancient Rome</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_2">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Praeneste_fibula.JPG"><img alt="Photograph of a gold brooch" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="851" data-file-width="1449" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Praeneste_fibula.JPG/220px-Praeneste_fibula.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Praeneste_fibula.JPG/330px-Praeneste_fibula.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Praeneste_fibula.JPG/440px-Praeneste_fibula.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Praeneste_fibula.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Praeneste fibula is believed to bear the oldest known Latin inscription. The inscription means "Manius made me for Numerius".</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The language of ancient Rome was Latin, a member of the <a href="/wiki/Italic_languages" title="Italic languages">Italic family of languages</a>. The earliest surviving inscription in Latin comes from the 7th century BC, on a <a href="/wiki/Praeneste_fibula" title="Praeneste fibula">brooch from Palestrina</a>. Latin from between this point and the early 1st century BC is known as <a href="/wiki/Old_Latin" title="Old Latin">Old Latin</a>. Most surviving Latin literature is <a href="/wiki/Classical_Latin" title="Classical Latin">Classical Latin</a>, from the 1st century BC to the 2nd century AD. Latin then evolved into <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">Late Latin</a>, in use during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_antiquity" title="Late antiquity">late antique period</a>. Late Latin survived long after the end of classical antiquity, and was finally replaced by written Romance languages around the 9th century AD. Along with literary forms of Latin, there existed various vernacular dialects, generally known as <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">Vulgar Latin</a>, in use throughout antiquity. These are mainly preserved in sources such as graffiti and the <a href="/wiki/Vindolanda_tablets" title="Vindolanda tablets">Vindolanda tablets</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature_2">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Latin_literature" title="Latin literature">Latin literature</a></div>
<p>The earliest surviving Latin authors, writing in Old Latin, include the playwrights <a href="/wiki/Plautus" title="Plautus">Plautus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Terence" title="Terence">Terence</a>. Much of the best known and most highly thought of Latin literature comes from the classical period, with poets such as <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horace" title="Horace">Horace</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>; historians such as <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a>; orators such as <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a>; and philosophers such as <a href="/wiki/Seneca_the_Younger" title="Seneca the Younger">Seneca the Younger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lucretius" title="Lucretius">Lucretius</a>. Late Latin authors include many Christian writers such as <a href="/wiki/Lactantius" title="Lactantius">Lactantius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tertullian" title="Tertullian">Tertullian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ambrose" title="Ambrose">Ambrose</a>; non-Christian authors, such as the historian <a href="/wiki/Ammianus_Marcellinus" title="Ammianus Marcellinus">Ammianus Marcellinus</a>, are also preserved.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_2">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timeline_of_ancient_Rome" title="Timeline of ancient Rome">Timeline of ancient Rome</a></div>
<p>According to legend, the city of Rome was founded in 753 BC;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> in reality, there had been a settlement on the site since around 1000 BC, when the <a href="/wiki/Palatine_Hill" title="Palatine Hill">Palatine Hill</a> was settled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> The city was originally ruled by kings, first Roman, and then Etruscan – according to Roman tradition, the first Etruscan king of Rome, Tarquinius Priscus, ruled from 616 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Over the course of the 6th century BC, the city expanded its influence over the entirety of <a href="/wiki/Latium" title="Latium">Latium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Around the end of the 6th century – traditionally in 510 BC – the kings of Rome were driven out, and the city became a republic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>Around 387 BC, Rome was sacked by the Gauls following the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Allia" title="Battle of the Allia">Battle of the Allia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> It soon recovered from this humiliating defeat, however, and in 381 the inhabitants of <a href="/wiki/Tusculum" title="Tusculum">Tusculum</a> in Latium were made Roman citizens. This was the first time Roman citizenship was extended in this way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Rome went on to expand its area of influence, until by 269 the entirety of the Italian peninsula was under Roman rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Soon afterwards, in 264, the <a href="/wiki/First_Punic_War" title="First Punic War">First Punic War</a> began; it lasted until 241.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Second_Punic_War" title="Second Punic War">Second Punic War</a> began in 218, and by the end of that year, the Carthaginian general <a href="/wiki/Hannibal" title="Hannibal">Hannibal</a> had invaded Italy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The war saw Rome's worst defeat to that point at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cannae" title="Battle of Cannae">Cannae</a>; the largest army Rome had yet put into the field was wiped out, and one of the two consuls leading it was killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> However, Rome continued to fight, annexing much of Spain<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> and eventually defeating Carthage, ending her position as a major power and securing Roman preeminence in the Western Mediterranean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_of_the_classical_world">Legacy of the classical world</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Legacy of the classical world">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The classical languages of the Ancient Mediterranean world influenced every European language, imparting to each a learned vocabulary of international application. Thus, Latin grew from a highly developed cultural product of the Golden and Silver eras of Latin literature to become the <i>international lingua franca</i> in matters diplomatic, scientific, philosophic and religious, until the 17th century. Long before this, Latin had evolved into <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">the Romance languages</a> and Ancient Greek into <a href="/wiki/Modern_Greek" title="Modern Greek">Modern Greek</a> and its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_Greek_dialects" title="Modern Greek dialects">dialects</a>. In the specialised science and technology vocabularies, the influence of <a href="/wiki/Latin_influence_in_English" title="Latin influence in English">Latin</a> and <a href="/wiki/English_words_of_Greek_origin" title="English words of Greek origin">Greek</a> is notable. <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_Latin" title="Ecclesiastical Latin">Ecclesiastical Latin</a>, the Roman Catholic Church's official language, remains a living legacy of the classical world in the contemporary world.</p>
<p>Latin had an impact far beyond the classical world. It continued to be the pre-eminent language for serious writings in Europe long after the fall of the Roman empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> The modern <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance languages</a> – such as French, Spanish, and Italian – all derive from Latin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> Latin is still seen as a foundational aspect of European culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>The legacy of the classical world is not confined to the influence of classical languages. The Roman empire was taken as a model by later European empires, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_empire" title="Spanish empire">Spanish</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_empire" title="British empire">British</a> empires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Classical art has been taken as a model in later periods – medieval Romanesque architecture<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> and Enlightenment-era neoclassical literature<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaminski_2007_57_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kaminski_2007_57-10">[10]</a></sup> were both influenced by classical models, to take but two examples, while <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">Joyce's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Ulysses_(novel)" title="Ulysses (novel)">Ulysses</a></i> is one of the most influential works of twentieth century literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
