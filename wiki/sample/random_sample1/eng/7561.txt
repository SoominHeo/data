<p><i><b>Bombus terrestris</b></i>, the <b>buff-tailed bumblebee</b> or <b>large earth bumblebee,</b> is one of the most numerous <a href="/wiki/Bumblebee" title="Bumblebee">bumblebee</a> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. It is one of the main species used in greenhouse <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a>, and so can be found in many countries and areas where it is not native, such as <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Semmens_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Semmens-1">[1]</a></sup> Moreover, it is a <a href="/wiki/Eusociality" title="Eusociality">eusocial</a> insect with an overlap of generations, a division of labor, and cooperative brood care. The queen is <a href="/wiki/Monandrous" title="Monandrous">monandrous</a> which means she mates with only one male. <i>B. terrestris</i> workers learn flower colors and forage efficiently.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_phylogenetics">Taxonomy and phylogenetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and phylogenetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>B. terrestris</i> is part of the order <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">Hymenoptera</a>, which is composed of ants, bees, wasps, and <a href="/wiki/Sawfly" title="Sawfly">sawflies</a>. The family <a href="/wiki/Apidae" title="Apidae">Apidae</a> specifically consists of bees. It is also part of the subfamily <a href="/wiki/Apinae" title="Apinae">Apinae</a>. There are 14 tribe lineages within <a href="/wiki/Apinae" title="Apinae">Apinae</a>, and <i>B. terrestris</i> is in the bumblebee tribe, <a href="/wiki/Bombini" title="Bombini">Bombini</a>. It is in the genus <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bombus" title="Bombus">Bombus</a></i>, which consists entirely of bumblebees, and the subgenus <i>Bombus sensu stricto</i>. This subgenus contains closely related species such as <i><a href="/wiki/Bombus_affinis" title="Bombus affinis">Bombus affinis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bombus_cryptarum" title="Bombus cryptarum">Bombus cryptarum</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bombus_franklini" title="Bombus franklini">Bombus franklini</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bombus_ignitus" title="Bombus ignitus">Bombus ignitus</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White-tailed_bumblebee" title="White-tailed bumblebee">Bombus lucorum</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bombus_magnus" title="Bombus magnus">Bombus magnus</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_bumblebee" title="Western bumblebee">Bombus occidentalis</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Bombus_terricola" title="Bombus terricola">Bombus terricola</a></i>. There are nine recognized subspecies: <i>B. terrestris africanus, B. terrestris audax, B. terrestris calabricus, B. terrestris canariensis, B. terrestris dalmatinus, B. terrestris lusitanicus, B. terrestris sassaricus, B. terrestris terrestris</i> and <i>B. terrestris xanthopus,</i> each with a distinctive coloration scheme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_identification">Description and identification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description and identification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dunkle_Erdhummel_(Bombus_terrestris)_ventral.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1514" data-file-width="1579" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Dunkle_Erdhummel_%28Bombus_terrestris%29_ventral.jpg/220px-Dunkle_Erdhummel_%28Bombus_terrestris%29_ventral.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Dunkle_Erdhummel_%28Bombus_terrestris%29_ventral.jpg/330px-Dunkle_Erdhummel_%28Bombus_terrestris%29_ventral.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Dunkle_Erdhummel_%28Bombus_terrestris%29_ventral.jpg/440px-Dunkle_Erdhummel_%28Bombus_terrestris%29_ventral.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

Ventral view of <i>B. terrestris</i></div>

<p><i>B. terrestris</i> are pollen-storing bees that generally feed and forage on nectar and pollen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goulson_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goulson-3">[3]</a></sup> The queen is between 20–22 mm long, males range from 14–16 mm, and workers from 11–17 mm. Workers have white-ended abdomens, and look just like workers of the white-tailed bumblebee, <i><a href="/wiki/Bombus_lucorum" title="Bombus lucorum">B. lucorum</a></i>, a close relative, apart from the yellowish bands of <i>B. terrestris</i> being darker in direct comparison. The queens of <i>B. terrestris</i> have the namesake <a href="/wiki/Buff_(colour)" title="Buff (colour)">buff</a>-white <a href="/wiki/Abdomen" title="Abdomen">abdomen</a> tip ("tail"); this area is white as in the workers in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White-tailed_bumblebee" title="White-tailed bumblebee">B. lucorum</a>.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <i>B. terrestris</i> is unique compared to other bees in that their caste of workers exhibit a wide variation in worker size, with <a href="/wiki/Thorax" title="Thorax">thorax</a> sizes ranging from 2.3 to 6.9 mm in length and masses ranging from 68 to 754 mg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goulson_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goulson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>B. terrestris</i> is most commonly found throughout Europe and generally occupies temperate climates. Because it can survive in a wide variety of habitats, there are populations in the <a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">near East</a>, the Mediterranean Islands, and Northern Africa as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Widmer_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Widmer-5">[5]</a></sup> Additionally, it has escaped captivity after being introduced as a greenhouse pollinator in countries where it is not native, so this bee is now considered an <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> in many of these places, including Japan, Chile, Argentina, and Tasmania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Semmens_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Semmens-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inoue_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Inoue-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Torretta_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Torretta-7">[7]</a></sup> Nests are usually found underground, such as in abandoned rodent dens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inoue_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Inoue-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dornhaus_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dornhaus-8">[8]</a></sup> Colonies form comb-like nest structures with egg cells each containing several eggs. The queen will lay egg cells on top of one another. Colonies produce between 300–400 bees on average, with a large variation in the number of workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duchateau_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Duchateau-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Colony_structure">Colony structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Colony structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_castes">Social castes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Social castes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like in most social bees<i>,</i> there are three main social caste divisions in <i>B. terrestris.</i> This ensures a division of labor and efficient colony functioning. Queens become the main female individual to reproduce in a future colony. There is only one per colony. Her sole responsibility is to lay eggs after she founds a nest. This fate is determined for larvae that receive more food, have longer <a href="/wiki/Instar" title="Instar">instar</a> stages, and higher levels of <a href="/wiki/Juvenile_hormone" title="Juvenile hormone">juvenile hormone</a> <a href="/wiki/Biosynthesis" title="Biosynthesis">biosynthesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Workers, an entirely female caste, mainly forage for food, defend the colony, and tend to the growing larvae. They are usually sterile for most of the colony cycle and do not raise their own young. Unlike queens and workers, which develop from fertilized <a href="/wiki/Ploidy" title="Ploidy">diploid</a> eggs, drones, or male bees, are born from unfertilized, <a href="/wiki/Ploidy" title="Ploidy">haploid</a> eggs. Drones leave the colony shortly after reaching adulthood to find a mate outside the nest. Mating is their sole role in the colony.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle">Life cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Life cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A solitary queen hatched from her abandoned colony initiates the colony cycle when she mates with a male and finds a nest. She will stay in this nest over winter and then will lay a small batch of <a href="/wiki/Ploidy" title="Ploidy">diploid</a> (female) eggs in the spring. Once these hatch, she tends the larvae, feeding them with nectar and pollen. When the larvae are grown, they <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pupate" title="Pupate">pupate</a>, and about two weeks later, the first workers emerge. This is known as the initiation phase of the colony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duchateau_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Duchateau-9">[9]</a></sup> Workers forage for nectar and pollen for the colony and tend later generations of larvae. The workers are smaller than the queen, and usually die while foraging in the jaws of predators like birds or robberflies. The foraging range and frequency of workers depends on the quality and distribution of available food, but most workers forage within a few hundred meters of their nest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>This first phase can last a variable amount of time in <i>B. terrestris</i>, after which a switch point is reached, and the queen begins to lay some unfertilized eggs, which develop into males.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duchateau_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Duchateau-9">[9]</a></sup> When the male drones emerge from the nest, they do not return, foraging only for themselves. They seek out emerging queens and mate with them. The remaining diploid eggs hatch into larvae that receive extra food and pupate to become new queens. The queen can use <a href="/wiki/Pheromone" title="Pheromone">pheromones</a> to discourage the workers' inclination to invest more in these larvae, thereby ensuring that not too many become queens. The resolution of this worker/queen conflict can be complex and is discussed below. The colony persists until fall in temperate zones and then workers begin to lay unfertilized eggs that if they mature will become males. At this point, outright aggression among workers and between the queen and workers begins. This is a predictable time point that occurs about 30 days into the colony cycle in very temperate climates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duchateau_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Duchateau-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Usually, the worker-queen conflict will force the queen out and the new workers will become queenless. A "false queen" might take control of the colony for a short period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Honk_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Honk-12">[12]</a></sup> The newly emerged queens sometimes act as workers and help to raise another brood of queens. During this time they daily leave the nest looking for food, during which time they may mate. Eventually they find a site to dig a “hibernaculum” where they will hibernate until the next spring, when they emerge, seek food — primarily to build up their ovaries — and soon seek a site to found a new nest. (In warmer climates they may skip the hibernation stage.) Almost always the old colony will have died out, and if the site is free of parasites one of the new queens will return and reuse that site.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproductive_behavior">Reproductive behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reproductive behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mating_system">Mating system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mating system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>B. terrestris</i> is a singly mating species. Mating with multiple males might provide benefits of genetic variability among the brood, but it does not happen in this or any but the most highly derived social bees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baer_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baer-13">[13]</a></sup> The lack of multiple matings by <i>B. terrestris</i> queens may be partly due to male interference. <i>B. terrestris</i> males plug the female's sexual tract with a sticky secretion during mating, which appears to reduce the female's ability to successfully mate with other males for several days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> However honeybee males also plug the female's reproductive tract to no avail; honeybees mate tens of times on one mating flight. While there may be genetic fitness benefits in colony <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterogeneity" title="Heterogeneity">heterogeneity</a> from a polyandrous mating system, bumblebees are also likely to be <a href="/wiki/Monandrous" title="Monandrous">monandrous</a> due to social constraints, risks associated with multiple matings, and phylogenetic inertia since the ancestral bees are singly mated. Finding multiple mates might be energetically costly and expose the queen to higher predation risks. Additionally, while queens may prefer multiple matings to ensure more genetic variability and viable offspring, workers are more closely related to full sisters than to paternal half sisters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baer_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baer-13">[13]</a></sup> This is due to <a href="/wiki/Haplodiploidy" title="Haplodiploidy">haplodiploidy</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">Hymenopteran</a> social insects in which males (drones) are haploid and females (workers and queens) are diploid. This confers greater genetic similarity between sister workers (relatedness of 0.75) than between mother and offspring (relatedness of 0.5), making the relatedness component of <a href="/wiki/Kin_selection" title="Kin selection">kin selection</a> higher between sisters.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Worker_egg_laying">Worker egg laying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Worker egg laying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to the queen, the workers can lay eggs . Since workers do not mate, all of their eggs are haploid and will develop into drones. There are multiple factors that determine whether a worker bee will become reproductively active. Workers born early in the first brood are more likely to become egg layers due to their increased size and age, which allows more time for ovarian development. Workers usually have to be at least 30 days old to become an egg layer. Individuals that spend less time foraging and more time near the queen are also more likely to become reproductive. Lastly, due to intense competition for the opportunity to reproduce, older workers often harass the queen by attacking her and buzzing loudly. Once this point is reached the colony is usually abandoned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-van_Honk_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-van_Honk-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sex_ratios">Sex ratios</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sex ratios">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to the variability in the switch point to male production from worker production of <i>B. terrestris</i> colonies, there are varying levels of sex ratios among nests. Early-switching colonies have a much smaller number of future queens compared to males (1:17.4), which may give them a competitive advantage in mating with later emerging queens. Late-switching colonies have fewer males and a more even sex ratio of 1:1:3, thus indicating the queen's control over her colony (she prefers a 1:1 ratio, since she is equally related to both sons and daughters). On the other hand, workers prefer a 3:1 ratio, as they are more related to each other than to their mother. Although early and late switching colonies are usually balanced equally in numbers in the population, the overall demographic in one study was found to be male biased, resulting in an overall sex ratio of 1:4 (female to males).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duchateau_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Duchateau-9">[9]</a></sup> However, most studies show that this balance of bimodal sex determination between early and late-switching colonies creates the queen's preferred 1:1 sex ratio in <i>B. terrestris</i> populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bourke_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bourke-16">[16]</a></sup> This is unusual for monogamous social insects, which usually have a 3:1 sex ratio indicative of worker colony control. <i>B. terrestris</i> often does not conform to standard predictions of sex ratios based on evolutionary theory and haplodiploid theory.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproductive_suppression">Reproductive suppression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reproductive suppression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Queen_suppression">Queen suppression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Queen suppression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Queen bees can control <a href="/wiki/Oogenesis" title="Oogenesis">oogenesis</a> in worker bees by suppressing <a href="/wiki/Juvenile_hormone" title="Juvenile hormone">juvenile hormone</a> (JH) in the workers, which regulates egg development. Among queenless <i>B. terrestris</i> workers, the corpora allata, which secrete JH, was noticeably enlarged compared to queenright workers. JH concentrations were also higher in the <a href="/wiki/Hemolymph" title="Hemolymph">hemolymph</a> of queenless workers. This suggests that the presence of a queen is enough to prevent workers from laying eggs, which helps her maintain genetic control over her colony's brood. The mechanism through which the queen induces this behavior is likely through pheromones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Worker_suppression">Worker suppression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Worker suppression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>While the queen controls much of the egg laying and larval development in the colony, it is likely that workers play a much bigger role in controlling egg laying than previously thought. Dominant workers will often inhibit younger workers from laying eggs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-van_Honk_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-van_Honk-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloch_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bloch-18">[18]</a></sup> Workers have low levels of JH and ovarian development during the early stages of the colony cycle and also after the competition point. Workers introduced into queenright and queenless colonies experience similar levels of inhibition from fellow workers during the competition point, indicating the key role of worker policing of fellow nest mates later in the colony cycle. This suggests that worker reproductive development will be highest between early development and the competition point in the colony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloch_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bloch-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Worker-queen_conflict">Worker-queen conflict</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Worker-queen conflict">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Conflict is expected between the queen and workers over the sex ratio and reproduction of males in the colony, especially in monandrous colonies where workers are more related to their own sons and nephews than to their brothers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloch_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bloch-18">[18]</a></sup> In early-switching colonies, workers might start laying eggs when they know it will be in their own genetic interests, perhaps from a cue that indicates the switch point has been reached and the queen is now laying <a href="/wiki/Ploidy" title="Ploidy">haploid</a> eggs. In late-switching colonies (where the competition point still occurs at the same time in the cycle), workers may start laying eggs when they detect a change in the queen’s pheromone that indicate larvae are developing into new queens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bourke_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bourke-16">[16]</a></sup> Thus, the outcome of this conflict is mediated through the dominance of the queen and the information available to the workers. While it is assumed that queens usually win this conflict, it is still unclear because some studies have indicated that up to 80% of males are produced by workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-van_Honk_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-van_Honk-15">[15]</a></sup> These asymmetries in the timing of egg lying and dominance in <i>B. terrestris</i> might explain why it often does not conform to predicted sex ratios and kin-selection hypotheses.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Worker-worker_conflict">Worker-worker conflict</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Worker-worker conflict">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although <i>B. terrestris</i> workers are most directly in competition with the queen for egg laying opportunities, they will still inhibit their sisters from laying eggs in order to have their own sons. This is beneficial to them because they will share more genes with their own son (.5) rather than their nephews (.375).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloch_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bloch-18">[18]</a></sup> However, kin theory states that in monandrous colonies, workers will be most closely related to each other, so they should spend the most time raising the queen's young, which are their full sisters. There are several issues here. One is what should the sex ratio be. From the worker's perspective, if the queen lays all the eggs, investment should be 3 times as much in females as males. But workers will always prefer to lay male eggs themself since they are related by 0.50 to their sons and only by 0.25 to their brothers. On a singly mated colony as typifies this species, workers are more related to brothers (0.50) than to nephews (0.375) and so should agree with their mother as to who produces males and should police (eat) the eggs laid by other workers. Still, each worker herself is favored to lay eggs, though they are likely to be eaten.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_and_foraging_behavior">Social and foraging behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Social and foraging behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dominance_hierarchy">Dominance hierarchy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Dominance hierarchy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Newly emerged workers start out at the bottom of the dominance hierarchy in the social colony. As they age, they move closer to the position of queen. Queen-side workers are often egg layers and interact more frequently with the queen. This social position may pay off later, after the competition point is reached. When the queen is overthrown by the aggression of the workers, the most dominant worker will have the best likelihood of contributing more eggs to the colony brood and will perhaps climb to the position of “false queen.” The queen appears to maintain a constant distance of social dominance from her workers at all points in the cycle, suggesting that she is displaced by the sheer number of workers later in the cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Honk_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Honk-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foraging_behavior">Foraging behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Foraging behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>B. terrestris</i> generally forage on a large variety of flower species. Their highest activity is in the morning, with their peak time being noted at around 7-8 am. This is likely because it gets progressively warmer in the afternoon, and foragers prefer ambient temperatures of around 25 °C during nectar and pollen collection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Alloethism">Alloethism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Alloethism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i>B. terrestris</i> bees exhibit alloethism, which is where different sized bees perform different tasks. This kind of behavior can be seen most often in foraging activities. Larger bees are more often found foraging outside the nest and will return to the nest with larger amounts of nectar and pollen. It is possible that larger bees might be able to withstand greater temperature variation, avoid predation, and travel larger distances making them selectively advantageous. Distinct social roles based on <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a> might also be beneficial for individuals of the colonies, by making the colony operate more efficiently. Small bees can be reared more cheaply and kept for in-nest tasks, while only some larvae will be fed enough to become large foraging bees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goulson_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Goulson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_alert">Food alert</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Food alert">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Individuals who return from the nest after a foraging run often recruit other bees in the colony to leave the nest and search for food. In <i>B. terrestris</i>, successful foragers will return to the nest and run around frantically and without a measurable pattern, unlike the ritualized dance of the honeybee. Although the mechanism by which this recruitment strategy functions is unclear, it is hypothesized that running around likely spreads a pheromone that encourages other bees to exit and forage by indicating the location and odor of food nearby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dornhaus_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dornhaus-8">[8]</a></sup> Colonies with lower food stores will often be more responsive to this foraging pheromone. Conversely, in colonies with ample food reserves bees will be less responsive to these pheromones likely to save time and energy from unnecessary foraging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Homing_ability">Homing ability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Homing ability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i>B. terrestris</i> has an impressive homing range, where bees displaced from their nests can relocate the colony from up to 9.8 km away. However, the return often takes several days, indicating <i>B. terrestris</i> might be utilizing familiar foliage and natural landmarks to find the nest. This may be a tedious process if an individual is outside the conventional foraging range of the nest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Another study indicated that these bees can navigate their way back to the nest from a distance as far away as 13 km (8.1 mi), although most forage within 5 km of their nest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> One <a href="/wiki/Mark_and_recapture" title="Mark and recapture">mark and recapture</a> study found their average foraging distance to be approximately 663 m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Male bees have also been found to have longer flight ranges than worker bees, likely because they move farther away from the nest to find mates. Males have flight distances of anywhere from 2.6-9.9 km. If males also contribute to <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a>, this might increase previously predicted pollen flow ranges based on worker flight behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Learning">Learning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Learning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bumblebees and honey bees are extremely influenced by an innate preference for blue and yellow color. When they have no training, they will often just visit flowers that naturally attract them. However, it is generally thought that bees will learn to visit more nectar rewarding flowers after experience associates the reward with the color of the petals. This has been demonstrated in <i>B. terrestris,</i> where bees trained on artificially colored flowers will pick a similar color to the one they were trained with when tested with an array of flower choices. If individuals were tested with flower colors significantly different than from what they were trained with, they just visited flowers most closely aligned with their innate color preferences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In addition to identifying specific colors for foraging purposes, it has also been shown that young worker bees have to learn complex motor skills in order to efficiently collect nectar and pollen from flowers. These skills might take several days to develop, as memory does not always hold perfectly on a day-to-day basis, sometimes deteriorating overnight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Even within a species, different populations have varying levels of innate blue preference and exhibit intraspecific variation in learning rate during association tasks. This is true of two subspecies of <i>B. terrestris</i>, <i>B. terrestris dalmatinus</i> and <i>B. terrestris audax.</i> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations_on_foraging_precision">Limitations on foraging precision</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Limitations on foraging precision">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>While bees are highly adept at discrimination tasks, they are still limited by the magnitude of difference needed in hue to properly carry out these tests. Error rates of color recognition decrease in <i>B. terrestris</i> when flower pigments are closer together on the color spectrum. This might have damaging effects on pollination efficiency if bees visit different flower species with similar, but distinct colors, which can only be mediated if the flowers have unique shapes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_learning">Social learning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Social learning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>While bees often forage alone, experiments demonstrate that young foragers might learn what flowers provide the most nectar more quickly when foraging with older workers. <i>B. terrestris</i> individuals have a faster learning curve for visiting unfamiliar, yet rewarding flowers, when they can see a conspecific foraging on the same species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The discovery of this type of associative learning is a novel insight into bee behavior and may supplement learning via color reward association.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parasites_and_disease">Parasites and disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Parasites and disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brood_parasites">Brood parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Brood parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>B. terrestris</i> is parasitized by <i><a href="/wiki/Bombus_bohemicus" title="Bombus bohemicus">B. bohemicus</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood-parasitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Cuckoo_bee" title="Cuckoo bee">Cuckoo bee</a> that invades <i>B. terrestris</i> hives and takes over reproductive dominance from the host queen, laying its own eggs that will be cared for by host workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Another brood parasite is the bee <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vestal_cuckoo_bumblebee" title="Vestal cuckoo bumblebee"><i>B. vestalis</i></a><i>.</i> Both of these are distributed in various regions of Europe. The difference between <i>B. bohemicus</i> and <i>B. vestalis</i> is that the former parasitizes several bumble bee species while <i>B. vestalis</i> exclusively parasitizes <i>B. terrestris.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_foraging_on_resistance">Effects of foraging on resistance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Effects of foraging on resistance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Foraging is considered energetically costly and it is possible that individuals that spend more time foraging suffer costs to their overall fitness. For example, <i>B. terrestris</i> is often vulnerable to parasitism by <a href="/wiki/Conopidae" title="Conopidae">conopid flies</a> in Central Europe, and it has been hypothesized that foragers might suffer higher incidences of parasites due to the increased metabolic costs of flying. This was demonstrated in a population in which foraging workers had significantly lower levels of encapsulation of an experimental parasitic egg when compared to non-foraging workers. This suggests that foragers have compromised immune systems due to increased energetic expenses and might be predisposed to fly parasites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_polyandry_on_resistance">Effects of polyandry on resistance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Effects of polyandry on resistance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While <i>B. terrestris</i> is a singly mating species, a polyandrous system would potentially be beneficial because it would be possible to attain greater genetic variability for resistance against disease. Accordingly, artificially increasing the number of mates a <i>B. terrestris</i> queen obtains through artificial insemination has shown that the increased genetic variability in her offspring confers greater resistance to the most common bumblebee parasite, <i><a href="/wiki/Crithidia" title="Crithidia">Crithidia</a> bombi.</i> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baer_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Baer-13">[13]</a></sup> However, the average reproductive success between one and multiple matings is not linear. Queens that mated once and mated four times had a higher fitness than those that mated twice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baer_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Baer-13">[13]</a></sup> This suggests that there might be a fitness barrier to increased matings, which might be why colonies are usually monandrous.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Immunocompetence">Immunocompetence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Immunocompetence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Surprisingly, the <a href="/wiki/Immunocompetence" title="Immunocompetence">immunocompetence</a>, as measured by the ability to encapsulate a novel <a href="/wiki/Antigen" title="Antigen">antigen</a>, does not vary based on the local environment. Experimental studies demonstrate that <i>B. terrestris</i> have equal levels of encapsulation in poor and stable environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmid_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schmid-33">[33]</a></sup> This is unexpected, because <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immunity</a> should be compromised in conditions where food supply is low in order to save energy. Perhaps encapsulation represents an invariable trait of bumblebees, or immunity is far too complex to characterize solely based on measurements of encapsulation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats_from_disease">Threats from disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Threats from disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Deformed_wing_virus" title="Deformed wing virus">Deformed wing virus</a> (DWV) is normally a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apis_mellifera" title="Apis mellifera">honey bee</a> pathogen that results in reduced and crumpled wings, making those individuals inviable. This virus is thought to have spread to <i>B. terrestris,</i> and in 2004, as many as 10% of queen bees bred commercially in Europe were found dead with deformed wings. This was confirmed as DWV when <i>B. terrestris</i> colonies tested positive for the presence of DWV <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a>. This could indicate that DWV is a broad range pathogen among bees, or perhaps it has recently been infecting new hosts after transmission from <a href="/wiki/Honey_bee" title="Honey bee">honey bees</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_concerns">Environmental concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Environmental concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Invasive_species">Invasive species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Invasive species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While native to Europe, <i>B. terrestris</i> has been introduced as a greenhouse pollinator into many foreign ecosystems. The presence of <i>B. terrestris</i> is becoming an ecological concern in many communities in which it is not native. It is classified as an "invasive alien species" in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matsumura_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Matsumura-35">[35]</a></sup> For example, <i>B. terrestris</i> has a large niche overlap with local Japanese bee species in terms of flower resources and nest sites. <i>B. terrestris</i> queens competing for local underground nest sites are displacing <i>B. hypocrita sapporoensis</i>. However, <i>B. pseudobaicalensis</i>, which visits similar flowers but only forms nests above ground, has not seen a rapid decline in population numbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inoue_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Inoue-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, the Australian government banned the live import of <i>B. terrestris</i> into Australia on the grounds that it would present a significant risk of becoming a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feral" title="Feral">feral</a> species and thereby present a threat to native fauna and flora.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In 2004, this bumblebee was classified as a 'Key Threatening Process' by the Scientific Committee of the New South Wales Department of Environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>This species was introduced to Chile in 1998. It has since crossed into Argentina, and is spreading at about 275 km per year. Its spread has been detrimental to populations of <i><a href="/wiki/Bombus_dahlbomii" title="Bombus dahlbomii">Bombus dahlbomii</a></i>, which is the only bumblebee species native to southern South America (Patagonia, Southern Chile and Argentina).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-one-38">[38]</a></sup> <i>Bombus terrestris</i> populations facilitated such massive and immediate population decline of <i><a href="/wiki/Bombus_dahlbomii" title="Bombus dahlbomii">Bombus dahlbomii</a></i> through competition and pathogen introduction/spillover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-one-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rufford_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-rufford-39">[39]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Bombus_ruderatus" title="Bombus ruderatus">Bombus ruderatus</a></i>, a bee previously introduced in 1982, is also seriously affected. The cause is thought to be the parasite <i><a href="/wiki/Apicystis_bombi" title="Apicystis bombi">Apicystis bombi</a></i>, an organism carried by the buff-tails, but which has no adverse effect on that species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colony_development_in_changing_environments">Colony development in changing environments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Colony development in changing environments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Temperate_climate" title="Temperate climate">temperate</a> areas, variable climates and environmental conditions occur during changing seasons. Lack of available food due to these unpredictable circumstances can often negatively affect colony growth, reproduction, and resistance to parasites. In poor environments with limited food, the few workers born are smaller than average. However, it appears that <i>B. terrestris</i> is well adapted to a changing environment, considering colony growth is higher under variable feeding conditions than under stable feeding conditions. Workers and reproductives are also heavier with a variable food supply when compared to stable food availability. This might indicate an adaptive strategy of increased provisioning to save for days it is hard to find food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmid_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schmid-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pesticide_exposure">Pesticide exposure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Pesticide exposure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In their 2014 study published in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Functional_Ecology" title="Functional Ecology">Functional Ecology</a></i> researchers using <a href="/wiki/Radio-frequency_identification" title="Radio-frequency identification">Radio-Frequency Identification (</a>RFID) tagging technology on the bees, found that a sublethal exposure to either a <a href="/wiki/Neonicotinoid" title="Neonicotinoid">neonicotinoid</a> (<a href="/wiki/Imidacloprid" title="Imidacloprid">imidacloprid</a>) and/or a <a href="/wiki/Pyrethroid" title="Pyrethroid">pyrethroid</a> (?-<a href="/wiki/Cyhalothrin" title="Cyhalothrin">cyhalothrin</a>) over a four-week period caused an impairment of the bumblebee's ability to <a href="/wiki/Forage" title="Forage">forage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gill_Raine_2014_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gill_Raine_2014-41">[41]</a></sup> Research published in 2015 showed that bees prefer solutions containing <a href="/wiki/Neonicotinoid" title="Neonicotinoid">neonicotinoids</a>, even though the consumption of these pesticides caused them to eat less food overall. This work implies that treating flowering crops with such pesticides presents a sizeable hazard to foraging bees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neonicotinoids_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Neonicotinoids-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_importance">Human importance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Human importance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_use">Commercial use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombus_terrestris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Commercial use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 1987, <i>B. terrestris</i> has been bred commercially for use as a pollinator in European greenhouse crops, particularly tomatoes—a task which was previously carried out by human hand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matsumura_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Matsumura-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> <i>B. terrestris</i> has been commercially reared in New Zealand since the early 1990s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> and is now used in at least North Africa, Japan, Korea, and Russia, with the global trade in bumblebee colonies probably exceeding 1 million nests per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> In Korea, however, some have chosen <i><a href="/wiki/Bombus_ignitus" title="Bombus ignitus">Bombus ignitus</a></i> over the already established commercial pollinator, <i>Bombus terrestris</i>, for fear of competition or genetic contamination by mating with native bumblebee species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Also, there has been a ban on importing <i>B. terrestris</i> into North America which resulted in higher interest in other species like <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_eastern_bumblebee" title="Common eastern bumblebee">B. impatiens</a></i> in North America<i>.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nonetheless, <i>B. terrestris</i> are key commercial pollinators in Europe, which has driven researchers to investigate the influence of agricultural land on the foraging and survival of this species. <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">Monoculture</a> reduces biodiversity in farmland areas, and likely decreases the number of flowering species bees can forage on. <i>B. terrestris</i> consequently exhibits greater nest growth in suburban areas than in farmland, because local suburban gardens promote more plant diversity for bees to feed from. Agriculture has a profound impact on many bumblebees, and is causing widespread decline in several species. However, <i>B. terrestris</i> is still widespread, likely because it can forage at very long distances, making it less sensitive to changes in <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> and the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
