<p><b>Calligraphy</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Visual_arts" title="Visual arts">visual art</a> related to <a href="/wiki/Writing" title="Writing">writing</a>. It is the design and execution of lettering with a broad tip instrument, <a href="/wiki/Brush" title="Brush">brush</a>, among other writing instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mediaville1996_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-mediaville1996-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:17</sup> A contemporary calligraphic practice can be defined as, "the art of giving form to signs in an expressive, harmonious, and skillful manner".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mediaville1996_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-mediaville1996-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:18</sup></p>
<p>Modern calligraphy ranges from functional inscriptions and designs to fine-art pieces where the letters may or may not be readable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mediaville1996_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-mediaville1996-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (August 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Classical calligraphy differs from <a href="/wiki/Typography" title="Typography">typography</a> and non-classical hand-lettering, though a calligrapher may practice both.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zapf2007_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-zapf2007-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zapf2006_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-zapf2006-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Calligraphy continues to flourish in the forms of <a href="/wiki/Wedding" title="Wedding">wedding</a> and event invitations, <a href="/wiki/Font" title="Font">font</a> design and typography, original hand-lettered <a href="/wiki/Logo" title="Logo">logo</a> design, <a href="/wiki/Religious_art" title="Religious art">religious art</a>, announcements, <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">graphic design</a> and commissioned calligraphic art, cut stone <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inscription" title="Inscription">inscriptions</a>, and memorial <a href="/wiki/Document" title="Document">documents</a>. It is also used for <a href="/wiki/Theatrical_property" title="Theatrical property">props</a> and moving images for film and television, <a href="/wiki/Testimonial" title="Testimonial">testimonials</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birth_certificate" title="Birth certificate">birth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Death_certificate" title="Death certificate">death</a> certificates, <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">maps</a>, and other written works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tools">Tools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Tools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pointed_pen_parts.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="340" data-file-width="340" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Pointed_pen_parts.svg/220px-Pointed_pen_parts.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Pointed_pen_parts.svg/330px-Pointed_pen_parts.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Pointed_pen_parts.svg/440px-Pointed_pen_parts.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pointed_pen_parts.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A calligraphic pen head, with parts names.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The principal tools for a calligrapher are the <a href="/wiki/Pen" title="Pen">pen</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Paintbrush" title="Paintbrush">brush</a>. Calligraphy pens write with <a href="/wiki/Nib_(pen)" title="Nib (pen)">nibs</a> that may be flat, round, or pointed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> For some decorative purposes, multi-nibbed pens—steel brushes—can be used. However, works have also been created with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Felt-tip_pen" title="Felt-tip pen">felt-tip</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ballpoint_pen" title="Ballpoint pen">ballpoint pens</a>, although these works do not employ angled lines. There are some styles of calligraphy, like Gothic script, which require a stub nib pen.</p>
<p>Writing ink is usually water-based and is much less viscous than the oil-based inks used in printing. High quality paper, which has good consistency of absorption,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> enables cleaner lines,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> although <a href="/wiki/Parchment" title="Parchment">parchment</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vellum" title="Vellum">vellum</a> is often used, as a knife can be used to erase imperfections and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light-box" title="Light-box">light-box</a> is not needed to allow lines to pass through it. Normally, light boxes and templates are used to achieve straight lines without pencil markings detracting from the work. Ruled paper, either for a light box or direct use, is most often ruled every quarter or half inch, although inch spaces are occasionally used. This is the case with <i>litterea unciales</i> (hence the name), and college-<a href="/wiki/Ruled_paper" title="Ruled paper">ruled paper</a> often acts as a guideline well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Common calligraphy pens and brushes are:</p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Quill" title="Quill">Quill</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dip_pen" title="Dip pen">Dip pen</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ink_brush" title="Ink brush">Ink brush</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Qalam" title="Qalam">Qalam</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fountain_pen" title="Fountain pen">Fountain pen</a></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_traditions">World traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: World traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Western_calligraphy" title="Western calligraphy">Western calligraphy</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LindisfarneFol27rIncipitMatt.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1797" data-file-width="1266" height="312" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/LindisfarneFol27rIncipitMatt.jpg/220px-LindisfarneFol27rIncipitMatt.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/LindisfarneFol27rIncipitMatt.jpg/330px-LindisfarneFol27rIncipitMatt.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/LindisfarneFol27rIncipitMatt.jpg/440px-LindisfarneFol27rIncipitMatt.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Western calligraphy is recognizable by the use of the <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin script</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin alphabet</a> appeared about 600 BC, in Rome, and by the first century<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2011)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> developed into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_imperial_capitals" title="Roman imperial capitals">Roman imperial capitals</a> carved on stones, <a href="/wiki/Rustic_capitals" title="Rustic capitals">Rustic capitals</a> painted on walls, and <a href="/wiki/Roman_cursive" title="Roman cursive">Roman cursive</a> for daily use. In the second and third centuries the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uncial" title="Uncial">uncial</a> lettering style developed. As writing withdrew to monasteries, uncial script was found more suitable for copying the Bible and other religious texts. It was the monasteries which preserved calligraphic traditions during the fourth and fifth centuries, when the Roman Empire fell and Europe entered the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Dark Ages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the height of the Empire, its power reached as far as Great Britain; when the empire fell, its literary influence remained. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-uncial" title="Semi-uncial">Semi-uncial</a> generated the Irish Semi-uncial, the small Anglo-Saxon. Each region developed its own standards following the main monastery of the region (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Merovingian_script" title="Merovingian script">Merovingian script</a>, Laon script, <a href="/wiki/Luxeuil_Abbey" title="Luxeuil Abbey">Luxeuil script</a>, <a href="/wiki/Visigothic_script" title="Visigothic script">Visigothic script</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beneventan_script" title="Beneventan script">Beneventan script</a>), which are mostly cursive and hardly readable.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Calligraphy.malmesbury.bible.arp.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1011" data-file-width="1500" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Calligraphy.malmesbury.bible.arp.jpg/220px-Calligraphy.malmesbury.bible.arp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Calligraphy.malmesbury.bible.arp.jpg/330px-Calligraphy.malmesbury.bible.arp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Calligraphy.malmesbury.bible.arp.jpg/440px-Calligraphy.malmesbury.bible.arp.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Calligraphy in a <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> of 1407 on display in <a href="/wiki/Malmesbury_Abbey" title="Malmesbury Abbey">Malmesbury Abbey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a>, England. This bible was hand written in Belgium, by Gerard Brils, for reading aloud in a <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastery</a>.</div>

<p>Christian churches promoted the development of writing through the prolific copying of the Bible, particularly the New Testament and other sacred texts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Two distinct styles of writing known as uncial and half-uncial (from the Latin "uncia," or "inch") developed from a variety of Roman bookhands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The 7th-9th centuries in northern Europe were the heyday of Celtic illuminated manuscripts, such as the Book of Durrow, <a href="/wiki/Lindisfarne_Gospels" title="Lindisfarne Gospels">Lindisfarne Gospels</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Kells" title="Book of Kells">Book of Kells</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a>'s devotion to improved scholarship resulted in the recruiting of "a crowd of scribes", according to <a href="/wiki/Alcuin" title="Alcuin">Alcuin</a>, the Abbot of <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Alcuin developed the style known as the Caroline or <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_minuscule" title="Carolingian minuscule">Carolingian minuscule</a>. The first manuscript in this hand was the Godescalc Evangelistary (finished 783) — a Gospel book written by the scribe Godescalc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Carolingian remains the one progenitor hand from which modern booktype descends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the eleventh century, the Caroline evolved into the <a href="/wiki/Blackletter" title="Blackletter">Gothic script</a>, which was more compact and made it possible to fit more text on a page.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lovett2000_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-lovett2000-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:72</sup> The Gothic calligraphy styles became dominant throughout Europe; and in 1454, when <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Gutenberg" title="Johannes Gutenberg">Johannes Gutenberg</a> developed the first printing press in Mainz, Germany, he adopted the Gothic style, making it the first <a href="/wiki/Typeface" title="Typeface">typeface</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lovett2000_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-lovett2000-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:141</sup></p>

The <a href="/wiki/Georgian_calligraphy" title="Georgian calligraphy">Georgian calligraphy</a> is centuries-old tradition of an artistic writing of the <a href="/wiki/Georgian_language" title="Georgian language">Georgian language</a> with its <a href="/wiki/Georgian_scripts" title="Georgian scripts">three scripts</a>.</div>

<p>In the 15th century, the rediscovery of old Carolingian texts encouraged the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Humanist_minuscule" title="Humanist minuscule">humanist minuscule</a> or <i>littera antiqua</i>. The 17th century saw the <a href="/wiki/Bastarda" title="Bastarda">Batarde script</a> from France, and the 18th century saw the <a href="/wiki/English_script_(calligraphy)" title="English script (calligraphy)">English script</a> spread across Europe and world through their books.</p>
<p>In the mid-1600s French officials, flooded with documents written in various hands and varied levels of skill, complained that many such documents were beyond their ability to decipher. The Office of the Financier thereupon restricted all legal documents to three hands, namely the Coulee, the Rhonde, (known as <a href="/wiki/Round_hand" title="Round hand">Round hand</a> in English) and a Speed Hand sometimes simply called the <a href="/wiki/Bastarda" title="Bastarda">Bastarda</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whalley_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whalley-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>While there were many great French masters at the time, the most influential in proposing these hands was Louis Barbedor, who published <i>Les Ecritures Financière Et Italienne Bastarde Dans Leur Naturel</i> circa 1650.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whalley_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Whalley-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the destruction of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camera_Apostolica" title="Camera Apostolica">Camera Apostolica</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Rome_(1527)" title="Sack of Rome (1527)">sack of Rome (1527)</a>, the capitol for writing masters moved to Southern France. By 1600, the Italic Cursiva began to be replaced by a technological refinement, the Italic Chancery <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Circumflessa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Circumflessa (page does not exist)">Circumflessa</a>, which in turn fathered the Rhonde and later English <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roundhand" title="Roundhand">Roundhand</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whalley_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Whalley-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In England, <a href="/wiki/Philip_Ayres_(poet)" title="Philip Ayres (poet)">Ayres</a> and Banson popularized the Round Hand while Snell is noted for his reaction to them, and warnings of restraint and proportionality. Still Edward Crocker began publishing his copybooks 40 years before the aforementioned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whalley_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-Whalley-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Westerncalligraphy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="166" data-file-width="400" height="91" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Westerncalligraphy.jpg/220px-Westerncalligraphy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Westerncalligraphy.jpg/330px-Westerncalligraphy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/57/Westerncalligraphy.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Westerncalligraphy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Modern Western calligraphy</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Sacred <a href="/wiki/Western_calligraphy" title="Western calligraphy">Western calligraphy</a> has some special features, such as the illumination of the first letter of each book or chapter in medieval times. A decorative "carpet page" may precede the literature, filled with ornate, geometrical depictions of bold-hued animals. The <a href="/wiki/Lindisfarne_Gospels" title="Lindisfarne Gospels">Lindisfarne Gospels</a> (715–720 AD) are an early example.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>As with <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calligraphy" title="Chinese calligraphy">Chinese</a> or <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calligraphy" title="Islamic calligraphy">Islamic calligraphy</a>, Western calligraphic script employed the use of strict rules and shapes. Quality writing had a rhythm and regularity to the letters, with a "geometrical" order of the lines on the page. Each character had, and often still has, a precise <a href="/wiki/Stroke_order" title="Stroke order">stroke order</a>.</p>
<p>Unlike a typeface, irregularity in the characters' size, style, and colors increases aesthetic value,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (August 2016)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Calligraphy#Dubious" title="Talk:Calligraphy">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> though the content may be illegible. Many of the themes and variations of today's contemporary Western calligraphy are found in the pages of <a href="/wiki/The_Saint_John%27s_Bible" title="The Saint John's Bible">The Saint John's Bible</a>. A particularly modern example is <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Botts" title="Timothy Botts">Timothy Botts</a>' illustrated edition of the Bible, with 360 calligraphic images as well as a calligraphy <a href="/wiki/Typeface" title="Typeface">typeface</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Several other Western styles use the same tools and practices, but differ by character set and stylistic preferences. For <a href="/wiki/Slavonic_lettering" title="Slavonic lettering">Slavonic lettering</a>, the history of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slavic_peoples" title="Slavic peoples">Slavonic</a> and consequently <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> <a href="/wiki/Writing_system" title="Writing system">writing systems</a> differs fundamentally from the one of the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin language</a>. It evolved from the 10th century to today.</p>
<p><span id="East_Asian_calligraphy"></span></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Asia">East Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: East Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calligraphy" title="Chinese calligraphy">Chinese calligraphy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_calligraphy" title="Japanese calligraphy">Japanese calligraphy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korean_calligraphy" title="Korean calligraphy">Korean calligraphy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_calligraphy" title="Vietnamese calligraphy">Vietnamese calligraphy</a></div>
<p>The Chinese name for calligraphy is <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Chinese transliteration">shūfǎ</span></i> (<span lang="zh-tw">書法</span> in Traditional Chinese, literally "the method or law of writing");<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> the Japanese name <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Japanese transliteration">shodō</span></i> (<span lang="ja">書道</span>, literally "the way or principle of writing"); the Korean is <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Korean transliteration">seoye</span></i> (<a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>: <span lang="ko">서예</span>/<span lang="ko-Hant-KR">書藝</span>, literally "the art of writing"); and the Vietnamese is <span lang="vt">Thư pháp</span> (<span class="Unicode">書法</span>, literally "the way of letters or words"). The calligraphy of <a href="/wiki/CJK_characters" title="CJK characters">East Asian characters</a> is an important and appreciated aspect of <a href="/wiki/East_Asian_cultural_sphere" title="East Asian cultural sphere">East Asian culture</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="History_2">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Van_Mieu_han_tu_5412916981_273dedbe99.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4752" data-file-width="3168" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Van_Mieu_han_tu_5412916981_273dedbe99.jpg/220px-Van_Mieu_han_tu_5412916981_273dedbe99.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Van_Mieu_han_tu_5412916981_273dedbe99.jpg/330px-Van_Mieu_han_tu_5412916981_273dedbe99.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Van_Mieu_han_tu_5412916981_273dedbe99.jpg/440px-Van_Mieu_han_tu_5412916981_273dedbe99.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_China" title="Ancient China">ancient China</a>, the oldest Chinese characters existing are <a href="/wiki/Oracle_bone_script" title="Oracle bone script">Jiǎgǔwén(甲骨文) characters</a> carved on ox <a href="/wiki/Scapula" title="Scapula">scapulae</a> and tortoise <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastrons" title="Plastrons">plastrons</a>, because the dominators in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shang_Dynasty" title="Shang Dynasty">Shang Dynasty</a> carved pits on such animals' bones and then baked them to gain auspice of military affairs, agricultural harvest, or even procreating and weather. During the divination ceremony, after the cracks were made, the characters were written with a brush on the shell or bone to be later carved.(Keightley, 1978). With the development of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronzeware_script" title="Bronzeware script">Jīnwén</a></i> (Bronzeware script) and <i><a href="/wiki/Large_Seal_Script" title="Large Seal Script">Dàzhuàn</a></i> (Large Seal Script) "cursive" signs continued. Moreover, each archaic kingdom of current China had its own set of characters.</p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_era_of_Chinese_history" title="Imperial era of Chinese history">Imperial China</a>, the graphs on old steles—some dating from 200 BC, and in Xiaozhuan style—are still accessible.</p>
<p>About 220 BC, the emperor <a href="/wiki/Qin_Shi_Huang" title="Qin Shi Huang">Qin Shi Huang</a>, the first to conquer the entire Chinese basin, imposed several reforms, among them <a href="/wiki/Li_Si" title="Li Si">Li Si</a>'s character unification, which created a set of 3300 standardized <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Chinese transliteration">Xiǎozhuàn（小篆）</span></i> characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Despite the fact that the main writing implement of the time was already the brush, few papers survive from this period, and the main examples of this style are on steles.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Clerical_script" title="Clerical script">Lìshū（隶书） style</a> (clerical script) which is more regularized, and in some ways similar to modern text, have been also authorised under Qin Shi Huangdi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blakney.2C_p6_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blakney.2C_p6-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Regular_script" title="Regular script">Kǎishū style</a> (traditional regular script)—still in use today—and attributed to <a href="/wiki/Wang_Xizhi" title="Wang Xizhi">Wang Xizhi</a> (<span lang="zh">王羲之</span>, 303–361) and his followers, is even more regularized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blakney.2C_p6_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blakney.2C_p6-25">[25]</a></sup> Its spread was encouraged by <a href="/wiki/Li_Siyuan" title="Li Siyuan">Emperor Mingzong of Later Tang</a> (926–933), who ordered the printing of the classics using new wooden blocks in Kaishu. Printing technologies here allowed a shape stabilization. The Kaishu shape of characters 1000 years ago was mostly similar to that at the end of Imperial China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blakney.2C_p6_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Blakney.2C_p6-25">[25]</a></sup> But small changes have been made, for example in the shape of <big><span lang="zh">广</span></big> which is not absolutely the same in the <a href="/wiki/Kangxi_Dictionary" title="Kangxi Dictionary">Kangxi Dictionary</a> of 1716 as in modern books. The Kangxi and current shapes have tiny differences, while stroke order is still the same, according to old style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Styles which did not survive include Bāfēnshū, a mix made of Xiaozhuan style at 80%, and Lishu at 20%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blakney.2C_p6_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-Blakney.2C_p6-25">[25]</a></sup> Some <a href="/wiki/Variant_Chinese_character" title="Variant Chinese character">variant Chinese characters</a> were unorthodox or locally used for centuries. They were generally understood but always rejected in official texts. Some of these unorthodox variants, in addition to some newly created characters, compose the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese" title="Simplified Chinese">Simplified Chinese</a> character set.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Technique">Technique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Technique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mi_Fu-On_Calligraphy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="653" data-file-width="1001" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Mi_Fu-On_Calligraphy.jpg/220px-Mi_Fu-On_Calligraphy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Mi_Fu-On_Calligraphy.jpg/330px-Mi_Fu-On_Calligraphy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Mi_Fu-On_Calligraphy.jpg/440px-Mi_Fu-On_Calligraphy.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mi_Fu-On_Calligraphy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>On Calligraphy</i> by <a href="/wiki/Mi_Fu" title="Mi Fu">Mi Fu</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" title="Song Dynasty">Song Dynasty</a></div>

<p>Traditional East Asian writing uses the <a href="/wiki/Four_Treasures_of_the_Study" title="Four Treasures of the Study">Four Treasures of the Study</a> (<span lang="zh-tw">文房四寶</span>/<span lang="zh-cn">文房四宝</span>): the <a href="/wiki/Ink_brush" title="Ink brush">ink brushes</a> known as máobǐ (<span lang="zn-cn">毛笔</span>) to write <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_character" title="Chinese character">Chinese characters</a>, Chinese ink, paper, and inkstone, known as the <i>Four Friends of the Study</i> (<a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>: <span lang="ko">문방사우</span>) in Korea. In addition to these four tools, desk pads and paperweights are also used.</p>
<p>The shape, size, stretch, and <a href="/wiki/Brush#Bristles" title="Brush">hair type</a> of the ink brush, the color, color density and water density of the ink, as well as the paper's water absorption speed and surface texture are the main physical parameters influencing the final result. The calligrapher's technique also influences the result. The calligrapher's work is influenced by the quantity of ink and water he lets the brush take, then by the pressure, inclination, and direction he gives to the brush, producing thinner or bolder strokes, and smooth or toothed borders. Eventually, the speed, accelerations, decelerations of the writer's moves, turns, and crochets, and the <a href="/wiki/Stroke_order" title="Stroke order">stroke order</a> give the "spirit" to the characters, by greatly influencing their final shapes.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Styles">Styles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Styles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Cursive styles such as <i><a href="/wiki/Semi-cursive_script" title="Semi-cursive script"><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Chinese transliteration">xíngshū</span></a></i> (semi-cursive or running script) and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grass_script" title="Grass script"><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Chinese transliteration">cǎoshū</span></a></i> (cursive or grass script) are less constrained and faster, where more movements made by the writing implement are visible. These styles' stroke orders vary more, sometimes creating radically different forms. They are descended from Clerical script, in the same time as Regular script (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">Han Dynasty</a>), but <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Chinese transliteration">xíngshū</span></i> and <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Chinese transliteration">cǎoshū</span></i> were used for personal notes only, and never used as a standard. The <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Chinese transliteration">cǎoshū</span></i> style was highly appreciated in <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wu_of_Han" title="Emperor Wu of Han">Emperor Wu of Han</a> reign (140–187 AD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blakney.2C_p6_25-4"><a href="#cite_note-Blakney.2C_p6-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Examples of modern printed styles are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_(typeface)" title="Ming (typeface)">Song</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" title="Song Dynasty">Song Dynasty</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Four_Great_Inventions_of_ancient_China#Printing" title="Four Great Inventions of ancient China">printing press</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Asian_sans-serif_typeface" title="East Asian sans-serif typeface">sans-serif</a>. These are not considered traditional styles, and are normally not written.</p>

<p>Japanese and Korean calligraphies were greatly influenced by Chinese calligraphy. The Japanese and Korean people have also developed specific sensibilities and styles of calligraphy. For example, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_calligraphy" title="Japanese calligraphy">Japanese calligraphy</a> go out of the set of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CJK_strokes" title="CJK strokes">CJK strokes</a> to also include local alphabets such as <a href="/wiki/Hiragana" title="Hiragana">hiragana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Katakana" title="Katakana">katakana</a>, with specific problematics such as new curves and moves, and specific materials (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_paper" title="Japanese paper">Japanese paper</a>, <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Japanese transliteration">washi</span></i> <span lang="ja">和紙</span>, and Japanese ink).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In the case of <a href="/wiki/Korean_calligraphy" title="Korean calligraphy">Korean calligraphy</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hangeul" title="Hangeul">Hangeul</a> and the existence of the circle required the creation of a new technique which usually confuses Chinese calligraphers.</p>
<p>Temporary calligraphy is a practice of water-only calligraphy on the floor, which dries out within minutes. This practice is especially appreciated by the new generation of retired Chinese in public parks of China. These will often open studio-shops in tourist towns offering traditional Chinese calligraphy to tourists. Other than writing the clients name, they also sell fine brushes as souvenirs and lime stone carved stamps.</p>
<p>Since late 1980s, a few Chinese artists have branched out traditional Chinese calligraphy to a new territory by mingling <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">Chinese characters</a> with English letters; notable new forms of calligraphy are <a href="/wiki/Xu_Bing" title="Xu Bing">Xu Bing</a>'s square calligraphy and DanNie's coolligraphy or cooligraphy.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mongolian_calligraphy" title="Mongolian calligraphy">Mongolian calligraphy</a> is also influenced by Chinese calligraphy, from tools to style.</p>
<p>Calligraphy has influenced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ink_and_wash_painting" title="Ink and wash painting">ink and wash painting</a>, which is accomplished using similar tools and techniques. Calligraphy has influenced most major art styles in <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ink_and_wash_painting" title="Ink and wash painting">ink and wash painting</a>, a style of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_painting" title="Chinese painting">Chinese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korean_painting" title="Korean painting">Korean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_painting" title="Japanese painting">Japanese painting</a>, and Vietnamese painting based entirely on calligraphy.</p>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A%C5%9Boka" title="Aśoka">Aśoka</a>'s edicts (c. 265–238 BC) were committed to stone. These inscriptions are stiff and angular in form. Following the Aśoka style of Indic writing, two new calligraphic types appear: <a href="/wiki/Kharosthi" title="Kharosthi">Kharoṣṭī</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Br%C4%81hm%C4%AB_script" title="Brāhmī script">Brāhmī</a>. Kharoṣṭī was used in the northwestern regions of India from the 3rd century BC to the 4th century of the Christian Era, and it was used in <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> until the 8th century.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>In many parts of ancient India, the inscriptions were carried out in smoke-treated palm leaves. This tradition dates back to over two thousand years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Even after the Indian languages were put on paper in the 13th century, palm leaves where considered a preferred medium of writing owing to its longevity (nearly 400 years) compared to paper. Both sides of the leaves were used for writing. Long rectangular strips were gathered on top of one another, holes were drilled through all the leaves, and the book was held together by string. Books of this manufacture were common to <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>. The palm leaf was an excellent surface for penwriting, making possible the delicate lettering used in many of the scripts of southern Asia.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>Burnt clay and <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> were a favoured material for Indic inscriptions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the north of India, birch bark was used as a writing surface as early as the 2nd century AD.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nepal">Nepal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Nepal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nepalese_calligraphy" title="Nepalese calligraphy">Nepalese calligraphy</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ranjana_script" title="Ranjana script">Ranjana script</a> is the primary form of Nepalese calligraphy. The script itself, along with its derivatives (like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lanydza_Script" title="Lanydza Script">Lantsa</a>, <a href="/wiki/%27Phags-pa_script" title="'Phags-pa script">Phagpa</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kutila" title="Kutila">Kutila</a>) are used in <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leh" title="Leh">Leh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, coastal Japan, and Korea to write "<a href="/wiki/Om_mani_padme_hum" title="Om mani padme hum">Om mani padme hum</a>" and other sacred <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_texts" title="Buddhist texts">Buddhist texts</a>, mainly those derived from <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pali" title="Pali">Pali</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Thailand">Thailand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Thailand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thai_calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thai calligraphy (page does not exist)">Thai calligraphy</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> is the primary form of Thai calligraphy. Historically Thai calligraphy has been limited to sacred texts of the Pali Canon with few wider artistic applications where graphic calligraphy representing figures and objects is produced. Calligraphy appears on the personal flag of each member of the Thai royal family bearing its owner's initials in calligraphy. The most obvious place in the country where calligraphy is present is in graffiti. A few books have been published with calligraphic compositions.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tibet">Tibet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Tibet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_calligraphy" title="Tibetan calligraphy">Tibetan calligraphy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BonpoBook.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="120" data-file-width="433" height="61" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/BonpoBook.jpg/220px-BonpoBook.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/BonpoBook.jpg/330px-BonpoBook.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/07/BonpoBook.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BonpoBook.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B%C3%B6n" title="Bön">Bön</a> text</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Calligraphy is central in <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibetan</a> culture. The script is derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indic_script" title="Indic script">Indic scripts</a>. The nobles of Tibet, such as the High <a href="/wiki/Lama" title="Lama">Lamas</a> and inhabitants of the <a href="/wiki/Potala_Palace" title="Potala Palace">Potala Palace</a>, were usually capable calligraphers. <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a> has been a center of <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a> for several centuries, and that religion places a great deal of significance on written word. This does not provide for a large body of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secular" title="Secular">secular</a> pieces, although they do exist (but are usually related in some way to Tibetan Buddhism). Almost all high religious writing involved calligraphy, including letters sent by the <a href="/wiki/Dalai_Lama" title="Dalai Lama">Dalai Lama</a> and other religious and secular authority. Calligraphy is particularly evident on their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prayer_wheels" title="Prayer wheels">prayer wheels</a>, although this calligraphy was forged rather than scribed, much like Arab and Roman calligraphy is often found on buildings. Although originally done with a reed, Tibetan calligraphers now use chisel tipped pens and markers as well.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islamic_world">Islamic world</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Islamic world">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calligraphy" title="Islamic calligraphy">Islamic calligraphy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Basmalah-1wm.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="672" data-file-width="512" height="289" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Basmalah-1wm.svg/220px-Basmalah-1wm.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Basmalah-1wm.svg/330px-Basmalah-1wm.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Basmalah-1wm.svg/440px-Basmalah-1wm.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Basmalah-1wm.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The phrase <a href="/wiki/Basmala" title="Basmala">Basmala</a> in an 18th-century Islamic calligraphy from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottoman_empire" title="Ottoman empire">Ottoman</a> region.</div>

<p>Islamic calligraphy (<i>calligraphy</i> in Arabic is <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Arabic transliteration">khatt ul-yad</span></i> <span dir="rtl" lang="ar">خط اليد</span>‎) has evolved alongside <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic language</a>. As it is based on Arabic letters, some call it "Arabic calligraphy". However the term "Islamic calligraphy" is a more appropriate term as it comprises all works of calligraphy by the Muslim calligraphers from <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusia</a> in modern <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> to China.</p>
<p>Islamic calligraphy is associated with geometric Islamic art (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabesque_(Islamic_art)" title="Arabesque (Islamic art)">arabesque</a>) on the walls and ceilings of <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">mosques</a> as well as on the page. Contemporary artists in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_world" title="Islamic world">Islamic world</a> draw on the heritage of calligraphy to use calligraphic inscriptions or abstractions.</p>
<p>Instead of recalling something related to the spoken word, calligraphy for <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslims</a> is a visible expression of the highest art of all, the art of the <a href="/wiki/Spirituality" title="Spirituality">spiritual</a> world. Calligraphy has arguably become the most venerated form of Islamic art because it provides a link between the languages of the Muslims with the religion of Islam. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">Qur'an</a> has played an important role in the development and evolution of the Arabic language, and by extension, calligraphy in the Arabic alphabet. Proverbs and passages from the Qur'an are still sources for Islamic calligraphy.</p>
<p>It is generally accepted that Islamic calligraphy excelled during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottoman_era" title="Ottoman era">Ottoman era</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (December 2016)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> Istanbul is an open exhibition hall for all kinds and varieties of calligraphy, from inscriptions in mosques to fountains, schools, houses, etc.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Persia">Persia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Persia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The history of calligraphy in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">Persia</a> dates back to the pre-Islam era. In <a href="/wiki/Zoroastrianism" title="Zoroastrianism">Zoroastrianism</a> beautiful and clear writings were always praised.</p>
<p>It is believed that <a href="/wiki/Old_Persian" title="Old Persian">ancient Persian script</a> was invented by about 600–500 BC to provide monument inscriptions for the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire#Achaemenid_kings_and_rulers" title="Achaemenid Empire">Achaemenid kings</a>. These scripts consisted of horizontal, vertical, and diagonal nail-shape letters, which is why it is called <a href="/wiki/Cuneiform_script" title="Cuneiform script">"script of nails/cuneiform script"</a> (<i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Persian transliteration">khat-e-mikhi</span></i>) in <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>. Centuries later, other scripts such as "<a href="/wiki/Pahlavi_scripts" title="Pahlavi scripts">Pahlavi</a>" and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avestan_script" title="Avestan script">Avestan</a>" scripts were used in ancient Persia.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_scripts">Contemporary scripts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Contemporary scripts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasta%27liq_script" title="Nasta'liq script">Nasta'liq</a> style is the most popular contemporary style among classical Persian calligraphy scripts; Persian calligraphers call it the "bride of calligraphy scripts". This calligraphy style has been based on such a strong structure that it has changed very little since. <a href="/wiki/Mir_Ali_Tabrizi" title="Mir Ali Tabrizi">Mir Ali Tabrizi</a> had found the optimum composition of the letters and graphical rules so it has just been fine-tuned during the past seven centuries. It has very strict rules for graphical shape of the letters and for combination of the letters, words, and composition of the whole calligraphy piece.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mayan_civilization">Mayan civilization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Mayan civilization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mayan calligraphy was expressed via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mayan_hieroglyphs" title="Mayan hieroglyphs">Mayan hieroglyphs</a>; modern Mayan calligraphy is mainly used on <a href="/wiki/Seal_(emblem)" title="Seal (emblem)">seals</a> and monuments in the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> in Mexico. Mayan hieroglyphs are rarely used in government offices; however in <a href="/wiki/Campeche" title="Campeche">Campeche</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_(state)" title="Yucatán (state)">Yucatán</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo" title="Quintana Roo">Quintana Roo</a>, Mayan calligraphy is written in Latin letters.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some commercial companies in southern Mexico use Mayan hieroglyphs as symbols of their business. Some community associations and modern Mayan brotherhoods use Mayan hieroglyphs as symbols of their groups.</p>
<p>Most of the archaeological sites in Mexico such as <a href="/wiki/Chichen_Itza" title="Chichen Itza">Chichen Itza</a>, Labna, <a href="/wiki/Uxmal" title="Uxmal">Uxmal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edzna" title="Edzna">Edzna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calakmul" title="Calakmul">Calakmul</a>, etc. have glyphs in their structures. Carved stone monuments known as <a href="/wiki/Stele" title="Stele">stele</a> are common sources of ancient Mayan calligraphy.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_calligraphy">Modern calligraphy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Modern calligraphy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Revival">Revival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Revival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Edward_Johnston.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="572" data-file-width="446" height="282" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Edward_Johnston.png/220px-Edward_Johnston.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Edward_Johnston.png/330px-Edward_Johnston.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Edward_Johnston.png/440px-Edward_Johnston.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Edward_Johnston.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Edward_Johnston" title="Edward Johnston">Edward Johnston</a>, founder of modern western calligraphy, at work in 1902.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">printing</a> became ubiquitous from the 15th century, the production of illuminated manuscripts began to decline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> However, the rise of printing did not mean the end of calligraphy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The modern revival of calligraphy began at the end of the 19th century, influenced by the aesthetics and philosophy of <a href="/wiki/William_Morris" title="William Morris">William Morris</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arts_and_Crafts_movement" title="Arts and Crafts movement">Arts and Crafts movement</a>. <a href="/wiki/Edward_Johnston" title="Edward Johnston">Edward Johnston</a> is regarded as being the father of modern calligraphy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ejf_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-ejf-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> After studying published copies of manuscripts by architect <a href="/wiki/William_Harrison_Cowlishaw" title="William Harrison Cowlishaw">William Harrison Cowlishaw</a>, he was introduced to <a href="/wiki/William_Lethaby" title="William Lethaby">William Lethaby</a> in 1898, principal of the <a href="/wiki/Central_School_of_Art_and_Design" title="Central School of Art and Design">Central School of Arts and Crafts</a>,who advised him to study manuscripts at the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>This triggered Johnston's interest in the art of calligraphy with the use of a broad edged pen. He began a teaching course in calligraphy at the Central School in <a href="/wiki/Southampton_Row" title="Southampton Row">Southampton Row</a>, London from September 1899, where he influenced the typeface designer and sculptor <a href="/wiki/Eric_Gill" title="Eric Gill">Eric Gill</a>. He was commissioned by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Pick" title="Frank Pick">Frank Pick</a> to design a <a href="/wiki/Johnston_(typeface)" title="Johnston (typeface)">new typeface</a> for <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a>, still used today (with minor modifications).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>He has been credited for reviving the art of modern <a href="/wiki/Penmanship" title="Penmanship">penmanship</a> and lettering single-handedly through his books and teachings - his handbook on the subject, <i>Writing &amp; Illuminating, &amp; Lettering</i> (1906) was particularly influential on a generation of British typographers and calligraphers, including <a href="/wiki/Graily_Hewitt" title="Graily Hewitt">Graily Hewitt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Morison" title="Stanley Morison">Stanley Morison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Gill" title="Eric Gill">Eric Gill</a> and Anna Simons. Johnston also devised the simply crafted round calligraphic handwriting style, written with a broad pen, known today as the <i>Foundational hand</i>. Johnston initially taught his students an uncial hand using a flat pen angle, but later taught his hand using a slanted pen angle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> He first referred to this hand as "Foundational Hand" in his 1909 publication, <i>Manuscript &amp; Inscription Letters for Schools and Classes and for the Use of Craftsmen</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_developments">Subsequent developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calligraphy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Subsequent developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Graily_Hewitt" title="Graily Hewitt">Graily Hewitt</a> taught at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_School_of_Arts_and_Crafts" title="Central School of Arts and Crafts">Central School of Arts and Crafts</a> and published together with Johnston throughout the early part of the century. Hewitt was central to the revival of <a href="/wiki/Gilding" title="Gilding">gilding</a> in calligraphy, and his prolific output on type design also appeared between 1915 and 1943. He is attributed with the revival of gilding with <a href="/wiki/Gesso" title="Gesso">gesso</a> and gold leaf on <a href="/wiki/Vellum" title="Vellum">vellum</a>. Hewitt helped to found the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Scribes_%26_Illuminators" title="Society of Scribes &amp; Illuminators">Society of Scribes &amp; Illuminators</a> (SSI) in 1921, probably the world's foremost calligraphy society.</p>

<p>Hewitt is not without both critics<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and supporters<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> in his rendering of <a href="/wiki/Cennino_Cennini" title="Cennino Cennini">Cennino Cennini</a>'s medieval gesso recipes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Donald_Jackson_(calligrapher)" title="Donald Jackson (calligrapher)">Donald Jackson</a>, a British calligrapher, has sourced his gesso recipes from earlier centuries a number of which are not presently in English translation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Graily Hewitt created the patent announcing the award to <a href="/wiki/Prince_Philip,_Duke_of_Edinburgh" title="Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh">Prince Philip</a> of the title of Duke of Edinburgh on November 19, 1947, the day before his marriage to Queen Elizabeth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Johnston’s pupil, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Simons&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anna Simons (page does not exist)">Anna Simons</a>, was instrumental in sparking off interest in calligraphy in Germany with her German translation of <i>Writing and Illuminating, and Lettering</i> in 1910.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ejf_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-ejf-32">[32]</a></sup> Austrian <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rudolf_Larisch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rudolf Larisch (page does not exist)">Rudolf Larisch</a>, a teacher of lettering at the Vienna School of Art, published six lettering books that greatly influenced German-speaking calligraphers. Because German-speaking countries had not abandoned the Gothic hand in printing, Gothic also had a powerful effect on their styles.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Koch" title="Rudolf Koch">Rudolf Koch</a> was a friend and younger contemporary of Larisch. Koch's books, type designs, and teaching made him one of the most influential calligraphers of the 20th century in northern Europe and later in the U.S. Larisch and Koch taught and inspired many European calligraphers, notably <a href="/wiki/Karlgeorg_Hoefer" title="Karlgeorg Hoefer">Karlgeorg Hoefer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Zapf" title="Hermann Zapf">Hermann Zapf</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Contemporary typefaces used by computers, from word processors like <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Word" title="Microsoft Word">Microsoft Word</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apple_Pages" title="Apple Pages">Apple Pages</a> to professional designers' software like <a href="/wiki/Adobe_InDesign" title="Adobe InDesign">Adobe InDesign</a>, owe a considerable debt to the past and to a small number of professional typeface designers today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mediaville1996_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-mediaville1996-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zapf2007_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-zapf2007-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> provides "Script" and "<a href="/wiki/Fraktur" title="Fraktur">Fraktur</a>" Latin alphabets that can be used for calligraphy. See <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_Alphanumeric_Symbols" title="Mathematical Alphanumeric Symbols">Mathematical Alphanumeric Symbols</a>.</p>
