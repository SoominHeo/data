<p><b>Yanghwajin Foreign Missionary Cemetery</b> (<a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>: <span lang="ko">양화진외국인선교사묘원</span>), also known as the <b>Hapjeong-dong</b> (<span lang="ko">합정동</span>) <b>International Cemetery</b>, is a cemetery overlooking the <a href="/wiki/Han_River_(Korea)" title="Han River (Korea)">Han River</a> in the district of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mapo-gu" title="Mapo-gu">Mapo-gu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>. Designated in 1890 as a site for foreign missionaries by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Gojong_of_the_Korean_Empire" title="Emperor Gojong of the Korean Empire">King Gojong</a>, the site is currently open to the public from 9:00am to 6:00pm and is located next to <a href="/wiki/Jeoldu-san" title="Jeoldu-san">Jeoldusan Martyr's Shrine</a>. It is estimated that approximately 30,000 Koreans and 500 foreigners visit every year.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yanghwajin_Foreign_Missionary_Cemetery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The death of Presbyterian minister John Heron in July 1890 prompted the small but growing foreign community in Seoul to look for a proper location for burials. Previous burials were conducted in modern-day <a href="/wiki/Incheon" title="Incheon">Incheon</a> at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chemulpo_Foreigners%27_Cemetery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chemulpo Foreigners' Cemetery (page does not exist)">Chemulpo Foreigners' Cemetery</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Horace_Newton_Allen" title="Horace Newton Allen">Dr. Horace Allen</a> obtained the land rights of the bluff overlooking the Han River and called it Yanghwajin; so named for an old ferry crossing that once existed nearby. The site for the cemetery already had historical significance: in 1839, a number of French Catholic missionaries were put to death there and in 1866, a number of Korean Catholics were also killed in a mass execution on the nearby riverbank.</p>
<p>The cemetery was also a victim of close quarters combat during the Korean War and war damage to many of the grave markers is quite evident. Attempts to repair the fractured markers are minimal at the request of community members.</p>
<p>Originally officially maintained by members of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kyungsung_European-American_Cemetery_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kyungsung European-American Cemetery Association (page does not exist)">Kyungsung European-American Cemetery Association</a> the 14,000 square meter (4000 <a href="/wiki/Pyeong" title="Pyeong">Pyeong</a>) grounds have been unofficially taken care by foreign diplomats, businesspeople, volunteer groundskeepers and missionaries since its founding.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yanghwajin_Foreign_Missionary_Cemetery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Originally built for the members of the foreign missionary community in Seoul, the Kyungsung European-American Cemetery Association maintained the grounds until a 1961 decree by President <a href="/wiki/Park_Chung-hee" title="Park Chung-hee">Park Chung-hee</a> stating that foreigners were not allowed to own land. The grounds technically belonged to no one until the city of Seoul designated it a public park in 1965. In 1968, when the South Korean government passed a law requiring foreigners to register all land, the cemetery was curiously never officially registered. In 1985, a committee called the <i>Council for the 100th Anniversary of the Korean Church</i> was asked by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Horace_Grant_Underwood_III&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Horace Grant Underwood III (page does not exist)">Horace Grant Underwood III</a> to register the cemetery on behalf of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Seoul_Union_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Seoul Union Church (page does not exist)">Seoul Union Church</a> with the understanding that the Seoul Union Church would be the unofficial caretakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The committee agreed and a year later built a joint-use chapel nearby called the Memorial Chapel.</p>
<p>In 2005, the predominantly foreign congregation of the Seoul Union Church began sharing the Memorial Chapel with a Korean congregation composed of the former <i>100th Anniversary Memorial Church Committee</i>. The two congregations coexisted amicably until the death of Dr. Horace Grant Underwood III in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, the two congregations then began to disagree about proper caretaking responsibilities as well as who officially takes care of the grounds. On August 5, 2007, the Seoul Union Church was officially removed from the grounds including the chapel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The church, cemetery and adjacent museum are since the property of the Memorial Church.</p>
<p>Conflicting reports from the Memorial Church further claim that some interments would be disinterred in the future<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Memorial church leader Lee Jae-chul referred to the change in cemetery caretakership similar to the "Chinese retaking Hong Kong".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Homer_Hulbert" title="Homer Hulbert">Homer Hulbert</a> (1863–1949) American missionary and journalist whose headstone proclaims "I would rather be buried in Korea than in Westminster Abbey."</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ernest_Bethell" title="Ernest Bethell">Ernest Bethell</a> (1872–1909) founder of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seoul_Sinmun" title="Seoul Sinmun">Daehan Maeil Sinbo</a> who died after being imprisoned by the Japanese army for exposing abuses against Korean civilians. Years after soldiers erased a defiant challenge to the Imperial Army on Bethell's grave marker, the words were replaced by officials from the Seoul Union Church.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Horace_Grant_Underwood" title="Horace Grant Underwood">Horace Grant Underwood</a> (1859–1916) founder of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Seoul_YMCA&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Seoul YMCA (page does not exist)">Seoul YMCA</a>, Saemunan Presbyterian Church and what eventually became <a href="/wiki/Yonsei_University" title="Yonsei University">Yonsei University</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Gerhard_Appenzeller" title="Henry Gerhard Appenzeller">Henry Gerhard Appenzeller</a> (1858–1902) (<a href="/wiki/Cenotaph" title="Cenotaph">cenotaph</a>) who greatly contributed to the foundation of <a href="/wiki/Pai_Chai_University" title="Pai Chai University">Pai Chai University</a></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Douglas_B._Avison&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Douglas B. Avison (page does not exist)">Douglas B. Avison</a> (1893–1952) who was a founder of <a href="/wiki/Severance_Hospital" title="Severance Hospital">Severance Hospital</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Clarence_Ridgeby_Greathouse" title="Clarence Ridgeby Greathouse">Clarence Ridgeby Greathouse</a> (1843–1899) supervisor to 1895 trial of the murder of <a href="/wiki/Empress_Myeongseong" title="Empress Myeongseong">Queen Min</a></li>
<li>Brevet Brigadier General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_W._Le_Gendre" title="Charles W. Le Gendre">Charles W. Le Gendre</a> (1830–1899) French-born American general, diplomat and advisor to King Kojong from 1890 to 1899.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Wilder_%22Bruce%22_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title='Albert Wilder "Bruce" Taylor (page does not exist)'>Albert Wilder "Bruce" Taylor</a> (1875–1948) American gold mining executive and UPA (later <a href="/wiki/United_Press_International" title="United Press International">UPI</a>) correspondent, lived in Korea for the majority of his life with his wife, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Linley_Taylor" title="Mary Linley Taylor">Mary Linley Taylor</a>. He was actively involved in the <a href="/wiki/Korean_independence_movement" title="Korean independence movement">Korean independence movement</a> and infamously photographed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gojong_of_the_Korean_Empire" title="Gojong of the Korean Empire">King Kojong</a>'s funeral procession.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
