<p><b>Suffolk</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Independent_city_(United_States)" title="Independent city (United States)">independent city</a> located in the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>. As of the 2014 census, the estimated population was 86,806 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QF_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-QF-3">[3]</a></sup> It is the largest city in Virginia by area as well as the 14th largest in the country.</p>
<p>Suffolk is located in the <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Roads" title="Hampton Roads">Hampton Roads</a> metropolitan area which also includes the independent cities of <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake,_Virginia" title="Chesapeake, Virginia">Chesapeake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hampton,_Virginia" title="Hampton, Virginia">Hampton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newport_News,_Virginia" title="Newport News, Virginia">Newport News</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth,_Virginia" title="Portsmouth, Virginia">Portsmouth</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Beach,_Virginia" title="Virginia Beach, Virginia">Virginia Beach</a>, as well as other smaller cities, counties, and towns of Hampton Roads. With miles of waterfront property on the <a href="/wiki/Nansemond_River" title="Nansemond River">Nansemond</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_River" title="James River">James River</a>, present day Suffolk was formed in 1974 after consolidating with <a href="/wiki/Nansemond_County,_Virginia" title="Nansemond County, Virginia">Nansemond County</a> and the towns of <a href="/wiki/Holland,_Virginia" title="Holland, Virginia">Holland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Whaleyville,_Virginia" title="Whaleyville, Virginia">Whaleyville</a>. The current mayor is Linda T. Johnson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SV_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-SV-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffolk,_Virginia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Suffolk,_Virginia" title="History of Suffolk, Virginia">History of Suffolk, Virginia</a></div>
<p>Suffolk was founded by English colonists in 1742 as a port town on the <a href="/wiki/Nansemond_River" title="Nansemond River">Nansemond River</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_Colony" title="Virginia Colony">Virginia Colony</a>. Originally known as Constant's Warehouse, for John Constant, Suffolk was renamed after Royal Governor <a href="/wiki/Sir_William_Gooch,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir William Gooch, 1st Baronet">William Gooch</a>'s home of <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a>, a county in <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. Before European contact, indigenous American tribes lived in the region for thousands of years. At the time of English settlement, the <a href="/wiki/Nansemond" title="Nansemond">Nansemond</a> Indians lived along the river. In the early colonial years, the English cultivated <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Commodity" title="Commodity">commodity</a> crop, but later turned to mixed farming. It became the county seat of <a href="/wiki/Nansemond_County,_Virginia" title="Nansemond County, Virginia">Nansemond County</a> in 1750.</p>
<p>Early in its history, Suffolk became a land transportation gateway to the areas east of it in <a href="/wiki/South_Hampton_Roads" title="South Hampton Roads">South Hampton Roads</a>. Before the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portsmouth_and_Roanoke_Railroad" title="Portsmouth and Roanoke Railroad">Portsmouth and Roanoke Railroad</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_and_Petersburg_Railroad" title="Norfolk and Petersburg Railroad">Norfolk and Petersburg Railroad</a> were built through Suffolk, early predecessors of 21st century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Class_1_railroad" title="Class 1 railroad">Class 1 railroads</a> operated by <a href="/wiki/CSX_Transportation" title="CSX Transportation">CSX Transportation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norfolk_Southern" title="Norfolk Southern">Norfolk Southern</a>, respectively. Other railroads and later major highways followed after the war.</p>
<p>Suffolk became an <a href="/wiki/Incorporated_town" title="Incorporated town">incorporated town</a> in 1808. In 1910, it incorporated as a city and separated from Nansemond County. However, it remained the seat of Nansemond County until 1972, when its former county became the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nansemond,_Virginia" title="Nansemond, Virginia">independent city of Nansemond</a>. In 1974, the independent cities of Suffolk and Nansemond merged under Suffolk's name and charter.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">Peanuts</a> grown in the surrounding areas became a major industry for Suffolk. Notably, <a href="/wiki/Planters" title="Planters">Planters' Peanuts</a> was established in Suffolk beginning in 1912. Suffolk was the 'birthplace' of <a href="/wiki/Mr._Peanut" title="Mr. Peanut">Mr. Peanut</a>, the mascot of Planters' Peanuts. For many years, the call-letters of local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AM_radio" title="AM radio">AM radio</a> station <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=WLPM&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="WLPM (page does not exist)">WLPM</a> stood for <b>W</b>orld's <b>L</b>argest <b>P</b>eanut <b>M</b>arket.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffolk,_Virginia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Suffolk is located at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Suffolk%2C_Virginia&amp;params=36.741347_N_-76.609881_E_type:city_region:US"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">36°44′29″N</span> <span class="longitude">76°36′36″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">36.741347°N 76.609881°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">36.741347; -76.609881</span></span></span></a></span> (36.741347, -76.609881).</p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the city has a total area of 429 square miles (1,110 km<sup>2</sup>), of which 400 square miles (1,000 km<sup>2</sup>) is land and 29 square miles (75 km<sup>2</sup>) (6.7%) is water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR1-5">[5]</a></sup> It is the largest city in Virginia by land area and second-largest by total area. Part of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Dismal_Swamp" title="Great Dismal Swamp">Great Dismal Swamp</a> is located in Suffolk.</p>

<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR8_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR8-11">[11]</a></sup> of 2010, there were 84,585 people, 23,283 households, and 17,718 families residing in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 159.2 people per square mile (61.5/km<sup>2</sup>). There were 24,704 housing units at an average density of 61.8 per square mile (23.8/km<sup>2</sup>). The racial makeup of the city was 50.1% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_American" title="White American">White</a>, 42.7% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American" title="African American">Black or African American</a>, 0.3% <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a>, 1.6% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_American" title="Asian American">Asian</a>, 0.1% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_American" title="Pacific Islander American">Pacific Islander</a>, 0.8% from <a href="/wiki/Race_and_ethnicity_in_the_United_States_Census" title="Race and ethnicity in the United States Census">other races</a>, and 2.3% from two or more races. <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_and_Latino_Americans" title="Hispanic and Latino Americans">Hispanics or Latinos</a> of any race were 2.9% of the population.</p>
<p>There were 23,283 households out of which 36.6% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 55.1% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 16.8% had a female householder with no husband present, and 23.9% were non-families. 20.2% of all households were made up of individuals and 8.1% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.69 and the average family size was 3.09.</p>
<p>The age distribution was 27.8% under the age of 18, 7.1% from 18 to 24, 31.1% from 25 to 44, 22.5% from 45 to 64, and 11.4% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 36 years. For every 100 females there were 91.4 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 87.4 males.</p>
<p>The median income for a household in the city was $41,115, and the median income for a family was $47,342. Males had a median income of $35,852 versus $23,777 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the city was $18,836. About 10.8% of families and 13.2% of the population were below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 18.2% of those under age 18 and 11.2% of those age 65 or over.</p>
<p>As of 2005, the city's median income jumped to $60,484 due to the influx of government-related high-tech jobs in the city's northern corridor and wealthy residents, causing it to be a close second to its neighbor Chesapeake in South Hampton Roads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008_tornado">2008 tornado</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffolk,_Virginia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2008 tornado">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The city <a href="/wiki/Tornadoes_of_2008#April_28" title="Tornadoes of 2008">was hit by</a> an <i>EF3</i> <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a> which produced a large swath of extensive damage through the city and nearby communities during the late afternoon of April 28, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p><br/>
After 4:00 PM EDT on April 28, a tornado touched down multiple times, causing damage and leaving over 200 injured in Suffolk along a path which passed north and west of the downtown area striking near Sentara Obici Hospital and in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unincorporated_community" title="Unincorporated community">unincorporated town</a> of <a href="/wiki/Driver,_Virginia" title="Driver, Virginia">Driver</a>. The storm seriously damaged over 120 homes and 12 businesses. The subdivisions of Burnett's Mill and Hillpoint Farms were damaged particularly heavily, as well as several of the older historic structures in Driver. However, near Driver, large radio and television broadcast towers located in an antenna farm serving most of Hampton Roads were spared serious damage.</p>
<p>Governor Timothy Kaine declared a state of emergency and directed state agencies to assist the recovery and cleanup efforts. Police officers and firefighters from across Hampton Roads were sent to Suffolk to help in the quarantine and cleanup of the damaged areas.</p>
<p>On May 1, the property damages were estimated at $20 million.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffolk,_Virginia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Suffolk_City_Public_Schools" title="Suffolk City Public Schools">Suffolk City Public Schools</a></div>
<p>Suffolk Public Schools, the local public school system, operates 12 <a href="/wiki/Elementary_school" title="Elementary school">elementary schools</a>, four <a href="/wiki/Middle_school" title="Middle school">middle schools</a>, three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_school" title="High school">high schools</a>, and one alternative school. <a href="/wiki/Nansemond-Suffolk_Academy" title="Nansemond-Suffolk Academy">Nansemond-Suffolk Academy</a> is a private college preparatory school located on Pruden Blvd.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffolk,_Virginia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Roads#Transportation" title="Hampton Roads">Transportation in the Hampton Roads region</a></div>
<p>Suffolk's early growth was fueled by its location and transportation considerations. These continue to be major factors in the 21st century.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bike_trails">Bike trails</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffolk,_Virginia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Bike trails">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Dismal Swamp National Wildlife Refuge includes dozens of miles of trails accessible via White Marsh Road at Washington Ditch and other entry sites. Additional bike trails can be found at Lone Star Lakes City Park off Godwin Blvd. This city park provides over 4 miles (6.4 km) of rock trails. There are many rural roads with light traffic available for road riding. Adjacent to Suffolk is <a href="/wiki/Smithfield,_Virginia" title="Smithfield, Virginia">Smithfield</a>, where a city facility called Nike Park includes a bike trail approximately 2<span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> miles in a loop.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Waterways">Waterways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffolk,_Virginia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Waterways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Suffolk was initially a port at the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_navigation" title="Head of navigation">head of navigation</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Nansemond_River" title="Nansemond River">Nansemond River</a>. The Nansemond flows into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_River_(Virginia)" title="James River (Virginia)">James River</a> near its mouth and the ice-free harbor of <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Roads" title="Hampton Roads">Hampton Roads</a>. Also Chesapeake is better.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Railroads">Railroads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffolk,_Virginia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Railroads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railroad" title="Railroad">railroads</a> completed through Suffolk before the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> were later joined by four more. These were eventually consolidated during the modern merger era of North American railroads which began around 1960. Today, Suffolk is served by three freight railroads, and is located on a potential line for high speed passenger rail service between Richmond and South Hampton Roads being studied by the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Department_of_Rail_and_Public_Transportation" title="Virginia Department of Rail and Public Transportation">Virginia Department of Rail and Public Transportation</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Highways">Highways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffolk,_Virginia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Highways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Suffolk is served by U.S. Highways <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Highway_17" title="U.S. Highway 17">17</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Highway_13" title="U.S. Highway 13">13</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Highway_58" title="U.S. Highway 58">58</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Highway_258" title="U.S. Highway 258">258</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Highway_460" title="U.S. Highway 460">460</a>. <a href="/wiki/Interstate_664" title="Interstate 664">Interstate 664</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Roads_Beltway" title="Hampton Roads Beltway">Hampton Roads Beltway</a>, crosses through the northeastern edge of the city. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Route_10_(Virginia)" title="Route 10 (Virginia)">State Route 10</a> is also a major highway in the area.</p>
<p>In 2006, Suffolk assumed control of its road system from the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Department_of_Transportation" title="Virginia Department of Transportation">Virginia Department of Transportation</a>, which is customary among Virginia's <a href="/wiki/Independent_city_(United_States)" title="Independent city (United States)">independent cities</a>, although since the <a href="/wiki/Byrd_Road_Act" title="Byrd Road Act">Byrd Road Act</a> of 1932 created Virginia's Secondary Roads System, which maintains the roads in most counties and town. An exception was made by the General Assembly when the former Nansemond County became an independent city and consolidated Suffolk in the 1970s. The state still maintained the primary and secondary routes in Suffolk until July 1, 2006.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bridges.2C_bridge-tunnel">Bridges, bridge-tunnel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffolk,_Virginia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Bridges, bridge-tunnel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Monitor%E2%80%93Merrimac_Memorial_Bridge%E2%80%93Tunnel" title="Monitor–Merrimac Memorial Bridge–Tunnel">Monitor–Merrimac Memorial Bridge–Tunnel</a> connects Suffolk to the independent city of <a href="/wiki/Newport_News,_Virginia" title="Newport News, Virginia">Newport News</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Peninsula" title="Virginia Peninsula">Virginia Peninsula</a> from <a href="/wiki/South_Hampton_Roads" title="South Hampton Roads">South Hampton Roads</a> and is part of the <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Roads_Beltway" title="Hampton Roads Beltway">Hampton Roads Beltway</a>, a circumferential interstate highway which links the seven largest cities of <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Roads" title="Hampton Roads">Hampton Roads</a>. Completed in 1992 it provided a third major vehicle crossing of the Hampton Roads harbor area and cost $400 million to build.</p>
<p>There have been conflicts with VDOT and the city over ownership and responsibility for the circa 1928 <a href="/wiki/Kings_Highway_Bridge" title="Kings Highway Bridge">Kings Highway Bridge</a> across the Nansemond River on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_Route_125_(Virginia)" title="State Route 125 (Virginia)">State Route 125</a>, which was closed in 2005 by VDOT for safety reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> About 3,300 motorists a day used the bridge that connected <a href="/wiki/Chuckatuck,_Virginia" title="Chuckatuck, Virginia">Chuckatuck</a> and <a href="/wiki/Driver,_Virginia" title="Driver, Virginia">Driver</a>. Now, they face detours of as much as 19 miles (31 km). The cost of a new bridge for the King's Highway crossing is estimated at $48 million, far more than could be recovered through collection of tolls at that location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In 2007, VDOT announced that it would contract for demolition and removal of the bridge. According to newspaper accounts, this will be the first time in VDOT's history of such action when no replacement facility was planned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Virginia is currently reviewing proposals under a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public-private_partnership" title="Public-private partnership">public-private partnership</a> for a major realignment and upgrade of U.S. 460 from Suffolk west to <a href="/wiki/Interstate_295_(Virginia)" title="Interstate 295 (Virginia)">Interstate 295</a> near <a href="/wiki/Petersburg,_Virginia" title="Petersburg, Virginia">Petersburg</a>. In 1995, the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_General_Assembly" title="Virginia General Assembly">Virginia General Assembly</a> passed the Public-Private Transportation Act allowing private entities to propose innovative solutions for designing, constructing, financing, and operating transportation improvements. The new roadway would be funded through collection of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toll_roads" title="Toll roads">tolls</a>.</p>
<p>As part of the Suffolk 2026 Comprehensive Plan, the city plans to bypass the crossroads community of <a href="/wiki/Whaleyville,_Virginia" title="Whaleyville, Virginia">Whaleyville</a> in southwestern Suffolk City. <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_13_in_North_Carolina" title="U.S. Route 13 in North Carolina">US 13</a> (along with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NC_11" title="NC 11">NC Highway 11</a>) is a strategic highway corridor in North Carolina towards <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenville,_NC" title="Greenville, NC">Greenville</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>In modern times, Suffolk remains a major <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanut</a> processing center and railroad and highway transportation hub. It hosts a diverse combination of industrial, manufacturing, distribution, retail, and hospitality businesses, as well as active farming.</p>
<p>In 2002, the new Louise Obici Memorial Hospital was completed and dedicated, acquired in 2005 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sentara_Health_System" title="Sentara Health System">Sentara Health System</a>. <a href="/wiki/Planters" title="Planters">Planters' Peanuts</a> has been a major employer, now owned by <a href="/wiki/Kraft_Foods" title="Kraft Foods">Kraft Foods</a>. Each fall since 1977, the City of Suffolk hosts Suffolk Festivals Incorporated's annual Peanut Fest. Other large employers in the City of Suffolk include <a href="/wiki/Unilever" title="Unilever">Unilever</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lipton" title="Lipton">Lipton Tea</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wal-Mart" title="Wal-Mart">Wal-Mart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Target_Corporation" title="Target Corporation">Target</a>, <a href="/wiki/QVC" title="QVC">QVC</a>, and two major modeling and simulation companies, <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Raytheon" title="Raytheon">Raytheon</a>. Lockheed Martin built its "Center for Innovation" around a lighthouse in Suffolk, for which the campus is called 'The Lighthouse'. Raytheon won a DoD contract to manufacture 'Miniature Air-Launched Decoy Jammers'(MALD-J), which it has been producing with Cobham Composite Products:202 vehicles for a price of $81 million .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-suff_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-suff-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Suffolk experienced a boom in its high tech economy given the presence of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Joint_Forces_Command" title="U.S. Joint Forces Command">U.S. Joint Forces Command</a> (JFCOM) facility near the intersection of US 17 and Interstate 664. In 1999, JFCOM stood up<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (October 2015)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and through the decade JFCOM employed more and more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defense_contractor" title="Defense contractor">defense contractors</a> until it reached over 3,000.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> By September 2010, US Secretary of Defense <a href="/wiki/Robert_Gates" title="Robert Gates">Robert Gates</a> recommended to decommission JFCOM as a matter of reallocating and rebalancing the U.S. Department of Defense budget to better address changing needs and fiscal demands. The announcement led to speculation about what impact the loss of JFCOM would have on the Hampton Roads economy in general and (more specifically) the sustainment of businesses located in the Harborview section of Suffolk. In August 2011 JFCOM was disestablished; many critical JFCOM functions like joint training, joint exercises and joint development were retained in the buildings vacated by JFCOM in north Suffolk under the auspices of the Joint Staff J7 Directorate, referred to as either "Pentagon South"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-suff_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-suff-20">[20]</a></sup> or "Joint and Coalition Warfighting".</p>
<p>By summer 2013, city officials expected the Naval Network Warfare Command, NNWC Global Network Operations Center Detachment, Navy Cyber Defense Operations Command and Navy Cyber Forces to occupy buildings vacated by JFCOM. These commands have been considered a boon to north Suffolk bringing an estimated 1,000 additional employees, counting military, civilians and contractors with an estimated annual payroll of $88.9 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-suff_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-suff-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffolk,_Virginia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Suffolk's daily newspapers are the local <i><a href="/wiki/Suffolk_News-Herald" title="Suffolk News-Herald">Suffolk News-Herald</a></i>, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginian-Pilot" title="Virginian-Pilot">Virginian-Pilot</a></i> from Norfolk and the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Press_(Virginia)" title="Daily Press (Virginia)">Daily Press</a></i> of Newport News. Other papers include the <i><a href="/wiki/New_Journal_and_Guide" title="New Journal and Guide">New Journal and Guide</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Inside_Business_(newspaper)" title="Inside Business (newspaper)">Inside Business</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hampton_Roads_News_Links_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hampton_Roads_News_Links-21">[21]</a></sup> <i>Coastal Virginia Magazine</i> serves as a bi-monthly regional magazine for Suffolk and the <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Roads" title="Hampton Roads">Hampton Roads</a> area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coastal_Virginia_Magazine_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coastal_Virginia_Magazine-22">[22]</a></sup> <a class="external text" href="http://www.hamptonroadstimes.com/" rel="nofollow">Hampton Roads Times</a> serves as an online magazine for all the Hampton Roads cities and counties. Suffolk is served by a variety of radio stations on the AM and FM dials, with towers located around the <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Roads" title="Hampton Roads">Hampton Roads</a> area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hampton_Roads_Radio_Links_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hampton_Roads_Radio_Links-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Suffolk is also served by several television stations. The Hampton Roads <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Designated_market_area" title="Designated market area">designated market area</a> (DMA) is the 42nd largest in the U.S. with 712,790 homes (0.64% of the total U.S.).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nielsen_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-nielsen-24">[24]</a></sup> The major network television affiliates are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WTKR-TV" title="WTKR-TV">WTKR-TV</a> 3 (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Columbia_Broadcasting_Service" title="Columbia Broadcasting Service">CBS</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WAVY" title="WAVY">WAVY</a> 10 (<a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WVEC-TV" title="WVEC-TV">WVEC-TV</a> 13 (<a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a>), <a href="/wiki/WGNT" title="WGNT">WGNT</a> 27 (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_CW_Television_Network" title="The CW Television Network">CW</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WTVZ" title="WTVZ">WTVZ</a> 33 (<a href="/wiki/MyNetworkTV" title="MyNetworkTV">MyNetworkTV</a>), <a href="/wiki/WVBT" title="WVBT">WVBT</a> 43 (<a href="/wiki/Fox_Broadcasting_Company" title="Fox Broadcasting Company">FOX</a>), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WPXV" title="WPXV">WPXV</a> 49 (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ION_Television" title="ION Television">ION Television</a>). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Broadcasting_Service" title="Public Broadcasting Service">Public Broadcasting Service</a> station is <a href="/wiki/WHRO-TV" title="WHRO-TV">WHRO-TV</a> 15. Suffolk residents also can receive independent stations, such as <a href="/wiki/WSKY-TV" title="WSKY-TV">WSKY</a> broadcasting on channel 4 from the Outer Banks of North Carolina and <a href="/wiki/WGBS-LD" title="WGBS-LD">WGBS-LD</a> broadcasting on channel 11 from <a href="/wiki/Hampton,_Virginia" title="Hampton, Virginia">Hampton</a>. Suffolk is served by Charter Communications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The City of Suffolk Media &amp; Community Relations Department operates Municipal Channel 8 on the local <a href="/wiki/Charter" title="Charter">Charter</a> <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">Cable television</a> system. Programming includes television coverage of many City activities and events, including live <a href="/wiki/Government-access_television" title="Government-access television">Government-access television</a> (GATV) broadcasts of all regular City Council meetings, and special features including "On The Scene", "Suffolk Seniorcize", and "Suffolk Business Today". <a href="/wiki/DirecTV" title="DirecTV">DirecTV</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dish_Network" title="Dish Network">Dish Network</a> are also popular as an alternative to cable television in Suffolk.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Boroughs">Boroughs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffolk,_Virginia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Boroughs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Suffolk is divided politically into seven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Borough_(Virginia)" title="Borough (Virginia)">boroughs</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> one corresponding to the former city of Suffolk and one corresponding to each of the six magisterial districts of the former <a href="/wiki/Nansemond_County,_Virginia" title="Nansemond County, Virginia">Nansemond County</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The boroughs are <a href="/wiki/Chuckatuck,_Virginia" title="Chuckatuck, Virginia">Chuckatuck</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Cypress,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Holy Neck,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nansemond,_Virginia" title="Nansemond, Virginia">Nansemond</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Sleepy Hole,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Suffolk,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Whaleyville,_Virginia" title="Whaleyville, Virginia">Whaleyville</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sister_cities">Sister cities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffolk,_Virginia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Sister cities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1981, Suffolk County, England became Suffolk's first sister city as a result of the personal interest in the Sister Cities concept by Virginia's Governor, Mills E. Godwin. A native of the city, Governor Godwin believed that Sister Cities would benefit the community culturally and educationally. Suffolk's second sister city relationship with Oderzo, Italy, began in 1995 because of one man, Amedeo Obici. Mr. Obici was a native of Oderzo and the founder of Planters Nut and Chocolate Company in Suffolk.</p>
<p>Suffolk Sister Cities International, Inc. (SSCI) is a 501 (c)(3) nonprofit established to promote international relationships as directed by Suffolk City Council through its appointed Suffolk Sister Cities Commission. Its membership is open to all who are interested in fostering the goals of the organization.</p>
<p>SSCI and its international youth association, SIYA, have won national awards for Youth and Education and for the Best Overall Program for cities with populations less than 100,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffolk,_Virginia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The climate in this area is characterized by hot, humid summers and generally mild to cool winters. According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_Climate_Classification" title="Köppen Climate Classification">Köppen Climate Classification</a> system, Suffolk has a <a href="/wiki/Humid_subtropical_climate" title="Humid subtropical climate">humid subtropical climate</a>, abbreviated "Cfa" on climate maps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
