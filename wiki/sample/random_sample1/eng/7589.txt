<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Surfboard</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>surfboard</b> is an elongated platform used in the sport of surfing. Surfboards are relatively light, but are strong enough to support an individual standing on them while riding a Ocean surface wave breaking wave. They were invented in ancient Hawaii, where they were known as <i>papa he'e nalu</i> in the Hawaiian language, they were usually made of <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a> from local trees, such as <a href="/wiki/Acacia_koa" title="Acacia koa">koa</a>, and were often over 15 feet (5Â m) in length and extremely heavy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Major advances over the years include the addition of one or more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fins" title="Fins">fins</a> on the bottom rear of the board to improve <a href="/wiki/Directional_stability" title="Directional stability">directional stability</a>, and numerous improvements in materials and shape.</p>
<p>Modern surfboards are made of <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">polyurethane</a> or <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">polystyrene</a> foam covered with layers of <a href="/wiki/Fiberglass" title="Fiberglass">fiberglass</a> cloth, and <a href="/wiki/Polyester" title="Polyester">polyester</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epoxy_resin" title="Epoxy resin">epoxy resin</a>. The result is a light and strong surfboard that is buoyant and maneuverable. Recent developments in surfboard technology have included the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber" title="Carbon fiber">carbon fiber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kevlar" title="Kevlar">kevlar</a> composites. Each year, approximately 400,000 surfboards are manufactured.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bottom">Bottom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Bottom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Surfboardbottoms.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="472" data-file-width="295" height="352" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Surfboardbottoms.jpg/220px-Surfboardbottoms.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/50/Surfboardbottoms.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The surface of the board that rests on the water, usually concave but sometimes convex.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Concave">Concave</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Concave">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Modern surfboards often contain multiple contours on the bottom of the board, termed <i>concaves</i>. These concaves have different uses and vary among different types of surfboards. Most concaves on the modern shortboard begin about twelve inches (300Â mm) back from the nose of the board on the bottom and then carry out through the middle to the tail of the surfboard. The purpose of concave is to direct water through the fins of the surfboard. Surfboard shapers sometimes experiment with concaves to create different drive and response characteristics on each individual surfboard.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Convex">Convex</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Convex">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some older and more traditional surfboards along with many modern boards that take inspiration from these older boards utilize a convex rather than concave design on the bottom of the surfboard. These boards displace more water and sit lower in the wave than a surfboard with a concave bottom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deck">Deck</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Deck">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>deck</i> is the surface of the board that the surfer stands on. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surfwax" title="Surfwax">Surfwax</a> is applied to this surface. Wax comes in different degrees of hardness allowing its application in differing water temperatures.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fins">Fins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Surf.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Surf.jpg/220px-Surf.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Surf.jpg/330px-Surf.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Surf.jpg/440px-Surf.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Surf.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A man standing on a board as the wave passes under him.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surfboard_Fins" title="Surfboard Fins">surfboard fin</a> is a stabilizing rudder fixed to the rear of the surfboard to prevent it from sliding sideways. In the early days, surfers would stabilize the board by hanging the toes of their back foot over the edge of the board and would steer by putting their foot in the water. The American surfer <a href="/wiki/Tom_Blake_(surfer)" title="Tom Blake (surfer)">Tom Blake</a> was the first to experiment with adding a fin to a surfboard, fastening the keel from an old speedboat to a surfboard in 1935.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> About one or two years later, <a href="/wiki/Woody_Brown_(surfer)" title="Woody Brown (surfer)">Woody "Spider" Brown</a> independently developed a similar design, but Brown himself gave Blake precedence: "(I made my first surfboard keel) about '36 or '37, somewhere in there; about the same time. But, I didn't know anything about (Blake) and his experiments with adding fins to surfboards. See, we were all separated out. I was in San Diego and he was in L.A., way up there.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This innovation revolutionized surfing, allowing surfers to direct the board's momentum and providing more balance when turning.</p>
<p>The template of the modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surfboard_Fins" title="Surfboard Fins">surfboard fin</a> was developed by <a href="/wiki/George_Greenough" title="George Greenough">George Greenough</a> in the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The single fin changed little until the late 70s, when a second was added and popularised by Australian <a href="/wiki/Mark_Richards_(surfer)" title="Mark Richards (surfer)">Mark Richards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In October 1980, after seeing a twin fin surfboard with a "trigger point" fin <a href="/wiki/Simon_Anderson" title="Simon Anderson">Simon Anderson</a> had the idea for a new, equal size, version of the existing three fin prototypes which was later dubbed the "thruster".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Surf_Transworld_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Surf_Transworld-8">[8]</a></sup> He created a prototype and 30 years later his "thruster" design is still the most popular fin design for surfboards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Surf_Transworld_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Surf_Transworld-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early 90s removable fin systems were developed and embraced. This provides a standardized system that allows fins to be easily removed or replaced, utilizing <a href="/wiki/Set_screw" title="Set screw">set screws</a> to hold the fins in place. These systems provided surfers with the ability to alter the riding characteristics of a surfboard, by changing the size and shape of fins used. This innovation opened the market to a range of fin designs, including single foiled fins, concave inside surfaces, and curved fins. Another variation of fin was later designed in the time frame known as the soul fin, a sleek bendable attachment.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tunnel_fins" title="Tunnel fins">Tunnel fins</a> were invented in the 60's by Richard Deese,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and were found on longboards by multiple manufacturers of that era, including Dewey Weber. Bob Bolen, A.K.A. 'the Greek', patented the "Turbo Tunnel" in the late 1990s. Since the mid 90s half tunnel fins have mainly been used on very long hollow wooden surfboards mainly surfed by Roy Stuart.</p>
<p>Bullet Fins were invented in the 2005 by Ron Pettibone to increase surfboard hull planing and rail-to-rail transition speed. The patent-pending fins are based on 50 years of hydrodynamic research on the bulbous bow hull design. Just as with the bow of a ship, the traditional surfboard fin creates a wave as it displaces the water in its path. The resulting turbulence places drag on the surfboard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The bulb of the Bullet Fin reduces this drag by creating a new (primary) fin wave in front of the original (secondary) wave. This new bulb wave is designed to be nearly 180 degrees out of phase with the original fin wave to subtract its turbulence thus reducing fin drag.</p>
<p>Winged fins are another type of surfboard fin, the genesis of which was America's Cup sailboat design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The Starfin was designed in the 1980s by the America's Cup yacht designer, <a href="/wiki/Ben_Lexcen" title="Ben Lexcen">Ben Lexcen</a>, who had designed the <a href="/wiki/Winged_keel" title="Winged keel">winged keel</a> for the America's Cup boat, Australia II. The small thruster-sized fin, the RedTip 3D is manufactured by FCS.</p>
<p>Fins with wingletsâtiny wingsâwere invented in 2005. The purpose of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winglets" title="Winglets">winglets</a>, as in airplane design, is to increase lift (horizontal turning force in the case of surfboard fins) while reducing drag, by reducing the fin-tip vortex.</p>
<p>Fins with a camber have an asymmetrical profile. In windsurfing camber is used to increase the lift to drag ratio of the fin and to minimise cavitation and the risk of spin-out. In particular windsurfers trying to improve speed records use camber fins, as the maximum performance is required on one down-wind course direction. As the camber is fixed to one side, performance when sailing in one direction is improved but performance in the other way is deteriorated.</p>
<p>Fins with self-adjusting camber offer the improved qualities in both port side and starboard side sailing directions.</p>
<p>Spitfire fins are based on the <a href="/wiki/Wing_configuration" title="Wing configuration">Wing configuration</a> used by Spitfire aeroplanes. The <a href="/wiki/Elliptical_wing" title="Elliptical wing">elliptical wing</a> shapes work very well as surfboard fins and several manufacturers make fins with this more upright stance, as it increases drive and maneuverability.</p>
<p>In 2004 Frank Fish <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> introduced the world to Whale Bumps with their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tubercle_Effect" title="Tubercle Effect">Tubercle Effect</a> Several fin manufacturers tried making some fins at that time, after the article was discussed on Swaylock's design Forum. The process of grinding bumps, which are properly foiled, into an existing fin is an arduous, time consuming task. Hand foiling tubercles can take up to 40 hours. Roy Stuart <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> worked on wooden prototypes for years before creating the first <a href="/wiki/Polycarbonate" title="Polycarbonate">polycarbonate</a>, 3d printed, whale bumped surfboard fins in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/3D_printing" title="3D printing">3D printing</a> may well be the next frontier in surf fin manufacture.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Thrusters_and_Tri-fins">Thrusters and Tri-fins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Thrusters and Tri-fins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The tri-fin's design attempts to incorporate the glide of a longboard and the performance of a shortboard into a single layout.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-15">[15]</a></sup> The additional fins ensure that even what riding down the line, two, or at least one, vertical control surface is in black water (not unstable foam) allows riders better turning capabilities. In a Thruster, the symmetric, usually larger, single center fin is flanked by twin asymmetric, cambered fins. The camber is angled front in and top in, directing energy from the incoming wave to lift the board and straighten it, not dissimilar from the force vectoring provided by the geometry of a rocket's nozzle.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Quad">Quad</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Quad">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A "Quad" four fins, typically arranged as two pairs of thrusters in wing formation, which are quick down the line but tend to lose energy through turns. The energy is lost as the board goes up the wave because the fins are now vectoring energy from the oncoming water toward the back of the board, bleeding speed.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leash">Leash</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Leash">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Surf_board_leash.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1674" data-file-width="1674" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Surf_board_leash.jpg/220px-Surf_board_leash.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Surf_board_leash.jpg/330px-Surf_board_leash.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Surf_board_leash.jpg/440px-Surf_board_leash.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Surf_board_leash.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The leash attaches a surfboard to the surfer.</div>

<p>A surfboard leash or <a href="/wiki/Leg_rope" title="Leg rope">leg rope</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Rope" title="Rope">cord</a> that attaches a surfboard to the surfer. It prevents the surfboard from being swept away by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_surface_wave" title="Ocean surface wave">waves</a> and stops runaway surfboards from hitting other surfers and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swimmer" title="Swimmer">swimmers</a>. Modern leashes comprise a <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">urethane</a> <a href="/wiki/Rope" title="Rope">cord</a> where one end has a band with a <a href="/wiki/Velcro" title="Velcro">velcro</a> strap attached to the surfer's trailing foot, and the opposite has a velcro strap attached to the tail end of the surfboard.</p>
<p>Prior to leashes' introduction in 1971, surfers who fell off their boards had to swim to retrieve them with runaway boards being an inconvenience to the surfer and a danger to other surfers. <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz,_California" title="Santa Cruz, California">Santa Cruz</a> resident Pat O'Neill is credited with popularizing the surf leash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-surfline_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-surfline-17">[17]</a></sup> His initial designs consisted of <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgical</a> cord attached to a board with a <a href="/wiki/Suction_cup" title="Suction cup">suction cup</a>. At the 1971 <a href="/wiki/Malibu,_California" title="Malibu, California">Malibu</a> international surfing competition, Pat offered leashes to his competitors in the event. Consequently, he was disqualified from the event for wearing his leash, dubbed a <i>kook cord</i> by those at the event. However, over the next year, the leash became a ubiquitous tool in the surfing world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-surfline_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-surfline-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jack_O%27Neill_(businessman)" title="Jack O'Neill (businessman)">Jack O'Neill</a> lost his left eye in a surf leash <a href="/wiki/Accident" title="Accident">accident</a> as the surgical tubing used in the early designs allowed the leash to <a href="/wiki/Deformation_(engineering)" title="Deformation (engineering)">overstretch</a>, causing the surfboard to fly back towards the surfer. Subsequent cords were made with less elastic materials such as <a href="/wiki/Bungee_cord" title="Bungee cord">bungee cords</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-surfline_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-surfline-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ultimately, urethane was the material of choice for leashes. The urethane design was patented by David Hattrick (Australian Patent 505,451 issued September 5, 1977). However, he built the prototype in 1972 while surfing an isolated surf break called Cactus in the Great Australian Bight. Necessity was the mother of invention as one of the largest breeding grounds for white pointer sharks was nearby.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> He then settled in the Margaret River area of Western Australian initially making leg ropes of this design for surfing friends. Later in the 70's, he established Pipe Lines surfing products and developed a design that could be patented. This design also won an Australian Design Award in 1979.</p>
<p>Leashes are still the source of some contention in surfing today as, although they are now accepted as mandatory equipment for shortboarders, many longboarders refuse to wear them, claiming it interferes with their ability to walk up and down the board.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> At crowded surf spots with large waves, it is argued that the freedom of not wearing a leash is secondary to the safety of others.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Leash_cup">Leash cup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Leash cup">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Also known as a "leash plug", is an indentation in the deck of the board close to the tail that contains a small metal bar that a short cord can be girth hitched to for attaching a leash.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nose">Nose</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Nose">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The front tip of the board. This can be pointed or rounded and can be made with a steep incline ("rocker", see below) or a gentle one.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tail">Tail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Tail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The shape of the tail affects how a board responds. Tail shapes vary from square, pin, squash, swallow, diamond, and so onâeach one in turn having its own family of smaller variants.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traction_Pad">Traction Pad</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Traction Pad">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Surfboard traction pads, deck grips, tailpads. There are several names for this piece of foam applied to the top of a surfboard to increase the grip and allow surfers to have more control and perform more high performance maneuvers. Traction pads are used on both shortboards and longboards, usually applied to the tail area of a surfboard. There are several brands of surfboard traction pads on the market, but the most popular brands include; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dakine" title="Dakine">Dakine</a>, Pro-Lite, OAM and Famous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rails">Rails</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Rails">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The edges of the board. A rounded rail is called "soft", while a more squared off rail is called "hard", and rails that are in between are termed "50/50" ("fifty-fifty"). Larger, fuller rails contain a greater volume of foam giving the board increased buoyancy along the edge, while sharper, narrower rails have less volume, making the board easier to "sink" and "lean on edge". While riding down the line, one rail is always in the water while the other is suspended freely in the air. Turns are largely a matter of transitioning from rail to tail and over to the contralateral (opposing) rail.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rocker">Rocker</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Rocker">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This refers to the vertical curve of the board between nose and tail. Rockers may be described as either heavy (steeply curved) or relaxed (less curved) and may be either continuous (a single curve between tip of nose and end of tail) or staged (distinct flat section in middle portion of board). The <i>nose rocker</i> or <i>flip</i> is the curve between the front tip and the middle or flattest portion of the board, and the <i>tail rocker</i> or <i>kick</i> is that between the tail and the middle/ flattest portion. An increase in flip helps keep the board from "pearling"; larger boards often require a greater flip. A larger kick adds maneuverability and lift to the tail at speed and provides tail sensitivity in critical turns. More relaxed rockers help the board to handle better on flatter sections of water, while heavy rockers increase the board's overall <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Form_drag" title="Form drag">form drag</a> but also give true <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift</a> when reaching <a href="/wiki/Planing_(boat)" title="Planing (boat)">planing speed</a> and have a smaller turning radius.</p>
<p>The board's rails and deck may also be referred to as having rocker. A board with a v-shaped tail, for example, has had the lower/ outer portion of its rear rails reduced, increasing its tail rail rocker. Having a flat, even deck rocker will increase a board's flexibility, while a convex deck rocker creates a board that is thicker along the board's stringer and stiffer in the water.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stringer">Stringer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Stringer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In board design, the "stringer" is a board's central plane of reflection, down the middle of its deck and its keel. In construction, the stringer can have no special parts, or can embed a stiff, thin, vertical slat, usually of wood but sometimes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber" title="Carbon fiber">carbon fiber</a>, running from nose to tail. The stringer serves to increase the board's overall strength and reduce its flexibility. Some boards have multiple stringers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To achieve positive buoyancy and a stiff deck, shapers have always reached for a foam, often hardened with a tensile skin, using <a href="/wiki/Toucan" title="Toucan">Toucan</a> beak engineering concepts.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polyurethane_.28P.U..29_boards">Polyurethane (P.U.) boards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Polyurethane (P.U.) boards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Surfboards have traditionally been constructed using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyurethane_foam" title="Polyurethane foam">polyurethane foam</a> and it remains a popular choice. They are made stronger with one or more stringers going down the middle of the board. The foam is molded into a "blank", in the rough shape of a surfboard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Once the blanks have been made they are given to shapers. Shapers then cut, plane, and sand the board to its specifications. Finally, the board is covered in one or more layers of <a href="/wiki/Fiberglass" title="Fiberglass">fiberglass</a> cloth and resin. It is during this stage that the fins or boxes for removable fins are attached and the leash plug installed. Another method of making boards is using epoxy resin and prolapse <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">polystyrene</a> foam, instead of polyester resin and polyurethane foam. In recent years, surfboards made out of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balsa" title="Balsa">balsa</a> and a polystyrene core are becoming more popular. Even solid balsa surfboards are available.</p>
<p>Although foam boards are usually shaped by hand, the use of machines to shape them has become increasingly popular. Vacuum forming and modern sandwich construction techniques borrowed from other industries have also become common. Many surfers have switched to riding sandwich-construction epoxy boards which have become especially popular with beginner surfers as they provide a durable, inexpensive, entry-level board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Balsa_boards">Balsa boards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Balsa boards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ochroma_pyramidale" title="Ochroma pyramidale">Ochroma pyramidale</a> wood's surfboard history originates in the Hawaiians, and the wood lead surfing's landfall on the US west coast in the 1940s. Being light and strong, balsa wood was long considered a perfect material for surfboards. Shapers could not use this fragile wood to make entire surfboards until after WWII, when fiberglass skins were invented.</p>
<p>Balsa wood boards are lighter, more buoyant and easier to handle than other boards. These boards have some disadvantages, however: they are not as sturdy as solid redwood boards.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hollow_wooden_boards">Hollow wooden boards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Hollow wooden boards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hollow wooden surfboards are made of wood and <a href="/wiki/Epoxy" title="Epoxy">epoxy</a> or oil (as a sustainable alternative to epoxy), and are a reversion to using wood after the foam became dominant in the 1950s. Hollow wooden surfboards specifically have no foam in their construction. (Boards made with foam and wood are commonly known as <i>compsands</i> or <i>veneer boards</i>.) Various construction methods are used to hollow the inside of the surfboard and lighten the weight of the completed board. Generally, a hollow wood surfboard is 30% to 300% heavier than a standard foam and resin surfboard. The main inspiration, apart from beauty, is that this is a more environmentally friendly method of construction (compared to epoxy and polyurethane methods) which uses fast-growing plantation wood such as <a href="/wiki/Paulownia" title="Paulownia">paulownia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cedrus" title="Cedrus">cedar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spruce" title="Spruce">spruce</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redwood" title="Redwood">redwood</a>, and, of course, balsa.</p>
<p>The current construction methods descend from the 1930s Tom Blake <a href="/wiki/Paddleboarding" title="Paddleboarding">paddleboarding</a> method, which favors a central stringer with individually shaped transverse ribs covered with a skin and rails. A modern interpretation of Tom Blake's work is the perimeter stringer method used by some manufacturers, utilizing laminated rails as stringers connected with a series of plywood ribs. This skeleton is then sheathed with 5mm-thick wood strips, creating a fast hollow board with good flex properties.</p>
<p>The parallel profile system was developed from cold molded (double diagonal) boat building, and uses at least four layers of material laminated over a male mold into a curved blank, including enough wood for rails, which are then shaped.</p>
<p>The chambering method follows a system in which planks of paulownia wood are selected and the rocker of the board is cut into each. The planks are then chambered to reduce weight, and then bonded together to form a hollow or "chambered" blank.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Board_types_and_variations">Board types and variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Board types and variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shortboard">Shortboard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Shortboard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since the late 1960s when Gordon Clark found the optimum formulation of urethane foam, many of the surfboards in common use have been of the shortboard variety between 6 feet and 7 feet in length with a pointed nose and a rounded or squarish tail, typically with three "skegs" (fins) but sometimes with two or as many as five. Surfers generally find a shortboard quick to maneuver compared with other types of surfboards, but because of a lack of flotation due to the smaller size, the shortboard is harder to catch waves with, often requiring steeper, larger and more powerful waves and very late takeoffs, where the surfer catches the wave at the critical moment before it breaks.</p>
<p>A <i>Bonzer</i> is a surfboard designed by the Campbell Brothers that can have three or five fins and is punctuated with a large center fin and 2-4 smaller side fins(side bites). This, combined with deep double concave channels creates a distinctive board. The manufacturer has shown that these channels create versital and controlled characteristics using the <a href="/wiki/Venturi_effect" title="Venturi effect">venturi effect</a> which guides the water off of the surface of the board through a narrowed passage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hybrid">Hybrid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Hybrid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Modern hybrid boards are usually 6 feet to 8 feet 6 inches (1.8â2.3Â m) in length with a more rounded profile and tail shape. Surfed in smaller waves with any fin set up. They are more about having fun than high performance or tricks. They can be easier to ride for beginning surfers and generally perform well in surfing conditions where the more traditional long and short boards might not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fish">Fish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Fish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Usually a short stubby board under 6 feet (1.8Â m) in length developed from kneeboards in 1967 by Steve Lis. Other prominent fish shapers include <a href="/wiki/Skip_Frye" title="Skip Frye">Skip Frye</a>, Larry Mabile and Steve Brom. Primarily a twin fin set up with a swallow tail shape and popular in smaller waves, the fish enjoyed a resurgence in popularity in the early 2000s after legendary surfer <a href="/wiki/Tom_Curren" title="Tom Curren">Tom Curren</a> rode one during an ASP event at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hossegor" title="Hossegor">Hossegor</a>. Note, any type of board (such as shortboard or mini-longboard) can have a fish tail, and these are commonly referred to as a "fish", but they lack the other properties of a traditional, or "retro" fish as described here.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Funboard">Funboard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Funboard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The funboard combines elements of both shortboards and longboards and are generally midsized, usually 7 to 8 feet (2.1 to 2.4Â m). The funboard's design allows waves to be caught more easily than a shortboard, yet with a shape that makes it more maneuverable than a longboard; hence it is a popular type of surfboard, especially among beginners or those transitioning from longboarding to the more difficult shortboarding. It is considered a good combination of the speed of a longboard and the maneuverability of a shortboard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gun">Gun</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Gun">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Big wave boards of length 7 to 12 feet (2.1 to 3.7Â m) are considered <i>guns</i>. These have a thin, almost needle-like profile with single, quad, or thruster fin set up. It has the appearance of a shortboard but at a longboard size. Guns are often used at big waves locations such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waimea_Bay" title="Waimea Bay">Waimea Bay</a>, Jaws (Hawaii), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mavericks_(location)" title="Mavericks (location)">Mavericks</a>.</p>

<p>The longboard is primarily a single-finned surfboard with large rounded nose and length of 9 to 12 feet (2.7 to 3.7Â m). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noseriding" title="Noseriding">Noseriders</a> are a class of longboards which enable the rider to walk to the tip and <a href="/wiki/Nose_ride" title="Nose ride">nose ride</a>. These are also called "Mals", a shortened form of "Malibu boards". They range from 9 to 14 feet (2.7 to 4.3Â m) long, or 3 feet (0.91Â m) taller than the rider in overall length. The advantage of a longboard is its substantial buoyancy and planing surface enable surfers to ride waves generally deemed too small to propel a shortboard. Longboards are more suitable for beginners because of the board's size and ease of catching waves. In the proper conditions, a skilled surfer can ride a wave standing on the nose of a longboard with their toes over the nose's edge: in this way, with the "toes on the nose", the surfer can "hang ten". Compared to the shortboards, longboards are considerably easier to learn on as they are capable of achieving stability, thus making the transition quite difficult.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Classic_longboards">Classic longboards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Classic longboards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Longboards are the original and very first variety of board used in standup surfing. Ever since the sixth-century <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">CE</a> the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Hawaii" title="Ancient Hawaii">ancient Hawaiians</a> have used 9-to-30-foot (2.7 to 9.1Â m) solid wooden boards when practicing their ancient art of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hoe_he%27e_nalu" title="Hoe he'e nalu">Hoe he'e nalu</a>. Surfing was brought to the Hawaiian Islands by Polynesians and has since become popular worldwide. The ancient boards were carved and fashioned out of solid wood, reaching lengths of 10 to 14 feet (3.0 to 4.3Â m) long and weighing as much as 150 pounds (68Â kg). Both men and women, royalty and commoners surfed. But the longest of boards (the Olo) was reserved for royalty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> During the 19th century, some extreme western missionaries actively discouraged surfing, viewing it as sinful. Surfing almost died out completely. In recent times replicas have been made of Olo's and alaia's by experienced surfers and shapers wishing to explore the roots of the sport.</p>
<p>By the early 20th century, only a handful of people surfed, mostly at Waikiki. But there, it started to grow again. Beginning in 1912, <a href="/wiki/Duke_Kahanamoku" title="Duke Kahanamoku">Duke Kahanamoku</a>, a Hawaiian Olympic swimmer in the early 1900s, brought surfing to mainland <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. Because of this, Duke is considered the "Father of Modern Surfing". From that point on, surfing became an integral part of the <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> beach lifestyle. In <a href="/wiki/Malibu,_California" title="Malibu, California">Malibu</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> county), the beach was so popular amongst the early surfers that it lent its name to the type of longboard, the Malibu Surfboard. In the 1920s boards made of <a href="/wiki/Plywood" title="Plywood">plywood</a> or planking called Hollowboards came into use. These were typically 15 to 20 feet (4.6 to 6.1Â m) in length and very light. During the 1950s, the surf trend took off dramatically as it obtained a substantial amount of popularity as a sport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The design and material of longboards in the 1950s changed from using solid wood, to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balsa" title="Balsa">balsa</a> wood. The length of the boards still remained the same at an average of 10.5 feet, and had then become widely produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was not until the late 1950s and early 1960s when the surfboard design had closely evolved into today's modern longboard. The introduction of <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">polyurethane</a> foam and <a href="/wiki/Fiberglass" title="Fiberglass">fiberglass</a> became the technological leap in design. In the 1960s, the longboard continued to remain popular as its material changed from balsa wood to fiberglass and polyurethane foam. In the 1960s, the introduction of the shortboard, averaging 6Â feet 6Â inches (1.98Â m), allowed surfers to make tighter turns, quicker maneuvers, and achieve faster speeds, thus radically changing the way people surfed. This "shortboard revolution" nearly made longboards obsolete for all practical purposes. But in the early 1990s, the longboard returned, integrating a number of the design features invented during the shortboard revolution. Surfers rediscovered the grace and poise â the "glide" â of the longboard, and the fun of classic maneuvers that are not possible on a shortboard. In some circles the battle between longboards and shortboards continues. But many surfers live by a philosophy of finding the joy of surfing a mix of boards and surfing styles to suit the waves of the day.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_longboards">Modern longboards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Modern longboards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The modern longboard has undergone many changes since its earlier models in the past. Today's longboard is much lighter than its predecessors. Its polyurethane foam and fiberglass design allows less drag on waves. Today's longboards are typically 8 to 10 feet (2.4 to 3.0Â m) long, although some ride boards up to 12 feet (3.7Â m) in length. Additionally, there is a revival of stand-up paddle-based surfing with boards up to 14 feet (4.3Â m) in length (for stability). The classic single-fin longboard retains much of its classic design including a single fin, weight, and considerable buoyancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-15">[15]</a></sup> A longboard with a single fin allows the board to pivot turn in order to remain in the curl of the wave. Due to recent advances in technology, the longboard has expanded its family into different variations of the classic longboard.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_2.2B1">The 2+1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: The 2+1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The 2+1 longboard is the most versatile board of the longboard family, offering greater maneuverability. Sometimes referred as a "single-fin with training wheels", the 2+1's fins actually takes the features of the classic longboard and the Tri-fin. The fins of the 2+1 takes the rigid stability of a classic longboard, and fuses with the strength and drive of a Tri-fin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mini_Tanker">Mini Tanker</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Mini Tanker">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The mini tanker is basically a shortened longboard shape that utilizes the same longboard design elements and enhanced maneuverability due to the shorter shape. These boards are normally ridden by women and children which provide smaller individuals with much more control than your traditional 9 foot longboard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Malibu">The Malibu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: The Malibu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Named after <a href="/wiki/Malibu,_California" title="Malibu, California">Malibu, California</a>, this racy longboard shape is a little narrower than most, with slightly pulled in nose and tail sections for extra maneuverability on Malibu's famous right hand point breaks. This classic shape has been ridden and praised by experienced surfers for its maneuverability and performance. Classic tricks that can be performed on a Malibu are "Hang Fives" and "Hang tens" whereby the surfer walks to the nose of the board and hangs 5 or 10 toes over the nose, the "soul arch", drop knee turns, head dips, switch stance maneuvers, and so on.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Olo">Olo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Olo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Originally reserved for Hawaiian royalty due to its size and weight, these wooden boards can exceed lengths of 24 feet (7.3Â m) and reach weights up to 200 pounds (91Â kg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Alaia">Alaia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Alaia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A traditional finless wooden surfboard, typically ridden by ancient native Hawaiians. The surfboard typically runs 17 feet (5.2Â m) 200 pounds (91Â kg)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>However modern day Alaias can be much thinner. Many are a mere 3/4 of an inch and can be as short as 6 feet (1.8Â m). Common woods used in current construction are paulownia, cedar, and other woods suitable for salty ocean waters. The tails come in different styles. The boards are notoriously difficult to ride.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tandem">Tandem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surfboard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Tandem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The first stand up surfboard ridden in Australia by <a href="/wiki/Duke_Kahanamoku" title="Duke Kahanamoku">Duke Kahanamoku</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isabel_Letham" title="Isabel Letham">Isabel Letham</a> is an oversized longboard with enough volume to support two people.</p>
