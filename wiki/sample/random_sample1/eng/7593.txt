<p><b>Sohae Satellite Launching Station</b> (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Chosŏn'gŭl</a>: <span lang="ko-Hang" xml:lang="ko-Hang">서해위성발사장</span>; <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">Hancha</a>: <span lang="ko-Hani" xml:lang="ko-Hani">西海衛星發射場</span>; <a href="/wiki/McCune%E2%80%93Reischauer" title="McCune–Reischauer">MR</a>: <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Korean transliteration">Sohae Wisŏng Palsajang</span></i>, also known as <b>Tongch'ang-dong Space Launch Center</b> and <b>Pongdong-ri</b>) is a rocket launching site in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cholsan" title="Cholsan">Cholsan County</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Pyongan" title="North Pyongan">North Pyongan</a> Province, <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>. The base is located among hills close to the northern border with <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. The spaceport was built on the site of the village Pongdong-ri which was displaced during construction. It was the site for the 13 April 2012 launch of the North Korean satellite <a href="/wiki/Kwangmy%C5%8Fngs%C5%8Fng-3" title="Kwangmyŏngsŏng-3">Kwangmyŏngsŏng-3</a>, which was launched to celebrate the 100th anniversary of the birth of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kim_Il-Sung" title="Kim Il-Sung">Kim Il-Sung</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naenara_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-naenara-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dprk_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-dprk-2">[2]</a></sup> The rocket launch failed, but on 12 December of the same year <a href="/wiki/Kwangmy%C5%8Fngs%C5%8Fng-3_Unit_2" title="Kwangmyŏngsŏng-3 Unit 2">Kwangmyŏngsŏng-3 Unit 2</a> was successfully launched and brought into Earth orbit.</p>
<p>The launches were controversial as they were dismissed by the US as tests of <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_missile" title="Ballistic missile">ballistic missile</a> technology and hence of breach of an agreement made between North Korea and the USA in February 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dprk2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-dprk2-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sohae_Satellite_Launching_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Signs of construction were visible during the early 1990s and became more pronounced by the early 2000s. A major progress in the construction was discovered in 2008 by <a href="/wiki/Jane%27s_Information_Group" title="Jane's Information Group">Jane's Information Group</a>, which requested imagery from the satellite company <a href="/wiki/DigitalGlobe" title="DigitalGlobe">DigitalGlobe</a>. Movements from the Sanum’dong missile research factory with the erector transporter and railroad, road transportation to the space center of a prototype <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unha-3" title="Unha-3">Unha-3</a> class booster first two stages initially took place over 29–31 May 2009 possibly for logistics testing as well as personnel facilities infrastructure testing training purposes. The results of those tests suggest that military and design specialists ultimately decided to build a facilities access railroad to cut down the 15 kilometer road access logistics issues to the space center.</p>
<p>By early 2011 it was reported that the construction was completed and that it had been under construction for a decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-reuters-4">[4]</a></sup> The first official mention of the site took place in March 2012 when North Korea announced it will launch from that site the satellite <a href="/wiki/Kwangmy%C5%8Fngs%C5%8Fng-3" title="Kwangmyŏngsŏng-3">Kwangmyŏngsŏng-3</a>. On April 2012, prior to the launch of the satellite, Jang Myong-jin, director of the Sohae, introduced the launching process of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unha-3" title="Unha-3">Unha-3</a> rocket (Korean: 은하-3호, 銀河-3) during a guided media tour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mail_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-mail-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naenara2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-naenara2-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first launch of Kwangmyongsong-3 on 12 April 2012 failed. On 1 December 2012, the Korean Central News Agency announced that a <a href="/wiki/Kwangmy%C5%8Fngs%C5%8Fng-3_Unit_2" title="Kwangmyŏngsŏng-3 Unit 2">second version</a> of Kwangmyongsong-3 was to be launched from Sohae between 10–22 December 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zarya-32_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-zarya-32-7">[7]</a></sup> North Korea declared the launch successful, and the South Korean military and <a href="/wiki/North_American_Aerospace_Defense_Command" title="North American Aerospace Defense Command">North American Aerospace Defense Command</a> (NORAD) reported that initial indications suggested that an object had achieved orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> North Korea had previously claimed the <a href="/wiki/Kwangmy%C5%8Fngs%C5%8Fng-1" title="Kwangmyŏngsŏng-1">Kwangmyŏngsŏng-1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kwangmy%C5%8Fngs%C5%8Fng-2" title="Kwangmyŏngsŏng-2">Kwangmyŏngsŏng-2</a> launches successful,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> despite American military sources confirming that they failed to achieve orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 7 February 2016, North Korea successfully launched a long-range rocket, carrying the satellite <a href="/wiki/Kwangmy%C5%8Fngs%C5%8Fng-4" title="Kwangmyŏngsŏng-4">Kwangmyongsong-4</a>, from the station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">[14]</a></sup> Critics suggest that the real purpose of the launch was test a <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_missile" title="Ballistic missile">ballistic missile</a>. The launch was strongly condemned by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">UN Security Council.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> A statement broadcast on <a href="/wiki/Korean_Central_Television" title="Korean Central Television">Korean Central Television</a> said that a new Earth observation satellite, Kwangmyongsong-4, had successfully been put into orbit less than 10 minutes after lift-off from the Sohae space centre in <a href="/wiki/North_Pyongan_Province" title="North Pyongan Province">North Phyongan province</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">[14]</a></sup> North Korea's <a href="/wiki/National_Aerospace_Development_Administration" title="National Aerospace Development Administration">National Aerospace Development Administratio</a>n stated the launch was "an epochal event in developing the country's science, technology, economy and defence capability by legitimately exercising the right to use space for independent and peaceful purposes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-18">[18]</a></sup> The launch prompted South Korea and the United States to discuss the possibility of placing an advanced missile defence system in South Korea,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> a move strongly opposed by both China and Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">Facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sohae_Satellite_Launching_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The entire facility occupies over six square kilometers, and consists of a launch site, a static rocket motor test stand, vehicle checkout and processing buildings, a launch control building, a large support area, a complex headquarters building and an entry control point. The site is five times larger than Tonghae Launch Site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kherald_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-kherald-21">[21]</a></sup> There is much speculation about the functions of different parts of the site <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msnbc_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-msnbc-22">[22]</a></sup> but the nuances of satellite photography based guesswork may not be communicated effectively in the mass media.</p>
<p>Western sources identified a building as a "high bay processing facility" which turned out to be the launch control centre when the site was visited by journalists. The building previously identified as the control centre was actually an observation point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msnbc_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-msnbc-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
