<p><b>Enhanced-definition television</b>, or <b>extended-definition television</b> (<b>EDTV</b>) is an American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consumer_Electronics_Association" title="Consumer Electronics Association">Consumer Electronics Association</a> (CEA) marketing shorthand term for certain <a href="/wiki/Digital_television" title="Digital television">digital television</a> (DTV) formats and devices. Specifically, this term defines formats that deliver a picture superior to that of <a href="/wiki/Standard-definition_television" title="Standard-definition television">standard-definition television</a> (SDTV) but not as detailed as <a href="/wiki/High-definition_television" title="High-definition television">high-definition television</a> (HDTV).</p>
<p>The term refers to devices capable of displaying 480-line or 576-line signals in <a href="/wiki/Progressive_scan" title="Progressive scan">progressive scan</a>, commonly referred to as <a href="/wiki/480p" title="480p">480p</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Television_System_Committee" title="National Television System Committee">NTSC-HQ</a>) and <a href="/wiki/576p" title="576p">576p</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phase_Alternating_Line" title="Phase Alternating Line">PAL</a>) respectively, as opposed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interlaced" title="Interlaced">interlaced</a> scanning, commonly referred to as <a href="/wiki/480i" title="480i">480i</a> (NTSC) or <a href="/wiki/576i" title="576i">576i</a> (PAL). <a href="/wiki/High-motion" title="High-motion">High-motion</a> is optional for EDTV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In other countries definitions may vary.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Connectivity">Connectivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced-definition_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Connectivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As EDTV signals require more <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(signal_processing)" title="Bandwidth (signal processing)">bandwidth</a> (due to frame doubling) than is feasible with SDTV connection standards (such as <a href="/wiki/Composite_video" title="Composite video">composite video</a>, <a href="/wiki/SCART" title="SCART">SCART</a> or <a href="/wiki/S-Video" title="S-Video">S-Video</a>), higher bandwidth media must be used to accommodate the additional data transfer. To achieve EDTV, consumer electronic devices such as a <a href="/wiki/Progressive_scan_DVD_player" title="Progressive scan DVD player">progressive scan DVD player</a> or modern <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a> consoles must be connected through at least a <a href="/wiki/Component_video" title="Component video">component video</a> cable (typically using 3 RCA cables for video), a <a href="/wiki/VGA_connector" title="VGA connector">VGA connector</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Digital_Visual_Interface" title="Digital Visual Interface">DVI</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-Definition_Multimedia_Interface" title="High-Definition Multimedia Interface">HDMI</a> connector. For over-the-air television broadcasts, EDTV content uses the same connectors as HDTV.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Broadcast_and_displays">Broadcast and displays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced-definition_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Broadcast and displays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>EDTV broadcasts use less digital bandwidth than HDTV, so TV stations can broadcast several EDTV stations at once. Like SDTV, EDTV signals are broadcast with non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square_pixel" title="Square pixel">square pixels</a>. Since the same number of horizontal pixels are used in 4:3 and 16:9 broadcasts, the 16:9 mode is sometimes referred to as <a href="/wiki/Anamorphic_widescreen" title="Anamorphic widescreen">anamorphic widescreen</a>. Most EDTV displays use square pixels, yielding a resolution of 852 × 480. However, since no broadcasts use this pixel count, such displays always scale anything they show. The only sources of 852 × 480 video are Internet downloads, such as from <a href="/wiki/ITunes" title="ITunes">iTunes</a>, and some video games. Unlike 1080i and SDTV formats, progressive displays (such as <a href="/wiki/Plasma_display" title="Plasma display">plasma displays</a> and LCDs) can show EDTV signals without the need to de-interlace them first. This can result in a reduction of motion artifacts. However to achieve this most progressive displays require the broadcast to be frame doubled (i.e., 25 to 50 and 30 to 60) to avoid the same motion flicker issues that interlacing fixes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="DVDs">DVDs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced-definition_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: DVDs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The progressive output of a <a href="/wiki/DVD_player" title="DVD player">DVD player</a> can be considered the baseline for EDTV. Movies shot at 24 frames-per-second (fps) are often encoded onto a DVD at 24 fps progressive, and most DVD players do the <a href="/wiki/Telecine" title="Telecine">2:2 or 3:2 pulldown</a> conversion internally, before feeding the output to (usually) an interlaced display, or here, a progressive 576p or 480p.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The progressive 24 fps DVD will have a unifying effect on <a href="/wiki/PAL" title="PAL">PAL</a> and <a href="/wiki/NTSC" title="NTSC">NTSC</a>, just as film does, perhaps requiring conversion of the number of lines but without a conflict between field and frame rate. The player converts the video to the more-conventional video formats, on the fly, by simply repeating each field. It converts for PAL (referring here to 625 line 575 active line used with PAL as well as the chrominance aspects), by repeating each frame twice with a corresponding interlace, or for NTSC, by repeating some 480p frames 2 times and others 3 times (3:2 pulldown), to make 24 fps material play at 30fps, or 60 <i>fields</i> per second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>On an EDTV display, or on HDTVs in 480p mode, DVD players can display progressive disc content without needing to convert it to interlaced format. Various signal processing tricks are then used to fake the progressive scan; the quality of this depends on the quality of the upconversion process.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray Disc</a> and <a href="/wiki/HD_DVD" title="HD DVD">HD DVD</a> formats can encode all EDTV forms, but because HDTV is a primary selling point of Blu-ray/HD DVDs, to date, this has been used only on certain bonus content.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gaming">Gaming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced-definition_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gaming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The video resolution of video game consoles reached EDTV specifications starting with the <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">Sega</a> <a href="/wiki/Dreamcast" title="Dreamcast">Dreamcast</a>, becoming the first mainstream console with a VGA output, supporting EDTV. The <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">PlayStation 2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a> <a href="/wiki/GameCube" title="GameCube">GameCube</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> <a href="/wiki/Xbox_(console)" title="Xbox (console)">Xbox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wii" title="Wii">Wii</a> are also EDTV compatible with a component connection. The <a href="/wiki/Xbox_360" title="Xbox 360">Xbox 360</a> can output 480p via <a href="/wiki/YPbPr" title="YPbPr">YP<sub>B</sub>P<sub>R</sub></a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Component_Video" title="Component Video">component</a>, <a href="/wiki/Video_Graphics_Array" title="Video Graphics Array">VGA</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-Definition_Multimedia_Interface" title="High-Definition Multimedia Interface">HDMI</a> (newer models only) cables. The <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a> outputs EDTV via its HDMI and component video (YP<sub>B</sub>P<sub>R</sub>) connections; 480p is only available on NTSC consoles while 576p is only available on PAL consoles.</p>
