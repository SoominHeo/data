<p>The <b>Xianbei</b> (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">鮮卑</span>; <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="Wade–Giles">Wade–Giles</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-wadegile" xml:lang="zh-Latn-wadegile"><b>Hsien-pi</b></span></i>) were <a href="/wiki/Proto-Mongols" title="Proto-Mongols">proto-Mongols</a> residing in what became today's eastern <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inner_Mongolia" title="Inner Mongolia">Inner Mongolia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Northeast_China" title="Northeast China">Northeast China</a>. Along with the <a href="/wiki/Xiongnu" title="Xiongnu">Xiongnu</a>, they were one of the major nomadic groups in northern China during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">Han Dynasty</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_dynasties" title="Chinese dynasties">subsequent dynastic periods</a>. They eventually established their own northern dynasties, including the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Wei" title="Northern Wei">Northern Wei</a> founded in the 4th century AD by the <a href="/wiki/Tuoba" title="Tuoba">Tuoba</a> clan.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xianbei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is generally accepted that the Xianbei spoke a language related to the <a href="/wiki/Mongolic_languages" title="Mongolic languages">Mongolic languages</a>. Claus Schönig writes:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The Xianbei derived from the context of the <a href="/wiki/Donghu_people" title="Donghu people">Donghu</a>, who are likely to have contained the linguistic ancestors of the <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongols</a>. Later branches and descendants of the Xianbei include the <a href="/wiki/Tuoba" title="Tuoba">Tabghach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Khitan_people" title="Khitan people">Khitan</a>, who seem to have been linguistically Para-Mongolic. [...] Opinions differ widely as to what the linguistic impact of the Xianbei period was. Some scholars (like Clauson) have preferred to regard the Xianbei and Tabghach (Tuoba) as Turks, or even as Bulghar Turks, with the implication that the entire layer of early Turkic borrowings in Mongolic would have been received from the Xianbei, rather than from the Xiongnu. However, since the Mongolic (or Para-Mongolic) identity of the Xianbei is increasingly obvious in the light of recent progress in <a href="/wiki/Khitan_language" title="Khitan language">Khitan</a> studies, it is more reasonable to assume (with Doerfer) that the flow of linguistic influence from Turkic (or Bulghar Turkic) into Mongolic was at least partly reversed during the Xianbei period, yielding the first identifiable layer of Mongolic (or Para-Mongolic) loanwords in Turkic. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJanhunen2006405-6_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJanhunen2006405-6-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xianbei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chinese historical texts unequivocally state that the Xianbei were descendants of the <a href="/wiki/Donghu_people" title="Donghu people">Donghu</a>, which used to be believed to represent the “Eastern Hu” based on the Chinese record. The Xianbei were a northern or northeastern Asian population according to modern Chinese and Russian anthropologists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Qin-Han_era">Qin-Han era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xianbei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Qin-Han era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the Donghu were defeated by <a href="/wiki/Modu_Chanyu" title="Modu Chanyu">Modu Chanyu</a> around 208 BCE, the Xianbei and Wuhuan survived as the main remnants of the confederation. The <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_the_Later_Han" title="Book of the Later Han">Book of the Later Han</a></i> says that “the language and culture of the Xianbei are the same as the <a href="/wiki/Wuhuan" title="Wuhuan">Wuhuan</a>”.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Records_of_the_Three_Kingdoms" title="Records of the Three Kingdoms">Records of the Three Kingdoms</a></i> say:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Tanshihuai of the Xianbei divided his territory into three sections: the eastern, the middle and the western. From the You Beiping to the Liao River, connecting the Fuyu and Mo to the east, it was the eastern section. There were more than twenty counties. The darens (chiefs) (of this section) were called Mijia, Queji, Suli and Huaitou. From the You Beiping to Shanggu to the west, it was the middle section. There were more than ten counties. The darens of this section were called Kezui, Queju, Murong, et al. From Shanggu to Dunhuang, connecting the Wusun to the west, it was the western section. There were more than twenty counties. The darens (of this section) were called Zhijian Luoluo, Rilü Tuiyan, Yanliyou, et al. These chiefs were all subordinate to Tanshihuai.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The <i>Book of the Later Han</i> records a memorial submitted in 177:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Ever since the [northern] <a href="/wiki/Xiongnu" title="Xiongnu">Xiongnu</a> ran away, the Xianbei have become powerful and populous, taking all the lands previously held by the Xiong-nu and claiming to have 100,000 warriors. … Refined metals and wrought iron have come into the possession of the [Xianbei] rebels. Han deserters also seek refuge [in the lands of the Xianbei] and serve as their advisers. Their weapons are sharper and their horses are faster than those of the Xiong-nu.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Another memorial submitted in 185 is recorded by the <i>Book of the Later Han</i>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The Xianbei people … invade our frontiers so frequently that hardly a year goes by in peace, and it is only when the trading season arrives that they come forward in submission. But in so doing they are only bent on gaining precious Chinese goods; it is not because they respect Chinese power or are grateful for Chinese generosity. As soon as they obtain all they possibly can [from trade], they turn in their tracks to start wreaking damage.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Around A.D. 155, the northern <a href="/wiki/Xiongnu" title="Xiongnu">Xiongnu</a> were "crushed and subjugated" by the Xianbei. Their chief, known by the Chinese as Tan-shih-huai, then advanced upon and defeated the <a href="/wiki/Wusun" title="Wusun">Wusun</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ili_Kazakh_Autonomous_Prefecture" title="Ili Kazakh Autonomous Prefecture">Ili</a> by A.D. 166. He then formed an allegiance with the southern Xiongnu to attack <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shensi" title="Shensi">Shensi</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kansu" title="Kansu">Kansu</a>. China successfully repulsed their attacks in 158, 177 and 279<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rene_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rene-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Between A.D. 155 and 166, T’an-shih-huai conducted a series of major military campaigns that led to the extension of Hsien-pi power over the Great Steppe as far as southern Siberia and from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ussuri" title="Ussuri">Ussuri</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>. Until the third decade of the third century A.D. the Hsien-pi was the leading power in Central Asia."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_state_formation:_Sixteen_Kingdoms_and_the_Northern_Wei">Early state formation: <a href="/wiki/Sixteen_Kingdoms" title="Sixteen Kingdoms">Sixteen Kingdoms</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Wei" title="Northern Wei">Northern Wei</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xianbei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early state formation: Sixteen Kingdoms and the Northern Wei">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 3rd century saw both the fragmentation of the Xianbei in 235 and the branching out of the various Xianbei tribes later to establish six significant empires of their own such as the <a href="/wiki/Former_Yan" title="Former Yan">Former Yan</a> (281-370), <a href="/wiki/Western_Yan" title="Western Yan">Western Yan</a> (384-394), <a href="/wiki/Later_Yan" title="Later Yan">Later Yan</a> (384-407), <a href="/wiki/Southern_Yan" title="Southern Yan">Southern Yan</a> (398-410), <a href="/wiki/Western_Qin" title="Western Qin">Western Qin</a> (385-430) and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Liang" title="Southern Liang">Southern Liang</a> (397-414).</p>
<p>Most of them were unified by the <a href="/wiki/Tuoba" title="Tuoba">Tuoba</a> Xianbei, who established the Northern Wei (386-535), which was the first of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Dynasties" title="Northern Dynasties">Northern Dynasties</a> (386-581) founded by the Xianbei.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BeltBuckleXianbei3-4thcentury.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="991" data-file-width="1649" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/BeltBuckleXianbei3-4thcentury.jpg/220px-BeltBuckleXianbei3-4thcentury.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/BeltBuckleXianbei3-4thcentury.jpg/330px-BeltBuckleXianbei3-4thcentury.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/BeltBuckleXianbei3-4thcentury.jpg/440px-BeltBuckleXianbei3-4thcentury.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BeltBuckleXianbei3-4thcentury.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Xianbei belt buckles, 3-4th century CE</div>

<p>In 534, the Northern Wei split into an <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Wei" title="Eastern Wei">Eastern Wei</a> (534-550) and a <a href="/wiki/Western_Wei" title="Western Wei">Western Wei</a> (535-556) after an uprising in the steppes of <a href="/wiki/North_China" title="North China">North China</a> inhabited by Xianbei and other nomadic peoples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The former evolved into the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Qi" title="Northern Qi">Northern Qi</a> (550-577), and the latter into the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Zhou" title="Northern Zhou">Northern Zhou</a> (557-581), while the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Dynasties" title="Southern Dynasties">Southern Dynasties</a> were pushed to the south of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yangtze_River" title="Yangtze River">Yangtze River</a>. In 581, the Prime Minister of Northern Zhou, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wen_of_Sui" title="Emperor Wen of Sui">Yang Jian</a>, founded the <a href="/wiki/Sui_dynasty" title="Sui dynasty">Sui dynasty</a> (581-618). His son, the future <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Yang_of_Sui" title="Emperor Yang of Sui">Emperor Yang of Sui</a>, annihilated the Southern Chen (557-589), the last kingdom of the Southern Dynasties, thereby unifying northern and southern China. After the Sui came to an end amidst peasant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rebellions" title="Rebellions">rebellions</a> and renegade troops, his cousin, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Li_Yuan" title="Li Yuan">Li Yuan</a>, founded the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a> (618-907); Li led China to develop into one of the most prosperous states in history. Sui and Tang dynasties were founded by Han Chinese generals who also served the Northern Wei dynasty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Through these political establishments, the Xianbei who entered China were largely merged with the Han, examples such as the wife of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Gaozu_of_Tang" title="Emperor Gaozu of Tang">Emperor Gaozu of Tang</a>, Duchess Dou and <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Taizong_of_Tang" title="Emperor Taizong of Tang">Emperor Taizong of Tang</a>'s (Li Shimin's) wife, <a href="/wiki/Empress_Zhangsun" title="Empress Zhangsun">Empress Zhangsun</a>, both have Xianbei ancestries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> while those who remained behind in the northern grassland emerged as later powers to rule over China.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art">Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xianbei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Art of the Xianbei portrayed their nomadic lifestyle and consisted primarily of metalwork and figurines. The style and subjects of Xianbei art were influenced by a variety of influences, and ultimately, the Xianbei were known for emphasizing unique nomadic motifs in artistic advancements such as leaf headdresses, crouching and geometricized animals depictions, animal pendant necklaces, and metal <a href="/wiki/Openwork" title="Openwork">openwork</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dawn_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-dawn-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leaf_headdresses">Leaf headdresses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xianbei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Leaf headdresses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The leaf headdresses were very characteristic of Xianbei culture, and they are found especially in Murong Xianbei tombs. Their corresponding ornamental style also links the Xianbei to Bactria. These gold hat ornaments represented trees and antlers and, in Chinese, they are referred to as buyao (“step sway”) since the thin metal leaves move when the wearer moves. Sun Guoping first uncovered this type of artifact, and defined three main styles: “Blossoming Tree” (huashu), which is mounted on the front of a cap near the forehead and has one or more branches with hanging leaves that are circle or droplet shaped, “Blossoming Top” (dinghua), which is worn on top of the head and resembles a tree or animal with many leaf pendants, and the rare “Blossoming Vine” (huaman), which consists of “gold strips interwoven with wires with leaves.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leaves_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-leaves-14">[14]</a></sup> Leaf headdresses were made with hammered gold and decorated by punching out designs and hanging the leaf pendants with wire. The exact origin, use, and wear of these headdresses is still being investigated and determined. However, headdresses similar to those later also existed and were worn by women in the courts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dawn_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-dawn-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leaves_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-leaves-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_iconography">Animal iconography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xianbei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Animal iconography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another key form of Xianbei art is animal iconography, which was implemented primarily in metalwork. The Xianbei stylistically portrayed crouching animals in geometricized, abstracted, repeated forms, and distinguished their culture and art by depicting animal predation and same-animal combat. Typically, sheep, deer, and horses were illustrated. The artifacts, usually plaques or pendants, were made from metal, and the backgrounds were decorated with openwork or mountainous landscapes, which harks back to the Xianbei nomadic lifestyle. With repeated animal imagery, an openwork background, and a rectangular frame, the included image of the three deer plaque is a paradigm of the Xianbei art style. Concave plaque backings imply that plaques were made using <a href="/wiki/Lost-wax_casting" title="Lost-wax casting">lost-wax casting</a>, or raised designs were impressed on the back of hammered metal sheets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nomadic_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-nomadic-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Horses">Horses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xianbei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Horses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The nomadic traditions of the Xianbei inspired them to portray horses in their artwork. It is obvious that the horse played a large role in the existence of the Xianbei as a nomadic people, and in one tomb, a horse skull lay atop Xianbei bells, buckles, ornaments, a saddle, and one gilded bronze stirrup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The Xianbei not only created art for their horses, but they also made art to depict horses. Another recurring motif was the winged horse. It has been suggested by archaeologist Su Bai that this symbol was a “heavenly beast in the shape of a horse” because of its prominence in Xianbei mythology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nomadic_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-nomadic-16">[16]</a></sup> This symbol is thought to have guided an early Xianbei southern migration, and is a recurring image in many Xianbei art forms.</p>

<p>Xianbei figurines help to portray the people of the society by representing pastimes, depicting specialized clothing, and implying various beliefs. Most figurines have been recovered from Xianbei tombs, so they are primarily military and musical figures meant to serve the deceased in afterlife processions and guard the tomb. Furthermore, the figurine clothing specifies the according social statuses: higher-ranking Xianbei wore long-sleeved robes with a straight neck shirt underneath, while lower-ranking Xianbei wore trousers and belted tunics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buddhist_Influences">Buddhist Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xianbei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Buddhist Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Xianbei Buddhist influences were derived from interactions with Han culture. The Han bureaucrats initially helped the Xianbei run their state, but eventually the Xianbei became Sinophiles and promoted Buddhism. The beginning of this conversion is evidenced by the Buddha imagery that emerges in Xianbei art. For instance, the included Buddha imprinted leaf headdress perfectly represents the Xianbei conversion and Buddhist synthesis since it combines both the traditional nomadic Xianbei leaf headdress with the new imagery of Buddha. This Xianbei religious conversion continued to develop in the Northern Wei dynasty, and ultimately led to the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Yungang_Grottoes" title="Yungang Grottoes">Yungang Grottoes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dawn_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-dawn-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Northern Wei's Eight Noble Xianbei surnames <a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%85%AB%E5%A4%A7%E8%B4%B5%E6%97%8F" title="zh:八大贵族">八大贵族</a> were the Buliugu 步六孤, Helai 賀賴, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dugu_(surname)" title="Dugu (surname)">Dugu</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E7%8D%A8%E5%AD%A4" title="zh:獨孤">獨孤</a>, Helou 賀樓, Huniu 忽忸, Qiumu 丘穆, Gexi 紇奚, and Yuchi <a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%B0%89%E9%81%B2" title="zh:尉遲">尉遲</a>.</p>
<p>The "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monguor" title="Monguor">Monguor</a>" (Tu) people in modern China may have descended from the Xianbei who were led by <a href="/wiki/Tuyuhun" title="Tuyuhun">Tuyuhun</a> Khan to migrate westward and establish the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuyuhun_Kingdom" title="Tuyuhun Kingdom">Tuyuhun Kingdom</a> (284-670) in the third century and <a href="/wiki/Western_Xia" title="Western Xia">Western Xia</a> (1038–1227) through the thirteenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Today they are primarily distributed in Qinghai and Gansu Province, and speak a Mongolic language.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xibe_people" title="Xibe people">Xibe</a> or "Xibo" people also believe they are descendants of the Xianbei, with considerable controversies that have attributed their origins to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jurchens" title="Jurchens">Jurchens</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Oroqen_people" title="Oroqen people">Elunchun</a>, and the Xianbei.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
