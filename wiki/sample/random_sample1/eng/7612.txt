<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Congenital disorder</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>congenital disorder</b>, also known as a <b>congenital disease</b>, <b>deformity</b>, <b>birth defect</b>, or <b>anomaly</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is a condition existing at or before <a href="/wiki/Birth" title="Birth">birth</a> regardless of cause. Of these disorders, those characterized by structural deformities are termed "congenital anomalies" and involve defects in a developing <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetus</a>. Birth defects vary widely in cause and symptoms. Any substance that causes birth defects is known as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teratogen" title="Teratogen">teratogen</a>. Some disorders can be detected before birth through <a href="/wiki/Prenatal_diagnosis" title="Prenatal diagnosis">prenatal diagnosis</a> (screening).</p>
<p>Birth defects may be the result of <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> or environmental factors. This includes errors of <a href="/wiki/Morphogenesis" title="Morphogenesis">morphogenesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epigenetics" title="Epigenetics">epigenetic</a> modifications on a parental germline, or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromosomal" title="Chromosomal">chromosomal</a> abnormality. The outcome of the disorder will depend on complex interactions between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prenatal" title="Prenatal">pre-natal</a> deficit and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postnatal" title="Postnatal">post-natal</a> environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdc_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-cdc-2">[2]</a></sup> Animal studies indicate that the mother's (and likely the father's) diet, <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">vitamin</a> intake, and <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> levels <i>prior</i> to <a href="/wiki/Ovulation" title="Ovulation">ovulation</a> and conception have long-term effects on fetal growth and adolescent and adult disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ConsultantLive.com_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ConsultantLive.com-3">[3]</a></sup> Animal studies have shown that paternal exposures prior to conception and during pregnancy result in increased risk of certain birth defects and cancers. This research suggests that paternal food deprivation, <a href="/wiki/Germline_mutation" title="Germline mutation">germ line mutations</a>, alcohol use, chemical mutagens, age, smoking habits and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epigenetic" title="Epigenetic">epigenetic</a> alterations can affect birth outcomes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abel_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Abel-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeSantis_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-DeSantis-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sartorius_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sartorius-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anderson_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-anderson-7">[7]</a></sup> However, the relationship between offspring health and paternal exposures, age, and lifestyle are still relatively weak. This is likely because paternal exposures and their effects on the fetus are studied far less extensively than maternal exposures.</p>
<p>Birth defects are present in about 3% of newborns in USA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Congenital anomalies resulted in about 632,000 deaths per year in 2013 down from 751,000 in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GBD204_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-GBD204-9">[9]</a></sup> The type with the greatest numbers of deaths are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congenital_heart_disease" title="Congenital heart disease">congenital heart disease</a> (323,000), followed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neural_tube_defects" title="Neural tube defects">neural tube defects</a> (69,000).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GBD204_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-GBD204-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Much of the language used for describing congenital conditions predates genomic mapping, and structural conditions are often considered separately from other congenital conditions. It is now known that many metabolic conditions may have subtle structural expression, and structural conditions often have genetic links. Still, congenital conditions are often classified in a structural basis, organized when possible by primary organ system affected.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primarily_structural">Primarily structural</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Primarily structural">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several terms are used to describe congenital abnormalities. (Some of these are also used to describe noncongenital conditions, and more than one term may apply in an individual condition.)</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>A <b>congenital physical anomaly</b> is an abnormality of the structure of a body part. An anomaly may or may not be perceived as a problem condition. Many, if not most, people have one or more <a href="/wiki/Minor_physical_anomalies" title="Minor physical anomalies">minor physical anomalies</a> if examined carefully. Examples of minor anomalies can include curvature of the 5th finger (<a href="/wiki/Clinodactyly" title="Clinodactyly">clinodactyly</a>), a third nipple, tiny indentations of the skin near the ears (preauricular pits), shortness of the 4th <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metacarpal" title="Metacarpal">metacarpal</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metatarsal" title="Metatarsal">metatarsal</a> bones, or dimples over the lower spine (<a href="/wiki/Sacral_dimple" title="Sacral dimple">sacral dimples</a>). Some minor anomalies may be clues to more significant internal abnormalities.</li>
<li><b>Birth defect</b> is a widely used term for a congenital malformation, i.e. a congenital, physical anomaly which is recognizable at <a href="/wiki/Birth" title="Birth">birth</a>, and which is significant enough to be considered a problem. According to the <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention" title="Centers for Disease Control and Prevention">CDC</a>, most birth defects are believed to be caused by a complex mix of factors including genetics, environment, and behaviors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdc_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-cdc-2">[2]</a></sup> though many birth defects have no known cause. An example of a birth defect is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleft_palate" title="Cleft palate">cleft palate</a>, which occurs during the fourth and seventh week of gestation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdc.gov_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-cdc.gov-10">[10]</a></sup> Body tissue and special cells from each side of the head grow toward the center of the face. They join together to make the face.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdc.gov_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-cdc.gov-10">[10]</a></sup> A cleft means a split or separation; the " roof" of the mouth is called the palate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>A <b>congenital malformation</b> is a congenital physical anomaly that is deleterious, i.e. a structural defect perceived as a problem. A typical combination of malformations affecting more than one body part is referred to as a <i>malformation syndrome</i>.</li>
<li>Some conditions are due to abnormal tissue development:
<ul>
<li>A <b>malformation</b> is associated with a disorder of tissue development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-7216-1489-2_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-7216-1489-2-12">[12]</a></sup> Malformations often occur in the first trimester.</li>
<li>A <b><a href="/wiki/Dysplasia" title="Dysplasia">dysplasia</a></b> is a disorder at the organ level that is due to problems with tissue development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-7216-1489-2_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-7216-1489-2-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>It is also possible for conditions to arise after tissue is formed:
<ul>
<li>A <b><a href="/wiki/Deformity" title="Deformity">deformation</a></b> is a condition arising from mechanical stress to normal tissue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-7216-1489-2_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-7216-1489-2-12">[12]</a></sup> Deformations often occur in the second or third trimester, and can be due to <a href="/wiki/Oligohydramnios" title="Oligohydramnios">oligohydramnios</a>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>A <b>disruption</b> involves breakdown of normal tissues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-7216-1489-2_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-7216-1489-2-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>When multiple effects occur in a specified order, it is known as a <a href="/wiki/Sequence_(medicine)" title="Sequence (medicine)">sequence</a>. When the order is not known, it is a <a href="/wiki/Syndrome" title="Syndrome">syndrome</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_primarily_structural_congenital_disorders">Examples of primarily structural congenital disorders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Examples of primarily structural congenital disorders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A limb anomaly is called a <a href="/wiki/Dysmelia" title="Dysmelia">dysmelia</a>. These include all forms of limbs anomalies, such as <a href="/wiki/Amelia_(birth_defect)" title="Amelia (birth defect)">amelia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ectrodactyly" title="Ectrodactyly">ectrodactyly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phocomelia" title="Phocomelia">phocomelia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polymelia" title="Polymelia">polymelia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polydactyly" title="Polydactyly">polydactyly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syndactyly" title="Syndactyly">syndactyly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polysyndactyly" title="Polysyndactyly">polysyndactyly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oligodactyly" title="Oligodactyly">oligodactyly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brachydactyly" title="Brachydactyly">brachydactyly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dwarfism" title="Dwarfism">achondroplasia</a>, congenital <a href="/wiki/Aplasia" title="Aplasia">aplasia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hypoplasia" title="Hypoplasia">hypoplasia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amniotic_band_syndrome" title="Amniotic band syndrome">amniotic band syndrome</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cleidocranial_dysostosis" title="Cleidocranial dysostosis">cleidocranial dysostosis</a>.</p>
<p>Congenital anomalies of the heart include <a href="/wiki/Patent_ductus_arteriosus" title="Patent ductus arteriosus">patent ductus arteriosus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atrial_septal_defect" title="Atrial septal defect">atrial septal defect</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ventricular_septal_defect" title="Ventricular septal defect">ventricular septal defect</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetralogy_of_fallot" title="Tetralogy of fallot">tetralogy of fallot</a>.</p>
<p>Congenital anomalies of the nervous system include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neural_tube_defects" title="Neural tube defects">neural tube defects</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Spina_bifida" title="Spina bifida">spina bifida</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meningocele" title="Meningocele">meningocele</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meningomyelocele" title="Meningomyelocele">meningomyelocele</a>, <a href="/wiki/Encephalocele" title="Encephalocele">encephalocele</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anencephaly" title="Anencephaly">anencephaly</a>. Other congenital anomalies of the nervous system include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arnold-Chiari_malformation" title="Arnold-Chiari malformation">Arnold-Chiari malformation</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dandy-Walker_malformation" title="Dandy-Walker malformation">Dandy-Walker malformation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrocephalus" title="Hydrocephalus">hydrocephalus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microencephaly" title="Microencephaly">microencephaly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Megalencephaly" title="Megalencephaly">megalencephaly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lissencephaly" title="Lissencephaly">lissencephaly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polymicrogyria" title="Polymicrogyria">polymicrogyria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holoprosencephaly" title="Holoprosencephaly">holoprosencephaly</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Agenesis_of_the_corpus_callosum" title="Agenesis of the corpus callosum">agenesis of the corpus callosum</a>.</p>
<p>Congenital anomalies of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_system" title="Gastrointestinal system">gastrointestinal system</a> include numerous forms of <a href="/wiki/Stenosis" title="Stenosis">stenosis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atresia" title="Atresia">atresia</a>, and perforation, such as <a href="/wiki/Gastroschisis" title="Gastroschisis">gastroschisis</a>.</p>
<p>Congenital anomalies of the kidney and urinary tract (CAKUT) include renal parenchyma, kidneys, and urinary collecting system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Defects can be bilateral or unilateral, and different defects often coexist in an individual child</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primarily_metabolic">Primarily metabolic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Primarily metabolic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Inborn_error_of_metabolism" title="Inborn error of metabolism">inborn error of metabolism</a></div>
<p>A <i>congenital metabolic disease</i> is also referred to as an <a href="/wiki/Inborn_error_of_metabolism" title="Inborn error of metabolism">inborn error of metabolism</a>. Most of these are single gene defects, usually heritable. Many affect the structure of body parts but some simply affect the function.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Other well defined genetic conditions may affect the production of hormones, receptors, structural proteins, and ion channels.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fetal_alcohol_exposure">Fetal alcohol exposure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fetal alcohol exposure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Fetal_alcohol_spectrum_disorder" title="Fetal alcohol spectrum disorder">Fetal alcohol spectrum disorder</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fetal_alcohol_syndrome" title="Fetal alcohol syndrome">Fetal alcohol syndrome</a></div>
<p>The mother's consumption of alcohol during pregnancy can cause a continuum of various permanent birth defects : cranofacial abnormalities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> brain damage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> intellectual disability,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> heart disease, kidney abnormality, skeletal anomalies, ocular abnormalities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The prevalence of children affected is estimated at least 1 percent in U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> as well in Canada.</p>
<p>Very few studies have investigated the links between paternal alcohol use and offspring health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeSantis_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-DeSantis-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, recent animal research has shown a correlation between paternal alcohol exposure and decreased offspring birth weight. Behavioral and cognitive disorders, including difficulties with learning and memory, hyperactivity, and lowered stress tolerance have been linked to paternal alcohol ingestion. The compromised stress management skills of animals whose male parent was exposed to alcohol are similar to the exaggerated responses to stress that children with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fetal_Alcohol_Syndrome" title="Fetal Alcohol Syndrome">Fetal Alcohol Syndrome</a> display because of maternal alcohol use. These birth defects and behavioral disorders were found in cases of both long- and short-term paternal alcohol ingestion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trasler_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trasler-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abel_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Abel-4">[4]</a></sup> In the same animal study, paternal alcohol exposure was correlated with a significant difference in organ size and the increased risk of the offspring displaying ventricular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Septal_defect" title="Septal defect">septal defects</a> (VSD) at birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abel_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Abel-4">[4]</a></sup> VSD has also been correlated with paternal alcohol abuse in humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Savitz_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Savitz-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxic_substances">Toxic substances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Toxic substances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Developmental_toxicity" title="Developmental toxicity">Developmental toxicity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drugs_in_pregnancy" title="Drugs in pregnancy">Drugs in pregnancy</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_toxins_and_fetal_development" title="Environmental toxins and fetal development">Environmental toxins and fetal development</a></div>
<p>Substances whose <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxicity</a> can cause congenital disorders are called "teratogens", and include certain pharmaceutical and recreational <a href="/wiki/Drugs_in_pregnancy" title="Drugs in pregnancy">drugs in pregnancy</a> as well as many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_toxins_in_pregnancy" title="Environmental toxins in pregnancy">environmental toxins in pregnancy</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A review published in 2010 identified 6 main teratogenic mechanisms associated with medication use: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folate_antagonism" title="Folate antagonism">folate antagonism</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neural_crest_cell" title="Neural crest cell">neural crest cell</a> disruption, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endocrine_disruption" title="Endocrine disruption">endocrine disruption</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxidative_stress" title="Oxidative stress">oxidative stress</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">vascular</a> disruption and specific receptor- or enzyme-mediated teratogenesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is estimated that 10% of all birth defects are caused by prenatal exposure to a teratogenic agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-471-38225-6_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-471-38225-6-22">[22]</a></sup> These exposures include, but are not limited to, medication or drug exposures, maternal infections and diseases, and environmental and occupational exposures. Paternal smoking use has also been linked to an increased risk of birth defects and childhood cancer for the offspring, where the paternal germ line undergoes oxidative damage due to cigarette use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhu_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zhu-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ji_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ji-24">[24]</a></sup> Teratogen-caused birth defects are potentially preventable. Studies have shown that nearly 50% of pregnant women have been exposed to at least one medication during gestation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid7266953_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid7266953-25">[25]</a></sup> During pregnancy, a female can also be exposed to teratogens from the contaminated clothing or toxins within the seminal fluid of a partner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anderson_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-anderson-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trasler_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Trasler-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chia_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-chia-26">[26]</a></sup> An additional study found that of 200 individuals referred for genetic counseling for a teratogenic exposure, 52% were exposed to more than one potential teratogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid3703408_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid3703408-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Medications_and_supplements">Medications and supplements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Medications and supplements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The most notorious teratogenic drug is the <a href="/wiki/Thalidomide" title="Thalidomide">thalidomide</a>, developed at the end of 1950 by Chemie Grűnenthal as a <a href="/wiki/Hypnotic" title="Hypnotic">hypnotic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antiemetic" title="Antiemetic">antiemetic</a> and therefore frequently prescribed to pregnant women in almost 50 countries worldwide between 1956–1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn978-80-246-1780-0_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn978-80-246-1780-0-28">[28]</a></sup> Until <a href="/wiki/William_McBride_(doctor)" title="William McBride (doctor)">William McBride</a> published the study leading to its withdrawal from the market at 1961, about 8- 10 000 severely malformed children were born. The most typical disorder induced by thalidomide were reductional deformities of the long bones of the extremities (<a href="/wiki/Phocomelia" title="Phocomelia">phocomelia</a>), otherwise a rare deformity, which therefore helped to recognise the teratogenic effect of the new drug. Among other malformations caused by thalidomide were those of ears, eyes, brain, kidney, heart, digestive and respiratory tract. 40% of the prenatally affected children died soon after birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn978-80-246-1780-0_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-isbn978-80-246-1780-0-28">[28]</a></sup> As thalidomide is used today as a treatment for <a href="/wiki/Multiple_myeloma" title="Multiple myeloma">multiple myeloma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leprosy" title="Leprosy">leprosy</a>, several births of affected children were described in spite of the strictly required use of contraception among female patients treated by it.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vitamin_A" title="Vitamin A">Vitamin A</a>, or retinol, is the sole vitamin which is embryotoxic even in a therapeutic dose, for example in <a href="/wiki/Multivitamin" title="Multivitamin">multivitamins</a>, because its metabolite, the <a href="/wiki/Retinoic_acid" title="Retinoic acid">retinoic acid</a>, plays an important role as a signal molecule in the development of several tisues and organs. Its natural precursor, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%92-carotene" title="Β-carotene">β-carotene</a>, is considered safe, whereas the consumption of animal liver can lead to malformation (liver stores lipofile vitamines including retinol).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn978-80-246-1780-0_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-isbn978-80-246-1780-0-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Isotretinoin" title="Isotretinoin">Isotretinoin</a> (13-cis-retinoic-acid; brand name Roaccutane), vitamine A analog, which is often used to treat severe <a href="/wiki/Acne_vulgaris" title="Acne vulgaris">acne</a>, is such a strong teratogen that just a single dose taken by a pregnant woman (even <a href="/wiki/Transdermal" title="Transdermal">transdermally</a>) may result in serious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birth_defect" title="Birth defect">birth defects</a>. Because of this effect, most countries have systems in place to ensure that it is not given to pregnant women, and that the patient is aware of how important it is to prevent pregnancy during and at least one month after treatment. Medical guidelines also suggest that pregnant women should limit <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_A" title="Vitamin A">vitamin A</a> intake to about 700 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%9Cg" title="Μg">μg</a>/day, as it has teratogenic potential when consumed in excess.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid8602195_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid8602195-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16028634_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16028634-30">[30]</a></sup> Vitamine A and similar substances can induce spontaneous abortions, premature births, defects of eyes (<a href="/wiki/Microphthalmia" title="Microphthalmia">microphthalmia</a>), ears, thymus, face deformities, neurological (<a href="/wiki/Hydrocephalus" title="Hydrocephalus">hydrocephalus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microcephalia" title="Microcephalia">microcephalia</a>) and cardiovascular defects, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mental_retardation" title="Mental retardation">mental retardation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn978-80-246-1780-0_28-3"><a href="#cite_note-isbn978-80-246-1780-0-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tetracycline" title="Tetracycline">Tetracycline</a>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotic</a>, should never be prescribed to women in the reproductive age or children, because of its negative impact on <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a> mineralization and <a href="/wiki/Human_tooth_development" title="Human tooth development">teeth mineralization</a>. The "tetracycline teeth" have brown or grey colour as a result of a defective development of both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dentine" title="Dentine">dentine</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enamel_of_teeth" title="Enamel of teeth">enamel of teeth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn978-80-246-1780-0_28-4"><a href="#cite_note-isbn978-80-246-1780-0-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anticonvulsants" title="Anticonvulsants">anticonvulsants</a> are known to be highly teratogenic. <a href="/wiki/Phenytoin" title="Phenytoin">Phenytoin</a>, also known as diphenylhydantoin, along with <a href="/wiki/Carbamazepine" title="Carbamazepine">carbamazepine</a> is responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Fetal_hydantoin_syndrome" title="Fetal hydantoin syndrome">fetal hydantoin syndrome</a>, which may typically include broad nose base, cleft lip and/or palate, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microcephalia" title="Microcephalia">microcephalia</a>, nails and fingers <a href="/wiki/Hypoplasia" title="Hypoplasia">hypoplasia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Small_for_gestational_age" title="Small for gestational age">intrauterine growth restriction</a> and mental retardation. <a href="/wiki/Trimethadione" title="Trimethadione">Trimethadione</a> taken during pregnancy is responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Fetal_trimethadione_syndrome" title="Fetal trimethadione syndrome">fetal trimethadione syndrome</a>, characterized by craniofacial, cardiovascular, renal and spine malformations, along with a delay in mental and physical development. <a href="/wiki/Valproate" title="Valproate">Valproate</a> has anti-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folate" title="Folate">folate</a> effects, leading to <a href="/wiki/Neural_tube" title="Neural tube">neural tube</a> closure-related defects such as <a href="/wiki/Spina_bifida" title="Spina bifida">spina bifida</a>. Lower <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IQ" title="IQ">IQ</a> and <a href="/wiki/Autism" title="Autism">autism</a> have recently also been reported as a result of intrauterine valproate exposure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn978-80-246-1780-0_28-5"><a href="#cite_note-isbn978-80-246-1780-0-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hormonal_contraception" title="Hormonal contraception">Hormonal contraception</a> is considered as harmless for the embryo. Peterka and Novotná<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn978-80-246-1780-0_28-6"><a href="#cite_note-isbn978-80-246-1780-0-28">[28]</a></sup> do however state that syntethic <a href="/wiki/Progestin" title="Progestin">progestines</a> used to prevent miscarriage in the past frequently caused masculinization of the outer reproductive organs of female newborns due to their <a href="/wiki/Androgen" title="Androgen">androgenic</a> activity. <a href="/wiki/Diethylstilbestrol" title="Diethylstilbestrol">Diethylstilbestrol</a> is a synthetic <a href="/wiki/Estrogen" title="Estrogen">estrogen</a> used from the 1940s to 1971 when the prenatal exposition has been linked to the <a href="/wiki/Clear-cell_adenocarcinoma_of_the_vagina" title="Clear-cell adenocarcinoma of the vagina">clear-cell adenocarcinoma of the vagina</a>. Following studies showed elevated risks for other tumors and congenital malformations of the sex organs for both sexes.</p>
<p>All <a href="/wiki/Chemotherapy" title="Chemotherapy">cytostatics</a> are strong teratogenes, <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a> is usually recommended when pregnancy is found during or before chemotherapy. <a href="/wiki/Aminopterin" title="Aminopterin">Aminopterin</a>, a cytostatic drug with anti-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folate" title="Folate">folate</a> effect, was used during the 1950s and 1960s to induce <a href="/wiki/Therapeutic_abortion" title="Therapeutic abortion">therapeutic abortions</a>. In some cases the abortion didn´t happen, but the newborns suffered a fetal aminopterin syndrome consisting of growth retardation, <a href="/wiki/Craniosynostosis" title="Craniosynostosis">craniosynostosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrocephalus" title="Hydrocephalus">hydrocephalus</a>, facial dismorphities, mental retardation and/or leg defomities<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn978-80-246-1780-0_28-7"><a href="#cite_note-isbn978-80-246-1780-0-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aminopterin_syndrome_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aminopterin_syndrome-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_toxical_substances">Environmental toxical substances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Environmental toxical substances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Drinking water is often a vessel through which harmful toxins travel. Studies have shown that heavy metals, elements, nitrates, nitrites, fluoride can be carried through water and cause congenital disorders.</p>
<p>Nitrate, which is found mostly in drinking water from ground sources, is a powerful teratogen. A case-control study in rural Australia that was conducted following frequent reports of prenatal mortality and congenital malformations found that those who drank the nitrate-infected groundwater, as opposed to rain water, ran the risk of giving birth to children with central nervous system disorders, muscoskeletal defects, and cardiac defects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chlorinated and aromatic solvents such as benzene and trichloroethylene sometimes enter the water supply due to oversights in waste disposal. A case-control study on the area found that by 1986, leukemia was occurring in the children of Woburn, Massachusetts at a rate that was four times the expected rate of incidence. Further investigation revealed a connection between the high occurrence of leukemia and an error in water distribution that delivered water to the town with significant contamination manufacturing waste containing trichloroethylene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> As an <a href="/wiki/Endocrine_disruptor" title="Endocrine disruptor">endocrine disruptor</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a> was shown to induce <a href="/wiki/Miscarriage" title="Miscarriage">miscarriages</a>, interfere with the development of the <a href="/wiki/Female_reproductive_system" title="Female reproductive system">female reproductive system</a>, cause the <a href="/wiki/Congenital_hypothyroidism" title="Congenital hypothyroidism">congenital hypothyroidism</a> and suspectibly <a href="/wiki/Childhood_obesity" title="Childhood obesity">childhood obesity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn978-80-246-1780-0_28-8"><a href="#cite_note-isbn978-80-246-1780-0-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fluoride, when transmitted through water at high levels, can also act as a teratogen. Two reports on fluoride exposure from China, which were controlled to account for the education level of parents, found that children born to parents who were exposed to 4.12 PPM fluoride grew to have IQs that were, on average, seven points lower than their counterparts whose parents consumed water that contained 0.91 PPM fluoride. In studies conducted on rats, higher PPM fluoride in drinking water lead to increased acetylcholinesterase levels, which can alter prenatal brain development. The most significant effects were noted at a level of 5 PPM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The fetus is even more susceptible to damage from carbon monoxide intake, which can be harmful when inhaled during pregnancy, usually through first or second-hand tobacco smoke. The concentration of carbon monoxide in the infant born to a non-smoking mother is around 2%, and this concentration drastically increases to a range of 6%-9% if the mother smokes tobacco. Other possible sources of prenatal carbon monoxide intoxication are exhaust gas from combustion motors, use of dichloromethane (paint thinner, varnish removers) in enclosed areas, defective gas hot water heaters, indoor barbeques, open flames in poorly-ventilated areas, atmospheric exposure in highly polluted areas. Exposure to carbon monoxide at toxic levels during the first two trimesters of pregnancy can lead to intrauterine growth restriction, leading to a baby that has stunted growth and is born smaller than 90% of other babies at the same gestational age. The effect of chronic exposure to carbon monoxide can depend on the stage of pregnancy in which the mother is exposed. Exposure during the embryonic stage can have neurological consequences, such as telencephalic dysgenesis, behavioral difficulties during infancy, and reduction of cerebellum volume. There are also possible skeletal defects that could result from exposure to carbon monoxide during the embryonic stage, such as hand and foot malformations, <a href="/wiki/Hip_dysplasia" title="Hip dysplasia">hip dysplasia</a>, hip subluxation, agenisis of a limb, and inferior maxillary atresia with <a href="/wiki/Glossoptosis" title="Glossoptosis">glossoptosis</a>. Also, carbon monoxide exposure between days 35 and 40 of embryonic development can lead to an increased risk of the child developing a cleft palate. Exposure to carbon monoxide or polluted ozone exposure can also lead to cardiac defects of the ventrical septal, pulmonary artery and heart valves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The effects of carbon monoxide exposure are decreased later in fetal development during the fetal stage, but they may still lead to <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">anoxic</a> <a href="/wiki/Encephalopathy" title="Encephalopathy">encephalopathy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Industrial pollution can also lead to congenital defects. Over a period of 37 years, the Chisso Corporation, a petrochemical and plastics company, contaminated the waters of Minamata Bay with an estimated 27 tons of methylmercury, contaminating the local water supply. This led to many people in the area developing what became known as the “Minamata Disease.” Because methylmercury is a teratogen, the mercury poisoning of those residing by the bay resulted in neurological defects in the offspring. Infants exposed to mercury poisoning in utero showed predispositions to <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_palsy" title="Cerebral palsy">cerebral palsy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ataxia" title="Ataxia">ataxia</a>, inhibited psychomotor development, and mental retardation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Landfill sites have been shown to have adverse effects on fetal development. Extensive research has been shown that landfills have several negative effects on babies born to mothers living near landfill sites: low birth weight, birth defects, spontaneous abortion, and fetal and infant mortality. Studies done around the <a href="/wiki/Love_Canal" title="Love Canal">Love Canal</a> site near Niagara Falls and the <a href="/wiki/Lipari_Landfill" title="Lipari Landfill">Lipari Landfill</a> in New Jersey have shown a higher proportion of low birth babies than communities farther away from landfills. A study done in California showed a positive correlation between time and quantity of dumping and low birth weights and neonatal deaths. A study in the United Kingdom showed a correspondence between pregnant women living near landfill sites and an increased risk of congenital disorders, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neural_tube_defects" title="Neural tube defects">neural tube defects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hypospadias" title="Hypospadias">hypospadias</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epispadia" title="Epispadia">epispadia</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abdominal_wall_defects" title="Abdominal wall defects">abdominal wall defects</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Gastroschisis" title="Gastroschisis">gastroschisis</a> and exomphalos. A study conducted on a Welsh community also showed an increase incidence of gastroschisis. Another study was done on twenty-one European hazardous waste sites and showed that those living within three kilometers had an increased risk of giving birth to infants with birth defects and that as distance from the land increased, the risk decreased. These birth defects included neural tube defects, malformations of the cardiac septa, anomalies of arteries and veins, and chromosomal anomalies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Looking at communities that live near landfill sites brings up environmental justice. A vast majority of sites are located near poor, mostly black, communities. For example, between the early 1920s and 1978, about 25% of Houston’s population was black. However, over 80% of landfills and incinerators during this time were located in these black communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another issue regarding <a href="/wiki/Environmental_justice" title="Environmental justice">environmental justice</a> is <a href="/wiki/Lead_poisoning" title="Lead poisoning">lead poisoning</a>. If the fetus is exposed to lead during the pregnancy, this can result in learning difficulties and slowed growth. A lot of paints (before 1978) and pipes contain lead. Therefore, pregnant women who live in homes with lead paint will inhale the dust containing lead, leading to lead exposure in the fetus. When lead pipes are used for drinking water and cooking water, this water is ingested, along with the lead, exposing the fetus to this toxin. This issue is more prevalent in poorer communities. This is because more well off families are able to afford to have their homes repainted and pipes renovated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Paternal_smoking">Paternal smoking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Paternal smoking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Paternal smoking prior to conception has been linked with the increased risk of congenital abnormalities in offspring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeSantis_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-DeSantis-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Smoking causes DNA mutations in the germ line of the father, which can be inherited by the offspring. Cigarette smoke acts as a chemical mutagen on germ cell DNA. The germ cells suffer oxidative damage, and the effects can be seen in altered mRNA production, infertility issues, and side effects in the embryonic and fetal stages of development. This <a href="/wiki/Oxidative_stress" title="Oxidative stress">oxidative damage</a> may result in epigenetic or genetic modifications of the father's germ line. Research has shown that fetal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lymphocytes" title="Lymphocytes">lymphocytes</a> have been damaged as a result of a father's smoking habits prior to conception.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anderson_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-anderson-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ji_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ji-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Correlations between paternal smoking and the increased risk of offspring developing childhood cancers (including acute <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">leukemia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brain_tumors" title="Brain tumors">brain tumors</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lymphoma" title="Lymphoma">lymphoma</a>) before age five have been established. However, further research is needed to confirm these findings. Little is currently known about how paternal smoking damages the fetus, and what window of time in which the father smokes is most harmful to offspring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ji_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ji-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infections">Infections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Infections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vertically_transmitted_infection" title="Vertically transmitted infection">Vertically transmitted infection</a></div>
<p>A <i>vertically transmitted infection</i> is an <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infection</a> caused by <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viruses</a> or, in rare cases, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">parasites</a> <a href="/wiki/Transmission_(medicine)" title="Transmission (medicine)">transmitted</a> directly from the mother to an <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetus</a> or baby during <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Childbirth" title="Childbirth">childbirth</a>. It can occur when the mother gets an infection as an <a href="/wiki/Intercurrent_disease_in_pregnancy" title="Intercurrent disease in pregnancy">intercurrent disease in pregnancy</a>.</p>
<p>Congenital disorders were initially believed to be the result of only hereditary factors. However, in the early 1940s, Australian pediatric ophthalmologist <a href="/wiki/Norman_Gregg" title="Norman Gregg">Norman Gregg</a> began recognizing a pattern in which the infants arriving at his surgery were developing congenital cataracts at a higher rate than those who developed it from hereditary factors. On October 15, 1941, Gregg delivered a paper which explained his findings-68 out of the 78 children who were afflicted with congenital cataracts had been exposed in utero due to an outbreak in Australian army camps. These findings confirmed, to Gregg, that there could, in fact, be environmental causes for congenital disorders.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rubella" title="Rubella">Rubella</a> is known to cause abnormalities of the eye, internal ear, heart, and sometimes the teeth. More specifically, fetal exposure to rubella during weeks five to ten of development (the sixth week particularly) can cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cataracts" title="Cataracts">cataracts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microphthalmia" title="Microphthalmia">microphthalmia</a> in the eyes. If the mother is infected with rubella during the ninth week, a crucial week for internal ear development, there can be destruction of the <a href="/wiki/Organ_of_Corti" title="Organ of Corti">organ of Corti</a>, causing deafness. In the heart the <a href="/wiki/Ductus_arteriosus" title="Ductus arteriosus">ductus arteriosus</a> can remain after birth, leading to hypertension. Rubella can also lead to atrial and ventricular septal defects in the heart. If exposed to rubella in the second trimester, the fetus can develop central nervous system malformations. However, because infections of rubella may remain undetected,misdiagnosed, or unrecognized in the mother, and/or some abnormalities are not evident until later in the child’s life, precise incidence of birth defects due to rubella are not entirely known. The timing of the mother’s infection during fetal development determines the risk and type of birth defect. As the embryo develops, the risk of abnormalities decreases. If exposed to the rubella virus during the first four weeks, the risk of malformations is 47 percent. Exposure during weeks five through eight creates a 22 percent chance, while weeks nine to twelve a seven percent chance exists, followed by a percentage of six if the exposure is during the thirteenth to sixteenth weeks. Exposure during the first eight weeks of development can also lead to prematurity and fetal death. These numbers are calculated from immediate inspection of the infant after birth. Therefore, mental defects are not accounted for in the percentages because they are not evident until later in the child’s life. If they were to be included, these numbers would be much higher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langman.27s_Medical_Embryology_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Langman.27s_Medical_Embryology-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other infectious agents include <a href="/wiki/Cytomegalovirus" title="Cytomegalovirus">cytomegalovirus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Herpes_simplex_virus" title="Herpes simplex virus">herpes simplex virus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyperthermia" title="Hyperthermia">hyperthermia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toxoplasmosis" title="Toxoplasmosis">toxoplasmosis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Syphilis" title="Syphilis">syphilis</a>. Mother exposure to cytomegalovirus can cause <a href="/wiki/Microcephaly" title="Microcephaly">microcephaly</a>, cerebral calcifications, blindess, <a href="/wiki/Chorioretinitis" title="Chorioretinitis">chorioretinitis</a> (which can cause blindness), <a href="/wiki/Hepatosplenomegaly" title="Hepatosplenomegaly">hepatosplenomegaly</a>, and meningoencephalitis in fetuses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langman.27s_Medical_Embryology_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Langman.27s_Medical_Embryology-41">[41]</a></sup> Microcephaly is a disorder in which the fetus has an atypically small head,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> cerebral calcifications means certain areas of the brain have atypical calcium deposits,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> and meningoencephalitis is the enlargement of the brain. All three disorders cause abnormal brain function or mental retardation. Hepatosplenomegaly is the enlargement of the liver and spleen which causes digestive problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> It can also cause some <a href="/wiki/Kernicterus" title="Kernicterus">kernicterus</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petechiae" title="Petechiae">petechiae</a>. Kernicterus causes yellow pigmentation of the skin, brain damage, and deafness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Petechaie is when the capillaries bleed resulting in red/purple spots on the skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> However, cytomegalovirus is often fatal in the embryo.</p>
<p>The herpes simplex virus can cause <a href="/wiki/Microcephaly" title="Microcephaly">microcephaly</a>, microphthalmus (abnormally small eyeballs),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> retinal dysplasia, <a href="/wiki/Hepatosplenomegaly" title="Hepatosplenomegaly">hepatosplenomegaly</a>, and mental retardation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langman.27s_Medical_Embryology_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-Langman.27s_Medical_Embryology-41">[41]</a></sup> Both microphthalmus and retinal dysplasia can cause blindness. However, the most common symptom in infants is an inflammatory response that develops during the first three weeks of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langman.27s_Medical_Embryology_41-3"><a href="#cite_note-Langman.27s_Medical_Embryology-41">[41]</a></sup> Hyperthermia causes <a href="/wiki/Anencephaly" title="Anencephaly">anencephaly</a>, which is when part of the brain and skull are absent in the infant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langman.27s_Medical_Embryology_41-4"><a href="#cite_note-Langman.27s_Medical_Embryology-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Mother exposure to toxoplasmosis can cause cerebral calcification, <a href="/wiki/Hydrocephalus" title="Hydrocephalus">hydrocephalus</a> (causes mental disabilities),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> and mental retardation in infants. Other birth abnormalities have been reported as well, such as <a href="/wiki/Chorioretinitis" title="Chorioretinitis">chorioretinitis</a>, microphthalmus, and ocular defects. Syphilis causes congenital deafness, mental retardation, and diffuse fibrosis in organs, such as the liver and lungs, if the embryo is exposed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langman.27s_Medical_Embryology_41-5"><a href="#cite_note-Langman.27s_Medical_Embryology-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lack_of_nutrients">Lack of nutrients</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Lack of nutrients">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutrition_in_pregnancy" title="Nutrition in pregnancy">Nutrition in pregnancy</a></div>
<p>For example, a lack of <a href="/wiki/Folic_acid" title="Folic acid">folic acid</a>, a vitamin B, in the diet of a mother can cause cellular <a href="/wiki/Neural_tube" title="Neural tube">neural tube</a> deformities that result in <a href="/wiki/Spina_bifida" title="Spina bifida">spina bifida</a>. Congenital disorders such as a neural tube deformity (NTD) can be prevented by 72% if the mother consumes 4 milligrams of folic acid before the conception and after 12 weeks of pregnancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-example1_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-example1-50">[50]</a></sup> Folic acid, or vitamin B<sub>12</sub>, aids the development of the foetal nervous system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-example1_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-example1-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Studies with mice have found that food deprivation of the male mouse prior to conception leads to the offspring displaying significantly lower blood glucose levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anderson2_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-anderson2-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_restraint">Physical restraint</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Physical restraint">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>External physical shocks or constrainment due to growth in a restricted space, may result in unintended deformation or separation of cellular structures resulting in an abnormal final shape or damaged structures unable to function as expected. An example is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potter_syndrome" title="Potter syndrome">Potter syndrome</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Oligohydramnios" title="Oligohydramnios">oligohydramnios</a>. This finding is important for future understandings of how genetics may predispose individuals for diseases like obesity, diabetes, and cancer.</p>
<p>For multicellular organisms that develop in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Womb" title="Womb">womb</a>, the physical interference or presence of other similarly developing organisms such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twins" title="Twins">twins</a> can result in the two cellular masses being integrated into a larger whole, with the combined cells attempting to continue to develop in a manner that satisfies the intended growth patterns of both cell masses. The two cellular masses can compete with each other, and may either duplicate or merge various structures. This results in conditions such as <a href="/wiki/Conjoined_twins" title="Conjoined twins">conjoined twins</a>, and the resulting merged organism may die at birth when it must leave the life-sustaining environment of the womb and must attempt to sustain its biological processes independently.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetic_causes">Genetic causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Genetic causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Genetic_disorder" title="Genetic disorder">Genetic disorder</a></div>
<p>Genetic causes of congenital anomalies include <a href="/wiki/Heredity" title="Heredity">inheritance</a> of abnormal <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a> from the mother or the father, as well as new <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">mutations</a> in one of the <a href="/wiki/Germ_cell" title="Germ cell">germ cells</a> that gave rise to the fetus. Male germ cells mutate at a much faster rate than female germ cells, and as the father ages, the DNA of the germ cells mutates quickly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sartorius_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sartorius-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhu_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zhu-23">[23]</a></sup> If an egg is fertilized with sperm that has damaged DNA, there is a possibility that the fetus could develop abnormally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sartorius_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sartorius-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Savitz_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Savitz-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Genetic_disorder" title="Genetic disorder">Genetic disorders</a> or diseases are all congenital, though they may not be expressed or recognized until later in life. Genetic diseases may be divided into single-gene defects, multiple-gene disorders, or chromosomal defects. Single-gene defects may arise from abnormalities of both copies of an <a href="/wiki/Autosome" title="Autosome">autosomal</a> gene (a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dominance_relationship#Recessive_allele" title="Dominance relationship">recessive</a> disorder) or of only one of the two copies (a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dominance_relationship#Dominant_allele" title="Dominance relationship">dominant</a> disorder). Some conditions result from deletions or abnormalities of a few genes located contiguously on a chromosome. Chromosomal disorders involve the loss or duplication of larger portions of a chromosome (or an entire chromosome) containing hundreds of genes. Large chromosomal abnormalities always produce effects on many different body parts and organ systems.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Socioeconomic_status">Socioeconomic status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Socioeconomic status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A low <a href="/wiki/Socioeconomic_status" title="Socioeconomic status">socioeconomic status</a> in a deprived neighborhood may include exposure to “environmental stressors and risk factors.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graaf_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graaf-52">[52]</a></sup> Socioeconomic inequalities are commonly measured by the Cartairs-Morris score, Index of Multiple Deprivation, Townsend deprivation index, and the Jarman score.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vos_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vos-53">[53]</a></sup> The Jarman score, for example, considers “unemployment, overcrowding, single parents, under-fives, elderly living alone, ethnicity, low social class and residential mobility.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vos_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vos-53">[53]</a></sup> In Vos’ meta-analysis these indices are used to view the effect of low SES neighborhoods on maternal health. In the meta-analysis, data from individual studies were collected from 1985 up until 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vos_53-2"><a href="#cite_note-Vos-53">[53]</a></sup> Vos concludes that a correlation exists between prenatal adversities and deprived neighborhoods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vos_53-3"><a href="#cite_note-Vos-53">[53]</a></sup> Other studies have shown that low SES is closely associated with the development of the fetus in utero and growth retardation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradley_and_Corwyn_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bradley_and_Corwyn-54">[54]</a></sup> Studies also suggest that children born in low SES families are “likely to be born prematurely, at low birth weight, or with asphyxia, a birth defect, a disability, fetal alcohol syndrome, or AIDS.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradley_and_Corwyn_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bradley_and_Corwyn-54">[54]</a></sup> Bradley and Corwyn also suggest that congenital disorders arise from the mother’s lack of nutrition, a poor lifestyle, maternal substance abuse and “living in a neighborhood that contains hazards affecting fetal development (toxic waste dumps).”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradley_and_Corwyn_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bradley_and_Corwyn-54">[54]</a></sup> In a meta-analysis that viewed how inequalities influenced maternal health, it was suggested that deprived neighborhoods often promoted behaviors such as smoking, drug and alcohol use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graaf_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-Graaf-52">[52]</a></sup> After controlling for socioeconomic factors and ethnicity, several individual studies demonstrated an association with outcomes such as perinatal mortality and preterm birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graaf_52-2"><a href="#cite_note-Graaf-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_of_radiation">Role of radiation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Role of radiation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For the survivors of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_bombing_of_Hiroshima" title="Atomic bombing of Hiroshima">atomic bombing of Hiroshima</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_bombing_of_Nagasaki" title="Atomic bombing of Nagasaki">Nagasaki</a>, who are known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Hibakusha" title="Hibakusha">Hibakusha</a></i>, no statistically demonstrable increase of birth defects/congenital malformations was found among their later conceived children, or found in the later conceived children of cancer survivors who had previously received <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiotherapy" title="Radiotherapy">radiotherapy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.ie_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.ie-57">[57]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> The surviving women of Hiroshima and Nagasaki who were able to conceive, though exposed to substantial amounts of radiation, later had children with no higher incidence of abnormalities/birth defects than in the Japanese population as a whole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>Relatively few studies have researched the effects of paternal radiation exposure on offspring. Following the <a href="/wiki/Chernobyl" title="Chernobyl">Chernobyl</a> disaster, it was found that the germ line of irradiated fathers suffered <a href="/wiki/Minisatellite" title="Minisatellite">minisatellite</a> mutations in the DNA, which was inherited by descendants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trasler_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Trasler-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dubrova_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-dubrova-61">[61]</a></sup> Animal studies have shown that the X-ray irradiation of male mice resulted in birth defects of the offspring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anderson_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-anderson-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1980s, a relatively high prevalence of pediatric leukemia cases in children living near a nuclear processing plant in West Cumbria, UK, led researchers to investigate whether the cancer was a result of paternal radiation exposure. A significant association between paternal irradiation and offspring cancer was found, but further research areas close to other nuclear processing plants did not produce the same results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anderson_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-anderson-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trasler_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-Trasler-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Father.27s_age">Father's age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Father's age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Paternal_age_effect" title="Paternal age effect">Paternal age effect</a></div>
<p>The effects of paternal age on offspring are not yet well understood and are studied far less extensively than the effects of maternal age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olshan_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-olshan-62">[62]</a></sup> Fathers contribute proportionally more DNA mutations to their offspring via their germ cells than the mother, with the paternal age governing how many mutations are passed on. This is because, as humans age, male germ cells acquire mutations at a much faster rate than female germ cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sartorius_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sartorius-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anderson_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-anderson-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhu_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Zhu-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Around a 5% increase in the incidence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Septal_defect" title="Septal defect">ventricular septal defects</a>, atrial septal defects, and <a href="/wiki/Patent_ductus_arteriosus" title="Patent ductus arteriosus">patent ductus arteriosus</a> in offspring has been found to be correlated with advanced paternal age. Advanced paternal age has also been linked to increased risk of <a href="/wiki/Achondroplasia" title="Achondroplasia">achondroplasia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apert_syndrome" title="Apert syndrome">Apert syndrome</a>. Offspring born to fathers under the age of 20 show increased risk of being affected by patent ductus arteriosus, ventricular septal defects, and the <a href="/wiki/Tetralogy_of_Fallot" title="Tetralogy of Fallot">tetralogy of Fallot</a>. It is hypothesized that this may be due to environmental exposures or lifestyle choices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olshan_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-olshan-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Research has found that there is a correlation between advanced paternal age and risk of birth defects such as <a href="/wiki/Dysmelia" title="Dysmelia">limb anomalies</a>, syndromes involving multiple systems, and <a href="/wiki/Down_syndrome" title="Down syndrome">Down's syndrome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sartorius_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Sartorius-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhu_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-Zhu-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yang_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-yang-63">[63]</a></sup> Recent studies have concluded that 5-9% of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Down%27s_syndrome" title="Down's syndrome">Down's syndrome</a> cases are due to paternal effects, but these findings are controversial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sartorius_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Sartorius-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Savitz_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Savitz-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhu_23-4"><a href="#cite_note-Zhu-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiener_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-wiener-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is concrete evidence that advanced paternal age is associated with the increased likelihood that a mother will suffer from a <a href="/wiki/Miscarriage" title="Miscarriage">miscarriage</a> or that <a href="/wiki/Perinatal_mortality" title="Perinatal mortality">fetal death</a> will occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sartorius_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Sartorius-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unknown_or_multifactorial">Unknown or multifactorial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Unknown or multifactorial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although significant progress has been made in identifying the etiology of some birth defects, approximately 65% have no known or identifiable cause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-471-38225-6_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-471-38225-6-22">[22]</a></sup> These are referred to as sporadic, a term that implies an unknown cause, random occurrence regardless of maternal living conditions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid10960809_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid10960809-65">[65]</a></sup> and a low recurrence risk for future children. For 20-25% of anomalies there seems to be a "multifactorial" cause, meaning a complex interaction of multiple minor genetic anomalies with environmental risk factors. Another 10-13% of anomalies have a purely environmental cause (e.g. infections, illness, or drug abuse in the mother). Only 12-25% of anomalies have a purely genetic cause. Of these, the majority are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromosomal_anomalies" title="Chromosomal anomalies">chromosomal anomalies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KAF473_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-KAF473-66">[66]</a></sup></p>

<p>Congenital anomalies resulted in about 632,000 deaths per year in 2013 down from 751,000 in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GBD204_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-GBD204-9">[9]</a></sup> The type with the greatest death are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congenital_heart_disease" title="Congenital heart disease">congenital heart disease</a> (323,000), followed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neural_tube_defects" title="Neural tube defects">neural tube defects</a> (69,000).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GBD204_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-GBD204-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many studies have found that the frequency of occurrence of certain congenital malformations depends on the sex of the child (table).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cui_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cui-72">[72]</a></sup> For example, pyloric stenosis occurs more often in males while congenital hip dislocation is four to five times more likely to occur in females. Among children with one kidney, there are approximately twice as many males, whereas among children with three kidneys there are approximately 2.5 times more females. The same pattern is observed among infants with excessive number of ribs, vertebrae, teeth and other organs which in a process of evolution have undergone reduction—among them there are more females. Contrarily, among the infants with their scarcity, there are more males. Anencephaly is shown to occur approximately twice as frequently in females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_reports_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_reports-73">[73]</a></sup> The number of boys born with 6 fingers is two times higher than the number of girls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Now various techniques are available to detect congenital anomalies in fetus before birth.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>About 3% of newborns have a "major physical anomaly", meaning a physical anomaly that has cosmetic or functional significance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Physical congenital abnormalities are the leading cause of infant mortality in the United States, accounting for more than 20% of all infant deaths. Seven to ten percent of all children<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title='Of literally "all" children or just "all" of the 3% above? (November 2014)'>clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> will require extensive medical care to diagnose or treat a birth defect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid2468064_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid2468064-76">[76]</a></sup></p>

<p>P. M. Rajewski and A. L. Sherman (1976) have analyzed the frequency of congenital anomalies in relation to the system of the organism. Prevalence of men was recorded for the anomalies of phylogenetically younger organs and systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rajewski_77-17"><a href="#cite_note-Rajewski-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<p>In respect of an etiology, sexual distinctions can be divided on appearing before and after differentiation of male's gonads in during embryonic development, which begins from eighteenth week. The testosterone level in male embryos thus raises considerably.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> The subsequent hormonal and physiological distinctions of male and female embryos can explain some sexual differences in frequency of congenital defects. It is difficult to explain the observed differences in the frequency of birth defects between the sexes by the details of the reproductive functions or the influence of environmental and social factors.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congenital_disorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The CDC and National Birth Defect Project studied the incidence of birth defects in the US. Key findings include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Down syndrome was the most common condition with an estimated prevalence of 14.47 per 10,000 live births, implying about 6,000 diagnoses each year.</li>
<li>About 7,000 babies are born with a cleft palate, cleft lip or both.</li>
