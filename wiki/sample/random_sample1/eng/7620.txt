<p><b>Pulpit</b> is a raised stand for <a href="/wiki/Preacher" title="Preacher">preachers</a> in a Christian church. The origin of the word is the Latin <i>pulpitum</i> (platform or staging).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The traditional pulpit is raised well above the surrounding floor for audibility and visibility, accessed by steps, with sides coming to about waist height. From the late medieval period onwards, pulpits have often had a canopy known as the <a href="/wiki/Sounding_board" title="Sounding board">sounding board</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abat-voix" title="Abat-voix">abat-voix</a> above and sometimes also behind the speaker, normally in wood. Though sometimes highly decorated, this is not purely decorative, but can have a useful acoustic effect in projecting the preacher's voice to the congregation below. Most pulpits have one or more book-stands for the preacher to rest his or her bible, notes or texts upon.</p>
<p>The pulpit is generally reserved for <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a>. This is mandated in the regulations of the Roman Catholic church, and several others (though not always strictly observed). Even in Welsh <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonconformism" title="Nonconformism">Nonconformism</a>, this was felt appropriate, and in some chapels a second pulpit was built opposite the main one for lay exhortations, testimonials and other speeches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Francis.2C_19_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Francis.2C_19-2">[2]</a></sup> Many churches have a second, smaller stand called the <a href="/wiki/Lectern" title="Lectern">lectern</a>, which can be used by lay persons, and is often used for all the readings and ordinary announcements. The traditional Catholic location of the pulpit to the side of the <a href="/wiki/Chancel" title="Chancel">chancel</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nave" title="Nave">nave</a> has been generally retained by episcopalian and some other Protestant denominations, while in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presbyterian" title="Presbyterian">Presbyterian</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evangelical" title="Evangelical">Evangelical</a> churches the pulpit has often replaced the altar at the centre.</p>
<p>Equivalent platforms for speakers are the <a href="/wiki/Bema" title="Bema">bema</a> (bima, bimah) of Ancient Greece and Jewish synagogues, and the <a href="/wiki/Minbar" title="Minbar">minbar</a> of Islamic mosques. <i>From the pulpit</i> is often used <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphorically</a> for something which is said with official church <a href="/wiki/Authority" title="Authority">authority</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location_of_pulpit_and_lectern">Location of pulpit and lectern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulpit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location of pulpit and lectern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In many Reformed and Evangelical Protestant denominations, the pulpit is at the centre of the front of the church (and any <a href="/wiki/Altar" title="Altar">altar</a> or <a href="/wiki/Communion_table" title="Communion table">communion table</a> off to one side), while in the Catholic, Lutheran and Anglican traditions the pulpit is placed to one side and the altar or communion table is in the centre. In many Christian churches, there are two speakers' stands at the front of the church. Often, the one on the left (as viewed by the congregation) is called the pulpit. Since the <a href="/wiki/Gospel" title="Gospel">Gospel</a> lesson is often read from the pulpit, the pulpit side of the church is sometimes called the <i>gospel side</i>.</p>
<p>In both Catholic and Protestant churches the pulpit may be located closer to the main congregation in the <a href="/wiki/Nave" title="Nave">nave</a>, either on the nave side of the <a href="/wiki/Crossing_(architecture)" title="Crossing (architecture)">crossing</a>, or at the side of the nave some way down. This is especially the case in large churches, to ensure the preacher can be heard by all the congregation. Fixed seating for the congregation came relatively late in the history of church architecture, so the preacher being behind some of the congregation was less of an issue than later. Fixed seating facing forward in the nave and modern electric amplification has tended to reduce the use of pulpits in the middle of the nave. Outdoor pulpits, usually attached to the exterior of the church, or at a <a href="/wiki/Preaching_cross" title="Preaching cross">preaching cross</a>, are also found in several denominations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Francis.2C_19_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Francis.2C_19-2">[2]</a></sup> If attached to the outside wall of a church, these may be entered from a dooorway in the wall, or by steps outside.</p>
<p>The other speaker's stand, usually on the right (as viewed by the congregation), is known as the <a href="/wiki/Lectern" title="Lectern">lectern</a>. The word <i>lectern</i> comes from the Latin word "lectus", past participle of legere, meaning "to read", because the lectern primarily functions as a reading stand. It is typically used by lay people to read the scripture lessons (except for the Gospel lesson), to lead the congregation in prayer, and to make announcements. Because the epistle lesson is usually read from the lectern, the lectern side of the church is sometimes called the <i>epistle side</i>. In other churches, the lectern, from which the <a href="/wiki/Epistle" title="Epistle">Epistle</a> is read, is located to the congregation's left and the pulpit, from which the sermon is delivered, is located on the right (the Gospel being read from either the centre of the chancel or in front of the altar).</p>
<p>Though unusual, movable pulpits with wheels were also found in English churches. They were either wheeled into place for each service where they would be used or, as at the hospital church in <a href="/wiki/Shrewsbury" title="Shrewsbury">Shrewsbury</a>, rotated to different positions in the church quarterly in the year, to allow all parts of the congregation a chance to have the best sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Francis.2C_18_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Francis.2C_18-3">[3]</a></sup> A portable outside pulpit of wood and canvas was used by <a href="/wiki/John_Wesley" title="John Wesley">John Wesley</a>, and a 19th-century Anglican vicar devised a folding iron pulpit for using outdoors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Francis.2C_18_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Francis.2C_18-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulpit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Ancient Greek <i><a href="/wiki/Bema" title="Bema">bema</a></i> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">βῆμα</span>) means both ‘platform’ and ‘step’, and was used for a variety of secular raised speaking platforms in ancient Greece and Rome, and from those times to today for the central raised platform in Jewish <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogues</a>. Modern synagougue bemas are often similar in form to centrally-placed pulpits in Evangelical churches.</p>
<p>The use of a bema carried over from Judaism into early Christian <a href="/wiki/Church_architecture" title="Church architecture">church architecture</a>. It was originally a raised platform, often large, with a lectern and seats for the clergy, from which <a href="/wiki/Lection" title="Lection">lessons</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scripture" title="Scripture">Scriptures</a> were read and the sermon was delivered. In <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">Western Christianity</a> the bema developed over time into the sanctuary and <a href="/wiki/Chancel" title="Chancel">chancel</a> (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presbytery_(architecture)" title="Presbytery (architecture)">presbytery</a>).</p>
<p>The next development was the <i>ambo</i>, from a Greek word meaning an elevation. This was originally a raised platform from which the <a href="/wiki/Epistle" title="Epistle">Epistle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gospel" title="Gospel">Gospel</a> would be read, and was an option to be used as a preacher's platform for <a href="/wiki/Homily" title="Homily">homilies</a>, though there were others. Saint <a href="/wiki/John_Chrysostom" title="John Chrysostom">John Chrysostom</a> (died 407) is recorded as preaching from the ambo, but this was probably uncommon at this date. In cathedrals early bishops seem often to have preached from their chair in the apse, echoing the position of magistrates in the secular basilicas whose general form most large early churches adopted. Often there were two ambos, one to each side, one used more as a platform on which the choir sang; sometimes the gospel was read, chanted or sung from one side and the epistle from the other. The location of the ambo within the church varied, with about the same range of places as modern pulpits. In ancient Syrian churches it was often placed in the centre of the nave (on both axes). Gradually the ambo came to resemble the modern pulpit in both form and function, though early examples in large churches are often large enough to accommodate several people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The steps up to the pulpit almost invariably approach it from the side or behind, and are often curved. The typical design of the Islamic minbar, where a straight flight of steps leads to the front of the pulpit, is very different.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ambon_of_Henry_II" title="Ambon of Henry II">Ambon of Henry II</a>, an Imperial gift of 1014 to <a href="/wiki/Aachen_Cathedral" title="Aachen Cathedral">Aachen Cathedral</a>, was originally installed centrally, but later moved to the side. It is richly decorated with sheets of gold, ivory, and gems, probably emulating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Justinian" title="Justinian">Justinian</a>'s lost pulpit of <a href="/wiki/Hagia_Sophia" title="Hagia Sophia">Hagia Sophia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, of which a description by <a href="/wiki/Paul_the_Silentiary" title="Paul the Silentiary">Paul the Silentiary</a> survives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In churches where there is only one speaker's stand at the front of the church, it serves the functions of both lectern and pulpit and may be called the ambo, which is still the official Catholic term for the place the gospel is read from.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protestantism">Protestantism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulpit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Protestantism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> churches, the pulpit is considered the most important piece of furniture in the sanctuary. It is located centrally in relation to the congregation and raised. In such churches it may be where the <a href="/wiki/Minister_(Christianity)" title="Minister (Christianity)">minister</a> stands for most of the service. In the eighteenth century, two-decker and triple-decker pulpits were often introduced in English-speaking countries. The three levels of lecterns were intended to show the relative importance of the readings delivered there. The bottom tier was for community announcements, the middle was for the gospel, and the top tier was reserved for the delivery of the sermon. A good example of a three-decker pulpit is found in <a href="/wiki/St_Andrew%27s_Church,_Slaidburn" title="St Andrew's Church, Slaidburn">St Andrew's Church, Slaidburn</a>, Lancashire. America's only surviving three-decker pulpit on the centerline of the church is at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trinity_Church,_Newport,_Rhode_Island" title="Trinity Church, Newport, Rhode Island">Trinity Church, Newport, Rhode Island</a>.</p>
<p>In many <a href="/wiki/Evangelicalism" title="Evangelicalism">Evangelical Christian</a> churches, the pulpit stands squarely in the centre of the platform, and is generally the largest piece of church furniture. This is to symbolize the proclamation of the Word of God as the central focus of the weekly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Service_of_worship" title="Service of worship">service of worship</a>. In more contemporary evangelical churches, the pulpit may be much smaller, if used at all, and is generally carried out after the end of the song service. However, it usually is placed in the centre of the platform as well.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Presbyterian_churches">Presbyterian churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulpit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Presbyterian churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:StAndrews-Rome2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1122" data-file-width="1542" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/StAndrews-Rome2.jpg/220px-StAndrews-Rome2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/StAndrews-Rome2.jpg/330px-StAndrews-Rome2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/StAndrews-Rome2.jpg/440px-StAndrews-Rome2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:StAndrews-Rome2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/St_Andrew%27s_Church,_Rome" title="St Andrew's Church, Rome">St Andrew's Church, Rome</a> (<a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scotland" title="Church of Scotland">Church of Scotland</a>): interior, showing central pulpit</div>

<p>Traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presbyterian" title="Presbyterian">Presbyterian</a> Churches in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and elsewhere often had a central pulpit, that is, the pulpit was located in the centre of the chancel in the position where most churches have the communion table or altar. The table could be situated in front of the pulpit or to the side, and sometimes was not in the chancel area at all.</p>
<p>This declares the Bible to be the foundation of the faith. Furthermore, the "Centrality of the Word" implies that the reading and preaching of the Bible is the centrepiece of a service of worship, and thus takes priority over the sacraments. The central pulpit is intended to give visual representation of this idea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the late 19th century, the fashion in the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scotland" title="Church of Scotland">Church of Scotland</a> and most other Presbyterian denominations has been for a return to the pre-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reformation" title="Reformation">Reformation</a> layout. Thus many buildings which once had a central pulpit now have a pulpit to the side. See for example <a href="/wiki/Skene_Parish_Church" title="Skene Parish Church">Skene Parish Church</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_West_Church,_Boston,_Massachusetts" title="Old West Church, Boston, Massachusetts">Old West Church, Boston, Massachusetts</a>.</p>
<p>This Presbyterian tradition is historically distinct from the tradition of the <a href="/wiki/Ambon_(liturgy)" title="Ambon (liturgy)">ambon</a> in Eastern Christianity.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_Catholicism">Roman Catholicism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulpit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Roman Catholicism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Roman Catholic</a> churches, the stand used for readings and <a href="/wiki/Homily" title="Homily">homilies</a> is formally called the <b>ambo</b>. Despite its name, this structure usually more closely resembles a lectern than the <a href="#Ambon">ambon</a> of the Eastern Rites. The readings are typically read from an ambo in the <a href="/wiki/Chancel" title="Chancel">chancel</a>, and depending on the arrangement of the church, the homily may be delivered from a raised pulpit where there is one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/General_Instruction_of_the_Roman_Missal" title="General Instruction of the Roman Missal">General Instruction of the Roman Missal</a> (GIRM) specifies:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>309. The dignity of the Word of God requires that in the church there be a suitable place from which it may be proclaimed and toward which the attention of the faithful naturally turns during the Liturgy of the Word. It is appropriate that generally this place be a stationary ambo and not simply a movable lectern. The ambo must be located in keeping with the design of each church in such a way that the ordained ministers and readers may be clearly seen and heard by the faithful. From the ambo only the readings, the Responsorial Psalm, and the <a href="/wiki/Exsultet" title="Exsultet">Easter Proclamation</a> (Exsultet) are to be proclaimed; likewise it may be used for giving the Homily and for announcing the intentions of the Universal Prayer. The dignity of the ambo requires that only a minister of the word should stand at it. ...<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GIRM_309_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-GIRM_309-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_Orthodoxy">Eastern Orthodoxy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulpit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Eastern Orthodoxy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ambon_(liturgy)" title="Ambon (liturgy)">Ambon (liturgy)</a></div>
<p>In modern <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Christianity" title="Eastern Christianity">Eastern Christianity</a> the area directly in front of the Beautiful Gates of the <a href="/wiki/Iconostasis" title="Iconostasis">iconostasis</a> from which the Gospel is typically read is called the <a href="/wiki/Ambon_(liturgy)" title="Ambon (liturgy)">ambon</a>, and the entire low elevation above the level of the <a href="/wiki/Nave" title="Nave">nave</a> in front of the iconostasis is called the <a href="/wiki/Soleas" title="Soleas">soleas</a>. In larger churches, the ambo might be distinguished by three curved steps by which one may reach it from the nave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In addition many Orthodox churches, especially Greek-speaking churches, have pulpits for preaching from, which are similar to those in <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">Western Christianity</a>.</p>
<p>In Eastern Orthodox Church <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">cathedrals</a> there is usually a low platform in the center of the nave called the <i>episcopal ambo</i> where the <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishop</a> is vested prior to the <a href="/wiki/Divine_Liturgy" title="Divine Liturgy">Divine Liturgy</a> and where he is enthroned until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entrance_(Liturgical)" title="Entrance (Liturgical)">Little Entrance</a>. If the bishop is serving in a simple parish church, an episcopal ambo is set temporarily in place.</p>
<p>In addition to the ambo, many major churches in Greece and Cyprus also have a raised pulpit on the left side of the nave, usually attached to a column and raised several feet high. This is reached by a narrow flight of stairs. It is considered an architectural element that is symmetrical to the bishop's throne, which is located in an equivalent position on the right. Pulpit and throne are usually similar in construction, usually made of either sculpted stone or sculpted wood. This pulpit was used mostly for sermons and in order to improve audibility, before the advent of modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_address" title="Public address">public address</a> systems in churches. Nowadays it is used rarely. Tradition dictates that it be used for the reading of the "12 Passion Gospels" during the <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday#Matins_of_Holy_and_Great_Friday" title="Good Friday">Matins of Holy Friday</a>, served late in the evening of <a href="/wiki/Maundy_Thursday" title="Maundy Thursday">Maundy Thursday</a>. This is done to signify that the Passion of Christ is being "broadcast" for all to know. In the same spirit, a phonetic transcription of the relevant Gospel passages is provided in several common languages (e.g. English, French, Russian, Arabic etc.), so that they may be read from this pulpit at the same time.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decoration">Decoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulpit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Decoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Siena.Duomo.pulpit02a.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="892" data-file-width="944" height="208" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Siena.Duomo.pulpit02a.jpg/220px-Siena.Duomo.pulpit02a.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Siena.Duomo.pulpit02a.jpg/330px-Siena.Duomo.pulpit02a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Siena.Duomo.pulpit02a.jpg/440px-Siena.Duomo.pulpit02a.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Siena.Duomo.pulpit02a.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Siena_Cathedral_Pulpit" title="Siena Cathedral Pulpit">Siena Cathedral Pulpit</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Nicola_Pisano" title="Nicola Pisano">Nicola Pisano</a>, 1268</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The exterior of a wood or stone pulpit may be decorated, especially with carved <a href="/wiki/Relief" title="Relief">reliefs</a>, and in the centuries after the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> these were sometimes, especially in Lutheran churches, one of the few areas of the church left with figurative decoration such as scenes from the <a href="/wiki/Life_of_Christ_in_art" title="Life of Christ in art">Life of Christ</a>. Pulpit reliefs were especially important at the start of the Italian Renaissance, including those from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pisa_Baptistry" title="Pisa Baptistry">Pisa Baptistry</a> (1260) and <a href="/wiki/Siena_Cathedral_Pulpit" title="Siena Cathedral Pulpit">Siena Cathedral Pulpit</a> (1265-68) by <a href="/wiki/Nicola_Pisano" title="Nicola Pisano">Nicola Pisano</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pulpit_of_Sant%27_Andrea,_Pistoia_(Giovanni_Pisano)" title="Pulpit of Sant' Andrea, Pistoia (Giovanni Pisano)">Pulpit of Sant' Andrea, Pistoia by Giovanni Pisano</a> (1301), and those by <a href="/wiki/Donatello" title="Donatello">Donatello</a></p>
<p>Elements of decoration shared between Catholic and Protestant denominations are the flowers that may be placed in front of the pulpit, and the <a href="/wiki/Antependium" title="Antependium">antependium</a> or "pulpit fall", a piece of cloth that covers the top of the book-stand in the pulpit and hangs down a short way at the front. It is often of a rich material and decorated with Christian symbols. Flags and banners used by church-related organizations may also stand on the floor around the pulpit.</p>
<p>In the Reformed tradition, though avoiding figurative art, pulpits were increasingly important as a focus for the church, with the sanctuary now comparatively bare and de-emphasized, and were often larger and more elaborately decorated than in medieval churches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The bookstand of the pulpit (usually in medieval churches) or lectern (common in Anglican churches) may be formed in the shape of an eagle. The eagle symbolizes the gospels, and shows where these were read from at the time the eagle was placed there. When pulpits like those by the Pisani with eagles in stone on them were built the gospel reading was done from the pulpit.</p>
<p>The spread of the sounding board offered artists decorating Catholic <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> churches a space for spectacular features of various types on top of it. An artistic conceit largely confined to the 18th-century <a href="/wiki/Rococo" title="Rococo">Rococo</a> churches of South Germany was to shape the body of the pulpit as a ship, to utilize the old metaphor of the <a href="/wiki/Navicella_(mosaic)" title="Navicella (mosaic)">church as a ship</a>. This allowed for fantastical plaster or wood decoration of sails and rigging manned by angels above, and apostles hauling in nets below.</p>
