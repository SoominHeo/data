<p><b>Sugar sculpture</b> is the art of producing artistic centerpieces entirely composed of <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> and sugar derivatives. There are many competitions that include sugar sculpture, and popular television networks, such as <a href="/wiki/Food_Network" title="Food Network">Food Network</a>, televise many of these events.</p>
<p>Sugar showpieces can be composed of several different types of sugar elements. All begin with cooking sugar, and possibly an acidic agent and/or non-<a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a> sugar product to avoid unwanted crystallization, to the <a href="/wiki/Candy#Sugar_stages" title="Candy">hard crack stage</a>, around 300 °F (149 °C).</p>
<p>When all components are completed, they are welded together using a gas torch. The sugar is melted, and then joined together.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugar_sculpture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pulled_sugar">Pulled sugar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugar_sculpture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pulled sugar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The sugar has been cooked, and the now-liquid sugar is poured onto a <a href="/wiki/Silicone_rubber" title="Silicone rubber">silicone rubber</a> mat (e.g., <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Silpat&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Silpat (page does not exist)">Silpat</a>). Any coloring is now added. The sugar is then folded repeatedly into itself, until the sugar is, while still flexible, cool enough to handle. The sugar is then stretched out and then folded on itself repeatedly. This process incorporates air into the sugar, and gives it a bright lustery sheen. The sugar can then be sculpted by hand into various shapes, made into ribbons, or blown.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blown_sugar">Blown sugar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugar_sculpture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Blown sugar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In blown sugar, a portion of pulled sugar is placed on a rubber pump which is tipped with either wood or metal. Pumps are most commonly hand pumps. While being blown, the sugar can be shaped, often into animals or flowers. Blown sugar cannot be quickly cooled by dipping it in water, so chefs must use fans to cool the sugar, all the while rotating it, so that it does not come out of shape. This technique is very useful in making balloons for wedding cakes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cast_sugar">Cast sugar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugar_sculpture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cast sugar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In this technique, sugar is poured into molds. This technique produces more sturdy pieces than pulled and blown sugar, and is almost always used for the base and structural elements of showpieces. Cast sugar can also be used in many recipes such as a simplicity called cake.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pastillage">Pastillage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugar_sculpture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pastillage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:S%C3%A8vres_-_Moulage-reparage_-_pastillage_27.jpg"><img alt="The hands of a person, shaping a lump of white paste" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="5184" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/S%C3%A8vres_-_Moulage-reparage_-_pastillage_27.jpg/220px-S%C3%A8vres_-_Moulage-reparage_-_pastillage_27.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/S%C3%A8vres_-_Moulage-reparage_-_pastillage_27.jpg/330px-S%C3%A8vres_-_Moulage-reparage_-_pastillage_27.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/S%C3%A8vres_-_Moulage-reparage_-_pastillage_27.jpg/440px-S%C3%A8vres_-_Moulage-reparage_-_pastillage_27.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:S%C3%A8vres_-_Moulage-reparage_-_pastillage_27.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Making a rose out of pastillage</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A thick sugar paste, similar to gum paste, is molded into shapes. When dried, it is hard and brittle. Made with gelatin, water and confectioner's sugar, it hardens quickly and can be shaped for a short while by hand, and after hardening, with electric grinders, cutters, sandpaper and assorted files. Some recipes will contain an acid<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> in the ingredients list, such as vinegar or cream of tartar.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pressed_sugar">Pressed sugar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugar_sculpture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pressed sugar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Granulated sugar is mixed with a minimal amount of water, and is put under pressure. It hardens into a solid piece. Though this is used for showpiece bases, it is less often used because of the time required to produce it, and its lesser aesthetic value.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rock_sugar">Rock sugar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugar_sculpture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rock sugar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The liquid sugar is blended with a small amount of <a href="/wiki/Royal_icing" title="Royal icing">royal icing</a>. The heat from the sugar causes the air incorporated in the icing to rapidly expand, causing the mixture to grow to several times its original volume. The mixture is quickly poured into a lined dish, and placed into a blast chiller to set. This process produces a sugar mass with the texture of volcanic <a href="/wiki/Pumice" title="Pumice">pumice</a>, the color of which is determined by the color of the sugar syrup.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spun_sugar">Spun sugar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugar_sculpture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Spun sugar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alinea_-_COURSE_24.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="649" data-file-width="800" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Alinea_-_COURSE_24.jpg/220px-Alinea_-_COURSE_24.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Alinea_-_COURSE_24.jpg/330px-Alinea_-_COURSE_24.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Alinea_-_COURSE_24.jpg/440px-Alinea_-_COURSE_24.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alinea_-_COURSE_24.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Spun sugar around a bite-size dessert</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Sugar syrup is made into long extremely thin strands which can be shaped to make things like birds nests. The sugar is gathered on a fork or a special tool designed for spinning sugar and is flicked in long strokes over succeeding pipes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugar_sculpture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sugar sculptures may be used as alternatives to a <a href="/wiki/Wedding_cake" title="Wedding cake">wedding cake</a>. However they are often much more expensive, as they are <a href="/wiki/Bespoke" title="Bespoke">made-to-order</a> and very time consuming to create.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sugar_people">Sugar people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugar_sculpture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sugar people">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sugar_people" title="Sugar people">Sugar people</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Ameya_by_Robert_Frederick_Blum.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3068" data-file-width="3809" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/The_Ameya_by_Robert_Frederick_Blum.jpg/220px-The_Ameya_by_Robert_Frederick_Blum.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/The_Ameya_by_Robert_Frederick_Blum.jpg/330px-The_Ameya_by_Robert_Frederick_Blum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/The_Ameya_by_Robert_Frederick_Blum.jpg/440px-The_Ameya_by_Robert_Frederick_Blum.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Ameya_by_Robert_Frederick_Blum.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
"The Ameya (Japanese candy man)" by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Frederick_Blum" title="Robert Frederick Blum">Robert Frederick Blum</a>, 1893</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This traditional Chinese art form uses heated, liquid sugar to create three dimensional, often blown figures. It's called "糖人(sugar people)". Japan has the similar tradition, which is called "<a href="/wiki/Amezaiku" title="Amezaiku">Amezaiku</a>（飴細工)". The sugar people's candy in China are brownish-yellow, while the ones in Japan are white because they use "sarashi-ame (whitish candy)," which is made by pulling and stretching out the sugar many times until white.</p>
