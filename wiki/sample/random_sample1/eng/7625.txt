<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Sulfoxide</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>sulfoxide</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> containing a <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfinyl" title="Sulfinyl">sulfinyl</a></b> (SO) <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functional group</a> attached to two carbon atoms. It is a polar functional group. Sulfoxides are the oxidized derivatives of <a href="/wiki/Thioether" title="Thioether">sulfides</a>. Examples of important sulfoxides are <a href="/wiki/Alliin" title="Alliin">alliin</a>, a precursor to the compound that gives freshly crushed garlic its aroma, and <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfoxide" title="Dimethyl sulfoxide">DMSO</a>, a common solvent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_bonding">Structure and bonding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure and bonding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sulfoxides feature a pyramidal sulfur center with relatively short S-O distances. In DMSO, the S-O distance is 1.531 Å.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sulfoxides are generally represented with the structural formula R–S(=O)–R', where R and R' are organic groups. The bond between the <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> atoms is intermediate of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dative_bond" title="Dative bond">dative bond</a> and a polarized <a href="/wiki/Double_bond" title="Double bond">double bond</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bonding_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-bonding-3">[3]</a></sup> The S–O interaction has an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrostatic" title="Electrostatic">electrostatic</a> aspect, resulting in significant <a href="/wiki/Bond_dipole_moment" title="Bond dipole moment">dipolar</a> character, with negative charge centered on oxygen. A <a href="/wiki/Lone_pair" title="Lone pair">lone pair</a> of electrons resides on the sulfur atom giving it tetrahedral electron pair geometry and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigonal_pyramidal" title="Trigonal pyramidal">trigonal pyramidal</a> shape (steric number 4 with one lone pair; see <a href="/wiki/VSEPR_theory" title="VSEPR theory">VSEPR theory</a>). When the two organic residues are dissimilar, the sulfur is a <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">chiral center</a>, for example, methylphenylsulfoxide.</p>
<p>The energy required to invert this <a href="/wiki/Stereocenter" title="Stereocenter">stereocenter</a> is sufficiently high that sulfoxides are optically stable, that is, the rate of <a href="/wiki/Racemization" title="Racemization">racemization</a> is slow at room temperature.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sulfoxides are typically prepared by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_oxidation" title="Organic oxidation">oxidation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">sulfides</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A typical oxidant is hydrogen peroxide. Oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Thioanisole" title="Thioanisole">thioanisole</a> can be effected with periodate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In these oxidations, care is required to avoid over oxidation to the sulfone. <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfide" title="Dimethyl sulfide">Dimethyl sulfide</a> is oxidized to <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfoxide" title="Dimethyl sulfoxide">dimethyl sulfoxide</a> and then to <a href="/wiki/Methylsulfonylmethane" title="Methylsulfonylmethane">dimethyl sulfone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Unsymmetrical sulfides are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prochiral" title="Prochiral">prochiral</a>, thus their oxidation gives chiral sulfoxides. Certain reagents or <a href="/wiki/Asymmetric_catalytic_oxidation" title="Asymmetric catalytic oxidation">catalysts</a> effect enantioselective oxidations.</p>
<p>Diaryl sulfoxides can be prepared by Friedel-Crafts arylation of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> using acid catalyst:</p>
<dl>
<dd>2 ArH + SO<sub>2</sub> → Ar<sub>2</sub>SO + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sulfoxides, such as DMSO, have basic character, being excellent <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligands</a> and readily alkylated. Similarly, they can be oxidized to sulfones.</p>
<p>Alkyl sulfoxides are susceptible to deprotonation of α-CH groups by strong bases, such as <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydride" title="Sodium hydride">sodium hydride</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>CH<sub>3</sub>S(O)CH<sub>3</sub> + NaH → CH<sub>3</sub>S(O)CH<sub>2</sub>Na + H<sub>2</sub></dd>
</dl>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Pummerer_rearrangement" title="Pummerer rearrangement">Pummerer rearrangement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alkyl" title="Alkyl">alkyl</a> sulfoxides react with acetic anhydride to give migration of the oxygen from sulfur to the adjacent carbon as an <a href="/wiki/Acetate" title="Acetate">acetate</a> ester.</p>
<dl>
<dd><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pummerer_Rearrangement_Scheme.png"><img alt="Pummerer Rearrangement Scheme.png" data-file-height="292" data-file-width="982" height="107" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Pummerer_Rearrangement_Scheme.png/360px-Pummerer_Rearrangement_Scheme.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Pummerer_Rearrangement_Scheme.png/540px-Pummerer_Rearrangement_Scheme.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Pummerer_Rearrangement_Scheme.png/720px-Pummerer_Rearrangement_Scheme.png 2x" width="360"/></a></dd>
</dl>
<p>Sulfoxides form complexes with transition metals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<dd>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chiral sulfoxides find application in certain drugs such as <a href="/wiki/Esomeprazole" title="Esomeprazole">esomeprazole</a> and <a href="/wiki/Armodafinil" title="Armodafinil">armodafinil</a>, and they are also employed as <a href="/wiki/Chiral_auxiliary" title="Chiral auxiliary">chiral auxiliaries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> DMSO is widely used as a solvent in the laboratory.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_occurrence">Natural occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Natural occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Methionine_sulfoxide" title="Methionine sulfoxide">Methionine sulfoxide</a> forms from the amino acid <a href="/wiki/Methionine" title="Methionine">methionine</a> and its accumulation is associated with aging. The enzyme <a href="/wiki/DMSO_reductase" title="DMSO reductase">DMSO reductase</a> catalyzes the interconversion of DMSO and dimethylsulfide.</p>
