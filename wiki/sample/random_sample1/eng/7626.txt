<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Island country</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>An <b>island country</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">country</a> whose primary territory consists of one or more <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">islands</a> or parts of islands. As of 2011, 46 (approximately 24%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Small_is_Democratic_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Small_is_Democratic-1">[1]</a></sup>) of the 193 <a href="/wiki/Member_states_of_the_United_Nations" title="Member states of the United Nations">UN member states</a> are island countries.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Island_country&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The percentage of island countries that are <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democratic</a> is higher than that of <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/continental#Adjective" title="wikt:continental">continental</a> countries. Historically they have been more prone to political stability than their continental counterparts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Small_is_Democratic_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Small_is_Democratic-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="War">War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Island_country&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Island countries have often been the basis of maritime conquest and historical rivalry between other countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_quest_for_identity_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_quest_for_identity-2">[2]</a></sup> Island countries are more susceptible to attack by large, continental countries due to their size and dependence on <a href="/wiki/Sea_lines_of_communication" title="Sea lines of communication">sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Line_of_communication" title="Line of communication">air lines of communication</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Measures_of_Effectiveness_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Measures_of_Effectiveness-3">[3]</a></sup> Many island countries are also vulnerable to predation by mercenaries and other foreign invaders,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mercenary_Holding_Island_Nation_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mercenary_Holding_Island_Nation-4">[4]</a></sup> although their isolation also makes them a difficult target.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_resources">Natural resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Island_country&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Natural resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many island countries rely heavily on <a href="/wiki/Fish_as_food" title="Fish as food">fish</a> for their main supply of food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OTTAWA_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-OTTAWA-5">[5]</a></sup> Some are turning to <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a>—such as <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydropower</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">geothermal power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a> from <a href="/wiki/Coconut_oil" title="Coconut oil">copra oil</a>—to combat the rise in <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a> prices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-renewable_energy_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-renewable_energy-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Island_country&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some island countries are more affected than other countries by climate change, which produces problems such as reduced land use, water scarcity and sometimes even resettlement issues. Some low-lying island countries are slowly being submerged by the rising water levels of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leader_of_disappearing_island_nation_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leader_of_disappearing_island_nation-7">[7]</a></sup> Climate change also impacts island countries by causing natural disasters such as <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>, hurricanes, <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">flash floods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climate_change_threatens_food_security_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Climate_change_threatens_food_security-8">[8]</a></sup> In 2011, the Center for Climate Change Law (CCCL) held a conference attended by 272 registrants from 39 island nations titled <i>Legal Issues for Threatened Island Nations</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Island_country&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many island countries rely heavily on imports and are greatly affected by changes in the global economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cctv_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-cctv-10">[10]</a></sup> The economies of (small) island countries are usually smaller and more vulnerable to shipping costs, environmental damage to infrastructure, and isolation from other economies than those of larger, continental countries; exceptions include Japan, Australia and the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trade_Policy_Course_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trade_Policy_Course-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZ_calls_for_global_solutions_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-NZ_calls_for_global_solutions-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-U.N._ambassador_gives_keynote_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-U.N._ambassador_gives_keynote-13">[13]</a></sup> The dominant industry for many island countries is <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tourist_destinations_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-tourist_destinations-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Island_country&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Island countries are typically small with low populations, although some, like <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> are notable exceptions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wen_pledges_new_aid_to_Pacific_countries_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wen_pledges_new_aid_to_Pacific_countries-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some island countries are centred on one or two major islands, such as the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bahrain" title="Bahrain">Bahrain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>. Others are spread out over hundreds or thousands of smaller islands, such as <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seychelles" title="Seychelles">Seychelles</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a>. Some island countries share one or more of their islands with other countries, such as the United Kingdom and <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>; <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, which shares islands with <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Timor" title="East Timor">East Timor</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>.</p>
<p>Geographically, the country of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> is considered a <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continental</a> landmass rather than an island, covering the largest landmass of the <a href="/wiki/Australia_(continent)" title="Australia (continent)">Australian continent</a>. In the past, however, it was considered an island country for tourism purposes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nla.gov.au_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-nla.gov.au-16">[16]</a></sup> (among others) and is still often referred to as such.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-China.2C_Pacific_island_countries_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-China.2C_Pacific_island_countries-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
