<p>The <b>ciliates</b> are a group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protozoan" title="Protozoan">protozoans</a> characterized by the presence of hair-like organelles called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cilia" title="Cilia">cilia</a>, which are identical in structure to <a href="/wiki/Flagellum" title="Flagellum">eukaryotic flagella</a>, but are in general shorter and present in much larger numbers, with a different <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/undulating" title="wikt:undulating">undulating</a> pattern than flagella. Cilia occur in all members of the group (although the peculiar <a href="/wiki/Suctoria" title="Suctoria">Suctoria</a> only have them for part of the <a href="/wiki/Biological_life_cycle" title="Biological life cycle">life-cycle</a>) and are variously used in swimming, crawling, attachment, feeding, and sensation. All behavioral patterns are coordinated by signaling processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ciliates are an important group of <a href="/wiki/Protist" title="Protist">protists</a>, common almost everywhere there is water — in lakes, ponds, oceans, rivers, and soils. About 3,500 species have been described, and the potential number of extant species is estimated at 30,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Included in this number are many <a href="/wiki/Ectosymbiosis" title="Ectosymbiosis">ectosymbiotic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endosymbiotic" title="Endosymbiotic">endosymbiotic</a> species, as well as some <a href="/wiki/Obligate_parasite" title="Obligate parasite">obligate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Facultative_parasite" title="Facultative parasite">opportunistic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">parasites</a>. Ciliate species range in size from as little as 10 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micrometres" title="Micrometres">µm</a> to as much as 4 mm in length, and include some of the most <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphologically</a> complex protozoans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In most systems of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_classification" title="Biological classification">taxonomy</a>, "<b>Ciliophora</b>" is ranked as a <a href="/wiki/Phylum" title="Phylum">phylum</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> under either the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_(biology)" title="Kingdom (biology)">kingdom</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protista" title="Protista">Protista</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid19121402_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid19121402-7">[7]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">Protozoa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17669163_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17669163-8">[8]</a></sup> In some systems of classification, ciliated protozoa are placed within the <a href="/wiki/Class_(biology)" title="Class (biology)">class</a> "<b>Ciliata,"</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urlIntroduction_to_the_Ciliata_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-urlIntroduction_to_the_Ciliata-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> (a term which can also refer to an unrelated <a href="/wiki/Ciliata_(genus)" title="Ciliata (genus)">genus of fish</a>). In the taxonomic scheme proposed by the International Society of Protistologists, which eliminates formal <a href="/wiki/Taxonomic_rank" title="Taxonomic rank">rank</a> designations such as "phylum" and "class", "Ciliophora" is an unranked <a href="/wiki/Taxonomic_rank" title="Taxonomic rank">taxon</a> within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolata" title="Alveolata">Alveolata</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclei">Nuclei</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciliate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nuclei">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unlike most other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eukaryotes" title="Eukaryotes">eukaryotes</a>, ciliates have two different sorts of <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">nuclei</a>: a tiny, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diploid" title="Diploid">diploid</a> <a href="/wiki/Micronucleus" title="Micronucleus">micronucleus</a> (reproduction), and a large, <a href="/wiki/Polyploid" title="Polyploid">polyploid</a> <a href="/wiki/Macronucleus" title="Macronucleus">macronucleus</a> (general cell regulation). The latter is generated from the micronucleus by amplification of the genome and heavy editing. The micronucleus serves as the germ line nucleus, but does not express its genes. The macronucleus provides the nuclear RNA for vegetative growth.</p>
<p>Division of the macronucleus occurs by amitosis, and the segregation of the chromosomes occurs by a process whose mechanism is unknown. This process is not perfect, and after about 200 generations the cell shows signs of aging. Periodically the macronuclei must be regenerated from the micronuclei. In most, this occurs during <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexual_conjugation" title="Sexual conjugation">conjugation</a></i>. Here two cells line up, the micronuclei undergo <a href="/wiki/Meiosis" title="Meiosis">meiosis</a>, some of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haploid" title="Haploid">haploid</a> daughters are exchanged and then fuse to form new micronuclei and macronuclei.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cytoplasm">Cytoplasm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciliate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cytoplasm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Food <a href="/wiki/Vacuole" title="Vacuole">vacuoles</a> are formed through <a href="/wiki/Phagocytosis" title="Phagocytosis">phagocytosis</a> and typically follow a particular path through the cell as their contents are digested and broken down by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lysosomes" title="Lysosomes">lysosomes</a> so the substances the vacuole contains are then small enough to <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffuse</a> through the membrane of the food vacuole into the cell. Anything left in the food vacuole by the time it reaches the cytoproct (anus) is discharged by <a href="/wiki/Exocytosis" title="Exocytosis">exocytosis</a>. Most ciliates also have one or more prominent contractile vacuoles, which collect water and expel it from the cell to maintain osmotic pressure, or in some function to maintain ionic balance. In some genera, such as <i>Paramecium</i>, these have a distinctive star shape, with each point being a collecting tube.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Specialized_structures_in_ciliates">Specialized structures in ciliates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciliate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Specialized structures in ciliates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cilia are arranged in rows called <i>kineties</i>. In some forms there are also body polykinetids, for instance, among the <a href="/wiki/Spirotrich" title="Spirotrich">spirotrichs</a> where they generally form bristles called <i>cirri</i>. More often body cilia are arranged in <i>mono-</i> and <i>dikinetids</i>, which respectively include one and two kinetosomes (basal bodies), each of which may support a cilium. These are arranged into rows called <i>kineties</i>, which run from the anterior to posterior of the cell. The body and oral kinetids make up the <i>infraciliature</i>, an organization unique to the ciliates and important in their classification, and include various fibrils and <a href="/wiki/Microtubule" title="Microtubule">microtubules</a> involved in coordinating the cilia.</p>
<p>The infraciliature is one of the main component of the cell cortex. Another are the <i>alveoli</i>, small vesicles under the cell membrane that are packed against it to form a <a href="/wiki/Protozoa#Pellicle" title="Protozoa">pellicle</a> maintaining the cell's shape, which varies from flexible and contractile to rigid. Numerous <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrion" title="Mitochondrion">mitochondria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Extrusome" title="Extrusome">extrusomes</a> are also generally present. The presence of alveoli, the structure of the cilia, the form of mitosis and various other details indicate a close relationship between the ciliates, <a href="/wiki/Apicomplexa" title="Apicomplexa">Apicomplexa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dinoflagellate" title="Dinoflagellate">dinoflagellates</a>. These superficially dissimilar groups make up the <a href="/wiki/Alveolate" title="Alveolate">alveolates</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciliate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most ciliates are <a href="/wiki/Heterotroph" title="Heterotroph">heterotrophs</a>, feeding on smaller organisms, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterium" title="Bacterium">bacteria</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alga" title="Alga">algae</a>, and detritus swept into the oral groove (mouth) by modified oral cilia. This usually includes a series of membranelles to the left of the mouth and a paroral membrane to its right, both of which arise from <i>polykinetids</i>, groups of many cilia together with associated structures. The food is moved by the cilia through the mouth pore into the gullet, which forms food vacuoles.</p>
<p>Feeding techniques vary considerably, however. Some ciliates are mouthless and feed by absorption (<a href="/wiki/Osmotrophy" title="Osmotrophy">osmotrophy</a>), while others are predatory and feed on other protozoa and in particular on other ciliates. Some ciliates parasitize <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>, although only one species, <i><a href="/wiki/Balantidium_coli" title="Balantidium coli">Balantidium coli</a></i>, is known to cause disease in humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lynn2008_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lynn2008-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction_and_sexual_phenomena">Reproduction and sexual phenomena</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciliate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reproduction and sexual phenomena">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Unk.cilliate.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="662" data-file-width="885" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Unk.cilliate.jpg/220px-Unk.cilliate.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Unk.cilliate.jpg/330px-Unk.cilliate.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Unk.cilliate.jpg/440px-Unk.cilliate.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Unk.cilliate.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ciliate undergoing the last processes of binary fission, a form of asexual reproduction</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Ciliates reproduce asexually, by various kinds of <a href="/wiki/Fission_(biology)" title="Fission (biology)">fission</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lynn2008_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lynn2008-13">[13]</a></sup> During fission, the micronucleus undergoes <a href="/wiki/Mitosis" title="Mitosis">mitosis</a> and the macronucleus elongates and undergoes <a href="/wiki/Amitosis" title="Amitosis">amitosis</a> (except among the <a href="/wiki/Karyorelictea" title="Karyorelictea">Karyorelictean</a> ciliates, whose macronuclei do not divide). The cell then divides in two, and each new cell obtains a copy of the micronucleus and the macronucleus.</p>
<p>Typically, the cell is divided transversally, with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anterior" title="Anterior">anterior</a> half of the ciliate (the <i>proter</i>) forming one new organism, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Posterior_(anatomy)" title="Posterior (anatomy)">posterior</a> half (the <i>opisthe</i>) forming another. However, other types of fission occur in some ciliate groups. These include <i>budding</i> (the emergence of small ciliated offspring, or "swarmers", from the body of a mature parent); <i><a href="/wiki/Strobilation" title="Strobilation">strobilation</a></i> (multiple divisions along the cell body, producing a chain of new organisms); and <i>palintomy</i> (multiple fissions, usually within a <a href="/wiki/Microbial_cyst" title="Microbial cyst">cyst</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lynn2008_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lynn2008-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fission may occur spontaneously, as part of the vegetative <a href="/wiki/Cell_cycle" title="Cell cycle">cell cycle</a>. Alternatively, it may proceed as a result of self-fertilization (<a href="/wiki/Autogamy" title="Autogamy">autogamy</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> or it may follow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexual_conjugation" title="Sexual conjugation">conjugation</a>, a sexual phenomenon in which ciliates of compatible <a href="/wiki/Mating_type" title="Mating type">mating types</a> exchange genetic material.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conjugation">Conjugation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciliate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conjugation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt>Overview</dt>
</dl>
<p>During conjugation, two cells form a bridge between their <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasm" title="Cytoplasm">cytoplasms</a>, the micronuclei undergo <a href="/wiki/Meiosis" title="Meiosis">meiosis</a>, the macronuclei disappear, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haploid" title="Haploid">haploid</a> micronuclei are exchanged over the bridge. In some ciliates (such as <i><a href="/wiki/Vorticella" title="Vorticella">Vorticella</a></i>), conjugating cells become permanently fused, and one conjugant is absorbed by the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In most ciliate groups, however, the cells separate after conjugation, and both form new macronuclei from their micronuclei.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urlIntroduction_to_the_Ciliata_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-urlIntroduction_to_the_Ciliata-9">[9]</a></sup> Conjugation and autogamy are always followed by fission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lynn2008_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lynn2008-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>More details</dt>
</dl>
<p>In general the process is as follows but the details may vary between species:</p>
<ul>
<li>Compatible mating strains meet and partly fuse.</li>
<li>The micronuclei undergo meiosis producing four haploid micronuclei per cell.</li>
<li>Three of these micronuclei disintegrate. The fourth undergoes mitosis.</li>
<li>The two cells exchange a micronucleus.</li>
<li>The cells then separate.</li>
<li>The micronuclei in each cell fuse.</li>
<li>This is followed by mitosis which occurs three times giving rise to eight micronuclei.</li>
<li>Four of the new micronuclei transform into macronuclei</li>
<li>Finally binary fission occurs twice yielding four daughter cells.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="DNA_Rearrangements_.28gene_scrambling.29">DNA Rearrangements (gene scrambling)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciliate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: DNA Rearrangements (gene scrambling)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Development_of_the_Oxytricha_macronuclear_genome.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1153" data-file-width="2049" height="281" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Development_of_the_Oxytricha_macronuclear_genome.jpg/500px-Development_of_the_Oxytricha_macronuclear_genome.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Development_of_the_Oxytricha_macronuclear_genome.jpg/750px-Development_of_the_Oxytricha_macronuclear_genome.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Development_of_the_Oxytricha_macronuclear_genome.jpg/1000px-Development_of_the_Oxytricha_macronuclear_genome.jpg 2x" width="500"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Development_of_the_Oxytricha_macronuclear_genome.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Development of the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxytricha_trifallax" title="Oxytricha trifallax">Oxytricha</a></i> macronuclear genome from micronuclear genome</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Ciliates contain two types of nuclei: <a href="/wiki/Somatic_(biology)" title="Somatic (biology)">somatic</a> "<a href="/wiki/Macronucleus" title="Macronucleus">macronucleus</a>" and the <a href="/wiki/Germline" title="Germline">germline</a> "<a href="/wiki/Micronucleus" title="Micronucleus">micronucleus</a>". Only the DNA in the micronucleus is passed on during sexual reproduction (conjugation). On the other hand, only the DNA in the macronucleus is actively expressed and results in the <a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotype</a> of the organism. Macronuclear DNA is derived from micronuclear DNA by amazingly extensive DNA rearrangement and amplification.</p>
<p>The macronucleus begins as a copy of the micronucleus. The micronuclear <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromosomes" title="Chromosomes">chromosomes</a> are fragmented into many smaller pieces and amplified to give many copies. The resulting macronuclear chromosomes often contain only a single gene. In <i><a href="/wiki/Tetrahymena" title="Tetrahymena">Tetrahymena</a></i>, the micronucleus has 10 chromosomes (5 per haploid genome), while the macronucleus has over 20,000 chromosomes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid21956937_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid21956937-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, the micronuclear genes are interrupted by numerous "Internal Eliminated Sequences" (IESs). During development of the macronucleus, IESs are deleted and the remaining gene segments, Macronuclear Destined Sequences (MDSs), are spliced together to give the operational gene. <i>Tetrahymena</i> has about 6000 IESs and about 15% of micronuclear DNA is eliminated during this process. The process is guided by small RNAs and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epigenetic" title="Epigenetic">epigenetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Chromatin" title="Chromatin">chromatin</a> marks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid21956937_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid21956937-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Spirotrich" title="Spirotrich">spirotrich</a> ciliates (such as <i><a href="/wiki/Oxytricha_trifallax" title="Oxytricha trifallax">Oxytricha</a></i>), the process is even more complex due to "gene scrambling": the MDSs in the micronucleus are often in different order and orientation from that in the macronuclear gene, and so in addition to deletion, DNA <a href="/wiki/Chromosomal_inversion" title="Chromosomal inversion">inversion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chromosomal_translocation" title="Chromosomal translocation">translocation</a> are required for "unscrambling". This process is guided by long RNAs derived from the parental macronucleus. More than 95% of micronuclear DNA is eliminated during spirotrich macronuclear development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid21956937_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-pmid21956937-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_record">Fossil record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciliate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fossil record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Until recently, the oldest ciliate fossils known were <a href="/wiki/Tintinnid" title="Tintinnid">tintinnids</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> Period. In 2007, Li <i>et al.</i> published a description of fossil ciliates from the <a href="/wiki/Doushantuo_Formation" title="Doushantuo Formation">Doushantuo Formation</a>, about 580 million years ago, in the <a href="/wiki/Ediacaran" title="Ediacaran">Ediacaran</a> Period. These included two types of tintinnids and a possible ancestral <a href="/wiki/Suctoria" title="Suctoria">suctorian</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
