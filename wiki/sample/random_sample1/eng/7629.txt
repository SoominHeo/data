<p>The <b>blue mussel</b> (<i>Mytilus edulis</i>), also known as the <b>common mussel</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is a medium-sized edible <a href="/wiki/Marine_(ocean)" title="Marine (ocean)">marine</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bivalve" title="Bivalve">bivalve</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mollusc" title="Mollusc">mollusc</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Mytilidae" title="Mytilidae">Mytilidae</a>, the mussels. Blue mussels are subject to commercial use and intensive <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Systematics_and_distribution">Systematics and distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_mussel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Systematics and distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Mytilus_edulis_complex">The <i>Mytilus edulis</i> complex</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_mussel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Mytilus edulis complex">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Systematically blue mussels consist of a group of (at least) three closely related taxa of mussels, known as the <b><i>Mytilus edulis</i> complex</b>. Collectively they occupy both coasts of the North Atlantic (including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>) and of the North <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a> in temperate to polar waters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> as well as coasts of similar nature in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>. The distribution of the component taxa has been recently modified as a result of human activity (<i><a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a></i>). The taxa can hybridise with each other, if present at the same locality.</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Mytilus edulis</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensu_stricto" title="Sensu stricto">sensu stricto</a>: Native to the North Atlantic.
<ul>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mytilus_edulis_platensis" title="Mytilus edulis platensis">Mytilus edulis platensis</a></i> ( = <i>Mytilus chilensis</i>), the Chilean mussel: Temperate waters in the Southern Hemisphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Borsa_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Borsa-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mytilus_galloprovincialis" title="Mytilus galloprovincialis">Mytilus galloprovincialis</a></i>, the Mediterranean mussel: Native in the Mediterranean, the Black Sea and Western Europe. Introduced in the temperate North Pacific, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> and elsewhere in the Southern Hemisphere. A distinct lineage native to the Southern Hemisphere also exists.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Mytilus_trossulus" title="Mytilus trossulus">Mytilus trossulus</a></i>: North Pacific, northern parts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mytilus_edulis.2C_strict_sense"><i>Mytilus edulis</i>, strict sense</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_mussel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mytilus edulis, strict sense">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Atlantic blue mussel is native on the North American Atlantic coast, but is found intermixed with <i>M. trossulus</i> north of <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>. In Europe it is found from French Atlantic coast northwards to <a href="/wiki/Novaya_Zemlya" title="Novaya Zemlya">Novaya Zemlya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, but not in the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>. In France and in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, it makes <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_zone" title="Hybrid zone">hybrid zones</a> with <i>M. galloprovincialis</i>, and also is sometimes intermixed with <i>M. trossulus</i>.</p>
<p>A genetically distinct lineage of <i>M. edulis</i> is present in the Southern Hemisphere, and has been attributed to subspecies <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mytilus_edulis_platensis" title="Mytilus edulis platensis">Mytilus edulis platensis</a></i>. This includes the <a href="/wiki/Mytilus_chilensis" title="Mytilus chilensis">Chilean mussel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Borsa_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Borsa-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_mussel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Blue mussels are boreo-temperate invertebrates that live in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intertidal" title="Intertidal">intertidal</a> areas attached to rocks and other hard substrates by strong (and somewhat elastic) thread-like structures called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byssal_thread" title="Byssal thread">byssal threads</a>, secreted by byssal glands located in the foot of the mussel.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_mussel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The shape of the shell is triangular and elongate with rounded edges. The shell is smooth with a <a href="/wiki/Sculpture_(mollusc)" title="Sculpture (mollusc)">sculpturing</a> of fine concentric growth lines but no radiating ribs. The shells of this species are purple, blue or sometimes brown in color, occasionally with radial stripes. The outer surface of the shell is covered by the <a href="/wiki/Periostracum" title="Periostracum">periostracum</a> which as eroded, exposes the colored prismatic calcitic layer. Blue Mussels are semi-sessile, having the ability to detach and reattach to a surface allowing the mollusk to reposition itself relative to the water position.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_mussel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mussels have separate sexes. Once the sperm and eggs are fully developed they are released into the water column for fertilization. Although there are about 10,000 sperm per egg,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> large proportions of eggs deposited by blue mussel are never fertilized. As few as 1% of larvae that do mature ever reach adulthood, the majority are eaten by predators before completing <a href="/wiki/Metamorphosis" title="Metamorphosis">metamorphosis</a>.</p>
<p>The reproductive strategy seen in blue mussels is characteristic of planktotrophs, by minimizing nutrients in egg production to the bare minimum they are able to maximize the number of gametes produced. If the adult mussels are stressed during the beginning of gametogenesis, the process is terminated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bayne_261_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bayne_261-5">[5]</a></sup> When stressed while fresh gametes are present, adult mussels reabsorb gametes. Larvae viability is also affected by the condition of parents: high water temperatures, pollutants and scarcity of food, during gamete production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bayne_261_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bayne_261-5">[5]</a></sup> The reduction in viability is probably due to the lack of <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipid</a> reserves distributed to the eggs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Larval_development">Larval development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_mussel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Larval development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Larval development can last from 15 to 35 days depending environmental conditions including salinity and temperature, as well as location. Larvae originating from <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a> mature normally at 15–20 °C (59–68 °F), though at 15 °C (59 °F) normal development occurs at salinities between 15 and 35 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_trillion" title="Parts per trillion">ppt</a> and 20 at 35 ppt at 20 °C (68 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first stage of development is the ciliated embryo, which in 24-hours for fertilization form the <a href="/wiki/Trochophore" title="Trochophore">trochophore</a>. At this point although mobile, it is still reliant on the yolk for nutrients. Characterized by a functional mouth and alimentary canal the veliger stage also has cilia which are used for filtering food as well as propulsion. A thin translucent shell is secreted by the shell gland forming the notable straight hinge of the prodissoconch I shell. The veliger continues to mature forming the prodissoconch II shell. In the end stage of veliger development photosensitive eye spots and elongated foot with a byssal gland are formed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Once the pediveliger is fully developed, its foot extends and makes contact with substrate. The initial contact with the substrate is loose. If the substrate is suitable, the larva will metamorphoses into the juvenile form, plantigrade, and attach byssus threads. The mussel will remain in that state until reaching 1-1.5mm in length. This attachment is the prerequisite for the foundation for the blue mussel population. In sheltered environments large masses sometimes form beds which offer shelter and food for other invertebrates. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byssal_thread" title="Byssal thread">Byssal thread</a> are secreted by byssal glands located in the foot of the mussel, and are made up of <a href="/wiki/Polyphenolic_protein" title="Polyphenolic protein">polyphenolic proteins</a> are proteins which serve as a bioadhesive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aggregation">Aggregation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_mussel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Aggregation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Blue mussels occasionally form clumps, or aggregates, of individuals when <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> is low.The mussels attach to one another via collagenous protein strands called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Byssal_threads&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Byssal threads (page does not exist)">byssal threads</a>. The aggregates are observed mostly in Mussel fields, which are short-lived populations of Mussels, usually exhibiting a clumped <a href="/wiki/Range_(biology)" title="Range (biology)">distribution pattern</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nehls_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nehls-8">[8]</a></sup> It is hypothesized that the mussels form these aggregates to increase reproductive success in low density populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This hypothesis, however, has yet to be conclusively proven. Alternative possible reasons for the behavior include resisting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wave_action" title="Wave action">wave action</a> and increasing water flow through the <a href="/wiki/Mussel" title="Mussel">siphons</a> of the mussel. The significance of the behavior is its relation to the formation of mussel beds from mussel fields. Mussel beds are persistent, dense mussel populations. Beds generally form from fields that persist long enough to establish a dense population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nehls_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nehls-8">[8]</a></sup> Thus in areas where blue mussels are threatened, such as the <a href="/wiki/Wadden_Sea" title="Wadden Sea">Wadden Sea</a>, it is of great importance to enhance the survival of mussel fields, of which mussel aggregates are the primary component.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators">Predators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_mussel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Predators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Predation of blue mussels is greatest during the 3 weeks it spends as a planktonic larva. During this stage it is susceptible to <a href="/wiki/Jellyfish" title="Jellyfish">jellyfish</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_larva" title="Fish larva">fish larvae</a> through adults. Once it metamorphoses the mussel is still restricted by predation, with smaller mussels with thinner, weaker shells most affected. Once the shells becomes stronger, blue mussels are preyed upon by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_stars" title="Sea stars">sea stars</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Asterias" title="Asterias">Asterias vulgaris</a></i> as well as by several species of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_gulls" title="Sea gulls">sea gulls</a>. The capability of shell thickening by mussels has become a very effective defense mechanism. In the presence of predators a mussel is able to increase shell thickness 5 to 10 percent, which in turn makes opening the shell take 50 percent more time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Small mussels are also eaten by the dog whelk, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucella_lapillus" title="Nucella lapillus">Nucella lapillus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The blue mussel is host to a wide range of parasites, but these parasites usually do not cause much damage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses_and_ecosystem_services">Uses and ecosystem services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_mussel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Uses and ecosystem services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Blue mussels are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filter_feeders" title="Filter feeders">filter feeders</a> and play a vital role in estuaries by removing bacteria and toxins. <i>Mytilus edulis</i> is commonly harvested for food throughout the world, from both wild and farmed sources. Mussels are a staple of many seafood dishes in various cuisines including <a href="/wiki/Spanish_cuisine" title="Spanish cuisine">Spanish</a> (especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galician_people" title="Galician people">Galician</a>), <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_cuisine" title="Portuguese cuisine">Portuguese</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_cuisine" title="French cuisine">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_cuisine" title="Dutch cuisine">Dutch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgian_cuisine" title="Belgian cuisine">Belgian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italian_cuisine" title="Italian cuisine">Italian</a>. They are also commonly used as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lab_animals" title="Lab animals">lab animals</a>. Blue mussels were also harvested by the indigenous peoples of North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
