<p>The <b>ribbon of Saint George</b> (also known as <b>Saint George's ribbon</b> and the <b>Georgian ribbon</b>; <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Георгиевская лента</span>, <i>Georgiyevskaya lenta</i>) is a widely recognized military symbol in Russia. The ribbon consists of a black and orange bicolour pattern, with three black and two orange stripes. It appears as a component of many high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_decorations" title="Military decorations">military decorations</a> awarded by the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, and the current <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian Federation</a>.</p>
<p>During Soviet times, the ribbon held no public significance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kp5_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-kp5-1">[1]</a></sup> The symbol was revived in Russia in 2005 as a response to the pro-democratic <a href="/wiki/Orange_Revolution" title="Orange Revolution">Orange Revolution</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kp5_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-kp5-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grps2005_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-grps2005-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grps2005_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-grps2005-2">[2]</a></sup> That year, Russian state media along with youth organizations launched the campaign ahead of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> memorial celebrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kp5_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-kp5-1">[1]</a></sup> It<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="Statement needs to be more specific about the content to which it refers. (September 2015)">specify</span></a></i>]</sup> was associated with units who were awarded the collective <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_guards" title="Russian guards">Guard</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_honours" title="Battle honours">battle honours</a> during the conflict, due to the usage of the color scheme in the <a href="/wiki/Medal_%22For_the_Victory_over_Germany_in_the_Great_Patriotic_War_1941%E2%80%931945%22" title='Medal "For the Victory over Germany in the Great Patriotic War 1941–1945"'>Great Patriotic War victory medal</a> awarded to all personnel, civilian or military, who aided the war effort.</p>
<p>In Russia, the ribbon of Saint George is also used by civilians as a <a href="/wiki/Patriotism" title="Patriotism">patriotic</a> symbol and as a symbol of <a href="/wiki/Loyalty" title="Loyalty">public support</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Russia" title="Government of Russia">Russian government</a>, particularly since 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OD150501_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-OD150501-3">[3]</a></sup> In Ukraine and the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_states" title="Baltic states">Baltic states</a> (<a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a>), the symbol has become widely associated with <a href="/wiki/Russophilia" title="Russophilia">Russian nationalist</a> and separatist sentiment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RFE150507_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-RFE150507-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribbon_of_Saint_George&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Georgian ribbon emerged as part of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_St._George" title="Order of St. George">Order of Saint George</a>, established in 1769 as the highest military decoration of <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Imperial Russia</a> (and re-established in 1998 by <a href="/wiki/Decree_of_the_President_of_Russia" title="Decree of the President of Russia">Presidential decree</a> signed by then <a href="/wiki/President_of_Russia" title="President of Russia">President of Russia</a> <a href="/wiki/Boris_Yeltsin" title="Boris Yeltsin">Boris Yeltsin</a>). While the Order of Saint George was normally not a collective award, the ribbon was sometimes granted to regiments and units that performed brilliantly during wartime and constituted an integral part of some collective battle honours (such as <a href="/wiki/Colours,_standards_and_guidons" title="Colours, standards and guidons">banners</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pennon" title="Pennon">pennants</a>). When not awarded the full Order, some distinguished officers were granted ceremonial swords, adorned with the Georgian ribbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ria_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ria-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1806, distinctive Georgian banners were introduced as a further battle honour awarded to meritorious Guards and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Imperial_Guard" title="Russian Imperial Guard">Leib Guard</a> regiments. The pike on which these flags were mounted was topped by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cross_of_Saint_George" title="Cross of Saint George">Cross of Saint George</a> and adorned with 4,44 cm wide Georgian ribbons. It remained the highest collective military award in the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Russian_Army" title="Imperial Russian Army">Imperial Russian Army</a> until the Revolution in 1917. It is worth noting that the tsarist version was yellow and black, not orange and black like the revived Soviet version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-facts_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-facts-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The symbolism of the orange and black (or yellow and black) is thought to represent fire and gunpowder of war, or the death and resurrection of <a href="/wiki/Saint_George" title="Saint George">Saint George</a>, or the colours of the original <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_the_Russian_Empire" title="Coat of arms of the Russian Empire">Russian imperial coat of arms</a> (black eagle on a golden background).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-facts_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-facts-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The title of the Soviet Guards was first introduced on 18 September 1941 in accordance with the decision of the Headquarters of the Supreme Commander-in-Chief (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Ставка Верховного Главнокомандующего</span>, <a href="/wiki/Stavka" title="Stavka">Stavka</a>, <i>Verkhovnogo Glavnokomanduyushchego</i>) and by the order No. 308 of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Commissar" title="People's Commissar">People's Commissar</a> of Defense for the distinguished services during the <a href="/wiki/Yelnya_Offensive" title="Yelnya Offensive">Yelnya Offensive</a>. The 100th, 127th, 153rd and 161st Rifle Divisions were renamed into the 1st, 2nd, 3rd and 4th Guards Divisions, respectively. The units and formations nominated for the Soviet Guard title received special Guards banner in accordance with the decision of the <a href="/wiki/Presidium_of_the_Supreme_Soviet" title="Presidium of the Supreme Soviet">Presidium of the Supreme Soviet</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">USSR</a>. On 21 May 1942, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presidium_of_the_Supreme_Soviet_of_the_USSR" title="Presidium of the Supreme Soviet of the USSR">Presidium</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supreme_Soviet_of_the_USSR" title="Supreme Soviet of the USSR">Supreme Soviet of the USSR</a> introduced Guards <a href="/wiki/Military_rank" title="Military rank">ranks</a> and Guards <a href="/wiki/Badge" title="Badge">badges</a> to be worn of the right side of the chest. Both included the Georgian ribbon pattern. In June 1943, they introduced the Guards Red Banners for the land forces and in February 1944 - for the naval forces. Georgian ribbons adorned the banners exactly as in the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-facts_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-facts-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>Established on 8 November 1943, the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Glory" title="Order of Glory">Order of Glory</a> (<i>Orden Slavy</i>) was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Order_(decoration)" title="Order (decoration)">order</a> of the Soviet Union. It was awarded to non-commissioned officers and the rank-and-file of the armed forces, as well as to junior lieutenants of the air force, for bravery in the face of the enemy. The ribbon of the Order was orange with three black stripes - the same as that of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cross_of_Saint_George" title="Cross of Saint George">Cross of Saint George</a>.</p>
<p>One of the most honourable medals in the Soviet Union, the medal <a href="/wiki/Medal_%22For_the_Victory_over_Germany_in_the_Great_Patriotic_War_1941%E2%80%931945%22" title='Medal "For the Victory over Germany in the Great Patriotic War 1941–1945"'>"For the Victory over Germany"</a> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">За победу над Германией</span>, <i>Za pobedu nad Germaniyey</i>) also features Saint George stripes. It was awarded to all the soldiers and officers who participated in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Front_(World_War_II)" title="Eastern Front (World War II)">Eastern Front</a> campaigns, and was the first award to be universally granted to all the veterans, for the most part, right after the end of the war.</p>
<p>The ribbon – along with the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Russia" title="Flag of Russia">Russian tricolored flag</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Navy_Ensign" title="Russian Navy Ensign">Russian Navy Ensign</a> – was also used by the <a href="/wiki/Anti-Sovietism" title="Anti-Sovietism">anti-Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/Russian_Liberation_Army" title="Russian Liberation Army">Russian Liberation Army</a> that fought alongside <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kp5_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-kp5-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2005_Georgievskaya_Lenta_Action">2005 Georgievskaya Lenta Action</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribbon_of_Saint_George&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 2005 Georgievskaya Lenta Action">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2005, the 60th anniversary of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victory_Day_(May_9)" title="Victory Day (May 9)">Victory Day</a>, news agency <a href="/wiki/RIA_Novosti" title="RIA Novosti">RIA Novosti</a> and a youth civic organization launched a campaign that called on volunteers to distribute ribbons in the streets ahead of Victory Day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kp5_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-kp5-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ria_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ria-5">[5]</a></sup> Since then the ribbon is worn by civilians in Russia and other former republics of the Soviet Union as an act of commemoration and remembrance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ria_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-ria-5">[5]</a></sup> For the naming of the ribbons the <a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">diminutive</a> form is used: георгиевская ленточка (<i>georgiyevskaya lentochka</i>, “small George ribbon”).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ria_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-ria-5">[5]</a></sup> Since 2005 the ribbon is distributed every year all over Russia, and around the world in the preparation for 9 May and is on that day widely to be seen on wrists, lapels, and cars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grps2005_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-grps2005-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ria_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-ria-5">[5]</a></sup> The motto that goes with it is "We remember, we are proud!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ria_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-ria-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Yulia_Latynina" title="Yulia Latynina">Yulia Latynina</a> and other journalists have speculated the Russian government introduced the ribbon as a <a href="/wiki/Public_relations" title="Public relations">public relations</a> response to the 2004 <a href="/wiki/Orange_Revolution" title="Orange Revolution">Orange Revolution</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> in which demonstrators had adopted <a href="/wiki/Orange_ribbon" title="Orange ribbon">orange ribbons</a> as their symbol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kp5_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-kp5-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grps2005_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-grps2005-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>During events of 2014 in Ukraine it was used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antimaidan" title="Antimaidan">Antimaidan</a> activists and pro-Russian population of Ukraine (especially in the south-east regions) as a symbol of pro-Russian and separatist sentiment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colUWP_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-colUWP-8">[8]</a></sup> The ribbon is also worn by members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Donbass_People%27s_Militia" title="Donbass People's Militia">Donbass People's Militia</a> paramilitary group. <a href="/wiki/Euromaidan" title="Euromaidan">Euromaidan</a> activists sometimes make derogatory reference to the Ribbon of Saint George as the "Colorado Ribbon", as the colors of the ribbon coincide with the colors of a <a href="/wiki/Colorado_potato_beetle" title="Colorado potato beetle">Colorado potato beetle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colUWP_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-colUWP-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Kropyvnytskyi" title="Kropyvnytskyi">Kirovohrad</a>, a decision was made by veterans to ban the symbol from <a href="/wiki/Victory_Day" title="Victory Day">Victory Day</a> celebrations "in order to prevent provocations between the activists of different movements". Instead, only Ukrainian state symbols would be used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Cherkasy" title="Cherkasy">Cherkasy</a>, authorities urged veterans and supporters not to wear the ribbon or any other party symbols.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Ukrainian government replaced the ribbon with a red-and-black poppy, like those associated with <a href="/wiki/Remembrance_Day" title="Remembrance Day">Remembrance Day</a> in Western Europe in 2014,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kp5_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-kp5-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> red and black also being the colours used by the <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_Insurgent_Army" title="Ukrainian Insurgent Army">Ukrainian Insurgent Army</a> during the conflict.</p>
<p>A female police captain from <a href="/wiki/Kharkiv" title="Kharkiv">Kharkiv</a> was fired "for stupidity" for giving out the ribbon at the police station on 23 February 2015, the day which until 2014 was celebrated (in Kharkiv) as <a href="/wiki/Defender_of_the_Fatherland_Day" title="Defender of the Fatherland Day">Defender of the Homeland Day</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Belarus">Belarus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribbon_of_Saint_George&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Belarus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 5 May 2014, the <a href="/wiki/Belarusian_Republican_Youth_Union" title="Belarusian Republican Youth Union">Belarusian Republican Youth Union</a> encouraged activists not to use the ribbon. Other officials reported that the decision not to use the symbol was related to the situation in Ukraine, "where the ribbon is used by militants and terrorists".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In time for Victory Day 2015, the ribbon's colors were replaced by the red, green and white from the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Belarus" title="Flag of Belarus">Flag of Belarus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> But the President of Belarus Alexander Lukashenko arrived on 7 May 2015 in Moscow with a Saint George ribbon combined with the flag of Belarus placed on the lapel of his jacket, thus showing his personal positive attitude to the ribbon.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribbon_of_Saint_George&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During preparation for the first Victory Day parade in the Canadian city of <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg" title="Winnipeg">Winnipeg</a> on 10 May 2014, the Russian embassy distributed Ribbons of Saint George to participants. The move was considered controversial in view of the ongoing events in Ukraine with the <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_Canadian_Congress" title="Ukrainian Canadian Congress">Ukrainian Canadian Congress</a> calling the ribbon a "symbol of terrorism."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribbon_of_Saint_George&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Kazakhstan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some parties made statements intended to discourage the use of the ribbon in Kazakhstan in 2014 for Victory Day celebrations, suggesting that red (of the Red Army) and turquoise (the national color of Kazakhstan) should be used instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> However, no official authority issued any comments.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latvia">Latvia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribbon_of_Saint_George&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Latvia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The government of Latvia proposed the ban on the use of any Ribbons of Saint George by law on May 2014; earlier, Latvia imposed a ban on the use of all Soviet symbols at public events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lithuania">Lithuania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribbon_of_Saint_George&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lithuania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The ban on similar grounds to that in Latvia has been discussed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Russian_nationalist_and_government_loyalist_symbol">Russian nationalist and government loyalist symbol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribbon_of_Saint_George&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Russian nationalist and government loyalist symbol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ribbon has been adopted by Russian nationalist and government loyalist groups. During the <a href="/wiki/2011%E2%80%9313_Russian_protests" title="2011–13 Russian protests">2011–13 Russian protests</a>, protestors demonstrating against electoral fraud in the <a href="/wiki/Russian_legislative_election,_2011" title="Russian legislative election, 2011">2011 elections</a> carried white ribbons. Supporters of Putin would counter-protest wearing Saint George's ribbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> On 28 April 2016, a group of people from the <a href="/wiki/Nashi_(youth_movement)" title="Nashi (youth movement)">National Freedom Movement</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lenta_280416_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-lenta_280416-21">[21]</a></sup> wearing St. George ribbons attacked a school competition organized by the <a href="/wiki/Memorial_(society)" title="Memorial (society)">Memorial</a> society, pouring toxic solution of <a href="/wiki/Brilliant_Green_(dye)" title="Brilliant Green (dye)">Brilliant Green</a> to writer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ludmila_Ulitskaya" title="Ludmila Ulitskaya">Ludmila Ulitskaya</a> and other guests and assaulting a journalist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The Russian ultranationalist group National Liberation Movement (NOD) has adopted the orange-black flag as its symbol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
