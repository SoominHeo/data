<p><b>Saint George's Day</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Calendar_of_saints" title="Calendar of saints">feast day</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saint_George" title="Saint George">Saint George</a> as celebrated by various <a href="/wiki/Christian_Church" title="Christian Church">Christian Churches</a> and by the several nations, kingdoms, countries, and cities of which Saint George is the <a href="/wiki/Patronages_of_Saint_George" title="Patronages of Saint George">patron saint</a>.</p>
<p>Saint George's Day is celebrated on 23 April, the traditionally accepted date of the saint's death in <a href="/wiki/Diocletianic_Persecution" title="Diocletianic Persecution">Diocletianic Persecution</a> of AD 303. For those <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Churches</a> which use the <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian calendar</a>, this date currently falls on the day of 6 May of the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a>. In Turkic culture the day is known as <a href="/wiki/H%C4%B1d%C4%B1rellez" title="Hıdırellez">Hıdırellez</a> or Xıdır Nəbi and is symbolic of spring renewal.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Date">Date</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_George%27s_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Date">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the General Calendar of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Rite" title="Roman Rite">Roman Rite</a>, the feast of Saint George is on 23 April. In the <a href="/wiki/Tridentine_Calendar" title="Tridentine Calendar">Tridentine Calendar</a> it was given the rank of "Semidouble". In <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XII" title="Pope Pius XII">Pope Pius XII</a>'s <a href="/wiki/General_Roman_Calendar_of_Pope_Pius_XII" title="General Roman Calendar of Pope Pius XII">1955 calendar</a> this rank is reduced to "Simple". In <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XXIII" title="Pope John XXIII">Pope John XXIII</a>'s <a href="/wiki/General_Roman_Calendar_of_1960" title="General Roman Calendar of 1960">1960 calendar</a> the celebration to just a "<a href="/wiki/Commemoration_(prayer)" title="Commemoration (prayer)">Commemoration</a>". In <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_VI" title="Pope Paul VI">Pope Paul VI</a>'s revision of the calendar, that came into force in the <a href="/wiki/Mysterii_Paschalis" title="Mysterii Paschalis">1969</a> it was given the equivalent rank of a "<a href="/wiki/Memorial_(liturgy)" title="Memorial (liturgy)">Memorial</a>", of optional use. In some countries, such as England, the rank is higher.</p>
<p>Since <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a> often falls close to Saint George's Day, the church celebration of the feast may be moved from 23 April: For 2011 and 2014 the <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">Anglican</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a> calendars celebrated Saint George's Day on the first Monday after the <a href="/wiki/Octave_of_Easter" title="Octave of Easter">Octave of Easter</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Holy_Week_in_Valladolid" title="Holy Week in Valladolid">Easter Week</a>) (2 May 2011 and 28 April 2014, respectively).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-churchofengland.org_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-churchofengland.org-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liturgyoffice1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-liturgyoffice1-3">[3]</a></sup> Similarly, the Eastern Orthodox celebration of the feast moves accordingly to the first Monday after Easter or, as it is sometimes called, to the Monday of <a href="/wiki/Bright_Week" title="Bright Week">Bright Week</a>.</p>
<p>Besides the 23 April feast, some Orthodox Churches have additional feasts dedicated to St George. The country of Georgia celebrates the feast of St. George on 23 April and, more prominently, 10 November (Julian calendar), which currently fall on 6 May and 23 November (Gregorian calendar), respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church" title="Russian Orthodox Church">Russian Orthodox Church</a> celebrates the dedication of the Church of St George in <a href="/wiki/Kiev" title="Kiev">Kiev</a> by <a href="/wiki/Yaroslav_the_Wise" title="Yaroslav the Wise">Yaroslav the Wise</a> in 1051 on 26 November (Julian calendar), which currently falls on 9 December on the Gregorian calendar.</p>

<p>The earliest documented mention of St. George in England comes from the venerable <a href="/wiki/Bede" title="Bede">Bede</a> (<a href="/wiki/Circa" title="Circa">c.</a> 673–735).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-5">[5]</a></sup> His feast day is also mentioned in the Durham Collectar, a ninth-century liturgical work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-St._George.27s_Day_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-St._George.27s_Day-6">[6]</a></sup> The will of <a href="/wiki/Alfred_the_Great" title="Alfred the Great">Alfred the Great</a> is said to refer to the saint, in a reference to the church of <a href="/wiki/Fordington,_Dorset" title="Fordington, Dorset">Fordington, Dorset</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-websters-online-dictionarycx_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-websters-online-dictionarycx-7">[7]</a></sup> At Fordington a stone over the south door records the miraculous appearance of St. George to lead crusaders into battle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-5">[5]</a></sup> Early (c. 10th century) dedications of churches to St. George are noted in England, for example at Fordingham, Dorset, at Thetford, Southwark and Doncaster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-websters-online-dictionarycx_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-websters-online-dictionarycx-7">[7]</a></sup> In the past, historians mistakenly pointed to the Synod of Oxford in 1222 as elevating the feast to special prominence, but the earliest manuscripts of the synod’s declaration do not mention the feast of St George.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The declarations of the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Canterbury" title="Province of Canterbury">Province of Canterbury</a> in 1415 and the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_York" title="Province of York">Province of York</a> in 1421 elevated the feast to a double major, and as a result, work was prohibited and <a href="/wiki/Church_attendance" title="Church attendance">church attendance</a> was mandatory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Edward III (1327–1377) put his <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Garter" title="Order of the Garter">Order of the Garter</a> (founded c. 1348) under the banner of St. George.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-5">[5]</a></sup> This order is still the foremost order of knighthood in England and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_George%27s_Chapel" title="St George's Chapel">St George's Chapel</a> at <a href="/wiki/Windsor_Castle" title="Windsor Castle">Windsor Castle</a> was built by Edward IV and Henry VII in honour of the order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-5">[5]</a></sup> The badge of the Order shows Saint George on horseback slaying the dragon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-5">[5]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Froissart" title="Froissart">Froissart</a> observed the English invoking St. George as a battle cry on several occasions during the Hundred Years' War (1337–1453).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-websters-online-dictionarycx_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-websters-online-dictionarycx-7">[7]</a></sup> Certain English soldiers also displayed the <a href="/wiki/Pennon" title="Pennon">pennon</a> of St George.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In his play <i><a href="/wiki/Henry_V_(play)" title="Henry V (play)">Henry V</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> famously invokes the Saint at <a href="/wiki/Harfleur" title="Harfleur">Harfleur</a> prior to the battle of <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Agincourt" title="Battle of Agincourt">Agincourt</a> (1415): "Follow your spirit, and upon this charge Cry 'God for Harry, England, and Saint George!'" At Agincourt many believed they saw him fighting on the English side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>St George's Day was a major feast and national holiday in England on a par with Christmas from the early 15th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The Cross of St. George was flown in 1497 by <a href="/wiki/John_Cabot" title="John Cabot">John Cabot</a> on his voyage to discover <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a> and later by Sir <a href="/wiki/Francis_Drake" title="Francis Drake">Francis Drake</a> and Sir <a href="/wiki/Walter_Raleigh" title="Walter Raleigh">Walter Raleigh</a>. In 1620 it was the flag that was flown on the foremast of the <a href="/wiki/Mayflower" title="Mayflower">Mayflower</a> (with the early Union Flag combining St. George's Cross of England with St. Andrew's Saltire of Scotland on the mainmast) when the Pilgrim Fathers arrived in Plymouth, Massachusetts.</p>
<p>The tradition of celebration St George's day had waned by the end of the 18th century after the union of England and Scotland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Nevertheless, the link with St. George continues today, for example <a href="/wiki/Salisbury" title="Salisbury">Salisbury</a> holds an annual St. George's Day pageant, the origins of which are believed to go back to the 13th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-websters-online-dictionarycx_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-websters-online-dictionarycx-7">[7]</a></sup> In recent years the popularity of St. George's Day appears to be increasing gradually. <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_3" title="BBC Radio 3">BBC Radio 3</a> had a full programme of St. George's Day events in 2006, and <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Rosindell" title="Andrew Rosindell">Andrew Rosindell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a> for <a href="/wiki/Romford_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Romford (UK Parliament constituency)">Romford</a>, has been putting the argument forward in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a> to make St. George's Day a public holiday. In early 2009, Mayor of London <a href="/wiki/Boris_Johnson" title="Boris Johnson">Boris Johnson</a> spearheaded a campaign to encourage the celebration of St. George's Day. Today, St. George's day may be celebrated with anything English including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morris_dancing" title="Morris dancing">morris dancing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Punch_and_Judy" title="Punch and Judy">Punch and Judy</a> shows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Additional celebrations may involve the commemoration of 23 April as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>'s birthday/death.</p>
<p>A traditional custom on St George's day is to wear a red <a href="/wiki/Rose" title="Rose">rose</a> in one's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacket_lapel" title="Jacket lapel">lapel</a>, though this is no longer widely practised. Another custom is to fly or adorn the <a href="/wiki/St_George%27s_Cross" title="St George's Cross">St George's Cross</a> <a href="/wiki/Flag" title="Flag">flag</a> in some way: <a href="/wiki/Pub" title="Pub">pubs</a> in particular can be seen on 23 April festooned with garlands of St George's crosses. It is customary for the hymn "<a href="/wiki/And_did_those_feet_in_ancient_time" title="And did those feet in ancient time">Jerusalem</a>" to be sung in cathedrals, churches and chapels on St George's Day, or on the Sunday closest to it. <a href="/wiki/English_cuisine" title="English cuisine">Traditional English food and drink</a> may be consumed.</p>
<p>There is a growing reaction to the recent indifference to St George's Day. Organisations such as <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_St_George" title="Royal Society of St George">Royal Society of St George</a> have been encouraging celebrations. There have also been calls to replace St. George as patron saint of England on the grounds that he was an obscure figure who had no direct connection with the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> However, there is no obvious consensus as to whom to replace him with, though names suggested include <a href="/wiki/Edmund_the_Martyr" title="Edmund the Martyr">Edmund the Martyr</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuthbert_of_Lindisfarne" title="Cuthbert of Lindisfarne">Cuthbert of Lindisfarne</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Saint_Alban" title="Saint Alban">Saint Alban</a>, with the last having topped a <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">BBC Radio 4</a> poll on the subject.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Recently there have been calls to reinstate St Edmund as the patron Saint of England as he was displaced by George some 400 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Religious observance of St George's day changes when it is too close to Easter. According to the Church of England's calendar, when St. George's Day falls between Palm Sunday and the Second Sunday of Easter inclusive, it is moved to the Monday after the Second Sunday of Easter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-churchofengland.org_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-churchofengland.org-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2011, for example, 23 April was Holy Saturday, so St George's Day was moved to Monday 2 May. The Catholic Church in <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a> has a similar practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liturgyoffice1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-liturgyoffice1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Scouting" title="Scouting">The Scout movement</a> has been celebrating St George's Day on 23 April since its first years, and St George is the patron saint of many other organisations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>St. George's Day is celebrated on 23 April. St. George's Day is not an official national holiday in Canada. It is, however, a provincial holiday in <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a>, where it is usually observed on the Monday nearest 23 April.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_Catholic_traditions">Roman Catholic traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_George%27s_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Roman Catholic traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_Europe">Central Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_George%27s_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Central Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the Czech Republic, Saint George's Day (<i>svátek sv. Jiří</i>) comes on 24 April. The reason why it was moved from 23 April is, because there is a day of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Adalbert_of_Prague" title="St. Adalbert of Prague">St. Adalbert of Prague</a> (in Czech Svatý Vojtěch), Czech national patron saint, who was martyred on 23 April 997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> It is celebrated in a special way.</p>
<p>In Hungary, 24 April is the day of Saint George the Dragonkiller, thus it is the <a href="/wiki/Name_day" title="Name day">name day</a> of men named György. It is also the Day of the Police, who honour him as a <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saint</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Iberian_peninsula">Iberian peninsula</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_George%27s_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Iberian peninsula">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Saint_George%27s_Day_(Spain)" title="Saint George's Day (Spain)">Saint George's Day (Spain)</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diada_de_Sant_Jordi" title="Diada de Sant Jordi">Diada de Sant Jordi</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Saint_George" title="Saint George">Saint George</a> is the patron saint of the former <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">Crown of Aragon</a>, since King <a href="/wiki/Peter_I_of_Aragon_and_Navarre" title="Peter I of Aragon and Navarre">Peter I of Aragon</a> won the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Alcoraz" title="Battle of Alcoraz">Battle of Alcoraz</a> with his patronage. He is also patron of several cities. In most cases, the reason for those cities' relation with the Saint as their holy Patron is linked to historic events which happened during the "<a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a>."</p>
<p>As in the rest of the historical territory of the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">Crown of Aragon</a>, the Feast of St George is celebrated enthusiastically in the Community of <a href="/wiki/Aragon" title="Aragon">Aragon</a>, being the country's patron saint and its national day. On 23 April, Aragon celebrates its "Día de Aragón" (Day of Aragon) in commemoration of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Alcoraz" title="Battle of Alcoraz">Battle of Alcoraz</a> (<i>Baralla d'Alcoraz</i> in Aragonese), on which Huesca was conquered by the Aragonese army and in which tradition says that St George appeared at a critical moment for the Christian Army.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">Catalonia</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diada_de_Sant_Jordi" title="Diada de Sant Jordi">Diada de Sant Jordi</a></i> is also known as <i>El Dia de la Rosa</i> (The Day of the Rose) or <i>El Dia del Llibre</i> (The Day of the Book), involving traditions similar to those of the Anglo-Saxon <a href="/wiki/Valentine%27s_Day" title="Valentine's Day">Valentine's Day</a>.</p>
<p>One notable celebration is in the <a href="/wiki/Valencia" title="Valencia">Valencian</a> city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoi" title="Alcoi">Alcoi</a>. There, Saint George's Day is commemorated as a thanksgiving celebration for the proclaimed aid the Saint provided to the Christian troops fighting the Muslims in the siege of the city. Its citizens commemorate the day with a festivity in which thousands of people parade in medieval costumes, forming two "armies" of <a href="/wiki/Moros_y_cristianos" title="Moros y cristianos">Moors and Christians</a> and re-enacting the siege that gave the city to the Christians.</p>
<p>Devotions to Saint George in Portugal date back to the twelfth century, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Constable" title="Saint Constable">Saint Constable</a> attributed the victory of the Portuguese against what is now mostly modern day Spain, in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Aljubarrota" title="Battle of Aljubarrota">battle of Aljubarrota</a> in the fourteenth century to Saint George. During the reign of King <a href="/wiki/John_I_of_Portugal" title="John I of Portugal">John I</a> (1357–1433) Saint George became the patron saint of Portugal and the King ordered that the saint's image on the horse be carried in the <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Christi_(feast)" title="Corpus Christi (feast)">Corpus Christi</a> procession. In fact, the Portuguese Army motto means Portugal and Saint George, in perils and in efforts of war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_Orthodox_tradition">Eastern Orthodox tradition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_George%27s_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Eastern Orthodox tradition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Under the <a href="/wiki/State_atheism" title="State atheism">state atheism</a> of former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treatment_of_Christians_in_Communist_Bloc_countries" title="Treatment of Christians in Communist Bloc countries">Eastern Bloc</a> countries, the celebration of Saint George's Day was historically suppressed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church" title="Russian Orthodox Church">Russian Orthodox Church</a>, which uses the <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian calendar</a>, has two important feasts of Saint George. Besides the feast of 23 April (6 May in the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a>), common through all Christendom, Russians also celebrate the anniversary of the dedication of the Church of St George in <a href="/wiki/Kiev" title="Kiev">Kiev</a> by <a href="/wiki/Yaroslav_the_Wise" title="Yaroslav the Wise">Yaroslav the Wise</a> (1051) on 26 November (Julian Calendar), which currently falls on 9 December. One of the Russian forms of the name George being <i>Yuri</i>, the two feasts are popularly known as <i>Vesenniy Yuriev Den</i> (Yuri's Day in the Spring) and <i><a href="/wiki/George%27s_Day_in_Autumn" title="George's Day in Autumn">Osenniy Yuriev Den</a></i> (Yuri's Day in the Fall).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Slavic_tradition_and_Balkanese_spring_festival">South Slavic tradition and Balkanese spring festival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_George%27s_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: South Slavic tradition and Balkanese spring festival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/%C4%90ur%C4%91evdan" title="Đurđevdan">Đurđevdan</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/H%C4%B1d%C4%B1rellez" title="Hıdırellez">Hıdırellez</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">Serbian</a>, St George's Day is called <i>Đurđevdan</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serbian_Cyrillic" title="Serbian Cyrillic">Cyrillic</a>: Ђурђевдан) and is celebrated on 6 May every year, as the <a href="/wiki/Serbian_Orthodox_Church" title="Serbian Orthodox Church">Serbian Orthodox Church</a> uses the Julian, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Style_calendar" title="Old Style calendar">Old Style calendar</a>. St George's Day is one of the most common <a href="/wiki/Slava" title="Slava">Slavas</a> (family patron day) among the <a href="/wiki/Serbs" title="Serbs">Serbs</a>. Đurđevdan is also celebrated by both Orthodox and Muslim <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Romani</a> and Muslim <a href="/wiki/Gorani_people" title="Gorani people">Gorani</a>. Đurđevdan is celebrated, especially, in the areas of <a href="/wiki/Ra%C5%A1ka_District" title="Raška District">Raška</a> in Serbia. Apart from being the Slava of many families, St George's Day is marked by morning picnics, music, and folk dances.</p>

</div>
<p>Possibly the most celebrated <a href="/wiki/Name_days_in_Bulgaria" title="Name days in Bulgaria">name day</a> in the country, St George's Day (Гергьовден, <i>Gergyovden</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_Bulgaria" title="Public holidays in Bulgaria">public holiday</a> that takes place on 6 May each year. A common ritual is to prepare and eat a whole <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lamb_(food)" title="Lamb (food)">lamb</a>, which is an ancient practice possibly related to <a href="/wiki/Slavic_mythology" title="Slavic mythology">Slavic pagan sacrificial traditions</a> and the fact that St George is the <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saint</a> of shepherds. It is also believed to be a magical day when all evil spells can be broken. It was believed that the saint helps the crops to grow and blesses the morning dew, so early in the morning they walked in the pastures and meadows and collected dew, washed their face, hands and feet in it for good luck and even in some rural parts of Bulgaria it was a custom to roll in it naked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>St George's Day is also <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Armed_Forces" title="Bulgarian Armed Forces">Bulgarian Armed Forces</a> Day, made official with a decree of Prince <a href="/wiki/Alexander_of_Battenberg" title="Alexander of Battenberg">Alexander of Battenberg</a> on 9 January 1880. Parades are organised in the capital <a href="/wiki/Sofia" title="Sofia">Sofia</a> to present the best of the equipment and manpower of the Bulgarian military, as well as in major cities nationwide.</p>
<p>St George's Day is also called <i>Đurđevdan</i> and is celebrated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bosnian_Serbs" title="Bosnian Serbs">Bosnian Serbs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Romani</a> (both Orthodox and Muslim), but also has been celebrated by the other ethnic groups in Bosnia and Herzegovina. Đurđevdan's widespread appeal can be seen in the folk song <a href="/wiki/Ederlezi_(song)" title="Ederlezi (song)">Đurđevdan</a> popularised by <a href="/wiki/Bijelo_Dugme" title="Bijelo Dugme">Bijelo Dugme</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Me%C5%A1a_Selimovi%C4%87" title="Meša Selimović">Meša Selimović</a>'s novel <i>Death and the Dervish</i>.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Church" title="Greek Orthodox Church">Greek Orthodox Church</a>, Saint George's Day is celebrated on 23 April, unless this is Easter Sunday (when it is celebrated on the first Monday after Easter Sunday).</p>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georgian_Church" title="Georgian Church">Georgian Church</a>, St. George the Victory-Bearer is commemorated twice a year: on November 23 (the Breaking on the Wheel of Holy Greatmartyr George) and May 6 (the Beheading of St. George).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Romanian_Orthodox_Church" title="Romanian Orthodox Church">Romanian Orthodox Church</a>, which uses the <a href="/wiki/Revised_Julian_calendar" title="Revised Julian calendar">Revised Julian calendar</a>, celebrates St. George's Day on 23 April.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_East">Middle East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_George%27s_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Middle East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Feast_of_Saint_George_(Palestine)" title="Feast of Saint George (Palestine)">Feast of Saint George (Palestine)</a></div>
<p>Veneration of St George as a martyr originates in the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a>, spreading spread from <a href="/wiki/Palestine_(region)" title="Palestine (region)">Palestine</a> through Lebanon to the rest of the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a> – though the martyr is not mentioned in the Syriac <i><a href="/wiki/Horologion" title="Horologion">Breviarium</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Butler_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Butler-24">[24]</a></sup> – and <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a> in the 4th to 5th centuries. In Georgia, the feast day on 23 November is credited to <a href="/wiki/Saint_Nino" title="Saint Nino">Saint Nino</a> of Cappadocia, who in Georgian hagiography is a relative of St George, credited with bringing Christianity to the Georgians in the fourth century. A <a href="/wiki/Titular_church" title="Titular church">titular church</a> built in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lydda" title="Lydda">Lydda</a> during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Constantine_the_Great" title="Constantine the Great">Constantine the Great</a> (reigned 306–37) was consecrated to "a man of the highest distinction"; the identity of this man with St George was asserted by the 7th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The church was destroyed by Muslims in 1010, but was later rebuilt and dedicated to Saint George by the <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusaders</a>. In 1191 and during the conflict known as the <a href="/wiki/Third_Crusade" title="Third Crusade">Third Crusade</a> (1189–92), the church was again destroyed by the forces of <a href="/wiki/Saladin" title="Saladin">Saladin</a>, Sultan of the <a href="/wiki/Ayyubid_dynasty" title="Ayyubid dynasty">Ayyubid dynasty</a> (reigned 1171–93). A new church was erected in 1872 and is still standing.</p>
<p>Christians in the Middle East continue to celebrate St George's Day, and the custom has been adopted in Muslim tradition via identification of the saint with the figure of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Khidr" title="Al-Khidr">Al-Khidr</a> and an association in <a href="/wiki/Folk_belief" title="Folk belief">folk belief</a> with medicine and healing. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palestinian_culture" title="Palestinian culture">Palestinian culture</a>, the feast is held on 5 May. The feast is held in the Palestinian town of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Khader,_Bethlehem" title="Al-Khader, Bethlehem">al-Khader</a>, just south of <a href="/wiki/Bethlehem" title="Bethlehem">Bethlehem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCHP_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-CCHP-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Palestinian folklore suggests that the feast of <i><a href="/wiki/Feast_of_Saint_George_(Palestine)" title="Feast of Saint George (Palestine)">al-Khader</a></i> originated during the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> rule of Palestine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historically, the feast attracted Arabs from throughout <a href="/wiki/Palestine_(region)" title="Palestine (region)">Palestine</a> to visit the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monastery_of_Saint_George,_al-Khader" title="Monastery of Saint George, al-Khader">Monastery of Saint George</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Al_Khader_VIllage_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Al_Khader_VIllage-28">[28]</a></sup> On the morning of 6 May, <a href="/wiki/Palestinian_Christians" title="Palestinian Christians">Palestinian Christians</a> from <a href="/wiki/Beit_Jala" title="Beit Jala">Beit Jala</a>, Bethlehem, <a href="/wiki/Beit_Sahour" title="Beit Sahour">Beit Sahour</a> and other parts of <a href="/wiki/Palestine_(region)" title="Palestine (region)">Palestine</a> would march in a procession to the monastery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCHP_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-CCHP-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mosul" title="Mosul">Mosul</a>, northern Iraq, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Churches_and_monasteries_of_Mosul#St._George.27s_Monastery" title="Churches and monasteries of Mosul">St. George Monastery</a> church was destroyed in November 2014 by ISIS militants.</p>
<p>Saint George's Day (<i>Jeries</i>) is celebrated widely in <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>, especially in a town near Amman called <a href="/wiki/Fuheis" title="Fuheis">Fuheis</a>. In Jordan, many churches are dedicated to St. George.</p>
<p>St George's Day is celebrated throughout <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, but especially in towns and villages where churches for St George have been erected.</p>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_Syria" title="Christianity in Syria">Christian denominations in Syria</a> celebrate St George's Day, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Homs" title="Homs">Homs</a> Governorate. They do this by dressing small children as dragons and chasing them through the streets whilst beating them with clubs and batons. It is a very special time of year, after the beatings folks will enjoy a sit down dinner and dancing. The monastery of Mar Jurjus <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.cometosyria.com/en/pages/St.+George+Monastery+syria/52/38" rel="nofollow">[2]</a> (St. George) dates back to the 6th century and is a regional center of Orthodox Christianity.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_literature">In literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_George%27s_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the book <i><a href="/wiki/Dracula" title="Dracula">Dracula</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Bram_Stoker" title="Bram Stoker">Bram Stoker</a>, evil things are said to occur on St. George's Day, beginning at midnight. The date of St. George's Day presented in the book, 5 May (on the Western <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a>), is St. George's Day as observed by the Eastern Orthodox churches of that era.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>(Excerpt from <i><a href="/wiki/Dracula" title="Dracula">Dracula</a></i>, 1897) "Do you know what day it is?" I answered that it was the fourth of May. She shook her head as she said again: "Oh, yes! I know that, I know that! but do you know what day it is?" On my saying that I did not understand, she went on: "It is the eve of St. George's Day. Do you not know that tonight, when the clock strikes midnight, all the evil things in the world will have full sway?"</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The play <i><a href="/wiki/Jerusalem_(play)" title="Jerusalem (play)">Jerusalem</a></i> (2009) by <a href="/wiki/Jez_Butterworth" title="Jez Butterworth">Jez Butterworth</a> takes place on St George's Day, 23 April, also the day of death and estimated birth day of <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>.</p>
<p>Additionally, the play <i><a href="/wiki/Andorra_(play)" title="Andorra (play)">Andorra</a></i> (1961) by <a href="/wiki/Max_Frisch" title="Max Frisch">Max Frisch</a> focusses greatly on the (fictionalised) Andorran celebrations of St. George's Day. The play begins and ends with references to a ceremonial whitewashing of houses by the town's virgins, again reflecting the day's central theme of purity.</p>
