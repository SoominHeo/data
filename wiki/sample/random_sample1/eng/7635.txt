<p><b>St. Florian's Cathedral</b>, more formally known as the <b>Cathedral of St. Michael the Archangel and St. Florian the Martyr</b> (<a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>: <span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl"><i>Katedra Świętego Michała Archanioła i Świętego Floriana</i></span>), is a Catholic church and historic landmark at ul. Floriańska 3 (English: 3 St. Florian's Street) in eastern <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a>. The 75-meter towers of St. Florian’s Cathedral dominate eastern Warsaw's <a href="/wiki/Praga" title="Praga">Praga</a> district and highlight the cathedral’s role as a form of protest against the erstwhile <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian</a> domination of <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-church_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-church-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Florian%27s_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There has been a Catholic church presence in or around the site of the future church since 1583, but the impetus for creating a lasting and substantial church did not arrive until the late 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-church_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-church-1">[1]</a></sup> The map of Europe was redrawn during the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Congress of Vienna</a> and the resulting territorial maneuvers placed the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Warsaw" title="Duchy of Warsaw">Duchy of Warsaw</a> under the control of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a>, transforming it into the <a href="/wiki/Congress_Poland" title="Congress Poland">Congress Poland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicolson_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nicolson-2">[2]</a></sup> Among other intrusions, over twenty <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox" title="Russian Orthodox">Russian Orthodox</a> churches were built in <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> To protest against the perceived imposition of a foreign church, and in direct reaction to the monumental Orthodox <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_St._Mary_Magdalene,_Warsaw" title="Cathedral of St. Mary Magdalene, Warsaw">Church of Mary Magdalene</a> built down the street, St Florian’s was built with two commanding 75-meter (250-foot) towers between 1897-1904.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-church_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-church-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The church is named after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Florian" title="St. Florian">St. Florian</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saint</a> of professions associated with fire, such as firefighters, steelworkers, <a href="/wiki/Chimney_sweep" title="Chimney sweep">chimney sweeps</a>, potters and bakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-church_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-church-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II_destruction">World War II destruction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Florian%27s_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: World War II destruction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During and after the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Warsaw_(1939)" title="Siege of Warsaw (1939)">Siege of Warsaw</a>, churches were used as a hiding place for Jews, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armia_Warszawa" title="Armia Warszawa">Warsaw Army</a> (<i>Armia Warszawa</i>) and as a general refuge for civilians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-giergon_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-giergon-5">[5]</a></sup> St. Florian's was destroyed by the Germans as they withdrew from Poland in 1944 after the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Uprising" title="Warsaw Uprising">Warsaw Uprising</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The church remained in ruins for several years, but by the 1950s a reconstruction effort slowly began with support from <a href="/wiki/Praga" title="Praga">Praga</a> residents. The rebuilt church was reopened in 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-church_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-church-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_church_hierarchy">Role in church hierarchy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Florian%27s_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Role in church hierarchy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>St Florian's is the cathedral church of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Warszawa-Praga" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Warszawa-Praga">Roman Catholic Diocese of Warszawa-Praga</a> and by order of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> was raised to the rank of <a href="/wiki/Minor_basilica" title="Minor basilica">minor basilica</a> in 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-church_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-church-1">[1]</a></sup> Over four hundred priests form the ministry in this diocese covering 1,274 square miles, divided into 160 parishes and serving approximately one million Polish Catholics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_features">Notable features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Florian%27s_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Notable features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>St Florian's is built in a <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival_architecture" title="Gothic Revival architecture">Gothic Revival</a> style distinguished by two twenty-story towers facing <a class="extiw" href="https://pl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aleja_%22Solidarno%C5%9Bci%22_w_Warszawie" title='pl:Aleja "Solidarności" w Warszawie'>Al. Solidarności</a> (<a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>: <i>Solidarity Avenue</i>), capped with bronze spires. Most of its exterior is made of red brick. Over the entrances are mosaic depictions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" title="Jesus Christ">Jesus Christ</a> and the emblem of the first bishop of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Warszawa-Praga" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Warszawa-Praga">Warsaw-Praga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kazimierz_Romaniuk" title="Kazimierz Romaniuk">Kazimierz Romaniuk</a>, while the interior is decorated in red or white plaster and brick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-church_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-church-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
