<p>The Christian holiday of <b>Pentecost</b> (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">AncientÂ Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Î ÎµÎ½ÏÎ·ÎºÎ¿ÏÏÎ® [á¼¡Î¼Î­ÏÎ±]</span>, <i>PentÄkostÄ [hÄmera]</i>, "the fiftieth [day]") is celebrated 50 days from <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a> Sunday, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclusive_counting" title="Inclusive counting">counting inclusive</a> of Easter Sunday itself, i. e. 49 days or 7 weeks <i>after</i> Easter Sunday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Therefore it always occurs on a Sunday. It is also the tenth day after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascension_Thursday" title="Ascension Thursday">Ascension Thursday</a>, which itself is 40 days <i>from</i> Easter, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclusive_counting" title="Inclusive counting">counting inclusive</a> of Easter Sunday itself.</p>
<p>The Christian Pentecost is based on the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, where it refers to the descent of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Spirit" title="Holy Spirit">Holy Spirit</a> upon the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apostle_(Christian)" title="Apostle (Christian)">Apostles</a> and other followers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" title="Jesus Christ">Jesus Christ</a>, as described in the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">Acts of the Apostles</a> <a class="external text" href="http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Acts+2:1â31:1&amp;version=nrsv" rel="nofollow">2:1â31</a>. According to Luke 22:12â13, the descent of the Holy Spirit occurred while the Apostles were celebrating the Jewish day of <a href="/wiki/Shavuot" title="Shavuot">Shavuot</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="he" xml:lang="he">×©×××¢××ª</span>ââ, lit. "Weeks"), the Feast of Weeks, a prominent feast in the calendar of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Israel" title="Ancient Israel">ancient Israel</a> celebrating the <a href="/wiki/Law_given_to_Moses_at_Sinai" title="Law given to Moses at Sinai">giving of the Law to Moses at Sinai</a>. Subsequently, "Pentecost" may refer to the Pentecost of the New Testament and <a href="/wiki/Shavuot" title="Shavuot">Shavuot</a> of the Old Testament. Shavuot is a significant event shared by Jews and Christians but Christians do not commonly celebrate it as a separate holiday.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> <a href="/wiki/Liturgical_year" title="Liturgical year">liturgical year</a> it became a feast commemorating what is described by some Christians as the "Birthday of the Church".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The holy day is also called "White Sunday" or "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whitsunday" title="Whitsunday">Whitsunday</a>", especially in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, where traditionally the next day, <a href="/wiki/Whit_Monday" title="Whit Monday">Whit Monday</a>, was also a public holiday. In <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Christianity" title="Eastern Christianity">Eastern Christianity</a>, Pentecost can also refer to the entire fifty days of <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a> through Pentecost inclusive; hence the book containing the liturgical texts for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paschaltide" title="Paschaltide">Paschaltide</a> is called the "<i><a href="/wiki/Pentecostarion" title="Pentecostarion">Pentecostarion</a></i>".</p>
<p>In Germany Pentecost is denominated "<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pfingsten" title="de:Pfingsten">Pfingsten</a>" and often coincides with scholastic holidays and the beginning of many outdoor and Spring activities, such as festivals and organized outdoor activities by youth organizations. The Monday after Pentecost is a legal holiday in many European nations.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_Testament">Old Testament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pentecost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Old Testament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Shavuot" title="Shavuot">Shavuot</a></div>
<p>Pentecost is the old Greek and Latin name for the Jewish Festival of Weeks (Hebrew ×× ××©×××¢××ª <i>Chag ha-<a href="/wiki/Shavuot" title="Shavuot">Shavuot</a></i>) which can be found in the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a>. It is called by that name in Exodus 34:22 and Deuteronomy 16:10. It is also called the Festival of Reaping (Hebrew: ×× ××§×¦××¨, Chag ha-Katsir) in Exodus 23:16, and Day of the First Fruits (Hebrew ××× ××××××¨××, Yom ha-Bikkurim) in Numbers 28:26.</p>
<p>Jews traditionally read the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Ruth" title="Book of Ruth">Book of Ruth</a> at Pentecost,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> as the story links with the grain harvest theme of the festival.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extra-Biblical_and_post-Biblical_Jewish_texts">Extra-Biblical and post-Biblical Jewish texts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pentecost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Extra-Biblical and post-Biblical Jewish texts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">Talmud</a> refers to Shavuot as <i>Atzeret</i> (Hebrew: ×¢×¦×¨×ª, literally, "refraining" or "holding back"), referring to the prohibition against work on this holiday and to the conclusion of the holiday and season of <a href="/wiki/Passover" title="Passover">Passover</a>. Since Shavuot occurs 49 days after the first day of Passover (i.e. the 50th day, including Passover itself), Hellenistic Jews gave it the name Pentecost (ÏÎµÎ½ÏÎ·ÎºÎ¿ÏÏÎ®, "fiftieth day").<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>According to Jewish tradition, Pentecost commemorates God's giving of the <a href="/wiki/Ten_Commandments" title="Ten Commandments">Ten Commandments</a> at <a href="/wiki/Mount_Sinai" title="Mount Sinai">Mount Sinai</a>, 49 days after <a href="/wiki/The_Exodus" title="The Exodus">the Exodus</a>. The Talmud derives this from a calculation based on Biblical texts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is a Jewish tradition that <a href="/wiki/David" title="David">King David</a> was born and died at Pentecost. In the Apostle <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Peter's</a> first sermon, recorded in Acts 2:14â39, he linked the life, death and <a href="/wiki/Ascension_of_Jesus" title="Ascension of Jesus">Ascension of Jesus</a> to King David's death, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tomb_of_David" title="Tomb of David">burial</a> and hope of <a href="/wiki/Immortality" title="Immortality">immortality</a>.</p>

<p>The biblical narrative of Pentecost is given in the second chapter of the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">Book of Acts</a>. Present were about one hundred and twenty followers of Christ (Acts 1:15), including the Twelve Apostles (i.e. the Eleven faithful disciples and <a href="/wiki/Matthias" title="Matthias">Matthias</a> who was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judas" title="Judas">Judas</a>' replacement) (Acts 1:13, 26), his mother Mary, various other women disciples and his brothers (Acts 1:14).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Their reception of the Holy Spirit in the Upper Room is recounted in Acts 2:1â6:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>And when the day of Pentecost was fully come, they were all with one accord in one place. And suddenly there came a sound from heaven as of a rushing mighty wind, and it filled all the house where they were sitting. And there appeared unto them cloven tongues like as of fire, and it sat upon each of them. And they were all filled with the Holy Spirit, and began to speak with other languages, as the Spirit gave them utterance. And there were dwelling at Jerusalem Jews, devout men, out of every nation under heaven. Now when this was noised abroad, the multitude came together, and were confounded, because that every man heard them speak in his own language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>While those on whom the Spirit had descended were speaking in many languages, the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Apostle Peter</a> stood up with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apostle_(Christian)" title="Apostle (Christian)">eleven</a> and proclaimed to the crowd that this event was the fulfillment of the prophecy ("I will pour out my spirit").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles#Peter_and_the_apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">Acts 2:17</a>, it reads: "'And in the last days,' God says, 'I will pour out my spirit upon every sort of flesh, and your sons and your daughters will prophesy and your young men will see visions and your old men will dream dreams." He also mentions (2:15) that it was the third hour of the day (about 9:00 am). Acts 2:41 then reports: "Then they that gladly received his word were baptized: and the same day there were added unto them about three thousand souls."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Peter stated that this event was the beginning of a continual outpouring that would be available to all believers from that point on, Jews and Gentiles alike.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Location_of_the_first_Pentecost">Location of the first Pentecost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pentecost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Location of the first Pentecost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jerusalem_Cenacle_BW_5.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3534" data-file-width="2358" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Jerusalem_Cenacle_BW_5.JPG/220px-Jerusalem_Cenacle_BW_5.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Jerusalem_Cenacle_BW_5.JPG/330px-Jerusalem_Cenacle_BW_5.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Jerusalem_Cenacle_BW_5.JPG/440px-Jerusalem_Cenacle_BW_5.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jerusalem_Cenacle_BW_5.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Cenacle" title="Cenacle">Cenacle</a> on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Zion" title="Mount Zion">Mount Zion</a>, claimed to be the location of the <a href="/wiki/Last_Supper" title="Last Supper">Last Supper</a> and Pentecost. <a href="/wiki/Bargil_Pixner" title="Bargil Pixner">Bargil Pixner</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pixner_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pixner-11">[11]</a></sup> claims the original <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Zion,_Jerusalem" title="Church of Zion, Jerusalem">Church of the Apostles</a> is located under the current structure.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Traditional interpretation holds that the Descent of the Holy Spirit took place in the Upper Room, or <a href="/wiki/Cenacle" title="Cenacle">Cenacle</a>, while celebrating the day of Pentecost (<a href="/wiki/Shavuot" title="Shavuot">Shavuot</a>). The Upper Room was first mentioned in Luke 22:12â13 ( "<i>And he shall shew you a large upper room furnished: there make ready. And they went, and found as he had said unto them: and they made ready the <a href="/wiki/Passover" title="Passover">passover</a></i>.").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This Upper Room was to be the location of the <a href="/wiki/Last_Supper" title="Last Supper">Last Supper</a> and the institution of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Communion" title="Holy Communion">Holy Communion</a>. The next mention of an Upper Room is in Acts 1:13â14, the continuation of the Luke narrative by the same writer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biblegateway1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-biblegateway1-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Here the <a href="/wiki/Disciple_(Christianity)" title="Disciple (Christianity)">disciples</a> and women wait and they gave themselves up to constant prayer: <i>"And when they were come in, they went up into an upper room, where abode both Peter, and James, and John, and Andrew, Philip, and Thomas, Bartholomew, and Matthew, James the son of Alphaeus, and Simon Zelotes, and Judas the brother of James. These all continued with one accord in prayer and supplication, with the women, and Mary the mother of Jesus, and with his brethren."</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biblegateway1_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-biblegateway1-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Then, in Acts 2:1â2, "<i>And when the day of Pentecost was fully come, they were all with one accord in one place. And suddenly there came a sound from heaven as of a rushing mighty wind, and it filled all the house where they were sitting.</i>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> "They" refers to the aforementioned disciples, and it includes the women. The "place" referring to the same Upper Room where these persons had "<i>continued with one accord in prayer and supplication</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Date">Date</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pentecost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Date">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the current Jewish Calendar, the date of Pentecost is fifty days from <a href="/wiki/Passover" title="Passover">Passover</a>. In Jewish antiquity dates were disputed, as in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dead_Sea_scrolls" title="Dead Sea scrolls">Dead Sea scrolls</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and in the <a href="/wiki/Mishnah" title="Mishnah">Mishnah</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Christian tradition Pentecost is part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paschal_Cycle" title="Paschal Cycle">moveable cycle</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_year" title="Ecclesiastical year">ecclesiastical year</a>: Pentecost is always seven weeks after <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a> Day: that is to say, 50 days after Easter (inclusive of Easter Day). In other words, it falls on the seventh Sunday after Easter Day. The date of Easter may be calculated using a procedure known as <a href="/wiki/Computus" title="Computus">Computus</a>.</p>
<p>Since the date of Easter is calculated differently in the East and West (see <a href="/wiki/Easter_controversy" title="Easter controversy">Easter controversy</a>), in most years the two traditions celebrate Pentecost on different days (though in some years the celebrations will coincide, as in 2010, 2011, and 2014). In the West, the earliest possible date is May 10 (as in 1818), and the latest possible date is June 13 (as in 1943 and 2038). In the 20th and 21st centuries, these two dates correspond to May 23 and June 26 in the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Liturgical_celebration">Liturgical celebration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pentecost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Liturgical celebration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_churches">Eastern churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pentecost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Eastern churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a>, Pentecost is one of the Orthodox <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Feasts" title="Great Feasts">Great Feasts</a> and is considered to be the highest ranking Great Feast of the Lord, second in rank only to <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Pascha</a>. The service is celebrated with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/All-night_Vigil" title="All-night Vigil">All-night Vigil</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Eve" title="Eve">eve</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feast_day" title="Feast day">feast day</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Divine_Liturgy" title="Divine Liturgy">Divine Liturgy</a> on the day of the feast itself. <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_church_architecture" title="Eastern Orthodox church architecture">Orthodox churches</a> are often decorated with greenery and flowers on this feast day, and the celebration is intentionally similar to the Jewish holiday of <a href="/wiki/Shavuot" title="Shavuot">Shavuot</a>, which celebrates the giving of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mosaic_Law" title="Mosaic Law">Mosaic Law</a>.</p>
<p>The feast itself lasts three days. The first day is known as "<a href="/wiki/Trinity_Sunday" title="Trinity Sunday">Trinity Sunday</a>"; the second day is known as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spirit_Monday" title="Spirit Monday">Spirit Monday</a>" (or "Monday of the Holy Spirit"); and the third day, Tuesday, is called the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Day_of_the_Trinity" title="Third Day of the Trinity">Third Day of the Trinity</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Afterfeast" title="Afterfeast">Afterfeast</a> of Pentecost lasts for one week, during which <a href="/wiki/Fasting" title="Fasting">fasting</a> is not permitted, even on Wednesday and Friday. In the Orthodox <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacred_Tradition" title="Sacred Tradition">Tradition</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liturgical_color" title="Liturgical color">liturgical color</a> used at Pentecost is <a href="/wiki/Green" title="Green">green</a>, and the clergy and faithful carry flowers and green branches in their hands during the services.</p>
<p>An extraordinary service called the Kneeling Prayer, is observed on the night of Pentecost. This is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Vespers" title="Great Vespers">Vespers</a> service to which are added three sets of long poetical prayers, the composition of Saint <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basil_the_Great" title="Basil the Great">Basil the Great</a>, during which everyone makes a full <a href="/wiki/Genuflection#Orthodox_Christianity" title="Genuflection">prostration</a>, touching their foreheads to the floor (prostrations in church having been forbidden from the day of <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Pascha</a> (Easter) up to this point). Uniquely, these prayers include a petition for all of those in hell, that they may be granted relief and even ultimate release from their confinement, if God deems this possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>All of the remaining days of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_year" title="Ecclesiastical year">ecclesiastical year</a>, until the preparation for the next <a href="/wiki/Great_Lent" title="Great Lent">Great Lent</a> are named for the day after Pentecost on which they occur (for example, the 13th Tuesday After Pentecost).</p>
<p>The Second Monday after Pentecost is the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Apostles%27_Fast" title="Apostles' Fast">Apostles' Fast</a> (which continues until the <a href="/wiki/Feast_of_Saints_Peter_and_Paul" title="Feast of Saints Peter and Paul">Feast of Saints Peter and Paul</a> on June 29). Theologically, Orthodox do not consider Pentecost to be the "birthday" of the Church; they see the Church as having existed before the creation of the world (cf. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shepherd_of_Hermas" title="The Shepherd of Hermas">The Shepherd of Hermas</a></i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Orthodox <a href="/wiki/Icon" title="Icon">icon</a> of the feast depicts the Twelve Apostles seated in a semicircle (sometimes the <a href="/wiki/Theotokos" title="Theotokos">Theotokos</a> (Virgin Mary) is shown sitting in the center of them). At the top of the icon, the Holy Spirit, in the form of tongues of fire, is descending upon them. At the bottom is an <a href="/wiki/Allegory" title="Allegory">allegorical</a> figure, called <i><a href="/wiki/Cosmos" title="Cosmos">Kosmos</a></i>, which symbolizes the world. Although Kosmos is crowned with earthly glory he sits in the darkness caused by the ignorance of God. He is holding a towel on which have been placed 12 scrolls, representing the teaching of the Twelve Apostles.</p>
<p>In the ancient <i>Coptic Orthodox Church of Alexandria</i>, Pentecost is one of the seven Major "Lord's Feasts". It is celebrated at the time of ninth hour (3:00pm) on the Sunday of Pentecost by a special three-segment prayer known as the "Office of Genuflection (Kneeling Prayer)". This feast is followed with the "Apostles Fast" which has a fixed end date on the fifth of the Coptic month of Epip [which currently falls on July 12, which is equivalent to June 29, due to the current 13-day Julian-Gregorian calendar offset]. The fifth of Epip is the commemoration of the Martyrdom of St. Peter and Paul.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_churches">Western churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pentecost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Western churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pentecost_Altar.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Pentecost_Altar.jpg/220px-Pentecost_Altar.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Pentecost_Altar.jpg/330px-Pentecost_Altar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Pentecost_Altar.jpg/440px-Pentecost_Altar.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The liturgical celebrations of Pentecost in <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">Western churches</a> are as rich and varied as those in the East. The main sign of Pentecost in the West is the color red. It symbolizes joy and the fire of the Holy Spirit. Priests or ministers, and choirs wear red <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vestments" title="Vestments">vestments</a>, and in modern times, the custom has extended to the <a href="/wiki/Laity" title="Laity">lay people</a> of the congregation wearing red clothing in celebration as well. Red banners are often hung from walls or ceilings to symbolize the blowing of the "mighty wind"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bibleverse.7CActs.7C2:2.7CKJV_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-bibleverse.7CActs.7C2:2.7CKJV-21">[21]</a></sup> and the free movement of the Spirit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The celebrations may depict symbols of the Holy Spirit, such as the dove or flames, symbols of the church such as <a href="/wiki/Noah%27s_Ark" title="Noah's Ark">Noah's Ark</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pomegranate" title="Pomegranate">Pomegranate</a>, or especially within Protestant churches of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reformed" title="Reformed">Reformed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Evangelicalism" title="Evangelicalism">Evangelical</a> traditions, words rather than images naming for example, the <a href="/wiki/Seven_gifts_of_the_Holy_Spirit" title="Seven gifts of the Holy Spirit">gifts</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fruits_of_the_Spirit" title="Fruits of the Spirit">Fruits of the Spirit</a>. Red flowers at the altar/preaching area, and red flowering plants such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geraniums" title="Geraniums">geraniums</a> around the church are also typical decorations for Pentecost masses/services. These symbolize the renewal of life, the coming of the warmth of summer, and the growth of the church at and from the first Pentecost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stcatherineofsweden1_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-stcatherineofsweden1-23">[23]</a></sup> In the southern hemisphere, for example, in southern Australia, Pentecost comes in the mellow autumntide, after the often great heat of summer, and the red leaves of the poinsettia have often been used to decorate churches then.</p>
<p>These flowers often play an important role in the ancestral rites, and other rites, of the particular congregation. For example, in both Protestant and Catholic churches, the plants brought in to decorate for the holiday may be each "sponsored" by individuals in memory of a particular loved one, or in honor of a living person on a significant occasion, such as their <a href="/wiki/Confirmation" title="Confirmation">Confirmation</a> day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stcatherineofsweden1_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-stcatherineofsweden1-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_language_in_Europe" title="German language in Europe">German speaking lands</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a>, and wherever the people of these nations have wandered, green branches are also traditionally used to decorate churches for Pentecost. <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">Birch</a> is the tree most typically associated with this practice in Europe, but other species are employed in different climates.</p>
<p>The singing of Pentecost hymns is also central to the celebration in the Western tradition. Hymns such as <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Komm,_Heiliger_Geist,_Herre_Gott" title="Komm, Heiliger Geist, Herre Gott">Komm, Heiliger Geist, Herre Gott</a>" (Come, Holy Spirit, God and Lord),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Charles_Wesley" title="Charles Wesley">Charles Wesley</a>'s "Spirit of Faith Come Down"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and "Come Holy Ghost Our Hearts Inspire"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hildegard_von_Bingen" title="Hildegard von Bingen">Hildegard von Bingen</a>'s "O Holy Spirit Root of Life"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> are popular. Some traditional hymns of Pentecost make reference not only to themes relating to the Holy Spirit or the church, but to folk customs connected to the holiday as well, such as the decorating with green branches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Consider "Oh that I had a Thousand Voices" ("<i>O daÃ ich tausend Zungen hÃ¤tte</i>")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> by German, Johann Mentzer Verse 2: "<i>Ye forest leaves so green and tender, that dance for joy in summer air</i>â¦" or "O Day Full of Grace" ("<i>Den signede Dag</i>")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Denmark" title="Kingdom of Denmark">Dane</a>, <a href="/wiki/N._F._S._Grundtvig" title="N. F. S. Grundtvig">N. F. S. Grundtvig</a> verse 3: "<i>Yea were every tree endowed with speech and every leaflet singing</i>â¦". In the Roman Catholic Church, <a href="/wiki/Veni_Sancte_Spiritus" title="Veni Sancte Spiritus">Veni Sancte Spiritus</a> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sequence_(poetry)" title="Sequence (poetry)">sequence hymn</a> for the Day of Pentecost. This has been translated into many languages and is sung in many denominations today. See also <a href="/wiki/Veni_Creator_Spiritus" title="Veni Creator Spiritus">Veni Creator Spiritus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Trumpeters or <a href="/wiki/Brass_instrument" title="Brass instrument">brass ensembles</a> are often specially contracted to accompany singing and provide special music at Pentecost services, recalling the Sound of the mighty wind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bibleverse.7CActs.7C2:2.7CKJV_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-bibleverse.7CActs.7C2:2.7CKJV-21">[21]</a></sup> While this practice is common among a wide spectrum of Western denominations (Eastern Churches do not employ instrumental accompaniment in their worship) it is particularly typical, and distinctive to the heritage of the <a href="/wiki/Moravian_Church_music" title="Moravian Church music">Moravian</a> Church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<p>Another custom is reading the appointed Scripture lessons in multiple foreign languages recounting the speaking in tongues recorded in <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Acts+2%3A4%E2%80%9312&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Acts 2:4â12</a></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, cathedrals and great churches throughout Western Europe were fitted with a peculiar architectural feature known as a Holy Ghost hole: a small circular opening in the roof that symbolized the entrance of the Holy Spirit into the midst of the congregation. At Pentecost, these Holy Ghost holes would be decorated with flowers, and sometimes a dove figure lowered through into the church while the narrative of Pentecost was read. Holy Ghost holes can still be seen today in European churches such as <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Cathedral" title="Canterbury Cathedral">Canterbury Cathedral</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="I can't find any scholarly references to this â 'Middle Ages' is a huge category with ecclesiastical and architectural shifts occurring in various countries at various times. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Similarly, a large two dimensional dove figure would be, and in some places still is, cut from wood, painted, and decorated with flowers, to be lowered over the congregation, particularly during the singing of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sequence_(poetry)" title="Sequence (poetry)">sequence hymn</a>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Veni_Creator_Spiritus" title="Veni Creator Spiritus">Veni Creator Spiritus</a></i>. In other places, particularly <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian peninsula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rosalia_(festival)#Rose_Sundays" title="Rosalia (festival)">rose petals</a> were and are thrown from the galleries over the congregation, recalling the tongues of fire. In modern times, this practice has been revived, and interestingly adapted as well, to include the strewing of <a href="/wiki/Origami" title="Origami">origami</a> doves from above or suspending them, sometimes by the hundreds, from the ceiling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In some cases, red fans,or red handkerchiefs are distributed to the congregation to be waved during the procession, etc. Other congregations have incorporated the use of red balloons, signifying the "Birthday of the Church". These may be borne by the congregants, decorate the sanctuary, or released all at once.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fasting.2C_baptisms.2C_and_confirmations">Fasting, baptisms, and confirmations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pentecost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fasting, baptisms, and confirmations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestants" title="Protestants">Protestants</a>, the nine days between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascension_Day" title="Ascension Day">Ascension Day</a>, and Pentecost are set aside as a time of <a href="/wiki/Fasting" title="Fasting">fasting</a> and universal prayer in honor of the disciples' time of prayer and unity awaiting the Holy Spirit. Similarly among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholics" title="Roman Catholics">Roman Catholics</a>, special Pentecost <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Novenas" title="Novenas">novenas</a> are prayed. The Pentecost Novena is considered the first novena, all other novenas prayed in preparation of various feasts deriving their practice from those original nine days of prayer observed by the disciples of Christ.</p>
<p>While the Eve of Pentecost was traditionally a day of fasting for Catholics, contemporary <a href="/wiki/Canon_law" title="Canon law">canon law</a> no longer requires it. Both Catholics and Protestants may hold spiritual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retreats" title="Retreats">retreats</a>, prayer <a href="/wiki/Vigil" title="Vigil">vigils</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Litanies" title="Litanies">litanies</a> in the days leading up to Pentecost. In some cases vigils on the Eve of Pentecost may last all night. Pentecost is also one of the occasions specially appointed for the Lutheran Litany to be sung.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>

<p>From the early days of Western Christianity, Pentecost became one of the days set aside to celebrate <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">Baptism</a>. In Northern Europe Pentecost was preferred even over Easter for this rite, as the temperatures in late spring might be supposed to be more conducive to outdoor <a href="/wiki/Immersion_baptism" title="Immersion baptism">immersion</a> as was then the practice. It is proposed that the term <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whit_Sunday" title="Whit Sunday">Whit Sunday</a> derives from the custom of the newly baptized wearing white clothing, and from the white vestments worn by the clergy in <a href="/wiki/Sarum_Rite" title="Sarum Rite">English liturgical uses</a>. The holiday was also one of the three days each year (along with <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a>) Roman Catholics were required to <a href="/wiki/Confession_(religion)" title="Confession (religion)">confess</a> and receive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Communion" title="Holy Communion">Holy Communion</a> in order to remain in good ecclesiastical standing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Holy Communion is likewise often a feature of the Protestant observance of Pentecost as well. It is one of the relatively few Sundays some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reformed" title="Reformed">Reformed</a> denominations may offer the communion meal, and is one of the days of the year specially appointed among <a href="/wiki/Moravian_Church" title="Moravian Church">Moravians</a> for the celebration of their Love Feasts. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ordinations" title="Ordinations">Ordinations</a> are celebrated across a wide array of Western denominations at Pentecost, or near to it. In some denominations, for example the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutheran" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a> Church, even if an ordination or consecration of a <a href="/wiki/Deaconess" title="Deaconess">deaconess</a> is not celebrated on Pentecost, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liturgical_color" title="Liturgical color">liturgical color</a> will invariably be red, and the theme of the service will be the Holy Spirit.</p>
<p>Above all, Pentecost is a day for the <a href="/wiki/Confirmation" title="Confirmation">Confirmation</a> celebrations of youths. Flowers, the wearing of white robes, or white dresses recalling Baptism, rites such as the <a href="/wiki/Laying_on_of_hands" title="Laying on of hands">laying on of hands</a>, and vibrant singing play prominent roles on these joyous occasions, the blossoming of Spring forming an equal analogy with the blossoming of youth.</p>
<p>The typical image of Pentecost in the West is that of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virgin_Mary" title="Virgin Mary">Virgin Mary</a> seated centrally and prominently among the disciples with flames resting on the crowns of their heads. Occasionally, parting clouds suggesting the action of the "mighty wind",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bibleverse.7CActs.7C2:2.7CKJV_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-bibleverse.7CActs.7C2:2.7CKJV-21">[21]</a></sup> rays of light and the Dove are also depicted. Of course, the Western iconographic style is less static and stylized than that of the East, and other very different representations have been produced, and, in some cases, have achieved great fame such as the Pentecosts by <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giotto" title="Giotto">Giotto</a>, and <a href="/wiki/El_Greco" title="El Greco">el Greco</a>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_of_Tarsus" title="Paul of Tarsus">St. Paul</a> already in the 1st century notes the importance of this festival to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Christian" title="Early Christian">early Christian</a> communities. (See: <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Acts+20%3A16&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Acts 20:16</a></span> &amp; <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=1+Corinthians+16%3A8&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">1 Corinthians 16:8</a></span>) Since the lifetime of some who may have been eyewitnesses, annual celebrations of the descent of the Holy Spirit have been observed. Before the <a href="/wiki/Second_Vatican_Council" title="Second Vatican Council">Second Vatican Council</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whitmonday" title="Whitmonday">Pentecost Monday</a> as well was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Day_of_Obligation" title="Holy Day of Obligation">Holy Day of Obligation</a> during which the Catholic Church addressed the newly baptized and confirmed. After the Council, Pentecost Monday is no longer solemnized.</p>
<p>Nevertheless, Pentecost Monday remains an official festival in many Protestant churches, such as the (Lutheran) <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Sweden" title="Church of Sweden">Church of Sweden</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_of_Finland" title="Evangelical Lutheran Church of Finland">Evangelical Lutheran Church of Finland</a>, and others. In the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Byzantine_Catholic_Church" title="Greek Byzantine Catholic Church">Byzantine Catholic Rite</a> Pentecost Monday is no longer a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Day_of_Obligation" title="Holy Day of Obligation">Holy Day of Obligation</a>, but rather a simple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_day" title="Holy day">holy day</a>. In the Extraordinary Form of the liturgy of the Roman Catholic Church, as at Easter, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ranks_of_Catholic_liturgical_days" title="Ranks of Catholic liturgical days">liturgical rank</a> of Monday and Tuesday of Pentecost week is a Double of the First Class<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> and across many Western denominations, Pentecost is celebrated with an <a href="/wiki/Octave_(liturgical)" title="Octave (liturgical)">octave</a> culminating on <a href="/wiki/Trinity_Sunday" title="Trinity Sunday">Trinity Sunday</a>. However,in the modern Roman Rite (Ordinary Form), Pentecost ends after Evening Prayer on the feast day itself, with <a href="/wiki/Ordinary_Time" title="Ordinary Time">Ordinary Time</a> resuming the next day.</p>

<p>Marking the festival's importance, in several denominations, such as the Lutheran, Episcopal, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Methodist" title="United Methodist">United Methodist</a> churches, and formerly in the Roman Catholic Church, all the Sundays from the holiday itself until <a href="/wiki/Advent" title="Advent">Advent</a> in late November or December are designated the 2nd, 3rd, Nth, Sunday after Pentecost, etc. Throughout the year, in Roman Catholic piety, Pentecost is the third of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glorious_Mysteries" title="Glorious Mysteries">Glorious Mysteries</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Rosary" title="Rosary">Holy Rosary</a>, as well as being one of the <a href="/wiki/Stations_of_the_Resurrection" title="Stations of the Resurrection">Stations of the Resurrection</a> or Via Lucis.</p>
<p>In some <a href="/wiki/Evangelicalism" title="Evangelicalism">Evangelical</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentecostal" title="Pentecostal">Pentecostal</a> churches, where there is less emphasis on the <a href="/wiki/Liturgical_year" title="Liturgical year">liturgical year</a>, Pentecost may still be one of the greatest celebrations in the year, such as in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> or <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>. In other cases, Pentecost may be ignored as a holy day in these churches. In many evangelical churches in the United States, the secular holiday, <a href="/wiki/Mother%27s_Day" title="Mother's Day">Mother's Day</a>, may be more celebrated than the ancient and biblical feast of Pentecost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Some evangelicals and Pentecostals are observing the liturgical calendar and observe Pentecost as a day to teach the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gifts_of_the_Holy_Spirit" title="Gifts of the Holy Spirit">Gifts of the Holy Spirit</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Across denominational lines Pentecost has been an opportunity for Christians to honor the role of the Holy Spirit in their lives, and celebrate the birth of the Church in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecumenical" title="Ecumenical">ecumenical</a> context.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_compositions_for_Pentecost">Classical compositions for Pentecost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pentecost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Classical compositions for Pentecost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheran</a> church of the <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> observed three days of Pentecost. Some composers wrote sacred <a href="/wiki/Cantata" title="Cantata">cantatas</a> to be performed in the church services of these days. <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Johann Sebastian Bach</a> composed several <a href="/wiki/Bach_cantata" title="Bach cantata">cantatas</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Bach_cantatas_by_liturgical_function#Pentecost" title="List of Bach cantatas by liturgical function">days of Pentecost</a>, including <i><a href="/wiki/Erschallet,_ihr_Lieder,_erklinget,_ihr_Saiten!_BWV_172" title="Erschallet, ihr Lieder, erklinget, ihr Saiten! BWV 172"><i>Erschallet, ihr Lieder, erklinget, ihr Saiten!</i> BWV 172</a></i> in 1714 and <a href="/wiki/Also_hat_Gott_die_Welt_geliebt,_BWV_68" title="Also hat Gott die Welt geliebt, BWV 68"><i>Also hat Gott die Welt geliebt</i>, BWV 68</a> in 1725. <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Heinrich_St%C3%B6lzel" title="Gottfried Heinrich StÃ¶lzel">Gottfried Heinrich StÃ¶lzel</a> wrote cantatas such as <i>Werdet voll Geistes</i> (Get full of spirit) in 1737.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Mozart composed an antiphon <i><a href="/wiki/Veni_Sancte_Spiritus_(Mozart)" title="Veni Sancte Spiritus (Mozart)">Veni Sancte Spiritus</a></i> in 1768.</p>

In 1964 <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Werner" title="Fritz Werner">Fritz Werner</a> wrote an <a href="/wiki/Oratorio" title="Oratorio">oratorio</a> for Pentecost <i>Veni, sancte spiritus</i> (Come, Holy Spirit) on the sequence <i><a href="/wiki/Veni_Sancte_Spiritus" title="Veni Sancte Spiritus">Veni sancte spiritus</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/Jani_Christou" title="Jani Christou">Jani Christou</a> wrote <i>Tongues of Fire</i>, a Pentecost oratorio. <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hillert" title="Richard Hillert">Richard Hillert</a> wrote a <i>Motet for the Day of Pentecost</i> for choir, vibraphone, and prepared electronic tape in 1969. <a href="/wiki/Violeta_Dinescu" title="Violeta Dinescu">Violeta Dinescu</a> composed <i>Pfingstoratorium</i>, an oratorio for Pentecost for five soloists, mixed chorus and small orchestra in 1993.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Customs_and_traditions">Customs and traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pentecost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Customs and traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> it was customary to scatter <a href="/wiki/Rose" title="Rose">rose</a> petals from the ceiling of the churches to recall the miracle of the fiery tongues; hence in <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> and elsewhere in Italy Whitsunday is called <i>Pasqua rosatum</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> name <i>Pasqua rossa</i> comes from the red colours of the vestments used on Whitsunday.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> it was customary to blow <a href="/wiki/Trumpet" title="Trumpet">trumpets</a> during Divine service, to recall the sound of the mighty wind which accompanied the Descent of the Holy Spirit.</p>
<p>In the north west of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, church and chapel parades called Whit Walks take place at Whitsun (sometimes on Whit Friday, the Friday after Whitsun).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Typically, the parades contain brass bands and choirs; girls attending are dressed in white. Traditionally, Whit Fairs (sometimes called Whitsun Ales)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> took place. Other customs such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morris_dancing" title="Morris dancing">morris dancing</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cheese_rolling" title="Cheese rolling">cheese rolling</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> are also associated with Whitsun. "Whitsunday" has been the name of the day in the Church of England. (The Book of Common Prayer only once uses the word "Pentecost" for the festival. Though some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (May 2015)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> think that name derives from white clothes worn by newly baptised in Eastertide, it may well be seen as derived from "wit", hence "wisdom", the reference being to Holy Wisdom (Sancta Sophia, Hagia Sophia), referred to in Proverbs and the Book of Wisdom, with which the Holy Spirit has often been identified.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> there is a saying known virtually by everyone which translates as "if one has no sweetheart until Pentecost, he/she will not have it during the whole summer."</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Port_Vila" title="Port Vila">Port Vila</a>, the capital of <a href="/wiki/Vanuatu" title="Vanuatu">Vanuatu</a>, people originating from <a href="/wiki/Pentecost_Island" title="Pentecost Island">Pentecost Island</a> usually celebrate their island's name-day with a special church service followed by cultural events such as dancing.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_holiday">Public holiday</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pentecost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Public holiday">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since Pentecost itself is on a Sunday, it is automatically considered to be a public holiday in countries with large Christian denominations.</p>
<p>Pentecost Monday is a public holiday in many <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> countries including <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> (since 2008), (most parts of) <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> and also in the African nations <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benin" title="Benin">Benin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Togo" title="Togo">Togo</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> it was also a public holiday, but Pentecost Monday (Annandag Pingst) was replaced by Swedish National Day on June 6, by a government decision on December 15, 2004. In <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>, it is no longer a public holiday. It was a public holiday in <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a> until 1973, when it was replaced by Early Summer Holiday on the first Monday in June. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> the day is known as <a href="/wiki/Whit_Monday" title="Whit Monday">Whit Monday</a>, and was a <a href="/wiki/Bank_holiday" title="Bank holiday">bank holiday</a> until 1967 when it was replaced by the Spring Bank Holiday on the last Monday in May. In France, following reactions to the implementation of the <i><a href="/wiki/Journ%C3%A9e_de_solidarit%C3%A9_envers_les_personnes_%C3%A2g%C3%A9es" title="JournÃ©e de solidaritÃ© envers les personnes Ã¢gÃ©es">JournÃ©e de solidaritÃ© envers les personnes Ã¢gÃ©es</a></i>, Pentecost Monday has been reestablished as a regular (not as a <i>working</i>) holiday on May 3, 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_allusions">Literary allusions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pentecost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Literary allusions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to legend, <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a> always gathered all his knights at the round table for a feast and a quest on Pentecost:</p>

<p><i>So ever the king had a custom that at the feast of Pentecost in especial, afore other feasts in the year, he would not go that day to meat until he had heard or seen of a great marvel.</i> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>"Pfingsten, das liebliche Fest", speaks of Pentecost as a time of greening and blooming in fields, woods, hills, mountains, bushes and hedges, of birds singing new songs, meadows sprouting fragrant flowers, and of festive sunshine gleaming from the skies and coloring the earth â iconic lines idealizing the Pentecost holidays in the German-speaking lands.</p>
<p>Further, Goethe records an old peasant proverb relating to Pentecost in his "Sankt-Rochus-Fest zu Bingen"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> â <i>Ripe strawberries at Pentecost mean a good wine crop.</i></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> mentions Pentecost in a line from <i><a href="/wiki/Romeo_and_Juliet" title="Romeo and Juliet">Romeo and Juliet</a></i> Act 1, Scene V.
