<p>The <b>nebular hypothesis</b> is the most widely accepted model in the field of <a href="/wiki/Cosmogony" title="Cosmogony">cosmogony</a> to explain the <a href="/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System" title="Formation and evolution of the Solar System">formation and evolution of the Solar System</a>. It suggests that the Solar System formed from nebulous material. The theory was developed by <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Immanuel Kant</a> and published in his <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allgemeine_Naturgeschichte_und_Theorie_des_Himmels" title="Allgemeine Naturgeschichte und Theorie des Himmels">Allgemeine Naturgeschichte und Theorie des Himmels</a></i> ("Universal Natural History and Theory of the Heavens"), published in 1755. Originally applied to the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, the process of planetary system formation is now thought to be at work throughout the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">Universe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup> The widely-accepted modern variant of the nebular hypothesis is the <b>solar nebular disk model</b> (SNDM) or <b>solar nebular model</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woolfson1993_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Woolfson1993-2">[2]</a></sup> It offered explanations for a variety of properties of the Solar System, including the nearly circular and coplanar orbits of the planets, and their motion in the same direction as the Sun's rotation. Some elements of the nebular hypothesis are echoed in modern theories of planetary formation, but most elements have been superseded.</p>
<p>According to the nebular hypothesis, stars form in massive and dense clouds of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_hydrogen" title="Molecular hydrogen">molecular hydrogen</a>—<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_molecular_cloud" title="Giant molecular cloud">giant molecular clouds</a> (GMC). These clouds are gravitationally unstable, and matter coalesces within them to smaller denser clumps, which then rotate, collapse, and form stars. Star formation is a complex process, which always produces a gaseous <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk" title="Protoplanetary disk">protoplanetary disk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proplyd" title="Proplyd">proplyd</a>, around the young star. This may give birth to planets in certain circumstances, which are not well known. Thus the formation of planetary systems is thought to be a natural result of star formation. A Sun-like star usually takes approximately 1 million years to form, with the protoplanetary disk evolving into a planetary system over the next 10–100 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The protoplanetary disk is an <a href="/wiki/Accretion_disk" title="Accretion disk">accretion disk</a> that feeds the central star. Initially very hot, the disk later cools in what is known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T_tauri_star" title="T tauri star">T tauri star</a> stage; here, formation of small <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dust</a> grains made of <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocks</a> and ice is possible. The grains eventually may coagulate into kilometer-sized <a href="/wiki/Planetesimal" title="Planetesimal">planetesimals</a>. If the disk is massive enough, the runaway accretions begin, resulting in the rapid—100,000 to 300,000 years—formation of Moon- to Mars-sized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_embryo" title="Planetary embryo">planetary embryos</a>. Near the star, the planetary embryos go through a stage of violent mergers, producing a few <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planet" title="Terrestrial planet">terrestrial planets</a>. The last stage takes approximately 100 million to a billion years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The formation of <a href="/wiki/Giant_planet" title="Giant planet">giant planets</a> is a more complicated process. It is thought to occur beyond the <a href="/wiki/Frost_line_(astrophysics)" title="Frost line (astrophysics)">frost line</a>, where planetary embryos mainly are made of various types of ice. As a result, they are several times more massive than in the inner part of the protoplanetary disk. What follows after the embryo formation is not completely clear. Some embryos appear to continue to grow and eventually reach 5–10 <a href="/wiki/Earth_mass" title="Earth mass">Earth masses</a>—the threshold value, which is necessary to begin accretion of the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>–<a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> gas from the disk. The accumulation of gas by the core is initially a slow process, which continues for several million years, but after the forming protoplanet reaches about 30 Earth masses (<a href="/wiki/Earth_mass" title="Earth mass"><i>M</i><sub>⊕</sub></a>) it accelerates and proceeds in a runaway manner. <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>- and <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>-like planets are thought to accumulate the bulk of their mass during only 10,000 years. The accretion stops when the gas is exhausted. The formed planets can migrate over long distances during or after their formation. <a href="/wiki/Ice_giant" title="Ice giant">Ice giants</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a> are thought to be failed cores, which formed too late when the disk had almost disappeared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nebular_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Solar_System_formation_and_evolution_hypotheses" title="History of Solar System formation and evolution hypotheses">History of Solar System formation and evolution hypotheses</a></div>
<p>There is evidence that <a href="/wiki/Emanuel_Swedenborg" title="Emanuel Swedenborg">Emanuel Swedenborg</a> first proposed parts of the nebular hypothesis in 1734.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swedenborg1734_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Swedenborg1734-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Httpwwwnewchurchhistoryorgarticlesglbbakerpdf_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Httpwwwnewchurchhistoryorgarticlesglbbakerpdf-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Immanuel Kant</a>, familiar with Swedenborg's work, developed the theory further in 1755, publishing his own <i>Universal Natural History and Theory of the Heavens</i>, wherein he argued that gaseous clouds (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nebulae" title="Nebulae">nebulae</a>) slowly rotate, gradually collapse and flatten due to <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a>, eventually forming <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woolfson1993_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Woolfson1993-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pierre-Simon_Laplace" title="Pierre-Simon Laplace">Pierre-Simon Laplace</a> independently developed and proposed a similar model in 1796<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woolfson1993_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Woolfson1993-2">[2]</a></sup> in his <i>Exposition du systeme du monde</i>. He envisioned that the Sun originally had an extended hot atmosphere throughout the volume of the Solar System. His theory featured a contracting and cooling protosolar cloud—the protosolar nebula. As this cooled and contracted, it flattened and spun more rapidly, throwing off (or shedding) a series of gaseous rings of material; and according to him, the planets condensed from this material. His model was similar to Kant's, except more detailed and on a smaller scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woolfson1993_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Woolfson1993-2">[2]</a></sup> While the Laplacian nebular model dominated in the 19th century, it encountered a number of difficulties. The main problem involved <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a> distribution between the Sun and planets. The planets have 99% of the angular momentum, and this fact could not be explained by the nebular model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woolfson1993_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Woolfson1993-2">[2]</a></sup> As a result, astronomers largely abandoned this theory of planet formation at the beginning of the 20th century.</p>
<p>A major critique came during the 19th century from <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">James Clerk Maxwell</a> (1831-1879), who maintained that <i>different rotation between the inner and outer parts of a ring</i> could not allow condensation of material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Astronomer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_David_Brewster" title="Sir David Brewster">Sir David Brewster</a> also rejected Laplace, writing in 1876 that "those who believe in the Nebular Theory consider it as certain that our Earth derived its solid matter and its atmosphere from a ring thrown from the Solar atmosphere, which afterwards contracted into a solid terraqueous sphere, from which the Moon was thrown off by the same process". He argued that under such view, "the Moon must necessarily have carried off water and air from the watery and aerial parts of the Earth and must have an atmosphere".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Brewster claimed that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Isaac_Newton" title="Sir Isaac Newton">Sir Isaac Newton</a>'s religious beliefs had previously considered nebular ideas as tending to atheism, and quoted him as saying that "the growth of new systems out of old ones, without the mediation of a Divine power, seemed to him apparently absurd".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The perceived deficiencies of the Laplacian model stimulated scientists to find a replacement for it. During the 20th century many theories addressed the issue, including the <i>planetesimal theory</i> of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Chrowder_Chamberlin" title="Thomas Chrowder Chamberlin">Thomas Chamberlin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forest_Ray_Moulton" title="Forest Ray Moulton">Forest Moulton</a> (1901), the <i>tidal model</i> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Jeans" title="James Jeans">Jeans</a> (1917), the <i>accretion model</i> of <a href="/wiki/Otto_Schmidt" title="Otto Schmidt">Otto Schmidt</a> (1944), the <i>protoplanet theory</i> of <a href="/wiki/William_McCrea_(astronomer)" title="William McCrea (astronomer)">William McCrea</a> (1960) and finally the <i>capture theory</i> of <a href="/wiki/Michael_Woolfson" title="Michael Woolfson">Michael Woolfson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woolfson1993_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Woolfson1993-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1978 <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Prentice" title="Andrew Prentice">Andrew Prentice</a> resurrected the initial Laplacian ideas about planet formation and developed the <i>modern Laplacian theory</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woolfson1993_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Woolfson1993-2">[2]</a></sup> None of these attempts proved completely successful, and many of the proposed theories were descriptive.</p>
<p>The birth of the modern widely accepted theory of planetary formation—the solar nebular disk model (SNDM)—can be traced to the Soviet astronomer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victor_Safronov" title="Victor Safronov">Victor Safronov</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewScientist_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewScientist-8">[8]</a></sup> His 1969 book <i>Evolution of the protoplanetary cloud and formation of the Earth and the planets</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Safronov1972_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Safronov1972-9">[9]</a></sup> which was translated to English in 1972, had a long-lasting effect on the way scientists think about the formation of the planets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Safronov_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Safronov-10">[10]</a></sup> In this book almost all major problems of the planetary formation process were formulated and some of them solved. Safronov's ideas were further developed in the works of <a href="/wiki/George_Wetherill" title="George Wetherill">George Wetherill</a>, who discovered <i><a href="/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)#Runaway_accretion" title="Accretion (astrophysics)">runaway accretion</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woolfson1993_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Woolfson1993-2">[2]</a></sup> While originally applied only to the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, the SNDM was subsequently thought by theorists to be at work throughout the Universe; as of 22 January 2017 astronomers have discovered 3,565 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" title="Extrasolar planet">extrasolar planets</a> in our <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopaedia_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopaedia-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_nebular_model:_achievements_and_problems">Solar nebular model: achievements and problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nebular_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Solar nebular model: achievements and problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Achievements">Achievements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nebular_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Achievements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The star formation process naturally results in the appearance of <a href="/wiki/Accretion_disk" title="Accretion disk">accretion disks</a> around young stellar objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andre1994_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Andre1994-12">[12]</a></sup> At the age of about 1 million years, 100% of stars may have such disks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haisch2001_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haisch2001-13">[13]</a></sup> This conclusion is supported by the discovery of the gaseous and dusty disks around <a href="/wiki/Protostar" title="Protostar">protostars</a> and <a href="/wiki/T_Tauri_star" title="T Tauri star">T Tauri stars</a> as well as by theoretical considerations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Padgett1999_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Padgett1999-14">[14]</a></sup> Observations of these disks show that the <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dust</a> grains inside them grow in size on short (thousand-year) time scales, producing 1 centimeter sized particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kessler-Silacci2006_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kessler-Silacci2006-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The accretion process, by which 1 km <a href="/wiki/Planetesimal" title="Planetesimal">planetesimals</a> grow into 1,000 km sized bodies, is well understood now.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kokubo2002_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kokubo2002-16">[16]</a></sup> This process develops inside any disk where the number density of planetesimals is sufficiently high, and proceeds in a runaway manner. Growth later slows and continues as oligarchic accretion. The end result is formation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_embryo" title="Planetary embryo">planetary embryos</a> of varying sizes, which depend on the distance from the star.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kokubo2002_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kokubo2002-16">[16]</a></sup> Various simulations have demonstrated that the merger of embryos in the inner part of the protoplanetary disk leads to the formation of a few Earth-sized bodies. Thus the origin of <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planet" title="Terrestrial planet">terrestrial planets</a> is now considered to be an almost solved problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raymond2006_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Raymond2006-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_issues">Current issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nebular_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Current issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The physics of accretion disks encounters some problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wurchterl2004_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wurchterl2004-18">[18]</a></sup> The most important one is how the material, which is accreted by the protostar, loses its <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a>. One possible explanation suggested by <a href="/wiki/Hannes_Alfv%C3%A9n" title="Hannes Alfvén">Hannes Alfvén</a> was that angular momentum was shed by the solar wind during its <a href="/wiki/T_Tauri" title="T Tauri">T Tauri</a> phase. The momentum is transported to the outer parts of the disk by viscous stresses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lynden-bell_1974_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-lynden-bell_1974-19">[19]</a></sup> Viscosity is generated by macroscopic turbulence, but the precise mechanism that produces this turbulence is not well understood. Another possible process for shedding angular momentum is <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_braking" title="Magnetic braking">magnetic braking</a>, where the spin of the star is transferred into the surrounding disk via that star's magnetic field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The main processes responsible for the disappearance of the gas in disks are viscous diffusion and photo-evaporation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dullemond_2007_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-dullemond_2007-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarke_2011_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-clarke_2011-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The formation of planetesimals is the biggest unsolved problem in the nebular disk model. How 1 cm sized particles coalesce into 1 km planetesimals is a mystery. This mechanism appears to be the key to the question as to why some stars have planets, while others have nothing around them, not even <a href="/wiki/Debris_disk" title="Debris disk">dust belts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Youdin2002_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Youdin2002-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The formation timescale of <a href="/wiki/Giant_planet" title="Giant planet">giant planets</a> is also an important problem. Old theories were unable to explain how their cores could form fast enough to accumulate significant amounts of gas from the quickly disappearing protoplanetary disk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kokubo2002_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kokubo2002-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inaba2003_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Inaba2003-24">[24]</a></sup> The mean lifetime of the disks, which is less than ten million (10<sup>7</sup>) years, appeared to be shorter than the time necessary for the core formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haisch2001_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Haisch2001-13">[13]</a></sup> Much progress has been done to solve this problem and current models of giant planet formation are now capable of forming <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> (or more massive planets) in about 4 million years or less, well within the average lifetime of gaseous disks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lhdb2009_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-lhdb2009-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bodenheimer2013_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-bodenheimer2013-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dangelo2014_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-dangelo2014-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another potential problem of giant planet formation is their <a href="/wiki/Planetary_migration" title="Planetary migration">orbital migration</a>. Some calculations show that interaction with the disk can cause rapid inward migration, which, if not stopped, results in the planet reaching the "central regions still as a sub-Jovian object."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> More recent calculations indicate that disk evolution during migration can mitigate this problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ddl2011_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-ddl2011-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_of_stars_and_protoplanetary_disks">Formation of stars and protoplanetary disks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nebular_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Formation of stars and protoplanetary disks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protostars">Protostars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nebular_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Protostars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">Stars</a> are thought to form inside <a href="/wiki/Molecular_cloud" title="Molecular cloud">giant clouds</a> of cold <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_hydrogen" title="Molecular hydrogen">molecular hydrogen</a>—<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_molecular_cloud" title="Giant molecular cloud">giant molecular clouds</a> roughly 300,000 times the mass of the Sun (<a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass"><i>M</i><sub>☉</sub></a>) and 20 <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsecs</a> in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pudritz2002_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pudritz2002-30">[30]</a></sup> Over millions of years, giant molecular clouds are prone to <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_collapse" title="Gravitational collapse">collapse</a> and fragmentation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clark2005_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clark2005-31">[31]</a></sup> These fragments then form small, dense cores, which in turn collapse into stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pudritz2002_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pudritz2002-30">[30]</a></sup> The cores range in mass from a fraction to several times that of the Sun and are called protostellar (protosolar) nebulae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup> They possess diameters of 0.01–0.1 pc (2,000–20,000 AU) and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particle_number_density" title="Particle number density">particle number density</a> of roughly 10,000 to 100,000 cm<sup>−3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[a]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pudritz2002_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pudritz2002-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Motte1998_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Motte1998-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The initial collapse of a solar-mass protostellar nebula takes around 100,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pudritz2002_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-Pudritz2002-30">[30]</a></sup> Every nebula begins with a certain amount of <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a>. Gas in the central part of the nebula, with relatively low angular momentum, undergoes fast compression and forms a hot <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrostatic" title="Hydrostatic">hydrostatic</a> (not contracting) core containing a small fraction of the mass of the original nebula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stahler1980_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stahler1980-34">[33]</a></sup> This core forms the seed of what will become a star.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stahler1980_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stahler1980-34">[33]</a></sup> As the collapse continues, conservation of angular momentum means that the rotation of the infalling envelop accelerates,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nakamoto1995_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nakamoto1995-35">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yorke1999_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yorke1999-36">[35]</a></sup> which largely prevents the gas from directly <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)" title="Accretion (astrophysics)">accreting</a> onto the central core. The gas is instead forced to spread outwards near its equatorial plane, forming a <a href="/wiki/Accretion_disk" title="Accretion disk">disk</a>, which in turn accretes onto the core.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nakamoto1995_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nakamoto1995-35">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yorke1999_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Yorke1999-36">[35]</a></sup> The core gradually grows in mass until it becomes a young hot <a href="/wiki/Protostar" title="Protostar">protostar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stahler1980_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stahler1980-34">[33]</a></sup> At this stage, the protostar and its disk are heavily obscured by the infalling envelope and are not directly observable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andre1994_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Andre1994-12">[12]</a></sup> In fact the remaining envelope's <a href="/wiki/Opacity_(optics)" title="Opacity (optics)">opacity</a> is so high that even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millimeter-wave" title="Millimeter-wave">millimeter-wave</a> radiation has trouble escaping from inside it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andre1994_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Andre1994-12">[12]</a></sup> Such objects are observed as very bright condensations, which emit mainly millimeter-wave and <a href="/wiki/Terahertz_radiation" title="Terahertz radiation">submillimeter-wave</a> radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Motte1998_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Motte1998-33">[32]</a></sup> They are classified as spectral Class 0 protostars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andre1994_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Andre1994-12">[12]</a></sup> The collapse is often accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Bipolar_outflow" title="Bipolar outflow">bipolar outflows</a>—<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jet_(gas)" title="Jet (gas)">jets</a>—that emanate along the <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">rotational</a> axis of the inferred disk. The jets are frequently observed in star-forming regions (see <a href="/wiki/Herbig%E2%80%93Haro_object" title="Herbig–Haro object">Herbig–Haro (HH) objects</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee2000_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lee2000-37">[36]</a></sup> The luminosity of the Class 0 protostars is high — a solar-mass protostar may radiate at up to 100 solar luminosities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andre1994_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Andre1994-12">[12]</a></sup> The source of this energy is <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_collapse" title="Gravitational collapse">gravitational collapse</a>, as their cores are not yet hot enough to begin <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stahler1980_34-3"><a href="#cite_note-Stahler1980-34">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stahler1988_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stahler1988-38">[37]</a></sup></p>

<p>As the infall of its material onto the disk continues, the envelope eventually becomes thin and transparent and the <a href="/wiki/Young_stellar_object" title="Young stellar object">young stellar object</a> (YSO) becomes observable, initially in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Far-infrared" title="Far-infrared">far-infrared</a> light and later in the visible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Motte1998_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-Motte1998-33">[32]</a></sup> Around this time the protostar begins to <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">fuse</a> <a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">deuterium</a>. If the protostar is sufficiently massive (above 80 Jupiter masses (<a href="/wiki/Jupiter_mass" title="Jupiter mass"><i>M</i><sub>J</sub></a>)), hydrogen fusion follows. Otherwise, if its mass is too low, the object becomes a <a href="/wiki/Brown_dwarf" title="Brown dwarf">brown dwarf</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stahler1988_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stahler1988-38">[37]</a></sup> This birth of a new star occurs approximately 100,000 years after the collapse begins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup> Objects at this stage are known as Class I protostars,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andre1994_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-Andre1994-12">[12]</a></sup> which are also called young <a href="/wiki/T_Tauri_star" title="T Tauri star">T Tauri stars</a>, evolved protostars, or young stellar objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andre1994_12-6"><a href="#cite_note-Andre1994-12">[12]</a></sup> By this time the forming star has already accreted much of its mass: the total mass of the disk and remaining envelope does not exceed 10–20% of the mass of the central YSO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Motte1998_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-Motte1998-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the next stage the envelope completely disappears, having been gathered up by the disk, and the protostar becomes a classical T Tauri star.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[b]</a></sup> This happens after about 1 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup> The mass of the disk around a classical T Tauri star is about 1–3% of the stellar mass, and it is accreted at a rate of 10<sup>−7</sup> to 10<sup>−9</sup> <i>M</i><sub>☉</sub> per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hartmann1998_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hartmann1998-42">[40]</a></sup> A pair of bipolar jets is usually present as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shu1997_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shu1997-43">[41]</a></sup> The accretion explains all peculiar properties of classical T Tauri stars: strong <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">flux</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_line" title="Emission line">emission lines</a> (up to 100% of the intrinsic <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminosity</a> of the star), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic" title="Magnetic">magnetic</a> activity, <a href="/wiki/Photometry_(astronomy)" title="Photometry (astronomy)">photometric</a> <a href="/wiki/Variable_star" title="Variable star">variability</a> and jets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muzerolle2001_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Muzerolle2001-44">[42]</a></sup> The emission lines actually form as the accreted gas hits the "surface" of the star, which happens around its <a href="/wiki/Poles_of_astronomical_bodies#Magnetic_poles" title="Poles of astronomical bodies">magnetic poles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muzerolle2001_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Muzerolle2001-44">[42]</a></sup> The jets are byproducts of accretion: they carry away excessive angular momentum. The classical T Tauri stage lasts about 10 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup> The disk eventually disappears due to accretion onto the central star, planet formation, ejection by jets and <a href="/wiki/Photoevaporation" title="Photoevaporation">photoevaporation</a> by UV-radiation from the central star and nearby stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adams2004_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Adams2004-45">[43]</a></sup> As a result, the young star becomes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weak-lined_T_Tauri_star" title="Weak-lined T Tauri star">weakly lined T Tauri star</a>, which slowly, over hundreds of millions of years, evolves into an ordinary Sun-like star.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stahler1980_34-4"><a href="#cite_note-Stahler1980-34">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protoplanetary_disks">Protoplanetary disks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nebular_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Protoplanetary disks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk" title="Protoplanetary disk">Protoplanetary disk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Planetesimal" title="Planetesimal">planetesimal</a></div>

<p>Under certain circumstances the disk, which can now be called protoplanetary, may give birth to a <a href="/wiki/Planetary_system" title="Planetary system">planetary system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup> Protoplanetary disks have been observed around a very high fraction of stars in young <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_clusters" title="Star clusters">star clusters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haisch2001_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Haisch2001-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Megeath2005_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Megeath2005-47">[45]</a></sup> They exist from the beginning of a star's formation, but at the earliest stages are unobservable due to the <a href="/wiki/Opacity_(optics)" title="Opacity (optics)">opacity</a> of the surrounding envelope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andre1994_12-7"><a href="#cite_note-Andre1994-12">[12]</a></sup> The disk of a Class 0 <a href="/wiki/Protostar" title="Protostar">protostar</a> is thought to be massive and hot. It is an <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)" title="Accretion (astrophysics)">accretion disk</a>, which feeds the central protostar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nakamoto1995_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nakamoto1995-35">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yorke1999_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-Yorke1999-36">[35]</a></sup> The temperature can easily exceed 400 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a> inside 5 AU and 1,000 K inside 1 AU.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chick1997_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chick1997-48">[46]</a></sup> The heating of the disk is primarily caused by the <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscous</a> <a href="/wiki/Dissipation" title="Dissipation">dissipation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulence</a> in it and by the infall of the gas from the nebula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nakamoto1995_35-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nakamoto1995-35">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yorke1999_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-Yorke1999-36">[35]</a></sup> The high <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> in the inner disk causes most of the <a href="/wiki/Volatiles" title="Volatiles">volatile</a> material—water, organics, and some <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocks</a> to evaporate, leaving only the most <a href="/wiki/Refractory" title="Refractory">refractory</a> elements like <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>. The ice can survive only in the outer part of the disk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chick1997_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chick1997-48">[46]</a></sup></p>

<p>The main problem in the physics of accretion disks is the generation of turbulence and the mechanism responsible for the high <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">effective viscosity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup> The turbulent viscosity is thought to be responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Transport_phenomena" title="Transport phenomena">transport</a> of the mass to the central protostar and momentum to the periphery of the disk. This is vital for accretion, because the gas can be accreted by the central protostar only if it loses most of its angular momentum, which must be carried away by the small part of the gas drifting outwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nakamoto1995_35-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nakamoto1995-35">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klahr2003_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klahr2003-49">[47]</a></sup> The result of this process is the growth of both the protostar and of the disk <a href="/wiki/Radius" title="Radius">radius</a>, which can reach 1,000 AU if the initial angular momentum of the nebula is large enough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yorke1999_36-4"><a href="#cite_note-Yorke1999-36">[35]</a></sup> Large disks are routinely observed in many star-forming regions such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orion_nebula" title="Orion nebula">Orion nebula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Padgett1999_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Padgett1999-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>The lifespan of the accretion disks is about 10 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haisch2001_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Haisch2001-13">[13]</a></sup> By the time the star reaches the classical T-Tauri stage, the disk becomes thinner and cools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hartmann1998_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hartmann1998-42">[40]</a></sup> Less volatile materials start to <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condense</a> close to its center, forming 0.1–1 μm dust grains that contain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystalline" title="Crystalline">crystalline</a> <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kessler-Silacci2006_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kessler-Silacci2006-15">[15]</a></sup> The transport of the material from the outer disk can mix these newly formed <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">dust grains</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primordial_elements" title="Primordial elements">primordial</a> ones, which contain organic matter and other volatiles. This mixing can explain some peculiarities in the composition of Solar System bodies such as the presence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_dust" title="Interstellar dust">interstellar</a> grains in the primitive <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">meteorites</a> and refractory inclusions in comets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chick1997_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chick1997-48">[46]</a></sup></p>

<p>Dust particles tend to stick to each other in the dense disk environment, leading to the formation of larger particles up to several centimeters in size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michikoshi2006_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Michikoshi2006-51">[49]</a></sup> The signatures of the dust processing and <a href="/wiki/Coagulation" title="Coagulation">coagulation</a> are observed in the infrared spectra of the young disks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kessler-Silacci2006_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kessler-Silacci2006-15">[15]</a></sup> Further aggregation can lead to the formation of <a href="/wiki/Planetesimal" title="Planetesimal">planetesimals</a> measuring 1 km across or larger, which are the building blocks of <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michikoshi2006_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-Michikoshi2006-51">[49]</a></sup> Planetesimal formation is another unsolved problem of disk physics, as simple sticking becomes ineffective as dust particles grow larger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Youdin2002_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Youdin2002-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>One hypothesis is formation by the <a href="/wiki/Jeans_instability" title="Jeans instability">gravitational instability</a>. Particles several centimeters in size or larger slowly settle near the middle plane of the disk, forming a very thin—less than 100 km—and dense layer. This layer is gravitationally unstable and may fragment into numerous clumps, which in turn collapse into planetesimals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-16"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Youdin2002_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Youdin2002-23">[23]</a></sup> However, the differing velocities of the gas disk and the solids near the mid-plane can generate turbulence which prevents the layer from becoming thin enough to fragment due to gravitational instability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johansen_etal_2006_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johansen_etal_2006-52">[50]</a></sup> This may limit the formation of planetesimals via gravitational instabilities to specific locations in the disk where the concentration of solids is enhanced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Protostars_and_Planets_2014_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Protostars_and_Planets_2014-53">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another possible mechanism for the formation of planetesimals is the <a href="/wiki/Streaming_instability" title="Streaming instability">streaming instability</a> in which the drag felt by particles orbiting through gas creates a feedback effect causing the growth of local concentrations. These local concentration push back on the gas creating a region where the headwind felt by the particles is smaller. The concentration is thus able to orbit faster and undergoes less radial drift. Isolated particles join these concentrations as they are overtaken or as they drift inward causing it to grow in mass. Eventually these concentrations form massive filaments which fragment and undergo gravitational collapse forming planetesimals the size of the larger asteroids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johansen_Jacquet_2015_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johansen_Jacquet_2015-54">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Planetary formation can also be triggered by gravitational instability within the disk itself, which leads to its fragmentation into clumps. Some of them, if they are dense enough, will <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_collapse" title="Gravitational collapse">collapse</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klahr2003_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-Klahr2003-49">[47]</a></sup> which can lead to rapid formation of <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">gas giant</a> planets and even <a href="/wiki/Brown_dwarf" title="Brown dwarf">brown dwarfs</a> on the timescale of 1,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boss2003_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boss2003-55">[53]</a></sup> If these clumps migrate inward as the collapse proceeds tidal forces from the star can result in a significant <a href="/wiki/Tidal_downsizing" title="Tidal downsizing">mass loss</a> leaving behind a smaller body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nayaksin_2010_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nayaksin_2010-56">[54]</a></sup> However it is only possible in massive disks—more massive than 0.3 <i>M</i><sub>☉</sub>. In comparison, typical disk masses are 0.01–0.03 <i>M</i><sub>☉</sub>. Because the massive disks are rare, this mechanism of the planet formation is thought to be infrequent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-17"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wurchterl2004_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wurchterl2004-18">[18]</a></sup> On the other hand, this mechanism may play a major role in the formation of <a href="/wiki/Brown_dwarf" title="Brown dwarf">brown dwarfs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stamatellosetal2007_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stamatellosetal2007-57">[55]</a></sup></p>

<p>The ultimate <a href="/wiki/Dissipation" title="Dissipation">dissipation</a> of protoplanetary disks is triggered by a number of different mechanisms. The inner part of the disk is either accreted by the star or ejected by the <a href="/wiki/Bipolar_outflow" title="Bipolar outflow">bipolar jets</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hartmann1998_42-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hartmann1998-42">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shu1997_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shu1997-43">[41]</a></sup> whereas the outer part can <a href="/wiki/Photoevaporation" title="Photoevaporation">evaporate</a> under the star's powerful <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">UV</a> <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> during the T Tauri stage<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Font2004_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Font2004-58">[56]</a></sup> or by nearby stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adams2004_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Adams2004-45">[43]</a></sup> The gas in the central part can either be accreted or ejected by the growing planets, while the small dust particles are ejected by the <a href="/wiki/Radiation_pressure" title="Radiation pressure">radiation pressure</a> of the central star. What is finally left is either a planetary system, a remnant disk of dust without planets, or nothing, if planetesimals failed to form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-18"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because planetesimals are so numerous, and spread throughout the protoplanetary disk, some survive the formation of a planetary system. <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">Asteroids</a> are understood to be left-over planetesimals, gradually grinding each other down into smaller and smaller bits, while comets are typically planetesimals from the farther reaches of a planetary system. Meteorites are samples of planetesimals that reach a planetary surface, and provide a great deal of information about the formation of the Solar System. Primitive-type meteorites are chunks of shattered low-mass planetesimals, where no thermal <a href="/wiki/Planetary_differentiation" title="Planetary differentiation">differentiation</a> took place, while processed-type meteorites are chunks from shattered massive planetesimals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bottke2005_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bottke2005-59">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_of_planets">Formation of planets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nebular_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Formation of planets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rocky_planets">Rocky planets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nebular_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Rocky planets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the solar nebular disk model, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocky_planet" title="Rocky planet">rocky planets</a> form in the inner part of the protoplanetary disk, within the <a href="/wiki/Frost_line_(astrophysics)" title="Frost line (astrophysics)">frost line</a>, where the temperature is high enough to prevent condensation of water ice and other substances into grains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raymond2007_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Raymond2007-60">[58]</a></sup> This results in coagulation of purely rocky grains and later in the formation of rocky planetesimals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[c]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raymond2007_60-2"><a href="#cite_note-Raymond2007-60">[58]</a></sup> Such conditions are thought to exist in the inner 3–4 AU part of the disk of a Sun-like star.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-19"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>After small planetesimals—about 1 km in diameter—have formed by one way or another, <i>runaway accretion</i> begins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kokubo2002_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kokubo2002-16">[16]</a></sup> It is called runaway because the mass growth rate is proportional to <span class="nowrap">R<sup>4</sup>~M<sup>4/3</sup></span>, where R and M are the radius and mass of the growing body, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thommes2003_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thommes2003-62">[59]</a></sup> It is obvious that the specific (divided by mass) growth accelerates as the mass increases. This leads to the preferential growth of larger bodies at the expense of smaller ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kokubo2002_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-Kokubo2002-16">[16]</a></sup> The runaway accretion lasts between 10,000 and 100,000 years and ends when the largest bodies exceed approximately 1,000 km in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kokubo2002_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-Kokubo2002-16">[16]</a></sup> Slowing of the accretion is caused by gravitational perturbations by large bodies on the remaining planetesimals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kokubo2002_16-6"><a href="#cite_note-Kokubo2002-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thommes2003_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thommes2003-62">[59]</a></sup> In addition, the influence of larger bodies stops further growth of smaller bodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kokubo2002_16-7"><a href="#cite_note-Kokubo2002-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The next stage is called <i>oligarchic accretion</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kokubo2002_16-8"><a href="#cite_note-Kokubo2002-16">[16]</a></sup> It is characterized by the dominance of several hundred of the largest bodies—oligarchs, which continue to slowly accrete planetesimals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kokubo2002_16-9"><a href="#cite_note-Kokubo2002-16">[16]</a></sup> No body other than the oligarchs can grow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thommes2003_62-2"><a href="#cite_note-Thommes2003-62">[59]</a></sup> At this stage the rate of accretion is proportional to R<sup>2</sup>, which is derived from the geometrical <a href="/wiki/Cross_section_(geometry)" title="Cross section (geometry)">cross-section</a> of an oligarch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thommes2003_62-3"><a href="#cite_note-Thommes2003-62">[59]</a></sup> The specific accretion rate is proportional to <span class="nowrap">M<sup>−1/3</sup></span>; and it declines with the mass of the body. This allows smaller oligarchs to catch up to larger ones. The oligarchs are kept at the distance of about <span class="nowrap">10·H<sub>r</sub></span> (<span class="nowrap">H<sub>r</sub></span>=<span class="nowrap">a(1-e)(M/3M<sub>s</sub>)<sup>1/3</sup></span> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hill_radius" title="Hill radius">Hill radius</a>, where a is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semimajor_axis" title="Semimajor axis">semimajor axis</a>, e is the <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">orbital eccentricity</a>, and M<sub>s</sub> is the mass of the central star) from each other by the influence of the remaining planetesimals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kokubo2002_16-10"><a href="#cite_note-Kokubo2002-16">[16]</a></sup> Their orbital eccentricities and inclinations remain small. The oligarchs continue to accrete until planetesimals are exhausted in the disk around them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kokubo2002_16-11"><a href="#cite_note-Kokubo2002-16">[16]</a></sup> Sometimes nearby oligarchs merge. The final mass of an oligarch depends on the distance from the star and surface density of planetesimals and is called the isolation mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thommes2003_62-4"><a href="#cite_note-Thommes2003-62">[59]</a></sup> For the rocky planets it is up to 0.1 <i>M</i><sub>⊕</sub>, or one <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-20"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup> The final result of the oligarchic stage is the formation of about 100 <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>- to Mars-sized planetary embryos uniformly spaced at about <span class="nowrap">10·H<sub>r</sub></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raymond2006_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Raymond2006-17">[17]</a></sup> They are thought to reside inside gaps in the disk and to be separated by rings of remaining planetesimals. This stage is thought to last a few hundred thousand years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-21"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kokubo2002_16-12"><a href="#cite_note-Kokubo2002-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The last stage of rocky planet formation is the <i>merger stage</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-22"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup> It begins when only a small number of planetesimals remains and embryos become massive enough to perturb each other, which causes their orbits to become <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaotic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raymond2006_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Raymond2006-17">[17]</a></sup> During this stage embryos expel remaining planetesimals, and collide with each other. The result of this process, which lasts for 10 to 100 million years, is the formation of a limited number of Earth sized bodies. Simulations show that the number of surviving planets is on average from 2 to 5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-23"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raymond2006_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-Raymond2006-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bottke2005_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bottke2005-59">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Petit2001_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Petit2001-63">[60]</a></sup> In the Solar System they may be represented by Earth and <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raymond2006_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-Raymond2006-17">[17]</a></sup> Formation of both planets required merging of approximately 10–20 embryos, while an equal number of them were thrown out of the Solar System.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bottke2005_59-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bottke2005-59">[57]</a></sup> Some of the embryos, which originated in the <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_belt" title="Asteroid belt">asteroid belt</a>, are thought to have brought water to Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raymond2007_60-3"><a href="#cite_note-Raymond2007-60">[58]</a></sup> Mars and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a> may be regarded as remaining embryos that survived that rivalry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bottke2005_59-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bottke2005-59">[57]</a></sup> Rocky planets, which have managed to coalesce, settle eventually into more or less stable orbits, explaining why planetary systems are generally packed to the limit; or, in other words, why they always appear to be at the brink of instability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raymond2006_17-5"><a href="#cite_note-Raymond2006-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Giant_planets">Giant planets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nebular_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Giant planets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fomalhaut_Circumstellar_Disk.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1500" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Fomalhaut_Circumstellar_Disk.jpg/250px-Fomalhaut_Circumstellar_Disk.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Fomalhaut_Circumstellar_Disk.jpg/375px-Fomalhaut_Circumstellar_Disk.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Fomalhaut_Circumstellar_Disk.jpg/500px-Fomalhaut_Circumstellar_Disk.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

</div>
<p>The formation of <a href="/wiki/Giant_planet" title="Giant planet">giant planets</a> is an outstanding problem in the <a href="/wiki/Planetary_science" title="Planetary science">planetary sciences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wurchterl2004_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wurchterl2004-18">[18]</a></sup> In the framework of the solar nebular model two theories for their formation exist. The first one is the <i>disk instability model</i>, where giant planets form in the massive protoplanetary disks as a result of its <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravitational</a> fragmentation (see above).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boss2003_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-Boss2003-55">[53]</a></sup> The second possibility is the <i>core accretion model</i>, which is also known as the <i>nucleated instability model</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wurchterl2004_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wurchterl2004-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ddl2011_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-ddl2011-29">[29]</a></sup> The latter scenario is thought to be the most promising one, because it can explain the formation of the giant planets in relatively low-mass disks (less than 0.1 <i>M</i><sub>☉</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ddl2011_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-ddl2011-29">[29]</a></sup> In this model giant planet formation is divided into two stages: a) accretion of a core of approximately 10 <i>M</i><sub>⊕</sub> and b) accretion of gas from the protoplanetary disk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-24"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wurchterl2004_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-Wurchterl2004-18">[18]</a></sup> Either method may also lead to the creation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brown_dwarfs" title="Brown dwarfs">brown dwarfs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bodenheimer2013_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-bodenheimer2013-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janson2011_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Janson2011-64">[61]</a></sup> Searches as of 2011 have found that core accretion is likely the dominant formation mechanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janson2011_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-Janson2011-64">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Giant planet core formation is thought to proceed roughly along the lines of the terrestrial planet formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kokubo2002_16-13"><a href="#cite_note-Kokubo2002-16">[16]</a></sup> It starts with planetesimals that undergo runaway growth, followed by the slower oligarchic stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thommes2003_62-5"><a href="#cite_note-Thommes2003-62">[59]</a></sup> Hypotheses do not predict a merger stage, due to the low probability of collisions between planetary embryos in the outer part of planetary systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thommes2003_62-6"><a href="#cite_note-Thommes2003-62">[59]</a></sup> An additional difference is the composition of the <a href="/wiki/Planetesimal" title="Planetesimal">planetesimals</a>, which in the case of giant planets form beyond the so-called <a href="/wiki/Frost_line_(astrophysics)" title="Frost line (astrophysics)">snow line</a> and consist mainly of ice—the ice to rock ratio is about 4 to 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inaba2003_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Inaba2003-24">[24]</a></sup> This enhances the mass of planetesimals fourfold. However, the minimum mass nebula capable of terrestrial planet formation can only form 1–2 <i>M</i><sub>⊕</sub> cores at the distance of Jupiter (5 AU) within 10 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thommes2003_62-7"><a href="#cite_note-Thommes2003-62">[59]</a></sup> The latter number represents the average lifetime of gaseous disks around Sun-like stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haisch2001_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-Haisch2001-13">[13]</a></sup> The proposed solutions include enhanced mass of the disk—a tenfold increase would suffice;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thommes2003_62-8"><a href="#cite_note-Thommes2003-62">[59]</a></sup> protoplanet migration, which allows the embryo to accrete more planetesimals;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inaba2003_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Inaba2003-24">[24]</a></sup> and finally accretion enhancement due to <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">gas drag</a> in the gaseous envelopes of the embryos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inaba2003_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-Inaba2003-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dangelo2014_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-dangelo2014-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fortier2007_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fortier2007-65">[62]</a></sup> Some combination of the above-mentioned ideas may explain the formation of the cores of gas giant planets such as <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> and perhaps even <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wurchterl2004_18-5"><a href="#cite_note-Wurchterl2004-18">[18]</a></sup> The formation of planets like <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a> is more problematic, since no theory has been capable of providing for the in situ formation of their cores at the distance of 20–30 AU from the central star.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-25"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup> One hypothesis is that they initially accreted in the Jupiter-Saturn region, then were scattered and migrated to their present location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thommes1999_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thommes1999-66">[63]</a></sup> Another possible solution is the growth of the cores of the giant planets via <a href="/wiki/Pebble_accretion" title="Pebble accretion">pebble accretion</a>. In pebble accretion objects between a cm and a meter in diameter falling toward a massive body are slowed enough by gas drag for them to spiral toward it and be accreted. Growth via pebble accretion may be as much as 1000 time faster than by the accretion of planesimals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lambrechts_Johansen_2012_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lambrechts_Johansen_2012-67">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once the cores are of sufficient mass (5–10 <i>M</i><sub>⊕</sub>), they begin to gather gas from the surrounding disk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montmerle2006_1-26"><a href="#cite_note-Montmerle2006-1">[1]</a></sup> Initially it is a slow process, increasing the core masses up to 30 <i>M</i><sub>⊕</sub> in a few million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inaba2003_24-4"><a href="#cite_note-Inaba2003-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fortier2007_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fortier2007-65">[62]</a></sup> After that, the accretion rates increase dramatically and the remaining 90% of the mass is accumulated in approximately 10,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fortier2007_65-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fortier2007-65">[62]</a></sup> The accretion of gas stops when the supply from the disk is exhausted. This happens gradually, due to the formation of a density gap in the protoplanetary disk and to disk dispersal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ddl2011_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-ddl2011-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Papaloizou2007_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Papaloizou2007-68">[65]</a></sup> In this model ice giants—Uranus and Neptune—are failed cores that began gas accretion too late, when almost all gas had already disappeared. The post-runaway-gas-accretion stage is characterized by migration of the newly formed giant planets and continued slow gas accretion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Papaloizou2007_68-1"><a href="#cite_note-Papaloizou2007-68">[65]</a></sup> Migration is caused by the interaction of the planet sitting in the gap with the remaining disk. It stops when the protoplanetary disk disappears or when the end of the disk is attained. The latter case corresponds to the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_Jupiters" title="Hot Jupiters">hot Jupiters</a>, which are likely to have stopped their migration when they reached the inner hole in the protoplanetary disk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Papaloizou2007_68-2"><a href="#cite_note-Papaloizou2007-68">[65]</a></sup></p>

In this artist's conception, a planet spins through a clearing (gap) in a nearby star's dusty, planet-forming disc.</div>

<p>Giant planets can significantly influence <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planet" title="Terrestrial planet">terrestrial planet</a> formation. The presence of giants tends to increase <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">eccentricities</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">inclinations</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Kozai_mechanism" title="Kozai mechanism">Kozai mechanism</a>) of planetesimals and embryos in the terrestrial planet region (inside 4 AU in the Solar System).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bottke2005_59-4"><a href="#cite_note-Bottke2005-59">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Petit2001_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-Petit2001-63">[60]</a></sup> If giant planets form too early, they can slow or prevent inner planet accretion. If they form near the end of the oligarchic stage, as is thought to have happened in the Solar System, they will influence the merges of planetary embryos, making them more violent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bottke2005_59-5"><a href="#cite_note-Bottke2005-59">[57]</a></sup> As a result, the number of terrestrial planets will decrease and they will be more massive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levinson2003_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Levinson2003-69">[66]</a></sup> In addition, the size of the system will shrink, because terrestrial planets will form closer to the central star. The influence of giant planets in the Solar System, particularly that of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>, is thought to have been limited because they are relatively remote from the terrestrial planets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levinson2003_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-Levinson2003-69">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>The region of a planetary system adjacent to the giant planets will be influenced in a different way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Petit2001_63-2"><a href="#cite_note-Petit2001-63">[60]</a></sup> In such a region, eccentricities of embryos may become so large that the embryos pass close to a giant planet, which may cause them to be ejected from the system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[d]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bottke2005_59-6"><a href="#cite_note-Bottke2005-59">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Petit2001_63-3"><a href="#cite_note-Petit2001-63">[60]</a></sup> If all embryos are removed, then no planets will form in this region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Petit2001_63-4"><a href="#cite_note-Petit2001-63">[60]</a></sup> An additional consequence is that a huge number of small planetesimals will remain, because giant planets are incapable of clearing them all out without the help of embryos. The total mass of remaining planetesimals will be small, because cumulative action of the embryos before their ejection and giant planets is still strong enough to remove 99% of the small bodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bottke2005_59-7"><a href="#cite_note-Bottke2005-59">[57]</a></sup> Such a region will eventually evolve into an <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_belt" title="Asteroid belt">asteroid belt</a>, which is a full analog of the asteroid belt in the Solar System, located from 2 to 4 AU from the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bottke2005_59-8"><a href="#cite_note-Bottke2005-59">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Petit2001_63-5"><a href="#cite_note-Petit2001-63">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meaning_of_accretion">Meaning of <i>accretion</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nebular_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Meaning of accretion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Use of the term <a href="/wiki/Accretion_disk" title="Accretion disk">accretion disk</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk" title="Protoplanetary disk">protoplanetary disk</a> leads to confusion over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_accretion" title="Planetary accretion">planetary accretion</a> process. The protoplanetary disk is sometimes referred to as an accretion disk, because while the young <a href="/wiki/T_Tauri_star" title="T Tauri star">T Tauri</a>-like protostar is still contracting, gaseous material may still be falling onto it, accreting on its surface from the disk's inner edge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yorke1999_36-5"><a href="#cite_note-Yorke1999-36">[35]</a></sup> In an <a href="/wiki/Accretion_disk" title="Accretion disk">accretion disk</a>, there is a net flux of mass from larger radii toward smaller radii.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lynden-bell_1974_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-lynden-bell_1974-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, that meaning should not be confused with the process of accretion forming the planets. In this context, accretion refers to the process of cooled, solidified grains of dust and ice orbiting the <a href="/wiki/Protostar" title="Protostar">protostar</a> in the protoplanetary disk, colliding and sticking together and gradually growing, up to and including the high-energy collisions between sizable <a href="/wiki/Planetesimal" title="Planetesimal">planetesimals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kokubo2002_16-14"><a href="#cite_note-Kokubo2002-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, the <a href="/wiki/Giant_planet" title="Giant planet">giant planets</a> probably had accretion disks of their own, in the first meaning of the word.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dangelo_podolak_2015_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-dangelo_podolak_2015-71">[67]</a></sup> The clouds of captured hydrogen and helium gas contracted, spun up, flattened, and deposited gas onto the surface of each giant <a href="/wiki/Protoplanet" title="Protoplanet">protoplanet</a>, while solid bodies within that disk accreted into the giant planet's regular moons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canup2002_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canup2002-72">[68]</a></sup></p>
