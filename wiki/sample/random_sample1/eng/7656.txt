<p><b>God the Son</b> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Θεός ὁ υἱός</span>) is the second person of the <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Trinity</a> in <a href="/wiki/Christian_theology" title="Christian theology">Christian theology</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Doctrine" title="Doctrine">doctrine</a> of the Trinity identifies <a href="/wiki/Jesus_in_Christianity" title="Jesus in Christianity">Jesus</a> as the metaphysical embodiment of <a href="/wiki/God_in_Christianity" title="God in Christianity">God</a> the Son, <i>united in essence but distinct in person</i> with regard to <a href="/wiki/God_the_Father" title="God the Father">God the Father</a> and God the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Spirit_(Christianity)" title="Holy Spirit (Christianity)">Holy Spirit</a> (the first and third persons of the Trinity).</p>
<p>In these teachings, God the Son <a href="/wiki/Pre-existence_of_Christ" title="Pre-existence of Christ">pre-existed</a> before incarnation, is co-eternal with God the Father (and the Holy Spirit), both before Creation and after the End (see <a href="/wiki/Eschatology" title="Eschatology">Eschatology</a>). <a href="/wiki/Son_of_God" title="Son of God">Son of God</a> for some draws attention to his humanity, whereas <i>God the Son</i> refers more generally to his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Divinity_of_Jesus" title="Divinity of Jesus">divinity</a>, including his pre-incarnate existence.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Source_of_the_term">Source of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=God_the_Son&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Source of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "God the Son" is not found in the Bible,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> but is a term found in later Christian sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> By scribal error the term is in one medieval manuscript, MS No.1985, where Galatians 2:20 has "Son of God" changed to "God the Son".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term in English follows Latin usage as found in the <a href="/wiki/Athanasian_Creed" title="Athanasian Creed">Athanasian Creed</a> and other texts of the early church:</p>
<ul>
<li>In Greek "God the Son" is <i>Theos o Iios</i>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%98%CE%B5%CF%8C%CF%82" title="wikt:Θεός">Θεός</a> ὁ <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%85%E1%BC%B1%CF%8C%CF%82" title="wikt:υἱός">υἱός</a> (as distinct from <i>o Iios</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nominative" title="Nominative">nominative</a> <i>tu Theu</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genitive" title="Genitive">genitive</a>, ὁ υἱός του Θεού, "<a href="/wiki/Son_of_God" title="Son of God">Son of God</a>").</li>
<li>In Latin "God the Son" is <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/deus#Inflection" title="wikt:deus">Deus</a> (nominative) <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/filius#Inflection" title="wikt:filius">Filius</a> (nominative). The term "deus filius" is found in the Athanasian Creed: "Et tamen non tres omnipotentes, sed unus omnipotens. Ita Deus Pater, Deus Filius, Deus [et] Spiritus Sanctus." (distinct from <i>filius Dei</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genitive" title="Genitive">genitive</a> "son of God"), but this phrase is also translated "So the Father is God: the Son is God: and the Holy Ghost is God".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The distinction holds true in other modern languages apart from English, for example:</p>
<ul>
<li>In Hebrew "God the Son" (<i>Elohim ha-Ben</i> אלוהים הבן) is used in modern Israeli Christian literature<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> in relation to the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Trinity" title="Holy Trinity">Holy Trinity</a>" (<i>ha-shilush ha-kadosh</i> השילוש הקדוש). As distinct from the term "son of God" (<i>ben Elohim</i> בן אלוהים) as found in the Hebrew New Testament.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_the_term">Use of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=God_the_Son&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/God_in_Christianity" title="God in Christianity">God in Christianity</a></div>
<p>The term "deus filius" is used in the <a href="/wiki/Athanasian_Creed" title="Athanasian Creed">Athanasian Creed</a> and formulas such as <i>Deus Pater, Deus Filius, Deus Spiritus Sanctus: Et non tres Dii, sed unus est Deus.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term is used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Augustine" title="Saint Augustine">Saint Augustine</a> in his <i>On the Trinity</i>, for example in discussion of the Son's obedience to God the Father: <i>deo patri deus filius obediens</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jacques_Forget" title="Jacques Forget">Jacques Forget</a> (1910) in the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Encyclopedia" title="Catholic Encyclopedia">Catholic Encyclopedia</a> article "Holy Ghost" notes that "Among the apologists, <a href="/wiki/Athenagoras_of_Athens" title="Athenagoras of Athens">Athenagoras</a> mentions the Holy Ghost along with, and on the same plane as, the Father and the Son. 'Who would not be astonished', says he (A Plea for the Christians 10), 'to hear us called atheists, us who confess God the Father, God the Son and the Holy Ghost, and hold them one in power and distinct in order.' "<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Augustine" title="Saint Augustine">Saint Augustine</a> in <i>Sermon 90 on the New Testament</i> says, "2. For hold this fast as a firm and settled truth, if you would continue Catholics, that God the Father begot God the Son without time, and made Him of a Virgin in time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Augsburg_Confession" title="Augsburg Confession">Augsburg Confession</a> (1530) adopted the phrase as <i>Gott der Sohn</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Monogen%C4%93s#Textual_issues_in_John_1:18" title="Monogenēs">A manuscript variant in John 1:18</a> (Θεὸν οὐδεὶς ἑώρακεν πώποτε· μονογενὴς Θεὸς ὁ ὢν εἰς τὸν κόλπον τοῦ Πατρὸς, ἐκεῖνος ἐξηγήσατο) has led to translations including "God the One and Only" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NIV" title="NIV">NIV</a>, 1984) referring to the Son.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>But the term "Son of God" is used to refer to Jesus in the first gospel of Mark at the beginning in verse 1:1 and at its end in chapter 15 verse 39. Max Botner wrote, "Indeed, if Mark 1:1 presents the "normative understanding" of Jesus' identity, then it makes a significant difference what the text includes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later theological use of this expression (compare Latin: <i>Deus Filius</i>) reflects what came to be the standard interpretation of New Testament references, understood to imply Jesus' Divinity, but with the distinction of his person from another Person of the Trinity called the Father. As such, the title is associated more with the development of the doctrine of the <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Trinity</a>. A clear expression of a Trinitarian belief is found in <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matthew+28%3A19&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Matthew 28:19</a></span>, "Go, therefore, and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit." <a href="/wiki/John_the_Evangelist" title="John the Evangelist">John the Evangelist</a> is understood to identify Jesus with the pre-existent Logos or Word, the second person of the Trinity, "In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God."<sup class="nourlexpansion reference"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=John+1%3A1&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">[John 1:1]</a></sup></p>
