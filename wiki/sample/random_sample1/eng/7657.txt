<p>"<b>The Stars and Stripes Forever</b>" is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriotic_music" title="Patriotic music">patriotic</a> <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/American_march_music" title="American march music">march</a> widely considered to be the <a href="/wiki/Masterpiece" title="Masterpiece">magnum opus</a> of composer <a href="/wiki/John_Philip_Sousa" title="John Philip Sousa">John Philip Sousa</a>. By a 1987 act of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">U.S. Congress</a>, it is the official National March of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States of America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-national_march_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-national_march-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Stars_and_Stripes_Forever&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his autobiography, <i>Marching Along</i>, Sousa wrote that he composed the march on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christmas_Day" title="Christmas Day">Christmas Day</a>, 1896. He was on an ocean liner on his way home from a vacation with his wife in Europe and had just learned of the recent death of David Blakely, the manager of the Sousa Band. He composed the march in his head and committed the notes to paper on arrival in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pbs_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-pbs-2">[2]</a></sup> It was first performed at <a href="/wiki/Willow_Grove_Park" title="Willow Grove Park">Willow Grove Park</a>, just outside Philadelphia, on May 14, 1897, and was immediately greeted with enthusiasm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-outryve_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-outryve-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Stars_and_Stripes_Forever&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"The Stars and Stripes Forever" follows the standard American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grandioso" title="Grandioso">military march form</a>. The march begins with a four-bar introduction, which is followed by a dotted, playful melody.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-outryve_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-outryve-3">[3]</a></sup> Its trio is the most famous part of the march. <a href="/wiki/Piccolo" title="Piccolo">Piccolo</a> players play the famous <a href="/wiki/Obbligato" title="Obbligato">obbligato</a> in the first repeat of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grandioso" title="Grandioso">trio</a> (the one after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breakstrain" title="Breakstrain">breakstrain</a>). In the final repeat of the trio (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grandioso" title="Grandioso">grandioso</a>), the low brass joins the piccolo players with a prominent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Countermelody" title="Countermelody">countermelody</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lyrics">Lyrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Stars_and_Stripes_Forever&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lyrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sousa wrote lyrics to the piece, although they are not as familiar as the music itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-works_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-works-4">[4]</a></sup> The typical pairing of Sousa's lyrics with the various sections of the march is noted in the square brackets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-choral_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-choral-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sousa.27s_lyrics">Sousa's lyrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Stars_and_Stripes_Forever&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sousa's lyrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="poem">
<p>[<i>First Strain</i>]<br/>
Let martial note in triumph float<br/>
And liberty extend its mighty hand<br/>
A flag appears 'mid thunderous cheers,<br/>
The banner of the Western land.<br/>
The emblem of the brave and true<br/>
Its folds protect no tyrant crew;<br/>
The red and white and starry blue<br/>
Is freedom's shield and hope.<br/>
<br/>
[<i>Second Strain</i>]<br/>
Other nations may deem their flags the best<br/>
And cheer them with fervid elation<br/>
But the flag of the North and South and West<br/>
Is the flag of flags, the flag of Freedom's nation.<br/>
<br/>
(repeats) Other nations may deem their flags the best<br/>
And cheer them with fervid elation,<br/>
But the flag of the North and South and West<br/>
Is the flag of flags, the flag of Freedom's nation.<br/>
<br/>
[<i>Trio</i>]<br/>
Hurrah for the flag of the free!<br/>
May it wave as our standard forever,<br/>
The gem of the land and the sea,<br/>
The banner of the right.<br/>
Let despots remember the day<br/>
When our fathers with mighty endeavor<br/>
Proclaimed as they marched to the fray<br/>
That by their might and by their right<br/>
It waves forever.<br/>
<br/>
[<i>Repeat of the First Strain</i>]<br/>
Let eagle shriek from lofty peak<br/>
The never-ending watchword of our land;<br/>
Let summer breeze waft through the trees<br/>
The echo of the chorus grand.<br/>
Sing out for liberty and light,<br/>
Sing out for freedom and the right.<br/>
Sing out for Union and its might,<br/>
O patriotic sons.<br/>
<br/>
[<i>Grandioso</i>]<br/>
Hurrah for the flag of the free.<br/>
May it wave as our standard forever<br/>
The gem of the land and the sea,<br/>
The banner of the right.<br/>
Let despots remember the day<br/>
When our fathers with mighty endeavor<br/>
Proclaimed as they marched to the fray,<br/>
That by their might and by their right<br/>
It waves forever.</p>
</div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tidmarsh.27s_additional_lyrics">Tidmarsh's additional lyrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Stars_and_Stripes_Forever&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tidmarsh's additional lyrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1942 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Church_Company" title="John Church Company">John Church Company</a> published a four-part choral version of the march with a piano arrangement by Elmer Arthur Tidmarsh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-choral_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-choral-5">[5]</a></sup> This arrangement has additional lyrics written by Tidmarsh for the Breakstrain section of the march.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_lyrics">Other lyrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Stars_and_Stripes_Forever&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other lyrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many other sets of lyrics, patriotic and otherwise, have been written for the last strain (trio/grandioso). Two of the most widely known such sets begin with the words "Three cheers for the red, white and blue" and "Be kind to your web-footed friends". The "web-footed friends" parody was sung at the end of every episode of the popular 1960s TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Mitch_Miller" title="Mitch Miller">Sing Along with Mitch</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It was recorded by <a href="/wiki/Mary_Healy_(entertainer)" title="Mary Healy (entertainer)">Mary Healy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Lind_Hayes" title="Peter Lind Hayes">Peter Lind Hayes</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> in 1954 and by <a href="/wiki/Homer_and_Jethro" title="Homer and Jethro">Homer and Jethro</a> in 1955 as "Crazy Mixed Up Song".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The parody lyrics are credited to <a href="/wiki/Charles_Randolph_Grean" title="Charles Randolph Grean">Charles Randolph Grean</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Joan_Javits&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Joan Javits (page does not exist)">Joan Javits</a>. It was also heard in the <i><a href="/wiki/Tiny_Toon_Adventures" title="Tiny Toon Adventures">Tiny Toon Adventures</a></i> episode "Hollywood Plucky", and an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Sesame_Street" title="Sesame Street">Sesame Street</a></i>; these parody lyrics themselves are well-known enough to have spawned many other parodies of their own.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations_and_notable_uses">Variations and notable uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Stars_and_Stripes_Forever&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Variations and notable uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"The Stars and Stripes Forever" is featured in many U.S. musical performances:</p>
<ul>
<li>There are several orchestral transcriptions of "The Stars and Stripes Forever", including one by <a href="/wiki/Conducting" title="Conducting">conductor</a> <a href="/wiki/Leopold_Stokowski" title="Leopold Stokowski">Leopold Stokowski</a> and one by Keith Brion and Loras Schissel. There was also an orchestral arrangement of the march by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Davis" title="Carl Davis">Carl Davis</a> and David Cullen for the album <i>Carl Conducts...Classical Festival Favourites</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>The tune is widely used by soccer fans, with the trio/grandioso section sung with the words "<a href="/wiki/Here_We_Go_(football_chant)" title="Here We Go (football chant)">Here We Go</a>". The supporters of Spanish side <a href="/wiki/Valencia_CF" title="Valencia CF">Valencia CF</a> used to sing it with the words <span style="padding-left:.2em;">"</span><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Che_(Spanish)#Other_uses" title="Che (Spanish)">Xe</a> que bó!</i><span style="padding-left:.2em;">"</span> which means something like "Oh! How good" in <a href="/wiki/Valencian" title="Valencian">Catalan</a>, and those words have become a symbol for the team. Another version uses the word <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Cheerio" title="Cheerio">cheerio</a> repeatedly, normally sung to players or coaches when they have been <a href="/wiki/Ejection_(sports)" title="Ejection (sports)">sent off</a> or occasionally when an <a href="/wiki/Underdog_(term)" title="Underdog (term)">underdog</a> has ended its opponent's <a href="/wiki/Single-elimination_tournament" title="Single-elimination tournament">cup</a> campaign.</li>
<li>In the classic 1933 film <i><a href="/wiki/Duck_Soup_(1933_film)" title="Duck Soup (1933 film)">Duck Soup</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Harpo_Marx" title="Harpo Marx">Harpo Marx</a>, playing Pinky, a spy infiltrating a house in the middle of the night, attempts to open what he believes to be a safe, but turns out to be a large radio, which loudly begins playing "Stars &amp; Stripes Forever" when he turns the knob. Pinky spends the next several moments futilely (and loudly) trying to quell the noise before throwing the radio out a nearby window.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Popeye_the_Sailor_(animated_cartoons)" title="Popeye the Sailor (animated cartoons)">Classic Popeye the Sailor cartoons</a> by Fleischer Studios make frequent use of the tune in the music score accompanying the climactic fight between Popeye and the villain starting with the moment Popeye gets a spinach power boost.</li>
<li>Parts of the tune feature in two <a href="/wiki/Laurel_and_Hardy" title="Laurel and Hardy">Laurel and Hardy</a> short films, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chimp" title="The Chimp">The Chimp</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Come_Clean_(film)" title="Come Clean (film)">Come Clean</a></i>.</li>
<li>In show business, particularly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theater" title="Theater">theater</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Circus" title="Circus">circus</a>, this piece is called "the Disaster March". It is a traditional code signaling a life-threatening emergency. This helps theater personnel to handle events and organize the audience's exit without panic. Circus bands never play it under any other circumstances. One memorable example of its use was at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hartford_Circus_Fire" title="Hartford Circus Fire">Hartford Circus Fire</a> in July 1944, in which at least 160 people were killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>The student band <a href="/wiki/Strindens_Promenade_Orchester" title="Strindens Promenade Orchester">Strindens Promenade Orchester</a> in <a href="/wiki/Trondheim" title="Trondheim">Trondheim</a>, Norway, has the world record in "speed playing" of "The Stars and Stripes Forever" (absolutely all notes must be played). The band calls their speedy rendering of the march "Stars and Stri<i>b</i>es", and performs the march at all Saturday parties at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studentersamfundet_i_Trondhjem" title="Studentersamfundet i Trondhjem">Trondheim Student Society</a>. Set during the fall term of 1999, the record time is 50.9 seconds (nominal time is 3 minutes 50 seconds). For this, the band is noted in the Norwegian edition of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinness_Book_of_Records" title="Guinness Book of Records">Guinness Book of Records</a>.</li>
<li>A 1952 biographical film, <i><a href="/wiki/Stars_and_Stripes_Forever_(film)" title="Stars and Stripes Forever (film)">Stars and Stripes Forever</a></i>, gives an account of the composer's life and music.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> born pianist <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Horowitz" title="Vladimir Horowitz">Vladimir Horowitz</a>, who lived most his life in the United States, wrote a famous transcription of "The Stars and Stripes Forever" for solo <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a> to celebrate his becoming an American citizen. In an interview, Horowitz opined that the march, being a military march, is meant to be played at a walking tempo. He complained that many conductors played the piece too fast, resulting in music that is "hackneyed".</li>
<li>In "<a href="/wiki/Evolution_(Star_Trek:_The_Next_Generation)" title="Evolution (Star Trek: The Next Generation)">Evolution</a>", the first episode of the <a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_The_Next_Generation_(season_3)" title="Star Trek: The Next Generation (season 3)">third season</a> of the television series <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_The_Next_Generation" title="Star Trek: The Next Generation">Star Trek: The Next Generation</a></i>, a malfunction in the ship's systems causes the main computer to play Sousa's march on all channels throughout the ship. The episode was first aired on September 25, 1989.</li>
<li>The song is usually played for the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a> after he gives a speech at a public forum, event, or ceremony, whereas "<a href="/wiki/Hail_to_the_Chief" title="Hail to the Chief">Hail to the Chief</a>" is played when the President is introduced.</li>
<li>In 2008, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muppets" title="Muppets">Muppets</a> performed a web version starring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sam_the_Eagle" title="Sam the Eagle">Sam the Eagle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beaker_(muppet)" title="Beaker (muppet)">Beaker</a>, a clucking <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">chicken</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bobo_the_Bear" title="Bobo the Bear">Bobo the Bear</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Swedish_Chef" title="The Swedish Chef">The Swedish Chef</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crazy_Harry" title="Crazy Harry">Crazy Harry</a>.</li>
<li>The march was adapted for the theme song to <a href="/wiki/The_Berenstain_Bears_(1985_TV_series)" title="The Berenstain Bears (1985 TV series)"><i>The Berenstain Bears</i></a> 1985 cartoon.</li>
<li>American composer <a href="/wiki/Robert_W._Smith_(musician)" title="Robert W. Smith (musician)">Robert W. Smith</a> parodied "Stars and Stripes Forever" along with "Jingle Bells" with his composition "Jingle Bells Forever", published by Alfred Publishing Co.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>At the conclusion of WWE's <a href="/wiki/Extreme_Rules_(2011)" title="Extreme Rules (2011)">Extreme Rules</a> pay-per-view in 2011, "Stars and Stripes Forever" played following <a href="/wiki/John_Cena" title="John Cena">John Cena</a> announcing the <a href="/wiki/Reactions_to_the_death_of_Osama_bin_Laden" title="Reactions to the death of Osama bin Laden">death of Osama Bin Laden</a>.</li>
<li>It was played during the January 5, 2015, episode of <a href="/wiki/WWE_Raw" title="WWE Raw">WWE Raw</a> when <a href="/wiki/Triple_H" title="Triple H">Triple H</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stephanie_McMahon" title="Stephanie McMahon">Stephanie McMahon</a>, known as The Authority, organized "John Cena Appreciation Night", only to slander Cena and (kayfabe) fire <a href="/wiki/Dolph_Ziggler" title="Dolph Ziggler">Dolph Ziggler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ryback" title="Ryback">Ryback</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Erick_Rowan" title="Erick Rowan">Erick Rowan</a> at the end of the night. Ziggler, Ryback, and Rowan (along with <a href="/wiki/Big_Show" title="Big Show">Big Show</a> before he turned his back on his team) were Cena's teammates in a match at the past <a href="/wiki/Survivor_Series_(2014)" title="Survivor Series (2014)">Survivor Series</a> that ultimately led to The Authority being stripped of their power, until Cena brought them back on the final Raw of 2014.</li>
<li>In Argentina, a sensationalist news channel <a href="/wiki/Cr%C3%B3nica_TV" title="Crónica TV">Crónica TV</a> always uses part of this march as a background music on reporting a breaking news.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Grateful_Dead" title="The Grateful Dead">The Grateful Dead</a> finished their 50th reunion concert on July 4, 2015 with fireworks accompanied by a recording of "Stars and Stripes Forever", in front of 70,000 people in <a href="/wiki/Soldier_Field" title="Soldier Field">Soldier Field</a> in Chicago. The recording followed an uncharacteristically predictable live encore performance of the band's tongue-in-cheek "U.S. Blues", which led to speculation about whether Sousa's anthem was being celebrated ironically, or championed as a piece of uniquely American entertainment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
