<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ciborium (container)</h1>

<p>A <b>ciborium</b> (plural <b>ciboria</b>; Latin from the Ancient Greek κιβώριον (kibōrion)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OED_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-OED-1">[1]</a></sup>) is a vessel, normally in metal. It was originally a particular shape of drinking cup in Ancient Greece and Rome, but later used to refer to a large covered cup designed to hold hosts for, and after, the <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Eucharist</a>, thus the equivalent for the bread of the <a href="/wiki/Chalice" title="Chalice">chalice</a> for the wine. The word is also used for a <a href="/wiki/Ciborium_(architecture)" title="Ciborium (architecture)">large canopy over the altar</a>, which was a common feature of Early Medieval church architecture, now relatively rare.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciborium_(container)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ancient Greek word referred to the cup-shaped seed vessel of the Egyptian water-lily <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nelumbium_speciosum" title="Nelumbium speciosum">nelumbium speciosum</a></i> and came to describe a drinking cup made from that seed casing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OED_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-OED-1">[1]</a></sup> or in a similar shape. These vessels were particularly common in Egypt and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_East" title="Greek East">Greek East</a>. The word "'ciborium'" was also used in classical Latin to describe such cups,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> although the only example to have survived is in one of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horace%E2%80%99s_odes" title="Horace’s odes">Horace’s odes</a> (2.7.21–22).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-west_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-west-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In medieval Latin, and in English, "Ciborium" more commonly refers to a covered container used in <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Roman Catholic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">Anglican</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheran</a>, and related churches to store the <a href="/wiki/Consecration" title="Consecration">consecrated</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Host_(Holy_Communion)" title="Host (Holy Communion)">hosts</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Sacrament" title="Sacrament">sacrament</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Communion" title="Holy Communion">Holy Communion</a>. It resembles the shape of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chalice_(cup)" title="Chalice (cup)">chalice</a> but its bowl is more round than conical, and takes its name from its cover,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2009)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> surmounted by a cross or other sacred design. In the early Christian Church, Holy Communion was not kept in churches for fear of sacrilege or desecration. Later, the first ciboria were kept at homes to be handy for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Last_Rites" title="Last Rites">Last Rites</a> where needed. In churches, a ciborium is usually kept in a <a href="/wiki/Church_tabernacle" title="Church tabernacle">tabernacle</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aumbry" title="Aumbry">aumbry</a>. In some cases, it may be veiled (see photograph below) to indicate the presence of the consecrated hosts. It is typically made, or at least plated, in a <a href="/wiki/Precious_metal" title="Precious metal">precious metal</a>.</p>
<p>Other containers for the host include the <a href="/wiki/Paten" title="Paten">paten</a> (a small plate) or a basin (for loaves of bread rather than wafers) used at the time of consecration and distribution at the main service of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Eucharist" title="Holy Eucharist">Holy Eucharist</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Pyx" title="Pyx">pyx</a> is a small, circular container into which a few consecrated hosts can be placed. Pyxes are typically used to bring communion to the sick or shut-in.</p>
