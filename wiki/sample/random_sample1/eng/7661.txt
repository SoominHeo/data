<p>A <b>triptych</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">TRIP</span></span></b>-tik</i></a></span>; from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> adjective <i>τρίπτυχον</i> "<i>triptukhon</i>" ("three-fold"), from <i>tri</i>, i.e., "three" and <i>ptysso</i>, i.e., "to fold" or <i>ptyx</i>, i.e., "fold")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is a work of <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a> (usually a <a href="/wiki/Panel_painting" title="Panel painting">panel painting</a>) that is divided into three sections, or three <a href="/wiki/Wood_carving" title="Wood carving">carved</a> panels that are hinged together and can be folded shut or displayed open. It is therefore a type of <a href="/wiki/Polyptych" title="Polyptych">polyptych</a>, the term for all multi-panel works. The middle panel is typically the largest and it is flanked by two smaller related works, although there are triptychs of equal-sized panels. The form can also be used for <a href="/wiki/Pendant" title="Pendant">pendant</a> jewelry.</p>
<p>Despite its connection to an art format, the term is sometimes used more generally to connote anything with three parts, particularly if they are integrated into a single unit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>The triptych form arises from early Christian art, and was a popular standard format for altar paintings from the Middle Ages onwards. Its geographical range was from the eastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine" title="Byzantine">Byzantine</a> churches to the Celtic churches in the west. Renaissance painters such as <a href="/wiki/Hans_Memling" title="Hans Memling">Hans Memling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hieronymus_Bosch" title="Hieronymus Bosch">Hieronymus Bosch</a> used the form. Sculptors also used it. Triptych forms also allow ease of transport.</p>
<p>From the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> period onward, both in Europe and elsewhere, altarpieces in churches and cathedrals were often in triptych form. One such cathedral with an altarpiece triptych is <a href="/wiki/Llandaff_Cathedral" title="Llandaff Cathedral">Llandaff Cathedral</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_Our_Lady_(Antwerp)" title="Cathedral of Our Lady (Antwerp)">Cathedral of Our Lady</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antwerp_(city)" title="Antwerp (city)">Antwerp</a>, Belgium, contains two examples by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">Rubens</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Notre_Dame_de_Paris" title="Notre Dame de Paris">Notre Dame de Paris</a> is another example of the use of triptych in architecture. One can also see the form echoed by the structure of many ecclesiastical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stained_glass_window" title="Stained glass window">stained glass windows</a>. Although strongly identified as an <a href="/wiki/Altarpiece" title="Altarpiece">altarpiece</a> form, triptychs outside that context have been created, some of the best-known examples being works by <a href="/wiki/Hieronymus_Bosch" title="Hieronymus Bosch">Hieronymus Bosch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Max_Beckmann" title="Max Beckmann">Max Beckmann</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon_(artist)" title="Francis Bacon (artist)">Francis Bacon</a>.</p>
<p>The then highest price ever paid for an artwork at auction was $142.4 million for a 1969 triptych, <i><a href="/wiki/Three_Studies_of_Lucian_Freud" title="Three Studies of Lucian Freud">Three Studies of Lucian Freud</a></i>, by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon_(artist)" title="Francis Bacon (artist)">Francis Bacon</a> in November 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The record was broken in May 2015 by $179.4 million for <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Picasso</a>'s 1955 painting <i>Les Femme d’Algers</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The format has migrated and been used in other religions, including <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>. For example: the triptych <i>Hilje-j-Sherif</i> displayed <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_Oriental_Art" title="National Museum of Oriental Art">National Museum of Oriental Art</a>, Rome Italy; and a page of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qu%27ran" title="Qu'ran">Qu'ran</a></i> at the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_and_Islamic_Arts_Museum" title="Turkish and Islamic Arts Museum">Museum of Turkish and Islamic Arts</a> in Istanbul, Turkey, exemplify <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Ottoman" title="Ottoman">Ottoman</a> religious art adapting the motif.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Likewise, Tibetan Buddhists have used it in traditional altars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

Modern photographic triptych</div>

<p>A photographic triptych is a common style used in modern commercial artwork. The photographs are usually arranged with a plain border between them. The work may consist of separate images that are variants on a theme, or may be one larger image split into three.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
