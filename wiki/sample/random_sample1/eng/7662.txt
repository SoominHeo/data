<p>The <b>Sega VR</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Virtual_reality_headset" title="Virtual reality headset">virtual reality headset</a> that was under development by <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">Sega</a>. Versions were planned for <a href="/wiki/Arcade_game" title="Arcade game">arcades</a> and <a href="/wiki/Video_game_console" title="Video game console">consoles</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sega_Genesis" title="Sega Genesis">Sega Genesis</a> and then <a href="/wiki/Sega_Saturn" title="Sega Saturn">Saturn</a>), but only the arcade version was released, while the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Home_console" title="Home console">home console</a> versions were cancelled.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sega_VR&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Sega VR was based on an IDEO <a href="/wiki/Virtual_reality" title="Virtual reality">virtual reality</a> headset (HMD) with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_crystal_display" title="Liquid crystal display">LCD</a> screens in the visor and stereo headphones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horowitz_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Horowitz-1">[1]</a></sup> Inertial sensors in the headset allowed the system to <a href="/wiki/Tracking_system" title="Tracking system">track</a> and react to the movements of the user's head.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sega_VR&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sega, flush with funds from the success of its <a href="/wiki/Sega_Genesis" title="Sega Genesis">Sega Genesis</a> (released as the Mega Drive outside of North America), announced the console in 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horowitz_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Horowitz-1">[1]</a></sup> It was later seen in early <a href="/wiki/1993_in_video_gaming" title="1993 in video gaming">1993</a>, at the Winter <a href="/wiki/Consumer_Electronics_Show" title="Consumer Electronics Show">Consumer Electronics Show</a> (Winter CES), where <i><a href="/wiki/Electronic_Gaming_Monthly" title="Electronic Gaming Monthly">Electronic Gaming Monthly</a></i> noted it was an adaptation of a similar headset that Sega were already using for <a href="/wiki/Amusement_arcade" title="Amusement arcade">arcades</a>. The magazine stated that a Mega Drive/Genesis version was planned for release in fall 1993 at $200 and would release with four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Launch_game" title="Launch game">launch games</a>, including a port of <a href="/wiki/Arcade_game" title="Arcade game">arcade game</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Virtua_Racing" title="Virtua Racing">Virtua Racing</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-egm_wces93_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-egm_wces93-2">[2]</a></sup> Sega later announced that it is scheduled for release in spring 1994, according to <i><a href="/wiki/Electronic_Games" title="Electronic Games">Electronic Games</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because of development difficulties, the console Sega VR headset remained only a prototype, and was never released to the general public. Then CEO Tom Kalinske stated that the system was not released due to it inducing motion sickness and severe headaches in users.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It was last seen at the 1993 Summer CES, where it was demonstrated by <a href="/wiki/Alan_Hunter_(VJ)" title="Alan Hunter (VJ)">Alan Hunter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It vanished from release schedules in 1994. Four games were apparently developed for the system, each using 16 <a href="/wiki/Megabyte" title="Megabyte">MB</a> cartridges that were to be bundled with the headset.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horowitz_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Horowitz-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The company claimed to have terminated the project because the virtual reality effect was too realistic. Users might move while wearing the headset and injure themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horowitz_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Horowitz-1">[1]</a></sup> The limited processing power of the system makes this claim unlikely, although there were reports of testers developing <a href="/wiki/Headache" title="Headache">headaches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Motion_sickness" title="Motion sickness">motion sickness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horowitz_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Horowitz-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mark_Pesce" title="Mark Pesce">Mark Pesce</a>, who worked on the Sega VR project, says <a href="/wiki/SRI_International" title="SRI International">SRI International</a>, a research institute, warned Sega of the 'hazards of prolonged use'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Games">Games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sega_VR&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Only four original games were known to be in development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>Nuclear Rush</i>: A simulation in which users pilot a hovercraft in a futuristic war.</li>
<li><i>Iron Hammer</i>: In this helicopter simulation, gamers pilot a flying gunship a la EA’s popular “Strike” series.</li>
<li><i>Matrix Runner</i>: reported to be a “<a href="/wiki/Cyberpunk" title="Cyberpunk">cyberpunk</a>” <a href="/wiki/Adventure_game" title="Adventure game">adventure game</a> inspired by <a href="/wiki/Hideo_Kojima" title="Hideo Kojima">Hideo Kojima</a>’s <i><a href="/wiki/Snatcher_(video_game)" title="Snatcher (video game)">Snatcher</a></i>.</li>
<li><i>Outlaw Racing</i>: Road Rash meets Rock -n- Roll Racing in this vehicle racing/combat game.</li>
</ul>
<p>In addition, Sega also announced a <a href="/wiki/Porting" title="Porting">port</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sega_AM2" title="Sega AM2">Sega AM2</a>'s hit <a href="/wiki/1992_in_video_gaming" title="1992 in video gaming">1992</a> <a href="/wiki/Arcade_game" title="Arcade game">arcade game</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Virtua_Racing" title="Virtua Racing">Virtua Racing</a></i> as a launch title for the device.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-egm_wces93_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-egm_wces93-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sega_VR&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sega went on to other VR projects for use in arcades and a similar add-on was reported but never seen for the <a href="/wiki/Sega_Saturn" title="Sega Saturn">Sega Saturn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horowitz_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Horowitz-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The project encouraged a brief flurry of other companies to offer VR products.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Sega's chief competitor, <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a>, would go on to release the ill-fated <a href="/wiki/Virtual_Boy" title="Virtual Boy">Virtual Boy</a> in 1995. The table-top device also brought discomfort after extended play.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WaPo_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-WaPo-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1994, Sega VR technology was utilized for the Sega VR-1 motion simulator <a href="/wiki/Arcade_game" title="Arcade game">arcade attraction</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> which was available at <a href="/wiki/SegaWorld" title="SegaWorld">SegaWorld</a> <a href="/wiki/Amusement_arcade" title="Amusement arcade">arcades</a>. It was able to track head movement and featured <a href="/wiki/3D_computer_graphics" title="3D computer graphics">3D polygon graphics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Stereoscopic_video_game" title="Stereoscopic video game">stereoscopic 3D</a>. A scaled-down version, <i>Dennoo Senki Net Merc</i>, was demonstrated at Japan's 1995 AOU (Amusement Operators Union) show, and it used the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sega_Model_1" title="Sega Model 1">Sega Model 1</a> <a href="/wiki/Arcade_system_board" title="Arcade system board">arcade system board</a> to produce the 3D graphics. However, the game's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flat_shading" title="Flat shading">flat-shaded</a> graphics were compared unfavourably to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sega_Model_2" title="Sega Model 2">Sega Model 2</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Texture_filtering" title="Texture filtering">textured-filtered</a> graphics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
