<p>The <b>World Economic Forum</b> (<b>WEF</b>) is a Swiss nonprofit <a href="/wiki/Foundation_(nonprofit)" title="Foundation (nonprofit)">foundation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cologny" title="Cologny">Cologny</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canton_of_geneva" title="Canton of geneva">Canton of geneva</a>. Recognized by the Swiss authorities as an international body,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> its mission is cited as "committed to improving the state of the world by engaging business, political, academic, and other leaders of society to shape global, regional, and industry agendas".</p>
<p>The Forum is best known for its annual meeting at the end of January in <a href="/wiki/Davos" title="Davos">Davos</a>, a mountain resort in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graub%C3%BCnden" title="Graubünden">Graubünden</a>, in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a> region of Switzerland. The meeting brings together some 2,500 top business leaders, international political leaders, <a href="/wiki/Economist" title="Economist">economists</a>, and journalists for up to four days to discuss the most pressing <a href="/wiki/Problem_solving" title="Problem solving">issues</a> facing the <a href="/wiki/World" title="World">world</a>. Often this location alone is used to identify meetings, participation, and participants with such phrases as, "a Davos panel" and "a Davos Man".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The organization also convenes some six to eight regional meetings each year in locations across Africa, East Asia and Latin America, as well as undertaking two further annual meetings in China and the United Arab Emirates. Beside meetings, the foundation produces a series of research reports and engages its members in sector specific initiatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pigman_p41-42_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pigman_p41-42-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>The forum was founded in 1971 by <a href="/wiki/Klaus_Schwab" title="Klaus Schwab">Klaus Schwab</a>, a German-born business professor at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Geneva" title="University of Geneva">University of Geneva</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> First named the "European Management Forum", it changed its name to the World Economic Forum in 1987 and sought to broaden its vision to include providing a platform for resolving international conflicts.</p>
<p>In the summer of 1971, Schwab invited 444 executives from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_European" title="Western European">Western European</a> firms to the first European Management Symposium held in the <a href="/wiki/Davos_Congress_Centre" title="Davos Congress Centre">Davos Congress Centre</a> under the patronage of the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> and European industrial associations, where Schwab sought to introduce European firms to American management practices. He then founded the WEF as a nonprofit organization based in Geneva and drew European business leaders to Davos for the annual meetings each January.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Schwab developed the <a href="/wiki/Stakeholder_theory" title="Stakeholder theory">"stakeholder" management approach</a>, which attributed corporate success to managers actively taking account of all interests: not merely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shareholders" title="Shareholders">shareholders</a>, clients, and customers, but also employees and the communities within which the firm is situated, including governments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Events in 1973, including the collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Bretton_Woods_system" title="Bretton Woods system">Bretton Woods</a> fixed-exchange rate mechanism and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab%E2%80%93Israeli_War" title="Arab–Israeli War">Arab–Israeli War</a>, saw the annual meeting expand its focus from management to economic and social issues, and, for the first time political leaders were invited to the annual meeting in January 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Political leaders soon began to use the annual meeting as a neutral platform. The <i>Davos Declaration</i> was signed in 1988 by <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, helping them turn back from the brink of war. In 1992, South African President <a href="/wiki/F._W._de_Klerk" title="F. W. de Klerk">F. W. de Klerk</a> met with <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Mandela" title="Nelson Mandela">Nelson Mandela</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mangosuthu_Buthelezi" title="Mangosuthu Buthelezi">Chief Mangosuthu Buthelezi</a> at the annual meeting, their first joint appearance outside South Africa. At the 1994 annual meeting, Israeli Foreign Minister <a href="/wiki/Shimon_Peres" title="Shimon Peres">Shimon Peres</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Palestine_Liberation_Organization" title="Chairman of the Palestine Liberation Organization">PLO chairman</a> <a href="/wiki/Yasser_Arafat" title="Yasser Arafat">Yasser Arafat</a> reached a draft agreement on <a href="/wiki/Gaza_City" title="Gaza City">Gaza</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jericho" title="Jericho">Jericho</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late 2015, the invitation was extended to include a North Korean delegation for the 2016 forum, "in view of positive signs coming out of the country," the WEF organizers noted. North Korea has not been attending the WEF since 1998. The invitation was accepted but after the <a href="/wiki/January_2016_North_Korean_nuclear_test" title="January 2016 North Korean nuclear test">January 2016 North Korean nuclear test</a> on 6 January, the invitation was revoked, and the country's delegation was made subject to "existing and possible forthcoming sanctions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Despite protests by North Korea calling the decision by the WEF managing board a "sudden and irresponsible" move, the WEF committee maintained the exclusion because "under these circumstances there would be no opportunity for international dialogue."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2017, the World Economic Forum in Davos attracted considerable attention when for the first time, a head of state from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> was invited to the alpine resort. With the backdrop of <a href="/wiki/Brexit" title="Brexit">Brexit</a>, an incoming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protectionist" title="Protectionist">protectionist</a> US administration and significant pressures on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_trade_zone" title="Free trade zone">free trade zones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trade_agreement" title="Trade agreement">trade agreements</a>, President <a href="/wiki/Xi_Jinping" title="Xi Jinping">Xi Jinping</a> defended the global economic scheme, and portrayed China as a responsible nation and a leader for environmental causes. He sharply rebuked the current populist movements that would introduce tariffs and hinder global commerce, warning that such protectionism could foster isolation and reduced economic opportunity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Headquartered in Cologny, the forum also has offices in New York, Beijing and Tokyo. On October 10, 2016, the Forum announced the opening of its new Center for the Fourth Industrial Revolution in San Francisco. According to the Forum, the center will "serve as a platform for interaction, insight and impact on the scientific and technological changes that are changing the way we live, work and relate to one another".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The World Economic Forum strives to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impartial" title="Impartial">impartial</a> and is not tied to any political, <a href="/wiki/Partisan_(political)" title="Partisan (political)">partisan</a>, or national interests. The foundation is "committed to improving the State of the World".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Until 2012, it had <a href="/wiki/Observer_status" title="Observer status">observer status</a> with the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Economic_and_Social_Council" title="United Nations Economic and Social Council">United Nations Economic and Social Council</a>; it is under the supervision of the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Federal_Council" title="Swiss Federal Council">Swiss Federal Council</a>. The foundation's highest governance body is the foundation board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>During its annual meeting, more than 2,500 participants from slightly fewer than 100 countries gather in Davos. Approximately 1,500 are business leaders,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> drawn from its members, 1,000 of the world's top companies. Besides these, participants included 219 public figures, including 40 heads of state or government, 64 cabinet ministers, 30 heads or senior officials of international organizations, and 10 ambassadors. More than 432 participants were from civil society, including 32 heads or representatives of non-governmental organizations, 225 media leaders, 149 leaders from academic institutions and think tanks, 15 religious leaders of different faiths, and 11 union leaders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (January 2012)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Membership">Membership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Membership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The foundation is funded by its 1,000 member companies, typically global enterprises with more than five billion dollars in turnover (varying by industry and region). These enterprises rank among the top companies within their industry and/or country and play a leading role in shaping the future of their industry and/or region. Membership is stratified by the level of engagement with forum activities, with the level of membership fees increasing as participation in meetings, projects, and initiatives rises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> As of 2011, an annual membership costs $52,000 for an individual member, $263,000 for "Industry Partner" and $527,000 for "Strategic Partner". An admission fee costs $19,000 per person.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 2014, WEF raised annual fees by 20 percent, making the cost for "Strategic Partner" from SFr500,000 ($523,000) to SFr600,000 ($628,000).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Activities">Activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Annual_meeting_in_Davos">Annual meeting in Davos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Annual meeting in Davos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The flagship event of the foundation is the invitation-only annual meeting held during the winter at the end of January in Davos, Switzerland, bringing together chief executive officers from its 1,000 member companies, as well as selected politicians, representatives from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academia" title="Academia">academia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NGOs" title="NGOs">NGOs</a>, religious leaders, and the media in an <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Alps" title="Swiss Alps">alpine</a> winter environment. The town is small enough to allow participants to meet anywhere outside the sessions and allows them the greatest opportunities to attend receptions organized by companies and countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The participants are also taking part in role playing events, such as the Investment Heat Map.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Informal winter meetings may have led to as many ideas and solutions as the official sessions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Approximately 2,200 participants gather for the five-day event and attend some of the 220 sessions in the official programme. The winter discussions focus around key issues of global concern (such as the globalization, capital markets, wealth management, international conflicts, environmental problems and their possible solutions).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pigman_p41-42_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pigman_p41-42-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>As many as 500 journalists from online, print, radio, and television take part, with access to all sessions in the official program, some of which are also <a href="/wiki/Webcast" title="Webcast">webcast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Not all the journalists are given access to all areas, however. This is reserved for white badge holders. "Davos runs an almost caste-like system of badges," according to BBC journalist Anthony Reuben. "A white badge means you're one of the delegates – you might be the chief executive of a company or the leader of a country (although that would also get you a little holographic sticker to add to your badge), or a senior journalist. An orange badge means you're just a run-of-the-mill working journalist."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>All <a href="/wiki/Plenary_session" title="Plenary session">plenary</a> debates from the annual meeting also are available on YouTube,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> with photographs at <a href="/wiki/Flickr" title="Flickr">Flickr</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and key quotes on Twitter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2007, the foundation established the Annual Meeting of the New Champions (also called Summer Davos), held annually in China, alternating between <a href="/wiki/Dalian" title="Dalian">Dalian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tianjin" title="Tianjin">Tianjin</a>, bringing together 1,500 participants from what the foundation calls Global Growth Companies, primarily from rapidly growing emerging countries such as China, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, Russia, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, but also including quickly growing companies from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developed_countries" title="Developed countries">developed countries</a>. The meeting also engages with the next generation of global leaders from fast-growing regions and competitive cities, as well as technology pioneers from around the globe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The Chinese <a href="/wiki/Premier_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Premier of the People's Republic of China">Premier</a> has delivered a plenary address at each annual meeting.</p>

<p>Every year regional meetings take place, enabling close contact among corporate business leaders, local government leaders, and NGOs. Meetings are held in Africa, East Asia, Latin America, and the Middle East. The mix of hosting countries varies from year to year, but consistently China and India have hosted throughout the decade since 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Young_Global_Leaders">Young Global Leaders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Young Global Leaders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The group's Forum of Young Global Leaders<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> consists of <a href="/wiki/List_of_Young_Global_Leaders" title="List of Young Global Leaders">800 people</a> chosen by the forum organizers as being representative of contemporary leadership, "coming from all regions of the world and representing all stakeholders in society", according to the organization. After five years of participation they are considered alumni.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_Entrepreneurs">Social Entrepreneurs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Social Entrepreneurs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 2000, the WEF has been promoting models developed by those in close collaboration with the <a href="/wiki/Schwab_Foundation_for_Social_Entrepreneurship" title="Schwab Foundation for Social Entrepreneurship">Schwab Foundation for Social Entrepreneurship</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> highlighting <a href="/wiki/Social_entrepreneurship" title="Social entrepreneurship">social entrepreneurship</a> as a key element to advance societies and address social problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Selected social entrepreneurs are invited to participate in the foundation's regional meetings and the annual meetings where they may meet chief executives and senior government officials. At the Annual Meeting 2003, for example, <a href="/wiki/Jeroo_Billimoria" title="Jeroo Billimoria">Jeroo Billimoria</a> met with Roberto Blois, deputy secretary-general of the <a href="/wiki/International_Telecommunication_Union" title="International Telecommunication Union">International Telecommunication Union</a>, an encounter that produced a key partnership for her organization <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Child_helpline_international" title="Child helpline international">Child helpline international</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_Shapers">Global Shapers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Global Shapers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2011, the World Economic Forum started a global network of people between the ages of 20 and 30 who have shown great potential for future leadership roles in society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The Community of <a href="/wiki/Global_Shapers" title="Global Shapers">Global Shapers</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> highlighting <a href="/wiki/Global_Shapers" title="Global Shapers">Global Shapers</a> is a network of self-organizing local hubs based in each major city around the world. They undertake events and activities intended by the Global Shapers to generate a positive impact within their local community.</p>
<p>As of 11 July 2016 there are 459 Hubs with more than 6,100 Shapers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thirst">Thirst</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Thirst">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Thirst" is an international non-profit organisation based in Beijing, China. It was set up in 2011 through the Water Security Council of the World Economic Forum. The organization educates 14–24-year olds about the idea of embedded water, the water crisis, and sustainable water usage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>

<p>The "Competitiveness Team"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> produces a range of annual economic reports (first published in brackets): the <a href="/wiki/Global_Competitiveness_Report" title="Global Competitiveness Report">Global Competitiveness Report</a> (1979) measured competitiveness of countries and economies; The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_Information_Technology_Report" title="Global Information Technology Report">Global Information Technology Report</a> (2001) assessed their competitiveness based on their IT readiness; the <a href="/wiki/Global_Gender_Gap_Report" title="Global Gender Gap Report">Global Gender Gap Report</a> examined critical areas of inequality between men and women; the <a href="/wiki/Global_Risks_Report" title="Global Risks Report">Global Risks Report</a> (2006) assessed key global risks; the <a href="/wiki/Travel_and_Tourism_Competitiveness_Report" title="Travel and Tourism Competitiveness Report">Global Travel and Tourism Report</a> (2007) measured travel and tourism competitiveness; the Financial Development Report (2008)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> aimed to provide a comprehensive means for countries to establish benchmarks for various aspects of their financial systems and establish priorities for improvement; and the <a href="/wiki/Global_Enabling_Trade_Report" title="Global Enabling Trade Report">Global Enabling Trade Report</a> (2008) presented a cross-country analysis of the large number of measures facilitating trade among nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The "Risk Response Network"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> produces a yearly report assessing risks which are deemed to be within the scope of these teams, have cross-industry relevance, are uncertain, have the potential to cause upwards of US$10 billion in economic damage, have the potential to cause major human suffering, and which require a multi-stakeholder approach for mitigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riskreport_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-riskreport-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Initiatives">Initiatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Initiatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Global Health Initiative was launched by <a href="/wiki/Kofi_Annan" title="Kofi Annan">Kofi Annan</a> at the annual meeting in 2002. The GHI's mission was to engage businesses in public-private partnerships to tackle HIV/AIDS, <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>, and health systems.</p>
<p>The Global Education Initiative (GEI), launched during the annual meeting in 2003, brought together international IT companies and governments in Jordan, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> that has resulted in new personal computer hardware being available in their classrooms and more local teachers trained in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-learning" title="E-learning">e-learning</a>. This is having a significant effect on the lives of children.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The GEI model, which is scalable and sustainable, now is being used as an educational blueprint in other countries including <a href="/wiki/Rwanda" title="Rwanda">Rwanda</a>.</p>
<p>The Environmental Initiative covers climate change and water issues. Under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2005_G8_Summit" title="2005 G8 Summit">Gleneagles Dialogue on Climate Change</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">U.K. government</a> asked the World Economic Forum at the <a href="/wiki/31st_G8_summit" title="31st G8 summit">G8 Summit in Gleneagles in 2005</a> to facilitate a dialogue with the business community to develop recommendations for reducing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas emissions">greenhouse gas emissions</a>. This set of recommendations, endorsed by a global group of CEOs, was presented to leaders ahead of the G8 Summit in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toyako" title="Toyako">Toyako</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hokkaido" title="Hokkaido">Hokkaido</a> held in July 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Water Initiative brings together diverse stakeholders such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcan_Inc." title="Alcan Inc.">Alcan Inc.</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Agency_for_Development_and_Cooperation" title="Swiss Agency for Development and Cooperation">Swiss Agency for Development and Cooperation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USAID" title="USAID">USAID</a> India, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNDP" title="UNDP">UNDP</a> India, <a href="/wiki/Confederation_of_Indian_Industry" title="Confederation of Indian Industry">Confederation of Indian Industry</a> (CII), Government of <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">Rajasthan</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NEPAD" title="NEPAD">NEPAD</a> Business Foundation to develop public-private partnerships on water management in South Africa and India.</p>
<p>In an effort to combat corruption, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Partnering_Against_Corruption_Initiative&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Partnering Against Corruption Initiative (page does not exist)">Partnering Against Corruption Initiative</a> (PACI) was launched by CEOs from the Engineering and Construction, Energy and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metals" title="Metals">Metals</a>, and Mining industries at the annual meeting in Davos during January 2004. PACI is a platform for peer exchange on practical experience and dilemma situations. Approximately 140 companies have joined the initiative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Environment_and_Natural_Resource_Security_Initiative&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Environment and Natural Resource Security Initiative (page does not exist)">Environment and Natural Resource Security Initiative</a> was emphasized for the 2017 meeting to achieve inclusive economic growth and sustainable practices for global industries. With increasing limitations on <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/World_trade" title="World trade">world trade</a> through national interests and trade barriers, the WEF has moved towards a more sensitive and socially minded approach for global businesses with a focus on the reduction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_emission" title="Carbon emission">carbon emissions</a> in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and other large industrial nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 19th 2017, the <a href="/wiki/Coalition_for_Epidemic_Preparedness_Innovations" title="Coalition for Epidemic Preparedness Innovations">Coalition for Epidemic Preparedness Innovations</a> (CEPI), a global initiative to fight epidemics, was launched at the forum in Davos. The internationally funded initiative aims at securing vaccine supplies for global emergencies and pandemics, and to research new vaccines for tropical diseases, that are now more menacing. The project is funded by private and governmental donors, with an initial investment of US$460m from the governments of Germany, Japan and Norway, plus the Bill &amp; Melinda Gates Foundation and the Wellcome Trust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology_Pioneers_Programme">Technology Pioneers Programme</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Technology Pioneers Programme">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Technology Pioneers Programme recognizes companies that are designing and developing new technologies. The award is given to 30–50 companies each year. Since 2000, Technology Pioneers have consisted of more than 400 companies from 5 continents.</p>
<p>The Tech Pioneers are integrated into programme activities with the objective to identify and address future-oriented issues on the global agenda in proactive, innovative, and entrepreneurial ways. By bringing these executives together with scientists, academics, NGOs, and foundation members and partners, the foundation's goal is to shed new light on how technologies may be used to address, for example, finding new vaccines, creating economic growth, and enhancement of global communication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Demo-gegen-wef.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Demo-gegen-wef.jpg/220px-Demo-gegen-wef.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Demo-gegen-wef.jpg/330px-Demo-gegen-wef.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Demo-gegen-wef.jpg/440px-Demo-gegen-wef.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Demo-gegen-wef.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Protest march against the WEF in <a href="/wiki/Basel" title="Basel">Basel</a>, 2006</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Transnational_Institute" title="Transnational Institute">Transnational Institute</a> describes the World Economic Forum’s main purpose as:</p>
<p>"to function as a socializing institution for the emerging global elite, globalization’s "Mafiocracy" of bankers, industrialists, oligarchs, technocrats and politicians. They promote common ideas, and serve common interests: their own."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>A study, published in the Journal of Commercial Research, investigated the sociological impact of the WEF. It concluded that the WEF do not solve issues such as poverty, global warming, chronic illness, or debt. They have simply shifted the burden for the solution of these problems from governments and business to "responsible consumers subjects: the bottom-of-the-pyramid consumer, the green consumer, the health-conscious consumer, and the financially literate consumer." They merely reframe the issues, and by so doing perpetuate them. Gore is singled out as a prime example. Gore's speeches deliberately shift focus away from the problems of unregulated markets and corporate activities to one of moral pathologies, individual greed, etc. In doing so he is actually promoting the creation of new markets, and hence perpetuating the same old problems in a new guise. New markets will follow the same patterns as the old ones because the core problem of corporate governance is never addressed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_criticism">History of criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: History of criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the late 1990s the foundation, along with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G7" title="G7">G7</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">World Trade Organization</a>, and <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a>, came under heavy criticism by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-globalization" title="Anti-globalization">anti-globalization</a> activists who claimed that capitalism and globalization were increasing poverty and destroying the environment. Ten thousand demonstrators disrupted the World Economic Forum in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, obstructing the path of two hundred delegates to the meeting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Repeatedly, demonstrations are held in Davos <i>(see <a href="/wiki/Anti-WEF_protests_in_Switzerland,_January_2003" title="Anti-WEF protests in Switzerland, January 2003">Anti-WEF protests in Switzerland, January 2003</a>)</i> to protest against what have been called the meetings of "fat cats in the snow", a <a href="/wiki/Tongue-in-cheek" title="Tongue-in-cheek">tongue-in-cheek</a> term used by rock singer Bono.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>After 2014, the protest movement against the World Economic Forum has largely died down, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swiss_police" title="Swiss police">Swiss police</a> noted a significant decline in attending protesters, 20 at most during the meeting in 2016. While protesters are still more numerous in large Swiss cities, the protest movement itself has undergone significant change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Around 150 <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_people" title="Tibetan people">Tibetans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uyghurs" title="Uyghurs">Uighurs</a> protested in Geneva and 400 Tibetans in Bern against the visit of the Chinese president for the 2017 meeting, with subsequent confrontations and arrests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Globalization_rationale_questioned">Globalization rationale questioned</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Globalization rationale questioned">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Professor Emeritus at <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">MIT</a> <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">Noam Chomsky</a>, an American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linguist" title="Linguist">linguist</a> and outspoken intellectual, has characterized the term "globalization" as propaganda when used in connection with trade policies advanced by the World Economic Forum:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The dominant propaganda systems have appropriated the term 'globalization' to refer to the specific version of international economic integration that they favor, which privileges the rights of investors and lenders, those of people being incidental. In accord with this usage, those who favor a different form of international integration, which privileges the rights of human beings, become 'anti-globalist.' This is simply vulgar propaganda, like the term 'anti-Soviet' used by the most disgusting commissars to refer to dissidents. It is not only vulgar, but idiotic. Take the <a href="/wiki/World_Social_Forum" title="World Social Forum">World Social Forum</a>, called 'anti-globalization' in the propaganda system—which happens to include the media, the educated classes, etc., with rare exceptions. The WSF is a paradigm example of globalization. It is a gathering of huge numbers of people from all over the world, from just about every corner of life one can think of, apart from the extremely narrow highly privileged elites who meet at the competing World Economic Forum, and are called 'pro-globalization' by the propaganda system. An observer watching this farce from Mars would collapse in hysterical laughter at the antics of the educated classes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_cost_of_security">Public cost of security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Public cost of security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In January 2000, a thousand protesters marched through the streets of Davos and smashed the window of the local <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald's">McDonald's</a> restaurant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> The tight security measures around the campus of Davos have kept demonstrators from the Alpine resort, and most demonstrations now are held in <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" title="Zürich">Zürich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bern" title="Bern">Bern</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Basel" title="Basel">Basel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The costs of the security measures, which are shared by the foundation and the Swiss cantonal and national authorities, frequently have been criticised in the Swiss national media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_vs_public_meetings">Private vs public meetings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Private vs public meetings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since the annual meeting in January 2003 in Davos, an <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Open_Forum_Davos&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Open Forum Davos (page does not exist)">Open Forum Davos</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> co-organized by the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Reformed_Church" title="Swiss Reformed Church">Federation of Swiss Protestant Churches</a>, is held concurrently with the Davos forum, opening up the debate about globalization to the general public. The Open Forum has been held in the local high school every year, featuring top politicians and business leaders. It is open to all members of the public free of charge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>The annual meeting of the forum also has been decried as a "mix of pomp and platitude", and criticized for moving away from serious economics and accomplishing little of substance, particularly with the increasing involvement of NGOs that have little or no expertise in economics. Instead of a discussion on the world economy with knowledgeable experts alongside key business and political players, the annual meeting of the forum now features the top political topics of the day appearing in media, such as global climate change and AIDS in Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_of_financial_supporters">Influence of financial supporters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Influence of financial supporters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Faculty member <a href="/wiki/Steven_Strauss" title="Steven Strauss">Steven Strauss</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvard_Kennedy_School" title="Harvard Kennedy School">Harvard Kennedy School</a>, has raised an additional concern, pointing out that many of the WEF's strategic partners (who in return for financing the annual meeting have the ability to set the intellectual agenda for the meeting) have been convicted of serious criminal, civil, or human rights violations, raising significant issues about the forum's legitimacy as a neutral convener on certain topics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_Eye_Awards">Public Eye Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Public Eye Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Eye_Awards" title="Public Eye Awards">Public Eye Awards</a> have been held every year since 2000. It is a counter-event to the annual meeting of the World Economic Forum (WEF) in Davos. Public Eye Awards is a public competition of the worst corporations in the world. In 2011, more than 50,000 people voted for companies that acted irresponsibly. At a ceremony at a Davos hotel, the "winners" in 2011 were named as Indonesian <a href="/wiki/Palm_oil" title="Palm oil">palm oil</a> diesel maker, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neste_Oil" title="Neste Oil">Neste Oil</a> in Finland, and mining company <a href="/wiki/AngloGold_Ashanti" title="AngloGold Ashanti">AngloGold Ashanti</a> in South Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Schweiz_aktuell" title="Schweiz aktuell">Schweiz aktuell</a> broadcast on 16 January 2015, a public presence during the WEF 2015, may not be guaranteed because the massively increased security in Davos. The Public Eye Award will be awarded for the last time in Davos: <i>Public Eyes says Goodbye to Davos</i>, confirmed by Rolf Marugg (now <i>Landrats</i> politician), by not directly engaged politicians, and by the police responsible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schweizaktuell-20150116_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-schweizaktuell-20150116-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Davos_Man.22">"Davos Man"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title='Edit section: "Davos Man"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Davos Man" is a <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologism</a> referring to the global elite of wealthy (predominantly) men, whose members view themselves as completely "international". It is similar to the term <i>Masters of the Universe</i>, applied to influential financiers on <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a>.</p>
<p>Davos men supposedly see their identity as a matter of personal choice, not an accident of birth. According to political scientist <a href="/wiki/Samuel_P._Huntington" title="Samuel P. Huntington">Samuel P. Huntington</a>, who is credited with inventing the phrase "Davos Man",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> they are people who "have little need for national loyalty, view national boundaries as obstacles that thankfully are vanishing, and see national governments as residues from the past whose only useful function is to facilitate the élite's global operations". In his 2004 article "Dead Souls: The Denationalization of the American Elite", Huntington argues that this international perspective is a minority elitist position not shared by the nationalist majority of the people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Fonte" title="John Fonte">John Fonte</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Institute" title="Hudson Institute">Hudson Institute</a> has suggested that the transnational ideology of <i>Davos Man</i> represents a major challenge to <a href="/wiki/Francis_Fukuyama" title="Francis Fukuyama">Francis Fukuyama</a>'s assertion that <a href="/wiki/Liberal_democracy" title="Liberal democracy">liberal democracy</a> represents the fulfillment of <i><a href="/wiki/The_End_of_History_and_the_Last_Man" title="The End of History and the Last Man">The End of History and the Last Man</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gender_debate">Gender debate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Gender debate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 2011, the World Economic Forum has been addressing its very own <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gender_quota" title="Gender quota">gender quota</a>, to introduce at least one woman for every five senior executives that attended. With apparent success, female participation increased significantly from 9% to 15% between 2001 and 2005. In 2016, 18% of the WEF attendees were female, this number increased to 21% in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stateless_elitism_erosion">Stateless elitism erosion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Economic_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Stateless elitism erosion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hernando_de_Soto_Polar" title="Hernando de Soto Polar">Hernando de Soto Polar</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_Liberty_and_Democracy" title="Institute for Liberty and Democracy">Institute for Liberty and Democracy</a> attributes a similar concept to <a href="/wiki/Fernand_Braudel" title="Fernand Braudel">Fernand Braudel</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> referring to it as the "bell jar". Although internationally connected, each country's elite lives in a <a href="/wiki/Bell_jar" title="Bell jar">bell jar</a> in the sense of being out of touch with its own populace. Their isolation fosters a tendency to be oblivious to the fate of their fellow citizens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Summers" title="Lawrence Summers">Lawrence Summers</a> refers to this concept as the "stateless elites", tied more to the success of the global economy than to any nation, and views it as eroding support for continuing globalization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
